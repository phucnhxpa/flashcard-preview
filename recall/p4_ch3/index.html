P4 Ch 3 — Coordinate Geometry / Parametric Equations

The whole chapter in one mental model. A curve can be described parametrically as $x=p(t),\ y=q(t)$, where $t$ runs through a domain. To go to Cartesian, eliminate $t$: rearrange one equation for $t$ and substitute, OR use a trig identity (most often $\sin^2 t+\cos^2 t=1$ or $1+\cot^2 t=\csc^2 t$ or $1+\tan^2 t=\sec^2 t$). The domain of the Cartesian curve = range of $x(t)$; the range = range of $y(t)$. Sketch by table-of-values when no familiar form pops out.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Parameter $t$ A third variable — neither $x$ nor $y$ — that controls a point's position on the curve. As $t$ varies, the point $(x(t),y(t))$ traces out the curve. Generally $t$ is not the $x$- or $y$-coordinate. 3.1
Parametric equations A pair $x=p(t)$ and $y=q(t)$ defining a curve as $t$ ranges over a domain. Useful when the Cartesian form is awkward (e.g. circles, ellipses, swirling shapes). 3.1
Cartesian equation An equation in $x$ and $y$ only (no $t$). The "implicit" or "explicit" form. To get from parametric → Cartesian, eliminate the parameter. 3.1
Domain (of parameter) The set of allowed $t$ values, e.g. $-3 3.1
Domain of Cartesian $f$ The set of $x$-values the curve covers. Equals range of $x(t)$ as $t$ varies through its parameter domain. 3.1
Range of Cartesian $f$ The set of $y$-values the curve covers. Equals range of $y(t)$. 3.1
Eliminate the parameter Algebraic step that turns parametric into Cartesian. Two main techniques: substitution (rearrange $t$ from one equation) or trig identity (when both equations involve trig functions). 3.1, 3.2
Pythagorean identity $\sin^2 t+\cos^2 t\equiv 1$. The workhorse for trig parametrics. 3.2
Reciprocal identities $1+\tan^2 t\equiv\sec^2 t$ and $1+\cot^2 t\equiv\csc^2 t$. Use when parametrics involve $\tan,\cot,\sec,\csc$. 3.2
Double angle $\sin 2t=2\sin t\cos t$, $\cos 2t=1-2\sin^2 t=2\cos^2 t-1$. Convert $\sin 2t,\cos 2t$ in terms of $\sin t,\cos t$ before eliminating. 3.2 Ex 4
Circle parametrisation $x=h+r\cos t,\ y=k+r\sin t$ traces the circle $(x-h)^2+(y-k)^2=r^2$ once as $t$ ranges over $[0,2\pi)$. 3.2 Ex 3
Ellipse parametrisation $x=h+a\cos t,\ y=k+b\sin t$ traces the ellipse $\dfrac{(x-h)^2}{a^2}+\dfrac{(y-k)^2}{b^2}=1$. 3.3 Ex 6
Trace direction The direction in which the curve is drawn as $t$ increases. Important when the same Cartesian curve can be parametrised differently. 3.3
Sketching by table When no recognisable Cartesian form, plot $(x(t),y(t))$ for several $t$ values and join the dots smoothly. Always sketch on a calculator first if allowed. 3.3

2. Elimination methods — choose by form

Spot this in parametric eqs Method
$x$ or $y$ is solvable for $t$ algebraically (linear, exp, log, polynomial) Substitution: rearrange for $t$, plug into the other
Both eqs involve $\sin t,\cos t$ Use $\sin^2 t+\cos^2 t=1$
Eqs involve $\sec t,\tan t$ Use $1+\tan^2 t=\sec^2 t$
Eqs involve $\csc t,\cot t$ Use $1+\cot^2 t=\csc^2 t$
One eq has $\sin 2t$ or $\cos 2t$ Expand via double angle, then eliminate
Eqs have $\sin(t+\phi)$ Expand via addition formula, then substitute
Eqs have $t^2$ on one side, $t$ on the other Solve linear for $t$, substitute and square

3. Standard parametric forms (memorise)

Curve Parametric form Cartesian
Line through $(x_0,y_0)$, direction $(a,b)$ $x=x_0+at,\ y=y_0+bt$ $\dfrac{x-x_0}{a}=\dfrac{y-y_0}{b}$
Circle, centre $(h,k)$, radius $r$ $x=h+r\cos t,\ y=k+r\sin t$, $0\le t<2\pi$ $(x-h)^2+(y-k)^2=r^2$
Ellipse, centre $(h,k)$, semi-axes $a,b$ $x=h+a\cos t,\ y=k+b\sin t$ $\dfrac{(x-h)^2}{a^2}+\dfrac{(y-k)^2}{b^2}=1$
Parabola $x=at^2,\ y=2at$ $y^2=4ax$
Rectangular hyperbola $x=ct,\ y=c/t$ $xy=c^2$
Vertical/horizontal shifts $x=p(t)+a,\ y=q(t)+b$ Translate Cartesian by $(a,b)$

4. Domain & range — the two-step rule

Step Action
1 Find range of $x=p(t)$ as $t$ varies through its parameter domain. This is the domain of $f(x)$.
2 Find range of $y=q(t)$ as $t$ varies. This is the range of $f(x)$.

Watch out for:

  • Endpoints: open interval < vs closed ≤. If $x(t)$ never attains its supremum, use <.
  • Non-monotonic $x(t)$: e.g. $x=t^2$ on $-3
  • Asymptotes: $x=\ln(t+3)$ as $t\to-3^+$ gives $x\to-\infty$.

5. Worked archetypes

# Setup Method
1 $x=2t,\ y=t^2,\ -3 $t=x/2$; $y=x^2/4$. Domain $-6
2 $x=\ln(t+3),\ y=1/(t+5),\ t>-2$ $t=e^x-3$; $y=1/(e^x+2)$. Domain $x>0$ (since $t=-2\Rightarrow x=0$), range $0
3 $x=\sin t+2,\ y=\cos t-3$ $\sin t=x-2,\cos t=y+3$. Identity: $(x-2)^2+(y+3)^2=1$ — circle
4 $x=\sin t,\ y=\sin 2t$, $-\pi/2\le t\le\pi/2$ $y=2\sin t\cos t=2x\sqrt{1-x^2}$. Domain $-1\le x\le 1$
5 $x=\cot t+2,\ y=\csc^2 t-2,\ 0 $\cot t=x-2,\csc^2 t=y+2$. Identity $1+\cot^2 t=\csc^2 t$: $1+(x-2)^2=y+2\Rightarrow y=x^2-4x+3$
6 $x=3\cos t+4,\ y=2\sin t,\ 0\le t\le 2\pi$ $\cos t=(x-4)/3,\ \sin t=y/2$; identity gives ellipse $\dfrac{(x-4)^2}{9}+\dfrac{y^2}{4}=1$

6. Decision flow

Spot this Do this
Both equations contain trig of the same angle $t$ Identity: $\sin^2+\cos^2$, or $1+\tan^2$, etc.
One equation contains $\sin 2t$ or $\cos 2t$ Double-angle expand first
One equation contains $\sin(t+\phi)$ Addition formula expand first
One equation linear/exp/log in $t$ Substitution: solve for $t$, plug in
Both equations rational in $t$ Solve one for $t$, substitute (may give complex Cartesian)
Need domain/range of $f$ Range of $x(t)$ → domain of $f$; range of $y(t)$ → range of $f$
Need to sketch but no familiar form Table of $t$ values; plot points; join smoothly
"Show curve is a circle/ellipse/line" Eliminate to standard form, identify centre/radius
Length of curve segment Often a circle arc or line segment — find endpoints (sub $t_{\min},t_{\max}$), use distance formula

7. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Substituting $\sin t=x$ then forgetting $\cos t=\pm\sqrt{1-x^2}$ has a sign Check the parameter domain to determine which sign is valid
Squaring an expression and introducing extraneous solutions Always verify with original parametric eqs
Using ≤ when the parameter interval is open < Match strict vs non-strict to the parameter domain exactly
Forgetting that range of $x(t)$ may not span $[\min,\max]$ for non-monotonic $x(t)$ Sketch $x(t)$ vs $t$; identify max/min on the interval
Forgetting to expand $\sin 2t$ before eliminating Always reduce to functions of $t$ first
Confusing domain of $f$ with parameter domain Domain of $f(x)$ = range of $x(t)$, NOT the parameter interval itself
Listing the wrong identity for the trig forms involved $1+\cot^2 t=\csc^2 t$ NOT $1+\tan^2 t=\csc^2 t$. Match the function names
Treating the parametric curve as one-to-one Some parametric curves cross themselves or repeat — sketch / table to verify
Forgetting trace direction If asked, indicate the direction of increasing $t$ on the sketch (arrow)

8. Big picture (30-second mental model)

Parametric ⇄ Cartesian is just substitution / identity:

  1. Read the form: algebraic eqs ⇒ rearrange & substitute; trig eqs ⇒ identity ($\sin^2+\cos^2=1$, $1+\tan^2=\sec^2$, $1+\cot^2=\csc^2$).
  2. Eliminate $t$. Solve one for $t$, plug into the other; or square-and-add for the Pythagorean kind.
  3. Domain/range come from $x(t),y(t)$ ranges, not from the parameter interval directly.
  4. Recognise standard forms: circle, ellipse, line, parabola, hyperbola.
  5. Sketch by table when no clean Cartesian.

Eliminate, identify, scope domains — that's the chapter.
