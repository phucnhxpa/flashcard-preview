P2 Ch 3 — Exponentials and Logarithms

The whole chapter in one mental model. Logarithm is the inverse of exponential: $a^x=n\Leftrightarrow\log_a n=x$. Reading "$\log_a n$" as "the power $a$ must be raised to to get $n$" makes everything else obvious. Three laws — multiplication, division, power — combined with $\log_a a=1$, $\log_a 1=0$, and the change-of-base $\log_a x=\dfrac{\log_b x}{\log_b a}$ are the entire toolkit. Use logs to (i) solve $a^x=b$, (ii) bring exponents down to multiplicative form (so they become solvable equations), (iii) handle disguised quadratics in $a^x$.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Exponential function $\mathrm{f}(x)=a^x$ for fixed positive base $a\ne 1$. The variable is in the exponent. Distinct from a "polynomial" (variable in the base). Always $\mathrm{f}(0)=1$ and $\mathrm{f}(x)>0$ for all real $x$. 3.1
Index / power / exponent Three names for the same thing: the $x$ in $a^x$. Tells you how many times $a$ multiplies itself (when $x$ is a positive integer); generalises to fractional, negative, or irrational $x$. 3.1
Base The fixed number $a$ in $a^x$ or $\log_a$. Must be positive and $\ne 1$. Common choices: $a=10$ ("common log", $\log$), $a=\mathrm{e}\approx 2.718$ ("natural log", $\ln$, in P3), $a=2$ (binary). 3.1 / 3.2
Asymptote (of $a^x$) The horizontal line $y=0$ ($x$-axis). The graph approaches but never touches it as $x\to-\infty$ (if $a>1$) or $x\to+\infty$ (if $0 3.1
Increasing / decreasing exponential $a>1$: increasing (grows without limit as $x\to\infty$, decays to $0$ as $x\to-\infty$). $0 3.1
Logarithm $\log_a n$ The power that base $a$ must be raised to to get $n$. Formally: $\log_a n=x\Leftrightarrow a^x=n$ (with $a>0$, $a\ne 1$, $n>0$). The inverse function of $a^x$. 3.2
Common logarithm Logarithm to base 10, written $\log x$ (no subscript). Default on most calculators with the "log" key. So $\log 100=2$, $\log 1000=3$. 3.2
Natural logarithm Logarithm to base $\mathrm{e}\approx 2.718$, written $\ln x$. The "ln" calculator button. Used heavily in P3 (when differentiating $\mathrm{e}^x$, etc.). 3.2 / 3.5
Multiplication law $\log_a x+\log_a y=\log_a(xy)$. Adding logs ↔ multiplying arguments. Comes from $a^m\cdot a^n=a^{m+n}$. 3.3
Division law $\log_a x-\log_a y=\log_a\!\left(\dfrac x y\right)$. Subtracting logs ↔ dividing arguments. From $\dfrac{a^m}{a^n}=a^{m-n}$. 3.3
Power law $\log_a(x^k)=k\log_a x$. Pulling exponent to the front. From $(a^m)^k=a^{mk}$. The most-used law: it's how you bring exponents out of equations. 3.3
Special cases $\log_a a=1$ (because $a^1=a$). $\log_a 1=0$ (because $a^0=1$). $\log_a\!\left(\dfrac 1 x\right)=-\log_a x$ (power law with $k=-1$). 3.3
Change of base $\log_a x=\dfrac{\log_b x}{\log_b a}$ — converts logs from base $a$ to base $b$. Used to compute $\log_8 11$ on a calculator (rewrite as $\dfrac{\log 11}{\log 8}$). Special: $\log_a b=\dfrac{1}{\log_b a}$ (reciprocal swap). 3.5
"Taking logs of both sides" Apply $\log$ to both sides of an equation: $\mathrm{f}(x)=\mathrm{g}(x)\Rightarrow\log\mathrm{f}(x)=\log\mathrm{g}(x)$. The crucial use: bring exponents down via the power law. Both sides must be the same base and positive. 3.4
Disguised quadratic in $a^x$ An equation like $5^{2x}-12(5^x)+20=0$ becomes $u^2-12u+20=0$ with $u=5^x$. Solve the quadratic for $u$, then back-substitute and take logs to get $x$. Two values of $u$ → two values of $x$ (unless $u\le 0$, which is invalid). 3.4 Ex 12
Domain restrictions $\log_a x$ defined only for $x>0$ (and $a>0,a\ne 1$). When solving log equations, always check candidate solutions don't make any argument $\le 0$. 3.3 (Watch out)
Same base required All log laws (multiply/divide/power) assume same base on both sides. You can't combine $\log_2 x+\log_3 y$ directly. Use change of base first if needed. 3.3

Exponential form Logarithm form

4. Method decision flow — solving exponential / log equations

Spot this Strategy Steps
$a^x=b$ ($b$ positive number) Direct log $x=\log_a b$, evaluate on calculator
$a^{\text{linear in }x}=b$, e.g. $5^{4x-1}=61$ Direct log + algebra $4x-1=\log_5 61$, then solve linear
$a^{2x}+B(a^x)+C=0$ (quadratic in $a^x$) Substitute $u=a^x$ Solve quadratic → two $u$-values → two $x$-values via $x=\log_a u$ (reject $u\le 0$)
$a^x=b^{x+k}$ (different bases) "Take logs of both sides" $x\log a=(x+k)\log b$, solve linear in $x$
Equation with $\log_a$ terms only Combine into single log via laws Then convert to exponential, solve
$\log_a A+\log_a B=\log_a C$ Multiplication law $\log_a(AB)=\log_a C\Rightarrow AB=C$
$\log_a A-\log_a B=k$ Division law + exponential $\log_a(A/B)=k\Rightarrow A/B=a^k$
$k\log_a A=\dots$ Power law $\log_a(A^k)=\dots$
Equation involving $\log_a x$ AND $\log_x a$ Use change of base / reciprocal swap $\log_x a=\dfrac{1}{\log_a x}$, substitute $u=\log_a x$ → quadratic
Equation with $\log_a x$ AND $\log_b x$ (different bases) Change of base Convert all logs to common base $b$ first

5. Worked archetype examples

# Equation Method Answer
1 $\log_3 81$ $3^?=81=3^4$ $4$
2 $\log_4 0.25$ $4^?=0.25=\tfrac14=4^{-1}$ $-1$
3 $\log_a 6+\log_a 7$ Multiplication law $\log_a 42$
4 $2\log_5 3+3\log_5 2$ Power law: $\log_5 9+\log_5 8$; multiplication $\log_5 72$
5 $\log_a\!\left(\dfrac{x\sqrt y}{z}\right)$ Apply division & power laws $\log_a x+\tfrac12\log_a y-\log_a z$
6 $\log_{10}4+2\log_{10}x=2$ Power → multiplication: $\log_{10}(4x^2)=2$, so $4x^2=100$ $x=5$ (reject $-5$)
7 $\log_3(x+11)-\log_3(x-5)=2$ Division law: $\dfrac{x+11}{x-5}=9$ $x=7$
8 $3^x=20$ Direct log: $x=\log_3 20$ $\approx 2.727$
9 $5^{4x-1}=61$ $4x-1=\log_5 61$ $x=\dfrac{\log_5 61+1}{4}\approx 0.889$
10 $5^{2x}-12(5^x)+20=0$ Sub $u=5^x$: $u^2-12u+20=0$, $u=10$ or $u=2$ $x=\log_5 10\approx 1.43$ or $x=\log_5 2\approx 0.431$
11 $3^x=2^{x+1}$ Take logs: $x\log 3=(x+1)\log 2$, isolate $x$ $x=\dfrac{\log 2}{\log 3-\log 2}\approx 1.7095$
12 $\log_8 11$ Change of base: $\dfrac{\log 11}{\log 8}$ $\approx 1.15$
13 $\log_5 x+6\log_x 5=5$ Sub $u=\log_5 x$: $u+\dfrac{6}{u}=5\Rightarrow u^2-5u+6=0\Rightarrow u=2,3$ $x=5^2=25$ or $x=5^3=125$

8. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Treating $\log(x+y)$ as $\log x+\log y$ The multiplication law applies to products, not sums. $\log(x+y)$ doesn't simplify
Forgetting domain check: $\log(x-5)$ requires $x>5$, but algebra gives $x=3$ After solving, check each $\log(\cdot)$ argument is positive — discard invalid $x$
Solving $x^2=25$ from log equation, taking only $x=5$ (or only $x=-5$) Take both, then check each in the original log equation. Negative argument → reject
In disguised quadratic, only computing one root's $x$ After solving for $u=a^x$, compute $x$ for both $u$-values (provided each $u>0$)
In quadratic $a^{2x}+\dots$: confusing $a^{2x}$ with $a^{x+1}$ or $2a^x$ $a^{2x}=(a^x)^2$ — square the substitution variable
Taking logs without a common base on both sides Logs on each side must use the same base. Default: $\log_{10}$
Power law sign error: $\log(x^{-1})=-\log x$, but $-\log(x^{-1})=\log x$ Be careful with parentheses
Using change of base with wrong order: $\log_a x=\dfrac{\log_a b}{\log_b x}$ (wrong) Correct: $\log_a x=\dfrac{\log_b x}{\log_b a}$. Mnemonic: "the base is the bottom"
Forgetting $\log_a$ is undefined at $x=0$, when answer = 0 If the algebra produces $x=0$ in $\log x$ argument → reject
Confusing "single log" simplification: $2\log x-\log(x+1)=\log\dfrac{x^2}{x+1}$ (correct) Apply power law first, then division
Computing $\log_2 50$ as roughly $25$ (mistaking log for division) $\log_2 50\approx 5.64$ because $2^5=32<50<64=2^6$
Mishandling $\log_x a$: substituting it as $a/x$ $\log_x a=\dfrac{1}{\log_a x}$ — use change of base
Quadratic in $a^x$ with negative $u$ root: trying to take $\log_a$ of negative $a^x>0$ always — discard negative $u$ values

9. Big picture (30-second mental model)

Logs and exponents are inverse: $a^x=n\Leftrightarrow\log_a n=x$. Memorise this shape — every conversion uses it.

The three laws (multiplication, division, power) translate algebraic operations on the insides of logs into operations on the outsides:

  • $\times$ inside ↔ $+$ outside (multiplication law)
  • $\div$ inside ↔ $-$ outside (division law)
  • $\,^k$ inside ↔ $k\times$ outside (power law)

To solve $a^x=b$: take logs of both sides → use the power law to bring $x$ down → solve a linear equation. To solve a "disguised quadratic" in $a^x$: substitute $u=a^x$, solve the quadratic, take logs again.

Logs make multiplicative problems additive — historically, this is exactly why John Napier invented them. Same trick is what makes them useful in calculus (P3) and statistics: any time something grows exponentially, take logs and you get a linear story.
