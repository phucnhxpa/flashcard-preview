P4 Ch 4 — Binomial Expansion (Generalised)

The whole chapter in one mental model. The standard binomial $(1+x)^n$ expansion extends to any real $n$ (negative, fractional) — but the expansion becomes an infinite series that only converges when $|x|<1$. For $(1+bx)^n$, validity is $|bx|<1\Leftrightarrow|x|<1/|b|$. For $(a+bx)^n$, factor out $a^n$ first to get $a^n(1+\tfrac{b}{a}x)^n$; validity becomes $|x|<|a/b|$. Partial fractions are the bridge for nasty rational functions: split first, expand each piece, combine — the validity range is the most restrictive of the pieces.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Binomial expansion (general) The infinite series $(1+x)^n=1+nx+\dfrac{n(n-1)}{2!}x^2+\dfrac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}x^3+\cdots$ valid for any real $n$ when $|x|<1$. Reduces to the finite Pascal's triangle expansion when $n\in\mathbb{N}$. 4.1
Index $n$ The exponent. Positive integer $n$ ⇒ finite expansion ($n+1$ terms). Negative or fractional $n$ ⇒ infinite series. 4.1
General term $\dfrac{n(n-1)\cdots(n-r+1)}{r!}x^r$ — the $x^r$ coefficient. The $r$-factor product in the numerator can produce signs from negative $n$. 4.1
Range of validity / convergence The $x$-values where the infinite series equals the function it expands. For $(1+x)^n$: $|x|<1$. For $(1+bx)^n$: $|x|<1/|b|$. For $(a+bx)^n$: $|x|<|a/b|$. 4.1
$|x|<1$ The "comfort zone": $-1 4.1
Factoring out $a^n$ The trick to expand $(a+bx)^n$: write as $a^n(1+\tfrac{b}{a}x)^n$, expand the bracket via the standard formula, multiply through. 4.2
Cubic approximation Truncating after the $x^3$ term. Standard exam request: "find the expansion up to and including the $x^3$ term". 4.1
Binomial of a sum of fractions When the expression is a complicated rational function: decompose into partial fractions first, then expand each simple fraction $\dfrac{A}{1+kx}$ as $A(1+kx)^{-1}$. 4.3
Validity intersection rule When summing two binomial series each valid on a range, the sum is valid only on the intersection of the two ranges (i.e. the smallest). 4.3
Percentage error $\dfrac{|\text{exact}-\text{approx}|}{|\text{exact}|}\times 100\%$. Use to gauge how good the truncated series is. Smaller $|x|$ ⇒ better. 4.1 Ex Q5
Square root via binomial $\sqrt{1+x}=(1+x)^{1/2}$, $\sqrt[3]{1-3x}=(1-3x)^{1/3}$, etc. The classic application: approximate $\sqrt{2}$ via $\sqrt{1-2(0.01)}\cdot(\text{rescaling})$. 4.1 Ex 3
Reciprocal via binomial $\dfrac{1}{(1+x)^k}=(1+x)^{-k}$. The single fastest source of negative-$n$ expansions. 4.1 Ex 1
Addition formula trick If a function is a sum/difference of fractions, expand each separately, then add coefficients term-by-term. 4.3

2. The master formula

$$(1+x)^n=1+nx+\frac{n(n-1)}{2!}x^2+\frac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}x^3+\cdots+\frac{n(n-1)\cdots(n-r+1)}{r!}x^r+\cdots$$

Valid for $|x|<1$, any real $n$.

General coefficient of $x^r$: $$\binom{n}{r}_{\text{generalised}}=\frac{n(n-1)(n-2)\cdots(n-r+1)}{r!}.$$

3. Form-by-form expansion templates

Form First steps First few terms
$(1+x)^n$ Apply formula directly $1+nx+\dfrac{n(n-1)}{2}x^2+\dfrac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{6}x^3+\cdots$
$(1+bx)^n$ Replace $x\to bx$ $1+nbx+\dfrac{n(n-1)}{2}b^2x^2+\cdots$. Valid for $|x|<1/|b|$
$(1-x)^n$ $x\to -x$ — sign flips on odd-power terms $1-nx+\dfrac{n(n-1)}{2}x^2-\dfrac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{6}x^3+\cdots$
$(a+bx)^n$ Factor: $a^n(1+\tfrac{b}{a}x)^n$ $a^n\left[1+n\tfrac{b}{a}x+\dfrac{n(n-1)}{2}(\tfrac{b}{a})^2x^2+\cdots\right]$. Valid for $|x|<|a/b|$
$\dfrac{1}{(1+x)^k}=(1+x)^{-k}$ Use formula with $n=-k$ $1-kx+\dfrac{k(k+1)}{2}x^2-\cdots$ (alternating signs)
$\sqrt{1+x}=(1+x)^{1/2}$ $n=1/2$ $1+\tfrac{1}{2}x-\tfrac{1}{8}x^2+\tfrac{1}{16}x^3-\cdots$
$\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1+x}}=(1+x)^{-1/2}$ $n=-1/2$ $1-\tfrac{1}{2}x+\tfrac{3}{8}x^2-\tfrac{5}{16}x^3+\cdots$

4. Validity rules (quick lookup)

Form Valid for
$(1+x)^n$ $|x|<1$
$(1-x)^n$ $|x|<1$
$(1+bx)^n$ $|x|<\dfrac{1}{|b|}$
$(a+bx)^n$ $|x|<\left|\dfrac{a}{b}\right|$
$(c+dx)^m+(e+fx)^p$ Both must converge: $|x|<\min\left(|c/d|,|e/f|\right)$
Polynomial $\times$ binomial Same as the binomial part

5. Algorithm for $(a+bx)^n$ with $n$ negative or fractional

Step Action
1 Write in index form: $\dfrac{1}{(\dots)^k}=(\dots)^{-k}$, $\sqrt{\dots}=(\dots)^{1/2}$, etc.
2 If $a\ne 1$, factor out $a^n$: $(a+bx)^n=a^n\left(1+\tfrac{b}{a}x\right)^n$
3 Identify $X=\tfrac{b}{a}x$ (or $bx$), substitute into standard formula
4 Compute terms up to required power, simplify each coefficient
5 Multiply each term by $a^n$
6 State validity: $|X|<1$ ⇔ $|x|<|a/b|$ (or $1/|b|$)

6. Partial fraction route for nasty rational functions

Step Action
1 Decompose $\dfrac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$ into partial fractions (Ch 2 method)
2 Each piece $\dfrac{A}{a+bx}$: rewrite as $\dfrac{A}{a}(1+\tfrac{b}{a}x)^{-1}$
3 Each piece $\dfrac{A}{(a+bx)^k}$: rewrite as $\dfrac{A}{a^k}(1+\tfrac{b}{a}x)^{-k}$
4 Expand each piece via the master formula
5 Sum the coefficients term-by-term
6 Validity = intersection: $|x|<\min$(each piece's range)

7. Worked archetypes

# Problem Answer
1 $\dfrac{1}{1+x}=(1+x)^{-1}$, first 4 terms $1-x+x^2-x^3+\cdots$, valid $|x|<1$
2 $(1-x)^{1/3}$, up to $x^3$ $1-\tfrac{1}{3}x-\tfrac{1}{9}x^2-\tfrac{5}{81}x^3+\cdots$, $|x|<1$
3 $(1+4x)^{-2}$, up to $x^3$ $1-8x+48x^2-256x^3+\cdots$, $|x|<\tfrac14$
4 $\sqrt{1-2x}$, up to $x^3$, then sub $x=0.01$ $1-x-\tfrac{x^2}{2}-\tfrac{x^3}{2}+\cdots$ valid $|x|<\tfrac12$. $\sqrt{0.98}\approx 0.9899495$, gives $\sqrt 2\approx 1.41421$
5 $\sqrt{4+x}=(4+x)^{1/2}=2(1+x/4)^{1/2}$ $2+\tfrac{x}{4}-\tfrac{x^2}{64}+\tfrac{x^3}{512}+\cdots$, $\lvert x\rvert<4$
6 $\dfrac{1}{(2+3x)^2}$ $\tfrac14(1+\tfrac{3x}{2})^{-2}=\tfrac14-\tfrac34 x+\tfrac{27}{16}x^2-\tfrac{27}{8}x^3+\cdots$, $\lvert x\rvert<\tfrac23$
7 $\dfrac{4-5x}{(1+x)(2-x)}$ PF: $\dfrac{3}{1+x}-\dfrac{2}{2-x}$. Combine: $2-\tfrac{7}{2}x+\tfrac{11}{4}x^2-\tfrac{25}{8}x^3+\cdots$, $\lvert x\rvert<1$
8 $(2+x)(1+5x)^{-1/2}$ — $x^2$ term $(1+5x)^{-1/2}=1-\tfrac{5x}{2}+\tfrac{75}{8}x^2+\cdots$. $x^2$ coefficient: $2\cdot\tfrac{75}{8}+1\cdot(-\tfrac52)=\tfrac{65}{4}$

Spot this Do this

$(1+bx)^n$ form Substitute $X=bx$; validity $|x|<1/|b|$

9. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Forgetting validity range Always state $|x|<\dots$ — exam reserves marks for it
Validity range for $(a+bx)^n$ stated as $|x|<1$ Actually $|x|<|a/b|$ — depends on the $a$
Forgetting to multiply expansion by $a^n$ after factoring Multiply EVERY term by $a^n$
Sign errors in $(1-x)^n$ or $(a-bx)^n$ Substitute $X=-x$ and propagate sign through powers carefully
Missing terms when multiplying $(2+x)\cdot(\text{expansion})$ Two ways to make $x^k$: from $2\cdot x^k$ piece and from $x\cdot x^{k-1}$ piece. Sum them
Using $(1+x)^n$ formula for $(2+3x)^n$ directly Factor out $2^n$ first
Forgetting that intersection of validity ranges is the most restrictive Take the smaller bound
Skipping factorial denominators in coefficients $\dfrac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}$ not $\dfrac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3}$. $3!=6$
Writing $(-1)\cdot(-2)/(2!)=−1$ instead of $+1$ Even number of negatives ⇒ positive
Substituting $x$ outside validity for an estimate Always check $|x|<$ validity bound first

10. Big picture (30-second mental model)

The generalised binomial expansion lets you replace any $(a+bx)^n$ with a polynomial approximation. The recipe:

  1. Reduce to $(1+X)^n$ form by factoring $a^n$ and setting $X=bx/a$.
  2. Apply the formula $1+nX+\dfrac{n(n-1)}{2!}X^2+\cdots$.
  3. Multiply through by $a^n$.
  4. Validity is $|X|<1$, i.e. $|x|<|a/b|$.
  5. For complicated rational functions, partial-fraction first, expand each piece, and intersect the validity ranges.
  6. Use cases: numerical approximations of $\sqrt N$ etc., estimating function values, computing percentage error.

Factor → expand → validity — that's the chapter.
