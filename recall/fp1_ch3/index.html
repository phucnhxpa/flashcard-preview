FP1 Ch 3 — Numerical Solutions of Equations

The whole chapter in one mental model. When you can't solve $\mathrm{f}(x)=0$ exactly, trap the root in an interval where $\mathrm{f}$ changes sign, then squeeze that interval down with a recipe: bisection (always halve), linear interpolation (similar triangles), or Newton–Raphson (slide along the tangent). Each method is just a rule that turns one approximation into a better one — repeat until accuracy is reached.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Root A value $x=\alpha$ where $\mathrm{f}(\alpha)=0$. Geometrically, where the graph $y=\mathrm{f}(x)$ crosses the $x$-axis. The chapter is about finding roots numerically when no algebraic formula is available — e.g. $\mathrm{e}^x=\ln x+5$ has no closed-form solution. 3.1
Continuous function on $[a,b]$ A function whose graph has no jumps, breaks, or vertical asymptotes inside $[a,b]$. You can draw it without lifting the pen. Crucial: change-of-sign root location only works on continuous intervals — a vertical asymptote can fake a sign change without a root. 3.1
Change-of-sign rule (IVT) If $\mathrm{f}$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and $\mathrm{f}(a),\mathrm{f}(b)$ have opposite signs, then at least one root lies in $(a,b)$. "At least one" — there might be three or five (always odd if signs differ). Conversely, no sign change does NOT mean no root: an even number of roots can hide. 3.1
Three traps in change-of-sign (i) Multiple roots with sign change → odd count (e.g. 3); (ii) Multiple roots without sign change → even count (e.g. 2 — both missed); (iii) Vertical asymptote inside the interval → sign changes but no root exists. Always check continuity. 3.1
Interval bisection Iterative recipe: take midpoint $m=(a+b)/2$, evaluate $\mathrm{f}(m)$. The root lies in whichever half has the sign change ($[a,m]$ if $\mathrm{f}(a)\mathrm{f}(m)<0$, else $[m,b]$). Replace the bad endpoint with $m$ and repeat. Each iteration halves the uncertainty. Reliable but slow (linear convergence). 3.2
Linear interpolation Replace $\mathrm{f}$ on $[a,b]$ with the straight line through $(a,\mathrm{f}(a))$ and $(b,\mathrm{f}(b))$, then take the line's $x$-intercept as the next approximation $x_1$. Found by similar triangles: $\dfrac{x_1-a}{b-x_1}=\dfrac{\lvert\mathrm{f}(a)\rvert}{\lvert\mathrm{f}(b)\rvert}$. Faster than bisection on smooth functions. 3.3
Newton–Raphson method Iterative recipe using the tangent line: from $x_n$, draw the tangent at $(x_n,\mathrm{f}(x_n))$; its $x$-intercept is the next approximation. Formula: $x_{n+1}=x_n-\dfrac{\mathrm{f}(x_n)}{\mathrm{f}'(x_n)}$. Quadratic convergence near a simple root — typically doubles the number of correct digits per iteration. 3.4
Tangent line at $x_n$ The straight line $y=\mathrm{f}(x_n)+\mathrm{f}'(x_n)(x-x_n)$. Setting $y=0$ and solving gives the Newton–Raphson formula. The geometric picture: the tangent points "downhill" toward the root if $x_n$ is on a smooth slope. 3.4
Failure modes of Newton–Raphson (i) $\mathrm{f}'(x_n)=0$ at any iteration → division by zero, the tangent is horizontal and never crosses the axis; (ii) $\mathrm{f}'(x_n)$ is small (near a turning point) → tangent skids off far from the root, may converge slowly or to a different root; (iii) function shape (e.g. inflection points) can send iterates oscillating away. 3.4
Convergence to a root Successive approximations $x_0,x_1,x_2,\dots$ get arbitrarily close to the actual root $\alpha$. Numerical methods are deemed "convergent at this accuracy" when two successive iterates agree to the required number of decimal places. 3.2–3.4
Verification by sign change After getting an approximation $\alpha\approx 1.856$ to 3 d.p., confirm by checking $\mathrm{f}(1.8555)$ and $\mathrm{f}(1.8565)$ have opposite signs — this proves the true root lies in $[1.8555,1.8565]$, hence rounds to $1.856$. The standard "show your answer is correct to N d.p." technique. 3.4 Ex 7c
Iterative formula / recurrence A rule of the form $x_{n+1}=g(x_n)$ that takes you from one term to the next. Each numerical method here is a different choice of $g$: bisection picks midpoint, NR uses tangent, linear interpolation uses chord intercept. 3.4
Stationary point Where $\mathrm{f}'(x)=0$. In Newton–Raphson, never use a stationary point as a starting value — division by zero. If $x_0$ is near a stationary point, the method may converge very slowly or jump far away. 3.4
First / second approximation $x_1$ is the first iterate (output of one application of the method to $x_0$). $x_2$ is the second iterate. Exam wording matters: "Apply the method twice" usually means produce $x_2$. 3.2–3.4

2. The four numerical methods — head-to-head comparison

Method Iteration formula Inputs needed Convergence rate When it shines When it fails
Change-of-sign root locator (none — just a check) $\mathrm{f}(a)\mathrm{f}(b)<0$ and continuity n/a (just locates an interval) Always — a starting tool Even number of roots; vertical asymptotes
Interval bisection $m=\dfrac{a+b}{2}$; replace whichever endpoint has the same sign as $\mathrm{f}(m)$ $\mathrm{f}$ alone (no derivative) Linear: error halves each step Robust — guaranteed to converge Slow; needs many iterations for high accuracy
Linear interpolation $x_1=\dfrac{a\,\mathrm{f}(b)-b\,\mathrm{f}(a)}{\mathrm{f}(b)-\mathrm{f}(a)}$ (chord's $x$-intercept) $\mathrm{f}$ alone Better than bisection, super-linear Smooth, near-linear $\mathrm{f}$ on the interval Endpoints stuck on one side; convergence can stall
Newton–Raphson $x_{n+1}=x_n-\dfrac{\mathrm{f}(x_n)}{\mathrm{f}'(x_n)}$ Both $\mathrm{f}$ and $\mathrm{f}'$ Quadratic: digits double Smooth $\mathrm{f}$ with computable derivative; near a simple root Stationary point → division by zero; near turning point → instability

3. Linear interpolation — similar triangles formula derivation

Picture: chord through $A=(a,\mathrm{f}(a))$ and $B=(b,\mathrm{f}(b))$ with $\mathrm{f}(a)<0<\mathrm{f}(b)$. The chord crosses the $x$-axis at $x_1$.

Quantity Expression Meaning
Distance from $a$ to $x_1$ $x_1-a$ "Horizontal run" on left side
Distance from $x_1$ to $b$ $b-x_1$ "Horizontal run" on right side
Vertical drop on left $\lvert\mathrm{f}(a)\rvert$ Negative $y$-value at $a$
Vertical rise on right $\lvert\mathrm{f}(b)\rvert$ Positive $y$-value at $b$
Similar-triangles equation $\dfrac{x_1-a}{\lvert\mathrm{f}(a)\rvert}=\dfrac{b-x_1}{\lvert\mathrm{f}(b)\rvert}$ The triangles share the chord's slope
Solving for $x_1$ $x_1=\dfrac{a\,\lvert\mathrm{f}(b)\rvert+b\,\lvert\mathrm{f}(a)\rvert}{\lvert\mathrm{f}(a)\rvert+\lvert\mathrm{f}(b)\rvert}$ Weighted average of $a,b$, weighted by opposite-side $\mathrm{f}$ values
Algebraic form (no abs values) $x_1=\dfrac{a\,\mathrm{f}(b)-b\,\mathrm{f}(a)}{\mathrm{f}(b)-\mathrm{f}(a)}$ Same thing using signed $\mathrm{f}$ values

After finding $x_1$, evaluate $\mathrm{f}(x_1)$. The new interval is whichever half contains the sign change. Iterate.

4. Newton–Raphson — geometric picture and formula derivation

Step Action Symbol/result
1 Start at point $P=(x_n,\mathrm{f}(x_n))$ on the curve $x_n$ given
2 Draw the tangent line at $P$ Slope $=\mathrm{f}'(x_n)$
3 Tangent equation $y-\mathrm{f}(x_n)=\mathrm{f}'(x_n)(x-x_n)$
4 Set $y=0$ to find where tangent hits $x$-axis $-\mathrm{f}(x_n)=\mathrm{f}'(x_n)(x_{n+1}-x_n)$
5 Solve for $x_{n+1}$ $x_{n+1}=x_n-\dfrac{\mathrm{f}(x_n)}{\mathrm{f}'(x_n)}$

Why it converges fast: if $\mathrm{f}$ is smooth and $x_n$ is close to a simple root $\alpha$, the error roughly squares each step. $0.1$ → $0.01$ → $0.0001$ → $10^{-8}$.

5. Worked archetypes (memorise the shape of each)

# Setup Method Single iteration
1 $\mathrm{f}(x)=x^3-4x^2+3x+1$, show root in $[1.4,1.5]$ Change of sign $\mathrm{f}(1.4)=0.104>0$, $\mathrm{f}(1.5)=-0.125<0$ → sign change → root exists
2 $\mathrm{f}(x)=2^x-x-3$, root in $[2,3]$ Bisection $m=2.5,\mathrm{f}(2.5)>0$ so root in $[2,2.5]$; $m=2.25,\mathrm{f}(2.25)<0$ so root in $[2.25,2.5]$
3 $\mathrm{f}(x)=3^x-5x$, root in $[2,3]$, $\mathrm{f}(2)=-1$, $\mathrm{f}(3)=12$ Linear interpolation $x_1=\dfrac{2\cdot 12-3\cdot(-1)}{12-(-1)}=\dfrac{27}{13}\approx 2.08$
4 $\mathrm{f}(x)=x^3+2x^2-5x-4$, $x_0=2$ Newton–Raphson $\mathrm{f}'=3x^2+4x-5$; $\mathrm{f}(2)=2,\mathrm{f}'(2)=15$; $x_1=2-2/15=1.8\overline{6}$
5 Verify $\alpha=1.856$ is correct to 3 d.p. Sign-change check $\mathrm{f}(1.8555)<0$ and $\mathrm{f}(1.8565)>0$ → true root in $[1.8555,1.8565]$, rounds to $1.856$

6. Decision flow — pick the right method

Situation Best method Why
Just need to locate a root quickly Change of sign on $\mathrm{f}(a),\mathrm{f}(b)$ One evaluation each, no iteration
Slow but guaranteed convergence, no derivative known Bisection Always halves; can't get worse
Smooth function, want faster than bisection, derivative not available Linear interpolation Uses function values cleverly via similar triangles
Smooth function, derivative computable, want fast convergence Newton–Raphson Quadratic convergence near simple roots
Function has vertical asymptote in $[a,b]$ NONE of these — exclude the asymptote first Sign change misleads otherwise
$\mathrm{f}'(x_0)=0$ or near-zero NOT Newton–Raphson; use bisection or pick different $x_0$ Division by zero / runaway tangent
Need to prove an answer is correct to N d.p. Sign-change verification on $[\alpha-5\times 10^{-(N+1)},\alpha+5\times 10^{-(N+1)}]$ Standard exam technique

7. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Claiming "no root" because no sign change Even number of roots can hide; must also check graph or use other evidence
Claiming "root exists" from sign change near a vertical asymptote First check continuity on the interval — exclude singularities
Using degrees instead of radians for $\sin x,\cos x,\tan x$ Always radians in numerical-methods questions
Newton–Raphson with $x_0$ at a turning point $\mathrm{f}'(x_0)=0$, division by zero — pick a different starting value
Rounding intermediate iterates aggressively Carry full calculator precision; only round the final answer
Reporting "$x_1=$ ..." when the question asked for $x_2$ "Apply the method twice" → produce $x_2$, not $x_1$
Forgetting the $-$ sign in $x_{n+1}=x_n-\mathrm{f}/\mathrm{f}'$ The sign comes from solving the tangent equation; mnemonic: tangent points toward the root, so subtract
Using bisection without showing a sign change at each step Each iteration must justify which half by stating signs of $\mathrm{f}$ at midpoint
Linear interpolation: forgetting to update the interval After finding $x_1$, evaluate $\mathrm{f}(x_1)$, identify the new sign-change interval, then iterate
Verifying "to 3 d.p." with the wrong half-width The bracket should be $\pm 5\times 10^{-4}$ around the rounded answer

8. Big picture (the 30-second mental model)

Every numerical-equations problem follows the same three-step shape:

  1. Locate — find an interval $[a,b]$ where $\mathrm{f}$ continuous and $\mathrm{f}(a)\mathrm{f}(b)<0$. Now a root is guaranteed inside (caveats: continuity, possibly multiple roots).
  2. Refine — apply an iterative method to shrink the bracket or improve the estimate:
    • Bisection: $x_{n+1}=(a_n+b_n)/2$, halve.
    • Linear interpolation: $x_{n+1}=$ chord's $x$-intercept, similar triangles.
    • Newton–Raphson: $x_{n+1}=x_n-\mathrm{f}(x_n)/\mathrm{f}'(x_n)$, tangent slide.
  3. Verify — check the sign of $\mathrm{f}$ at the rounded-up and rounded-down boundaries to prove the answer's accuracy to the stated number of decimal places.

Reliability ↘ vs speed ↗: bisection (slow, safe) → linear interpolation (medium) → Newton–Raphson (fast but can fail). Choose by what's available (derivative? smooth? near a turning point?) and what the exam asks.
