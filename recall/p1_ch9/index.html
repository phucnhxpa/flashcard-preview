P1 Ch 9 — Integration

The whole chapter in one mental model. Integration is the reverse of differentiation: given a gradient function $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}$, recover $y$. The single workhorse rule is the reverse power rule: $\displaystyle\int x^n\,\mathrm{d}x=\dfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}+c$ for $n\ne -1$ — "add one to the power, divide by the new power, add $+c$". Always include the constant of integration $c$ — without further info, infinitely many curves share the same gradient. Boundary condition (any point $(x,y)$ on the curve, or value $\mathrm{f}(a)=k$): substitute → solve for $c$ → unique curve.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Integration The reverse process of differentiation. If $\mathrm{F}'(x)=\mathrm{f}(x)$ then "$\mathrm{F}$ is an integral of $\mathrm{f}$". Compared to differentiation: differentiation = "drop power, subtract one"; integration = "add one to power, divide by new power". They undo each other. 9.1
Antiderivative Same as integral. Any function whose derivative is the integrand. Two antiderivatives differ by a constant — that's why $+c$ appears. 9.1
Indefinite integral An integral without specified limits — the result is a family of functions $\mathrm{F}(x)+c$, not a number. Notation: $\displaystyle\int\mathrm{f}(x)\,\mathrm{d}x=\mathrm{F}(x)+c$. (Definite integrals — with limits — appear in P2.) 9.2
Constant of integration ($c$) The arbitrary additive constant added when integrating. Required because differentiating any constant gives 0, so the constant is "lost" in differentiation and must be reinstated. Skip the $+c$ → lose marks. 9.1
Integral sign $\int$ Elongated "S" (for sum) representing integration. Always paired with $\mathrm{d}x$ telling you the variable of integration: $\displaystyle\int\dots\,\mathrm{d}x$ means "integrate with respect to $x$". 9.2
Integrand The thing being integrated — the function written between $\int$ and $\mathrm{d}x$. In $\int 3x^2\,\mathrm{d}x$, the integrand is $3x^2$. 9.2
Reverse power rule $\displaystyle\int x^n\,\mathrm{d}x=\dfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}+c$ for $n\ne -1$. With a coefficient: $\displaystyle\int kx^n\,\mathrm{d}x=\dfrac{k}{n+1}x^{n+1}+c$. The constant $k$ rides through unchanged. 9.1
Linearity (sum/constant rule) Integration is linear: $\displaystyle\int(\mathrm{f}\pm\mathrm{g})\,\mathrm{d}x=\int\mathrm{f}\,\mathrm{d}x\pm\int\mathrm{g}\,\mathrm{d}x$ and $\displaystyle\int k\,\mathrm{f}(x)\,\mathrm{d}x=k\int\mathrm{f}(x)\,\mathrm{d}x$. So integrate term-by-term, just like differentiation. 9.2
Excluded power $n=-1$ The reverse power rule fails when $n=-1$ (you'd divide by zero). $\displaystyle\int\dfrac{1}{x}\,\mathrm{d}x=\ln|x|+c$ — but not in P1 (covered in P3). For P1, $\dfrac{1}{x}$ doesn't appear as an integrand. 9.1
Constant integrand $\int k\,\mathrm{d}x$ Treat $k=k\cdot x^0$ → $\displaystyle\int k\,\mathrm{d}x=kx+c$. So integrating "5" gives "$5x+c$", not "5". 9.1 Ex 2
Variable of integration The $\mathrm{d}t$, $\mathrm{d}x$, $\mathrm{d}u$ at the end. Tells you which letter is being integrated; other letters are treated as constants. So $\displaystyle\int p^2\,\mathrm{d}x=p^2 x+c$ (treat $p$ as a number). 9.2 Ex 4
Constant of integration vs constant in expression When $\int(p^2x^{-2}+q)\,\mathrm{d}x=-p^2x^{-1}+qx+c$ — the $c$ is the integration constant, $p$ and $q$ are problem-specific constants treated as fixed numbers. They co-exist. 9.2 Ex 4c
Boundary / initial condition A given fact like "the curve passes through $(2,5)$" or "$\mathrm{f}(3)=7$" or "$y=10$ when $x=4$". Substituting into the integrated expression gives a numerical equation in $c$, which determines $c$ uniquely. 9.3
Equation of a curve The "general solution" $y=\mathrm{F}(x)+c$ with $c$ specified — i.e. the unique curve with the given gradient function passing through the given point. 9.3
Power-law preprocessing Before integrating, rewrite each term as $kx^n$. So $\sqrt{x}\to x^{1/2}$, $\dfrac{1}{x^3}\to x^{-3}$, $\dfrac{x-2}{x^2}\to x^{-1}-2x^{-2}$. Without this, the reverse power rule can't be applied directly. 9.2 Ex 5
Expanding products / splitting fractions $(2x+3)^2$ must be expanded → $4x^2+12x+9$, then integrate. Quotients like $\dfrac{2x^3+3}{x^2}$ must be split → $2x+3x^{-2}$. (No product/quotient rules in P1 integration.) 9.2 Ex 5
Integration as anti-rate Physical interpretation: if $\mathrm{f}'(t)$ is velocity (rate of change of position), $\mathrm{f}(t)$ is position. If $\mathrm{f}'(t)$ is acceleration, $\mathrm{f}(t)$ is velocity. Often combined with $\mathrm{f}(0)=$ "initial value" boundary condition. 9.3 Q7-8
Family of curves $y=\mathrm{F}(x)+c$ for varying $c$ describes infinitely many curves — all vertical translations of one another. Choosing $c$ picks out the one curve through a specified point. 9.3

4. Multi-term integration — pre-processing playbook

Before integrating, force every term into the form $kx^n$.

If integrand contains... Rewrite as Then integrate
$\sqrt{x}$ $x^{1/2}$ $\tfrac{2}{3}x^{3/2}+c$
$\dfrac{1}{x^k}$ $x^{-k}$ $\dfrac{x^{1-k}}{1-k}+c$ (provided $k\ne 1$)
$\dfrac{p(x)}{x^k}$ Distribute: $\sum c_i x^{n_i-k}$ Term-by-term
Product $(2x+3)^2$ Expand to $4x^2+12x+9$ Term-by-term
Product $x(x^2+\tfrac{2}{x})$ Distribute: $x^3+2$ Term-by-term
$(2x)^2$ Compute power: $4x^2$ Then $\tfrac{4}{3}x^3+c$
Quotient $\dfrac{2x^3+3}{x^2}$ Split: $2x+3x^{-2}$ $x^2-3x^{-1}+c$
$\dfrac{(2x+1)^2}{\sqrt x}$ Expand & split: $\dfrac{4x^2+4x+1}{x^{1/2}}=4x^{3/2}+4x^{1/2}+x^{-1/2}$ Term-by-term
Surd: $\sqrt{x}(\sqrt{x}+3)^2$ Expand: $\sqrt{x}(x+6\sqrt x+9)=x^{3/2}+6x+9x^{1/2}$ Term-by-term
Other variable $\mathrm{d}t$ Replace $x$ with $t$ everywhere Same rule
Mixed-letter $\int(p^2 x^{-2}+q)\,\mathrm{d}x$ Treat $p,q$ as constants $-p^2 x^{-1}+qx+c$

5. "Find the curve" recipe — using a boundary condition

When you have $\mathrm{f}'(x)$ AND a point $(x_0,y_0)$:

Step Action
1 Rewrite $\mathrm{f}'(x)$ as a sum of $kx^n$ terms (split fractions, expand brackets, etc.)
2 Integrate term-by-term: $\mathrm{f}(x)=\mathrm{F}(x)+c$
3 Substitute the given point: $y_0=\mathrm{F}(x_0)+c$
4 Solve for $c$: $c=y_0-\mathrm{F}(x_0)$
5 Write the equation of the curve: $y=\mathrm{F}(x)+c$ with the numerical $c$
6 (If asked for $y$ at another $x$) substitute that $x$ into the final $y=\mathrm{F}(x)+c$

Mechanics flavour: $\mathrm{f}'(t)=$ velocity, $\mathrm{f}(0)=$ initial position. Integrate, sub $t=0$ to determine $c$, then evaluate at desired $t$.

7. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Forgetting $+c$ Always add $+c$ for indefinite integrals — drop one mark per omission
Multiplying $c$ by $k$: writing $\int kx^n=\dfrac{k}{n+1}x^{n+1}+kc$ $c$ is generic; just leave as $+c$
Dividing instead of multiplying when raising power: $\int x^{1/2}=\dfrac{x^{3/2}}{3/2}=\tfrac32 x^{3/2}+c$ Rule says divide by new power: $\dfrac{x^{3/2}}{3/2}=\tfrac{2}{3}x^{3/2}$ — multiply by reciprocal
Trying to integrate $\dfrac{1}{x}$ in P1 (it's $\ln\lvert x\rvert$, not in syllabus) Not asked in P1 — if you see one, you've made an error in pre-processing
Integrating $(2x+3)^2$ as $\tfrac{(2x+3)^3}{3}$ (chain rule shortcut) This shortcut needs a $\dfrac{1}{2}$ factor (P2 reverse chain rule). For P1, expand the bracket first, then term-by-term
Failing to expand/split before integrating $\int x(x+1)\,\mathrm{d}x\ne x\cdot\tfrac{x^2}{2}$. Expand: $\int(x^2+x)\,\mathrm{d}x=\tfrac13 x^3+\tfrac12 x^2+c$
Integrating $\sqrt{x}$ as $\tfrac32 x^{3/2}$ instead of $\tfrac{2}{3}x^{3/2}$ Add 1 to power: $\tfrac12+1=\tfrac32$. Divide by new power $\tfrac32$, i.e. multiply by $\tfrac{2}{3}$
Boundary substitution: getting confused with $4^{5/2}$ etc. $4^{5/2}=(\sqrt 4)^5=2^5=32$. Or $4^{5/2}=4^2\cdot\sqrt 4=16\cdot 2=32$
Variable of integration confusion: $\int p^2\,\mathrm{d}x=p^2x+c$ but writing $\tfrac{p^3}{3}+c$ The $\mathrm{d}x$ tells you to integrate with respect to $x$; $p$ is a constant
Skipping rewrite step, then trying to use power rule on $\dfrac{2x+3}{\sqrt x}$ directly You must split: $\dfrac{2x+3}{\sqrt x}=2x^{1/2}+3x^{-1/2}$, then integrate term-by-term
Omitting the constant term when integrating a constant: $\int 5\,\mathrm{d}x=5$ $\int 5\,\mathrm{d}x=5x+c$ — constant integrates to $cx$, not stays as $c$
Sign errors with negative powers: $\int x^{-3}\,\mathrm{d}x=\dfrac{x^{-2}}{-2}=-\tfrac12 x^{-2}+c$ Add 1: $-3+1=-2$. Divide by $-2$: gives $-\tfrac12$ as coefficient
Substituting $(x,y)=(0,1)$ when integrand has $x^{-1/2}$ etc. (undefined at 0) Check domain — sometimes the boundary is at $x=0$ for a function defined only for $x>0$. Reread problem

8. Verification habit

Differentiate your final $\mathrm{F}(x)+c$ — you should recover the original integrand. This catches algebra errors instantly. E.g. answered $\tfrac{2}{3}x^{3/2}-2x^{-1/2}+c$? Differentiate: $\tfrac{2}{3}\cdot\tfrac{3}{2}x^{1/2}-2\cdot(-\tfrac12)x^{-3/2}=x^{1/2}+x^{-3/2}$. Compare to integrand. ✓ or ✗.

9. Big picture (30-second mental model)

Integration undoes differentiation. The single rule for P1 is the reverse power rule: $$\int x^n\,\mathrm{d}x=\frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}+c\quad(n\ne -1),$$ with linearity (term-by-term, constants pull out) and the mandatory $+c$.

Three workflow steps for every P1 integration question:

  1. Rewrite the integrand in $kx^n$ form (convert surds, split fractions, expand brackets).
  2. Apply the rule term-by-term (add 1 to power; divide by new power).
  3. Add $+c$ — and if a boundary point is given, substitute to find the numerical $c$.

The chapter is one rule plus pre-processing skill. Master those two and every P1 integration question reduces to bookkeeping. Definite integrals (with limits, areas) come in P2 — the antiderivative skill from this chapter is the foundation.
