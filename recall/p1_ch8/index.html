P1 Ch 8 — Differentiation

The whole chapter in one mental model. Differentiation = "find the gradient function" of a curve. The derivative $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}$ tells you the slope of the tangent at every $x$. The power rule $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(ax^n)=anx^{n-1}$ does almost everything in this chapter — combined with "differentiate term-by-term", you can handle any polynomial or any sum of $x$-powers (positive, negative, fractional). Three core applications: (1) gradient at a point — substitute $x$-value into $\mathrm{f}'(x)$; (2) tangent / normal lines — use point-slope with $m=\mathrm{f}'(a)$ for tangent, $m=-1/\mathrm{f}'(a)$ for normal; (3) second derivative — differentiate twice for "rate of change of slope" (curvature/acceleration).

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Tangent to a curve at a point The unique straight line that touches the curve at the point and has the same gradient as the curve there. Visually: zoom in until the curve looks straight; that line is the tangent. The tangent may cut the curve again at another point — only locally is it a "kiss". 8.1
Gradient of a curve at a point The gradient (slope) of the tangent at that point. Unlike a straight line, this gradient varies as $x$ changes — it's a function of $x$, not a single number. 8.1
Chord (secant) A straight line joining two points on a curve. Its gradient $\dfrac{\mathrm{f}(x+h)-\mathrm{f}(x)}{h}$ approximates the tangent gradient, becoming exact in the limit $h\to 0$. 8.1
Limit ($\lim_{h\to 0}$) The fixed value an expression approaches as $h$ gets arbitrarily close to $0$ (without ever equalling $0$). E.g. $\lim_{h\to 0}(2x+h)=2x$ — the $h$ "disappears" because it's vanishingly small. 8.2
Derivative / gradient function The function that gives the gradient of $y=\mathrm{f}(x)$ at every $x$. Defined by $\mathrm{f}'(x)=\lim_{h\to 0}\dfrac{\mathrm{f}(x+h)-\mathrm{f}(x)}{h}$. Two notations: $\mathrm{f}'(x)$ (functional) or $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}$ (Leibniz). 8.2
Differentiation The operation of finding the derivative. The opposite operation is integration (Ch 9). To "differentiate" a function = compute its derivative. 8.2
Differentiation from first principles Computing the derivative directly from the limit definition (no shortcut rules). Used as a proof technique, not for everyday computation. Examinable as a "show that" question. 8.2
Power rule $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(x^n)=nx^{n-1}$ for any real $n$ (positive, negative, fractional). Memorise as "drop the power, subtract one". With a constant: $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(ax^n)=anx^{n-1}$. 8.3
Linearity (sum/difference rule) If $y=\mathrm{f}(x)\pm\mathrm{g}(x)$ then $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}=\mathrm{f}'(x)\pm\mathrm{g}'(x)$. Differentiate term-by-term. Constants come out: $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(k\,\mathrm{f}(x))=k\,\mathrm{f}'(x)$. 8.5
Constant differentiates to zero $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(c)=0$ for any constant $c$. Geometrically: the line $y=c$ is horizontal, gradient zero. So additive constants disappear when you differentiate. 8.4
Linear term differentiates to its coefficient $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(ax)=a$. Comes from the power rule with $n=1$: $a\cdot 1\cdot x^0=a$. 8.4
Equation of a tangent at $x=a$ $y-\mathrm{f}(a)=\mathrm{f}'(a)(x-a)$. Point-slope form using point $(a,\mathrm{f}(a))$ and gradient $m=\mathrm{f}'(a)$. 8.6
Normal The line through a point on the curve perpendicular to the tangent there. Its gradient is $-\dfrac{1}{\mathrm{f}'(a)}$ (negative reciprocal). Equation: $y-\mathrm{f}(a)=-\dfrac{1}{\mathrm{f}'(a)}(x-a)$. 8.6
Stationary point A point where $\mathrm{f}'(x)=0$ — the tangent is horizontal. Includes maxima, minima, and points of inflection. (Classification of which type uses $\mathrm{f}''$ — see Section 8.7 / chapter 9 in some editions.) 8.4 Q7
Second derivative The derivative of the derivative: $\mathrm{f}''(x)$ or $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}^2y}{\mathrm{d}x^2}$. Measures the rate of change of the gradient — concavity/curvature. In mechanics: position → velocity → acceleration. 8.7
Power-law preprocessing Before differentiating, rewrite each term as $ax^n$. So $\sqrt{x}\to x^{1/2}$, $\dfrac{1}{x^2}\to x^{-2}$, $\dfrac{x-2}{x^2}\to x^{-1}-2x^{-2}$. Without this rewrite, the power rule can't be applied. 8.5 Ex 9
Rewriting a quotient or product Don't try to differentiate a product or quotient directly (no rule for them yet — that's P3). Instead expand $(x^3)(3x+1)=3x^4+x^3$ or split $\dfrac{x-2}{x^2}=\dfrac{1}{x}-\dfrac{2}{x^2}=x^{-1}-2x^{-2}$, then differentiate term-by-term. 8.5
Rate of change The derivative $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}$ literally measures "how fast $y$ changes per unit change in $x$". When $x$ is time, the derivative is a rate (e.g. velocity = derivative of displacement). 8.7

3. Multi-term differentiation — playbook

If $y=\mathrm{f}(x)\pm\mathrm{g}(x)$: differentiate each term separately and combine.

Form of expression Pre-processing step Then differentiate
Polynomial (sum of $ax^n$ terms) Already in form Term-by-term power rule
Product, e.g. $x^3(3x+1)$ Expand: $3x^4+x^3$ Term-by-term
Quotient $\dfrac{p(x)}{x^k}$ Split into individual fractions: $\dfrac{x-2}{x^2}=x^{-1}-2x^{-2}$ Term-by-term
Quotient $\dfrac{p(x)}{\sqrt x}$ Split: $\dfrac{2x^3+3x}{\sqrt x}=2x^{5/2}+3x^{1/2}$ Term-by-term
Surds Rewrite as fractional power: $3\sqrt x=3x^{1/2}$ Power rule
Reciprocal of polynomial If $y=\dfrac{1}{ax+b}$ — not in P1 (use chain rule, P3). For $\dfrac{1}{x^k}$: $x^{-k}$ then power rule —
Quadratic $ax^2+bx+c$ Already simple $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}=2ax+b$
Cubic $ax^3+bx^2+cx+d$ Already simple $3ax^2+2bx+c$

4. Tangents and normals — recipe

Step Tangent at $x=a$ Normal at $x=a$
1 Find $y$-coordinate: $y_0=\mathrm{f}(a)$ Same
2 Find gradient function: $\mathrm{f}'(x)$ Same
3 Evaluate at $x=a$: $m_T=\mathrm{f}'(a)$ $m_T=\mathrm{f}'(a)$ then $m_N=-\dfrac{1}{m_T}$
4 Equation: $y-y_0=m_T(x-a)$ Equation: $y-y_0=m_N(x-a)$
5 Rearrange to required form ($y=mx+c$, $ax+by+c=0$, etc.) Same

Special cases:

Situation Treatment
Tangent is horizontal $\mathrm{f}'(a)=0$ → tangent is $y=y_0$
Tangent is vertical $\mathrm{f}'(a)$ undefined ($x^{-1/2}$ at $x=0$, etc.) — tangent $x=a$, no normal in the standard sense
Normal is horizontal $\mathrm{f}'(a)\to\infty$ → normal is $y=y_0$
Normal is vertical $\mathrm{f}'(a)=0$ → normal is $x=a$
"Tangent passes through origin" Equate: $0-y_0=m_T(0-a)\Rightarrow y_0=m_T a$, gives equation in $a$
"Tangent / normal meets curve again at $Q$" Substitute the line equation into curve equation, solve resulting polynomial; one root is the original $x=a$, the other is $Q$

5. Higher-order derivatives

Notation Meaning Physical (motion) meaning
$\mathrm{f}(x)$, $y$ Function Position
$\mathrm{f}'(x)$, $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}$, $y'$ First derivative — gradient function Velocity
$\mathrm{f}''(x)$, $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}^2y}{\mathrm{d}x^2}$, $y''$ Second derivative — rate of change of gradient Acceleration
$\mathrm{f}^{(n)}(x)$ $n$-th derivative (jerk, snap, etc.)
Sign of $\mathrm{f}''$ $\mathrm{f}''>0$ → concave up (curve "smiles"); $\mathrm{f}''<0$ → concave down (curve "frowns") Useful for classifying stationary points (P3)

Tip: when computing $\mathrm{f}''(x)$, leave $\mathrm{f}'(x)$ in $ax^n$ form — don't convert to fractions. Easier to differentiate again.

6. Differentiation from first principles — proof template

To show $\mathrm{f}'(x)=$ something for $\mathrm{f}(x)=$ given:

Step Action
1 Write $\mathrm{f}'(x)=\lim_{h\to 0}\dfrac{\mathrm{f}(x+h)-\mathrm{f}(x)}{h}$
2 Substitute the function and expand $\mathrm{f}(x+h)$ (binomial expansion for powers)
3 Subtract $\mathrm{f}(x)$ — many terms should cancel (the $x^n$ terms)
4 Factor an $h$ out of the numerator
5 Cancel the $h$ in numerator with the $h$ in denominator
6 Take the limit: any term still containing $h,h^2,\dots$ goes to $0$; rest is your derivative

Worked: $\mathrm{f}(x)=x^3$. $(x+h)^3=x^3+3x^2h+3xh^2+h^3$. Subtract $x^3$: $3x^2h+3xh^2+h^3$. Divide by $h$: $3x^2+3xh+h^2$. Limit $h\to 0$: $3x^2$. ✓

7. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Differentiating $\dfrac{1}{x^3}$ as $\dfrac{1}{3x^2}$ (just dividing) Convert to $x^{-3}$, then power rule: $-3x^{-4}=-\dfrac{3}{x^4}$
Differentiating $(x^2)(x^3)$ as $(2x)(3x^2)$ First simplify: $x^5$, then $5x^4$. (Product rule is P3, not P1.)
Forgetting to rewrite $\sqrt x$ as $x^{1/2}$ before differentiating Always convert surds and roots to fractional indices first
Mishandling negative exponents: $-2x^{-3}$ has $-2$ as coefficient and $-3$ as new power Power rule: drop power, subtract one. $-2-1=-3$. Sign of new coefficient = sign of (old coef × power)
Constant term carried into derivative Constants vanish: $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(5)=0$
Computing $\mathrm{f}'(a)$ but then using $\mathrm{f}'(x)$ form in tangent equation Plug in numeric $\mathrm{f}'(a)$ as a constant gradient
Normal gradient sign error: writing $\dfrac{1}{\mathrm{f}'(a)}$ instead of $-\dfrac{1}{\mathrm{f}'(a)}$ The negative reciprocal — multiply gradients to check: $m_T\cdot m_N=-1$
Splitting $\dfrac{x-2}{x^2}$ as $1-\dfrac{2}{x^2}$ instead of $\dfrac{1}{x}-\dfrac{2}{x^2}$ Both terms get divided by $x^2$ — be careful with each
Differentiating $f(x)=\sqrt{36x^3}$ as $\sqrt{36}\cdot\sqrt{x^3}$ but forgetting $\sqrt{x^3}=x^{3/2}$ Always express in $ax^n$ form
Skipping "find $y$-coordinate" step in tangent/normal Need both $\mathrm{f}(a)$ AND $\mathrm{f}'(a)$ for the line equation
First-principles: not factoring $h$ out before taking limit Algebraic step is mandatory — until $h$ cancels, you can't take the limit
Finding the gradient at the wrong $x$-value (substituting $y$-value instead) Read carefully: the gradient at the point uses the $x$-coordinate of that point

10. Big picture (30-second mental model)

Differentiation gives you a gradient calculator for any nice function. It's defined by a limit (chord → tangent), but in practice you use the power rule $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(ax^n)=anx^{n-1}$ and linearity (term-by-term, constants pull out, additive constants vanish). Three routine applications:

  1. Gradient at a point: substitute $x$-value into $\mathrm{f}'(x)$.
  2. Tangent / normal lines: point-slope with $m_T=\mathrm{f}'(a)$ or $m_N=-1/\mathrm{f}'(a)$.
  3. Second derivative: differentiate the derivative — measures concavity (P1) and acceleration (mechanics).

Two tricks that make 90% of problems work: rewrite as $ax^n$ first (convert surds, fractions, splits), and expand products / split quotients before differentiating (no product/quotient rules in P1 — those are P3).

The chapter is essentially: master one rule, learn to prepare the function before applying it, and use the result for tangent/normal/optimisation downstream.
