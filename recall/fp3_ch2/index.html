1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Conic section A curve obtained by slicing a double cone with a plane: parabola, ellipse, hyperbola (and degenerate cases: circle, point, line pair). All have a focus-directrix definition controlled by eccentricity $e$. 2.1
Ellipse The closed curve $\dfrac{x^2}{a^2}+\dfrac{y^2}{b^2}=1$. Two perpendicular axes of symmetry, semi-axes $a$ (horizontal) and $b$ (vertical). When $a=b$ it's a circle. Parametric: $(a\cos t,b\sin t)$. 2.1
Hyperbola The pair of opposite curves $\dfrac{x^2}{a^2}-\dfrac{y^2}{b^2}=1$. Crosses $x$-axis at $\pm a$; never crosses $y$-axis. Parametric: $(\pm a\cosh t,b\sinh t)$ or $(a\sec\theta,b\tan\theta)$. 2.2
Rectangular hyperbola A hyperbola whose asymptotes are perpendicular. Two flavours: $xy=c^2$ (asymptotes are the axes) and $x^2-y^2=a^2$ (asymptotes $y=\pm x$). The first has parametric $(ct,c/t)$; from FP1. 2.2
Asymptote (hyperbola) A line the curve approaches but never reaches as $\lvert x\rvert\to\infty$. For $\dfrac{x^2}{a^2}-\dfrac{y^2}{b^2}=1$, asymptotes are $y=\pm\dfrac{b}{a}x$. 2.2
Major / minor axis Major = the longer axis of an ellipse, length $2a$ if $a>b$ (else $2b$); minor = the shorter one. The foci sit on the major axis. 2.3
Focus $S$ / directrix A focus is a fixed point; the directrix is a fixed line. Every conic is the locus of points $P$ where $\dfrac{PS}{PM}=e$ (constant), with $M$ the foot of perpendicular from $P$ to the directrix. 2.3
Eccentricity $e$ The constant ratio $PS/PM$ that defines a conic. $0 ellipse, $e=1$ parabola, $e>1$ hyperbola. ($e=0$ is a circle, $e\to\infty$ is a line.) Measures "how stretched" the conic is. 2.3
Foci formula (ellipse) For $\dfrac{x^2}{a^2}+\dfrac{y^2}{b^2}=1$ with $a>b$: $b^2=a^2(1-e^2)$, foci at $(\pm ae,0)$, directrices $x=\pm a/e$. If $b>a$: swap roles â†’ foci $(0,\pm be)$, directrices $y=\pm b/e$, and $a^2=b^2(1-e^2)$. 2.3
Foci formula (hyperbola) For $\dfrac{x^2}{a^2}-\dfrac{y^2}{b^2}=1$: $b^2=a^2(e^2-1)$, foci at $(\pm ae,0)$, directrices $x=\pm a/e$. ($a$ is "transverse" semi-axis, where the curve crosses.) Foci stay on $x$-axis even if $b>a$. 2.3
Sum-of-distances property (ellipse) For any $P$ on the ellipse, $PS+PS'=2a$ (sum of distances to the two foci is constant). The "string-and-two-pins" definition. 2.3 Ex 7
Difference-of-distances property (hyperbola) $\lvert PS-PS'\rvert=2a$ for any $P$ on the hyperbola. 2.3
Latus rectum The chord through a focus, perpendicular to the major axis. For ellipse: length $2b^2/a$. Useful in some sketches and proofs. Ex 2C Q8
Tangent / normal Tangent = touches the curve once at $P$ with matching gradient; normal = perpendicular to the tangent at $P$. To find them: differentiate (parametric or implicit), use $y-y_1=m(x-x_1)$, simplify. 2.4, 2.5
Implicit differentiation Differentiate both sides of a Cartesian equation w.r.t. $x$ treating $y$ as a function of $x$: e.g. $\dfrac{2x}{a^2}+\dfrac{2y}{b^2}\cdot\dfrac{\mathrm dy}{\mathrm dx}=0$, giving $\dfrac{\mathrm dy}{\mathrm dx}=-\dfrac{b^2x}{a^2y}$. 2.4
Parametric differentiation $\dfrac{\mathrm dy}{\mathrm dx}=\dfrac{\mathrm dy/\mathrm dt}{\mathrm dx/\mathrm dt}$. Often cleaner than implicit when the parametrisation is given. 2.4
Tangency condition ($y=mx+c$) A line $y=mx+c$ is tangent to a conic iff the discriminant of the resulting quadratic is zero. Ellipse: $b^2+a^2m^2=c^2$. Hyperbola: $b^2+c^2=a^2m^2$. 2.4 Ex 13, 2.5 Ex 18
Locus (loci, plural) The set of points satisfying a geometric condition. Find by giving the moving point coordinates $(X,Y)$ in terms of a parameter $t$, then eliminate $t$ to get a Cartesian equation in $X,Y$. 2.6

3. Tangent and normal formulas (to memorise)

For the standard forms $\dfrac{x^2}{a^2}\pm\dfrac{y^2}{b^2}=1$:

Curve At point Tangent Normal
Ellipse $(a\cos t,b\sin t)$ $bx\cos t+ay\sin t=ab$ $ax\sin t-by\cos t=(a^2-b^2)\cos t\sin t$
Ellipse (Cartesian) $(x_1,y_1)$ $\dfrac{xx_1}{a^2}+\dfrac{yy_1}{b^2}=1$ $\dfrac{a^2x}{x_1}-\dfrac{b^2y}{y_1}=a^2-b^2$
Hyperbola $(a\cosh t,b\sinh t)$ $bx\cosh t-ay\sinh t=ab$ $ax\sinh t+by\cosh t=(a^2+b^2)\sinh t\cosh t$
Hyperbola $(a\sec\theta,b\tan\theta)$ $bx\sec\theta-ay\tan\theta=ab$ $by+ax\sin\theta=(a^2+b^2)\tan\theta$
Parabola $(at^2,2at)$ $ty=x+at^2$ $y+tx=2at+at^3$
Rect. hyp. $(ct,c/t)$ $x+t^2 y=2ct$ $t^3 x-ty=c(t^4-1)$

Memory aid: the tangent formula is "$x_1$-replaced-by-coordinate-form, $=$ constant" â€” just like the Cartesian tangent at $(x_1,y_1)$.

4. Standard procedure to find tangent/normal

Step Action
1 Identify form: parametric or Cartesian; pick whichever differentiation is cleaner
2 Compute $\mathrm dy/\mathrm dx$ at $P$: parametric $\dfrac{\mathrm dy/\mathrm dt}{\mathrm dx/\mathrm dt}$, or implicit $-\dfrac{b^2 x}{a^2 y}$ etc.
3 For normal: take negative reciprocal of tangent gradient
4 Apply $y-y_1=m(x-x_1)$ with $P$'s coordinates
5 Multiply through to clear fractions; use the conic identity ($\cos^2+\sin^2=1$, $\cosh^2-\sinh^2=1$, $\sec^2-\tan^2=1$) to simplify
6 If finding intersection with axis: set $y=0$ or $x=0$ in the tangent/normal equation
7 If $y=mx+c$ given: substitute into conic equation; tangent â‡” discriminant $=0$

7. Loci: standard recipe (Section 2.6)

Step Action
1 Identify the moving point â€” call its coordinates $(X,Y)$
2 Parametrise the original conic (use $t$ or $\theta$); write tangent / normal / intersection / midpoint formula
3 Express $X$ and $Y$ in terms of the parameter
4 Eliminate the parameter using the parametric identity ($\cos^2+\sin^2=1$, etc.)
5 Result: a Cartesian equation in $X,Y$ â€” that's the locus
6 If asked, identify the locus's shape (ellipse / hyperbola / parabola / line)

Worked snippet (Ex 21): The tangent at $(a\cos t,b\sin t)$ to ellipse meets axes at $A(a\sec t,0)$ and $B(0,b\,\mathrm{cosec}\,t)$. Midpoint $(X,Y)=(\tfrac{a\sec t}{2},\tfrac{b\,\mathrm{cosec}\,t}{2})$. So $\cos t=\tfrac{a}{2X},\ \sin t=\tfrac{b}{2Y}$. Using $\cos^2 t+\sin^2 t=1$ â†’ $\tfrac{a^2}{4X^2}+\tfrac{b^2}{4Y^2}=1$.

9. Common pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Confusing $b^2=a^2(1-e^2)$ (ellipse) with $b^2=a^2(e^2-1)$ (hyperbola) Sign of $1-e^2$ tells you: $<1$ â†’ ellipse, $>1$ â†’ hyperbola
For ellipse with $b>a$, putting foci on $x$-axis Foci sit on the major axis â€” if $b>a$, foci are on $y$-axis
For hyperbola with $b>a$, putting foci on $y$-axis Hyperbola's foci stay on the transverse axis (always $x$-axis for $\tfrac{x^2}{a^2}-\tfrac{y^2}{b^2}=1$)

10. Big picture (the 30-second mental model)

A conic is one curve, three personalities (ellipse / parabola / hyperbola), distinguished by eccentricity:

  1. Cartesian form is a quadratic in $x,y$. Standard ellipse: $\tfrac{x^2}{a^2}+\tfrac{y^2}{b^2}=1$; standard hyperbola: $\tfrac{x^2}{a^2}-\tfrac{y^2}{b^2}=1$. The sign-flip is the only structural difference.
  2. Parametric form uses trig (ellipse) or hyperbolic/sec-tan (hyperbola), exploiting $\cos^2+\sin^2=1$ or $\cosh^2-\sinh^2=1$ to satisfy the conic identity automatically.
  3. Focus-directrix definition unifies all three: $PS/PM=e$, with $e<1,=1,>1$. From this you derive the foci-and-directrix formulas in terms of $a,b,e$.
  4. Tangent/normal: differentiate parametrically or implicitly, apply point-slope, simplify with the conic identity. There are clean formulas to memorise but you can always re-derive in 30 seconds.
  5. Locus problems: parametrise, write the moving point's coordinates in terms of the parameter, eliminate.
