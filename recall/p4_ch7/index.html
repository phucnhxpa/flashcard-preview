P4 Ch 7 — Vectors (2D & 3D)

The whole chapter in one mental model. A vector has magnitude and direction. In 2D/3D it is encoded as a column $\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\\z\end{pmatrix}$ or in $\mathbf{i,j,k}$ form. Three core formula families: (1) arithmetic — add component-wise, scalar-multiply, $\overrightarrow{AB}=\mathbf{b}-\mathbf{a}$, magnitude $|\mathbf{a}|=\sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}$, unit $\hat{\mathbf{a}}=\mathbf{a}/|\mathbf{a}|$. (2) Lines — $\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}+\lambda\mathbf{b}$ for "point + direction"; or $\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{c}+\lambda(\mathbf{d}-\mathbf{c})$ through two points. Two lines may intersect, be parallel, or be skew (3D only). (3) Scalar product $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\cos\theta=a_1b_1+a_2b_2+a_3b_3$ — the bridge from coordinates to angles, and the test for perpendicularity ($\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=0$).

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Vector Quantity with both magnitude (length) and direction. Written as a bold letter $\mathbf{a}$ or as $\overrightarrow{PQ}$. Encoded in 3D as $\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\\z\end{pmatrix}$. 7.1
Scalar A plain number with no direction (e.g. $\lambda=3,-2.5$). Multiplying $\lambda\mathbf{a}$ stretches/flips the vector but keeps the line of action. 7.1
Magnitude $\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert$ The length of the vector. For $\mathbf{a}=\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\\z\end{pmatrix}$, $\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert=\sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}$ by 3D Pythagoras. 7.3, 7.4
Unit vector $\hat{\mathbf{a}}$ A vector of length 1 in the direction of $\mathbf{a}$: $\hat{\mathbf{a}}=\dfrac{\mathbf{a}}{\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert}$. Useful for direction, separately from magnitude. 7.3
i, j, k Standard unit vectors along $x,y,z$ axes: $\mathbf{i}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\\0\end{pmatrix},\mathbf{j}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\\0\end{pmatrix},\mathbf{k}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\0\\1\end{pmatrix}$. 7.4
Position vector The vector from the origin $O$ to a point $P$, written $\overrightarrow{OP}$ or $\mathbf{p}$. Identifies $P$ uniquely. 7.7
Displacement vector $\overrightarrow{AB}=\mathbf{b}-\mathbf{a}$ — the "trip" from $A$ to $B$, found by end minus start in position vectors. 7.7
Triangle law Vector addition: $\overrightarrow{AB}+\overrightarrow{BC}=\overrightarrow{AC}$. Sum is the resultant, head-to-tail. 7.1
Parallel vectors $\mathbf{a}\parallel\mathbf{b}\iff\mathbf{a}=\lambda\mathbf{b}$ for some scalar $\lambda$ — one is a multiple of the other. 7.4
Collinear points $A,B,C$ on the same straight line — equivalently $\overrightarrow{AB}\parallel\overrightarrow{AC}$. 7.9
Direction vector The vector $\mathbf{b}$ in $\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}+\lambda\mathbf{b}$ — points along the line. Any non-zero scalar multiple of $\mathbf{b}$ is also a valid direction vector. 7.9
Vector equation of a line $\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}+\lambda\mathbf{b}$ — "general point on line" = "fixed point" + scalar $\lambda$ × direction. Generates every point on the line as $\lambda$ varies. 7.9
Skew lines (3D only.) Two lines that don't intersect AND aren't parallel — they pass at different "heights" in 3D. 7.10
Scalar (dot) product $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert\lvert\mathbf{b}\rvert\cos\theta$ — produces a number from two vectors. Encodes the angle between them. Algebraic form: $a_1b_1+a_2b_2+a_3b_3$. 7.11
Perpendicular test $\mathbf{a}\perp\mathbf{b}\iff\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=0$ (for non-zero vectors). 7.11
Angle between vectors $\cos\theta=\dfrac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert\lvert\mathbf{b}\rvert}$. Acute if $\cos\theta>0$, obtuse if $<0$, right if $=0$. 7.11
Direction cosines $\cos\theta_x=\dfrac{x}{\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert}$ etc. — the cosines of the angles a vector makes with the $x$-, $y$-, $z$-axes. 7.4
Ratio division of a line If $P$ divides $AB$ in ratio $m:n$, then $\overrightarrow{OP}=\dfrac{n\mathbf{a}+m\mathbf{b}}{m+n}$. 7.5, 7.6

2. Vector arithmetic (Sections 7.1, 7.2, 7.4)

Operation Formula
Addition $\begin{pmatrix}a_1\\a_2\\a_3\end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}b_1\\b_2\\b_3\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}a_1+b_1\\a_2+b_2\\a_3+b_3\end{pmatrix}$
Scalar multiplication $\lambda\begin{pmatrix}a_1\\a_2\\a_3\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}\lambda a_1\\\lambda a_2\\\lambda a_3\end{pmatrix}$
Negation $-\mathbf{a}=(-1)\mathbf{a}$
Subtraction $\mathbf{a}-\mathbf{b}=\mathbf{a}+(-\mathbf{b})$
Magnitude (2D) $\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$
Magnitude (3D) $\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert=\sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}$
Unit vector $\hat{\mathbf{a}}=\dfrac{1}{\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert}\mathbf{a}$
Distance $A$ to $B$ $\lvert\overrightarrow{AB}\rvert=\lvert\mathbf{b}-\mathbf{a}\rvert$

3. Position vectors (Section 7.7)

Quantity Formula
Position vector of $P=(x,y,z)$ $\overrightarrow{OP}=x\mathbf{i}+y\mathbf{j}+z\mathbf{k}=\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\\z\end{pmatrix}$
Displacement $\overrightarrow{AB}$ $\mathbf{b}-\mathbf{a}$ (end minus start)
Midpoint of $AB$ $\dfrac{1}{2}(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b})$
$P$ divides $AB$ in ratio $m:n$ (internal) $\overrightarrow{OP}=\dfrac{n\mathbf{a}+m\mathbf{b}}{m+n}$
$P$ divides $AB$ such that $AP:PB=m:n$ Same as above
Triangle inequality $\lvert\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}\rvert\le\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert+\lvert\mathbf{b}\rvert$

4. Equation of a line (Section 7.9)

Setup Vector equation
Through point $A$ (pos vec $\mathbf{a}$), parallel to $\mathbf{b}$ $\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}+\lambda\mathbf{b}$
Through points $C,D$ (pos vecs $\mathbf{c},\mathbf{d}$) $\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{c}+\lambda(\mathbf{d}-\mathbf{c})$
Through $A$, parallel to $z$-axis $\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}+\lambda\mathbf{k}$
Test: does $P$ lie on line $\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}+\lambda\mathbf{b}$? Equate components $\to$ all three give same $\lambda$
Cartesian form (eliminate $\lambda$) $\dfrac{x-a_1}{b_1}=\dfrac{y-a_2}{b_2}=\dfrac{z-a_3}{b_3}$ (when no $b_i=0$)

Two equations of the same line can look different — pick any point on the line as base, any non-zero scalar multiple of the direction as direction.

5. Lines in 3D — relative positions (Section 7.10)

Status Test What to do
Parallel and distinct Direction vectors are scalar multiples; no common point No intersection
Coincident (same line) Direction vectors are scalar multiples AND a point on $l_1$ lies on $l_2$ Same line
Intersecting Solve $\mathbf{a}_1+\lambda\mathbf{b}_1=\mathbf{a}_2+\mu\mathbf{b}_2$ — 3 equations, 2 unknowns $\lambda,\mu$. If 2 give a $(\lambda,\mu)$ pair that satisfies the 3rd, lines meet Sub $\lambda$ back to find intersection point
Skew (3D only) Direction vectors NOT parallel AND no $(\lambda,\mu)$ satisfies all 3 component equations No intersection, not parallel

Procedure:

  1. Set the two parametric forms equal component-wise → 3 equations.
  2. Solve two of them simultaneously for $\lambda,\mu$.
  3. Check the third equation. Consistent ⇒ intersect; inconsistent ⇒ skew (or parallel if directions are multiples).

6. Scalar (dot) product (Section 7.11)

Definition (geometric): $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert\lvert\mathbf{b}\rvert\cos\theta$.

Definition (algebraic): $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=a_1b_1+a_2b_2+a_3b_3$.

Property Formula
Commutative $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{a}$
Distributive $\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}+\mathbf{c})=\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}+\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{c}$
Scalar factor $(\lambda\mathbf{a})\cdot\mathbf{b}=\lambda(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})$
Self-dot $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{a}=\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert^2$
Perpendicular $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=0\iff\mathbf{a}\perp\mathbf{b}$ (for non-zero vectors)
Parallel (same direction) $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert\lvert\mathbf{b}\rvert$
Parallel (opposite) $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=-\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert\lvert\mathbf{b}\rvert$
Unit-vector dots $\mathbf{i}\cdot\mathbf{i}=\mathbf{j}\cdot\mathbf{j}=\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{k}=1$; $\mathbf{i}\cdot\mathbf{j}=\mathbf{j}\cdot\mathbf{k}=\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{i}=0$

Angle between two vectors:

$$\cos\theta=\frac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert\lvert\mathbf{b}\rvert}.$$

Angle between two lines = angle between their direction vectors (use the acute angle, take absolute value of $\cos$ if needed).

7. Direction angles (Section 7.4)

For $\mathbf{a}=x\mathbf{i}+y\mathbf{j}+z\mathbf{k}$:

Angle Formula
With positive $x$-axis $\cos\theta_x=\dfrac{x}{\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert}$
With positive $y$-axis $\cos\theta_y=\dfrac{y}{\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert}$
With positive $z$-axis $\cos\theta_z=\dfrac{z}{\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert}$
Identity $\cos^2\theta_x+\cos^2\theta_y+\cos^2\theta_z=1$

10. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Forgetting $\mathbf{j}$-component when given $\mathbf{a}=4\mathbf{i}-5\mathbf{k}$ Read as $4\mathbf{i}+0\mathbf{j}-5\mathbf{k}$
Squaring negative components wrong $(-3)^2=9$ — always positive
Confusing scalar product with regular multiplication Scalar product outputs a scalar, not a vector; sums products of components
Using same $\lambda$ in two different line equations Use $\lambda$ for $l_1$ and $\mu$ for $l_2$
Saying "no intersection ⇒ parallel" in 3D Could be skew — 3D-only case
Assuming the angle between vectors is always acute $\cos\theta$ can be negative ⇒ obtuse
Forgetting to take $\lvert\cos\theta\rvert$ for "angle between lines" (needs acute) Take absolute value if asked for the acute angle
Mixing up $\overrightarrow{AB}$ and $\overrightarrow{BA}$ $\overrightarrow{BA}=-\overrightarrow{AB}=\mathbf{a}-\mathbf{b}$
Forgetting to normalise to get unit vector Divide each component by $\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert$
Assuming "$\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=0$ ⇒ vectors are zero" NO — perpendicular
Assuming "$\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}>0$ means parallel" NO — only acute angle. Parallel needs equality $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert\lvert\mathbf{b}\rvert$
Solving 3D simultaneous and forgetting to check 3rd equation Skew lines satisfy 2 of 3 — must verify

11. Big picture (30-second mental model)

A vector is a directed magnitude. The three pillars:

  1. Algebra: add/subtract component-wise. Magnitude via Pythagoras. Unit vector by dividing by magnitude.
  2. Lines: $\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}+\lambda\mathbf{b}$ — point + direction. Two lines may meet, be parallel, or be skew.
  3. Dot product: $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=\lvert\mathbf{a}\rvert\lvert\mathbf{b}\rvert\cos\theta=a_1b_1+a_2b_2+a_3b_3$. Bridge between geometry (angles) and algebra (components). Test for perpendicularity.

Two formulas, three pillars — that's the chapter.
