FP2 Ch 7 — Maclaurin and Taylor Series

The whole chapter in one mental model. Any "nice" function (smooth, derivatives finite at the chosen base point) can be re-written as an infinite polynomial in powers of $(x-a)$. Maclaurin is the special case $a=0$. The coefficients are forced: the $r$-th one is $\dfrac{f^{(r)}(a)}{r!}$. You either (i) compute derivatives directly, (ii) substitute into a standard series from the formula booklet, or (iii) bootstrap a series solution to a DE by repeatedly differentiating the equation and reading off $y, y', y'', \dots$ at the base point.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
$n$-th derivative $f^{(n)}(x)$ The result of differentiating $f$ a total of $n$ times. Notations: $f',f'',f''',f^{(4)},\dots$. The chain & product rules are unchanged — you just keep going. Beyond third order, primes get clumsy so the bracketed-superscript is preferred. 7.1
Power series An infinite sum of the form $a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2+\dots$ — a polynomial with infinitely many terms. Treat as a function whose value at $x$ is the limit of partial sums (when that limit exists). It only "is" the function it represents inside its interval of convergence. 7.2
Maclaurin series The power series $f(x)=\sum_{r\ge0}\dfrac{f^{(r)}(0)}{r!}x^r$ — i.e. a Taylor series centred at $0$. It uses only values of $f$ and its derivatives at $x=0$ to predict $f$ everywhere (within validity). Best near $x=0$, worse further out. 7.2
Taylor series at $a$ $f(x)=\sum_{r\ge0}\dfrac{f^{(r)}(a)}{r!}(x-a)^r$. A "Maclaurin centred elsewhere": uses derivative-data at $x=a$ instead of $0$. Two equivalent forms: in powers of $(x-a)$ (form B), or by substituting $x\to x+a$ in powers of $x$ (form A). 7.4
Maclaurin polynomial of degree $r$ The truncation $f(0)+f'(0)x+\dots+\dfrac{f^{(r)}(0)}{r!}x^r$. A finite polynomial that approximates $f$. Adding more terms generally improves accuracy near $0$. 7.2
Interval of validity / convergence The set of $x$-values where the infinite series equals $f(x)$. E.g. $\ln(1+x)$: $-1diverges — adding more terms makes the answer worse, not better. 7.2
Convergent / divergent A series converges if its partial sums approach a finite limit; it diverges otherwise. E.g. $1+x+x^2+\dots$ converges to $\dfrac{1}{1-x}$ for $\lvert x\rvert<1$ but diverges for $\lvert x\rvert\ge 1$. 7.2
Ratio test (challenge box) A sufficient test for convergence: $\sum a_r$ converges if $\lim_{r\to\infty}\left\lvert\dfrac{a_{r+1}}{a_r}\right\rvert<1$, diverges if $>1$, inconclusive at $=1$. Used to derive validity intervals. 7.2 Challenge
Compound function A function built from a standard one by substitution, multiplication, etc., e.g. $\cos(2x^2)$, $e^{px}\sin 3x$, $\ln\!\sqrt{\tfrac{1+x}{1-x}}$. Their series are obtained by combining known series, not by direct differentiation. 7.3
Alternating sequence trick $(-1)^r$ flips sign each $r$, $(-1)^{r-1}$ starts positive then alternates. Useful for capturing $\ln(1+x)$, $\sin x$, $\cos x$, $\arctan x$ in closed-form general-term notation. 7.2 Ex 4
Formulae-booklet standard series Five Maclaurins given in the exam booklet: $e^x,\ \ln(1+x),\ \sin x,\ \cos x,\ \arctan x$. You may quote these without proof. Substituting $g(x)$ for $x$ creates compound expansions instantly. 7.3
Series-solution method (DE) Use a Taylor expansion as the unknown function: write $y=y_0+(x-x_0)y'_0+\tfrac{(x-x_0)^2}{2!}y''_0+\dots$, then differentiate the DE itself to find each $y^{(r)}(x_0)$ from previous ones. Builds an approximate $y$ as a polynomial without ever solving the DE in closed form. 7.5
Initial conditions The "seed" data $x_0, y_0$ (and possibly $y'_0$) used to launch the series-solution recursion. The DE supplies $y'_0$ directly (if first-order) or $y''_0$ (if second-order) from these. 7.5
Notation $\left.\dfrac{\mathrm dy}{\mathrm dx}\right\rvert_{x_0}$ "$\mathrm dy/\mathrm dx$ evaluated at $x=x_0$" — a single number, not a function. Used in series-solution work to denote the Taylor coefficients. 7.5
Ascending / descending powers "Ascending" $=$ low-to-high powers ($a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2+\dots$). Exam questions almost always ask for ascending powers, which matches Taylor/Maclaurin order. 7.2
Limit problem via Maclaurin A common application: $\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{f(x)}{x^k}$ is found by expanding $f$ to order $x^k$, simplifying. E.g. Ex 8: $\lim_{x\to 0}\dfrac{3\sin x-4x\cos x+x}{x^3}=\tfrac32$. 7.3 Q4, Q8
Watch out: radians Trig Maclaurins assume $x$ is in radians. To approximate $\sin 10°$ use $x=\pi/18$, not $10$. 7.2 Ex 5

2. Maclaurin recipe (Section 7.2)

For a function whose derivatives are all finite at $0$:

Step Action Output
1 Compute $f(0), f'(0), f''(0), \dots$ until you have enough List of derivative values at $0$
2 Slot into $f(x)=\sum_{r\ge 0}\dfrac{f^{(r)}(0)}{r!}x^r$ Polynomial in $x$
3 Identify general term using factorials and $(-1)^r$ if alternating Closed form for $r$-th coefficient
4 State validity (use ratio test or quote standard) Interval like $\lvert x\rvert<1$

3. Standard Maclaurin series (formulae booklet — memorise!)

Function Series Validity
$e^x$ $\displaystyle 1+x+\dfrac{x^2}{2!}+\dfrac{x^3}{3!}+\dots+\dfrac{x^r}{r!}+\dots$ all $x\in\mathbb R$
$\ln(1+x)$ $\displaystyle x-\dfrac{x^2}{2}+\dfrac{x^3}{3}-\dots+(-1)^{r+1}\dfrac{x^r}{r}+\dots$ $-1
$\sin x$ $\displaystyle x-\dfrac{x^3}{3!}+\dfrac{x^5}{5!}-\dots+(-1)^{r}\dfrac{x^{2r+1}}{(2r+1)!}+\dots$ all $x\in\mathbb R$
$\cos x$ $\displaystyle 1-\dfrac{x^2}{2!}+\dfrac{x^4}{4!}-\dots+(-1)^{r}\dfrac{x^{2r}}{(2r)!}+\dots$ all $x\in\mathbb R$
$\arctan x$ $\displaystyle x-\dfrac{x^3}{3}+\dfrac{x^5}{5}-\dots+(-1)^{r}\dfrac{x^{2r+1}}{2r+1}+\dots$ $-1\le x\le 1$
$(1+x)^n$ (binomial, $n\notin\mathbb N$) $\displaystyle 1+nx+\dfrac{n(n-1)}{2!}x^2+\dots$ $\lvert x\rvert<1$

Pattern recognition: $e^x$ has every $x^r/r!$. $\sin/\cos$ skip even/odd respectively and alternate. $\ln$ and $\arctan$ have plain denominators (not factorials).

4. Compound-function tactics (Section 7.3)

Compound shape Strategy Validity adjustment
$f(kx)$, $f(x^k)$ Substitute $kx$ or $x^k$ into the standard series Old condition becomes condition on new argument; e.g. $\ln(1-3x)$: $-1<-3x\le 1$ → $-\tfrac13\le x<\tfrac13$
$\ln(a+bx)$ with $a\ne 1$ Factor: $\ln(a+bx)=\ln a+\ln\!\left(1+\tfrac{bx}{a}\right)$, then expand the log Same as for $\ln(1+u)$ with $u=bx/a$
$\ln\!\dfrac{f}{g}$ or $\ln(fg)$ Use $\ln\dfrac{f}{g}=\ln f-\ln g$, then expand each separately, then combine Intersection of the two intervals
$f(x)\cdot g(x)$ (e.g. $e^{2x}\sin x$) Multiply two series, collect terms up to required power, drop higher Stay within the smaller interval
$e^{f(x)}$ where $f(0)=0$ Write $f$ as series, use $e^{a+b}=e^a e^b$ to split, multiply series Inherits $f$'s validity
Even powers only, e.g. $\sin^2 x$ Use $\sin^2 x=\tfrac{1-\cos 2x}{2}$, expand $\cos 2x$ All $x$
Limit $\lim_{x\to 0}\dfrac{f(x)}{x^k}$ Expand $f$ to order $x^k$, divide term-by-term, set $x=0$ n/a (just a value)

5. Taylor recipe at $x=a$ (Section 7.4)

Use when $f^{(r)}(0)$ blows up (e.g. $\ln x$ at $0$) or when you want accuracy near a point $a\ne 0$.

Form Formula Use when
(A) $f(x+a)$ in powers of $x$ $\displaystyle f(x+a)=f(a)+f'(a)x+\dfrac{f''(a)}{2!}x^2+\dots$ Question gives shifted argument like $\tan(x+\tfrac{\pi}{4})$
(B) $f(x)$ in powers of $(x-a)$ $\displaystyle f(x)=f(a)+f'(a)(x-a)+\dfrac{f''(a)}{2!}(x-a)^2+\dots$ Question asks "in powers of $(x-a)$"
(C) Maclaurin-as-Taylor Set $a=0$ in (B) → recovers Maclaurin When derivatives exist at $0$

Procedure: compute $f(a), f'(a), f''(a), \dots$, plug into (A) or (B). For $\sqrt x$ at $a=1$, $\ln x$ at $a=e$, etc., choose $a$ so that $f(a)$ is a clean number.

6. Series solution of a DE (Section 7.5)

For $\dfrac{\mathrm dy}{\mathrm dx}=f(x,y)$ with $y(x_0)=y_0$ — or a 2nd-order DE with $y(x_0)$ AND $y'(x_0)$ given:

Step First-order $\mathrm dy/\mathrm dx=f(x,y)$ Second-order $y''=f(x,y,y')$
1 Read off $x_0,y_0$ from initial conditions Read off $x_0,y_0,y'_0$
2 Sub into the DE → $y'(x_0)$ Sub into the DE → $y''(x_0)$
3 Differentiate the DE to get $y''$ in terms of $x,y,y'$, sub previous values → $y''(x_0)$ Differentiate again to get $y'''$, sub previous values → $y'''(x_0)$
4 Repeat differentiating to required order Repeat to required order
5 Plug into Taylor: $y=y_0+y'_0(x-x_0)+\tfrac{y''_0}{2!}(x-x_0)^2+\dots$ Same Taylor formula

When $x_0=0$, $(x-x_0)$ collapses to $x$ → Maclaurin form. When $x_0\neq 0$ (e.g. Ex 13, $x_0=1$), keep $(x-1)^k$ — do NOT silently expand to powers of $x$.

Watch out: when differentiating the DE implicitly, treat $y$ as a function of $x$ — you'll need product rule on terms like $xy$, chain rule on $\sin y$, etc.

8. Worked-out archetypes (memorise the shape)

# Problem Method Result
1 Maclaurin of $\ln(1+x)$ Differentiate $r$ times → $f^{(r)}(0)=(-1)^{r-1}(r-1)!$ $x-\tfrac{x^2}{2}+\tfrac{x^3}{3}-\dots$
2 Maclaurin of $\sin x$ Cycle $\sin,\cos,-\sin,-\cos$ at 0 gives $0,1,0,-1$ $x-\tfrac{x^3}{3!}+\tfrac{x^5}{5!}-\dots$

4 $\ln\!\tfrac{\sqrt{1+2x}}{1-3x}$ Split as $\tfrac12\ln(1+2x)-\ln(1-3x)$, expand each, intersect intervals $4x+\tfrac72 x^2+\tfrac{31}{3}x^3+\dots$, valid $-\tfrac13\le x<\tfrac13$
5 $e^{\sin x}$ to $x^4$ Expand $\sin x$, then $e^{\sin x}=e^{x}\cdot e^{-x^3/6}\cdot\dots$, multiply, drop $x^{\ge5}$ $1+x+\tfrac{x^2}{2}-\tfrac{x^4}{8}$
6 Taylor of $e^{-x}$ in powers of $(x+4)$ $a=-4$; derivatives alternate sign, all magnitude $e^4$ $e^4\!\bigl(1-(x+4)+\tfrac12(x+4)^2-\tfrac16(x+4)^3+\dots\bigr)$
7 Taylor of $\tan(x+\tfrac{\pi}{4})$ to $x^3$ $a=\pi/4$, $\tan a=1$, $\sec^2 a=2$, etc. $1+2x+2x^2+\tfrac83 x^3+\dots$
8 Series solution of $y''=y-\sin x$, $y(0)=1, y'(0)=2$, to $x^3$ At 0: $y''=1-0=1$. Differentiate: $y'''=y'-\cos x\Rightarrow y'''(0)=2-1=1$ $1+2x+\tfrac{x^2}{2}+\tfrac{x^3}{6}+\dots$
9 Series solution of $y'+2y'-xy=0$ at $x_0=1$, $y(1)=1, y'(1)=2$ Get $y''(1)=-3$; differentiate DE for $y'''(1)=9, y^{(4)}(1)=-17$ $1+2(x-1)-\tfrac32(x-1)^2+\tfrac32(x-1)^3-\tfrac{17}{24}(x-1)^4+\dots$

9. Pitfalls (where marks evaporate)

Pitfall Fix
Expanding $\ln 1.8$ from $\ln(1+x)$ at $x=0.8$ — slow convergence The further $x$ from 0, the worse Maclaurin; use Taylor at a closer base point or $\ln\!\tfrac{1+x}{1-x}$ trick
Using degrees in Maclaurin of $\sin x$ Always radians — convert $10°\to\pi/18$
Forgetting validity intersection when combining two series When using $\ln A-\ln B$, take overlap of validity intervals
Using $\ln x$ in a Maclaurin (centred at 0) Impossible — $\ln(0)$ undefined; switch to Taylor centred at $a>0$
Confusing Taylor (A) and (B) — substituting $x$ instead of $x-a$ Read the question: "in powers of $x$" → form (A); "in powers of $(x-a)$" → form (B)
In series-solution: forgetting product rule when differentiating $xy$ $\dfrac{\mathrm d}{\mathrm dx}(xy)=y+x\dfrac{\mathrm dy}{\mathrm dx}$
In series-solution at $x_0\ne 0$: writing answer in powers of $x$ Must keep $(x-x_0)^k$ — that's the whole point of Taylor
Forgetting to simplify coefficients after multiplying series E.g. $\tfrac{1}{2}\cdot\tfrac{(2x)^2}{2}=x^2$ — don't leave it ugly
Multiplying two infinite series and including $x^5$ contribution by accident Stop at the requested order; drop higher powers as you multiply
In limit problems: expanding $f$ to too low an order Need at least up to the $x^k$ term that survives division

10. Big picture (the 30-second mental model)

Think of a function near a point $a$ as a polynomial whose coefficients are just its derivative-values at that point, scaled by factorials:

$$f(x)\approx f(a)+f'(a)(x-a)+\dfrac{f''(a)}{2!}(x-a)^2+\dots$$

  1. Maclaurin is the case $a=0$. Five canonical series ($e^x,\ln(1+x),\sin,\cos,\arctan$) are given to you in the formulae booklet — everything else is built from these by substitution / multiplication / log laws.
  2. Taylor lifts the centre to any point $a$ where the derivatives are finite — needed when Maclaurin can't see a function (e.g. $\ln x$).
  3. Validity matters: outside the convergence interval, more terms makes things worse.
  4. DE series solutions flip the trick: instead of computing $y$ first and then expanding, you read $y, y', y'', \dots$ directly off the differential equation by repeated differentiation, and assemble the Taylor polynomial. No closed form needed.

Two recipes (Maclaurin / Taylor), six standard series, one substitution toolkit — that's the chapter.
