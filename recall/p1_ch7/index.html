P1 Ch 7 — Radians

The whole chapter in one mental model. Replace degrees with radians ($\pi=180°$, so 1 rad $=\dfrac{180°}{\pi}\approx 57.3°$) and the formulas for circular geometry become beautifully simple: arc length $\ell=r\theta$, sector area $A=\tfrac12 r^2\theta$, segment area $A=\tfrac12 r^2(\theta-\sin\theta)$. All require $\theta$ in radians — putting degrees in breaks every formula. Composite-shape problems (perimeter, area of "a sector minus a triangle", "two arcs", etc.) reduce to adding/subtracting sectors, segments, triangles, and straight chords/radii.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Radian The angle subtended at the centre of a circle by an arc whose length equals the radius. So if you wrap a length-$r$ piece of string round a circle of radius $r$, it spans 1 rad at the centre. Roughly $57.3°$. Notation: $1\,\text{rad}$ or $1^c$. 7.1
Conversion factor $360°=2\pi$ rad, so $\pi$ rad $=180°$. To go degrees → radians multiply by $\dfrac{\pi}{180°}$. To go radians → degrees multiply by $\dfrac{180°}{\pi}$. The factor $\pi/180$ is so common it's worth memorising. 7.1
Exact angles Common values to memorise: $30°=\tfrac{\pi}{6}$, $45°=\tfrac{\pi}{4}$, $60°=\tfrac{\pi}{3}$, $90°=\tfrac{\pi}{2}$, $180°=\pi$, $360°=2\pi$. Plus useful extras: $120°=\tfrac{2\pi}{3}$, $135°=\tfrac{3\pi}{4}$, $150°=\tfrac{5\pi}{6}$. 7.1
Subtended angle The angle made at a point (usually the centre $O$) by the two endpoints of an arc or chord. The "angle the arc/chord 'sees' from $O$". 7.1
Arc A portion of a circle's circumference between two points. Minor arc = the shorter; major arc = the longer. The full circumference $2\pi r$ is the arc of the whole circle ($\theta=2\pi$). 7.2
Chord The straight line segment between two points on a circle. The chord and its arc together bound a segment. Chord length $=2r\sin(\theta/2)$ for centre angle $\theta$. 7.3
Sector The "pie slice" bounded by two radii and an arc. Like a pizza slice. Minor sector = small slice (angle $<\pi$), major sector = large slice (angle $>\pi$). Sectors $\ne$ segments. 7.3
Segment The "cap" bounded by a chord and the arc that the chord cuts off — the bit you'd slice off if you cut the pizza along a straight chord rather than along radii. Minor segment = the smaller cap. Don't confuse with sector. 7.3
Arc length formula $\ell=r\theta$ where $\theta$ is in radians. Comes from the definition: full circumference $2\pi r$ corresponds to $\theta=2\pi$, so length scales linearly with $\theta$. If you used degrees, you'd need $\ell=\dfrac{\pi r\theta_{\deg}}{180}$ — much uglier. 7.2
Sector area formula $A=\tfrac12 r^2\theta$ ($\theta$ in radians). Comes from "fraction of circle" times "area of circle": $A=\dfrac{\theta}{2\pi}\cdot\pi r^2=\tfrac12 r^2\theta$. With degrees: $A=\dfrac{\pi r^2\theta_{\deg}}{360}$. 7.3
Segment area formula $A=\tfrac12 r^2(\theta-\sin\theta)$. Derivation: area of sector minus area of triangle $OAB$. The triangle has area $\tfrac12 r\cdot r\cdot\sin\theta=\tfrac12 r^2\sin\theta$. Subtract: $\tfrac12 r^2\theta-\tfrac12 r^2\sin\theta$. 7.3
Perimeter of sector Arc + two radii: $P=r\theta+2r=r(\theta+2)$. Inverse: $r=\dfrac{P}{\theta+2}$. Don't forget the two radii! 7.2 Ex 5
Perimeter of segment Arc + chord: $P=r\theta+2r\sin(\theta/2)$. Half-angle for chord because $\sin(\theta/2)=\dfrac{\text{half-chord}}{r}$. 7.3
Reflex angle An angle greater than $\pi$ rad ($180°$). The major arc/sector subtends a reflex angle: if minor arc has angle $\theta$, major has $2\pi-\theta$. 7.2 Ex 6
Angle in a semicircle A geometric fact (circle theorem): the angle subtended by a diameter at any point on the circumference is $\tfrac{\pi}{2}$ (a right angle). Often combined with sector/segment problems. 7.3 Ex 12
Equilateral / isosceles in sector problems If you have an isosceles triangle $OAB$ ($OA=OB=r$), drop a perpendicular from $O$ to $AB$ to get two congruent right triangles — useful for finding $\theta$ given chord length: $\sin(\theta/2)=\dfrac{|AB|/2}{r}$. 7.2 Ex 6
Tangent (to circle) A line that touches the circle at exactly one point and is perpendicular to the radius at that point. Crucial in shaded-region problems where tangents form right angles with radii. 7.3 Q11
Calculator mode When evaluating $\sin\theta,\cos\theta,\tan\theta$ for $\theta$ in radians, calculator must be in radian mode (RAD), not degrees. Wrong mode → wildly wrong answers, often the most-common sectional pitfall. 7.2 Ex 6

3. The four core formulas — cross-tabulated by quantity

Quantity Formula (θ in radians) Solve-for-$r$ rearrangement Solve-for-$\theta$ rearrangement
Arc length $\ell=r\theta$ $r=\dfrac{\ell}{\theta}$ $\theta=\dfrac{\ell}{r}$
Sector area $A=\tfrac12 r^2\theta$ $r=\sqrt{\dfrac{2A}{\theta}}$ $\theta=\dfrac{2A}{r^2}$
Sector perimeter $P=r(\theta+2)=r\theta+2r$ $r=\dfrac{P}{\theta+2}$ $\theta=\dfrac{P}{r}-2$
Triangle area $OAB$ (with $OA=OB=r$) $T=\tfrac12 r^2\sin\theta$ $r=\sqrt{\dfrac{2T}{\sin\theta}}$ $\sin\theta=\dfrac{2T}{r^2}$
Segment area $A=\tfrac12 r^2(\theta-\sin\theta)$ (transcendental in $r$) (transcendental in $\theta$ — solve numerically)
Chord length $\text{chord}=2r\sin(\theta/2)$ $r=\dfrac{\text{chord}}{2\sin(\theta/2)}$ $\sin(\theta/2)=\dfrac{\text{chord}}{2r}$
Useful identity: $A_{\text{sector}}=\tfrac12 \ell r$ (arc-length form of sector area) when arc length $\ell$ known directly replaces $\theta=\ell/r$

5. Composite-shape playbook (Section 7.3 + chapter review)

Most exam problems are decomposed into building blocks. Always draw and label first.

Composite shape Decomposition Total formula
Sector Arc + 2 radii Perim $=r\theta+2r$, Area $=\tfrac12 r^2\theta$
Segment (chord-cut "cap") Sector $-$ triangle Area $=\tfrac12 r^2(\theta-\sin\theta)$, Perim $=r\theta+2r\sin(\theta/2)$
"Lens" (overlap of two circles) Two segments back-to-back $A=2\times\tfrac12 r^2(\theta-\sin\theta)$ (if same radius)
Annulus sector (ring slice) Big sector $-$ small sector Area $=\tfrac12(R^2-r^2)\theta$, Perim $=R\theta+r\theta+2(R-r)$
Sector minus inscribed triangle Sector $-$ triangle Same as segment
Tangent + sector ("kite-and-arc") Two right triangles + sector Use $r\perp\text{tangent}$; areas add
"Garden patio" with sector lawn Triangle $-$ sector Area $=\tfrac12 ab\sin C-\tfrac12 r^2\theta$
"Earring" (two straight + arc) 2 triangles + arc segment Find via cosine rule for missing angles

7. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Calculator in degrees mode while computing $\sin\theta$ for $\theta$ in radians Always set RAD before starting; double-check by computing $\sin(\pi/2)$ — should give $1$
Plugging $\theta$ in degrees into $\ell=r\theta$ or $A=\tfrac12 r^2\theta$ These formulas only work in radians; convert first
Forgetting the $2r$ in sector perimeter $P=r\theta+2r$ — arc plus two radii. Just $r\theta$ is the arc alone
Confusing segment and sector Sector = pie slice (2 radii + arc); segment = cap (chord + arc)
In segment-area question, computing $\theta-\sin\theta$ where you forget $\theta$ is radians for $\theta$ but degrees for $\sin\theta$ Both must be radians; $\sin(1.2\text{ rad})\ne\sin(1.2°)$
For "major sector/segment", using minor angle Use $\theta_{\text{major}}=2\pi-\theta_{\text{minor}}$
Forgetting circle theorems (e.g. angle in semicircle = $\pi/2$, tangent ⊥ radius) These often supply the missing right angle; review when stuck
Using cosine rule then forgetting calculator returned answer in radians vs degrees Inverse-cosine in radian mode → answer in radians, ready to use
Rounding $\theta$ early to e.g. 2 d.p. then propagating through $A=\tfrac12 r^2(\theta-\sin\theta)$ Use full calculator precision; round only at the end
Picking up $\sin(\theta/2)$ chord formula but using $\sin\theta$ instead Half-angle: chord $=2r\sin(\theta/2)$, not $r\sin\theta$
Forgetting that formula $A=\tfrac12 r^2\theta$ requires $\theta$ in radians (Q14 chapter review: student wrote 50° but formula needs $50\pi/180$) Convert: $50°=\tfrac{5\pi}{18}$ rad → correct area $\tfrac12\cdot 9\cdot\tfrac{5\pi}{18}=\tfrac{5\pi}{4}\approx 3.93$ cm²

8. Big picture (30-second mental model)

A radian is a natural unit of angle that makes circle geometry linear: arc length = radius × angle, sector area = $\tfrac12$ × radius² × angle. With degrees you'd need a $\pi/180$ factor everywhere, so radians clean things up.

The chapter has three formulas ($\ell=r\theta$, $A_{\text{sector}}=\tfrac12 r^2\theta$, $A_{\text{seg}}=\tfrac12 r^2(\theta-\sin\theta)$) and one decomposition principle (segment = sector − triangle; bigger composite = sum/difference of these). All exam problems are some assembly of: drawing the picture, labelling angles in radians, applying the right formula, and (if a triangle's missing angle is needed) using the cosine rule on the isosceles triangle $OAB$ with two sides $=r$.

If you put the calculator in radian mode and remember the three formulas plus the decomposition, the chapter is essentially geometry-times-trigonometry, with no surprises.
