P3 Ch 1 — Algebraic Methods

The whole chapter in one mental model. Algebra gymnastics for rational expressions (fractions whose numerator/denominator are polynomials). Three operations to master — multiply (cancel & combine), divide (flip & multiply), add/subtract (find common denominator) — plus one structural trick: any improper fraction (numerator-degree $\ge$ denominator-degree) must first be split into quotient + proper fraction before further manipulation. The split uses algebraic long division or the identity $\mathrm{F}(x)\equiv\mathrm{Q}(x)\cdot(\text{divisor})+\text{remainder}$.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Algebraic fraction A fraction whose numerator and/or denominator is a polynomial expression — e.g. $\dfrac{x+1}{x^2-1}$. Treated by the same rules as numeric fractions, except you must factorise to cancel and you must restrict $x$ from values that make the denominator zero. 1.1
Polynomial An expression of the form $a_n x^n+a_{n-1}x^{n-1}+\dots+a_1 x+a_0$ where the $a_i$ are constants and $n$ is a non-negative integer. The highest power $n$ is the degree. 1.2
Degree The largest exponent of $x$ that appears in a polynomial: $\deg(3x^2-7)=2$, $\deg(x^4+x-1)=4$. The degree decides whether a fraction is proper or improper. 1.2
Proper fraction A rational expression $\dfrac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$ where $\deg P<\deg Q$ — e.g. $\dfrac{2x+1}{x^2-3x+2}$. Cannot be simplified by polynomial division (no quotient piece). 1.2
Improper fraction A rational expression where $\deg P\ge\deg Q$ — e.g. $\dfrac{x^3+x^2-7}{x-3}$ (degree 3 over degree 1). Must be converted via long division to quotient + proper fraction before further work. 1.2
Quotient $\mathrm{Q}(x)$ The polynomial answer of the division of $P(x)$ by $D(x)$. Has degree $\deg P-\deg D$. In $\dfrac{x^3+x^2-7}{x-3}=x^2+4x+12+\dfrac{29}{x-3}$, the quotient is $x^2+4x+12$. 1.2
Remainder What's left over after dividing — a polynomial of degree strictly less than the divisor. So dividing by a linear gives a constant remainder; dividing by a quadratic gives a linear-or-constant remainder. 1.2
Divisor The polynomial you're dividing by — i.e. the denominator. In $\dfrac{x^3+x^2-7}{x-3}$, the divisor is $x-3$. 1.2
Algebraic long division The polynomial analogue of numeric long division: divide highest term of dividend by highest term of divisor, multiply back, subtract, repeat until the working polynomial has degree less than the divisor. 1.2
Identity ≡ The triple-bar symbol means "equal for ALL values of $x$" (not just for some). Used when stating $P(x)\equiv \mathrm{Q}(x)\cdot D(x)+R(x)$ — true regardless of $x$. Lets you compare coefficients on both sides. 1.2
Comparing coefficients Method 2 for splitting an improper fraction: write $P(x)=Q(x)\cdot D(x)+R$, expand the RHS, and equate coefficients of like powers (e.g. $x^3$, $x^2$, …) on both sides. Yields a system of equations for the unknowns. 1.2
Substitution method Quick trick within "compare coefficients": substitute clever $x$-values (often $x=$ root of divisor) to make terms vanish, isolating an unknown. E.g. setting $x=3$ in $P=Q(x-3)+R$ gives $P(3)=R$ directly. 1.2
Reciprocal The fraction flipped: reciprocal of $\dfrac{a}{b}$ is $\dfrac{b}{a}$. Dividing by a fraction means multiplying by its reciprocal. 1.1
Lowest common multiple (LCM) / common denominator The smallest expression that both fraction denominators divide into. For $\dfrac{2}{x+3}-\dfrac{1}{x+1}$, the LCM is $(x+3)(x+1)$ — multiply both fractions to that common base. 1.1
Cancelling common factors Dividing top and bottom by the same factor, e.g. $\dfrac{(x+1)\cdot 3}{2(x+1)(x-1)}=\dfrac{3}{2(x-1)}$. Only valid for factors — never "cancel" through addition/subtraction. 1.1
Factorise Rewrite a polynomial as a product of factors so that common pieces become visible: $x^2-1=(x+1)(x-1)$, $3x+6=3(x+2)$. Crucial step before multiplying or cancelling fractions. 1.1
Restrictions on $x$ Values where any denominator becomes zero are excluded from the domain. For $\dfrac{x+2}{x+4}\div\dfrac{3x+6}{x^2-16}$: $x\neq-4,4,-2$ (one of each from the original denominators and the introduced reciprocal). Often stated explicitly: "$x\in\mathbb{R}, x\neq-2, x\neq 4$". 1.1

2. The three arithmetic operations on fractions

Operation Rule Method (in order) Trigger example
Multiplication $\dfrac{a}{b}\times\dfrac{c}{d}=\dfrac{ac}{bd}$ 1. Factorise everything. 2. Cancel common factors across top/bottom. 3. Multiply remaining numerators and denominators. $\dfrac{x+1}{2}\times\dfrac{3}{x^2-1}=\dfrac{x+1}{2}\times\dfrac{3}{(x+1)(x-1)}=\dfrac{3}{2(x-1)}$
Division $\dfrac{a}{b}\div\dfrac{c}{d}=\dfrac{a}{b}\times\dfrac{d}{c}$ 1. Flip the second fraction (reciprocal). 2. Convert ÷ to ×. 3. Then proceed as multiplication: factorise → cancel → combine. $\dfrac{x+2}{x+4}\div\dfrac{3x+6}{x^2-16}=\dfrac{x+2}{x+4}\times\dfrac{(x+4)(x-4)}{3(x+2)}=\dfrac{x-4}{3}$
Addition / Subtraction $\dfrac{a}{b}\pm\dfrac{c}{d}=\dfrac{ad\pm bc}{bd}$ (or LCM denominator) 1. Factorise denominators. 2. Find LCM denominator. 3. Multiply each fraction's top and bottom to bring to the LCM. 4. Add/subtract numerators. 5. Simplify (factorise & cancel). $\dfrac{2}{x+3}-\dfrac{1}{x+1}=\dfrac{2(x+1)-1(x+3)}{(x+3)(x+1)}=\dfrac{x-1}{(x+3)(x+1)}$

Pitfall when subtracting: distribute the minus sign through the entire second numerator. $\dfrac{P}{D}-\dfrac{Q}{D}=\dfrac{P-Q}{D}$, with parentheses around $Q$ if it has multiple terms.

3. Improper fraction → mixed form: two methods

For $\dfrac{P(x)}{D(x)}$ with $\deg P\ge\deg D$, rewrite as $\mathrm{Q}(x)+\dfrac{R(x)}{D(x)}$ where $\deg R<\deg D$.

Method Setup When to use
1. Algebraic long division Set up like numeric long division: $D(x)\overline{)P(x)}$. Divide leading term of running dividend by leading term of $D$, multiply $D$ by that term, subtract, bring down next, repeat until running degree $<\deg D$. The collected leading-term-quotients form $\mathrm{Q}(x)$; the final running polynomial is the remainder $R(x)$. Recommended for clean numeric divisions; faster when divisor is monic and small-degree.
2. Identity & coefficient comparison Write $P(x)\equiv \mathrm{Q}(x)\cdot D(x)+R(x)$ with the shape of $\mathrm{Q}$ and $R$ given (degrees inferable). Choose substitution values (e.g. roots of $D$) to extract individual constants, then compare coefficients of $x^k$ on both sides for the rest. Recommended when the question gives you the form with unknowns $A,B,C,D,…$ to find. Substituting at root of $D$ kills the $\mathrm{Q}\cdot D$ term and isolates $R$.

Form rules (degrees of $\mathrm{Q}$ and $R$):

$\deg P$ $\deg D$ $\deg \mathrm{Q}$ $\deg R$ (max)
3 1 2 0 (constant)
4 2 2 1 (linear)
$n$ $m$, $n\ge m$ $n-m$ $m-1$

Standard rewrite identity: $\boxed{\dfrac{P(x)}{D(x)}\equiv \mathrm{Q}(x)+\dfrac{R(x)}{D(x)}}$ where $\deg R<\deg D$.

4. Worked archetype examples

# Problem Method Answer
1 Multiply: $\dfrac{x+1}{2}\times\dfrac{3}{x^2-1}$ Factorise $x^2-1=(x+1)(x-1)$, cancel $(x+1)$ $\dfrac{3}{2(x-1)}$
2 Divide: $\dfrac{a}{b}\div\dfrac{a}{c}$ Flip $\dfrac{a}{c}$ to $\dfrac{c}{a}$, multiply, cancel $a$ $\dfrac{c}{b}$
3 Add: $\dfrac{a}{2x}+\dfrac{b}{3x}$ LCM is $6x$; multiply $\dfrac{a}{2x}\cdot\dfrac{3}{3}+\dfrac{b}{3x}\cdot\dfrac{2}{2}$ $\dfrac{3a+2b}{6x}$
4 Subtract: $\dfrac{3}{x+1}-\dfrac{4x}{x^2-1}$ Factorise $x^2-1=(x+1)(x-1)$; LCM $(x+1)(x-1)$; numerator $3(x-1)-4x=-x-3$ $\dfrac{-x-3}{(x+1)(x-1)}$
5 Long-divide: $\dfrac{x^3+x^2-7}{x-3}$ $x^3\div x=x^2$, multiply back $x^3-3x^2$, subtract → $4x^2$; continue: $4x$, then $12$. Remainder $29$ $x^2+4x+12+\dfrac{29}{x-3}$
6 Find $A,B,C,D$ in $x^3+x^2-7\equiv (Ax^2+Bx+C)(x-3)+D$ Sub $x=3$: $D=29$. Sub $x=0$: $-7=-3C+29\Rightarrow C=12$. Coeff $x^3$: $A=1$. Coeff $x^2$: $1=-3A+B\Rightarrow B=4$ $A=1,B=4,C=12,D=29$
7 $\dfrac{x^4+x^3+x-10}{x^2+2x-3}$ in form $Ax^2+Bx+C+\dfrac{Dx+E}{x^2+2x-3}$ Long-divide; quotient $x^2-x+5$, remainder $-12x+5$ $A=1,B=-1,C=5,D=-12,E=5$
8 $g(x)=x+\dfrac{6}{x+2}+\dfrac{36}{x^2-2x-8}$. Show $g(x)=\dfrac{x^3-2x^2-2x+12}{(x+2)(x-4)}$ Factorise $x^2-2x-8=(x+2)(x-4)$. LCM = $(x+2)(x-4)$. Combine all 3 over LCM as stated
9 Show $\dfrac{x^2-64}{x^2-36}\div\dfrac{64-x^2}{x^2-36}=-1$ Flip second, simplify: $\dfrac{(x^2-64)(x^2-36)}{(x^2-36)(64-x^2)}$. $64-x^2=-(x^2-64)$ $-1$
10 Express $\dfrac{x^4+2}{x^2-1}\equiv Ax^2+Bx+C+\dfrac{D}{x^2-1}$ Long-divide. Quotient $x^2+1$, remainder $3$ $A=1,B=0,C=1,D=3$

5. Decision flow — what method to use

Spot this Do this
$\times$ between two fractions Factorise everything, cancel common factors, multiply (§2)
$\div$ between two fractions Flip second fraction, convert to $\times$, then proceed (§2)
$+$ or $-$ between two fractions Factorise denominators, find LCM, bring to common denominator, combine numerators (§2)
$\dfrac{P(x)}{D(x)}$ where $\deg P\ge \deg D$ Convert via long division (§3, Method 1) — improper fraction must be split
Question gives "$\equiv (Ax^2+Bx+C)(x-k)+D$" with unknowns Use substitution + coefficient comparison (§3, Method 2)
Three or more fractions added/subtracted Find LCM of all denominators in one go, then combine
"Show that this fraction simplifies to …" Often: factorise both numerator and denominator, watch for $-(x-a)=a-x$ swaps
"Hence differentiate …" appears later First simplify the fraction (using these methods) until it's a polynomial — then differentiate the polynomial term-by-term
Simplification under $\ln$ or $\mathrm{e}$ or $=$ to constant Simplify the fraction first, then apply the $\ln$ / e / equation step

6. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Cancelling terms instead of factors: writing $\dfrac{x+3}{x+6}=\dfrac{1+3}{1+6}$ Only cancel multiplicative factors. $x+3$ and $x+6$ have no common factor unless you factorise differently
Forgetting parentheses on subtracted numerators: $\dfrac{a}{D}-\dfrac{b+c}{D}=\dfrac{a-b+c}{D}$ ❌ Should be $\dfrac{a-(b+c)}{D}=\dfrac{a-b-c}{D}$. Use brackets when subtracting multi-term numerators
Multiplying numerator and denominator by different expressions to bring to common denominator Both must be multiplied by the same expression — you're using $\dfrac{n}{n}=1$, so the multiplier is the same on top and bottom
Trying to do partial fractions on an improper fraction directly First split into $\mathrm{Q}(x)+\dfrac{R(x)}{D(x)}$ via long division, then apply partial fractions to the proper part (Ch 2)
Long-division: forgetting to write a placeholder for missing power (e.g. $x^3+0\cdot x^2-7$) Always insert $0\cdot x^k$ for any missing power to keep alignment
Stopping long division too early Stop only when running polynomial has $\deg<\deg D$. For divisor $x^2+\dots$ remainder can be degree 0 or 1
Assuming $\dfrac{a-b}{b-a}=1$ Actually equals $-1$ (since $a-b=-(b-a)$). Common in factorisation tricks
Forgetting to state restrictions on $x$ when denominator factor is zero Always state $x\neq r$ for each root $r$ of any denominator that appears in working (not just the final)
In coefficient comparison, miscounting terms in expansion Carefully expand: $(Ax^2+Bx+C)(x-3)=Ax^3+(-3A+B)x^2+(-3B+C)x-3C$ — both $A$ and $B$ contribute to $x^2$
Forgetting that comparing coefficients works ONLY when the equation is an identity $\equiv$, not an equation $=$ $\equiv$ means "true for all $x$"; $=$ means "true for some $x$". Coefficient-matching needs $\equiv$
Adding $\dfrac{1}{x}+\dfrac{1}{y}=\dfrac{1}{x+y}$ ❌ $\dfrac{1}{x}+\dfrac{1}{y}=\dfrac{x+y}{xy}$. Common denominator first

7. Big picture (30-second mental model)

This is the algebra plumbing chapter — three fluencies you'll reuse everywhere in P3 and P4:

  1. Multiply / divide rational expressions: factorise, cancel, combine.
  2. Add / subtract rational expressions: find common denominator, combine numerators carefully.
  3. Improper → mixed: any fraction where top-degree ≥ bottom-degree must be split before further work (especially before partial fractions in Ch 2). Use long division or the identity $P\equiv Q\cdot D+R$ with substitution and coefficient matching.

The single most reused identity is $\dfrac{P(x)}{D(x)}\equiv\mathrm{Q}(x)+\dfrac{R(x)}{D(x)}$ with $\deg R<\deg D$ — the polynomial analogue of $\dfrac{17}{5}=3+\dfrac{2}{5}$. This sets up Ch 2 (partial fractions) and surfaces in differentiation, integration, series expansions, and complex-fraction modelling throughout the rest of the course.
