FP2 Ch 8 — Polar Coordinates

The whole chapter in one mental model. Re-coordinatise the plane: instead of $(x,y)$ use $(r,\theta)=$ (distance from origin, anticlockwise angle from $+x$-axis). A polar curve $r=f(\theta)$ is then "for each direction $\theta$, how far out is the curve?" The full toolkit: convert ↔ Cartesian via $x=r\cos\theta,\ y=r\sin\theta,\ r^2=x^2+y^2$; sketch by recognising standard families ($r=a$, $r=a\theta$, cardioid, $n$-petal rose); compute enclosed area via $\tfrac12\int r^2\,\mathrm d\theta$; find horizontal/vertical tangents by setting $\mathrm dy/\mathrm d\theta=0$ or $\mathrm dx/\mathrm d\theta=0$.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Pole The reference point (origin $O$) from which all distances $r$ are measured. Plays the role of "the origin" in polar geometry. Every polar coordinate's first number $r$ is the distance from this single point. 8.1
Initial line The reference direction (usually the positive $x$-axis) from which the angle $\theta$ is measured anticlockwise in radians. Every polar coordinate's second number $\theta$ is the angle relative to this line. 8.1
Polar coordinates $(r,\theta)$ A point's position written as (distance, angle) instead of (across, up). Convention: $r\ge 0$ and $\theta$ usually in $[0,2\pi)$ or $(-\pi,\pi]$. The same point has many representations: $(r,\theta)\equiv(r,\theta+2\pi)$. 8.1
Polar equation $r=f(\theta)$ A curve specified as "for each direction $\theta$, the distance from $O$ is $f(\theta)$". Plot by sweeping $\theta$ and at each angle marking radius $f(\theta)$. Examples: $r=2\cos\theta$, $r=1+2\theta$, $r=3$. 8.1
Cartesian-polar conversion The bridge formulas: $x=r\cos\theta$, $y=r\sin\theta$, $r^2=x^2+y^2$, $\tan\theta=y/x$. ALWAYS sketch when going Cartesian→polar to nail the correct quadrant for $\theta$ — pure $\arctan(y/x)$ misses the 2nd & 3rd quadrants. 8.1
Cardioid A heart-shaped curve $r=a(1+\cos\theta)$ (or $\sin$ variant). Maximum $r=2a$ at $\theta=0$ (or wherever $\cos$ peaks); passes through pole at $\theta=\pi$. Special case of $r=a(p+q\cos\theta)$ with $p=q$. 8.2 Ex 6
Limaçon Family $r=a(p+q\cos\theta)$ with $p,q>0$, $p\ge q$. Three sub-types: $p\ge 2q$ → convex ("egg"); $qdimpled (curve concave near $\theta=\pi$); $p=q$ → cardioid; $p 8.2/8.4 Ex 14
Convex / dimpled Convex = no inward bulge (egg-shaped, only 2 vertical-tangent points). Dimpled = there is a concave indentation at $\theta=\pi$ (so 4 vertical tangents). Test on $r=a(p+q\cos\theta)$: convex iff $p\ge 2q$. 8.4 Ex 14
Rose / petal curve $r=a\sin n\theta$ or $r=a\cos n\theta$ has $n$ petals symmetrically arranged around $O$ (when sketched only for $r\ge 0$). Each petal lies in a wedge of angular width $\pi/n$. 8.2 Ex 6
Spiral $r=a\theta$ An Archimedean spiral starting at the pole when $\theta=0$. Each rotation increases $r$ by $2\pi a$. Other spirals: $r=e^{k\theta}$ (logarithmic, exponential growth). 8.2
Symmetry test If swapping $\theta\to-\theta$ leaves $r=f(\theta)$ unchanged → curve is symmetric about the initial line. If $\theta\to\pi-\theta$ leaves it unchanged → symmetric about the line $\theta=\pi/2$. Use to halve the integration range. 8.3
Sector area formula $\text{Area}=\tfrac12\int_\alpha^\beta r^2\,\mathrm d\theta$ — the polar analogue of $\int y\,\mathrm dx$. Comes from approximating the region as thin pie-slice sectors of central angle $\mathrm d\theta$ and radius $r$, area $\tfrac12 r^2\mathrm d\theta$. 8.3
Loop limits For petal curves $r=a\sin n\theta$, one loop runs between consecutive zeros of $r$ where $r\ge 0$ in between. Solve $\sin n\theta=0$: e.g. for $r=a\sin 4\theta$, one loop is $0\le\theta\le \pi/4$. 8.3 Ex 10
Negative $r$ pitfall If you blindly integrate $\tfrac12\int_0^{2\pi}r^2\mathrm d\theta$ for $r=a\sin n\theta$ you double-count: the negative-$r$ "half" of each cycle traces out the same petals. Restrict to ranges where $r\ge 0$. 8.3
Parametric form of polar curve Rewriting $r=f(\theta)$ as $x(\theta)=f(\theta)\cos\theta,\ y(\theta)=f(\theta)\sin\theta$ — treating $\theta$ as the parameter. Lets you use the standard parametric chain rule for tangents: $\dfrac{\mathrm dy}{\mathrm dx}=\dfrac{\mathrm dy/\mathrm d\theta}{\mathrm dx/\mathrm d\theta}$. 8.4
Horizontal / vertical tangent Horizontal (parallel to initial line): $\mathrm dy/\mathrm d\theta=0$. Vertical (perpendicular): $\mathrm dx/\mathrm d\theta=0$. Both being $0$ at a point usually means a pole or cusp — analyse separately. 8.4
Intersection of two polar curves Set $f(\theta)=g(\theta)$ and solve for $\theta$, then substitute back. Watch out: the pole $O$ may be on both curves at different $\theta$ values — sketch to confirm. 8.3 Ex 11

2. Conversion table — coordinates and equations

Going from To Formula / Rule
Polar $(r,\theta)$ Cartesian $(x,y)$ $x=r\cos\theta,\ y=r\sin\theta$
Cartesian $(x,y)$ Polar $(r,\theta)$ $r=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$, $\theta$ from $\arctan(y/x)$ + quadrant adjustment
Polar equation $r=f(\theta)$ → Cartesian Replace $r\cos\theta\to x$, $r\sin\theta\to y$, $r^2\to x^2+y^2$ E.g. $r=5\Rightarrow r^2=25\Rightarrow x^2+y^2=25$
Polar containing $\cos2\theta,\sin2\theta$ Use $\cos 2\theta=\cos^2\theta-\sin^2\theta$, $\sin 2\theta=2\sin\theta\cos\theta$ Then multiply by $r^2$ to reach $(x^2+y^2)$, $xy$, etc.
Cartesian → Polar Replace $x\to r\cos\theta$, $y\to r\sin\theta$, then simplify trig E.g. $x^2-y^2=5\Rightarrow r^2\cos 2\theta=5\Rightarrow r^2=5\sec 2\theta$
$ay+bx=c$ (line) → polar $ar\sin\theta+br\cos\theta=c$ → $r=c/(a\sin\theta+b\cos\theta)$, often written $r=k\,\mathrm{cosec}(\theta-\phi)$ via $R\sin(\theta\pm\phi)$ E.g. Ex 4c: $y\sqrt 3=x+4 \Rightarrow r=2\,\mathrm{cosec}(\theta-\pi/6)$

3. Standard polar curve zoo (Section 8.2)

Memorise these shapes — sketch questions usually ask one of them.

Polar equation Shape Key features
$r=a$ Circle, centre $O$, radius $a$ All $\theta$ have same $r=a$
$\theta=\alpha$ Half-line through $O$ at angle $\alpha$ NOT a full line — only $r\ge 0$ portion
$r=a\theta$ ($\theta\ge 0$) Archimedean spiral Crosses initial line at $r=0,2\pi a,4\pi a,\dots$
$r=e^{k\theta}$ Logarithmic spiral Faster than Archimedean; never reaches pole
$r=2a\cos\theta$ Circle, radius $a$, centre $(a,0)$ Passes through pole at $\theta=\pm\pi/2$
$r=2a\sin\theta$ Circle, radius $a$, centre $(0,a)$ Passes through pole at $\theta=0,\pi$
$r=a(1+\cos\theta)$ Cardioid Max $2a$ at $\theta=0$, pole at $\theta=\pi$
$r=a(p+q\cos\theta),\ p>q$ Limaçon (no inner loop) Convex if $p\ge 2q$; dimpled if $p<2q$
$r=a\sin n\theta$ or $r=a\cos n\theta$ $n$-petal rose One petal per $\pi/n$ angular wedge
$r^2=a^2\cos 2\theta$ Lemniscate (figure-8) Two loops along initial line; $r$ undefined for $\cos 2\theta<0$

4. Sketching procedure

Step Action
1 Identify the family from the form of $r=f(\theta)$ — match against §3
2 Find $r$ at key angles $\theta=0,\pi/2,\pi,3\pi/2$ — table of values
3 Find $\theta$ values where $r=0$ (pole crossings) and where $r$ is maximal
4 Use symmetry: $f(-\theta)=f(\theta)$ → mirror about initial line; $f(\pi-\theta)=f(\theta)$ → mirror about $\theta=\pi/2$
5 Restrict to $r\ge 0$ ranges; mark each petal/loop within its range
6 For limaçons: check $p$ vs $2q$ — convex or dimpled

5. Polar area — sector formula and tactics (Section 8.3)

$$\boxed{\text{Area}=\frac12\int_\alpha^\beta r^2\,\mathrm d\theta}\qquad(r\ge 0\text{ throughout the interval})$$

Situation Limits to use Trick
Whole curve $r=a(1+\cos\theta)$ $0\to 2\pi$ (or $0\to\pi$ doubled by symmetry) Expand $(1+\cos\theta)^2$, use $\cos^2\theta=(1+\cos 2\theta)/2$
One loop of $r=a\sin n\theta$ $0\to \pi/n$ (the first zero-to-zero range) $\sin^2 n\theta=(1-\cos 2n\theta)/2$
Region inside both of two curves Split: integrate inner curve where it's smaller, switch to other where it's smaller Find intersection $f(\theta)=g(\theta)$ first
Region between two curves $\tfrac12\int(R_{\text{outer}}^2-r_{\text{inner}}^2)\,\mathrm d\theta$ Make sure both curves are over same $\theta$-range
Argand diagram region (complex) Convert $\lvert z-z_0\rvert=R$ into polar form first E.g. $\lvert z-4+3i\rvert=5\Rightarrow r=8\cos\theta-6\sin\theta$

Key trig identities for polar integrals:

  • $\cos^2\theta=\dfrac{1+\cos 2\theta}{2}$, $\sin^2\theta=\dfrac{1-\cos 2\theta}{2}$
  • $(p+q\cos\theta)^2=p^2+2pq\cos\theta+q^2\cos^2\theta$
  • $\sin 2\theta=2\sin\theta\cos\theta$
  • General useful: $(p+q\cos\theta)$ averages to $\tfrac{2p^2+q^2}{2}\pi a^2$ over a full period (Ex Q2).

6. Tangent equations (Section 8.4)

Treat $r=f(\theta)$ as parametric: $x=f(\theta)\cos\theta,\ y=f(\theta)\sin\theta$.

Tangent type Condition Example outcome
Parallel to initial line (horizontal) $\dfrac{\mathrm dy}{\mathrm d\theta}=0$ E.g. cardioid: $\theta=\pm\pi/3$ → points $(\tfrac{3a}{2},\pm\tfrac{\pi}{3})$
Perpendicular to initial line (vertical) $\dfrac{\mathrm dx}{\mathrm d\theta}=0$ E.g. limaçon convexity test
Pole tangents Solve $r=0$; tangent at pole is the line $\theta=\theta_0$ where $r=0$ E.g. $r=a\sin 2\theta=0$ at $\theta=0,\pi/2,\dots$
Convexity test (limaçon $r=p+q\cos\theta$) Count solutions of $\mathrm dx/\mathrm d\theta=0$. Two → convex; four → dimpled $\sin\theta(p+2q\cos\theta)=0$; second factor real-soluble iff $\lvert p/2q\rvert\le 1$, i.e. $p\le 2q$ → dimple

Equation of tangent line — once you have a tangent point $(r_0,\theta_0)$ horizontal: $y=r_0\sin\theta_0$ → in polar: $r=\dfrac{r_0\sin\theta_0}{\sin\theta}=r_0\sin\theta_0\,\mathrm{cosec}\,\theta$ Vertical: $x=r_0\cos\theta_0$ → $r=r_0\cos\theta_0\,\sec\theta$.

8. Pitfalls (common ways to lose marks)

Pitfall Fix
Using $\arctan(y/x)$ blindly: missing the 2nd / 3rd quadrant Always sketch; in 2nd/3rd quadrant add or subtract $\pi$
Using $\theta$ in degrees Always radians for polar; integrals require radians
Negative-$r$ blowing up area integral Restrict integration to ranges where $r\ge 0$; use $\sin n\theta\ge 0$ to find them
Forgetting the $\tfrac12$ factor in $\tfrac12\int r^2\,\mathrm d\theta$ Memorise as "half-r-squared-d-theta"
Squaring $(p+q\cos\theta)$ but forgetting the cross-term $2pq\cos\theta$ Use $(a+b)^2=a^2+2ab+b^2$ carefully
Treating $\theta=\alpha$ as a full line It's a half-line through $O$; the other half is $\theta=\alpha+\pi$
Counting all $n$ petals as $n\times(\text{one-loop area})$ but using full $0\to 2\pi$ integral Either find one-loop area ($0\to\pi/n$) and multiply, OR integrate $\tfrac12\int_{\text{positive-}r}r^2\,\mathrm d\theta$
Tangent: using $\mathrm dy/\mathrm dx=\tan\theta$ — wrong Use $\mathrm dy/\mathrm dx=\dfrac{\mathrm dy/\mathrm d\theta}{\mathrm dx/\mathrm d\theta}$ on the parametric form
Intersection of curves: missing the pole as a shared point Check separately whether $r=0$ at some $\theta_1$ for one curve and some $\theta_2$ for the other

9. Big picture (the 30-second mental model)

Polar coordinates re-describe each point by direction-and-distance from $O$. With this lens:

  1. Curves are radius-as-a-function-of-angle. Sweep $\theta$ from $0$ to $2\pi$ (or wherever needed) and at each angle plot the prescribed $r$. Standard families (circle, line, spiral, cardioid, rose, limaçon, lemniscate) cover ~all exam shapes.
  2. Cartesian conversion is just trigonometry: $x=r\cos\theta$, $y=r\sin\theta$, $r^2=x^2+y^2$. Always sketch when going from $(x,y)$ to $(r,\theta)$ to choose the right quadrant.
  3. Area is a sum of pie-slice sectors: $\tfrac12\int r^2\mathrm d\theta$. Pay attention to validity ($r\ge 0$) and use trig identities to integrate $r^2$.
  4. Tangents become parametric: differentiate $x(\theta)$ and $y(\theta)$, set the appropriate derivative to zero. Counting vertical tangents on a limaçon distinguishes "egg" (convex) from "dimpled".

Two coordinate transformations, one integration formula, ten standard curves — that's the chapter.
