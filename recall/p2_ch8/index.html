P2 Ch 8 — Integration

The whole chapter in one mental model. Integration is the inverse of differentiation: where P1 Ch9 introduced indefinite integrals (anti-derivatives + $C$), this chapter pins down two real values via definite integration $\int_a^b\mathrm{f}(x)\,\mathrm{d}x=[\mathrm{F}(x)]_a^b=\mathrm{F}(b)-\mathrm{F}(a)$ — the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus. Geometrically it computes the signed area between curve and $x$-axis. The chapter walks through five area scenarios (above-axis, below-axis, between line and curve, between two curves, complex composite shapes) plus one numerical fallback (the trapezium rule) for cases that can't be integrated algebraically.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Definite integral An integral evaluated between two specific limits $a$ and $b$, giving a number (not a function): $\int_a^b\mathrm{f}'(x)\,\mathrm{d}x=\mathrm{f}(b)-\mathrm{f}(a)$. The $+C$ from indefinite integration cancels in the subtraction, so you don't write it. 8.1
Indefinite integral An anti-derivative — produces a function $\mathrm{F}(x)+C$, not a number. The $C$ is essential because differentiating any constant gives 0. 8.1 (P1 recap)
Limits of integration The two numbers $a$ (lower) and $b$ (upper) on $\int_a^b\dots\mathrm{d}x$. They specify the $x$-range over which the integral is evaluated. The convention $a 8.1
Fundamental theorem of calculus Differentiation and integration are inverses: if $\mathrm{F}'=\mathrm{f}$ then $\int_a^b\mathrm{f}(x)\,\mathrm{d}x=\mathrm{F}(b)-\mathrm{F}(a)$. The link between rates-of-change (derivatives) and accumulation (integrals). 8.1
Integrand The function being integrated — i.e., what sits between $\int$ and $\mathrm{d}x$. In $\int(2x^3-1)\mathrm{d}x$ the integrand is $2x^3-1$. 8.1
Square-bracket notation $[\mathrm{F}(x)]_a^b$ means "evaluate $\mathrm{F}$ at $b$ then subtract $\mathrm{F}$ at $a$". A neat way to keep working organised between integrating and substituting. 8.1
Signed area The value $\int_a^b y\,\mathrm{d}x$ counts area above the $x$-axis as positive and area below as negative. So a single integral across the axis gives a net (often partly cancelled) result, not the total area. 8.3
Total / geometric area The actual size of a shaded region, always positive. To get it when a curve crosses the $x$-axis: split the integral at the roots, take the magnitude of each piece, then add. Never just take the absolute value of one big integral — pieces cancel inside. 8.3
Area under a curve For a curve $y=\mathrm{f}(x)$ that stays $\ge 0$ on $[a,b]$, this is the area between the curve, the $x$-axis, $x=a$ and $x=b$, equal to $\int_a^b y\,\mathrm{d}x$. The reason: $\frac{\mathrm{d}A}{\mathrm{d}x}=y$ (a thin rectangle of width $\delta x$ has height $\approx y$). 8.2
Area between two curves The area enclosed where $\mathrm{f}(x)\ge\mathrm{g}(x)$ on $[a,b]$: $\int_a^b[\mathrm{f}(x)-\mathrm{g}(x)]\,\mathrm{d}x$ — top minus bottom. Limits $a,b$ are usually the $x$-coordinates where the curves meet. 8.5
Area between a curve and a line A composite area found by combining triangles/trapeziums (for the line piece) with definite integrals (for the curve piece) — usually "area under the line minus area under the curve" or vice-versa. 8.4
Trapezium rule A numerical approximation for $\int_a^b y\,\mathrm{d}x$: divide $[a,b]$ into $n$ equal-width strips of width $h=\frac{b-a}{n}$, evaluate $y$ at each boundary, and sum trapezium areas. Formula: $\int_a^b y\,\mathrm{d}x\approx\tfrac{1}{2}h\bigl(y_0+2(y_1+\dots+y_{n-1})+y_n\bigr)$. Used when algebraic integration is impossible/hard. 8.6
Number of strips $n$ How many equal sub-intervals you split $[a,b]$ into. There are $n+1$ ordinates $y_0,y_1,\dots,y_n$ (one more than strips). More strips → better accuracy. 8.6
Strip width $h$ $h=\dfrac{b-a}{n}$. The horizontal width of each trapezium. Common pitfall: forgetting to halve $h$ when doubling the strips. 8.6
Ordinate A $y$-value at one of the strip boundaries: $y_i=\mathrm{f}(a+ih)$. "$n$ strips needs $n+1$ ordinates." 8.6
Over-estimate / under-estimate The trapezium rule over-estimates when the curve is convex (cups upward, ∪) over the interval — chords lie above the curve. It under-estimates when the curve is concave (cups downward, ∩) — chords lie below. 8.6
Concave / convex A curve is concave on $[a,b]$ if $\mathrm{f}''\le 0$ (curve bends downward, like $-x^2$); convex if $\mathrm{f}''\ge 0$ (curve bends upward, like $x^2$). Sign convention sometimes opposite — check by sketching. 8.6
Finite region A bounded enclosed area, fully closed off by curves/lines/axes. The standard exam phrase "the finite region bounded by …" means find the limits yourself by spotting where the bounding curves meet. 8.2–8.5

2. The five steps for evaluating any definite integral

Step Action Example $\int_0^1(x^{1/3}-1)^2\mathrm{d}x$
1 If integrand is a product/quotient, expand or simplify into a sum of $x^n$ terms $(x^{1/3}-1)^2=x^{2/3}-2x^{1/3}+1$
2 Integrate term-by-term using $\int x^n\,\mathrm{d}x=\dfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}$ ($n\neq -1$) $\bigl[\tfrac35 x^{5/3}-\tfrac32 x^{4/3}+x\bigr]_0^1$
3 Square-bracket the antiderivative with limits as above
4 Substitute upper limit $b$, then lower limit $a$, subtract $\bigl(\tfrac35-\tfrac32+1\bigr)-(0)$
5 Simplify — give exact form (no calculator!) $\dfrac{1}{10}$

Algebraic prep tactics:

Form of integrand Convert to Why
$\dfrac{a}{x^k}$ $a\,x^{-k}$ Apply $\int x^n=\frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}$
$\sqrt[k]{x^m}$ $x^{m/k}$ Same — fractional powers
$(\text{poly})^2$ Expand to sum Term-by-term integration
$\dfrac{P(x)}{x}$ or $\dfrac{P(x)}{x^k}$ Polynomial division → sum of $x^n$ Each term gets standard rule
Constants times integrand Pull constant outside $\int$ $\int kf=k\int f$

3. Area between curve and $x$-axis — sign-aware recipe

Position of curve on $[a,b]$ Formula for geometric area Why
Curve entirely above $x$-axis $\text{Area}=\displaystyle\int_a^b y\,\mathrm{d}x$ Integral is already positive
Curve entirely below $x$-axis $\text{Area}=\bigl\lvert\displaystyle\int_a^b y\,\mathrm{d}x\bigr\rvert=-\displaystyle\int_a^b y\,\mathrm{d}x$ Integral is negative; flip sign
Curve crosses $x$-axis at root(s) $r_i\in[a,b]$ Split: $\bigl\lvert\int_a^{r_1}y\,\mathrm{d}x\bigr\rvert+\bigl\lvert\int_{r_1}^{r_2}y\,\mathrm{d}x\bigr\rvert+\dots$ Otherwise positive and negative areas cancel inside one integral

Procedure: sketch the curve, find $x$-axis intercepts (roots of $\mathrm{f}(x)=0$), use those as integration limits / split points, evaluate each piece, take magnitudes, sum.

Example (Ch §8.3): for $y=x(x-1)(x+3)$, the finite region above $-3\le x\le 0$ gives $\int_{-3}^{0}=\tfrac{45}{4}$, below $0\le x\le 1$ gives $\int_0^1=-\tfrac{7}{12}$. Total area $=\tfrac{45}{4}+\tfrac{7}{12}=\tfrac{71}{6}$.

4. Areas involving lines and curves — combination tactics

Scenario Formula / Method Trigger sketch
Region between curve $y=\mathrm{f}(x)$ above and line $y=mx+c$ below $\int_a^b[\mathrm{f}(x)-(mx+c)]\,\mathrm{d}x$ where $a,b$ are intersection $x$'s Crescent-shaped region
Region between line above and curve below $\int_a^b[(mx+c)-\mathrm{f}(x)]\,\mathrm{d}x$ Top minus bottom
Region between two curves $\mathrm{f}\ge\mathrm{g}$ on $[a,b]$ $\int_a^b[\mathrm{f}(x)-\mathrm{g}(x)]\,\mathrm{d}x$ Stretched between two curves
Composite (curve + axis + line) — irregular shape Split into pieces: triangle/trapezium for line piece (area = $\tfrac12bh$, $\tfrac12(a+b)h$); definite integral for curve piece. Add or subtract appropriately. Always sketch first
Line cuts curve at known points First, find intersections by setting $\mathrm{f}(x)=\mathrm{g}(x)$ → solve quadratic / cubic for $x$-values These become limits

Strategy reminder: For composite shapes, always draw the picture, decide whether the curve sits above or below the line on the relevant interval, and identify the basic shapes. "Area = (whole bounding shape) − (parts not wanted)" or "sum of pieces" both work.

5. Trapezium rule — numerical approximation

Element Formula / Rule
Strip width $h=\dfrac{b-a}{n}$
Ordinates $y_i=\mathrm{f}(a+ih)$ for $i=0,1,2,\dots,n$
Number of ordinates $n+1$ (one more than strips!)
Approximation $\displaystyle\int_a^b y\,\mathrm{d}x\approx\tfrac{1}{2}h\bigl(y_0+2(y_1+y_2+\cdots+y_{n-1})+y_n\bigr)$
Mnemonic "End ordinates count once, interior ordinates count twice, multiply by $\tfrac12 h$."

Convex vs concave estimate behaviour:

Curve shape on $[a,b]$ $\mathrm{f}''$ sign Trapezium estimate vs true Reason
Convex (∪, e.g. $y=2^x$, $x^2$) $\mathrm{f}''\ge 0$ Over-estimate Trapezium chords lie above the curve
Concave (∩, e.g. $y=\sqrt{2x+3}$, $\ln x$) $\mathrm{f}''\le 0$ Under-estimate Trapezium chords lie below the curve
Mixed (inflection inside $[a,b]$) changes sign Indeterminate without more analysis Sketch and consider both pieces

6. Decision flow — which method?

Question phrasing / spotted pattern Method
"Evaluate $\int_a^b\dots\mathrm{d}x$" with $a,b$ given Direct definite integral (§2)
"Find the area between curve and $x$-axis" — curve stays positive $\int_a^b y\,\mathrm{d}x$, no absolute values needed
"Find the area between curve and $x$-axis" — curve crosses axis Split at roots (§3); take magnitudes, then sum
Line meets curve, area enclosed Combination of triangle + integral (§4)
"Curve $C$ meets curve $S$, find enclosed area" $\int_a^b[\text{top}-\text{bottom}]\,\mathrm{d}x$ (§4) — find $a,b$ from intersection equation
"Use the trapezium rule with $n$ strips" §5 — fill in the table; show all working
"Cannot be integrated algebraically" hints (e.g., $\sqrt{x^3+1}$, $2^x$, $\frac{1}{x^2+1}$) Trapezium rule (§5)
"Find $k$ such that $\int_0^k\dots\mathrm{d}x=\dots$" Integrate, sub limits, set equal, solve for $k$
Constant in integrand ("given $A$ is a constant") Treat as a constant; integral becomes function of $A$; equate to RHS, solve quadratic

8. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Including $+C$ in a definite integral Drop it — $C$ cancels in $\mathrm{F}(b)-\mathrm{F}(a)$
Treating area below $x$-axis as negative without flipping sign State area as positive; flip sign of the integral if necessary
Computing $\int$ across the $x$-axis without splitting Cancellation hides true area; always sketch and split at roots
Forgetting to use intersection $x$-values as limits First step in any "area between" question: find $\mathrm{f}=\mathrm{g}\Rightarrow x$-values
Putting line equation in wrong place when subtracting Always top minus bottom on the interval
Wrong number of ordinates with trapezium rule $n$ strips → $n+1$ ordinates. Double-check by counting
Using calculator for definite integrals (exam rule) Show all algebraic working — substitution, simplification
Not simplifying powers before integrating Convert $\dfrac{1}{x^2}\to x^{-2}$, $\sqrt{x}\to x^{1/2}$ first
Adding $\frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}$ when $n=-1$ $n=-1$ rule fails — but for P2 this case isn't tested (it's $\ln$, a P3 topic)
Forgetting whether trapezium rule under- or over-estimates Sketch curve; check concavity. Concave (∩) → under; convex (∪) → over
Integrating $(x-a)^2$ as $\frac{(x-a)^3}{3}$ without verifying the chain Works for linear inside — chain rule cancels the coefficient. For $(2x-1)^2$ etc., the formula gives $\frac{(2x-1)^3}{3\cdot 2}$
Mixing up areas with units missing Areas have units like m² — state if the problem uses physical units
Negative $h$ from swapped limits Always integrate with $a
Computing trapezium rule with $h$ from wrong endpoints $h=(b-a)/n$ — the integration limits, not problem context
In two-curve question, taking absolute values of integrand Don't — set up top-minus-bottom correctly first; the integrand is positive on $[a,b]$

9. Big picture (30-second mental model)

Definite integration is anti-differentiation evaluated between limits — the Fundamental Theorem turns it into $\mathrm{F}(b)-\mathrm{F}(a)$. Geometrically it's the signed area under $y=\mathrm{f}(x)$ between $x=a$ and $x=b$.

For area problems the core algorithm is: sketch → find boundaries (intersections/roots) → set up integral with correct limits and sign convention → integrate → evaluate. Above-axis pieces are positive; below-axis pieces need a sign flip. Composite shapes split into integrals + triangles/trapeziums.

For areas between two curves, integrate (top − bottom) over the intersection range. For areas between a curve and a line, often easier to subtract two areas: one under the line (a basic trapezium/triangle) and one under the curve (an integral).

When integration is impossible algebraically, fall back to the trapezium rule: $h=(b-a)/n$, $\tfrac12 h(y_0+2\cdot\text{interior}+y_n)$. Concave curves give under-estimates; convex curves give over-estimates.

Three keywords: sketch, split, sign. Every other detail follows.
