FP3 Ch 4 — Integration

The whole chapter in one mental model. This chapter extends the integration toolkit with three big new toys: (i) standard integrals of hyperbolic and inverse-hyp/inverse-trig form (reverses of Ch 3), (ii) substitution for any quadratic-under-a-square-root pattern — choose $x=a\sin\theta,\ a\sinh u$ etc. matched to the radical, (iii) reduction formulae that recursively drop a power index $n$ via integration by parts. Then two applications: arc length $s=\int\sqrt{1+(\mathrm dy/\mathrm dx)^2}\,\mathrm dx$ and surface area of revolution $S=\int 2\pi y\,\mathrm ds$.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Standard integral A pre-derived antiderivative that you quote without re-deriving. The chapter adds 11 new ones (hyperbolic, inverse-hyperbolic, inverse-trig); some are in the formula booklet, marked (*). 4.1
Booklet integrals (12-17) The "general $a$" forms: $\int\frac{1}{\sqrt{a^2-x^2}}\mathrm dx=\arcsin(x/a)$, $\int\frac{1}{a^2+x^2}\mathrm dx=\frac{1}{a}\arctan(x/a)$, $\int\frac{1}{\sqrt{a^2+x^2}}\mathrm dx=\operatorname{arsinh}(x/a)$, $\int\frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2-a^2}}\mathrm dx=\operatorname{arcosh}(x/a)$, plus partial-fraction logs $\frac{1}{2a}\ln\lvert\frac{a+x}{a-x}\rvert$. Only #13 has a $1/a$ factor outside. 4.3, 4.4
$\int f'(x)[f(x)]^n\mathrm dx$ Reverse of chain rule on a power: $=\frac{[f(x)]^{n+1}}{n+1}+C$. Spot when an integrand is "function-with-its-derivative-attached" — e.g. $\int x(1+x^2)^{-1/2}\mathrm dx$ with $f(x)=1+x^2,\ f'=2x$. 4.1
$\int\frac{f'(x)}{f(x)}\mathrm dx$ Reverse of $\ln$: $=\ln\lvert f(x)\rvert+C$. Watch out: a $1+x^2$ denominator does NOT auto-imply $\arctan$ — check if the numerator is the derivative of the denominator. 4.1
Trig substitution For $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}$ try $x=a\sin\theta$ (then $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}=a\cos\theta$); for $a^2+x^2$ try $x=a\tan\theta$. Uses $\sin^2+\cos^2=1$ and $\sec^2=1+\tan^2$ to simplify the radical. 4.3
Hyperbolic substitution For $\sqrt{a^2+x^2}$ try $x=a\sinh u$ (then $\sqrt{a^2+x^2}=a\cosh u$); for $\sqrt{x^2-a^2}$ try $x=a\cosh u$. Uses $1+\sinh^2=\cosh^2$ and $\cosh^2-1=\sinh^2$. 4.3
Completing the square Convert $px^2+qx+r$ into $p\left[(x+\tfrac{q}{2p})^2+\tfrac{4pr-q^2}{4p^2}\right]$. Used to rewrite quadratic radicals/denominators into the booklet form $a^2\pm u^2$. 4.4
Reduction formula A recursive identity $I_n=g(x)+kI_{n-2}$ (or $I_{n-1}$) that drops the index $n$ by 1 or 2. Built by integration by parts on a power-times-something integrand. After enough reductions, $I_n$ collapses to a known $I_0$ or $I_1$. 4.6
Walliss-product trick For $I_n=\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^n x\,\mathrm dx$, the recurrence $nI_n=(n-1)I_{n-2}$ gives nice products: $I_{2k+1}=\frac{2k}{2k+1}\cdot\frac{2k-2}{2k-1}\cdots\frac{2}{3}$, $I_{2k}=\frac{2k-1}{2k}\cdot\frac{2k-3}{2k-2}\cdots\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{\pi}{2}$. Same recurrence for $\cos^n$ on $[0,\pi/2]$. 4.6 Ex 22
Arc length $s=\int_{x_A}^{x_B}\sqrt{1+(\mathrm dy/\mathrm dx)^2}\,\mathrm dx$ (Cartesian) or $s=\int_{t_A}^{t_B}\sqrt{(\mathrm dx/\mathrm dt)^2+(\mathrm dy/\mathrm dt)^2}\,\mathrm dt$ (parametric). Derived from Pythagoras on a tiny chord $\mathrm ds^2=\mathrm dx^2+\mathrm dy^2$. 4.7
$\mathrm ds$ "differential of arc" A tiny piece of arc length: $\mathrm ds=\sqrt{1+(\mathrm dy/\mathrm dx)^2}\,\mathrm dx$. The fundamental object — every arc length / surface formula is some integral of $\mathrm ds$ weighted appropriately. 4.7
Surface of revolution When a curve is spun about an axis, each tiny slice of arc $\mathrm ds$ traces a frustum of area $\approx 2\pi y\,\mathrm ds$ (about $x$-axis) or $2\pi x\,\mathrm ds$ (about $y$-axis). Integrate to get total $S$. 4.8
Frustum surface area The lateral area of the cone-slice between two circles of radius $r$ and $r+\delta r$ separated by slant length $l$ is $\pi(2r+\delta r)l$ — generalising the cone formula $\pi rl$. 4.8

5. Completing-the-square workflow (Section 4.4)

Use this for $\int\dfrac{1}{px^2+qx+r}\,\mathrm dx$ or $\int\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{px^2+qx+r}}\,\mathrm dx$:

Step Action
1 Factor out leading coeff: $px^2+qx+r=p\left[x^2+(q/p)x+(r/p)\right]$
2 Complete the square: $x^2+(q/p)x=(x+q/(2p))^2-(q/(2p))^2$
3 Get expression in form $A^2\pm(x+\alpha)^2$
4 Substitute $u=x+\alpha$, $\mathrm du=\mathrm dx$
5 Apply the matching booklet integral (12–17)

Worked tag: $\int\dfrac{\mathrm dx}{x^2+6x+13}=\int\dfrac{\mathrm dx}{(x+3)^2+4}=\tfrac12\arctan\!\dfrac{x+3}{2}+C$ (using #13 with $a=2$).

6. Reduction formula recipe (Section 4.6)

Step Action
1 Identify $I_n$ (and $I_{n-1}$ or $I_{n-2}$) — the family
2 Choose how to split: $\sin^n x=\sin^{n-1}x\sin x$, $\tan^n x=\tan^{n-2}x\tan^2 x$, $x^n e^x=x^n\cdot e^x$, etc.
3 Apply integration by parts on the split: $u=$ powers piece, $\mathrm dv=$ derivative-friendly piece
4 Use a Pythagorean identity ($\sin^2=1-\cos^2$, $\tan^2=\sec^2-1$, $\cosh^2-\sinh^2=1$) to express $\cos^2$ etc. in terms of $\sin^n$
5 Collect terms — both $I_n$ and $I_{n-2}$ may appear; rearrange to isolate $I_n$
6 Apply the recurrence to step down from $I_n$ to $I_0$ or $I_1$

Common reduction formulas (memorise the structure):

Family Formula
$I_n=\int x^n e^x\,\mathrm dx$ $I_n=x^n e^x-nI_{n-1}$
$I_n=\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^n x\,\mathrm dx$ $nI_n=(n-1)I_{n-2}$
$I_n=\int_0^{\pi/2}\cos^n x\,\mathrm dx$ $nI_n=(n-1)I_{n-2}$ (same)
$I_n=\int\tan^n x\,\mathrm dx$ $I_n=\dfrac{\tan^{n-1}x}{n-1}-I_{n-2}$
$I_n=\int\tanh^n x\,\mathrm dx$ $I_n=I_{n-2}-\dfrac{\tanh^{n-1}x}{n-1}$ (sign flip vs trig)
$I_n=\int\sinh^n x\,\mathrm dx$ $nI_n=\sinh^{n-1}x\cosh x-(n-1)I_{n-2}$
$I_n=\int_0^a(a^2-x^2)^n\,\mathrm dx$ $I_n=\dfrac{2na^2}{2n+1}I_{n-1}$

7. Arc length (Section 4.7)

Pythagoras at infinitesimal scale: $\mathrm ds^2=\mathrm dx^2+\mathrm dy^2$ → divide by $\mathrm dx^2$ etc.

Form of curve Arc length formula
Cartesian $y=f(x)$ $s=\displaystyle\int_{x_A}^{x_B}\sqrt{1+(\mathrm dy/\mathrm dx)^2}\,\mathrm dx$
Cartesian $x=g(y)$ $s=\displaystyle\int_{y_A}^{y_B}\sqrt{1+(\mathrm dx/\mathrm dy)^2}\,\mathrm dy$
Parametric $x(t),y(t)$ $s=\displaystyle\int_{t_A}^{t_B}\sqrt{(\mathrm dx/\mathrm dt)^2+(\mathrm dy/\mathrm dt)^2}\,\mathrm dt$

Tactical tip: if the integrand $\sqrt{1+(y')^2}$ doesn't simplify nicely, try a hyperbolic substitution (often $x=\sinh u$ when integrand has $\sqrt{1+x^2}$). Sometimes $1+(y')^2$ becomes a perfect square — always check.

Worked archetype: $y=\tfrac12 x^2$ from $0$ to $4$: $\mathrm dy/\mathrm dx=x$ → $s=\int_0^4\sqrt{1+x^2}\,\mathrm dx$. Sub $x=\sinh u$: integral becomes $\int_0^{\operatorname{arsinh}4}\cosh^2 u\,\mathrm du=\tfrac12[u+\sinh u\cosh u]_0^{\operatorname{arsinh}4}=\tfrac12\operatorname{arsinh}4+2\sqrt{17}=\tfrac12\ln(4+\sqrt{17})+2\sqrt{17}$.

8. Surface of revolution (Section 4.8)

Axis of rotation Form Formula
$x$-axis, Cartesian $y=f(x)$ $S=\displaystyle\int 2\pi y\,\mathrm ds$ $S=2\pi\displaystyle\int_{x_A}^{x_B} y\sqrt{1+(\mathrm dy/\mathrm dx)^2}\,\mathrm dx$
$x$-axis, parametric same $S=2\pi\displaystyle\int_{t_A}^{t_B} y\sqrt{(\mathrm dx/\mathrm dt)^2+(\mathrm dy/\mathrm dt)^2}\,\mathrm dt$
$y$-axis, Cartesian $S=\displaystyle\int 2\pi x\,\mathrm ds$ $S=2\pi\displaystyle\int_{x_A}^{x_B} x\sqrt{1+(\mathrm dy/\mathrm dx)^2}\,\mathrm dx$
$y$-axis, parametric same $S=2\pi\displaystyle\int_{t_A}^{t_B} x\sqrt{(\mathrm dx/\mathrm dt)^2+(\mathrm dy/\mathrm dt)^2}\,\mathrm dt$

Mnemonic: $S=\int 2\pi r\,\mathrm ds$ where $r$ is the perpendicular distance from the curve point to the axis (so $r=y$ for $x$-axis rotation, $r=x$ for $y$-axis).

10. Common pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Seeing $1+x^2$ in denominator and assuming $\arctan$ Check whether numerator is $1$ or a derivative — could be $f'/f$ or $f'f^n$ form
Using booklet #13 without the $1/a$ factor $\int\frac{1}{a^2+x^2}\mathrm dx=\frac{1}{a}\arctan(x/a)$ — only #13 has $1/a$ outside
Failing to manipulate $25+9x^2$ into $9(\tfrac{25}{9}+x^2)$ first Need form $k(a^2+x^2)$ before applying #13
Forgetting the $a$ scaling in $\int\sqrt{a^2-x^2}\mathrm dx$-type subs $x=a\sin\theta$ gives $\mathrm dx=a\cos\theta\,\mathrm d\theta$ AND $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}=a\cos\theta$ — two factors of $a$
Reduction: stopping after one application Step down to $I_0$ or $I_1$ (something integrable)
Mixing trig/hyperbolic identities — using $\sin^2+\cos^2=1$ on $\sinh,\cosh$ $\cosh^2-\sinh^2=1$ (note minus!)
Arc length: forgetting to take positive root $\sqrt{1+(y')^2}\ge 1$, always positive
Surface of revolution: using wrong $r$ About $x$-axis: $r=y$; about $y$-axis: $r=x$
Reduction formula with definite integrals: forgetting boundary terms Evaluate $[uv]_a^b$ explicitly — often vanishes but check
Using $\arcsin,\arccos$ outside their range $\arcsin/\arccos$ defined only $\lvert x\rvert<1$; check before substituting
Completing the square: sign errors $x^2-6x+13=(x-3)^2+4$ (note plus $4$, since $13-9=4$)
Ignoring the modulus on $\ln\lvert f\rvert$ Required when $f$ can be negative; for $\cosh x>0$, modulus optional

11. Big picture (the 30-second mental model)

This chapter is a decision tree for integrating expressions with quadratics, radicals, or powers:

  1. Standard antiderivatives (booklet 12–17): match the integrand directly. Memorise the four root forms and the two log forms (16, 17).
  2. Substitution when standard forms don't fit but a quadratic-radical pattern does:
    • $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}$ → $x=a\sin\theta$
    • $a^2+x^2$ → $x=a\tan\theta$ (or $a\sinh u$)
    • $\sqrt{x^2-a^2}$ → $x=a\cosh u$
    • Mixed quadratic → complete the square first.
  3. Reduction formulae when the integrand has $f^n(x)$ and parts is cleaner than evaluating directly. Iterate down to a base case.
  4. Hyperbolic identities ($\cosh^2-\sinh^2=1$, $1+\sinh^2=\cosh^2$) replace trig identities to evaluate integrals like $\int\sqrt{1+x^2}\,\mathrm dx$.
  5. Applications: arc length and surface of revolution both reduce to integrals of $\mathrm ds=\sqrt{1+(\mathrm dy/\mathrm dx)^2}\,\mathrm dx$ — the same building block.

Six booklet integrals + three substitution patterns + reduction recursion = the chapter.
