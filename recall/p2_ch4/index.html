P2 Ch 4 — The Binomial Expansion

The whole chapter in one mental model. $(a+b)^n$ for positive integer $n$ expands into $n+1$ terms whose coefficients are Pascal's triangle / binomial coefficients $\binom{n}{r}=\dfrac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$. The full formula: $$(a+b)^n=\sum_{r=0}^{n}\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r=a^n+\binom{n}{1}a^{n-1}b+\binom{n}{2}a^{n-2}b^2+\dots+b^n.$$ Two main tasks: (1) expand completely or up to a given power; (2) find the coefficient of a specific term using the general term $\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r$. Plus a third application: approximation — for small $x$, drop high powers and substitute a numerical $x$ to estimate things like $0.975^{10}$.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Binomial expression An expression with two terms, e.g. $a+b$, $1+x$, $3-2x$. The "bi" = two. The whole chapter is about powers of these. 4.0
Binomial expansion Multiplying out $(a+b)^n$ to give a polynomial of $n+1$ terms in descending powers of $a$ (ascending powers of $b$). For $n\in\mathbb{N}$, expansion terminates. 4.3
Pascal's triangle A triangular array where each entry is the sum of the two directly above. The $(n+1)$-th row gives the coefficients of $(a+b)^n$. Rows: $\{1\}$, $\{1,1\}$, $\{1,2,1\}$, $\{1,3,3,1\}$, $\{1,4,6,4,1\}$, etc. 4.1
Factorial $n!$ $n!=n\times(n-1)\times(n-2)\times\dots\times 2\times 1$. By convention $0!=1$ and $1!=1$. So $5!=120$. The number of orderings of $n$ distinct objects. 4.2
Binomial coefficient $\binom{n}{r}$ or ${}^nC_r$ "$n$ choose $r$": the number of ways to choose $r$ items from $n$, formula $\binom{n}{r}=\dfrac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$. Equals the $(r+1)$-th entry in the $(n+1)$-th row of Pascal's triangle. Symmetric: $\binom{n}{r}=\binom{n}{n-r}$. 4.2
Symmetric property $\binom{n}{r}=\binom{n}{n-r}$ — choosing $r$ to include is same as choosing $n-r$ to exclude. So $\binom{10}{3}=\binom{10}{7}=120$. Useful for shortcuts. 4.2 Challenge
General term The $(r+1)$-th term of $(a+b)^n$ is $\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r$. To find the coefficient of a specific power of $x$, set up the term and solve for $r$. 4.4
Total index In $\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r$, the powers add to $(n-r)+r=n$. Every term has total degree $n$. Useful sanity check. 4.1
Ascending powers of $x$ Listing terms from lowest power to highest: $1+8qx+28q^2x^2+\dots$ This is the standard order for "first four terms" questions. 4.3 Ex 5
Coefficient (of $x^k$) The numerical multiplier in front of $x^k$ in the expanded form. To extract: identify the right $r$, compute $\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}\cdot(\text{coefficient of }b)^r$. 4.4
Like terms (combining when expanding $(a+x)(b+\dots)^n$) When the binomial is multiplied by another factor $(p+qx)$, the coefficient of $x^k$ comes from multiple cross-products. E.g. $x^3$ in $(2+x)(\dots)^n$ = $2\cdot[x^3 \text{ term}] + 1\cdot[x^2 \text{ term}]$. 4.4 Ex 6
Binomial approximation (small $x$) When $x$ is small (say $\lvert x\rvert<1$), high powers $x^3,x^4,\dots$ become negligible. Truncate the expansion to first few terms; substitute numerical $x$ to approximate $(1+x)^n$. Higher accuracy = more terms kept. 4.5
"Substitute appropriate $x$" To estimate $0.975^{10}$ using $\left(1-\tfrac{x}{4}\right)^{10}$: set $1-\tfrac x 4 = 0.975$ → $x=0.1$. Always reverse-solve. 4.5 Ex 9
$\mathbb{N}$ (natural numbers) The positive integers $\{1,2,3,\dots\}$ (sometimes including $0$). The condition "$n\in\mathbb{N}$" means the binomial expansion terminates. For non-integer $n$ (e.g. $\tfrac12$, $-1$), there's an infinite series version (P3). 4.3

$n$ Row Sum $=2^n$
0 $1$ 1

Rule: each entry is the sum of the two diagonally above. Row sum $=2^n$ (substituting $a=b=1$ in $(a+b)^n$). Symmetry within each row.

3. The Binomial Theorem — formula table

Quantity Formula
Full expansion $(a+b)^n=\displaystyle\sum_{r=0}^{n}\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r$
First few terms $a^n+\binom{n}{1}a^{n-1}b+\binom{n}{2}a^{n-2}b^2+\binom{n}{3}a^{n-3}b^3+\dots$
General term ($(r+1)$-th term) $T_{r+1}=\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r$
Coefficient of $x^k$ in $(1+x)^n$ $\binom{n}{k}$
Coefficient of $x^k$ in $(p+qx)^n$ $\binom{n}{k}p^{n-k}q^k$
Total number of terms $n+1$
Sum of all coefficients (set $a=b=1$) $2^n$
Sum of even-indexed coefficients = sum of odd-indexed each $=2^{n-1}$
Symmetry $\binom{n}{r}=\binom{n}{n-r}$
Special: $\binom{n}{0}=\binom{n}{n}=1$ First & last coefficients always 1
Special: $\binom{n}{1}=\binom{n}{n-1}=n$ Linear coefficient is $n$
Special: $\binom{n}{2}=\dfrac{n(n-1)}{2}$ Quadratic coefficient

4. Computing $\binom{n}{r}$ — three methods

Method When to use
Pascal's triangle (lookup) Small $n$ ($n\le 10$ or so) — quick mental arithmetic
Factorial formula $\binom{n}{r}=\dfrac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$ Medium $n$, with calculator
Calculator $\binom{n}{r}$ button ($nCr$) Large $n$ — most efficient
Identity $\binom{n}{r}=\binom{n}{n-r}$ If $r$ is large, switch to smaller equivalent: $\binom{20}{17}=\binom{20}{3}=1140$
Cancellation in factorial form $\dfrac{10!}{4!\cdot 6!}=\dfrac{10\cdot 9\cdot 8\cdot 7}{4!}=\dfrac{5040}{24}=210$

5. Method decision table — common binomial tasks

Spot this Strategy
"Expand $(a+b)^n$ fully", small $n$ Use Pascal's triangle row to write each $\binom{n}{r}$
"Expand fully", larger $n$ Use formula $\binom{n}{r}=\dfrac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$ for each term
"First four terms in ascending powers of $x$" $T_1=a^n,T_2=\binom{n}{1}a^{n-1}b,T_3=\binom{n}{2}a^{n-2}b^2,T_4=\binom{n}{3}a^{n-3}b^3$
"Find coefficient of $x^k$" in $(1+px)^n$ $\binom{n}{k}p^k$; equate to given value
"Find coefficient of $x^k$" in $(p+qx)^n$ $\binom{n}{k}p^{n-k}q^k$
"$x^k$ coefficient in $(p+qx)(\text{linear binomial})^n$" Expand the binomial up to $x^k$, then multiply linear factor and collect $x^k$ terms
"Coefficient of $x^k$ in $\left(\dfrac{a}{x}+bx^m\right)^n$" General term $\binom{n}{r}\left(\dfrac a x\right)^{n-r}(bx^m)^r=\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r x^{(m+1)r-n}$. Set exponent $=k$, solve for $r$
"Estimate $(1+\epsilon)^n$" for small $\epsilon$ Use approximation: $\approx 1+n\epsilon+\binom{n}{2}\epsilon^2+\binom{n}{3}\epsilon^3+\dots$
"Estimate $0.975^{10}$" Reverse-solve: $1-\tfrac x 4=0.975$ → $x=0.1$, sub into expansion
"Coefficient ratio: $x^a$ coefficient is $k$ times $x^b$ coefficient" Form ratio $\dfrac{\binom{n}{a}p^a}{\binom{n}{b}p^b}=k$, solve for $p$

7. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Forgetting brackets when substituting $b=2y$ etc. Always wrap: $(2y)^2=4y^2$, $(-2x)^3=-8x^3$
Sign error with negative $b$: $(-5)^2=25$ but $(-5)^3=-125$ Track sign: even power → +, odd → −
Counting wrong row of Pascal's triangle: $(a+b)^4$ uses the 5th row $n$-th power → $(n+1)$-th row (since rows are 0-indexed)
Computing $\binom{n}{r}$ with $r!$ in numerator instead of denominator $\binom{n}{r}=\dfrac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$ — both factorials in denominator
Asking "first three terms" but giving four (or vice versa) Read carefully: "first four terms" = $T_1,T_2,T_3,T_4$ (i.e. constant through $x^3$)
Putting coefficient of $x^k$ as $\binom{n}{k}$ when binomial is $(p+qx)^n$ rather than $(1+x)^n$ Coefficient is $\binom{n}{k}p^{n-k}q^k$ — must include $p^{n-k}$ and $q^k$
Expanding $(2+x)(3-2x)^7$ by trying to expand the entire thing Expand $(3-2x)^7$ up to needed power, then multiply by $(2+x)$, then collect like terms
Coefficient of $x^k$ in product $(\dots)\cdot(\dots)$ — only multiplying terms that add to $x^k$ All cross-products: $x^a\cdot x^{k-a}$ for each $a$ in the first factor
In approximation, including too few terms for required accuracy More terms = more accurate. For 4 d.p., usually 4 terms suffices for $\lvert x\rvert<0.1$
Reversing the substitution to find the right $x$: e.g. for $0.99^6$ via $(1-\tfrac{x}{10})^6$, taking $x=0.99$ $1-\tfrac x{10}=0.99\Rightarrow x=0.1$
Forgetting to simplify each term: leaving $\binom{10}{3}1^7(2x)^3$ unsimplified Compute the numbers; reduce to standard $a x^k$ form
Confusing the general term index: "$T_{r+1}$ is the $(r+1)$-th term, with $r=0$ for the first term" First term: $r=0$, gives $a^n$. $r$ is the power of $b$, NOT the term number
Trying to apply binomial theorem with $n$ a fraction or negative integer in P2 That's the infinite series version (P3 only). P2 only handles $n\in\mathbb N$

8. Coefficient-extraction cheatsheet

Form Coefficient of $x^k$
$(1+x)^n$ $\binom{n}{k}$
$(1+ax)^n$ $\binom{n}{k}a^k$
$(a+x)^n$ $\binom{n}{k}a^{n-k}$
$(a+bx)^n$ $\binom{n}{k}a^{n-k}b^k$
$(p+qx)(a+bx)^n$ $p\binom{n}{k}a^{n-k}b^k+q\binom{n}{k-1}a^{n-k+1}b^{k-1}$

9. Big picture (30-second mental model)

Multiplying $(a+b)^n$ out gives $n+1$ terms because each of the $n$ brackets contributes either $a$ or $b$. The number of ways to get a term with $r$ "$b$"s and $(n-r)$ "$a$"s is $\binom{n}{r}$ — the count of selections. Hence:

$$(a+b)^n=\sum_{r=0}^n\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r.$$

Three universal tasks:

  1. Expand: read coefficients from Pascal's triangle (small $n$) or compute $\binom{n}{r}$ (large $n$).
  2. Find a specific coefficient: use general term $\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r$ — solve for $r$ if $b$ contains $x^m$.
  3. Approximate $(1+\epsilon)^n$ for small $\epsilon$: truncate after a few terms (high powers of $\epsilon$ are negligible), substitute numerical $\epsilon$.

For products of binomial $\times$ polynomial, expand the binomial up to the needed power, then multiply and collect like terms.

The same coefficients $\binom{n}{r}$ pop up everywhere — combinatorics ("choose $r$ from $n$"), probability (binomial distribution), differentiation/integration shortcuts, and Pascal's identity. Memorise the formula, the symmetry, and the row sum $=2^n$ — those are the "DNA" of the chapter.
