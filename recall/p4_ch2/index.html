P4 Ch 2 — Partial Fractions

The whole chapter in one mental model. Every proper rational function can be split into a sum of simple "partial" pieces matching the factor structure of the denominator: each distinct linear factor $(ax+b)$ contributes $\dfrac{A}{ax+b}$; each repeated factor $(ax+b)^k$ contributes $\dfrac{A_1}{ax+b}+\dfrac{A_2}{(ax+b)^2}+\dots+\dfrac{A_k}{(ax+b)^k}$. Improper fractions (deg num ≥ deg den) need polynomial division first to peel off a polynomial part. Solve for the unknowns $A_i$ by substitution (clever $x$ values that kill all but one) or equating coefficients.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Rational function A ratio $\dfrac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$ of polynomials. The numerator $P$ and denominator $Q$. Every $P/Q$ can in principle be written as a polynomial plus a sum of simpler fractions. 2.1
Linear factor A factor of the form $(ax+b)$ — a polynomial of degree 1. Example: $(x-3),\ (2x+1)$. The "atomic" denominators in P4 partial fractions. 2.1
Distinct linear factors Linear factors that are different (not multiples of each other). $(x-1)(x+2)(2x-1)$ is three distinct linear factors. 2.1
Repeated (linear) factor A linear factor raised to a power $\ge 2$ in the denominator, e.g. $(x+3)^2$ or $(2x-1)^3$. Needs the special "ladder" decomposition. 2.2
Proper fraction $\deg(\text{numerator}) < \deg(\text{denominator})$. Decomposes directly into pure partial fractions (no polynomial part). 2.3
Improper fraction $\deg(\text{numerator})\ge \deg(\text{denominator})$. Must polynomial-divide first to extract a quotient $Q(x)$ plus a proper-fraction remainder. 2.3
Partial fraction decomposition The standard form $\sum$ of $\dfrac{A_i}{(\text{linear})^j}$ pieces matching the factorisation of $Q(x)$. The unique answer (modulo ordering). 2.1
Method of substitution Solve for unknowns by choosing values of $x$ that make all but one term vanish. Quickest when the denominator has distinct linear factors. 2.1 Ex 1a
Method of equating coefficients Expand and compare powers of $x$ on both sides, get a linear system in $A,B,C,\dots$. Always works; usually slower. 2.1 Ex 1b
Cover-up rule Shortcut for finding $A_i$ when the factor $(x-r)$ is non-repeated: $A_i=\dfrac{P(r)}{Q(r)/(x-r)}$, i.e. cover the factor and substitute $x=r$. implicit in Ex 1
Polynomial / algebraic division Long division of polynomials, used to convert improper to proper. Result: $\dfrac{P}{Q}=\text{quotient}(x)+\dfrac{\text{remainder}(x)}{Q(x)}$, with $\deg(\text{remainder})<\deg Q$. 2.3
Identity $\equiv$ "Equal for all $x$ in the domain" — used because we are equating two expressions for partial fractions, not solving an equation for $x$. Both sides must agree everywhere. 2.1
Application: integration $\displaystyle\int\dfrac{A}{ax+b}\,dx=\dfrac{A}{a}\ln|ax+b|$ and $\displaystyle\int\dfrac{A}{(ax+b)^k}\,dx=\dfrac{-A}{a(k-1)(ax+b)^{k-1}}$. Partial fractions make most rational integrals tractable. Links to Ch6
Application: binomial expansion Each $\dfrac{1}{ax+b}$ piece can be re-expanded via $(1+x)^{-1}$ binomial series — relies on partial fractions to split a complex denominator first. Links to Ch4

2. The decomposition templates

Match the denominator's factor structure exactly:

Denominator factor structure Partial fraction template
$(ax+b)(cx+d)$ — two distinct linear $\dfrac{A}{ax+b}+\dfrac{B}{cx+d}$
$(ax+b)(cx+d)(ex+f)$ — three distinct $\dfrac{A}{ax+b}+\dfrac{B}{cx+d}+\dfrac{C}{ex+f}$
$(ax+b)^2$ — repeated linear $\dfrac{A}{ax+b}+\dfrac{B}{(ax+b)^2}$
$(ax+b)^3$ — triple linear $\dfrac{A}{ax+b}+\dfrac{B}{(ax+b)^2}+\dfrac{C}{(ax+b)^3}$
$(ax+b)(cx+d)^2$ — distinct + repeated $\dfrac{A}{ax+b}+\dfrac{B}{cx+d}+\dfrac{C}{(cx+d)^2}$

Rule: for $(ax+b)^k$, use all powers $1,2,\dots,k$ in the decomposition (the "ladder").

3. Improper fraction templates (do polynomial division first)

Numerator vs denominator degrees Initial form
$\deg P=\deg Q$ (e.g. linear/linear, quadratic/quadratic) Constant + proper. e.g. $\dfrac{2x}{x+1}=A+\dfrac{B}{x+1}$
$\deg P=\deg Q+1$ (e.g. cubic/quadratic) Linear + proper. e.g. $\dfrac{3x^3}{(x-1)(x-2)}=Ax+B+\dfrac{C}{x-1}+\dfrac{D}{x-2}$
$\deg P=\deg Q+2$ Quadratic + proper
etc. Polynomial of deg $(\deg P-\deg Q)$ + proper

Procedure: either do explicit long division, or write template with unknown polynomial part and solve.

4. Solution methods — comparison

Method When best How
Substitution Distinct linear factors Multiply both sides by $Q(x)$, then sub $x=$ root of each factor to isolate one $A_i$ at a time
Cover-up Distinct linear, only need one constant "Cover" the factor $(x-r)$ in the original, evaluate the rest at $x=r$
Equating coefficients Repeated factors, irreducible quadratics, or as a check Expand RHS, collect powers of $x$, equate to LHS coefficients, solve linear system
Hybrid Repeated factor + others Substitute roots to find easy ones, then equate one extra coefficient (often $x^{\max}$) for the awkward $A_i$

Pro tip on repeated factors: substituting $x=r$ where $(x-r)$ is the repeated factor gives the highest-order coefficient ($B$ in $\dfrac{B}{(x-r)^k}$). For the lower ones (like $A$), use equating coefficients or another value of $x$.

5. Step-by-step algorithm

Step Action
1 Check proper vs improper. If improper, polynomial-divide (or write polynomial-part template).
2 Fully factorise the denominator into linear factors (use factor theorem if needed).
3 Write the partial fraction template based on the factor structure.
4 Multiply through by the original denominator to clear fractions: get a polynomial identity.
5 Choose values of $x$ (roots of factors) to isolate constants via substitution.
6 For repeated factors, also equate leading coefficient (or another power) for the lower-order constant.
7 Verify by combining the partial fractions back into one — should equal the original.

6. Worked archetypes

# Setup Method
1 $\dfrac{6x-2}{(x-3)(x+1)}\equiv\dfrac{A}{x-3}+\dfrac{B}{x+1}$ $x=3:\ 16=4A\Rightarrow A=4$. $x=-1:\ -8=-4B\Rightarrow B=2$
2 $\dfrac{6x^2+5x-2}{x(x-1)(2x+1)}\equiv\dfrac{A}{x}+\dfrac{B}{x-1}+\dfrac{C}{2x+1}$ $x=0\Rightarrow A=2$; $x=1\Rightarrow B=3$; $x=-\tfrac12\Rightarrow C=-4$
3 $\dfrac{11x^2+14x+5}{(x+1)^2(2x+1)}\equiv\dfrac{A}{x+1}+\dfrac{B}{(x+1)^2}+\dfrac{C}{2x+1}$ $x=-1\Rightarrow B=-2$; $x=-\tfrac12\Rightarrow C=3$. Then equate $x^2$: $11=2A+C\Rightarrow A=4$
4 $\dfrac{2x}{x+1}$ — improper (linear/linear). Form $A+\dfrac{B}{x+1}$. $x=-1\Rightarrow B=-2$. $x=0\Rightarrow A=2$. So $\dfrac{2x}{x+1}=2-\dfrac{2}{x+1}$
5 $\dfrac{3x^3}{(x-1)(x-2)}$ — cubic/quadratic. Form $Ax+B+\dfrac{C}{x-1}+\dfrac{D}{x-2}$. Leading: $3=A$. $x=1\Rightarrow C=-3$; $x=2\Rightarrow D=24$; $x=0\Rightarrow B=9$
6 $\dfrac{x^2}{(x+1)^2}$ — improper (deg 2 / deg 2). Form $A+\dfrac{B}{x+1}+\dfrac{C}{(x+1)^2}$. Leading: $A=1$. $x=-1\Rightarrow C=1$. $x=0\Rightarrow 0=1+B+1\Rightarrow B=-2$

7. Decision flow — pick the right setup

Spot this Do this
Denominator already factorised, all distinct linear Distinct-factor template; substitution
Denominator has $(\dots)^2$ or higher Repeated-factor "ladder" template
Denominator not factorised Factor first (factor theorem if cubic)
Numerator's degree $\ge$ denominator's Improper — polynomial-divide or use polynomial-prefix template
Two distinct linear factors, fast hand calc needed Cover-up rule
Awkward fractions / $x=\tfrac12$ etc. Substitution still works; or equate coefficients
Mixture of distinct + repeated Hybrid: substitute for distinct AND repeated-top constant; equate for the awkward repeated-bottom

8. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Writing $\dfrac{B}{x+3}+\dfrac{C}{x+3}$ for a repeated factor This is just $\dfrac{B+C}{x+3}$ — wrong structure. Use $\dfrac{B}{x+3}+\dfrac{C}{(x+3)^2}$
Forgetting to polynomial-divide an improper fraction Always check degrees first; improper ⇒ polynomial part needed
Forgetting to factorise the denominator before decomposing E.g. $\dfrac{6x+6}{x^2-9}$: factor as $(x-3)(x+3)$ first
Using only $\dfrac{C}{(x+3)^2}$ for $(x+3)^2$ — missing the $\dfrac{B}{x+3}$ piece Repeated factor needs all powers $1,\dots,k$
Substituting $x$ into the original fraction (which has 0 denominator at the root) Multiply out first to get a polynomial identity, then substitute
Equating coefficients but forgetting one term Expand carefully; double-check coefficient counts match
Misidentifying the leading coefficient of the polynomial part in improper case Match the leading powers: $\dfrac{3x^3}{x^2}=3x$, so the polynomial part starts $3x+\dots$
Simplifying the answer wrongly (e.g. cancelling $x$'s in numerator and denominator) Once decomposed, leave each piece as $\dfrac{A_i}{(\text{linear})^k}$
Quadratic factor in denominator Beyond P4 — only linear factors here

9. Big picture (30-second mental model)

Partial fractions = "factor the denominator, decompose to match":

  1. Proper vs improper: improper ⇒ peel off polynomial via division.
  2. Factor the denominator into linear factors.
  3. Match the template: distinct factor ⇒ single piece; repeated factor of multiplicity $k$ ⇒ ladder of $k$ pieces with powers 1 to $k$.
  4. Solve for constants via substitution (clever roots) or equating coefficients (always works).
  5. Use it for integration ($\ln$ for distinct, $-1/[(k-1)(ax+b)^{k-1}]$ for repeated) or for binomial expansion.

Factor → template → solve — that's the chapter.
