FP1 Ch 2 — Roots of Quadratic Equations

The whole chapter in one mental model. For $ax^2+bx+c=0$ with roots $\alpha,\beta$: sum $\alpha+\beta=-b/a$ and product $\alpha\beta=c/a$. Every symmetric function of $\alpha,\beta$ — $\alpha^2+\beta^2$, $\alpha^3+\beta^3$, $1/\alpha+1/\beta$, $\alpha-\beta$, etc. — can be rewritten in terms of these two quantities, which lets you reconstruct equations whose roots are transformed versions of $\alpha,\beta$ without ever solving the original.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Roots of a quadratic The two solutions $\alpha,\beta$ of $ax^2+bx+c=0$. They may be (i) two distinct reals, (ii) one repeated real, or (iii) a complex conjugate pair when the coefficients are real. The chapter treats them as algebraic objects without solving for them numerically. 2.1
Sum of roots $\alpha+\beta=-b/a$. Comes from comparing $a(x-\alpha)(x-\beta)=ax^2-a(\alpha+\beta)x+a\alpha\beta$ with $ax^2+bx+c$. Real even when $\alpha,\beta$ are complex (because complex roots come in conjugate pairs whose sum is $2\operatorname{Re}(\alpha)$). 2.1
Product of roots $\alpha\beta=c/a$. Same comparison gives this. Also real for complex conjugate roots: $(p+q\mathrm{i})(p-q\mathrm{i})=p^2+q^2\in\mathbb{R}$. 2.1
Symmetric function of roots An expression in $\alpha,\beta$ that is unchanged if you swap $\alpha\leftrightarrow\beta$. Examples: $\alpha+\beta$, $\alpha\beta$, $\alpha^2+\beta^2$, $\alpha^3+\beta^3$, $1/\alpha+1/\beta$. Crucially, every such symmetric function can be written purely in terms of $\alpha+\beta$ and $\alpha\beta$. 2.1
Non-symmetric expression An expression that does change under $\alpha\leftrightarrow\beta$, e.g. $\alpha-\beta$ or $\alpha^2-\beta^2$. To get these you typically square first (giving $(\alpha-\beta)^2=(\alpha+\beta)^2-4\alpha\beta$, which is symmetric) then take the square root, choosing the sign by convention. Ch review Q3
Reciprocal roots The pair $1/\alpha$, $1/\beta$. Their sum is $\dfrac{\alpha+\beta}{\alpha\beta}$ and their product is $\dfrac{1}{\alpha\beta}$. The new equation's coefficients are the reverse of the old: if $ax^2+bx+c=0$, the reciprocal-root equation is $cx^2+bx+a=0$. 2.1 Q3, 2.2 Ex 3
Squared roots The pair $\alpha^2,\beta^2$. Sum $(\alpha+\beta)^2-2\alpha\beta$; product $(\alpha\beta)^2$. Common exam transformation. 2.2 Ex 3
Cubed roots The pair $\alpha^3,\beta^3$. Sum $(\alpha+\beta)^3-3\alpha\beta(\alpha+\beta)$; product $(\alpha\beta)^3$. 2.2 Ex 4
Linearly transformed roots New roots like $k\alpha+m,\ k\beta+m$ (e.g. $2\alpha+1,2\beta+1$). Sum $k(\alpha+\beta)+2m$; product $k^2\alpha\beta+km(\alpha+\beta)+m^2$. 2.2 Q1
Newton's identity (for $\alpha^2+\beta^2$) $(\alpha+\beta)^2=\alpha^2+2\alpha\beta+\beta^2$, so $\alpha^2+\beta^2=(\alpha+\beta)^2-2\alpha\beta$. The most-used identity in the chapter. 2.1 Ex 1d
Newton's identity (for $\alpha^3+\beta^3$) $(\alpha+\beta)^3=\alpha^3+3\alpha^2\beta+3\alpha\beta^2+\beta^3$, so $\alpha^3+\beta^3=(\alpha+\beta)^3-3\alpha\beta(\alpha+\beta)$. Memorise — it appears every paper. 2.2 Ex 4
Difference squared identity $(\alpha-\beta)^2=(\alpha+\beta)^2-4\alpha\beta$. Useful for finding $\lvert\alpha-\beta\rvert$ without solving. Equals $(b^2-4ac)/a^2$ — the discriminant scaled. Ch review Q3
Forming a new quadratic from $S,P$ If a new quadratic has roots with sum $S$ and product $P$, the equation is $x^2-Sx+P=0$ (monic), or $kx^2-kSx+kP=0$ for any non-zero $k$. Multiply through by an integer $k$ to clear fractions and get integer coefficients. 2.2
Vieta's formulas (degree 2) Synonym for the sum/product rules: the elementary symmetric polynomials in the roots equal $\pm$(coefficients)/(leading coefficient). For higher-degree polynomials, FP3 generalises this. 2.1

2. The two foundational identities (memorise these EXACTLY)

Identity Statement Used for
Sum/product from coefficients $\alpha+\beta=-\dfrac{b}{a}$, $\quad\alpha\beta=\dfrac{c}{a}$ Reading off symmetric data without solving
Quadratic from sum & product $x^2-Sx+P=0$ where $S=\alpha+\beta$, $P=\alpha\beta$ Constructing equations with given roots

3. Symmetric-function toolbox — every expression in terms of $S=\alpha+\beta$ and $P=\alpha\beta$

Expression Rewritten Memory aid
$\alpha+\beta$ $S$ Definition
$\alpha\beta$ $P$ Definition
$\dfrac{1}{\alpha}+\dfrac{1}{\beta}$ $\dfrac{S}{P}$ Common-denominator
$\dfrac{1}{\alpha\beta}$ $\dfrac{1}{P}$ Direct
$\alpha^2+\beta^2$ $S^2-2P$ Expand $(\alpha+\beta)^2$
$\alpha^2\beta^2=(\alpha\beta)^2$ $P^2$ Direct
$\alpha^3+\beta^3$ $S^3-3PS$ Expand $(\alpha+\beta)^3$ then collect
$\alpha^3\beta^3$ $P^3$ Direct
$(\alpha-\beta)^2$ $S^2-4P$ $(\alpha-\beta)^2=(\alpha+\beta)^2-4\alpha\beta$

4. Forming a new quadratic from transformed roots (Section 2.2)

Old roots New roots New sum $S'$ New product $P'$ New equation
$\alpha,\beta$ $k\alpha,\ k\beta$ (scale) $kS$ $k^2P$ $x^2-kSx+k^2P=0$
$\alpha,\beta$ $\alpha+m,\ \beta+m$ (shift) $S+2m$ $P+mS+m^2$ $x^2-(S+2m)x+(P+mS+m^2)=0$
$\alpha,\beta$ $k\alpha+m,\ k\beta+m$ (affine) $kS+2m$ $k^2P+kmS+m^2$ Combine the two
$\alpha,\beta$ $\alpha^2,\beta^2$ $S^2-2P$ $P^2$ $x^2-(S^2-2P)x+P^2=0$
$\alpha,\beta$ $\alpha^3,\beta^3$ $S^3-3PS$ $P^3$ $x^2-(S^3-3PS)x+P^3=0$
$\alpha,\beta$ $1/\alpha,\ 1/\beta$ $S/P$ $1/P$ $Px^2-Sx+1=0$ (or equivalently $cx^2+bx+a=0$ if original was $ax^2+bx+c=0$)
$\alpha,\beta$ $\alpha^2\beta,\alpha\beta^2$ $PS$ $P^3$ $x^2-PSx+P^3=0$
$\alpha,\beta$ $\alpha\beta,\ \alpha^2\beta^2$ $P+P^2$ $P^3$ $x^2-(P+P^2)x+P^3=0$

General algorithm:

  1. Read off $S=\alpha+\beta$, $P=\alpha\beta$ from the original.
  2. Compute new sum $S'$ in terms of $S,P$ (use the toolbox in §3).
  3. Compute new product $P'$ in terms of $S,P$.
  4. Write $x^2-S'x+P'=0$.
  5. Multiply through by an integer to clear fractions → integer coefficients.

5. Worked archetypes (memorise the shape)

# Original equation Transformation Key step New equation
1 $2x^2-5x-4=0$ Find $\alpha^2+\beta^2$ $S=5/2$, $P=-2$, then $S^2-2P=25/4+4=41/4$ (value $41/4$)
2 $3x^2+x-6=0$ New roots $\alpha^2,\beta^2$ $S=-1/3$, $P=-2$; new $S'=37/9$, new $P'=4$ $9x^2-37x+36=0$
3 $3x^2+x-6=0$ New roots $1/\alpha,1/\beta$ New $S'=S/P=1/6$, new $P'=1/P=-1/2$ $6x^2-x-3=0$
4 $2x^2+5x-4=0$ New roots $\alpha^3,\beta^3$ $S=-5/2$, $P=-2$; $S^3-3PS=-245/8$, $P^3=-8$ $8x^2+245x-64=0$
5 Roots $-3/2,5/4$ Construct equation $S=-1/4$, $P=-15/8$; mult by 8 $8x^2+2x-15=0$
6 $\alpha+\beta=-7/3$, $\alpha\beta=-2$, $\alpha>\beta$ Show $\alpha-\beta=11/3$ $(\alpha-\beta)^2=S^2-4P=49/9+8=121/9$, take +ve root $\alpha-\beta=11/3$

6. Decision flow — what to do when you see…

Spot this Do this
"Without solving" + "find $\alpha^2+\beta^2$" or similar Read off $S,P$ from coefficients, plug into Newton identity
"Find a quadratic with roots $f(\alpha),f(\beta)$" Compute new sum $S'$ and new product $P'$ in terms of $S,P$
Transformed roots involve $1/\alpha,1/\beta$ $S'=S/P$, $P'=1/P$ — equivalently, reverse the coefficients
Transformed roots involve $\alpha^2,\beta^2$ $S'=S^2-2P$, $P'=P^2$
Transformed roots involve $\alpha^3,\beta^3$ $S'=S^3-3PS$, $P'=P^3$
Asked for $\alpha-\beta$ (sign matters) Compute $(\alpha-\beta)^2=S^2-4P$, take +ve square root if told $\alpha>\beta$
Coefficients contain unknowns and you're given a relation between roots (e.g. "roots are reciprocals") Translate to a relation between $S,P$ — e.g. reciprocals ⇒ $\alpha\beta=1$ ⇒ $c/a=1$
Asked to find integer $a,b,c$ from given $\alpha,\beta$ Compute $S,P$, write $x^2-Sx+P=0$, multiply through by LCM of denominators
Final new equation has fractional coefficients Multiply through by LCM to get integer coefficients (any non-zero scaling preserves the roots)

7. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Sign error: writing $\alpha+\beta=b/a$ It's $-b/a$ — the minus comes from comparing $-a(\alpha+\beta)$ with $b$
Treating $\alpha-\beta$ as symmetric It's not. Square it first: $(\alpha-\beta)^2=S^2-4P$, then choose sign
Forgetting to clear fractions for "integer coefficients" After writing $x^2-S'x+P'=0$, multiply by LCM of the denominators of $S'$ and $P'$
Wrong identity for $\alpha^3+\beta^3$ It is $S^3-3PS$, NOT $S^3-3P$. The factor of $S$ matters.
Assuming $S^2-4P\geqslant 0$ If the original quadratic has complex roots, $S^2-4P<0$ and $\alpha-\beta$ is imaginary.
Reading "roots of $ax^2+bx+c=0$" but using $x^2+bx+c=0$ form Always divide through by $a$ first: $S=-b/a$, $P=c/a$
Confusing "sum of squares" $\alpha^2+\beta^2$ with "square of sum" $(\alpha+\beta)^2$ These differ by $2\alpha\beta$: $\alpha^2+\beta^2=(\alpha+\beta)^2-2\alpha\beta$
When constructing a new equation, missing "$-$" before $S'$ The standard form is $x^2-S'x+P'=0$ — minus sum, plus product
Trying to "solve first then transform" Wastes time and risks irrational/complex algebra. Always transform via $S,P$ directly.

8. Big picture (the 30-second mental model)

The chapter trades explicit roots for two summary statistics $(S,P)=(\alpha+\beta,\alpha\beta)$:

  1. Coefficients → $(S,P)$ via $S=-b/a,\ P=c/a$.
  2. Any symmetric function of $\alpha,\beta$ → expression in $(S,P)$ via Newton-style identities.
  3. Transformed roots → new $(S',P')$ → new quadratic $x^2-S'x+P'=0$.
  4. Multiply through by integers to clean up.

Two formulas, two identities ($\alpha^2+\beta^2$ and $\alpha^3+\beta^3$), and one workflow. Don't solve — transform. (FP3 will extend this from quadratics to cubics and quartics.)
