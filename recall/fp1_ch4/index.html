FP1 Ch 4 — Coordinate Systems

The whole chapter in one mental model. Two specific curves dominate this chapter: the parabola $y^2=4ax$ and the rectangular hyperbola $xy=c^2$. Both come with a parametric form (parameter $t$) that makes points easy to write and tangent/normal slopes easy to compute by differentiation. Standard exam recipe: parametrise, differentiate to get the tangent slope, write the line equation; or use the focus/directrix property of the parabola to set up locus problems.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Parametric equations A pair $x=\mathrm{f}(t),\ y=\mathrm{g}(t)$ that describe a curve by sweeping the parameter $t$ through its allowed range. Each $t$ value gives one point $(x,y)$. The Cartesian equation comes from eliminating $t$ between the two equations. Useful because tangent slope $=\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}t}{\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}t}$ falls out cleanly. 4.1
Cartesian equation An equation in $x$ and $y$ alone (no $t$). Found from parametric form by eliminating $t$ — usually solving one equation for $t$ and substituting into the other, or multiplying/combining them so $t$ cancels. 4.1
Parabola The locus of points equidistant from a fixed point (the focus) and a fixed line (the directrix). Standard right-opening form: $y^2=4ax$ with focus $S=(a,0)$ and directrix $x=-a$. Parametric: $x=at^2,y=2at$. Symmetric about the $x$-axis. 4.2
Focus $S$ The fixed point in the focus-directrix definition. For $y^2=4ax$, $S=(a,0)$. The "focus" name comes from optics: parallel rays hitting a parabolic mirror reflect through $S$ — used in headlights, satellite dishes, telescopes. 4.2
Directrix The fixed line in the focus-directrix definition. For $y^2=4ax$, the directrix has equation $x=-a$ (or equivalently $x+a=0$). Always perpendicular to the parabola's axis of symmetry, on the opposite side of the vertex from the focus. 4.2
Vertex The single point where the parabola changes direction, sitting on the axis of symmetry midway between focus and directrix. For $y^2=4ax$, vertex is the origin $(0,0)$. 4.2
Locus of points The set of all points obeying a given geometric rule. The parabola, ellipse, hyperbola are each defined as a locus. The standard locus problem: given a rule (e.g. "distance to point $S$ = distance to line $\ell$"), derive the equation. 4.2
Focus-directrix property For any point $P$ on a parabola: $SP=PX$ where $X$ is the foot of the perpendicular from $P$ to the directrix. This is the definition of a parabola. Standard derivation trick: square both sides of $SP=PX$ to remove square roots, expand, simplify. 4.2
Rectangular hyperbola The curve $xy=c^2$ where $c>0$. "Rectangular" because its two asymptotes (the $x$- and $y$-axes) are perpendicular. Parametric form: $x=ct,y=c/t$ ($t\neq 0$). Two disconnected branches in the first and third quadrants. 4.3
Asymptote A straight line that the curve approaches arbitrarily closely but never touches. For $xy=c^2$, the asymptotes are $x=0$ (the $y$-axis) and $y=0$ (the $x$-axis). 4.3
General point A typical point on the curve written using the parameter — e.g. $P(at^2,2at)$ on $y^2=4ax$, or $P(ct,c/t)$ on $xy=c^2$. Lets you write proofs that are valid for every point on the curve simultaneously. 4.2–4.3
Tangent line at $P$ The straight line through $P$ that touches the curve at $P$ and has the same gradient as the curve there. Found by computing $\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x$ at $P$, then using $y-y_1=m_T(x-x_1)$. 4.3
Normal line at $P$ The straight line through $P$ perpendicular to the tangent at $P$. Gradient $m_N=-1/m_T$ (negative reciprocal). Then $y-y_1=m_N(x-x_1)$. 4.3
Chord A straight line segment joining two distinct points on the curve. To find its equation, use the two-point form. To find where a chord meets the curve again, substitute the line into the curve equation and solve. 4.3
Parametric tangent of parabola At $P(at^2,2at)$ on $y^2=4ax$, the tangent has equation $ty=x+at^2$. Memorise this — it's a standard exam shortcut. The slope is $1/t$. 4.3 Ex 11
Parametric normal of parabola At $P(at^2,2at)$ on $y^2=4ax$, the normal has equation $y+tx=2at+at^3$. Slope $-t$. Combined with the tangent formula above, all parabola tangent/normal questions become substitution. 4.3 Ex 11
Parametric tangent of rect. hyp. At $P(ct,c/t)$ on $xy=c^2$, the tangent has equation $x+t^2 y=2ct$. Slope $-1/t^2$. 4.3 Ex 12
Parametric normal of rect. hyp. At $P(ct,c/t)$ on $xy=c^2$, the normal has equation $t^3 x-ty=c(t^4-1)$. Slope $t^2$. Ch review Q29

3. Eliminating the parameter — three standard tricks

Parametric form Trick Cartesian result
$x=at^2,\ y=2at$ Solve $y=2at$ for $t$ → $t=y/(2a)$, sub into $x=at^2$ $y^2=4ax$
$x=ct,\ y=c/t$ Multiply: $xy=ct\cdot c/t=c^2$ $xy=c^2$
Mixed (e.g. $x=t-1,y=t^2$) Solve simpler one for $t$, substitute Depends on form
Trig parametric (later, FP3) Use $\sin^2+\cos^2=1$ Ellipse / hyperbola

4. Tangent & normal — the universal recipe

Given a curve and a point $P=(x_0,y_0)$ on it:

Step Action
1 Rearrange curve into form $y=f(x)$ (or use parametric $\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x=(\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}t)/(\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}t)$)
2 Differentiate to get $\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x$
3 Substitute $P$'s coordinates → tangent slope $m_T$
4 Tangent equation: $y-y_0=m_T(x-x_0)$
5 Normal slope: $m_N=-1/m_T$
6 Normal equation: $y-y_0=m_N(x-x_0)$
7 Rearrange into requested form (e.g. $ax+by+c=0$ with integer $a,b,c$)

Worked tangent slopes:

Curve $\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x$ formula Slope at point
$y^2=4ax$, upper half $y=2\sqrt{ax}$ → $\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x=\sqrt{a/x}$ At $(at^2,2at)$: $\sqrt{a/(at^2)}=1/t$
$y^2=4ax$, lower half $y=-2\sqrt{ax}$ → $\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x=-\sqrt{a/x}$ At $(at^2,-2at)$: $-1/t$ (so general formula is $1/t$ once signed $t$)
$xy=c^2$ $y=c^2/x$ → $\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x=-c^2/x^2$ At $(ct,c/t)$: $-c^2/(c^2t^2)=-1/t^2$

5. Focus & directrix problems (Section 4.2)

Given Find Method
Focus $(a,0)$, directrix $x=-a$ Parabola equation $y^2=4ax$ — read off $a$
Equation $y^2=4ax$ Focus, directrix $a$ from $4a=$ coefficient of $x$; focus $(a,0)$, directrix $x=-a$
Equation $y^2=kx$ Focus, directrix $a=k/4$; focus $(k/4,0)$, directrix $x=-k/4$
Locus rule "$SP=PX$" with $S=(p,0)$, line $x=-p$ Locus equation Set $\sqrt{(x-p)^2+y^2}=x+p$, square both sides → $y^2=4px$, so $a=p$
Distance from a point on parabola to focus Use focus-directrix $SP=$ distance to directrix $=x_P+a$ (since vertex is at origin) — so $SP=x_P+a$, no $\sqrt{}$ needed

6. Worked archetypes (memorise the shape)

# Setup Method
1 Parabola from focus $(7,0)$, directrix $x=-7$ $a=7$, equation $y^2=28x$
2 Convert $y^2=24x$ to focus & directrix $4a=24$ so $a=6$; focus $(6,0)$, directrix $x=-6$
3 $P(8,-8)$ on $y^2=8x$, find tangent at $P$ Differentiate $y=-2\sqrt{2x}$ at $x=8$: slope $-1/\sqrt{4}=-1/2$? No, redo via parametric $t$: $y=-8\Rightarrow t=-2$, slope $1/t=-1/2$, line $y+8=-\tfrac12(x-8)$
4 $P(2,4)$ on $xy=8$, find tangent $y=8/x$, $\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x=-8/x^2=-2$ at $x=2$; tangent $y-4=-2(x-2)$ → $2x+y-8=0$
5 $P(2,4)$ on $xy=8$, find normal Normal slope $1/2$; normal $y-4=\tfrac12(x-2)$ → $x-2y+6=0$
6 Show locus of "$P$ equidistant from $(6,0)$ and $x=-6$" is $y^2=24x$ Set $(x-6)^2+y^2=(x+6)^2$, expand, simplify → $y^2=24x$
7 Tangent of $y^2=4ax$ at $(at^2,2at)$ in standard form Slope $1/t$; line: $y-2at=\tfrac1t(x-at^2)$ → $ty=x+at^2$
8 Normal of $y^2=4ax$ at $(at^2,2at)$ Slope $-t$; line: $y-2at=-t(x-at^2)$ → $y+tx=2at+at^3$
9 Tangent of $xy=c^2$ at $(ct,c/t)$ Slope $-1/t^2$; line: $y-c/t=-(1/t^2)(x-ct)$ → $x+t^2 y=2ct$
10 Two tangents from external point $(-1,7)$ to $xy=9$ Tangent eqn $x+t^2y=6t$; sub $(-1,7)$: $7t^2-6t-1=0$, factor $(7t+1)(t-1)=0$, $t=-1/7,1$. Points $(−3/7,−21)$, $(3,3)$

7. Decision flow — what to do when you see…

Spot this Do this
$y^2=4ax$ with given $a$ Identify focus $(a,0)$, directrix $x=-a$, parametric form $(at^2,2at)$
$xy=c^2$ with given $c$ Identify branches in Q1, Q3; asymptotes are axes; parametric $(ct,c/t)$
Parametric $(at^2,2at)$ given You're on $y^2=4ax$ — use tangent $ty=x+at^2$, normal $y+tx=2at+at^3$
Parametric $(ct,c/t)$ given You're on $xy=c^2$ — use tangent $x+t^2 y=2ct$, normal $t^3x-ty=c(t^4-1)$
"Distance from $P$ to focus" Use focus-directrix: $SP=x_P+a$ on $y^2=4ax$ — saves square-rooting
Locus problem with two distance equalities Set distances equal, square both sides, expand, simplify
Two distinct tangents meet at a point Substitute the meeting point into the parametric tangent equation → quadratic in $t$ → two roots → two contact points
Chord meets curve again Substitute line into curve equation, factor out the known intersection, solve for the other
"Find tangent in form $ax+by+c=0$ with integer $a,b,c$" Multiply through to clear fractions/decimals; rearrange
Question gives parametric equations and asks for Cartesian Eliminate $t$ — solve one for $t$, substitute, OR multiply if it cancels (rect. hyp. trick)

8. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Using the wrong sign on the lower half of the parabola $y=2\sqrt{ax}$ for $y>0$, $y=-2\sqrt{ax}$ for $y<0$ — always check the $y$-coordinate
Forgetting to square in locus derivation $SP=PX$ means $SP^2=PX^2$ — square BEFORE expanding to avoid surds
Confusing focus and directrix Focus is a point, directrix is a line. They're equidistant from the vertex and on opposite sides
Reading $y^2=24x$ as $a=24$ $4a=24$ so $a=6$ — don't drop the factor 4
Tangent slope sign error on rect. hyp. $\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x=-c^2/x^2<0$ always — both branches slope downward
Using $(at^2,2at)$ formula for $xy=c^2$ Wrong curve — use $(ct,c/t)$
When asked "give answer in form $ax+by+c=0$", leaving fractions Multiply through by the LCM of the denominators
Picking wrong $\pm$ when finding tangent slope from $y^2=4ax$ Decide first whether the point is on the upper ($y>0$) or lower ($y<0$) half; that fixes the sign
Forgetting $t\neq 0$ on rect. hyp. parametric The parameter cannot be 0 — that would put the point at infinity
In locus problems, distance to a vertical line is $\lvert x-c\rvert$ NOT $\sqrt{(x-c)^2+y^2}$ — distance from point to a vertical line is just horizontal separation

9. Big picture (the 30-second mental model)

  1. Curves and their two faces: parabola $y^2=4ax\Leftrightarrow(at^2,2at)$; rectangular hyperbola $xy=c^2\Leftrightarrow(ct,c/t)$. Cartesian for plotting/checking, parametric for differentiating cleanly.

  2. Focus-directrix defines the parabola: every point is equidistant from $S=(a,0)$ and the line $x=-a$. Any locus question that says "distance from a point = distance from a line" is a parabola in disguise.

  3. Tangent/normal recipe: differentiate, find slope at $P$, write line through $P$ with that slope (tangent) or its negative reciprocal (normal). On the standard curves, the parametric forms collapse this into memorisable formulas:

    • Parabola: $ty=x+at^2$ (tangent), $y+tx=2at+at^3$ (normal)
    • Rect. hyp.: $x+t^2y=2ct$ (tangent), $t^3x-ty=c(t^4-1)$ (normal)

  4. Locus + tangent + chord are the three problem types. Practice rotating between Cartesian and parametric until either feels equally natural.
