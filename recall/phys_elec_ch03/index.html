The whole topic in one mental model. Resistance $R = V/I$ is a property of a *component* — change its length or thickness and $R$ changes. Resistivity $\rho$ is the deeper, intrinsic property of the *material* itself: it strips out the geometry. The bridge between them is $R = \rho l / A$ — a longer wire has more $R$ ($R \propto l$), a fatter wire has less ($R \propto 1/A$), and the constant of proportionality is $\rho$. Core Practical 7 is just this equation turned into a straight-line graph: plot $R$ (y) against $l$ (x), the gradient is $\rho/A$, so $\rho = \text{gradient} \times A$, where the area $A = \pi d^2/4$ comes from a micrometer reading of the diameter (in mm, converted with $\times 10^{-3}$). Two recall anchors rule everything: $R = V/I$ never $R = VI$; and the unit of $\rho$ is $\Omega\,\text{m}$ never $\Omega/\text{m}$.

4. Big picture (last-minute recall)

  • $R = V/I$ never $R = VI$ (Phuc's verified lesson mistake — never again).
  • Resistance is a property of a *component* (depends on shape); resistivity $\rho$ is intrinsic to the *material* (independent of shape).
  • $R = \rho l/A$: longer wire $\Rightarrow$ more $R$; fatter wire $\Rightarrow$ less $R$. Rearrange to $\rho = RA/l$.
  • Unit of $\rho$ is $\Omega\,\text{m}$ (ohm-metres), never $\Omega/\text{m}$.
  • Area $A = \pi d^2/4 = \pi r^2$ — uses the radius; never put diameter into $\pi r^2$.
  • Diameter is in mm ($\times10^{-3}$ to metres), NOT $\mu$m ($\times10^{-6}$).
  • CP7: plot $R$ (y) vs $l$ (x); gradient $= \rho/A$; so $\rho = \text{gradient} \times A$ — you MUST multiply by $A$.
  • A non-zero $R$-intercept on the CP7 graph = contact resistance; the gradient is immune to it (why the graphical method beats a single reading).
  • Metal: $T\uparrow \Rightarrow \rho\uparrow \Rightarrow R\uparrow$. NTC semiconductor: $T\uparrow \Rightarrow \rho\downarrow \Rightarrow R\downarrow$.
  • Ohmic = straight $I$–$V$ line *through the origin* (constant $R$); say "directly proportional", not just "linear".
