FP2 Ch 4 — Further Argand Diagrams (Loci & Transformations)

The whole chapter in one mental model. A complex number $z$ is a point in the plane, so equations and inequalities involving $z$ trace out shapes (loci) and define regions. The chapter has two parts: (1) loci — translate every condition $|z-a|=r$, $|z-a|=|z-b|$, $\arg(z-a)=\theta$, $|z-a|=k|z-b|$, $\arg\frac{z-a}{z-b}=\theta$ into a circle, line, half-line, or arc, and combine them; (2) transformations $w=f(z)$ — a rule that maps points in the $z$-plane to points in the $w$-plane. Linear ones $w=az+b$ are translation+rotation+enlargement; Möbius ones $w=\frac{az+b}{cz+d}$ are deeper — invert by solving for $z$ and substitute the locus equation.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Argand diagram The plane where $z=x+iy$ is plotted at $(x,y)$. Real axis = $x$, imaginary axis = $y$. Distances and angles between points carry geometric meaning. The locus of $z$ is the set of all $z$-points satisfying a given condition. 4.1
Locus The set of all points $z$ in the Argand diagram satisfying a condition. Examples: $|z-z_1|=r$ → circle; $\arg(z-z_1)=\theta$ → half-line. The plural is loci. 4.1
Distance interpretation $|z_1-z_2|$ $|z_1-z_2|$ = distance between the two points $z_1,z_2$ on the Argand diagram. Crucial for converting $|z-a|=r$ to "distance from $a$ is $r$" → circle of radius $r$ centred at $a$. 4.1
Circle locus $|z-z_1|=r$ Circle centred at $z_1=(x_1,y_1)$, radius $r$. Cartesian: $(x-x_1)^2+(y-y_1)^2=r^2$. 4.1
Perpendicular bisector $|z-z_1|=|z-z_2|$ Set of points equidistant from $z_1$ and $z_2$ — the perpendicular bisector of the segment $z_1z_2$. 4.1
Half-line $\arg(z-z_1)=\theta$ Ray starting from $z_1$ (open: $z_1$ is not included), making angle $\theta$ with the positive real axis direction at $z_1$. Cartesian: $\dfrac{y-y_1}{x-x_1}=\tan\theta$ on the appropriate side of $z_1$. 4.1
Apollonius circle $|z-a|=k|z-b|$ For $k>0,\ k\neq 1$: locus is a circle (not a line!). Found by squaring both sides and rearranging to Cartesian form. The case $k=1$ degenerates to the perpendicular bisector. 4.2
Arc-of-circle locus $\arg\dfrac{z-a}{z-b}=\theta$ Arc of a circle through points $a$ and $b$ — the set of points subtending angle $\theta$ at the segment $ab$. Endpoints $a,b$ excluded. By inscribed-angle theorem: minor arc if $\theta>\pi/2$, major if $\theta<\pi/2$, semicircle if $\theta=\pi/2$. 4.2
Half-plane region $|z-z_1|<|z-z_2|$ Region of points closer to $z_1$ than $z_2$ — one side of the perpendicular bisector. The boundary $=$ is dashed (excluded) for strict, solid (included) for non-strict. 4.3
Disk region $|z-z_1|\leq r$ Closed disk: interior + boundary of the circle. Strict $<$ gives open disk (boundary excluded). 4.3
Vertical strip $p\leq\text{Re}(z)\leq q$ Region $p\leq x\leq q$ — a vertical strip (or horizontal for $\text{Im}$ conditions). Combine with other loci to get more complex regions. 4.4
Sector / wedge $\alpha\leq\arg(z-z_1)\leq\beta$ Region bounded by two half-lines from $z_1$ — looks like a slice of pie. The vertex $z_1$ is excluded (it has no defined argument). 4.4
Transformation $w=f(z)$ A rule mapping each point $z$ in the $z$-plane to a single point $w$ in the $w$-plane. Studies how curves and regions are mapped. Inverse direction: solve $w=f(z)$ for $z$ in terms of $w$. 4.5
Translation $w=z+a+ib$ Shifts $z$ by the vector $\binom{a}{b}$. Preserves shapes; only repositions. 4.5
Enlargement (with rotation) $w=kz$ for $k\in\mathbb{R}$ Scales by $k$ about origin. If $k>0$: pure enlargement. If $k<0$: enlargement with $\pi$ rotation. 4.5
Rotation $w=iz$ Anticlockwise rotation by $\pi/2$ about origin. More generally $w=e^{i\alpha}z$ rotates by $\alpha$. 4.5
Linear transformation $w=az+b$ Composition of enlargement (factor $\|a\|$), rotation (angle $\arg a$), and translation by $b$. Preserves shapes (lines→lines, circles→circles). 4.5
Möbius transformation $w=\dfrac{az+b}{cz+d}$ More flexible. Lines and circles map to lines or circles (collectively "circlines"). Standard exam treatment: solve for $z$ in $w$, substitute into locus equation, simplify. 4.5
$w$-plane Same Argand-style plane but coordinates $(u,v)$ where $w=u+iv$. Plotting "in the $w$-plane" means using $u,v$ axes. 4.5

2. Loci cheat-sheet — the FIVE standard cases

Condition Locus How to derive Cartesian
$\lvert z-z_1\rvert=r$ Circle, centre $z_1$, radius $r$ $(x-x_1)^2+(y-y_1)^2=r^2$
$\lvert z-z_1\rvert=\lvert z-z_2\rvert$ Perpendicular bisector of segment $z_1z_2$ Square both sides, expand, simplify (cross-terms cancel)
$\arg(z-z_1)=\theta$ Half-line from $z_1$ (open, no endpoint) at angle $\theta$ $\dfrac{y-y_1}{x-x_1}=\tan\theta$, with sign restriction on $x-x_1$ depending on quadrant of $\theta$
$\lvert z-a\rvert=k\lvert z-b\rvert$ ($k>0$, $k\neq 1$) Apollonius circle Square both sides, expand $\lvert\cdot\rvert^2$, complete the square — circle in $(u,v)$
$\arg\dfrac{z-a}{z-b}=\theta$ Arc of circle through $a$ and $b$ subtending angle $\theta$, endpoints excluded Tangent of $\theta$ via $\tan(\arg(z-a)-\arg(z-b))$; or use circle properties

3. Sketching loci — what to mark

Locus type Mark on sketch
Circle Centre (cross), radius (arrow), label as "$\lvert z-z_1\rvert=r$"
Perpendicular bisector Draw the segment $z_1z_2$ dashed (helper line), then perpendicular bisector solid
Half-line Open circle at $z_1$, ray extending in direction $\theta$
Half-line: special angles $\theta=0$: positive $x$-direction; $\theta=\pi/2$: upwards; $\theta=\pi$: leftwards; $\theta=-\pi/2$: downwards
Apollonius circle Mark $a$ and $b$, find centre/radius algebraically, then plot
Arc Mark $a$ and $b$ as open circles; semicircle if $\theta=\pi/2$; minor arc above the chord if $\theta>\pi/2$; major arc if $\theta<\pi/2$. Note: the arc is on the side of $ab$ opposite to where the angle "opens"

4. Combining loci — find the intersection

Type of question Method
Solve $\lvert z-z_1\rvert=r$ AND $\arg(z-z_1)=\theta$ The half-line from $z_1$ at angle $\theta$ hits the circle at one point: $z=z_1+re^{i\theta}$
Solve $\lvert z-z_1\rvert=r$ AND $\arg z=\phi$ Parametrize the half-line $z=t e^{i\phi}$, $t>0$; substitute into $\lvert z-z_1\rvert^2=r^2$, solve quadratic in $t$
Min/max $\lvert z\rvert$ subject to circle locus $\lvert z-z_1\rvert=r$ $\lvert z\rvert_{\max}=\lvert z_1\rvert+r$, $\lvert z\rvert_{\min}=\lvert z_1\rvert-r$ (if origin is outside) or $r-\lvert z_1\rvert$ (if origin is inside)
Min/max $\arg z$ subject to circle The extreme argument occurs when the line from $O$ to $z$ is tangent to the circle. Use right-triangle geometry: $\sin(\arg z-\theta_C)=r/\lvert z_1\rvert$ where $\theta_C=\arg z_1$
Two perpendicular bisector / line conditions Solve the simultaneous linear equations for $(x,y)$, give as $z=x+iy$
Two circle conditions Subtract the circle equations to get a line, solve with one of the circle equations

5. Regions on Argand diagrams

Inequality Region
$\lvert z-z_1\rvert\leq r$ Closed disk: interior + boundary
$\lvert z-z_1\rvert Open disk: interior only
$\lvert z-z_1\rvert\geq r$ Exterior of circle (incl. boundary)
$\lvert z-z_1\rvert\leq\lvert z-z_2\rvert$ Closed half-plane: side of perpendicular bisector closer to $z_1$
$\alpha\leq\arg(z-z_1)\leq\beta$ Wedge (sector) bounded by two half-lines from $z_1$, NOT including $z_1$
$p\leq\text{Re}(z)\leq q$ Vertical strip $p\leq x\leq q$
$p\leq\text{Im}(z)\leq q$ Horizontal strip $p\leq y\leq q$
Two regions intersected Use ∩: shade only where both conditions hold

Convention: solid boundary = boundary included, dashed = excluded. Endpoint of half-line ALWAYS open (excluded).

6. Transformations cheat-sheet

Transformation Geometric effect
$w=z+a+ib$ Translate by $\binom{a}{b}$
$w=kz$, $k\in\mathbb{R},\ k>0$ Enlarge by factor $k$, centre $O$
$w=iz$ Rotate $\pi/2$ anticlockwise about $O$
$w=e^{i\alpha}z$ Rotate $\alpha$ about $O$
$w=\bar z$ Reflect in the real axis
$w=az+b$ general Enlarge by $\|a\|$, rotate by $\arg a$, then translate by $b$
$w=\dfrac{1}{z}$ Inversion in unit circle, then reflection in real axis
$w=z^2$ Squares the modulus, doubles the argument; loops circle twice; the real axis maps to non-negative reals; imaginary axis maps to non-positive reals
$w=\dfrac{az+b}{cz+d}$ Möbius — sends "circlines" to "circlines"

7. Möbius transformation method (the universal recipe)

For $w=\dfrac{az+b}{cz+d}$, given a locus in $z$, find the locus in $w$:

Step Action
1 Invert algebraically: solve $w=\dfrac{az+b}{cz+d}$ for $z$ → get $z=\dfrac{-dw+b}{cw-a}$ (or by cross-multiplying and isolating $z$)
2 Substitute $z$-locus equation. E.g. $\lvert z\rvert=k$ becomes $\left\lvert \dfrac{-dw+b}{cw-a}\right\rvert=k$, i.e. $\lvert -dw+b\rvert=k\lvert cw-a\rvert$
3 Square both sides (sometimes), or move to Cartesian by writing $w=u+iv$ and computing $\lvert\cdot\rvert^2$
4 Expand, collect, complete the square → equation in $(u,v)$
5 Identify: linear → line; quadratic with equal $u^2,v^2$ coefficients → circle; classify by completing the square for centre and radius
6 Sketch in $w$-plane (with $u,v$ axes)

Key identity used: $\left\lvert\dfrac{p}{q}\right\rvert=\dfrac{\lvert p\rvert}{\lvert q\rvert}$ for any complex $p,q$.

General rule of Möbius transformations:

  • Lines map to lines or circles
  • Circles map to lines or circles
  • Combined: "circlines" (the union of both) is preserved

5 $\arg\frac{z-a}{z-b}=\frac{\pi}{2}$ Arc subtending $\pi/2$ Semicircle on $ab$ as diameter
6 $w=\frac{5iz+i}{z+1}$, $\lvert z\rvert=1$ Solve: $z=\frac{i-w}{w-5i}$; substitute, simplify Line $v=3$ in $w$-plane
7 $w=\frac{3z-2}{z+1}$, $x^2+y^2=4$ Solve: $z=\frac{w+2}{3-w}$; $\lvert z\rvert=2$; expand Circle centre $(\frac{14}{3},0)$, radius $\frac{10}{3}$

10. Common pitfalls (and the marks they cost)

Pitfall Fix
Drawing the half-line $\arg(z-z_1)=\theta$ with closed circle at $z_1$ $z_1$ is excluded; use open circle
Drawing as a full line instead of half-line Only one direction! Half-line goes from $z_1$ outward in direction $\theta$
For Apollonius $|z-a|=k|z-b|$ with $k=1$, claiming circle $k=1$ is the degenerate case — perpendicular bisector (a line, not circle)
Forgetting to sketch in $w$-plane (sketching in $z$) for transformation problems Label axes $u,v$ in $w$-plane; the image is plotted there
Confusing $w=iz$ (rotate $\pi/2$ ccw) with $w=-iz$ (rotate $\pi/2$ cw) $i$ multiplies argument by adding $\pi/2$; $-i$ subtracts $\pi/2$
For Möbius: dividing instead of multiplying $\bar w$ to clear fractions When taking modulus: $\left\lvert\dfrac{p}{q}\right\rvert=\dfrac{\lvert p\rvert}{\lvert q\rvert}$, not $\dfrac{\lvert q\rvert}{\lvert p\rvert}$
Squaring $\lvert z-a\rvert=k\lvert z-b\rvert$ with $k$ instead of $k^2$ Square both sides; the right side becomes $k^2\lvert z-b\rvert^2$
Sign error in completing the square for Apollonius Double-check by expanding back
Half-line range restriction missing $\arg(z+3+2i)=3\pi/4$ → Cartesian $y=-x-5$ for $x<-3$ (only the half) — must specify range
Forgetting $\arg z_1=$ argument of CENTRE for tangent calculations The tangent from origin: $\arg z_{\max}=\arg z_1+\arcsin(r/\lvert z_1\rvert)$
For $\arg\frac{z-a}{z-b}=\theta$, drawing the wrong arc Inscribed-angle theorem: $\theta>\pi/2$ → minor arc; $\theta<\pi/2$ → major arc; $\theta=\pi/2$ → semicircle
Including endpoints $a,b$ in arc locus They are excluded — open circles
For region $\alpha\leq\arg(z-z_1)\leq\beta$, including $z_1$ itself $z_1$ is excluded (no defined argument from itself)
Getting Cartesian eq of perpendicular bisector via gradient method when squaring is faster Squaring both sides of $\lvert z-z_1\rvert^2=\lvert z-z_2\rvert^2$ — the $x^2+y^2$ terms cancel, giving a linear equation directly

11. Big picture (the 30-second mental model)

  1. Loci = geometric translation of algebraic conditions. $|z-a|$ is distance, $\arg(z-a)$ is angle. Five standard loci: circle, perp. bisector, half-line, Apollonius circle, circle arc.
  2. Regions = inequalities. Replace $=$ with $\leq$/$<$/$\geq$/$>$ and shade interior/exterior/half-plane.
  3. Transformations $w=f(z)$ map shapes from $z$-plane to $w$-plane.
    • Linear $w=az+b$: enlarge by $|a|$, rotate by $\arg a$, then translate. Shapes preserved (line→line, circle→circle).
    • Möbius $w=\frac{az+b}{cz+d}$: invert algebraically ($z=\frac{-dw+b}{cw-a}$), substitute the original locus equation, simplify in $u,v$. "Circlines" map to "circlines".
  4. Combining conditions: circle ∩ half-line = single point; multiple loci on same diagram = sketch each, find intersection algebraically.
  5. Optimisation: min/max $|z|$ on a circle = $|z_1|\pm r$. Min/max $\arg z$ on a circle uses tangent geometry.

Two ideas, one toolkit: distances and arguments. Everything else is bookkeeping.
