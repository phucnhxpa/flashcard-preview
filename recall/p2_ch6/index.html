P2 Ch 6 — Trigonometric Identities and Equations

The whole chapter in one mental model. Generalise sin/cos/tan from right triangles to any angle via the unit circle: $\cos\theta=x$, $\sin\theta=y$, $\tan\theta=y/x$. The CAST diagram tells you sign by quadrant. Two universal identities follow from the unit circle: $\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta\equiv 1$ and $\tan\theta\equiv\sin\theta/\cos\theta$. Solving $\sin\theta=k$, $\cos\theta=k$, $\tan\theta=p$ in an interval: get the principal value from your calculator, then use graph symmetry or CAST to find every other solution in range. For $\sin n\theta=k$ or $\sin(\theta+\alpha)=k$: substitute $X=n\theta$ or $X=\theta+\alpha$, scale or shift the interval, solve, then convert back. For mixed equations like $a\sin\theta=b\cos\theta$: divide by $\cos$ to get $\tan$. For $a\sin^2\theta+b\sin\theta+c=0$: treat as quadratic in $\sin\theta$.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Unit circle Circle of radius 1 centred at origin: $x^2+y^2=1$. The angle $\theta$ is measured anticlockwise from the positive $x$-axis to the ray $OP$. Coordinates of $P$ are $(\cos\theta,\sin\theta)$. 6.1
Quadrant One of four regions of the coordinate plane: Q1 ($\theta\in(0,90°)$), Q2 ($(90°,180°)$), Q3 ($(180°,270°)$), Q4 ($(270°,360°)$). Angles outside $0$–$360°$ are reduced mod $360°$ (or $2\pi$). 6.1
CAST diagram Mnemonic for sign of trig ratios in each quadrant: Q1 All positive; Q2 Sin only; Q3 Tan only; Q4 Cos only. Reading anti-clockwise from Q4 spells "CAST". 6.1
Acute angle with $x$-axis Given $\theta$ in any quadrant, the related acute angle $\alpha$ between ray $OP$ and the $x$-axis. Then $\sin\theta=\pm\sin\alpha$, etc., with sign from CAST. 6.1
Periodicity $y=\sin\theta$ and $y=\cos\theta$ have period $2\pi$ (or $360°$): adding a full revolution gives the same value. $\tan\theta$ has period $\pi$ (or $180°$). 6.1
Principal value The output of $\sin^{-1},\cos^{-1},\tan^{-1}$ on a calculator. Restricted ranges: $\cos^{-1}\in[0,\pi]$, $\sin^{-1}\in[-\tfrac\pi2,\tfrac\pi2]$, $\tan^{-1}\in(-\tfrac\pi2,\tfrac\pi2)$. May not lie in your problem's interval — treat as a seed, then find others by symmetry. 6.4
Inverse trig (arcsin etc.) Names for $\sin^{-1}$, $\cos^{-1}$, $\tan^{-1}$. Read as "the angle whose sine is …". 6.4
Pythagorean identity $\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta\equiv 1$. From $x^2+y^2=1$. The most-used trig identity. Equivalent rearrangements: $\sin^2\theta=1-\cos^2\theta$, $\cos^2\theta=1-\sin^2\theta$. 6.3
Quotient identity $\tan\theta\equiv\dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}$ (whenever $\cos\theta\ne 0$). Lets you convert between sin/cos and tan in equations. 6.3
Identity ($\equiv$ vs $=$) $\equiv$ means "true for all values" (identity), e.g. $\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta\equiv 1$. $=$ means "true for some specific $\theta$" (equation), e.g. $\sin\theta=\tfrac12$. 6.3
Exact values (key angles) Memorise $\sin/\cos/\tan$ of $30°/45°/60°$ in degrees and $\pi/6,\pi/4,\pi/3$ in radians. From equilateral triangle bisected (gives $30°,60°$) and isosceles right triangle (gives $45°$). 6.2
Solution interval The range over which you must find $\theta$, e.g. $0\le\theta\le 360°$ or $0<\theta\le 2\pi$. Answer must lie inside; principal value alone is rarely sufficient. 6.4
Symmetry of sin/cos Sine: $\sin(\pi-\theta)=\sin\theta$; second solution in interval $[0,2\pi]$ is $\pi-\text{principal}$. Cosine: $\cos(2\pi-\theta)=\cos\theta$; second solution is $2\pi-\text{principal}$. Tan: $\tan(\theta+\pi)=\tan\theta$; second solution is $\pi+\text{principal}$. 6.4
Substitution $X=n\theta$ or $X=\theta+\alpha$ For equations like $\sin 3\theta=k$ or $\cos(\theta-30°)=k$. Let $X$ be the inside; solve $\sin X=k$ over the transformed interval ($X$-interval = $n\times$original or shifted by $\alpha$); convert each $X$ back to $\theta$. 6.5
Quadratic in $\sin\theta$ (or $\cos$, $\tan$) Equation of form $a\sin^2\theta+b\sin\theta+c=0$. Treat as $au^2+bu+c=0$ with $u=\sin\theta$; factorise; solve each linear sub-equation (each may give multiple $\theta$). 6.6
Mixed equation $a\sin\theta=b\cos\theta$ Divide by $\cos\theta$ (provided it's nonzero) to get $\tan\theta=b/a$. Easiest route. 6.4

4. Symmetry / reduction formulae (acute-angle reductions)

Let $\theta$ be acute. Then:

Identity Use
$\sin(-\theta)=-\sin\theta$, $\cos(-\theta)=\cos\theta$, $\tan(-\theta)=-\tan\theta$ Negative angle
$\sin(180°-\theta)=\sin\theta$, $\cos(180°-\theta)=-\cos\theta$, $\tan(180°-\theta)=-\tan\theta$ Q2 reduction
$\sin(180°+\theta)=-\sin\theta$, $\cos(180°+\theta)=-\cos\theta$, $\tan(180°+\theta)=\tan\theta$ Q3 reduction
$\sin(360°-\theta)=-\sin\theta$, $\cos(360°-\theta)=\cos\theta$, $\tan(360°-\theta)=-\tan\theta$ Q4 reduction
$\sin(\theta+360°k)=\sin\theta$, $\cos(\theta+360°k)=\cos\theta$, $\tan(\theta+180°k)=\tan\theta$ Period

5. The two essential identities

Identity Form Rearrangement
Pythagorean $\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta\equiv 1$ $\sin^2\theta\equiv 1-\cos^2\theta$; $\cos^2\theta\equiv 1-\sin^2\theta$
Quotient $\tan\theta\equiv\dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}$ $\sin\theta\equiv\tan\theta\cos\theta$; useful: divide $\sin=k\cos$ to get $\tan=k$

Derived: divide Pythagorean identity by $\cos^2\theta$ → $\tan^2\theta+1\equiv\sec^2\theta$ (P3, not in this chapter); by $\sin^2\theta$ → $1+\cot^2\theta\equiv\csc^2\theta$ (P3).

6. Method decision flow — solving trig equations

Equation form Strategy
$\sin\theta=k$ Calc principal $\theta_0=\sin^{-1}k$. In $[0,2\pi]$: solutions are $\theta_0$ and $\pi-\theta_0$ (mod $2\pi$). Add $2\pi k$ for other ranges
$\cos\theta=k$ Calc principal $\theta_0=\cos^{-1}k$. In $[0,2\pi]$: solutions are $\theta_0$ and $2\pi-\theta_0$
$\tan\theta=p$ Calc principal $\theta_0=\tan^{-1}p$. Solutions are $\theta_0+k\pi$ for integer $k$, lying in interval
$\sin n\theta=k$ Substitute $X=n\theta$, scale interval to $[0,n\cdot\text{upper}]$, solve $\sin X=k$, convert each $X$ back
$\sin(\theta+\alpha)=k$ Substitute $X=\theta+\alpha$, shift interval, solve $\sin X=k$, subtract $\alpha$ from each
$a\sin\theta=b\cos\theta$ Divide by $\cos\theta$: $\tan\theta=b/a$. Caveat: check $\cos\theta=0$ doesn't give a solution
$a\sin^2\theta+b\sin\theta+c=0$ Treat as quadratic in $\sin\theta$, factor or use formula, then solve each linear
Mixed $\sin^2,\cos^2$: $a\sin^2\theta+b\cos^2\theta=c$ Use $\cos^2=1-\sin^2$ (or vice versa) to reduce to one variable
$\sin\theta=\sin\alpha$ $\theta=\alpha$ or $\theta=\pi-\alpha$ (plus periodicity)
$\cos\theta=\cos\alpha$ $\theta=\alpha$ or $\theta=2\pi-\alpha$ (i.e., $\theta=-\alpha$ mod $2\pi$)
$\tan\theta=\tan\alpha$ $\theta=\alpha+k\pi$
$\tan^2\theta=k^2$ $\tan\theta=\pm k$ — solve both signs
Squaring a trig equation Caveat: introduces extraneous solutions. Always check candidates

8. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Stopping at the principal value Always check the interval: list ALL solutions, using symmetry/periodicity
Quoting $\sin^{-1}(k)$ for the second solution as $180°-\sin^{-1}(k)$ when sine is negative If $\sin\theta=k<0$, principal value is negative. The two solutions in $[0,2\pi]$ are $\pi-\theta_0$ and $2\pi+\theta_0$
For $\cos$: writing the second solution as $180°-\theta_0$ instead of $360°-\theta_0$ Cosine symmetry is about $\theta=180°$ in different sense — use $360°-\theta_0$ in $[0,360°]$
Forgetting to scale the interval when substituting $X=n\theta$ $0\le\theta\le 2\pi\Rightarrow 0\le X\le 2n\pi$. List all solutions, then divide each by $n$
Forgetting to shift the interval when substituting $X=\theta+\alpha$ $0\le\theta\le 2\pi\Rightarrow\alpha\le X\le 2\pi+\alpha$
Squaring an equation $\sin\theta=\sqrt 3\cos\theta$ to get $\sin^2=3\cos^2$ → introduces extraneous solutions of opposite sign Better: divide by $\cos\theta$ to get $\tan\theta=\sqrt 3$. If you must square, check each candidate in the original
Dividing by $\cos\theta$ without checking $\cos\theta=0$ If $\cos\theta=0$ is a possible solution (substitute and check), include it explicitly
Mixing degrees and radians Read the question's interval — match calculator MODE accordingly
In quadratic in $\sin\theta$, only solving one factor's equation Both factors give solutions: $(\sin\theta-a)(\sin\theta-b)=0$ → both $\sin\theta=a$ and $\sin\theta=b$
Quadratic in $\sin\theta$ has $\sin\theta=k>1$ — discarding is correct, but failing to discard can produce nonsense $\lvert\sin\theta\rvert\le 1$ and $\lvert\cos\theta\rvert\le 1$; reject any candidate outside $[-1,1]$
Forgetting endpoints in interval (e.g., $0\le\theta\le 360°$ vs $0<\theta<360°$) Read inequalities carefully — strict vs non-strict matters for boundary solutions
When using $\tan^{-1}$, missing solutions in Q3 (or Q1) Tan has period $\pi$ — every solution has another at $+\pi$ (mod the interval)
Confusing $\sin^{-1}$ with $1/\sin$ $\sin^{-1}$ = inverse function (arcsin). $(\sin\theta)^{-1}=\csc\theta=1/\sin\theta$
Computing $\cos 30°$ as $\tfrac12$ (it's $\tfrac{\sqrt 3}{2}$ — confused with sin) Memorise: $\sin$ ascending $\tfrac12,\tfrac{\sqrt 2}{2},\tfrac{\sqrt 3}{2}$ for $30,45,60$; cos is the reverse

9. Big picture (30-second mental model)

The unit circle is the unifying definition: $(\cos\theta,\sin\theta)$ are the coordinates on the circle at angle $\theta$ from the positive $x$-axis. From this:

  • $\sin^2+\cos^2\equiv 1$ is just $x^2+y^2=1$.
  • $\tan\theta=y/x$ is the gradient of $OP$.
  • Sign by quadrant is just sign of coordinates: Q1 (++), Q2 (−+), Q3 (−−), Q4 (+−). CAST records which ratio is positive.

Solving $\sin\theta=k$, $\cos\theta=k$, or $\tan\theta=p$ in an interval has a fixed recipe:

  1. Principal value from calculator.
  2. CAST diagram identifies sign-allowed quadrants.
  3. Symmetry within those quadrants gives all solutions:
    • Sine symmetric about $\theta=\pi/2$: second solution $\pi-\theta_0$.
    • Cosine symmetric about $\theta=\pi$: second solution $2\pi-\theta_0$ (equivalently $-\theta_0$).
    • Tan periodic with period $\pi$: solutions $\theta_0+k\pi$.
  4. Periodicity: shift by $2\pi$ (or $\pi$ for tan) to reach all solutions in interval.

For transformed equations ($\sin n\theta$, $\sin(\theta+\alpha)$): substitute, transform the interval, solve, then transform back.

For mixed equations involving sin AND cos: try divide-by-cos (yielding tan) or Pythagorean identity (eliminating one of sin/cos).

For quadratic in trig: factor as quadratic in the trig variable; then solve each linear case.

The chapter is essentially: (i) generalised definitions on the unit circle, (ii) the two identities, and (iii) one universal solving recipe applied with care to interval and signs.
