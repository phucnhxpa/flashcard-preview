P3 Ch 6 — Differentiation

The whole chapter in one mental model. P3 differentiation = library of standard derivatives ($\sin$, $\cos$, $e^{kx}$, $\ln x$, $a^{kx}$, $\tan$, $\sec$, $\csc$, $\cot$, inverse trig) + three composition rules (chain, product, quotient). Every problem is "spot the structure" → pick the right rule → grind. Always work in radians for trig.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Derivative The instantaneous rate of change of $y$ with respect to $x$, written $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}$ or $f'(x)$. Geometrically: gradient of the tangent line at a point. The chapter teaches you to find it for every function in the IAL library, not just polynomials. 6.1
First principles Computing the derivative directly from the limit $f'(x)=\lim_{h\to 0}\dfrac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$ instead of using a standard rule. Used in proofs that establish the rules; rarely in routine problems. 6.1 Ex 1
Small angle approximations When $x$ is in radians and small: $\sin x\approx x$, $\cos x\approx 1-\tfrac12 x^2$. The two limits $\dfrac{\sin h}{h}\to 1$ and $\dfrac{\cos h-1}{h}\to 0$ as $h\to 0$ are consequences of these — they're what makes the first-principles proofs of $(\sin x)'=\cos x$ work. 6.1
Standard result (in formula booklet) A derivative you don't have to derive — just quote it. P3 booklet gives $\tan,\csc,\sec,\cot$ derivatives and inverse trig derivatives. Worth memorising anyway for speed. 6.1–6.6
Chain rule $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}=\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}u}\cdot\dfrac{\mathrm{d}u}{\mathrm{d}x}$ — for composite functions $y=f(g(x))$. "Differentiate the outside, leave the inside, then multiply by the derivative of the inside." 6.3
Composite function A function inside another: $y=(3x+1)^5$, $y=\sin(2x^2)$, $y=e^{\cos x}$. Spot it: a function whose argument isn't just $x$. Triggers the chain rule, NOT the product rule. 6.3
Product rule $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(uv)=u\dfrac{\mathrm{d}v}{\mathrm{d}x}+v\dfrac{\mathrm{d}u}{\mathrm{d}x}$ — for two functions multiplied together: $y=x^2\sin x$, $y=e^x\ln x$. Spot it: two distinct factors, each a function of $x$. 6.4
Quotient rule $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}\!\left(\dfrac{u}{v}\right)=\dfrac{v\,u'-u\,v'}{v^2}$. Order matters — minus sign in the numerator means denominator's derivative comes second. Always usable but often: rewrite as a product with negative power and use product rule + chain rule instead, especially when $v$ is simple. 6.5
Inverse function differentiation Useful identity: $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}=\dfrac{1}{\,\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}y\,}$. Reverses the role of $x$ and $y$. The trick to deriving $(\arcsin x)'$, $(\arctan x)'$ etc. — write $x=\sin y$, differentiate, flip. 6.3, 6.6
Stationary point A point where $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}=0$ (zero gradient). Could be max, min, or point of inflection. Find by setting derivative to 0 and solving. Coordinates need both $x$ and $y$ — substitute $x$ back into original $y$. 6.1 Ex 3
Tangent / Normal At point $(a,b)$: tangent has gradient $f'(a)$, equation $y-b=f'(a)(x-a)$. Normal is perpendicular: gradient $-1/f'(a)$, equation $y-b=-\dfrac{1}{f'(a)}(x-a)$. 6.1 Ex 7
Implicit / inverse When you can't get $y=f(x)$ explicitly (e.g. $y^3+y=x$), or it's easier to write $x=g(y)$: differentiate w.r.t. $y$, then use $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}=\dfrac{1}{\,\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}y\,}$ to flip. 6.3 Ex 9
Reciprocal trig functions $\sec x=\dfrac{1}{\cos x}$, $\csc x=\dfrac{1}{\sin x}$, $\cot x=\dfrac{\cos x}{\sin x}$. Their derivatives all share a sign-pattern: $\sec\to+\sec\tan$, $\csc\to-\csc\cot$, $\cot\to-\csc^2$. The minus signs go with the "co-" functions (csc, cot — both "co-functions"). 6.6
Inverse trig functions $\arcsin x$, $\arccos x$, $\arctan x$ — defined on restricted ranges so they're invertible. Their derivatives are algebraic (no trig), which is why they appear in integrals like $\int\dfrac{1}{1+x^2}\mathrm{d}x=\arctan x+C$. 6.6
Radians (mandatory for calculus) The standard derivative $(\sin x)'=\cos x$ is only true when $x$ is in radians. If a question gives degrees, convert first ($x°=\tfrac{\pi}{180}x$ rad) — otherwise you'll be off by a factor of $\tfrac{\pi}{180}$. 6.1 Watch out
Logarithmic differentiation (implicit) When $y=a^{kx}$: take $\ln$ of both sides → $\ln y=kx\ln a$ → differentiate implicitly. Gives the rule $(a^{kx})'=a^{kx}\cdot k\ln a$. Generalises to messier products/powers. 6.2 Ex 4
Constant rule reminder The derivative of any constant ($k$, $\ln 7$, $e^2$) is $0$. Don't forget when differentiating expressions like $4\ln x+\ln 7$ — only $4\ln x$ contributes. 6.2 Ex 5

2. Standard derivatives library (memorise all)

This is the core toolkit. Every more complex problem reduces to applying these via chain/product/quotient.

Family $y$ $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}$ Notes
Trig $\sin kx$ $k\cos kx$ Radians only
$\cos kx$ $-k\sin kx$ Minus sign
$\tan kx$ $k\sec^2 kx$ Booklet
$\csc kx$ $-k\csc kx\cot kx$ Booklet; minus
$\sec kx$ $k\sec kx\tan kx$ Booklet; positive
$\cot kx$ $-k\csc^2 kx$ Booklet; minus
Exponential $e^{kx}$ $k e^{kx}$ Self-reproducing
$a^{kx}$ ($a>0$) $a^{kx}\cdot k\ln a$ Extra $\ln a$ factor
Logarithm $\ln x$ $\dfrac{1}{x}$ $x>0$
$\ln(kx)$ $\dfrac{1}{x}$ $\ln k$ is constant
Inverse trig $\arcsin x$ $\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}$ $|x|<1$
$\arccos x$ $-\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}$ Same magnitude, opposite sign
$\arctan x$ $\dfrac{1}{1+x^2}$ All real $x$
Power $x^n$ $nx^{n-1}$ Any real $n$

Sign mnemonic for trig: "co-" functions ($\cos$, $\csc$, $\cot$, $\arccos$) all carry a minus. Non-"co-" functions are positive.

3. The three rules — when to use which

Rule Trigger pattern Formula Watch-out
Chain Function inside function: $f(g(x))$, e.g. $\sin(2x)$, $(3x^2+1)^5$, $e^{\cos x}$, $\ln(\sec x)$ $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}=\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}u}\cdot\dfrac{\mathrm{d}u}{\mathrm{d}x}$ where $u=g(x)$ Don't forget the inner derivative
Product Two distinct functions multiplied: $y=u\cdot v$, e.g. $x^2\sin x$, $e^x\ln x$, $(2x+1)^3 e^{4x}$ $u'v+uv'$ (or $u\,v'+v\,u'$) Often need chain rule inside product
Quotient Fraction: $y=\dfrac{u}{v}$, e.g. $\dfrac{\sin x}{x}$, $\dfrac{e^{2x}}{x^2-1}$ $\dfrac{v\,u'-u\,v'}{v^2}$ Order: $v\,u'$ FIRST. Often easier as product with $v^{-1}$
Reciprocal of inverse $y$ defined implicitly via $x=g(y)$ $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}=\dfrac{1}{\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}y}$ Final answer in terms of $y$ — substitute back if needed

Rule of thumb: Spot outermost structure first. $y=\dfrac{e^{2x}\sin x}{1+x^2}$ → outermost is quotient, numerator is a product, $e^{2x}$ inside the product is a chain. Decompose top-down.

4. Worked archetypes — pattern → method → result

# Function Pattern Method $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}$
1 $y=\sin 4x$ Trig of $kx$ Standard $4\cos 4x$
2 $y=(3x^2+1)^5$ Power of polynomial Chain ($u=3x^2+1$) $5(3x^2+1)^4\cdot 6x=30x(3x^2+1)^4$

4 $y=e^{\cos x}$ Exp of trig Chain ($u=\cos x$) $-\sin x\cdot e^{\cos x}$

6 $y=\dfrac{x}{2x+5}$ Linear ÷ linear Quotient $\dfrac{5}{(2x+5)^2}$

Fraction with $x$ on top and bottom Quotient (or rewrite as product) Both vary
$\sin($ anything other than $x$ or $kx$ $)$ Chain Composite trig
$e^{(\text{anything})}$ where exponent has $x$ Chain Composite exp
$\ln($ anything $)$ Chain + $(\ln u)'=u'/u$ Composite log

$\arcsin$, $\arccos$, $\arctan$ of anything other than $x$ Chain on inverse trig Composite

6. Stationary points and tangents — workflow

Step Action
1 Differentiate $y$ with respect to $x$ (apply rules)
2 Set $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}=0$
3 Solve the resulting equation (often trig — work in radians, find all solutions in given interval)
4 For each $x$-value, substitute back into original $y(x)$ to get $y$-coordinate
5 (If asked) Use $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}^2y}{\mathrm{d}x^2}$ to classify: $>0$ minimum, $<0$ maximum, $=0$ inconclusive
6 For tangent at $(a, b)$: $y-b=f'(a)(x-a)$. Normal: replace $f'(a)$ with $-1/f'(a)$

7. Common pitfalls

Mistake What goes wrong Fix
Differentiating in degrees $(\sin x)'\neq\cos x$ in degrees Always work in radians
Forgetting inner derivative (chain rule) $(\sin 2x)'\to\cos 2x$ instead of $2\cos 2x$ After "differentiate outer", multiply by inner derivative
Confusing product vs chain $x^2\sin x$ treated as composite Two distinct $x$-factors → product; one inside the other → chain
Quotient rule sign error $u'v - uv'$ written as $u'v+uv'$ Memorise: "denominator times derivative of numerator minus numerator times derivative of denominator"
Forgetting that $\ln k$ is a constant $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(\ln 7x)$ written as $\dfrac{7}{x}$ $\ln 7x=\ln 7+\ln x\Rightarrow\dfrac{1}{x}$
Wrong sign on co-trig derivatives $(\cos x)' = +\sin x$, $(\csc x)' = +\csc\cot$ "**Co-**functions ($\cos,\csc,\cot,\arccos$) get a minus"
Substituting $x$-coord into derivative for $y$-coord of stationary point Returns $0$ (the gradient), not the height Substitute $x$ back into original $y(x)$
Not including coefficient $k$ on inner derivative $(\sin 4x)'\to\cos 4x$ instead of $4\cos 4x$ The $k$ from $\sin kx\to k\cos kx$ is the chain inner derivative in disguise
Using $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}=\dfrac{1}{\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}y}$ but forgetting to convert back to $x$ Answer left in $y$, examiner wants $x$ Use the original relation to substitute $y\to f^{-1}(x)$
In $\arcsin u$, forgetting chain rule Drops factor $u'$ $(\arcsin u)'=\dfrac{u'}{\sqrt{1-u^2}}$

8. Big picture (30-second mental model)

P3 differentiation is library lookup + composition:

  1. Library: memorise the 14 standard derivatives (§2). These are non-negotiable.
  2. Three composition rules: chain (composite), product (×), quotient (÷). Pick by spotting outer structure.
  3. Workflow for any messy function: identify outer structure → apply the matching rule → recurse on inner pieces → simplify.
  4. For inverse / implicit: use $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}=\dfrac{1}{\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}y}$.
  5. Stationary points: set $f'(x)=0$, solve in radians, sub $x$ back into original $y$ for the $y$-coordinate.
  6. Always radians for trig calculus. "Co-" trig derivatives carry a minus.

If you can recite the library + spot the right rule in 5 seconds, every P3 differentiation question is mechanical.
