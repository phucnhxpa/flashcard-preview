P2 Ch 7 — Differentiation: Applications

The whole chapter in one mental model. Building on P1 Ch 8 (the rules of differentiation), this chapter is about what the derivative tells you:

  • Sign of $\mathrm{f}'(x)$ tells you whether $\mathrm{f}$ is increasing ($\ge 0$) or decreasing ($\le 0$) on an interval.
  • $\mathrm{f}'(x)=0$ locates stationary points (max, min, or point of inflection).
  • Sign of $\mathrm{f}''(x)$ at a stationary point classifies it: $\mathrm{f}''(a)>0$ → minimum, $\mathrm{f}''(a)<0$ → maximum, $\mathrm{f}''(a)=0$ → inconclusive (use first-derivative test on either side).
  • Modelling: real-world rates of change are derivatives. Optimisation problems are find-and-classify-the-stationary-point problems in disguise: build a one-variable function, differentiate, set $=0$, solve, classify, interpret.
  • Graph sketching of $y=\mathrm{f}'(x)$ from $y=\mathrm{f}(x)$: maxima/minima → $x$-intercepts; positive gradient → above $x$-axis; etc.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Derivative $\mathrm{f}'(x)$ The function giving the gradient (slope) of $y=\mathrm{f}(x)$ at each $x$. From P1 Ch8: $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}x^n=nx^{n-1}$, sums and constant-multiples extend term-by-term. P1 Ch8
Increasing function $\mathrm{f}$ is increasing on $[a,b]$ if $\mathrm{f}'(x)\ge 0$ for all $x\in(a,b)$. Strictly increasing if $\mathrm{f}'(x)>0$ throughout. Geometrically: graph rises as $x$ increases. 7.1
Decreasing function $\mathrm{f}$ is decreasing on $[a,b]$ if $\mathrm{f}'(x)\le 0$ for all $x\in(a,b)$. Strictly decreasing if $<0$. Graph falls as $x$ increases. 7.1
Interval $[a,b]$ The set of all real numbers $x$ with $a\le x\le b$. Square brackets = endpoints included; round brackets $(a,b)$ = excluded. 7.1
Stationary point A point on $y=\mathrm{f}(x)$ where $\mathrm{f}'(x)=0$ — the curve has zero gradient (horizontal tangent). Three types: local max, local min, point of inflection. 7.2
Local maximum A stationary point where the curve goes from increasing (gradient +) to decreasing (gradient −). The $y$-value is the highest in its immediate neighbourhood (not necessarily globally). Often called "max", "turning point". 7.2
Local minimum A stationary point where the curve goes from decreasing to increasing. Lowest $y$-value in its neighbourhood. 7.2
Point of inflection (stationary) A stationary point where the gradient does not change sign — same sign before and after. Curve flattens out then continues in the same direction. NOT all points of inflection are stationary; non-stationary inflections also exist (where $\mathrm{f}''=0$ but $\mathrm{f}'\ne 0$). 7.2
Turning point Synonym for local max or local min — the gradient turns. Excludes points of inflection. 7.2
Second derivative $\mathrm{f}''(x)$ or $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}^2 y}{\mathrm{d}x^2}$ Differentiate $\mathrm{f}'(x)$ again. Measures the rate of change of the gradient — concavity. Positive: concave up (cup); negative: concave down (cap). 7.2
Second-derivative test At a stationary point $x=a$: $\mathrm{f}''(a)>0\Rightarrow$ local min; $\mathrm{f}''(a)<0\Rightarrow$ local max; $\mathrm{f}''(a)=0\Rightarrow$ inconclusive (could be any of three — use first-derivative test). 7.2
First-derivative test Check sign of $\mathrm{f}'$ at $x=a-h$ and $x=a+h$ (small positive $h$). $+ \to 0 \to -$ = local max; $- \to 0 \to +$ = local min; same sign both sides = point of inflection. 7.2
Gradient function The graph of $y=\mathrm{f}'(x)$. Tells you the gradient of $\mathrm{f}$ at each point. Maxima/minima of $\mathrm{f}$ appear as zeros (x-intercepts) of $\mathrm{f}'$. 7.3
Rate of change The derivative $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}$ interpreted as "how fast does $y$ change with respect to $x$". Units: ($y$-units) per ($x$-unit). $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}V}{\mathrm{d}t}$ = volume per time. 7.4
Optimisation Maximising or minimising a quantity subject to a constraint. Strategy: express the quantity in one variable, differentiate, set derivative to zero, solve, classify (using second derivative or first-derivative test), interpret. 7.4
Constraint A relationship between variables (e.g., perimeter = 80) used to eliminate variables until the function depends on one variable. 7.4
Maximum value (of function) The value of $\mathrm{f}$ at a local max — the largest $y$ attained in the interval. Globally maximum or just locally — read carefully. 7.4
Cuboid / cylinder volume formulas Cuboid: $V=lwh$; cylinder: $V=\pi r^2 h$; sphere: $V=\tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3$, surface $4\pi r^2$. Used regularly in optimisation problems. 7.4
Asymptote (behaviour at infinity) A line the graph approaches but never meets. Vertical: domain excludes $x$ where denominator = 0. Horizontal: $y$-value as $x\to\pm\infty$. The gradient function shares vertical asymptotes; horizontal asymptotes of $\mathrm{f}$ become $y=0$ asymptotes of $\mathrm{f}'$. 7.3
Continuity A curve with no jumps. All polynomials are continuous everywhere; rational functions are continuous except at vertical asymptotes. Required for sketching $\mathrm{f}'$ smoothly. 7.3

2. Increasing / decreasing — decision flow

Information Conclusion
$\mathrm{f}'(x)\ge 0$ on $(a,b)$ $\mathrm{f}$ is increasing on $[a,b]$
$\mathrm{f}'(x)>0$ on $(a,b)$ $\mathrm{f}$ is strictly increasing on $[a,b]$
$\mathrm{f}'(x)\le 0$ on $(a,b)$ $\mathrm{f}$ is decreasing on $[a,b]$
$\mathrm{f}'(x)<0$ on $(a,b)$ $\mathrm{f}$ is strictly decreasing on $[a,b]$
$\mathrm{f}'(x)=0$ at isolated $x=a$ Stationary point (could be max, min, or inflection)

Method to find the interval where $\mathrm{f}$ increases/decreases:

  1. Compute $\mathrm{f}'(x)$.
  2. Find roots of $\mathrm{f}'(x)=0$ (boundary points).
  3. Test sign of $\mathrm{f}'$ in each resulting interval (substitute a sample point or use factorisation).
  4. Combine: intervals with $\mathrm{f}'\ge 0$ → increasing; intervals with $\mathrm{f}'\le 0$ → decreasing.

3. Classifying stationary points — cross-table

Type $\mathrm{f}'(a-h)$ $\mathrm{f}'(a)$ $\mathrm{f}'(a+h)$ $\mathrm{f}''(a)$
Local max $+$ $0$ $-$ $<0$
Local min $-$ $0$ $+$ $>0$
Stationary inflection $-$ $0$ $-$ $0$ (sometimes)
Stationary inflection $+$ $0$ $+$ $0$ (sometimes)

Notes:

  • Second-derivative test is faster when $\mathrm{f}''(a)\ne 0$.
  • If $\mathrm{f}''(a)=0$, fall back to first-derivative test (check signs on either side).
  • "Stationary point of inflection" requires $\mathrm{f}'(a)=0$ AND no sign change of $\mathrm{f}'$.

4. Method recipe — finding & classifying stationary points

Step Action
1 Compute $\mathrm{f}'(x)$
2 Solve $\mathrm{f}'(x)=0$ for $x$
3 Substitute each $x$ back into $\mathrm{f}(x)$ to get the $y$-coordinate
4 Classify: compute $\mathrm{f}''(x)$ at each stationary $x$
5 If $\mathrm{f}''(a)>0$: local min; if $<0$: local max; if $=0$: use first-derivative test
6 (For sketching) determine asymptotes, behaviour as $x\to\pm\infty$, and intercepts
7 State coordinates and nature clearly

5. Sketching the gradient function $y=\mathrm{f}'(x)$ from $y=\mathrm{f}(x)$

Feature of $\mathrm{f}(x)$ Corresponding feature of $\mathrm{f}'(x)$
Local max or local min $x$-intercept of $\mathrm{f}'$ (gradient = 0 there)
Stationary point of inflection $\mathrm{f}'$ touches the $x$-axis (tangent to it)
$\mathrm{f}$ has positive gradient $\mathrm{f}'$ above $x$-axis
$\mathrm{f}$ has negative gradient $\mathrm{f}'$ below $x$-axis
Vertical asymptote of $\mathrm{f}$ Vertical asymptote of $\mathrm{f}'$ at same $x$
Horizontal asymptote of $\mathrm{f}$ ($y=k$) Horizontal asymptote of $\mathrm{f}'$ at $y=0$
Linear $\mathrm{f}$ (constant gradient) $\mathrm{f}'$ is a horizontal line
Quadratic $\mathrm{f}$ (one turning point) $\mathrm{f}'$ is linear (one $x$-intercept)
Cubic $\mathrm{f}$ (two turning points) $\mathrm{f}'$ is quadratic (parabola, two $x$-intercepts)
Quartic $\mathrm{f}$ (up to three turning points) $\mathrm{f}'$ is cubic
Note: $x$-intercepts of $\mathrm{f}$ are NOT generally significant for $\mathrm{f}'$ Different curves entirely

6. Optimisation problems — universal recipe

Step Action
1 Identify the objective (what to maximise/minimise: area, volume, perimeter, …)
2 Express the objective $Q$ in terms of the relevant variables (often two: e.g., $V=x^2 y$)
3 Use the constraint (given relationship: e.g., $54=2x^2+3xy$) to eliminate one variable, solving for it: $y=\dfrac{54-2x^2}{3x}$
4 Substitute back to express $Q$ as a function of one variable: $V(x)=18x-\tfrac{2}{3}x^3$
5 Differentiate: $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}Q}{\mathrm{d}x}=18-2x^2$
6 Set $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}Q}{\mathrm{d}x}=0$, solve for $x$ (reject negative or invalid roots based on context)
7 Classify with $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}^2 Q}{\mathrm{d}x^2}$ at the critical $x$: if max wanted, want $<0$; if min, $>0$
8 Substitute back to find the optimal value of $Q$
9 Always state units in real-world problems

8. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Forgetting to compute $y$-coordinate of stationary point — only finding $x$ Always substitute $x$-value back into the original $\mathrm{f}(x)$, not into $\mathrm{f}'(x)$
Using $\mathrm{f}'(x)$ to test concavity instead of $\mathrm{f}''(x)$ Concavity comes from second derivative; sign of $\mathrm{f}'$ tells about increasing/decreasing
Using second-derivative test when $\mathrm{f}''(a)=0$ and concluding "inflection" $\mathrm{f}''(a)=0$ is inconclusive — fallback to first-derivative test on either side
Confusing "point of inflection" with "stationary point" Not all inflections are stationary. Stationary inflection: $\mathrm{f}'=0$ AND no sign change. General inflection: just $\mathrm{f}''=0$ with sign change of $\mathrm{f}''$
In optimisation, forgetting to check the answer is a max (when min wanted) or vice versa Always classify with $\mathrm{f}''$ or sign test
Forgetting the constraint exists — trying to optimise a 2-variable function Use constraint to reduce to one variable first
Reject-or-keep a root: e.g. $x^2=9\Rightarrow x=\pm 3$, but $x$ is a length so $x=3$ Use physical context (lengths > 0, probabilities $\in[0,1]$, etc.)
Forgetting units in modelling answers Volume = m³, area = m², rate = m/s, etc.
Reading interval as strict vs non-strict "Increasing on $[a,b]$" = $\mathrm{f}'\ge 0$ on $(a,b)$. Edge cases of equality OK
Incorrect derivative for fractional/negative powers: $\dfrac{1}{x}=x^{-1}$, derivative $-x^{-2}$ Rewrite as power then apply $nx^{n-1}$
Skipping check that $\mathrm{f}''(x)$ at $x=a$ is consistent with type Once you've classified, ask: does the curve shape make sense?
For sketching $\mathrm{f}'$: drawing a curve through the $\mathrm{f}$'s $x$-intercepts Those don't matter for $\mathrm{f}'$. Only $\mathrm{f}$'s stationary points become $\mathrm{f}'$'s $x$-intercepts
Forgetting that "minimum" can be a maximum's negative — e.g. find minimum of $\mathrm{f}$ vs maximum of $-\mathrm{f}$ Same critical points; flip sign of values
Differentiating $\dfrac{1}{x}$ as $\dfrac{1}{x^2}$ instead of $-\dfrac{1}{x^2}$ Sign matters: $\dfrac{d}{dx}x^{-1}=-x^{-2}$

9. Big picture (30-second mental model)

The derivative $\mathrm{f}'(x)$ tells you the slope at each $x$; its sign determines monotonicity (increasing/decreasing). The places where $\mathrm{f}'(x)=0$ are stationary points — candidates for max/min/inflection. To classify: either look at the sign of $\mathrm{f}'$ on either side (first-derivative test) or compute $\mathrm{f}''$ at the stationary point (second-derivative test).

For real-world optimisation: identify what to optimise (the objective); use the constraint to reduce to one variable; differentiate; set $=0$; solve; classify; interpret with units.

For sketching $\mathrm{f}'$ from $\mathrm{f}$: stationary points become $x$-intercepts; positive gradient regions become "above $x$-axis"; concavity changes (inflection) become turning points of $\mathrm{f}'$.

This entire chapter is applied differentiation — extracting geometric/physical meaning from $\mathrm{f}'$ and $\mathrm{f}''$. P3 will extend the toolkit to $\mathrm{e}^x$, $\ln x$, $\sin x$, $\cos x$ and add chain/product/quotient rules; the interpretation (sign analysis, stationary points, optimisation) stays identical.
