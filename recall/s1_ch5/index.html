S1 Ch 5 — Correlation and Regression

The whole chapter in one mental model. Given bivariate data $(x,y)$, three statistics summarise the linear relationship: the gradient $b=S_{xy}/S_{xx}$ of the least-squares regression line $y=a+bx$ (with $a=\bar y - b\bar x$), and the product-moment correlation coefficient $r=S_{xy}/\sqrt{S_{xx}S_{yy}}$ which measures the strength of the linear fit (-1 to +1). Coding rescales the variables; the regression coefficients change predictably, but $r$ is invariant. Predict only inside the data range (interpolation); extrapolation is unreliable.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Bivariate data Data consisting of paired observations $(x_i,y_i)$ on two variables — e.g. (height, weight) for each person. Each individual contributes one point to a scatter diagram. 5.1
Scatter diagram A 2-D plot with one cross per data point: independent variable on $x$-axis, dependent on $y$-axis. Reveals shape (linear or non-linear), strength of correlation, and outliers. 5.1
Independent / explanatory variable The variable the experimenter controls or that causes — plotted on the $x$-axis. The "input." 5.1
Dependent / response variable The variable that responds or is measured — plotted on the $y$-axis. The "output." 5.1
Correlation The strength and direction of a linear relationship. Positive: $y$ rises with $x$. Negative: $y$ falls with $x$. No (linear) correlation: points scatter randomly — but a non-linear pattern may still exist. 5.1
Causal relationship When $x$ truly causes $y$. Correlation alone does not prove causation — confounding variables can produce spurious correlations. Always check the context. 5.1
Line of best fit / Regression line A straight line passing through the data, used as a linear model. The least-squares regression line of $y$ on $x$ minimises $\sum\varepsilon_i^2$ where $\varepsilon_i$ is the vertical distance from $(x_i,y_i)$ to the line. 5.2 / 5.3
Residual $\varepsilon_i$ The vertical gap between an observed $y_i$ and the predicted $\hat y_i$ from the regression line: $\varepsilon_i = y_i - \hat y_i$. Can be positive (point above line) or negative (below). The regression line minimises $\sum\varepsilon_i^2$. 5.3
Regression line of $y$ on $x$ The line $y=a+bx$ with $b=\dfrac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}}$ and $a=\bar y - b\bar x$. Used to predict $y$ from $x$. Different from the regression line of $x$ on $y$. 5.3
Summary statistics $S_{xx},S_{yy},S_{xy}$ Convenient sums: $S_{xx}=\sum x^2-\dfrac{(\sum x)^2}{n}$, $S_{yy}=\sum y^2-\dfrac{(\sum y)^2}{n}$, $S_{xy}=\sum xy-\dfrac{\sum x\sum y}{n}$. They are the building blocks for both the regression line and $r$. 5.3
Interpolation Predicting a $y$ for an $x$ that lies inside the range of observed $x$-values. Generally reliable provided the linear model fits. 5.2
Extrapolation Predicting a $y$ for an $x$ outside the range. Unreliable — the linear pattern may not extend. Always flag this as a limitation. 5.2
Product moment correlation coefficient (PMCC), $r$ $r=\dfrac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx}S_{yy}}}\in[-1,1]$. Measures linear correlation strength. +1: perfect positive line. -1: perfect negative line. 0: no linear relationship (still possibly non-linear). 5.4
Coding (linear) A change of scale $p = \dfrac{x-a}{b}$ (and similar for $y$) to keep numbers small. Affects the regression coefficients (you must decode), but does not affect $r$. 5.3 / 5.4
Bivariate normal distribution The theoretical assumption underlying $r$: both variables, when considered separately, are normally distributed. PMCC is technically valid only under this. 5.4
Interpretation of $b$ "For every unit increase in $x$, $y$ changes by $b$." Always state in context with units. 5.2
Interpretation of $a$ "When $x=0$, the predicted $y$ is $a$." Often unrealistic if $x=0$ is far outside the data — flag this. 5.2

2. Summary statistics — formulae cheat sheet

$$S_{xx}=\sum (x-\bar x)^2 = \sum x^2 - \frac{(\sum x)^2}{n}$$ $$S_{yy}=\sum (y-\bar y)^2 = \sum y^2 - \frac{(\sum y)^2}{n}$$ $$S_{xy}=\sum (x-\bar x)(y-\bar y) = \sum xy - \frac{\sum x\sum y}{n}$$

Quantity Use
$S_{xx}$ Denominator of regression slope; spread of $x$
$S_{yy}$ Denominator of $r$ alongside $S_{xx}$; spread of $y$
$S_{xy}$ Numerator for both slope and $r$; covariance of $x,y$
$\bar x = \dfrac{\sum x}{n}$, $\bar y = \dfrac{\sum y}{n}$ Means used in regression intercept

Sign of $S_{xy}$: matches the direction of correlation — positive ⇒ positive correlation, negative ⇒ negative.

3. Least squares regression — building blocks

Step Formula Note
1 $\bar x = \sum x/n$ and $\bar y = \sum y/n$ Compute means first
2 $S_{xx} = \sum x^2 - (\sum x)^2/n$ "Sum of squares" of $x$ deviations
3 $S_{xy} = \sum xy - (\sum x)(\sum y)/n$ Cross-product sum of deviations
4 Slope $b = \dfrac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}}$ The "rise per unit run"
5 Intercept $a = \bar y - b\bar x$ Forces the line through $(\bar x, \bar y)$
6 Regression equation: $y=a+bx$ Predict $y$ for new $x$ via plugging in

Interpretation template:

  • $b$: "An increase of 1 [unit] in $x$ is associated with a change of $b$ [unit] in $y$, on average."
  • $a$: "When $x=0$, the predicted $y$ is $a$ [unit]." (often unrealistic; mention if outside data range)

4. Product moment correlation coefficient $r$

Property Detail
Formula $r = \dfrac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx}\,S_{yy}}}$
Range $-1 \le r \le 1$
$r=+1$ Perfect positive linear: all points on a line with positive gradient
$r=-1$ Perfect negative linear: line with negative gradient
$r=0$ No linear correlation; data may still follow a curve
$|r|>0.9$ Strong correlation
$0.5<|r|<0.9$ Moderate
$|r|<0.5$ Weak
Sign Same as $S_{xy}$, same as slope $b$
Affected by linear coding? No — $r$ is invariant
Affected by swapping $x,y$? No — symmetric in $x,y$

5. Coding — how each statistic transforms

For coding $p=\dfrac{x-a_1}{b_1}$ and $q=\dfrac{y-a_2}{b_2}$:

Statistic Coded ↔ Original
$\bar x$ $\bar p=\dfrac{\bar x - a_1}{b_1}$, so $\bar x = b_1\bar p + a_1$
$\bar y$ similarly
$S_{xx}$ $S_{pp}=\dfrac{S_{xx}}{b_1^2}$, so $S_{xx}=b_1^2 S_{pp}$
$S_{yy}$ $S_{qq}=\dfrac{S_{yy}}{b_2^2}$
$S_{xy}$ $S_{pq}=\dfrac{S_{xy}}{b_1 b_2}$
Slope $b$ $b_{q\,\text{on}\,p}=\dfrac{S_{pq}}{S_{pp}}=\dfrac{b_1}{b_2}\cdot \dfrac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}}$
PMCC $r$ Unchanged — $r_{pq}=r_{xy}$
Regression line Substitute $p,q$ into the coded equation, then rearrange to get $y$ in terms of $x$

Procedure to decode a regression line:

  1. Write the coded line, e.g. $q = \alpha + \beta p$.
  2. Substitute $p=\dfrac{x-a_1}{b_1},\; q=\dfrac{y-a_2}{b_2}$.
  3. Expand and rearrange into $y = A + Bx$.

6. Decision flow — what's being asked?

Spot this Do this
"Describe the correlation" Look at $r$ (or scatter diagram): direction (sign) + strength (magnitude); state in context
"Interpret the gradient/intercept" Sentence in context with units, referring back to $x$ and $y$
"Find the regression line" Compute $S_{xx},S_{xy},\bar x,\bar y$ → $b=S_{xy}/S_{xx}$ → $a=\bar y - b\bar x$
"Predict $y$ when $x=...$" Plug into $y=a+bx$; check whether $x$ is inside data range (interpolation = OK, extrapolation = flag)
"Predict $x$ when $y=...$" Don't use $y$-on-$x$ line! Need regression of $x$ on $y$
"Find PMCC" $r=\dfrac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx}S_{yy}}}$
"Coded data given" Compute everything in coded variables; use coding rules to decode if asked
"Comment on causation" Correlation ≠ causation; identify possible confounders, sample issues

7. Worked archetypes

# Setup Steps
1 $n=5$; $\sum x=300,\sum x^2=22000,\sum y=288.6,\sum xy=18238$. Find regression line. $S_{xx}=22000-300^2/5=4000$; $S_{xy}=18238-300(288.6)/5=922$; $b=922/4000=0.2305$; $\bar x=60,\bar y=57.72$ → $a=57.72-0.2305(60)=43.89$. $y=43.89+0.2305x$
2 $r$ with $\sum x=176.9,\sum y=679,\sum x^2=2576.47,\sum y^2=39771,\sum xy=9915.3$, $n=15$. $S_{xx}=490.23$; $S_{yy}=9034.93$; $S_{xy}=1907.63$; $r=1907.63/\sqrt{490.23\cdot 9034.93}=0.906$
3 Coding: $x=10c,\ y=(m-700)/5$. Coded line: $y=36.2-4.05x$. Decode for $m,c$. Substitute: $(m-700)/5 = 36.2 - 4.05(10c)$ ⇒ $m=881.07-202.4c$
4 $r$ unchanged under coding. Coded $r_{AB}=0.973$. Find $r_{xy}$. $r_{xy}=0.973$ (invariant)
5 Predict $y$ at $x=58$ where data range is $20\le x\le 100$. Interpolation; reliable. Plug in: $y=43.89+0.2305(58)=57.3$
6 Predict $y$ at $x=130$ where data range is $20\le x\le 100$. Extrapolation; flag as unreliable

8. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Using regression line of $y$ on $x$ to predict $x$ from $y$ Need a separate regression line of $x$ on $y$ — coefficients differ
Confusing causation with correlation $r$ near $\pm 1$ shows linear association, never that one variable causes the other
Forgetting that $r$ is unchanged under linear coding If you computed $r$ in coded variables, that is $r$ for the original variables
Stating "perfect" correlation when $r=0.99$ Reserve "perfect" for $r=\pm 1$ exactly; otherwise "very strong"
Forgetting to check interpolation vs extrapolation Always quote the data range $x_{\min}\le x\le x_{\max}$ and check
Quoting interpretation without units / context "$b=0.5$" is wrong — say "an extra gram of supplement increases shell hardness by 0.5 units"
Using formula $r=S_{xy}/\sqrt{S_{xx}+S_{yy}}$ The denominator is $\sqrt{S_{xx}\cdot S_{yy}}$ (product, not sum)
Computing $a$ before $b$ Order matters: $b=S_{xy}/S_{xx}$ first, then $a=\bar y - b\bar x$
Dropping decimal places mid-calculation Keep at least 4 s.f. throughout; round only the final answer (3 s.f. typical)
Saying "the regression line shows that $x$ causes $y$" Regression assumes a model — does not establish causality

9. Big picture (30-second mental model)

Bivariate analysis = three numbers that summarise a linear relationship:

  1. $S_{xx}, S_{yy}, S_{xy}$ — three "centred sums of products" computed from the data.
  2. Regression line $y=a+bx$: $b=S_{xy}/S_{xx}$ (slope), $a=\bar y - b\bar x$ (forces line through centroid). Use it to predict $y$ from $x$ inside the data range.
  3. PMCC $r=S_{xy}/\sqrt{S_{xx}S_{yy}}\in[-1,1]$: measures strength of linear fit. Same sign as slope $b$. Invariant under linear coding.
  4. Coding simplifies arithmetic: rescales $\bar x, \bar y, S_{xx}$ etc. predictably; decode by substituting back.
  5. Two warnings: correlation does not imply causation; and predictions outside the data range (extrapolation) are unreliable.

Three sums, two formulas, one invariant — that's the chapter.
