FP1 Ch 7 â€” Series

The whole chapter in one mental model. Every finite-sum question in FP1 reduces to four standard formulas for $\sum_{r=1}^n 1$, $\sum_{r=1}^n r$, $\sum_{r=1}^n r^2$, $\sum_{r=1}^n r^3$. Use linearity ($\sum kf(r)=k\sum f(r)$, $\sum (f+g)=\sum f+\sum g$) to break a polynomial sum into pieces, plug the formulas in, and factor aggressively to land on the target form. Sums not starting at $r=1$ become differences: $\sum_{r=k}^n=\sum_{r=1}^n-\sum_{r=1}^{k-1}$.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Sequence An ordered list of numbers, e.g. $1,4,9,16,\dots$. The $r$-th term is some function of $r$, e.g. $a_r=r^2$. (Series, by contrast, is the sum of a sequence.) 7.1
Series The sum of the terms of a sequence. Finite series have a last term; infinite series may or may not converge. In FP1, all series are finite. Notation: $\sum_{r=1}^n a_r=a_1+a_2+\cdots+a_n$. 7.1
Sigma notation $\sum_{r=1}^n f(r)$ Compact way to write $f(1)+f(2)+\cdots+f(n)$. The variable $r$ is the dummy index, $1$ is the lower limit, $n$ the upper limit. Number of terms = upper $-$ lower $+1$. The dummy can be any letter; you can shift indices. 7.1
Dummy index / index variable The $r$ in $\sum_{r=1}^n$. It varies through integer values from lower to upper limit, taking every integer in between. The choice of letter doesn't matter: $\sum_{r=1}^n r=\sum_{k=1}^n k$. 7.1
Natural numbers $\mathbb{N}$ Positive integers $1,2,3,\dots$ (in this textbook's convention). The "first $n$ natural numbers" are $1,2,\dots,n$. 7.1
Standard result $\sum 1=n$ Adding 1 a total of $n$ times. Trivial but essential â€” used whenever a sum has a constant term, e.g. $\sum (3r+5)=3\sum r+5\sum 1=3\cdot\tfrac{n(n+1)}{2}+5n$. 7.1
Standard result $\sum r$ $\sum_{r=1}^n r=\tfrac{1}{2}n(n+1)$. The triangular numbers. Famous Gauss-pairing proof: pair $1+n$, $2+(n-1)$, etc., each pair sums to $n+1$, total $\tfrac{n}{2}(n+1)$. 7.1
Standard result $\sum r^2$ $\sum_{r=1}^n r^2=\tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)(2n+1)$. The square pyramidal numbers. The product of three factors of consecutive sizes makes $n+1$ a useful common factor when combining with $\sum r$. 7.2
Standard result $\sum r^3$ $\sum_{r=1}^n r^3=\tfrac{1}{4}n^2(n+1)^2=\big(\sum_{r=1}^n r\big)^2$. The "sum of cubes equals square of sum of integers" is a beautiful identity. 7.2
Linearity of $\sum$ $\sum (af(r)+bg(r))=a\sum f(r)+b\sum g(r)$. Pull constants out, split sums of sums. The whole chapter relies on this rule. 7.1
Splitting a non-zero-start sum $\sum_{r=k}^n f(r)=\sum_{r=1}^n f(r)-\sum_{r=1}^{k-1} f(r)$. Important: subtract up to $k-1$, NOT $k$, otherwise you remove the term $f(k)$ which you wanted to keep. 7.1
"Show that" / "Hence" structure Common exam pattern: part (a) asks for a closed form $\sum_{r=1}^n f(r)=$ specific factored expression; part (b) "hence" â€” use part (a) to evaluate a different sum (e.g. $\sum_{r=20}^{50}$) by splitting. Always plug values into the part (a) formula in part (b), don't restart. 7.1 Ex 4
Factoring sigma results Final answer is normally given as $\tfrac{1}{c}n\cdot(\text{factored polynomial})$. Pull common factors $\tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)$ etc. out of partial sums, then expand the rest, factor the leftover quadratic. 7.2
Verifying with small $n$ After deriving a closed form, substitute $n=1,2,3$ to check. This doesn't prove correctness, but catches algebra slips. 7.2 Ex 6

2. The four standard sums (memorise EXACTLY)

Power $k$ $\sum_{r=1}^n r^k$ Closed form Factored form (use this)
$0$ (constants) $\sum_{r=1}^n 1$ $n$ $n$
$1$ $\sum_{r=1}^n r$ $\dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}$ $\tfrac{1}{2}n(n+1)$
$2$ $\sum_{r=1}^n r^2$ $\dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}$ $\tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)(2n+1)$
$3$ $\sum_{r=1}^n r^3$ $\left(\dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}\right)^{\!2}$ $\tfrac{1}{4}n^2(n+1)^2$

Useful observation: $\sum r^3=(\sum r)^2$. So if you remember $\sum r$, the cube sum is just its square.

3. The two operating rules â€” linearity

Rule Formula Use
Pull out constant $\sum_{r=1}^n k\,f(r)=k\sum_{r=1}^n f(r)$ Removes scalar multiples
Split sum $\sum_{r=1}^n (f(r)+g(r))=\sum_{r=1}^n f(r)+\sum_{r=1}^n g(r)$ Breaks polynomial into terms
Combined linearity $\sum (af(r)+bg(r))=a\sum f(r)+b\sum g(r)$ One step covers both
Constant subtraction $\sum (f(r)-c)=\sum f(r)-cn$ Constant term: subtract $cn$

Implication: Any polynomial sum $\sum_{r=1}^n (a_3 r^3+a_2 r^2+a_1 r+a_0)$ becomes $a_3\sum r^3+a_2\sum r^2+a_1\sum r+a_0 n$ â€” direct substitution.

4. Splitting non-standard ranges

Range Identity
$\sum_{r=k}^n$ $\sum_{r=1}^n - \sum_{r=1}^{k-1}$
$\sum_{r=n+1}^{2n}$ $\sum_{r=1}^{2n}-\sum_{r=1}^n$ (very common in exam)
$\sum_{r=n}^{2n}$ $\sum_{r=1}^{2n}-\sum_{r=1}^{n-1}$
$\sum_{r=0}^n$ $\sum_{r=1}^n + f(0)$ â€” add the missing $r=0$ term
$\sum_{r=1}^n + \sum_{r=n+1}^{N}$ $=\sum_{r=1}^{N}$ (joining adjacent ranges)

Pitfall warning: when starting from $r=k$, the lower piece you SUBTRACT is up to $r=k-1$, not $r=k$. (Example: to get $\sum_{r=20}^{50}$, subtract $\sum_{r=1}^{19}$ from $\sum_{r=1}^{50}$.)

5. The standard "show that" recipe

Step Action
1 Expand any brackets: $r(r+3)(2r-1)\to 2r^3+5r^2-3r$ etc.
2 Apply linearity: $\sum=2\sum r^3+5\sum r^2-3\sum r$
3 Substitute the four standard formulas
4 Find a common factor (often $\tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)$ or $\tfrac{1}{12}n(n+1)$) and pull it out front
5 Expand the residual polynomial inside the bracket
6 Combine like terms
7 Factor the residual (often a quadratic in $n$)
8 Match against the target form to identify constants $a,b$

7. Decision flow â€” what to do in 5 seconds

Spot this Do this
$\sum$ of constant Use $\sum 1=n$
$\sum$ of polynomial in $r$ (linear, quad, cubic) Expand brackets, apply linearity, substitute four standard sums
Sum doesn't start at $r=1$ $\sum_{r=k}^n=\sum_{r=1}^n-\sum_{r=1}^{k-1}$
"$\sum_{r=1}^n f(r)=\frac{1}{c}n\cdot(\text{poly})$, find $a,b$" Show LHS in factored form, match coefficients of poly to identify $a,b$
"Hence evaluate $\sum_{r=k}^N$" Use part (a) formula with $n=N$ minus same formula with $n=k-1$
"Find $n$ such that $\sum=$ value" Set closed form equal to value, expand, solve polynomial in $n$ (quadratic or cubic)
Sum of even/odd numbers Even: $\sum 2r=2\sum r=n(n+1)$; odd: $\sum (2r-1)=n^2$
Sum of squares of odds $\sum (2r-1)^2$ Expand: $4r^2-4r+1$, then linearity. Should give $\tfrac{1}{3}n(2n-1)(2n+1)$.
$\sum r(r+a)$, $\sum (r+a)(r+b)$ Expand, then linearity.
Telescoping (rarer in FP1) If $f(r)-f(r-1)$ pattern, sum collapses to $f(n)-f(0)$.

8. Common pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Forgetting $\sum 1=n$ â€” leaving the constant term out Every polynomial sum has a $\sum 1$ piece â€” count it
Using $\sum_{r=k}^n=\sum_{r=1}^n-\sum_{r=1}^k$ (off-by-one) Subtract up to $k-1$, not $k$
Reading $\sum r^2$ as $(\sum r)^2$ Wrong â€” that's $\sum r^3$. $\sum r^2=\tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)(2n+1)$
Mis-typing $\sum r^2 = \tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)(2n+1)$ as $\tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)(2n-1)$ Plug $n=1$: should give $1$. Check: $\tfrac{1}{6}\cdot 1\cdot 2\cdot 3=1$ âś“
Forgetting the square in $\sum r^3=\tfrac{1}{4}n^2(n+1)^2$ Plug $n=1$: $\tfrac{1}{4}\cdot 1\cdot 4=1$ âś“
Not factoring the final result Examiner usually wants the answer in the form $\tfrac{1}{c}n(n+a)(n+b)$ etc. â€” factor aggressively
Bringing brackets inside $\sum$ illegally $\sum f(r)g(r) \neq \sum f(r)\cdot \sum g(r)$ â€” products don't commute with sums
Treating $r$ as if it could be a non-integer $r$ in $\sum$ is strictly an integer index
Index shift errors $\sum_{r=1}^n f(r-1)=\sum_{s=0}^{n-1} f(s)$ â€” change BOTH index and limits when shifting
Plugging $n=$ value too early in "show that" Keep $n$ symbolic until the final step, then substitute
Using the formula for $\sum_{r=0}^n$ from $\sum_{r=1}^n$ Add $f(0)$ â€” for $f(r)=r^2$, $f(0)=0$ so no change; but for $f(r)=r-2$, $f(0)=-2$ matters

10. Big picture (the 30-second mental model)

  1. Four formulas to memorise: $\sum 1=n,\ \sum r=\tfrac{n(n+1)}{2},\ \sum r^2=\tfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6},\ \sum r^3=\tfrac{n^2(n+1)^2}{4}$.
  2. Linearity lets you split any polynomial sum into a linear combination of these four.
  3. Range shifts ($r=k$ start) are handled by subtraction: $\sum_k^n=\sum_1^n-\sum_1^{k-1}$.
  4. Factoring is the final step. Pull out common factors (often $\tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)$), expand the residue, factor any quadratic that remains.
  5. Verification with small $n$ catches arithmetic errors before you commit.

That's the entire chapter: four formulas, two rules, one subtraction trick.
