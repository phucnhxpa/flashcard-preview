P3 Ch 4 — Trigonometric Addition Formulae

The whole chapter in one mental model. Three families of identities to know cold:

  1. Addition (compound-angle) formulae for $\sin(A\pm B)$, $\cos(A\pm B)$, $\tan(A\pm B)$.
  2. Double-angle formulae (set $B=A$ in addition formulae) — including the three flavours of $\cos 2A$.
  3. Harmonic form: $a\cos x\pm b\sin x=R\cos(x\mp\alpha)$ or $R\sin(x\pm\alpha)$ with $R=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ and $\tan\alpha=b/a$ (or as appropriate).

Together these tools let you (i) prove identities, (ii) simplify expressions, (iii) find exact values like $\sin 15°$, (iv) solve trig equations not solvable by P2 methods, and (v) find max/min of $a\cos\theta+b\sin\theta$.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Addition / compound-angle formula An identity expanding $\sin(A\pm B)$, $\cos(A\pm B)$, or $\tan(A\pm B)$ as combinations of $\sin A,\cos A,\sin B,\cos B,\tan A,\tan B$. They allow a trig function of a sum/difference to be re-expressed using individual angles. Provable by geometry (right-triangle picture) or by Euler's formula. 4.1
Identity ($\equiv$) An equation that is true for every value of the variable, not just some — e.g. $\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta\equiv 1$. The triple-bar $\equiv$ distinguishes from a conditional equation $=$. 4.1
Double-angle formula A special case of addition formulae with $A=B$: $\sin 2A=2\sin A\cos A$, etc. Three forms of $\cos 2A$ exist: $\cos^2A-\sin^2A$, $2\cos^2A-1$, $1-2\sin^2A$ — pick whichever is most useful. 4.3
Harmonic form / single-sinusoid form Rewriting $a\cos x+b\sin x$ (or $a\sin x+b\cos x$) as $R\cos(x-\alpha)$ or $R\sin(x+\alpha)$. Compresses two waves into one — the curve is a sinusoid of amplitude $R$, phase-shifted by $\alpha$. 4.5
Amplitude $R$ The maximum absolute value of $a\sin x\pm b\cos x$. Computed as $R=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ by Pythagoras. The minimum is $-R$. 4.5
Phase shift $\alpha$ The horizontal shift in the harmonic form. Found from $\tan\alpha=b/a$ (or $a/b$ — depends on which form). Always in the first quadrant: $0<\alpha<90°$ (or $\pi/2$). 4.5
Reciprocal trig functions $\sec\theta=\dfrac{1}{\cos\theta}$, $\operatorname{cosec}\theta=\dfrac{1}{\sin\theta}$, $\cot\theta=\dfrac{1}{\tan\theta}=\dfrac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}$. Often appear when proving identities or as final-answer forms. 4.6 (P2 recap)
CAST diagram A device for placing solutions: divides the $0–360°$ range into four $90°$ quadrants; "All" (Q1), "Sin" (Q2), "Tan" (Q3), "Cos" (Q4) functions are positive in their labelled quadrant. Used to choose correct sign of $\sin/\cos$ given an angle range. 4.2
Pythagorean identity $\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta\equiv 1$. Also the derived forms $1+\tan^2\theta\equiv\sec^2\theta$ and $1+\cot^2\theta\equiv\operatorname{cosec}^2\theta$. The most-used identity in trig. 4.6
Quadrant analysis Given a constraint like "$180°signs of the other ratios using CAST or a sketch. Essential for getting the sign of $\cos A$, $\tan A$ right. 4.2
Triangle proof of compound-angle A right-triangle diagram with two stacked angles $\alpha,\beta$ — gives geometric proofs of $\sin(\alpha+\beta)$ and $\cos(\alpha+\beta)$ by labelling sides using sine/cosine in each sub-triangle, then adding lengths. 4.1
"Show that" identity proof Manipulate one side (usually LHS) until it equals the other. Standard moves: write $\tan=\sin/\cos$, find common denominators, apply Pythagoras, apply addition formulae, apply double-angle formulae. Reverse direction if more convenient. 4.6
Eliminating a parameter Given $x$ and $y$ each as functions of $\theta$, use a trig identity (often $\sin^2+\cos^2=1$ or $\cos 2\theta=1-2\sin^2\theta$) to remove $\theta$ and get a Cartesian relation $y=\mathrm{f}(x)$. 4.3
"Express $\tan x$ in terms of $\tan y$" A typical exam ask: given a relation between $\sin/\cos$ at $x,y$, divide through by $\cos x\cos y$ to convert into $\tan x$, $\tan y$ terms, then solve for one. 4.1 Ex 4
"Hence solve" follow-up After proving an identity, use it to simplify an equation into a tractable form (often quadratic in $\sin\theta$ or linear in some compound angle). The whole point of the identity proof. 4.4

2. The addition (compound-angle) formulae — memorise these six

Identity Sign pattern memo
$\sin(A+B)\equiv\sin A\cos B+\cos A\sin B$ "sin–cos + cos–sin" — symmetric in form, plus sign
$\sin(A-B)\equiv\sin A\cos B-\cos A\sin B$ Same as above with minus
$\cos(A+B)\equiv\cos A\cos B-\sin A\sin B$ "cos–cos − sin–sin" — minus sign for $A+B$ (counter-intuitive!)
$\cos(A-B)\equiv\cos A\cos B+\sin A\sin B$ Plus sign for $A-B$
$\tan(A+B)\equiv\dfrac{\tan A+\tan B}{1-\tan A\tan B}$ Same sign as the sum, opposite in denominator
$\tan(A-B)\equiv\dfrac{\tan A-\tan B}{1+\tan A\tan B}$ Mirror: minus on top, plus on bottom

Memory tricks:

  • $\sin$: signs agree (plus with plus, minus with minus).
  • $\cos$: signs disagree (plus on input → minus on output; minus on input → plus on output).
  • $\tan$: same sign on top, opposite sign on bottom.

3. Double-angle formulae

Set $B=A$ in the addition formulae:

Identity Derivation
$\sin 2A\equiv 2\sin A\cos A$ From $\sin(A+A)$
$\cos 2A\equiv \cos^2 A-\sin^2 A$ From $\cos(A+A)$
$\cos 2A\equiv 2\cos^2 A-1$ Sub $\sin^2A=1-\cos^2A$
$\cos 2A\equiv 1-2\sin^2 A$ Sub $\cos^2A=1-\sin^2A$
$\tan 2A\equiv\dfrac{2\tan A}{1-\tan^2 A}$ From $\tan(A+A)$

Which form of $\cos 2A$ to use?

Substituting into expression containing Use
only $\cos$ terms $\cos 2A=2\cos^2 A-1$
only $\sin$ terms $\cos 2A=1-2\sin^2 A$
both / mixed $\cos 2A=\cos^2 A-\sin^2 A$
Need to introduce $\cos 2A$ from $\cos^2 A$ or $\sin^2 A$ Reverse: $\cos^2 A=\tfrac{1+\cos 2A}{2}$, $\sin^2 A=\tfrac{1-\cos 2A}{2}$ ("power-reduction")

Half-angle (special case): setting $A\to\theta/2$ in double-angle formulae gives $\cos\theta=2\cos^2(\theta/2)-1=1-2\sin^2(\theta/2)$ and $\sin\theta=2\sin(\theta/2)\cos(\theta/2)$.

5. Solving trig equations using addition / double-angle formulae

Spot this Strategy
$a\cos\theta-b\sin\theta=c$ (or sin/cos mixed = constant) Convert to harmonic form $R\cos(\theta\pm\alpha)=c$ — single trig function
$\sin 2\theta=k\sin\theta$ Sub $\sin 2\theta=2\sin\theta\cos\theta$, factor: $\sin\theta(2\cos\theta-k)=0$
$\cos 2\theta=k\sin\theta$ or $k\cos\theta$ Sub the appropriate form of $\cos 2\theta$ to make it a quadratic in $\sin\theta$ or $\cos\theta$
$\sin(A\pm\beta)=k$ where $\beta$ is fixed If $\beta$ is a known angle, expand using addition formula and convert to single trig
Equation involves $\tan(\theta\pm\beta)$ Either expand $\tan(\theta\pm\beta)$ or work backwards from the answer form
$\sin n\theta=\sin m\theta$ Use $\sin n\theta-\sin m\theta=0$ and sum-to-product, or transform to a polynomial via expansion
$\sin\theta=\sqrt{1-\cos^2\theta}$ but with sign issue Always state the quadrant and choose sign — sketch CAST or graph

Typical procedure (Example 14b): "Solve $5\sin\theta+12\cos\theta=k$" → harmonic form $13\sin(\theta+\alpha)=k$ where $\alpha=\tan^{-1}(12/5)$ → $\sin(\theta+\alpha)=k/13$ → solve, subtract $\alpha$ at end.

6. Harmonic form $a\cos\theta\pm b\sin\theta$

Original form Target Compute $R$ Compute $\alpha$
$a\sin\theta+b\cos\theta$ $R\sin(\theta+\alpha)$ $R=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ $\tan\alpha=b/a$ ($R\cos\alpha=a$, $R\sin\alpha=b$)
$a\sin\theta-b\cos\theta$ $R\sin(\theta-\alpha)$ same same — $\tan\alpha=b/a$
$a\cos\theta+b\sin\theta$ $R\cos(\theta-\alpha)$ $R=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ $\tan\alpha=b/a$ ($R\cos\alpha=a$, $R\sin\alpha=b$)
$a\cos\theta-b\sin\theta$ $R\cos(\theta+\alpha)$ same same

Note the $\mp$ pattern: $a\cos\theta\pm b\sin\theta=R\cos(\theta\mp\alpha)$ — sign flips in the bracket (because $\cos(A-B)=\cos A\cos B+\sin A\sin B$).

Procedure:

  1. Choose target form (often dictated by question; otherwise pick the form that matches the leading function — e.g., $3\sin x+4\cos x$ → use $R\sin(x+\alpha)$ form).
  2. Expand target: $R\sin(\theta+\alpha)=R\sin\theta\cos\alpha+R\cos\theta\sin\alpha$.
  3. Equate coefficients of $\sin\theta$ and $\cos\theta$ → $R\cos\alpha=a$, $R\sin\alpha=b$.
  4. Divide the two: $\tan\alpha=b/a$ — choose $\alpha\in(0,\pi/2)$.
  5. Square and add: $R^2(\cos^2\alpha+\sin^2\alpha)=a^2+b^2\Rightarrow R=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$.

Max / min of $a\sin\theta+b\cos\theta$:

  • Maximum value $=R$, attained when bracket $=$ first place where the function is +1 (e.g. $\theta+\alpha=2k\pi$ for $\sin$ form; or $=2k\pi$ for $\cos$ form).
  • Minimum value $=-R$.
  • Useful for max/min of fractions like $\dfrac{c}{p+a\cos\theta+b\sin\theta}$: max numerator over min denominator = max of $\dfrac{c}{p-R}$, etc.

8. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Sign error: writing $\cos(A+B)=\cos A\cos B+\sin A\sin B$ (should be minus) Memorise: "$\cos$ flips sign" — opposite of input sign
Choosing wrong sign of $\cos A$ when only $\sin A$ given Use given range / CAST diagram first; then choose $\pm$ root
Assuming $\sin(A+B)=\sin A+\sin B$ Wrong — counter-example: $A=B=\pi/2$ gives $\sin\pi=0$ but $\sin(\pi/2)+\sin(\pi/2)=2$
For $\cos 2A$, blindly using $\cos^2 A-\sin^2 A$ Match to the rest of the equation: $\sin$-only, $\cos$-only, or mixed
Harmonic form: confusing which $a$ goes with $\cos\alpha$ vs $\sin\alpha$ Expand the target form first to see which coefficient pairs with which
Forgetting the bracket-sign flip $\pm\to\mp$ in $a\cos\pm b\sin=R\cos(\theta\mp\alpha)$ Memorise: $\cos(A-B)=\cos A\cos B+\sin A\sin B$ — plus inside brackets when subtracting
Solving $\sin 2\theta=\sin\theta$ by dividing by $\sin\theta$ Loses solutions $\sin\theta=0$. Always factor, never divide by something that could be zero
Solving $\cos 2\theta=\dots$ over $0\le\theta<2\pi$ but only finding $0\le 2\theta<2\pi$ solutions The doubled angle ranges over $[0,4\pi)$ — find all in that range, then halve
Forgetting $\alpha$ must be in $(0,90°)$ If $\tan\alpha<0$ or $a<0$ or $b<0$, change target form rather than allow $\alpha$ outside
In "hence solve" questions, not using the previous part Always use the proven identity to simplify before solving
Using calculator for exact-value questions Show clearly all addition-formula manipulations
Squaring both sides — extraneous solutions After solving, substitute back to check; rejection of false roots required
In CAST, forgetting that obtuse means Q2 $90°<\theta<180°$: $\sin>0$, $\cos<0$, $\tan<0$
Half-angle: forgetting $\sin(\theta/2)$ depends on quadrant of $\theta/2$, not $\theta$ Track which quadrant $\theta/2$ is in (e.g. if $\theta\in[0,2\pi)$ then $\theta/2\in[0,\pi)$ — mostly Q1/Q2)

9. Big picture (30-second mental model)

The chapter is built around three identity families plus their applications:

  1. Addition formulae $\sin/\cos/\tan(A\pm B)$ — six identities; memorise sign patterns.
  2. Double-angle formulae — set $B=A$. Three forms of $\cos 2A$ — choose based on what's in the rest of the equation. Power-reduction: $\sin^2,\cos^2$ → $\cos 2A$.
  3. Harmonic form $a\cos\theta\pm b\sin\theta=R\cos(\theta\mp\alpha)$ with $R=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$, $\tan\alpha=b/a$ — collapses two terms into a single phase-shifted sinusoid.

Applications:

  • Find exact values of trig at unusual angles (15°, 75°, etc.)
  • Solve trig equations that mix multiple angles or both sin and cos
  • Find max/min of $a\cos+b\sin$ (= $\pm R$)
  • Prove identities by manipulating using these tools
  • Eliminate parameters in parametric equations

The big skill is pattern recognition: spotting when a problem is asking for an addition, double-angle, or harmonic-form trick, and which one will simplify the algebra.
