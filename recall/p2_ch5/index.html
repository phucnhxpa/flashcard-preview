P2 Ch 5 — Sequences and Series

The whole chapter in one mental model. Two flavours of "regular" sequences:

  • Arithmetic — each term increases by a constant common difference $d$: $a,\;a+d,\;a+2d,\dots$
  • Geometric — each term multiplies by a constant common ratio $r$: $a,\;ar,\;ar^2,\dots$

Each has formulas for (i) the $n$-th term $u_n$ and (ii) the sum of the first $n$ terms $S_n$. Geometric series additionally have sum to infinity $S_\infty=\dfrac{a}{1-r}$ if and only if $\lvert r\rvert<1$ (convergent). Sigma notation $\sum$ packages a sum compactly. Recurrence relations $u_{n+1}=\mathrm{f}(u_n)$ define sequences inductively (need a first term). Modelling: real-world growth/decay → geometric; constant change → arithmetic.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Sequence An ordered list of numbers (terms) $u_1,u_2,u_3,\dots$ generated by a rule. The position is the index $n$; the value at position $n$ is the $n$-th term $u_n$. All
Series The sum of the terms of a sequence: $u_1+u_2+\dots+u_n$. The "series" is a number (the running total); the "sequence" is the list. 5.2
Arithmetic sequence A sequence where each term differs from the previous by a fixed amount $d$ (the common difference). $u_n-u_{n-1}=d$ for all $n\ge 2$. Examples: $3,7,11,15,\dots$ ($d=4$); $20,17,14,\dots$ ($d=-3$). 5.1
Common difference $d$ The constant gap between consecutive terms in an arithmetic sequence: $d=u_2-u_1=u_3-u_2=\dots$. May be negative (decreasing) or zero (constant). 5.1
Geometric sequence A sequence where each term is a fixed multiple $r$ of the previous (the common ratio). $u_n/u_{n-1}=r$ for all $n\ge 2$, with $u_1\ne 0$ and $r\ne 0$. Examples: $3,6,12,24,\dots$ ($r=2$); $100,10,1,0.1,\dots$ ($r=0.1$). 5.3
Common ratio $r$ The constant multiplier between consecutive terms: $r=u_2/u_1=u_3/u_2=\dots$. Can be negative (alternating signs); $\lvert r\rvert<1$ gives decay; $\lvert r\rvert>1$ gives growth. 5.3
First term $a$ (or $u_1$) The initial term of the sequence. Both arithmetic and geometric formulas need $a$. 5.1 / 5.3
$n$-th term formula Closed-form expression for $u_n$ in terms of $n$, allowing direct computation without listing all earlier terms. Arithmetic: $u_n=a+(n-1)d$. Geometric: $u_n=ar^{n-1}$. 5.1 / 5.3
Partial sum $S_n$ The sum of the first $n$ terms: $S_n=u_1+u_2+\dots+u_n$. Has a closed-form formula for arithmetic and geometric series. 5.2 / 5.4
Sum to infinity $S_\infty$ Limit of $S_n$ as $n\to\infty$. Exists only for convergent geometric series ($\lvert r\rvert<1$). Formula: $S_\infty=\dfrac{a}{1-r}$. 5.5
Convergent / divergent A series converges if its partial sums approach a finite limit. A geometric series converges $\Leftrightarrow\lvert r\rvert<1$. Otherwise diverges (sum grows without bound or oscillates). 5.5
Sigma notation $\sum$ Greek capital sigma. $\sum_{r=1}^{n}u_r$ means: substitute $r=1,2,\dots,n$ into $u_r$ and add. Limits go on top (upper) and bottom (lower) of $\sum$. 5.6
Index variable The dummy letter (often $r$ or $k$ or $i$) in $\sum$. Ranges from lower limit to upper limit, integer-valued. Doesn't appear in the final value. 5.6
Standard sigma results $\sum_{r=1}^{n}1=n$ and $\sum_{r=1}^{n}r=\dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}$. Useful constant building-blocks. 5.6
Splitting sum $\sum_{r=k}^{n}u_r=\sum_{r=1}^{n}u_r-\sum_{r=1}^{k-1}u_r$. Allows evaluating sums that don't start at 1 by subtracting the missing front piece. 5.6
Recurrence relation A rule expressing $u_{n+1}$ in terms of $u_n$ (and possibly earlier terms): $u_{n+1}=\mathrm{f}(u_n)$. Generates a sequence given a starting value $u_1$. Different $u_1$ → different sequence even with same rule. 5.7
Increasing / decreasing / periodic sequence Increasing: $u_{n+1}>u_n$ for all $n$. Decreasing: $u_{n+1} 5.7
Order of a periodic sequence The smallest $k$ such that the pattern repeats every $k$ terms. E.g., $3,5,3,5,\dots$ has order 2. 5.7
Convergent (recurrence) A sequence is convergent if $u_n$ approaches a finite limit $L$ as $n\to\infty$. At the limit, $L=\mathrm{f}(L)$. 5.7
Modelling with series Translating real-world problems (savings, populations, debt) into arithmetic or geometric series. Constant per-period change → arithmetic; constant percentage change → geometric. 5.8

2. Arithmetic sequence formulas

Quantity Formula Notes
$n$-th term $u_n=a+(n-1)d$ $a$ = first term, $d$ = common difference
Common difference $d=u_2-u_1=u_{n+1}-u_n$ Constant for all consecutive pairs
Sum of first $n$ terms $S_n=\dfrac{n}{2}\bigl(2a+(n-1)d\bigr)$ Using $a$ and $d$
Alternative sum form $S_n=\dfrac{n}{2}(a+l)$ Where $l=u_n$ is the last term
Sum derivation Pair first + last, second + second-last … each pair sums to $a+l$ "Gauss trick"
Term from sum $u_n=S_n-S_{n-1}$ Useful when $S_n$ is given as a function of $n$

3. Geometric sequence formulas

Quantity Formula Notes
$n$-th term $u_n=ar^{n-1}$ $a$ = first term, $r$ = common ratio
Common ratio $r=\dfrac{u_2}{u_1}=\dfrac{u_{n+1}}{u_n}$ Constant ratio
Sum of first $n$ terms $S_n=\dfrac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r},\;r\ne 1$ Use when $\lvert r\rvert<1$
Equivalent $S_n=\dfrac{a(r^n-1)}{r-1},\;r\ne 1$ Use when $\lvert r\rvert>1$ (numerator positive)
Sum to infinity $S_\infty=\dfrac{a}{1-r}$ Only if $\lvert r\rvert<1$
Convergence condition $\lvert r\rvert<1$ Equivalently $-1
If $r=1$ All terms equal $a$; $S_n=na$ Not a "true" geometric in the usual sense
Three terms in GP If $a,b,c$ in GP then $b^2=ac$ Useful identity

4. Sigma notation — operations cheatsheet

Operation Formula
Constant series $\sum_{r=1}^{n}1=n$
Sum of first $n$ integers $\sum_{r=1}^{n}r=\dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}$
Linearity (constant out) $\sum c\cdot u_r=c\sum u_r$
Linearity (split sum) $\sum(u_r+v_r)=\sum u_r+\sum v_r$
Re-indexing / shift $\sum_{r=k}^{n}u_r=\sum_{r=1}^{n}u_r-\sum_{r=1}^{k-1}u_r$
Number of terms in $\sum_{r=k}^{n}$ $n-k+1$
Arithmetic series sum (sigma form) $\sum_{r=1}^{n}\bigl(a+(r-1)d\bigr)=\dfrac n 2(2a+(n-1)d)$
Geometric series (finite, sigma) $\sum_{r=1}^{n}ar^{r-1}=\dfrac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r}$
Geometric infinite (sigma) $\sum_{r=1}^{\infty}ar^{r-1}=\dfrac{a}{1-r}$ if $\lvert r\rvert<1$

6. Method decision flow

Spot this Strategy
Given two terms, find $a$ and $d$ (arithmetic) Set up $u_n=a+(n-1)d$ for both, solve simultaneously
Given two terms, find $a$ and $r$ (geometric) Divide the equations $ar^{m-1}=A$, $ar^{n-1}=B$: $r^{m-n}=A/B$
"Sum of first $n$ exceeds $K$": find smallest $n$ Set $S_n>K$, take logs (geometric) or solve quadratic (arithmetic)
"Sum to infinity equals $K$" $\dfrac{a}{1-r}=K$, possibly with another condition to find $a$ and $r$
Sum starts at $r=k$, not $r=1$ Use splitting: $\sum_{k}^{n}=\sum_{1}^{n}-\sum_{1}^{k-1}$
Compound interest, growth/decay rate $p\%$ Geometric, $r=1+p/100$ (or $1-p/100$ for decay)
Salary increases by fixed amount each year Arithmetic
Salary increases by fixed percent each year Geometric
Half-life / radioactive decay Geometric, $r=\tfrac12$ per half-life
Recurrence: find 4 terms Substitute $u_1$ into $u_2=\mathrm{f}(u_1)$, then $u_3=\mathrm{f}(u_2)$, etc.
Recurrence: find limit Solve $L=\mathrm{f}(L)$

8. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Using $u_n=a+nd$ instead of $u_n=a+(n-1)d$ Off-by-one: the first term has zero $d$'s added. $(n-1)$, not $n$
Using $u_n=ar^n$ instead of $u_n=ar^{n-1}$ Same off-by-one: first term is $ar^0=a$. Power is $n-1$
Computing $S_n$ for geometric without checking $r=1$ If $r=1$, formula divides by zero. $S_n=na$ in that case
Applying $S_\infty=\dfrac{a}{1-r}$ when $\lvert r\rvert\ge 1$ $S_\infty$ only defined for $\lvert r\rvert<1$. Otherwise series diverges
Forgetting the alternative sum form: $S_n=\tfrac n 2(a+l)$ when given last term Saves work when last term is given but $d$ isn't
Sigma sum starting at index $k\ne 1$: just plugging into $S_n$ formula Use splitting: $\sum_{r=k}^{n}=\sum_{r=1}^{n}-\sum_{r=1}^{k-1}$, not $S_{n-k+1}$ directly
Confusing arithmetic vs geometric in modelling: "$2\%$ increase" = geometric, "$\$200$ increase" = arithmetic Read carefully: percentage → multiply; flat amount → add
In geometric word problems, treating "$n$-th year" inconsistently with the indexing Clarify: is "year 1" the start or after one year? Define $u_1$ explicitly
In log inequalities like $r^{n-1}>K$: forgetting log direction If $0
Recurrence: forgetting that you need $u_1$ as a starting point Always check the question gives an initial term
Solving recurrence's "$L=\mathrm f(L)$" — getting two solutions and not knowing which is the limit The limit (if it exists) is the one the sequence is approaching from $u_1$. Compute first few terms
Sum to infinity question: not checking convergence Always state $\lvert r\rvert<1$ (and pick the root satisfying it)
In sigma notation, miscounting number of terms: $\sum_{r=k}^{n}$ has $n-k+1$ terms (not $n-k$) Double-check by listing first/last index
Mixing up sigma's index variable $\sum_{r=1}^{n}r$ is a function of $n$, not of $r$. The $r$ is internal

9. Big picture (30-second mental model)

A sequence is just a function $\mathbb{N}\to\mathbb{R}$. Two famous shapes:

  • Arithmetic — additive growth. $u_n=a+(n-1)d$, $S_n=\tfrac n 2(2a+(n-1)d)$. Linear in $n$.
  • Geometric — multiplicative growth. $u_n=ar^{n-1}$, $S_n=\dfrac{a(r^n-1)}{r-1}$. Exponential in $n$.

For geometric, the infinite sum exists if and only if $\lvert r\rvert<1$, and equals $\dfrac{a}{1-r}$. Otherwise the sum diverges.

Sigma notation $\sum$ is just shorthand for "add these up". Linearity, splitting, and the standard results $\sum 1=n$, $\sum r=\tfrac{n(n+1)}{2}$ are the algebra of $\sum$.

A recurrence relation $u_{n+1}=\mathrm f(u_n)$ defines a sequence inductively from $u_1$. If $u_n\to L$, then $L=\mathrm f(L)$ — fixed-point equation.

In modelling: ask "constant amount per period" (arithmetic) vs "constant percentage per period" (geometric). Compound interest, population growth, and radioactive decay are all geometric. Salary increments and stacked layers are arithmetic.
