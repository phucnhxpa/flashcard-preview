FP3 Ch 6 — Further Matrix Algebra

The whole chapter in one mental model. Lift FP1's $2\times 2$ matrix toolkit up to $3\times 3$: transpose, determinant (by cofactor expansion), inverse (via cofactors → transpose → divide by det), then use these to represent 3D linear transformations $T:\mathbb R^3\to\mathbb R^3$ and reverse them with $\mathbf T^{-1}$. The crown jewels are eigenvalues/eigenvectors $\mathbf{Ax}=\lambda\mathbf x$ — directions that don't change under $T$ — found via the characteristic equation $\det(\mathbf A-\lambda\mathbf I)=0$. For symmetric matrices, the eigenvectors are mutually orthogonal, so $\mathbf P^\top\mathbf{AP}=\mathbf D$ diagonalises $\mathbf A$, with the eigenvalues sitting on the diagonal.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Transpose $\mathbf A^\top$ Swap rows and columns. $n\times m$ becomes $m\times n$. Rule: $(\mathbf{AB})^\top=\mathbf B^\top\mathbf A^\top$ (note the flipped order). $(\mathbf A^\top)^\top=\mathbf A$. Both $\mathbf{AA}^\top$ and $\mathbf A^\top\mathbf A$ are symmetric. 6.1
Symmetric matrix $\mathbf A=\mathbf A^\top$, i.e. mirror-image across the leading diagonal. $3\times 3$ form $\begin{pmatrix}a&b&c\\b&d&e\\c&e&f\end{pmatrix}$. Eigenvalues are real, eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal — basis for diagonalisation. 6.1, 6.7
Identity matrix $\mathbf I$ Square matrix with 1s on leading diagonal, 0s elsewhere. $\mathbf{IA}=\mathbf{AI}=\mathbf A$. The "do-nothing" matrix. 6.1
Determinant A scalar built from a square matrix. For $3\times 3$ via cofactor expansion along row 1: $\det\mathbf A=a\det M_a-b\det M_b+c\det M_c$ where $M_x$ is the $2\times 2$ minor obtained by deleting the row+column of element $x$. Watch the alternating signs: $+,-,+$. 6.2
Singular $\det\mathbf A=0$ — the matrix has no inverse. Geometrically: collapses 3D space onto a plane/line/point (loses a dimension). One eigenvalue is $0$. 6.2
Non-singular $\det\mathbf A\ne 0$ — invertible. Multiplication by it preserves dimension. 6.2
Multiplicative property $\det(\mathbf{AB})=\det(\mathbf A)\det(\mathbf B)$ — a clean way to verify a product computation, and shows that singular×non-singular = singular. Also $\det(\mathbf A^\top)=\det(\mathbf A)$. 6.2
Minor The $2\times 2$ determinant left after deleting the row and column of a chosen element. There are 9 minors for a $3\times 3$. 6.3
Cofactor A minor with the chequerboard sign $\begin{pmatrix}+&-&+\\-&+&-\\+&-&+\end{pmatrix}$ applied. The matrix of cofactors $\mathbf C$ has these signed minors as entries. 6.3
Adjugate (transpose of cofactor) $\mathbf C^\top$ — the matrix obtained by taking the cofactor matrix and transposing it. Sometimes written $\operatorname{adj}\mathbf A$. 6.3
Inverse $\mathbf A^{-1}$ Defined by $\mathbf{AA}^{-1}=\mathbf A^{-1}\mathbf A=\mathbf I$. Formula: $\mathbf A^{-1}=\dfrac{1}{\det\mathbf A}\,\mathbf C^\top$. Exists iff $\det\mathbf A\ne 0$. Properties: $(\mathbf{AB})^{-1}=\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf A^{-1}$, $(\mathbf A^{-1})^\top=(\mathbf A^\top)^{-1}$. 6.3
Linear transformation Map $T:\mathbb R^3\to\mathbb R^3$ satisfying $T(k\mathbf x)=kT(\mathbf x)$ and $T(\mathbf x_1+\mathbf x_2)=T(\mathbf x_1)+T(\mathbf x_2)$. Equivalently: $T(\mathbf x)=\mathbf{Tx}$ for some matrix $\mathbf T$. 6.4
Image The output point $T(\mathbf x)=\mathbf{Tx}$ — where the input point gets sent. Lines map to lines, planes to planes (unless $\mathbf T$ is singular, when planes can collapse to lines). 6.4
Composition If $T$ has matrix $\mathbf T$ and $U$ has $\mathbf U$, then $U\circ T$ ("apply $T$ first, then $U$") has matrix $\mathbf{UT}$ (read right-to-left). 6.4
Eigenvector Non-zero vector $\mathbf x$ satisfying $\mathbf{Ax}=\lambda\mathbf x$ — a direction that the transformation preserves (only stretches/squashes). Any non-zero scalar multiple is also an eigenvector. 6.6
Eigenvalue $\lambda$ The stretch factor for an eigenvector: how much the eigenvector is scaled by. Can be positive, negative or zero ($\lambda=0$ ⇔ matrix is singular). 6.6
Characteristic equation $\det(\mathbf A-\lambda\mathbf I)=0$ — the polynomial equation whose roots are the eigenvalues. For $2\times 2$: quadratic in $\lambda$. For $3\times 3$: cubic in $\lambda$ (use factor theorem to find one root, then factor out). 6.6
Normalised vector A vector divided by its own magnitude: $\hat{\mathbf x}=\mathbf x/\lvert\mathbf x\rvert$. Has length 1. Used when assembling an orthogonal matrix from eigenvectors. 6.6
Invariant line A line through the origin every point of which is mapped to another point on the same line — the line of action of an eigenvector. If $\lambda=1$, every point is fixed (line of invariant points). 6.6
Orthogonal matrix Square matrix $\mathbf M$ with $\mathbf{MM}^\top=\mathbf I$, equivalently $\mathbf M^{-1}=\mathbf M^\top$. Columns are mutually perpendicular unit vectors. Geometrically: a rotation/reflection (preserves lengths and angles). 6.7
Diagonal matrix Square matrix with all off-diagonal entries zero. Easy to manipulate: $\mathbf D^n$ just raises each diagonal entry to the power $n$. 6.7
Diagonalisation For symmetric $\mathbf A$: $\mathbf P^\top\mathbf{AP}=\mathbf D$, where $\mathbf P$'s columns are normalised eigenvectors of $\mathbf A$ and $\mathbf D$'s diagonal entries are the corresponding eigenvalues (in matching order). $\mathbf P$ is automatically orthogonal so $\mathbf P^\top=\mathbf P^{-1}$. 6.7

2. Determinant of a $3\times 3$ matrix (Section 6.2)

For $\mathbf A=\begin{pmatrix}a&b&c\\d&e&f\\g&h&i\end{pmatrix}$, expand along row 1:

$$\det\mathbf A=a\det\begin{pmatrix}e&f\\h&i\end{pmatrix}-b\det\begin{pmatrix}d&f\\g&i\end{pmatrix}+c\det\begin{pmatrix}d&e\\g&h\end{pmatrix}$$

Step Action Watch out
1 Pick top row, walk left→right: $a,b,c$ Could expand along any row/column with the right sign pattern
2 Each gets a sign: $+a,-b,+c$ Middle term is negative
3 Each gets a $2\times 2$ minor: cross out its row+column Use $ad-bc$ for the $2\times 2$
4 Sum the three signed $2\times 2$ determinants Algebra mistakes most common in negatives

Key identities:

  • $\det(\mathbf{AB})=\det\mathbf A\cdot\det\mathbf B$
  • $\det(\mathbf A^\top)=\det\mathbf A$
  • $\det(k\mathbf A)=k^n\det\mathbf A$ for an $n\times n$ matrix (so $k^3$ for $3\times 3$)
  • $\det\mathbf A=0$ ⇔ rows (or columns) are linearly dependent ⇔ matrix is singular

3. Inverse of a $3\times 3$ matrix — the 5-step recipe (Section 6.3)

Step Action Output
1 Compute $\det\mathbf A$ Scalar (must be $\ne 0$)
2 Form matrix of minors $\mathbf M$: replace each entry $a_{ij}$ with the $2\times 2$ minor (delete row $i$, col $j$) $3\times 3$ matrix
3 Apply chequerboard $\begin{pmatrix}+&-&+\\-&+&-\\+&-&+\end{pmatrix}$ → matrix of cofactors $\mathbf C$ $3\times 3$ matrix
4 Transpose: $\mathbf C^\top$ (also called the adjugate) $3\times 3$ matrix
5 Divide by determinant: $\mathbf A^{-1}=\dfrac{1}{\det\mathbf A}\,\mathbf C^\top$ The inverse

Inverse identities:

Identity Use
$(\mathbf{AB})^{-1}=\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf A^{-1}$ Reverse compositions: apply inverses in reverse order
$(\mathbf A^{-1})^{-1}=\mathbf A$ Inverse of inverse is original
$(\mathbf A^\top)^{-1}=(\mathbf A^{-1})^\top$ Transpose and inverse commute
$\det(\mathbf A^{-1})=1/\det\mathbf A$ Quick check
$\mathbf{AA}^{-1}=\mathbf A^{-1}\mathbf A=\mathbf I$ Definition; useful for solving $\mathbf{Ax}=\mathbf b$

Solving systems: $\mathbf{Ax}=\mathbf b\Rightarrow\mathbf x=\mathbf A^{-1}\mathbf b$ when $\mathbf A$ is non-singular.

4. Linear transformations in 3D (Section 6.4)

Concept Formula / fact
Matrix from rule If $T:(x,y,z)\mapsto(Lx+My+Nz,\ldots)$, write coefficients as rows
Matrix from images Image of $\mathbf i$ = column 1 of $\mathbf T$; image of $\mathbf j$ = col 2; image of $\mathbf k$ = col 3
Image of a point $\mathbf{Tx}$
Image of a line $\mathbf r=\mathbf a+t\mathbf b$ $\mathbf r=\mathbf{Ta}+t(\mathbf{Tb})$
Image of a plane $\mathbf r=\mathbf a+s\mathbf b+t\mathbf c$ $\mathbf r=\mathbf{Ta}+s\mathbf{Tb}+t\mathbf{Tc}$
Composition $U\circ T$ $\mathbf{UT}$ — apply $T$ first, left-multiply by $U$
Inverse transformation Has matrix $\mathbf T^{-1}$, undoing $T$
Singular $\mathbf T$ Plane can collapse to a line; line can collapse to a point
Volume scaling factor $\lvert\det\mathbf T\rvert$ — image of unit cube has this volume
Sign of $\det\mathbf T$ Positive: orientation preserved. Negative: orientation flipped (involves a reflection).

Finding $\mathbf T$ from data: if you're told three image-pre-image pairs, set $\mathbf T=\begin{pmatrix}a&b&c\\d&e&f\\g&h&i\end{pmatrix}$ and equate $\mathbf{Tx}_k=\mathbf y_k$ to extract 9 simultaneous equations.

5. Eigenvalues and eigenvectors (Section 6.6)

Step Action Notes
1 Form $\mathbf A-\lambda\mathbf I$ — subtract $\lambda$ from each diagonal entry $\mathbf I$ is the right-sized identity
2 Compute $\det(\mathbf A-\lambda\mathbf I)$ — the characteristic polynomial Quadratic for $2\times 2$, cubic for $3\times 3$
3 Solve $\det(\mathbf A-\lambda\mathbf I)=0$ — the characteristic equation Roots are the eigenvalues
4 For each $\lambda$: solve $(\mathbf A-\lambda\mathbf I)\mathbf x=\mathbf 0$, i.e. $\mathbf{Ax}=\lambda\mathbf x$ At least one variable is free; choose convenient values
5 (Optional) Normalise: divide by $\lvert\mathbf x\rvert$ Required for diagonalisation
Setup Characteristic polynomial degree Number of eigenvalues
$2\times 2$ quadratic in $\lambda$ up to 2 (real)
$3\times 3$ cubic in $\lambda$ up to 3 (real)
Triangular matrix $(\lambda-d_1)(\lambda-d_2)(\lambda-d_3)$ diagonal entries are eigenvalues (free!)

$3\times 3$ tip: Often a hint or factor theorem reveals one eigenvalue (try $\lambda=1,-1,2,\ldots$); polynomial-divide it out, then quadratic-formula the remaining factor.

Eigenvector facts:

  • Eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are linearly independent.
  • For a symmetric matrix, eigenvectors of distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal.
  • Any non-zero scalar multiple of an eigenvector is itself an eigenvector (with the same $\lambda$).
  • $\lambda=0$ ⇔ $\mathbf A$ is singular ⇔ eigenvector is in the kernel of $\mathbf A$.
  • $\lambda=1$ ⇔ line of invariant points (every point on the line is fixed).

6. Diagonalising a symmetric matrix (Section 6.7)

For symmetric $\mathbf A$:

Step Action Output
1 Find all eigenvalues $\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\lambda_3$ from $\det(\mathbf A-\lambda\mathbf I)=0$ Three real numbers
2 Find an eigenvector $\mathbf v_k$ for each $\lambda_k$ Three vectors
3 Normalise: $\hat{\mathbf v}_k=\mathbf v_k/\lvert\mathbf v_k\rvert$ Three unit vectors
4 Build $\mathbf P=(\hat{\mathbf v}_1\mid\hat{\mathbf v}_2\mid\hat{\mathbf v}_3)$ — eigenvectors as columns $3\times 3$ orthogonal matrix
5 Compute $\mathbf P^\top$ — automatic since $\mathbf P^\top=\mathbf P^{-1}$ (Just transpose)
6 $\mathbf D=\mathbf P^\top\mathbf{AP}$ — diagonal with eigenvalues on the diagonal in matching order The diagonalised form

Why it works: $\mathbf{AP}$ has columns $\mathbf A\hat{\mathbf v}_k=\lambda_k\hat{\mathbf v}_k$; left-multiplying by $\mathbf P^\top$ projects each column onto its own eigenvector, leaving $\lambda_k$ on the diagonal.

Orthogonal matrix tests (any equivalent):

Test Statement
Algebraic $\mathbf{MM}^\top=\mathbf I$
Inverse identity $\mathbf M^{-1}=\mathbf M^\top$
Column structure Columns are mutually perpendicular unit vectors: $\hat{\mathbf v}_i\cdot\hat{\mathbf v}_j=\delta_{ij}$
Row structure Same for rows
Determinant $\det\mathbf M=\pm 1$
Geometric Represents a rotation ($\det=+1$) or rotation-with-reflection ($\det=-1$)

7. Cross-cutting recall — what each property tells you

Property of $\mathbf A$ Implication
$\det\mathbf A=0$ Singular; one eigenvalue is 0; not invertible; columns linearly dependent
$\det\mathbf A\ne 0$ Invertible; eigenvalues all non-zero; $\mathbf{Ax}=\mathbf b$ has unique solution
$\mathbf A=\mathbf A^\top$ (symmetric) Real eigenvalues; eigenvectors orthogonal; diagonalisable by orthogonal $\mathbf P$
$\mathbf{AA}^\top=\mathbf I$ (orthogonal) $\mathbf A^{-1}=\mathbf A^\top$; preserves lengths/angles; det $=\pm 1$
$\det\mathbf A=1$ Volume- and orientation-preserving; rotation if also orthogonal
$\det\mathbf A=-1$ Volume-preserving but orientation-flipping (reflection component)
$\lambda=1$ is an eigenvalue Has a line of fixed points (line of invariant points)
$\lambda=-1$ is an eigenvalue Reverses direction along that line; suggests a 180° rotation/reflection axis
Trace = sum of eigenvalues $\operatorname{tr}\mathbf A=\sum\lambda_k$
Determinant = product of eigenvalues $\det\mathbf A=\prod\lambda_k$

9. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Forgetting middle minus sign in cofactor expansion Pattern is $+,-,+$ along row 1
Computing $(\mathbf{AB})^\top=\mathbf A^\top\mathbf B^\top$ (wrong order) $(\mathbf{AB})^\top=\mathbf B^\top\mathbf A^\top$ — flip the order
Computing $(\mathbf{AB})^{-1}=\mathbf A^{-1}\mathbf B^{-1}$ (wrong order) $(\mathbf{AB})^{-1}=\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf A^{-1}$ — same flip rule
Forgetting to transpose the cofactor matrix when inverting Always do step 4 (the transpose) before dividing by det
Forgetting the $\dfrac{1}{\det\mathbf A}$ factor Inverse formula is $\dfrac{1}{\det\mathbf A}\mathbf C^\top$
Solving $\mathbf{Ax}=\lambda\mathbf x$ with $\mathbf x=\mathbf 0$ Eigenvectors are by definition non-zero; the trivial solution doesn't count
Using only one equation for $3\times 3$ eigenvector Usually need 2 of the 3; the third is redundant. Sometimes all 3 give same info — then $x$ is free.
Writing composition $U\circ T$ as $\mathbf{TU}$ It's $\mathbf{UT}$ — the matrix of "$T$ then $U$" is "$U$ multiplied on the left of $T$"
Forgetting to normalise eigenvectors before forming $\mathbf P$ Diagonalisation needs $\mathbf P$ orthogonal — only true if columns are unit vectors
Putting eigenvectors as rows in $\mathbf P$ They go as columns
Mismatching order of $\lambda$s in $\mathbf D$ to columns in $\mathbf P$ $\mathbf D$'s $k$-th diagonal must match $\mathbf P$'s $k$-th column eigenvalue
Forgetting that singular $\mathbf T$ collapses planes to lines Image of plane under singular $\mathbf T$ may not be a plane
Sign errors in $\mathbf A-\lambda\mathbf I$ Subtract $\lambda$ only from the diagonal; off-diagonal entries unchanged

10. Big picture (the 30-second mental model)

A $3\times 3$ matrix $\mathbf A$ is a 3D linear transformation: tells you where the unit vectors $\mathbf i,\mathbf j,\mathbf k$ go (those are its three columns). From there:

  1. Determinant = scalar measuring how much $\mathbf A$ stretches volumes (with sign for orientation). Zero → collapsed dimension → singular → no inverse.
  2. Inverse $\mathbf A^{-1}=\dfrac{1}{\det\mathbf A}\mathbf C^\top$ undoes $\mathbf A$. Found via cofactor matrix, transposed, scaled.
  3. Eigenvectors are special directions $\mathbf x$ with $\mathbf{Ax}=\lambda\mathbf x$ — the transformation only stretches them by $\lambda$. Found by solving the characteristic equation $\det(\mathbf A-\lambda\mathbf I)=0$.
  4. Symmetric matrices are extra-nice: their eigenvectors are mutually perpendicular, so we can build an orthogonal $\mathbf P$ with eigenvectors as columns and diagonalise: $\mathbf P^\top\mathbf{AP}=\mathbf D$ (eigenvalues on diagonal).
  5. The whole chapter is: extend $2\times 2$ tricks → 3D, then learn to find directions that don't change (eigenvectors), and exploit that for symmetric matrices via diagonalisation.
