The whole topic in one mental model. Electrical power is just *energy delivered per second* — and there are three faces of the same idea: $P = VI$ (the master equation, always true), $P = I^2R$ (the heating-effect form, for what's wasted as heat), and $P = V^2/R$ (the voltage-driven form). They are not three separate laws — substitute Ohm's law $V = IR$ into $P = VI$ and the other two pop out. The microscopic story is electrons colliding with the lattice ions, making them vibrate (= heat). Multiply any power formula by time and you get energy ($W = Pt = VIt = I^2Rt$). The whole topic is: pick the right power formula for the quantities you're given, never take the roundabout route to energy, get fuses right ($I = P/V$ then round *up*), and quote efficiency as a percentage.

5. Big picture (last-minute recall)

  • Three power formulas are one equation in disguise: $P = VI$, then sub $V = IR$ to get $P = I^2R$, or $I = V/R$ to get $P = V^2/R$.
  • Pick by your givens: $I,V\to VI$; $I,R\to I^2R$; $V,R\to V^2/R$.
  • Scaling: double $I$ ⇒ ×4 power; double $V$ ⇒ ×4 power ($P\propto I^2$, $P\propto V^2$).
  • Energy: go direct — $W = Pt = VIt = I^2Rt$. Only use $E = QV$ when charge $Q$ is given.
  • Heating effect: electrons collide with lattice ions ⇒ ions vibrate more ⇒ heat; rate $= I^2R$. Electrons are slowed, not stored (charge in = charge out).
  • Fuse: $I = P/V$ (not $V/P$), then choose the next standard rating above (3 A / 5 A / 13 A).
  • Resistance from rating: $R = V^2/P$ (voltage squared on top).
  • Efficiency: $\eta = P_{useful}/P_{total}\times100\%$ — always a percentage, never $>100\%$.
  • Watch the slips: $R = V/I$ not $IV$; $V = W/Q$ not $W/t$; expand prefixes ($250$ mW $= 0.25$ W) before substituting.
