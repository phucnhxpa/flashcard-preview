FP1 Ch 1 тАФ Complex Numbers

The whole chapter in one mental model. Extend $\mathbb{R}$ by inventing $\mathrm{i}=\sqrt{-1}$, so every quadratic is solvable. A complex number $z=a+b\mathrm{i}$ has a Cartesian face $(a,b)$ on the Argand diagram and a polar face $(r,\theta)=(|z|,\arg z)$. The real-coefficient rule "non-real roots come in conjugate pairs" then collapses cubic/quartic root-finding into easy algebra: pair up $z$ with $z^*$, multiply, divide out a quadratic factor.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Imaginary unit $\mathrm{i}$ The invented number satisfying $\mathrm{i}^2=-1$, equivalently $\mathrm{i}=\sqrt{-1}$. It's "the missing square root" тАФ there is no real number whose square is negative, so we name one and treat it algebraically like any other letter. Powers cycle every 4: $\mathrm{i},-1,-\mathrm{i},1$. 1.1
Imaginary number A real multiple of $\mathrm{i}$, i.e. $b\mathrm{i}$ with $b\in\mathbb{R}$. Examples: $5\mathrm{i}$, $-\sqrt{7}\mathrm{i}$, $0$. NOT just "any complex number" тАФ imaginary numbers have zero real part. 1.1
Complex number A number $z=a+b\mathrm{i}$ where $a,b\in\mathbb{R}$. Real part $\operatorname{Re}(z)=a$, imaginary part $\operatorname{Im}(z)=b$ (note: $b$, not $b\mathrm{i}$). The set of all such numbers is denoted $\mathbb{C}$, which contains $\mathbb{R}$ as the special case $b=0$. 1.1
Real / imaginary part For $z=a+b\mathrm{i}$: $\operatorname{Re}(z)=a$ is a real number; $\operatorname{Im}(z)=b$ is also a real number (the coefficient of $\mathrm{i}$, not the term $b\mathrm{i}$). They cannot be combined into a single term тАФ they live on independent axes. 1.1
Complex conjugate $z^*$ For $z=a+b\mathrm{i}$, the conjugate is $z^*=a-b\mathrm{i}$ тАФ flip the sign of the imaginary part. Geometrically, reflection in the real axis. Two key results: $z+z^*=2a$ (real, twice the real part) and $zz^*=a^2+b^2=|z|^2$ (real, the modulus squared). 1.3
Conjugate pair The two numbers $z$ and $z^*$ taken together. Any non-real root of a real-coefficient polynomial comes paired with its conjugate тАФ that's why complex roots always show up "two at a time" in real-coefficient cubics and quartics. 1.3, 1.7
Argand diagram The 2D plane used to picture complex numbers: horizontal "real axis" (Re), vertical "imaginary axis" (Im). The number $z=x+\mathrm{i}y$ sits at the point $(x,y)$, or equivalently as the vector $\binom{x}{y}$ from the origin. Adding/subtracting complex numbers = adding/subtracting their position vectors. 1.4
Modulus $\lvert z\rvert$ The distance from the origin to $z$ on the Argand diagram, $\lvert z\rvert=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}=r$. Always non-negative. Multiplicative: $\lvert z_1 z_2\rvert=\lvert z_1\rvert\lvert z_2\rvert$. Think of it as the "size" or "magnitude" of the complex vector. 1.5
Argument $\arg z$ The angle the position vector makes with the positive real axis, measured anticlockwise in radians. The principal argument is the unique value in $(-\pi,\pi]$. Found via $\tan\theta=y/x$ but you must check the quadrant тАФ the formula alone gives the wrong sign half the time. 1.5
Principal argument The argument restricted to the range $-\pi<\theta\leqslant\pi$. Two arguments differing by $2\pi$ describe the same complex number, but the principal one is the canonical representative. Worth committing $-\pi$ vs $\pi$ inclusion to memory. 1.5
ModulusтАУargument form The polar representation $z=r(\cos\theta+\mathrm{i}\sin\theta)$, where $r=\lvert z\rvert$ and $\theta=\arg z$. Equivalent to Cartesian via $a=r\cos\theta$, $b=r\sin\theta$. Useful because multiplication becomes "multiply moduli, add arguments" (you'll see this fully in FP2). 1.6
Discriminant $b^2-4ac$ for $az^2+bz+c=0$. If $<0$, the two roots are a non-real complex conjugate pair $\alpha\pm\beta\mathrm{i}$. The quadratic formula still works тАФ just write $\sqrt{-k}=\mathrm{i}\sqrt{k}$ for positive $k$. 1.1
Cubic equation $az^3+bz^2+cz+d=0$. With real coefficients, exactly three roots (counted with multiplicity), and either (i) all three real or (ii) one real + a complex conjugate pair. Cannot have just one non-real root alone. 1.8
Quartic equation $az^4+bz^3+cz^2+dz+e=0$. With real coefficients: four roots forming one of three patterns тАФ (i) four real, (ii) two real + one conjugate pair, (iii) two conjugate pairs. 1.8
Factor (quadratic from conjugate pair) If $\alpha=p+q\mathrm{i}$ is a root of a real-coefficient polynomial, then $(z-\alpha)(z-\alpha^*)=z^2-2pz+(p^2+q^2)$ is a real quadratic factor. Memorising this shortcut saves time vs. expanding from scratch. 1.7, 1.8
Sum & product of roots (quadratic) If a monic quadratic has roots $\alpha,\beta$ then it equals $z^2-(\alpha+\beta)z+\alpha\beta$. So sum-of-roots = $-b/a$, product-of-roots = $c/a$. Lets you reconstruct the equation from its two roots without expanding factors. 1.7
Principal square root (of a complex number) The square root with positive real part. So $\sqrt{40-42\mathrm{i}}$ has a unique answer (the one with $a>0$ in $a-b\mathrm{i}$), not two. Distinguishes which sign you take when extracting roots. 1.2 challenge

2. Algebra of complex numbers (Sections 1.1тАУ1.3)

Operation Formula Result type Key insight
Add $z_1+z_2$ $(a+b\mathrm{i})+(c+d\mathrm{i})=(a+c)+(b+d)\mathrm{i}$ Complex Add component-wise; same as vector addition
Subtract $z_1-z_2$ $(a+b\mathrm{i})-(c+d\mathrm{i})=(a-c)+(b-d)\mathrm{i}$ Complex Same тАФ component-wise
Scalar multiply $kz$ $k(a+b\mathrm{i})=ka+kb\mathrm{i}$ Complex Distribute as normal
Multiply $z_1 z_2$ $(a+b\mathrm{i})(c+d\mathrm{i})=(ac-bd)+(ad+bc)\mathrm{i}$ Complex Expand brackets, use $\mathrm{i}^2=-1$
Conjugate $z^*$ $(a+b\mathrm{i})^*=a-b\mathrm{i}$ Complex Flip sign of imaginary part
Sum with conjugate $z+z^*$ $2a$ Real "Realification" тАФ used in roots problems
Product with conjugate $zz^*$ $a^2+b^2=\lvert z\rvert^2$ Real, $\geqslant 0$ "Magnitude squared" тАФ engine of division
Divide $z_1/z_2$ $\dfrac{a+b\mathrm{i}}{c+d\mathrm{i}}\times\dfrac{c-d\mathrm{i}}{c-d\mathrm{i}}=\dfrac{(ac+bd)+(bc-ad)\mathrm{i}}{c^2+d^2}$ Complex Multiply top & bottom by $z_2^*$ тАФ denominator becomes real
Powers of $\mathrm{i}$ $\mathrm{i}^1=\mathrm{i}$, $\mathrm{i}^2=-1$, $\mathrm{i}^3=-\mathrm{i}$, $\mathrm{i}^4=1$ Cycles mod 4 $\mathrm{i}^{4k+r}=\mathrm{i}^r$. E.g. $\mathrm{i}^{253}=\mathrm{i}^{4\cdot63+1}=\mathrm{i}$

3. Modulus & argument: Cartesian тЖФ polar (Sections 1.5тАУ1.6)

Direction Formula Watch out
Cartesian тЖТ modulus $r=\lvert z\rvert=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ Always positive; a length
Cartesian тЖТ argument $\tan\theta=b/a$, then adjust by quadrant (table below) $\arctan(b/a)$ alone gives wrong sign in Q2/Q3
Polar тЖТ Cartesian $a=r\cos\theta$, $b=r\sin\theta$ Substitute and simplify
Modulus product rule $\lvert z_1 z_2\rvert=\lvert z_1\rvert\lvert z_2\rvert$ Multiplicative тАФ same for $\lvert z^n\rvert=\lvert z\rvert^n$
Modulus quotient rule $\lvert z_1/z_2\rvert=\lvert z_1\rvert/\lvert z_2\rvert$ Useful in modulus shortcuts

Argument-by-quadrant table (let $\alpha=\arctan\lvert b/a\rvert\in[0,\pi/2)$ be the acute reference angle):

Quadrant of $z$ Sign of $a$ Sign of $b$ $\arg z$
Q1 $a>0$ $b>0$ $\alpha$
Q2 $a<0$ $b>0$ $\pi-\alpha$
Q3 $a<0$ $b<0$ $-(\pi-\alpha)$
Q4 $a>0$ $b<0$ $-\alpha$
Positive real axis $a>0$ $b=0$ $0$
Negative real axis $a<0$ $b=0$ $\pi$
Positive imag axis $a=0$ $b>0$ $\pi/2$
Negative imag axis $a=0$ $b<0$ $-\pi/2$

Mnemonic: sketch the Argand diagram first, then write $\arg z=\pm(\pi-\alpha)$ or $\pm\alpha$ by reading off where the point sits.

4. Solving polynomial equations with complex roots (Sections 1.7тАУ1.8)

Equation type Number of roots Possible patterns Strategy
Quadratic $az^2+bz+c=0$ 2 (a) two real, (b) one conjugate pair $\alpha\pm\beta\mathrm{i}$ Quadratic formula; if $b^2-4ac<0$ use $\sqrt{-k}=\mathrm{i}\sqrt{k}$
Cubic $az^3+bz^2+cz+d=0$ 3 (a) three real, (b) one real + one conjugate pair Find one real root by inspection / factor theorem тЖТ divide out тЖТ solve resulting quadratic
Quartic $az^4+bz^3+cz^2+dz+e=0$ 4 (a) four real, (b) two real + one pair, (c) two pairs Either: (i) given one complex root тЖТ form quadratic factor $z^2-2pz+(p^2+q^2)$, divide out, solve other quadratic; (ii) factorise into two quadratics by inspection

The conjugate-pair shortcut: if you're told $\alpha=p+q\mathrm{i}$ is a root of a real-coefficient polynomial, immediately:

$$(z-\alpha)(z-\alpha^*)=z^2-2pz+(p^2+q^2)$$

is a real quadratic factor тАФ divide out and solve what remains.

7. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Writing $\operatorname{Im}(a+b\mathrm{i})=b\mathrm{i}$ $\operatorname{Im}$ returns a real number: $\operatorname{Im}(a+b\mathrm{i})=b$
Using $\arctan(b/a)$ blindly for argument Always sketch and quadrant-adjust тАФ Q2/Q3 need $\pi$-corrections
Forgetting the principal range $(-\pi,\pi]$ Convert any answer outside this range by $\pm 2\pi$
Combining real and imaginary parts $a+b\mathrm{i}\to (a+b)$ They live on independent axes тАФ never merge
Dropping the "$\pm$" when taking $\sqrt{-k}$ Two roots: $\pm\mathrm{i}\sqrt{k}$
Writing "the other complex root" of a real cubic but the given root was real If the given root is real, no conjugate pair is forced тАФ find the other roots from the quadratic factor
Ambiguity in $2\sqrt{7}\mathrm{i}$ vs $2\sqrt{7\mathrm{i}}$ Write as $2\mathrm{i}\sqrt{7}$ тАФ the $\mathrm{i}$ comes before the surd
Using calculator for division but not showing the conjugate trick In "no-calculator" exercises, always show $\times\dfrac{z_2^*}{z_2^*}$ step
Cubic has no real coefficients but you assumed conjugate pair The conjugate-pair rule only holds when all coefficients are real

8. Big picture (the 30-second mental model)

  1. Existence: invent $\mathrm{i}$ with $\mathrm{i}^2=-1$ тЖТ every real polynomial now has its full quota of roots.
  2. Two faces of $z$: Cartesian $(a,b)$ for arithmetic (add, subtract, multiply, divide), polar $(r,\theta)$ for geometric intuition (length and angle).
  3. The conjugate $z^*$ is the bridge between them: $zz^*=\lvert z\rvert^2$ turns "complex denominator" into "real denominator", and $z^*$ is the reflection of $z$ across the real axis.
  4. Real-coefficient polynomials force conjugate pairing тАФ the operative theorem: if all coefficients are real and $z_0$ is a root, then $z_0^*$ is too. This collapses cubic/quartic problems to "find one root, divide, solve a quadratic."
  5. Argand diagram + quadrant table = the safe route to the argument. Always sketch first.

The chapter is one trick (introduce $\mathrm{i}$) plus its consequences for arithmetic, geometry, and root-finding.
