FP3 Ch 5 — Vectors

The whole chapter in one mental model. Vectors in 3D get two product structures: the familiar dot product $\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf b=\lvert\mathbf a\rvert\lvert\mathbf b\rvert\cos\theta$ (scalar output, "how parallel?") and the new cross product $\mathbf a\times\mathbf b=\lvert\mathbf a\rvert\lvert\mathbf b\rvert\sin\theta\,\hat{\mathbf n}$ (vector output, "perpendicular and proportional to area"). Combined, they handle every geometric question: areas via $\lvert\mathbf a\times\mathbf b\rvert$; volumes via the scalar triple product $\mathbf a\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c)$; equations of lines/planes via $(\mathbf r-\mathbf a)\times\mathbf b=\mathbf 0$ or $\mathbf r\cdot\mathbf n=p$; angles and distances via projections onto a normal or a common-perpendicular vector.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Vector (cross) product $\mathbf a\times\mathbf b=\lvert\mathbf a\rvert\lvert\mathbf b\rvert\sin\theta\,\hat{\mathbf n}$ where $\hat{\mathbf n}$ is the unit vector perpendicular to both, with direction set by right-hand rule (turn $\mathbf a$ to $\mathbf b$ — thumb shows $\hat{\mathbf n}$). Output is a vector, contrast with dot which gives a scalar. Anti-commutative: $\mathbf b\times\mathbf a=-\mathbf a\times\mathbf b$. 5.1
Determinant form The clean way to compute: $\mathbf a\times\mathbf b=\det\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf i&\mathbf j&\mathbf k\\a_1&a_2&a_3\\b_1&b_2&b_3\end{pmatrix}=(a_2b_3-a_3b_2)\mathbf i+(a_3b_1-a_1b_3)\mathbf j+(a_1b_2-a_2b_1)\mathbf k$. Expand along top row, alternating signs. 5.1
$\mathbf a\times\mathbf b=\mathbf 0$ Either $\mathbf a=\mathbf 0$, $\mathbf b=\mathbf 0$, or $\mathbf a\parallel\mathbf b$ — the cross product vanishes iff vectors are parallel (since $\sin\theta=0$ at $\theta=0,\pi$). Compare: $\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf b=0$ means perpendicular. 5.1
Right-hand rule First finger = $\mathbf a$, second = $\mathbf b$, thumb = $\mathbf a\times\mathbf b$. Equivalently, point fingers from $\mathbf a$ towards $\mathbf b$ (curl), thumb is $\hat{\mathbf n}$. 5.1
Unit vector $\hat{\mathbf n}$ A vector of length 1 in a chosen direction. To get one perpendicular to two given vectors $\mathbf a,\mathbf b$: compute $\mathbf a\times\mathbf b$, divide by its magnitude. 5.1 Ex 4
Scalar triple product $\mathbf a\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c)=\det\begin{pmatrix}a_1&a_2&a_3\\b_1&b_2&b_3\\c_1&c_2&c_3\end{pmatrix}$. Cyclic: $\mathbf a\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c)=\mathbf b\cdot(\mathbf c\times\mathbf a)=\mathbf c\cdot(\mathbf a\times\mathbf b)$. Any repeat → $0$. Sign tells "handedness". 5.3
Parallelepiped A 3D solid with six parallelogram-shaped faces (a "skewed cuboid"). Volume from three edge vectors $\mathbf a,\mathbf b,\mathbf c$ at a vertex: $V=\lvert\mathbf a\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c)\rvert$. 5.3
Tetrahedron A 3D solid with four triangular faces (4 vertices, 6 edges). Volume = $\tfrac16\lvert\mathbf a\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c)\rvert$ from three edge vectors at one vertex. (Comes from $\tfrac13\cdot\tfrac12\lvert\mathbf b\times\mathbf c\rvert\cdot h$.) 5.3
Vector equation of line $\mathbf r=\mathbf a+\lambda\mathbf b$ (parametric) or $(\mathbf r-\mathbf a)\times\mathbf b=\mathbf 0$ (cross-product form, no parameter) or $\mathbf r\times\mathbf b=\mathbf a\times\mathbf b$ — all describe the same line through point $\mathbf a$ in direction $\mathbf b$. 5.4
Cartesian equation of line $\dfrac{x-a_1}{b_1}=\dfrac{y-a_2}{b_2}=\dfrac{z-a_3}{b_3}$. Read off: a point $(a_1,a_2,a_3)$ on the line, direction $(b_1,b_2,b_3)$. 5.4
Scalar product form of plane $\mathbf r\cdot\mathbf n=p$ where $\mathbf n$ is a normal to the plane, $p=\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf n$ for any point $\mathbf a$ on the plane. The form to use for distance/angle calculations. 5.5
Vector equation of plane $\mathbf r=\mathbf a+\lambda\mathbf b+\mu\mathbf c$ where $\mathbf b,\mathbf c$ are two non-parallel vectors lying in the plane. Two parameters. 5.5
Cartesian equation of plane $ax+by+cz+d=0$, or equivalently $ax+by+cz=p$. The normal is $(a,b,c)$. 5.5
Distributive law $\mathbf a\times(\mathbf b+\mathbf c)=\mathbf a\times\mathbf b+\mathbf a\times\mathbf c$ — assumed. Combined with anti-commutativity, lets you expand brackets. 5.1
Skew lines Two lines in 3D that are NOT parallel and do NOT intersect. They have a unique common perpendicular, of length given by the skew-line distance formula. 5.6
Common perpendicular The unique line segment perpendicular to both of two skew lines. Direction is $\mathbf b\times\mathbf d$ (where $\mathbf b,\mathbf d$ are the two line directions). 5.6 Ex 33
$\mathbf r\cdot\hat{\mathbf n}=d$ form If you write the plane with a unit normal $\hat{\mathbf n}$, the constant $d=$ perpendicular distance from origin to plane. To get this form: divide $\mathbf r\cdot\mathbf n=p$ by $\lvert\mathbf n\rvert$. 5.6
Projection Component of one vector along another: $\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf b}\mathbf a=\dfrac{\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf b}{\lvert\mathbf b\rvert}$ (signed length). Used in distance derivations. 5.6

2. Cross product — computation & key properties (Section 5.1)

Property Formula / value Use
Definition $\mathbf a\times\mathbf b=\lvert\mathbf a\rvert\lvert\mathbf b\rvert\sin\theta\,\hat{\mathbf n}$ gives perpendicular to both
Determinant form $\mathbf a\times\mathbf b=\det\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf i&\mathbf j&\mathbf k\\a_1&a_2&a_3\\b_1&b_2&b_3\end{pmatrix}$ fastest computation
Anti-commutativity $\mathbf b\times\mathbf a=-\mathbf a\times\mathbf b$ order matters
Self-cross $\mathbf a\times\mathbf a=\mathbf 0$ parallel ⇒ zero
Distributive $\mathbf a\times(\mathbf b+\mathbf c)=\mathbf a\times\mathbf b+\mathbf a\times\mathbf c$ expand brackets
$\mathbf i\times\mathbf j=\mathbf k$ $\mathbf j\times\mathbf k=\mathbf i$, $\mathbf k\times\mathbf i=\mathbf j$ cyclic positive
Reverse: $\mathbf j\times\mathbf i=-\mathbf k$ $\mathbf k\times\mathbf j=-\mathbf i$, $\mathbf i\times\mathbf k=-\mathbf j$ reverse cycle
Magnitude $\lvert\mathbf a\times\mathbf b\rvert=\lvert\mathbf a\rvert\lvert\mathbf b\rvert\sin\theta$ gives $\sin\theta$ for angle finding
Self-zero $\mathbf i\times\mathbf i=\mathbf j\times\mathbf j=\mathbf k\times\mathbf k=\mathbf 0$ basic identity

Sine of angle between vectors: $\sin\theta=\dfrac{\lvert\mathbf a\times\mathbf b\rvert}{\lvert\mathbf a\rvert\lvert\mathbf b\rvert}$. (Use cosine via dot product to disambiguate acute/obtuse.)

3. Areas and volumes (Sections 5.2–5.3)

Shape Formula Notes
Triangle $OAB$ (one vertex at origin) $\tfrac12\lvert\mathbf a\times\mathbf b\rvert$ $\mathbf a,\mathbf b$ position vectors
Triangle $ABC$ (general) $\tfrac12\lvert\overrightarrow{AB}\times\overrightarrow{AC}\rvert=\tfrac12\lvert(\mathbf b-\mathbf a)\times(\mathbf c-\mathbf a)\rvert$ pick any vertex as base
Parallelogram $OABC$ $\lvert\mathbf a\times\mathbf b\rvert$ twice the triangle
Parallelogram $ABCD$ $\lvert\overrightarrow{AB}\times\overrightarrow{AD}\rvert$ adjacent sides
Parallelepiped (3 edges $\mathbf a,\mathbf b,\mathbf c$ at vertex) $\lvert\mathbf a\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c)\rvert$ base area $\times$ perp height
Tetrahedron (3 edges at one vertex) $\tfrac16\lvert\mathbf a\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c)\rvert$ $\tfrac13\cdot\tfrac12\lvert\mathbf b\times\mathbf c\rvert\cdot h$
Tetrahedron (4 vertices $A,B,C,D$) $\tfrac16\lvert\overrightarrow{AB}\cdot(\overrightarrow{AC}\times\overrightarrow{AD})\rvert$ use one vertex as origin
Pyramid with non-tetrahedral base Split into 2 tetrahedra sum the volumes

4. Equations of lines (Section 5.4)

Form Equation When to use
Parametric vector $\mathbf r=\mathbf a+\lambda\mathbf b$ most general
Cross-product (no parameter) $(\mathbf r-\mathbf a)\times\mathbf b=\mathbf 0$ exam-favourite alt form
Equivalent $\mathbf r\times\mathbf b=\mathbf a\times\mathbf b$ manipulating cross-form
Cartesian $\dfrac{x-a_1}{b_1}=\dfrac{y-a_2}{b_2}=\dfrac{z-a_3}{b_3}$ if all $b_i\ne 0$

Conversion tip: If you see $(\mathbf r-\mathbf a)\times\mathbf b=\mathbf 0$, that means $\mathbf r-\mathbf a$ is parallel to $\mathbf b$ — so $\mathbf r-\mathbf a=\lambda\mathbf b$, giving the parametric form directly.

5. Equations of planes (Section 5.5)

Form Equation Variables / inputs
Scalar product $\mathbf r\cdot\mathbf n=p$ $\mathbf n$ = normal, $p=\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf n$
Cartesian $ax+by+cz+d=0$ or $ax+by+cz=p$ normal is $(a,b,c)$
Vector parametric $\mathbf r=\mathbf a+\lambda\mathbf b+\mu\mathbf c$ $\mathbf a$ = point in plane, $\mathbf b,\mathbf c$ = two non-parallel directions
Distance form $\mathbf r\cdot\hat{\mathbf n}=d$ $\hat{\mathbf n}=\mathbf n/\lvert\mathbf n\rvert$, $d$ = signed dist from origin

To get the normal of a plane through 3 points $A,B,C$: $\mathbf n=\overrightarrow{AB}\times\overrightarrow{AC}$.

To get the normal of plane containing line $\mathbf r=\mathbf a+\lambda\mathbf b$ and external point $P$: $\mathbf n=\mathbf b\times(\overrightarrow{AP})$ where $A$ is any point on the line.

6. Angles between things (Section 5.6)

Between Formula Direction
Two lines (directions $\mathbf a,\mathbf b$) $\cos\theta=\left\lvert\dfrac{\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf b}{\lvert\mathbf a\rvert\lvert\mathbf b\rvert}\right\rvert$ cosine, use modulus for acute
Line ($\mathbf b$) and plane (normal $\mathbf n$) $\sin\theta=\left\lvert\dfrac{\mathbf b\cdot\mathbf n}{\lvert\mathbf b\rvert\lvert\mathbf n\rvert}\right\rvert$ sine — angle between line & plane = $90^\circ$ − angle between line & normal
Two planes (normals $\mathbf n_1,\mathbf n_2$) $\cos\theta=\left\lvert\dfrac{\mathbf n_1\cdot\mathbf n_2}{\lvert\mathbf n_1\rvert\lvert\mathbf n_2\rvert}\right\rvert$ cosine, modulus for acute

Mnemonic: "Lines & planes: dot the directions/normals, divide by lengths, modulus for acute. Sine when one of them is a line vs plane (because plane's representative is its normal)."

7. Distances (Section 5.6)

What Formula Method
Origin to plane $\mathbf r\cdot\mathbf n=p$ $d=\dfrac{p}{\lvert\mathbf n\rvert}$ rewrite as $\mathbf r\cdot\hat{\mathbf n}=d$
Point $\mathbf q$ to plane $\mathbf r\cdot\mathbf n=p$ $d=\dfrac{\lvert\mathbf q\cdot\mathbf n-p\rvert}{\lvert\mathbf n\rvert}$ shift and re-divide
Two parallel planes $\mathbf r\cdot\mathbf n=p_1$ and $\mathbf r\cdot\mathbf n=p_2$ $\dfrac{\lvert p_1-p_2\rvert}{\lvert\mathbf n\rvert}$ difference of distances from origin
Two skew lines $\mathbf r=\mathbf a+\lambda\mathbf b$, $\mathbf r=\mathbf c+\mu\mathbf d$ $\left\lvert\dfrac{(\mathbf a-\mathbf c)\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf d)}{\lvert\mathbf b\times\mathbf d\rvert}\right\rvert$ project $(\mathbf a-\mathbf c)$ onto common perpendicular
Two parallel lines (same direction $\mathbf b$, points $\mathbf a,\mathbf c$) $\dfrac{\lvert(\mathbf a-\mathbf c)\times\mathbf b\rvert}{\lvert\mathbf b\rvert}$ OR minimise via calculus
Point $P$ to line $\mathbf r=\mathbf a+\mu\mathbf b$ Min $\lvert\overrightarrow{AP}+\mu\mathbf b\rvert^2$ via calculus, OR $\dfrac{\lvert\overrightarrow{AP}\times\mathbf b\rvert}{\lvert\mathbf b\rvert}$ use Pythag on triangle $A,P,$ foot

Skew-line distance proof sketch: common perpendicular has direction $\mathbf b\times\mathbf d$; project $\mathbf a-\mathbf c$ onto unit vector $\dfrac{\mathbf b\times\mathbf d}{\lvert\mathbf b\times\mathbf d\rvert}$ to get the shortest-distance length.

8. Intersections (Section 5.6)

Setup Method Output
Two lines $\mathbf r=\mathbf a+\lambda\mathbf b$ and $\mathbf r=\mathbf c+\mu\mathbf d$ Equate components → 3 simultaneous equations in $\lambda,\mu$. Solve any two; check the third is consistent. Point of intersection (if consistent) or "skew" (if not)
Line $\mathbf r=\mathbf a+\lambda\mathbf b$ and plane $\mathbf r\cdot\mathbf n=p$ Substitute parametric line into plane equation, solve for $\lambda$ Single point (or all of line if $\mathbf b\cdot\mathbf n=0$ AND $\mathbf a$ is on plane)
Two planes $\mathbf r\cdot\mathbf n_1=p_1$, $\mathbf r\cdot\mathbf n_2=p_2$ Convert to Cartesian, eliminate one variable, parametrise. OR direction of intersection line is $\mathbf n_1\times\mathbf n_2$. Line of intersection $\mathbf r=\mathbf a+\lambda(\mathbf n_1\times\mathbf n_2)$

10. Common pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Computing $\mathbf a\times\mathbf b$ by hand and getting sign wrong Use the determinant; remember the negative sign on the $\mathbf j$ term
Treating $\mathbf a\times\mathbf b$ like dot product (commutative) $\mathbf b\times\mathbf a=-\mathbf a\times\mathbf b$ — order matters!
Forgetting $\mathbf i\times\mathbf k=-\mathbf j$ (NOT $+\mathbf j$) Only the cyclic order $\mathbf i\to\mathbf j\to\mathbf k\to\mathbf i$ gives positive
Using cross product to find angle without disambiguating Cross gives $\sin\theta$ — both acute and obtuse have positive sine. Use dot product for unambiguity.
Triangle area $=\lvert\mathbf a\times\mathbf b\rvert$ (forgot the half) Triangle: $\tfrac12$. Parallelogram: full.
Tetrahedron volume $=\lvert\mathbf a\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c)\rvert$ (missing $\tfrac16$) Tetrahedron: $\tfrac16$. Parallelepiped: full.
In plane-through-3-points: forgetting to plug back to find $p$ $p=\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf n$ for any of the three points
Getting line-plane angle from $\cos$ instead of $\sin$ Plane is represented by its normal; the angle between line and plane is complementary to the angle between line and normal: $\sin\alpha=\cos\theta$
Skew-line formula — using $\mathbf c-\mathbf a$ instead of $\mathbf a-\mathbf c$ Take modulus at the end so sign doesn't matter — but the symbol is $\lvert\dots\rvert$
Distance from point to plane: forgetting to subtract $p$ $d=\lvert\mathbf q\cdot\mathbf n-p\rvert/\lvert\mathbf n\rvert$ — don't forget the $-p$
Confusing line $\mathbf r\cdot\hat{\mathbf n}=d$ with $\mathbf r\cdot\mathbf n=p$ The unit-normal version's RHS is the actual distance from origin
Two lines: testing only 2 of 3 component equations Solve two; verify the third before claiming intersection

11. Big picture (the 30-second mental model)

Two products generate all the geometry:

  1. Dot product: $\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf b=\lvert\mathbf a\rvert\lvert\mathbf b\rvert\cos\theta$ → tests parallelism (vanishes when perpendicular). Gives angles via cosine.
  2. Cross product: $\mathbf a\times\mathbf b=\lvert\mathbf a\rvert\lvert\mathbf b\rvert\sin\theta\,\hat{\mathbf n}$ → tests perpendicularity (vanishes when parallel). Gives a vector normal to both. Magnitude = parallelogram area.
  3. Triple product $\mathbf a\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c)$ → parallelepiped volume; cyclic; vanishes when vectors are coplanar.
  4. Lines: $\mathbf r=\mathbf a+\lambda\mathbf b$ — direction $\mathbf b$, point $\mathbf a$.
  5. Planes: $\mathbf r\cdot\mathbf n=p$ — normal $\mathbf n$, scalar $p$.
  6. Angles: dot for line-line and plane-plane (cosine); sine for line-plane (because we measure to the plane, not its normal).
  7. Distances: project onto a unit normal (plane) or onto the common perpendicular $\mathbf b\times\mathbf d$ (skew lines).
  8. Intersections: substitute parametric form into the other equation, solve for parameter(s).

Two products + two equations (line, plane) + two scalar manipulations (dot, cross) = the chapter.
