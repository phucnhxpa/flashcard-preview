FP2 Ch 6 — Second-Order Differential Equations

Recall sheet · Edexcel IAL · Exam: 1 Jun 2026

Mental model: linear 2nd-order DE with constant coefficients $ay''+by'+cy=f(x)$ → solve homogeneous part by auxiliary equation $am^2+bm+c=0$ (gives CF), then bolt on a PI matching $f(x)$. Two arbitrary constants → two boundary conditions. Variable-coefficient DEs (e.g. Cauchy–Euler) get a substitution to flatten them to constant-coefficient form first.

1 · Jargon glossary

TermMeaningWhere
Second-order DEA differential equation whose highest derivative is $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}^2y}{\mathrm{d}x^2}=y''$. The "order" = the highest derivative that shows up. First-order has only $y'$, second-order goes up to $y''$.6.1
Linear$y$, $y'$, $y''$ each appear to power 1 and aren't multiplied together. No $yy'$, no $(y')^2$, no $\sin y$, no $y^3$. The DE is a linear combination of $y$ and its derivatives.6.1
Constant coefficientsThe numbers $a,b,c$ multiplying $y'',y',y$ are plain real constants — not functions of $x$. (Contrast: Cauchy–Euler has $x^2,x,1$ as coefficients.)6.1
HomogeneousRHS = 0, i.e. $ay''+by'+cy=0$. No "external forcing" — only the unknown $y$ and its derivatives. Free, undriven motion.6.1
Non-homogeneousRHS = some non-zero function $f(x)$, e.g. $f(x)=e^{2x}$ or $3\sin x$ or $5x^2$. The $f(x)$ is the forcing term — an external input driving the system.6.2
Auxiliary equationThe quadratic $am^2+bm+c=0$ you get by guessing $y=e^{mx}$ and asking "for which $m$ does this guess satisfy the homogeneous DE?" Sometimes called the characteristic equation.6.1
Roots $\alpha,\beta$ (a.k.a. $m$)The two solutions of the auxiliary equation. Their type — real distinct, real repeated, or complex conjugate — fixes the shape of the CF.6.1
Discriminant$b^2-4ac$ — the same quantity from the quadratic formula. Its sign tells you which of the three CF cases you're in (D / R / C).6.1
Complementary Function (CF)The general solution of the homogeneous version $ay''+by'+cy=0$. Carries the two arbitrary constants $A,B$. Think: the system's natural response when nothing is driving it.6.2
Particular Integral (PI)One specific function that satisfies the full non-homogeneous equation $ay''+by'+cy=f(x)$. No arbitrary constants — just a single solution. Think: the forced response that matches $f(x)$.6.2
General solution$y=\text{CF}+\text{PI}$. Natural response + forced response. Still has $A,B$ free until boundary conditions pin them.6.2
Particular solutionThe general solution after using boundary conditions to pin $A,B$ to specific numbers. One unique curve, not a family. (Don't confuse with particular integral — different thing.)6.3
Boundary / initial conditionsTwo given facts about $y$ — typically $y$ and $y'$ at the same $x$, or $y$ at two different $x$. Two are needed because the general solution has two unknown constants.6.3
Principle of superpositionLinearity at work: if $\mathrm{f}(x)$ and $\mathrm{g}(x)$ each solve the homogeneous DE, so does any linear combo $A\mathrm{f}(x)+B\mathrm{g}(x)$. This is why the CF has its two-term structure.6.1 Q7
Collision (with CF)Informal name for: your trial PI happens to already live inside the CF → substituting it gives $0$ instead of $f(x)$, so it can't match. Fix: multiply the trial by $x$ (or $x^2$ if there's a repeated root collision).6.2 Ex 9–10
Reducible 2nd-order DEA 2nd-order DE that isn't constant-coefficient on its face, but becomes constant-coefficient after a substitution. The exam question always hands you the substitution.6.4
Cauchy–Euler equationA DE of the form $ax^2y''+bxy'+cy=f(x)$ — the coefficients are powers of $x$. The substitution $x=e^u$ (so $u=\ln x$) flattens it to a constant-coefficient DE in $u$.6.4 Ex 13
Damping (physical context)When the DE models a vibrating system (mass-spring with friction): the three discriminant cases correspond to over-damped (Case 1, exponential decay, no oscillation), critically damped (Case 2, on the edge), and under-damped (Case 3, decaying oscillation).6.1 Ex 5

2 · Auxiliary equation → CF (§6.1)

For $ay''+by'+cy=0$, write $am^2+bm+c=0$. Three cases:

CaseDiscriminantRootsGeneral solution (CF)BehaviourTrigger example
1. Distinct real$b^2-4ac>0$$\alpha\neq\beta$ real$y=Ae^{\alpha x}+Be^{\beta x}$Two competing exponentials$2m^2+5m+3=0\to m=-\tfrac32,-1$
2. Repeated real$b^2-4ac=0$$\alpha=\beta$ (one root, double)$y=(A+Bx)e^{\alpha x}$Critical case; $x e^{\alpha x}$ piece is essential$m^2+8m+16=0\to m=-4$
3. Complex conjugate$b^2-4ac<0$$p\pm q\mathrm{i}$$y=e^{px}(A\cos qx+B\sin qx)$Decaying ($p<0$) or growing ($p>0$) oscillation$m^2-6m+34=0\to m=3\pm 5\mathrm{i}$
3a. Pure imaginary ($p=0$)$b=0,\ ac>0$$\pm q\mathrm{i}$$y=A\cos qx+B\sin qx$Undamped SHM$m^2+16=0\to m=\pm 4\mathrm{i}$

Mnemonic: D-R-C = Distinct (sum of exps), Repeated (multiply one by $x$), Complex (oscillate, with optional exponential envelope $e^{px}$).

3 · Particular Integral trial table (§6.2)

For $ay''+by'+cy=f(x)$, choose a trial PI shaped like $f(x)$ with all lower-order terms of the same family:

Form of $f(x)$Trial PINotes
$p$ (constant)$\lambda$Single unknown
$p+qx$ (linear)$\lambda+\mu x$Two unknowns
$p+qx+rx^2$ (quadratic)$\lambda+\mu x+\nu x^2$Three unknowns
$p\,e^{kx}$$\lambda e^{kx}$Single unknown — derivatives reproduce $e^{kx}$
$p\cos\omega x+q\sin\omega x$ (or one)$\lambda\cos\omega x+\mu\sin\omega x$Always include both sine and cosine, even if $f$ has only one
$p\,x\,e^{kx}$$(\lambda+\mu x)e^{kx}$Polynomial × exponential — match polynomial degree
$e^{kx}\cos\omega x$$e^{kx}(\lambda\cos\omega x+\mu\sin\omega x)$Combine families

Procedure: substitute trial into LHS, expand, equate coefficients of like terms (constants, $x$, $x^2$, $\sin$, $\cos$, $e^{kx}$, …) → solve simultaneous equations for $\lambda,\mu,\nu,\dots$.

4 · Collision rule — when trial PI is already in CF

SituationTrial PI rescueWhy
$f(x)=p\,e^{kx}$ and $k$ is a single root of auxiliaryUse $\lambda x\,e^{kx}$$\lambda e^{kx}$ satisfies homogeneous → gives 0
$f(x)=p\,e^{kx}$ and $k$ is the repeated rootUse $\lambda x^2 e^{kx}$Both $e^{kx}$ and $xe^{kx}$ are in CF
$f(x)$ polynomial AND root of auxiliary is $0$ (CF contains constant $A$)Multiply trial polynomial by $x$ (e.g. $\lambda x$ instead of $\lambda$)CF already supplies the constant
$f(x)=p\cos\omega x$ AND auxiliary roots are $\pm\omega \mathrm{i}$ (CF $=A\cos\omega x+B\sin\omega x$)Use $x(\lambda\cos\omega x+\mu\sin\omega x)$Resonance — same trick as exponential collision

Rule of thumb: if your trial PI shows up in the CF, multiply it by the smallest power of $x$ that takes it out of the CF.

5 · Solution-building algorithm (§6.2 + §6.3)

StepActionOutput
1Read off $a,b,c,f(x)$Equation in canonical form
2Write & solve auxiliary $am^2+bm+c=0$Roots $\alpha,\beta$
3Pick CF case (D / R / C) from §2$y_{\text{CF}}=A\cdot(\dots)+B\cdot(\dots)$
4Pick PI trial from §3, check §4 collision$y_{\text{PI}}$ with unknowns $\lambda,\dots$
5Differentiate trial twice, sub into LHS, equate coefficientsValues of $\lambda,\dots$
6Write general solution $y=y_{\text{CF}}+y_{\text{PI}}$Two arbitrary constants $A,B$ remain
7Apply boundary condition #1 (e.g. $y(0)=$ given)Equation in $A,B$
8Apply boundary condition #2 (e.g. $y'(0)=$ given) — differentiate first, then subSecond equation in $A,B$
9Solve simultaneous system$A,B$ pinned
10Write the particular solutionFinal $y(x)$

6 · Reducible 2nd-order DE — substitutions (§6.4)

Original DESubstitutionResultWhy
Cauchy–Euler: $ax^2y''+bxy'+cy=f(x)$$x=e^u$, so $u=\ln x$Constant-coefficient in $u$:
$a\,\dfrac{\mathrm{d}^2y}{\mathrm{d}u^2}+(b-a)\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}u}+cy=f(e^u)$		Each $x^k\dfrac{\mathrm{d}^k}{\mathrm{d}x^k}$ → polynomial in $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}u}$
Substitution given in question (e.g. $y=z/x$, $y=z/x^2$, $z=\sin x$, $x=ut$)Use exactly as givenTransforms into constant-coefficient or simpler DE in new variableStandard exam trick — problem hands you the right substitution

Cauchy–Euler chain rule kit (memorise):

$$x\frac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}=\frac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}u},\qquad x^2\frac{\mathrm{d}^2y}{\mathrm{d}x^2}=\frac{\mathrm{d}^2y}{\mathrm{d}u^2}-\frac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}u}.$$

After solving in $u$, substitute back $u=\ln x$ to express $y$ in terms of $x$.

10 · Big picture (30-second mental model)

The differential operator $L[y]=ay''+by'+cy$ is linear, so:

  1. Homogeneous core $L[y]=0$ has a 2-dimensional solution space → CF carries two basis functions and two constants.
  2. The basis is built from $e^{mx}$ trials → quadratic auxiliary $am^2+bm+c=0$.
  3. Adding a forcing term $f(x)$ (the RHS) shifts the solution by any one particular function $y_{\text{PI}}$; the full solution is CF + PI.
  4. Collisions happen when the natural trial for $f(x)$ already lives in the kernel of $L$ → "augmented" trial (multiply by $x$).
  5. Variable coefficients (Cauchy–Euler) → substitute to flatten into the constant-coefficient world, solve there, substitute back.

Two rules and three tables — that's the chapter.
