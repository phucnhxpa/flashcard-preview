FP2 Ch 2 — Series (Method of Differences)

The whole chapter in one mental model. A finite sum $\sum_{r=1}^n u_r$ collapses dramatically if you can express the general term as a difference $u_r=f(r)-f(r+1)$ — then writing out the sum, almost all middle terms cancel, leaving just $f(1)-f(n+1)$. The chapter is one technique applied to two flavours: partial-fraction telescoping ($\tfrac{1}{r(r+1)}=\tfrac{1}{r}-\tfrac{1}{r+1}$) and algebraic-identity telescoping ($r^3$ as a difference of cubes). Same recipe; different ways to spot the difference structure.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Series The sum of the terms of a sequence. In FP2 we focus on finite series $\sum_{r=1}^n u_r$ (sum of the first $n$ terms). The summand $u_r$ is the general term — a function of the index $r$. 2.1
General term $u_r$ The expression for the $r$-th term of the sequence. Whole game: rewrite $u_r$ as a difference $f(r)-f(r+1)$ (or $f(r+1)-f(r)$) so the sum collapses. 2.1
Method of differences (telescoping) Identifying $u_r=f(r)-f(r+1)$ and summing. After cancellation $\sum_{r=1}^n u_r=f(1)-f(n+1)$. The metaphor: the sum collapses like a telescope sliding shut, leaving only the endpoints. 2.1
Telescoping cancellation When you write the sum out: $u_1=f(1)-f(2),\ u_2=f(2)-f(3),\ \dots$ — each $f(2),f(3),\dots,f(n)$ appears once positive and once negative and cancels. Only $f(1)$ (from the first term) and $-f(n+1)$ (from the last) survive. 2.1
Partial fractions Decomposing $\dfrac{P(r)}{Q(r)}$ where $\deg P<\deg Q$ into a sum of simpler fractions, each with a linear denominator. The standard FP2 trick: $\dfrac{1}{r(r+1)}=\dfrac{1}{r}-\dfrac{1}{r+1}$ — automatically a difference structure. 2.1
Two-step difference $f(r)-f(r+2)$ When the natural decomposition skips by 2 (e.g. $\tfrac{2}{(r+1)(r+3)}=\tfrac{1}{r+1}-\tfrac{1}{r+3}$), only every other term cancels. After summation, four boundary terms survive: $f(1)+f(2)-f(n+1)-f(n+2)$. 2.1
Telescoping by algebraic identity Spotting that $4r^3=r^2(r+1)^2-(r-1)^2 r^2$, i.e. $u_r=g(r)-g(r-1)$ where $g(r)=r^2(r+1)^2$. Sum collapses to $g(n)-g(0)$. Used to prove standard sum formulas (e.g. $\sum r^2$, $\sum r^3$) without induction. 2.1 Ex 1
Identity $\equiv$ Same notation as equation $=$ but read as "is identically equal to" — true for every $r$, not just at solution values. Often used in FP2 to emphasise the algebraic structure. 2.1
Boundary terms / surviving terms After cancellation, the remaining unfolded terms. For 1-step difference: $f(1),\ -f(n+1)$. For 2-step: $f(1),\ f(2),\ -f(n+1),\ -f(n+2)$. Identifying these correctly is where most algebraic mistakes happen. 2.1 Ex 4
Common-denominator simplification After getting the closed form (e.g. $\tfrac{5}{6}-\tfrac{1}{n+2}-\tfrac{1}{n+3}$), combine into a single fraction with denominator $6(n+2)(n+3)$ and simplify the numerator. Final answer should be a single algebraic fraction. 2.1 Ex 4
Partial sum from $r=k$ to $r=N$ $\sum_{r=k}^N u_r=\sum_{r=1}^N u_r-\sum_{r=1}^{k-1}u_r$. Use the closed form once for upper limit, once for the offset, subtract. Same trick as FP1 §7. 2.1 Ex 4c
Verifying with small $n$ Test the closed form at $n=1,2$ to catch slips. E.g. $\sum_{r=1}^1 \tfrac{1}{r(r+1)}=\tfrac{1}{2}$, and the closed form $1-\tfrac{1}{n+1}$ at $n=1$ gives $\tfrac{1}{2}$ ✓ 2.1
Common spotting trick $u_r=\dfrac{1}{r(r+a)}$ Always partial-fractions to $\dfrac{1}{a}\left(\dfrac{1}{r}-\dfrac{1}{r+a}\right)$ — the $\dfrac{1}{a}$ pulls out front, then telescoping with skip $a$. 2.1 Ex 3

2. The core formulas (memorise these two)

Form of $u_r$ After summing
$u_r=f(r)-f(r+1)$ $\sum_{r=1}^n u_r=f(1)-f(n+1)$
$u_r=f(r+1)-f(r)$ $\sum_{r=1}^n u_r=f(n+1)-f(1)$
$u_r=f(r)-f(r+2)$ (skip 2) $\sum_{r=1}^n u_r=f(1)+f(2)-f(n+1)-f(n+2)$
$u_r=f(r)-f(r+k)$ (skip $k$) $\sum_{r=1}^n u_r=\big(f(1)+\dots+f(k)\big)-\big(f(n+1)+\dots+f(n+k)\big)$
$u_r=g(r)-g(r-1)$ (one-step backward) $\sum_{r=1}^n u_r=g(n)-g(0)$

3. The four-step recipe (memorise)

Step Action
1 Identify the difference structure. If $u_r$ is a partial fraction, do the partial-fraction decomposition. If it's polynomial, look for an algebraic identity (cube/square diffs).
2 Write out the first few terms explicitly: $u_1, u_2, u_3$, AND the last few: $u_{n-1}, u_n$.
3 Cancel the matching middle terms (cross out in pencil so it's still readable).
4 Read off the surviving terms = the closed form. Combine into a single algebraic fraction if asked.

4. Standard partial-fraction telescopes (memorise)

$u_r$ Decomposition Telescope result
$\dfrac{1}{r(r+1)}$ $\dfrac{1}{r}-\dfrac{1}{r+1}$ $1-\dfrac{1}{n+1}=\dfrac{n}{n+1}$
$\dfrac{1}{r(r+2)}$ $\dfrac{1}{2}\left(\dfrac{1}{r}-\dfrac{1}{r+2}\right)$ $\dfrac{1}{2}\left(\dfrac{3}{2}-\dfrac{1}{n+1}-\dfrac{1}{n+2}\right)=\dfrac{n(3n+5)}{4(n+1)(n+2)}$
$\dfrac{1}{(r+1)(r+2)}$ $\dfrac{1}{r+1}-\dfrac{1}{r+2}$ $\dfrac{1}{2}-\dfrac{1}{n+2}=\dfrac{n}{2(n+2)}$
$\dfrac{1}{(2r-1)(2r+1)}$ $\dfrac{1}{2}\left(\dfrac{1}{2r-1}-\dfrac{1}{2r+1}\right)$ $\dfrac{1}{2}\left(1-\dfrac{1}{2n+1}\right)=\dfrac{n}{2n+1}$
$\dfrac{1}{(2r+1)(2r+3)}$ $\dfrac{1}{2}\left(\dfrac{1}{2r+1}-\dfrac{1}{2r+3}\right)$ $\dfrac{1}{2}\left(\dfrac{1}{3}-\dfrac{1}{2n+3}\right)=\dfrac{n}{3(2n+3)}$
$\dfrac{1}{r(r+1)(r+2)}$ $\dfrac{1}{2}\left(\dfrac{1}{r(r+1)}-\dfrac{1}{(r+1)(r+2)}\right)$ $\dfrac{1}{4}-\dfrac{1}{2(n+1)(n+2)}$
$\dfrac{r}{(r+1)!}$ $\dfrac{1}{r!}-\dfrac{1}{(r+1)!}$ $1-\dfrac{1}{(n+1)!}$
$\dfrac{2r+1}{r^2(r+1)^2}$ $\dfrac{1}{r^2}-\dfrac{1}{(r+1)^2}$ $1-\dfrac{1}{(n+1)^2}=\dfrac{n(n+2)}{(n+1)^2}$

5. Algebraic-identity telescopes — proving FP1 standard sums

Sum to prove Identity to use $f(r)$ After telescoping
$\sum_{r=1}^n r$ $r=\tfrac{1}{2}(r(r+1)-r(r-1))$ $f(r)=r(r-1)$ $\tfrac{1}{2}(n(n+1)-0)=\tfrac{1}{2}n(n+1)$
$\sum_{r=1}^n r^2$ $6r^2+2=(r+1)^3-(r-1)^3$, or $r^2=\tfrac{1}{3}(r(r+1)(r+2)-...)$ Various $\tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)(2n+1)$
$\sum_{r=1}^n r^3$ $4r^3=r^2(r+1)^2-(r-1)^2r^2$ $f(r)=(r-1)^2r^2$ $4\sum=n^2(n+1)^2$, so $\sum=\tfrac{1}{4}n^2(n+1)^2$

The algebraic-identity approach gives an alternative (non-induction) proof of the standard sums.

6. The two-step skip — boundary book-keeping

When $u_r=\dfrac{1}{(r+a)(r+b)}$ with $b-a\geq 2$, the partial fraction $u_r=\tfrac{1}{b-a}\big(\tfrac{1}{r+a}-\tfrac{1}{r+b}\big)$ has skip $k=b-a$. Summing:

Skip $k$ Surviving terms
$k=1$ $f(1)$ at front, $f(n+1)$ at back
$k=2$ $f(1)+f(2)$ at front, $f(n+1)+f(n+2)$ at back
$k=3$ $f(1)+f(2)+f(3)$ at front, $f(n+1)+f(n+2)+f(n+3)$ at back
general $k$ $\sum_{j=1}^k f(j)$ at front, $-\sum_{j=1}^k f(n+j)$ at back

Worked example (Ex 4): $\sum_{r=1}^n \tfrac{2}{(r+1)(r+3)}=\sum \big(\tfrac{1}{r+1}-\tfrac{1}{r+3}\big)$. Skip 2. Surviving: $\tfrac{1}{2}+\tfrac{1}{3}-\tfrac{1}{n+2}-\tfrac{1}{n+3}=\tfrac{5}{6}-\tfrac{1}{n+2}-\tfrac{1}{n+3}$. Combine: $\tfrac{n(5n+13)}{6(n+2)(n+3)}$.

7. Decision flow — what to do in 5 seconds

Spot this Do this
$u_r$ is a fraction with quadratic denominator Partial-fractions → look for the "$f(r)-f(r+k)$" form
$u_r=\tfrac{1}{r(r+1)}$, $\tfrac{1}{(r+1)(r+2)}$ etc. Decompose to $\tfrac{1}{r}-\tfrac{1}{r+1}$ pattern (skip 1)
Difference of squares in denominator: $4r^2-1$ Factor: $(2r-1)(2r+1)$, then partial fractions
Higher-order: $\tfrac{1}{r(r+1)(r+2)}$ Use the identity given in question, or decompose to $\tfrac{1}{r(r+1)}-\tfrac{1}{(r+1)(r+2)}$ form
Polynomial $r^k$ Look for an algebraic identity expressing it as a difference of consecutive $g(r),\ g(r-1)$
"$\sum_{r=k}^n$" instead of from $r=1$ Use $\sum_{r=k}^n=\sum_{r=1}^n-\sum_{r=1}^{k-1}$, plug both into closed form
Question says "show that $\sum=\tfrac{n(an+b)}{\dots}$, find $a,b$" Derive closed form, simplify to common denominator, match form to identify $a,b$
Verify result via induction (alternative method) The textbook flag: this should also work — sometimes mentioned as an alternative
Question gives $\equiv$ identity to prove first Verify the identity (expand both sides), then use it for the sum

9. Common pitfalls (and the marks they cost)

Pitfall Fix
Stopping after writing $u_1,u_2,u_3$ — not enough to see cancellation Write out 3-4 terms at front AND 1-2 at back, with $\vdots$ in between
For skip-2, only writing $f(1)$ and $-f(n+1)$ surviving Skip 2 → FOUR boundary terms: $f(1),f(2),-f(n+1),-f(n+2)$. Don't skip $f(2)$
Partial-fraction error: getting wrong sign on $A,B$ Check by adding back: $\tfrac{A}{r}+\tfrac{B}{r+1}$ should reproduce $\tfrac{1}{r(r+1)}$. Plug $r=1$ both sides
For $\tfrac{1}{(2r-1)(2r+1)}$ — forgetting the $\tfrac{1}{2}$ multiplier Numerator of difference of two reciprocals is $\tfrac{(2r+1)-(2r-1)}{(2r-1)(2r+1)}=\tfrac{2}{(2r-1)(2r+1)}$, so $\tfrac{1}{(2r-1)(2r+1)}=\tfrac{1}{2}(\dots)$
Forgetting to combine into single fraction at the end If question wants $\tfrac{n(an+b)}{c(\dots)}$, you must combine and identify $a,b,c$
Mixing up $r=k$ vs $r=k-1$ when subtracting partial sums $\sum_{r=k}^n=\sum_{r=1}^n-\sum_{r=1}^{k-1}$ — subtract up to $k-1$ NOT $k$
Telescoping in wrong direction: $f(r+1)-f(r)$ vs $f(r)-f(r+1)$ Check signs: writing out $u_1,u_2$ should reveal which terms cancel
Treating $\equiv$ as if it were an equation to be solved $\equiv$ is an identity — true for all $r$. You verify by expansion, you don't solve
For factorial telescope $\tfrac{r}{(r+1)!}$: simply attempting partial fractions Write $r=(r+1)-1$, then $\tfrac{r}{(r+1)!}=\tfrac{(r+1)-1}{(r+1)!}=\tfrac{1}{r!}-\tfrac{1}{(r+1)!}$
Robin's mistake (Q7 chapter review): assuming partial fractions exist for any rational Need $\deg(\text{num}) < \deg(\text{denom})$; for $\tfrac{2r+1}{r(r+1)}$ check first — actually here $\deg=1 < 2$ so it works, but the actual mistake was in computing $A,B$ values

10. Big picture (the 30-second mental model)

  1. The whole technique: rewrite $u_r$ as $f(r)-f(r+1)$. The sum then telescopes to $f(1)-f(n+1)$.
  2. How to spot the difference structure:
    • Fractional $u_r$ → partial fractions (90% of FP2 cases)
    • Polynomial $u_r$ → algebraic identity (cube-of-difference, square-of-difference, factorial trick)
  3. Skip-$k$ generalisation: if $u_r=f(r)-f(r+k)$, sum is $\sum_{j=1}^k f(j) - \sum_{j=1}^k f(n+j)$. Watch boundary book-keeping.
  4. Procedure: write out first 3 + last 2 terms, cancel middle, simplify boundary, combine into single fraction.
  5. Always usable, always faster than induction for closed-form sum problems. Induction works as backup but can't find the closed form.

One technique, two flavours: telescoping is the entire chapter.
