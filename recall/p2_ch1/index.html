P2 Ch 1 — Algebraic Methods

The whole chapter in one mental model. Two big ideas: polynomial algebra (simplify fractions, divide polynomials, factor cubics via factor/remainder theorems) and mathematical proof (deduction, exhaustion, counter-example, identity proof). The factor theorem $\mathrm{f}(p)=0\Leftrightarrow(x-p)\mid\mathrm{f}(x)$ is the workhorse for cubic factorisation. The remainder theorem $\mathrm{f}(b/a)=$ remainder when divided by $(ax-b)$ avoids long division when only the remainder is wanted. Proof techniques: deduction (start from facts), identity ($\equiv$, manipulate one side), exhaustion (split into cases), counter-example (one bad example kills the statement).

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Polynomial A finite expression with non-negative integer powers of $x$. Examples: $2x+4$, $x^3-5x^2+1$, $7$. NOT polynomials: $\sqrt x$, $\dfrac{4}{x}$, $x^{-2}$. The "degree" is the highest power. 1.2
Algebraic fraction A fraction with polynomials in numerator and/or denominator, e.g. $\dfrac{x^2-9}{x^2-3x}$. Simplified by factorising both top and bottom and cancelling common factors (only over factors, never over sums). 1.1
Long division (polynomials) A step-by-step procedure mirroring long division of integers: divide leading term, multiply, subtract, "bring down" next term, repeat. Used to divide $\mathrm{f}(x)$ by $(x\pm p)$ or $(ax\pm b)$. Always include $0x^k$ for missing powers in $\mathrm{f}(x)$. 1.2
Quotient The result of polynomial division (the answer "on top"). If $\mathrm{f}(x)=(x-p)\cdot q(x)+r$ then $q(x)$ is the quotient and $r$ the remainder. Degree of quotient = degree of $\mathrm{f}$ minus degree of divisor. 1.2
Remainder The "left over" after polynomial division — a constant if dividing by linear $(ax\pm b)$. Zero remainder ⟺ the divisor is a factor. 1.2
Factor theorem $(x-p)$ is a factor of $\mathrm{f}(x)$ iff $\mathrm{f}(p)=0$. Generalisation: $(ax-b)$ is a factor iff $\mathrm{f}(b/a)=0$. The "iff" works both ways: substitute to test, or use the root to factor. 1.3
Remainder theorem When $\mathrm{f}(x)$ is divided by $(ax-b)$, the remainder is $\mathrm{f}(b/a)$. Special case $(x-p)$: remainder is $\mathrm{f}(p)$. Lets you find the remainder without doing long division. 1.4
Root / zero A value $p$ such that $\mathrm{f}(p)=0$. By the factor theorem, every root corresponds to a factor $(x-p)$. A cubic has up to 3 real roots; an $n$-th degree polynomial has up to $n$ real roots. 1.3
Identity ($\equiv$) An equation that holds for every value of the variable, e.g. $(a+b)^2\equiv a^2+2ab+b^2$. The symbol $\equiv$ ("is identically equal") emphasises this. Don't try to solve an identity; prove it by manipulating one side until it matches the other. 1.5
Conjecture A mathematical statement that is believed but not yet proven. Once proven, becomes a theorem. Examples: every even number > 2 is the sum of two primes (Goldbach — still a conjecture). 1.5
Theorem A statement that has been proven. The proof remains valid forever (assuming the foundations don't change), so theorems compose to build new mathematics. 1.5
Proof by deduction Start with known facts/axioms/theorems, apply logical steps, arrive at the desired conclusion. Most school proofs are deduction. State assumptions, show every step, write a concluding statement. 1.5
Proof by exhaustion Split the claim into a finite number of cases and verify each one separately. Useful when the variable takes only finitely many values, or splits naturally (odd/even, $n\equiv 0,1,2\pmod 3$, etc.). 1.6
Counter-example A specific instance that fails the claim. One counter-example disproves a universal statement. (You can't prove a universal statement by examples — only disprove it.) 1.6
Demonstration vs proof Showing one example ($5\times 7=35$ odd) demonstrates a special case; doesn't prove the general claim. Proof requires showing the conclusion holds for every allowed value. 1.5
Modelling odd / even integers Any odd integer = $2n+1$; any even integer = $2n$ (where $n\in\mathbb Z$). Substituting these algebraic forms is how you cover "all" odd/even cases at once. 1.5–1.6
Discriminant in proofs $b^2-4ac$ controls roots: $<0$ → no real roots; $=0$ → repeated; $>0$ → two distinct. Used to prove inequalities for parameter $k$ (e.g. "the equation $kx^2+\dots$ has no real roots → solve $b^2-4ac<0$"). Watch the edge case when leading coefficient is itself $0$. 1.5 Ex 14
Geometric proof (gradients/lengths) Right angle ⟺ product of gradients $=-1$; parallel ⟺ equal gradients; isosceles ⟺ two equal lengths (Pythagoras). Set up coordinates, compute, conclude. 1.5 Ex 13
"Jottings" (proof scratchpad) Working backwards from the target inequality to find a starting expression (often $(\dots)^2\ge 0$). Then re-write as a forward proof. The jottings are NOT the proof — only your forward chain is. 1.6 Ex 17

2. Polynomial division — long division algorithm

Goal: divide $\mathrm{f}(x)$ (degree $n$) by linear $(ax-b)$ (degree 1) to get quotient $q(x)$ (degree $n-1$) and remainder $r$ (constant).

Step Action
1 Write $\mathrm{f}(x)$ in descending powers, filling in $0x^k$ for any missing terms (e.g. $x^3-1=x^3+0x^2+0x-1$)
2 Divide leading term of dividend by leading term of divisor → first term of quotient
3 Multiply divisor by that quotient term, write below
4 Subtract → new dividend (one degree lower)
5 "Bring down" next term — repeat steps 2–4
6 Stop when remainder has degree < degree of divisor (i.e. is a constant for linear divisor)

Verify: $\mathrm{f}(x)\stackrel{?}{=}(\text{divisor})\cdot q(x)+r$. Multiply out and compare.

3. Factor theorem & remainder theorem — cross-table

Tool Statement Use case
Factor theorem $(x-p)$ factor of $\mathrm{f}(x)\Leftrightarrow\mathrm{f}(p)=0$ Test if $(x-p)$ is a factor → just compute $\mathrm{f}(p)$
Generalised factor theorem $(ax-b)$ factor of $\mathrm{f}(x)\Leftrightarrow\mathrm{f}(b/a)=0$ Test if $(2x-1)$ is a factor → compute $\mathrm{f}(\tfrac12)$
Remainder theorem Remainder when $\mathrm{f}(x)\div(x-p)$ is $\mathrm{f}(p)$ Find remainder without long division
Generalised remainder theorem Remainder when $\mathrm{f}(x)\div(ax-b)$ is $\mathrm{f}(b/a)$ E.g. $\mathrm{f}(x)\div(2x+1)$: remainder is $\mathrm{f}(-\tfrac12)$

Hunting for a factor of a cubic: try $\mathrm{f}(\pm 1),\mathrm{f}(\pm 2),\mathrm{f}(\pm 3),\dots$; for general $\mathrm{f}(b/a)$ with leading coefficient $A$ and constant $C$, try $b\mid C$ and $a\mid A$ ("rational root theorem") — this limits search to a finite list.

4. Cubic factorisation recipe

Given a cubic $\mathrm{f}(x)=ax^3+bx^2+cx+d$:

Step Action
1 Hunt for a root: try $\mathrm{f}(\pm 1),\mathrm{f}(\pm 2),\dots$ (factors of constant term $d$ over factors of leading coefficient $a$)
2 Once $\mathrm{f}(p)=0$ found, $(x-p)$ is a factor (factor theorem)
3 Divide $\mathrm{f}(x)$ by $(x-p)$ using long division → get a quadratic $\mathrm{q}(x)$
4 Factorise $\mathrm{q}(x)$ using normal techniques (factorising trinomials, quadratic formula, completing the square)
5 Final answer: $\mathrm{f}(x)=(x-p)\cdot\mathrm{q}(x)$ — fully factorised cubic
6 Solutions of $\mathrm{f}(x)=0$ are $x=p$ and the roots of $\mathrm{q}(x)=0$

If $\mathrm{q}(x)$ has discriminant $<0$, the cubic has only one real root ($x=p$).

5. Algebraic fractions — simplification techniques

Form Method
$\dfrac{ax^n+\dots}{x}$ (single $x$ denominator) Divide each term: $\dfrac{ax^n}{x}+\dots=ax^{n-1}+\dots$
$\dfrac{(x+a)\mathrm{p}(x)}{(x+a)\mathrm{q}(x)}$ (factored both sides) Cancel common factor $(x+a)$
$\dfrac{x^2+bx+c}{x+p}$ (numerator quadratic, denominator linear) Factorise numerator → cancel; or use long division
$\dfrac{x^2+\dots}{x^2+\dots}$ (both quadratics) Factorise each → cancel common factors
$\dfrac{ax^2+bx+c}{(x+p)(x+q)}$ where denominator already factored Factorise numerator using $ax^2$ techniques (split middle term), match with denominator factors

NEVER cancel over sums: $\dfrac{x+3}{x}\ne 3$. Cancellation only works over factors (multiplicative parts).

6. Proof method decision table

Situation Method Template
Prove $\mathrm{f}\equiv\mathrm{g}$ (identity) Manipulate one side Start with LHS, expand/simplify until = RHS
Prove "for all $n\in\mathbb Z$, P(n)" Deduction (algebraic) Express $n$ generically; manipulate; show conclusion
Prove "for all odd integers, P" Deduction with $n=2k+1$ Substitute, expand, show
Prove "for all even integers, P" Deduction with $n=2k$ Substitute, expand, show
Prove statement true for finitely many $n$ Exhaustion Test each case
Prove "for all $n$, statement splits by parity/mod" Exhaustion (split into cases) Handle each case algebraically
Disprove a universal claim Counter-example Find one specific value that fails
Prove inequality $A\ge B$ Consider $A-B\ge 0$, often via $(\dots)^2\ge 0$ "Consider $(x-y)^2\ge 0$, expand, rearrange to give $A\ge B$"
Prove geometric property (e.g. right angle, parallelogram) Coordinate computation Use gradient/length formulas, conclude
Prove parameter inequality (e.g. "no real roots → $0\le k<\tfrac{8}{9}$") Discriminant + edge case $b^2-4ac<0$ → factor as quadratic in $k$; check $k=0$ separately

7. Algebraic identities & worked archetypes

# Type Example Method
1 Cancel fraction $\dfrac{x^2+6x+5}{x^2+3x-10}$ Factor: $\dfrac{(x+5)(x+1)}{(x+5)(x-2)}=\dfrac{x+1}{x-2}$
2 Long division $x^3+2x^2-17x+6\div(x-3)$ Quotient $x^2+5x-2$, remainder $0$
3 Factor theorem $\mathrm{f}(x)=2x^3+3x^2-8x+3$, factor $(2x-1)$? $\mathrm{f}(\tfrac12)=2(\tfrac18)+3(\tfrac14)-4+3=0$ → yes
4 Remainder theorem Remainder when $x^3-20x+3$ is divided by $(x-4)$ $\mathrm{f}(4)=64-80+3=-13$
5 Find $a$ from remainder $8x^4-4x^3+ax^2-1\div(2x+1)$ rem $=3$ $\mathrm{f}(-\tfrac12)=3$ → solve for $a$
6 Factorise cubic $3x^3-14x^2-47x-14$ given $(x+2)$ factor Long divide → $3x^2-20x-7=(3x+1)(x-7)$
7 Identity proof $(3x+2)(x-5)(x+7)\equiv 3x^3+8x^2-101x-70$ Expand LHS step-by-step
8 Inequality proof Prove $(x+6)^2\ge 2x+11$ for all $x$ Expand: $x^2+12x+36\ge 2x+11\Leftrightarrow(x+5)^2\ge 0$ ✓
9 Discriminant proof $kx^2+3kx+2=0$ no real roots → $0\le k<\tfrac{8}{9}$ $9k^2-8k<0\Rightarrow k(9k-8)<0\Rightarrow 0
10 Geometric proof $A(1,1),B(3,3),C(4,2)$ right-angled? Gradients $1,-1,\tfrac13$; product $1\times(-1)=-1$ → $\angle ABC=90°$
11 Counter-example Disprove "$3n+3$ multiple of 6" $n=2$ gives $9$, not a multiple of 6

8. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Forgetting $0x^k$ filler in long division Always rewrite $\mathrm{f}(x)$ with all powers explicit, including 0-coefficient ones
Trying to "solve" an identity instead of proving it Identities aren't solved — manipulate one side to match the other
Cancelling over a sum: $\dfrac{x+3}{x}\to 3$ Cancellation only over factors (multiplicative units)
Sign error with $\mathrm{f}(b/a)$: e.g. $(2x+1)\to a=2,b=-1$, so use $\mathrm{f}(-\tfrac12)$ Compare to $(ax-b)$: $(2x+1)=(2x-(-1))$ → $b/a=-\tfrac12$
One example used as proof One example demonstrates, doesn't prove. Use algebra ($n=2k$ etc.) to cover all cases
In discriminant inequality proof, ignoring $k=0$ edge case The discriminant assumes $k\ne 0$; check $k=0$ separately and combine intervals
In geometric proof, omitting "the gradients differ → not collinear" before claiming triangle Triangles need non-collinearity: state this
Using $\ne$ instead of $\not\equiv$ $\equiv$ is for identities, $=$ is for equations; respect convention
Working backwards from goal in inequality proof, presenting it as forward proof Use jottings privately; restart from $(\dots)^2\ge 0$ moving forward to goal
Counter-example: giving multiple instances when one suffices One counter-example is enough; just verify it fails
Cubic factorisation: dividing by $(x+p)$ when factor is $(x-p)$ $\mathrm{f}(p)=0$ → factor is $(x-p)$, not $(x+p)$
In remainder theorem with $(ax-b)$: dividing answer by $a$ The remainder is just $\mathrm{f}(b/a)$ — no further scaling

9. Big picture (30-second mental model)

Polynomial side: long division reduces a high-degree polynomial to (linear divisor) × (lower-degree quotient) + remainder. The factor and remainder theorems give shortcuts: $\mathrm{f}(p)=0$ ⟺ $(x-p)$ is a factor; otherwise $\mathrm{f}(p)$ is the remainder. Cubics are factorised by hunting for a rational root, then dividing out.

Proof side: a proof is a logical chain from known facts to a conclusion that holds in every case. Three positive techniques: (i) deduction — algebraic manipulation from generic representation; (ii) identity proof — manipulate one side until it matches the other; (iii) exhaustion — split into cases. One negative technique: counter-example — disprove with a single failing case.

The chapter is essentially "polynomial bookkeeping" + "logical hygiene". Both come up everywhere later: factor theorem powers cubic equation solving (P3 binomial, integration), and proof-writing reappears throughout pure maths and FP1/FP2.
