FP3 Ch 1 â€” Hyperbolic Functions

The whole chapter in one mental model. Hyperbolic functions are exponentials in disguise: $\sinh,\cosh,\tanh$ are built from $e^x\pm e^{-x}$ and behave almost like trig functions but with $-\sin\sin$ wherever you'd see $\sin\sin$ (Osborn's rule). Their inverses are logs in disguise: $\operatorname{arsinh}x=\ln(x+\sqrt{x^2+1})$, etc. So everything in this chapter reduces to manipulating $e^x$ and $\ln$.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
$\sinh x$ "Hyperbolic sine" â€” defined as $\sinh x \equiv \dfrac{e^x-e^{-x}}{2}$. Odd function, $\sinh 0=0$, $\sinh(-x)=-\sinh x$. Range all of $\mathbb R$. Pronounced "shine". Resembles $\sin$ but is unbounded â€” grows like $\tfrac12 e^x$ for large $x$. 1.1
$\cosh x$ "Hyperbolic cosine" â€” defined as $\cosh x \equiv \dfrac{e^x+e^{-x}}{2}$. Even function, $\cosh 0=1$, $\cosh(-x)=\cosh x$. Range $[1,\infty)$. The shape of a hanging chain (catenary). Always $\ge 1$. 1.1
$\tanh x$ $\tanh x \equiv \dfrac{\sinh x}{\cosh x}=\dfrac{e^x-e^{-x}}{e^x+e^{-x}}=\dfrac{e^{2x}-1}{e^{2x}+1}$. Odd, asymptotes $y=\pm 1$, range $(-1,1)$. Used in neural-network activation; resembles a smoothed step function. 1.1
$\operatorname{cosech}x,\ \operatorname{sech}x,\ \coth x$ Reciprocals: $\operatorname{cosech}x=1/\sinh x$, $\operatorname{sech}x=1/\cosh x$, $\coth x=1/\tanh x$. Same role as cosec, sec, cot in trig. Note $\operatorname{sech}x$ is even (range $(0,1]$), $\operatorname{cosech}x$ and $\coth x$ are odd. 1.1
Catenary The curve a uniform hanging chain forms under gravity â€” graphically $y=a\cosh(x/a)$. Inverted, it's the most efficient self-supporting arch (St Louis Gateway, Casa MilÃ ). Intro
Inverse hyperbolic $\operatorname{arsinh},\operatorname{arcosh},\operatorname{artanh}$ The inverse functions, named "ar-" not "arc-" (because there's no arc â€” they're related to areas of hyperbolas). Reflect $y=\sinh x$ etc. in the line $y=x$. Sometimes written $\sinh^{-1}, \cosh^{-1}, \tanh^{-1}$. 1.3
Domain restriction for $\operatorname{arcosh}$ Since $\cosh x$ is even (not 1-1), restrict to $x\ge 0$ before inverting. So $\operatorname{arcosh}x$ is defined for $x\ge 1$ and gives $y\ge 0$. 1.3
Logarithmic forms (formula booklet) $\operatorname{arsinh}x=\ln(x+\sqrt{x^2+1})$, $\operatorname{arcosh}x=\ln(x+\sqrt{x^2-1})$ ($x\ge 1$), $\operatorname{artanh}x=\tfrac12\ln\!\dfrac{1+x}{1-x}$ ($\lvert x\rvert<1$). Derived by solving $y=\sinh x$ etc. as a quadratic in $e^y$. 1.3
Pythagorean-style identity $\cosh^2 A-\sinh^2 A\equiv 1$ (booklet). Plays the role of $\sin^2+\cos^2=1$ but with a minus â€” that's why $(\cosh,\sinh)$ parametrise a hyperbola, not a circle. 1.4
Addition formulas $\sinh(A\pm B)\equiv \sinh A\cosh B\pm\cosh A\sinh B$ and $\cosh(A\pm B)\equiv \cosh A\cosh B\pm \sinh A\sinh B$. Watch the cosh sign: it has $+\sinh\sinh$ in $\cosh(A+B)$, opposite to trig. 1.4
Osborn's rule Convert any trig identity into the hyperbolic version: replace $\cos\to\cosh$, $\sin\to\sinh$, then negate every product (or implied product, like $\tan^2$) of two sin/sinh terms. Why: $\sinh(ix)=i\sin x$, so each $\sinh\sinh$ pair carries an $i^2=-1$. 1.4
Implied sinh-product A "hidden" product of two $\sinh$ inside a quotient â€” e.g. $\tan^2 A=\dfrac{\sin^2 A}{\cos^2 A}$ becomes $-\tanh^2 A$ in the hyperbolic version. Same for $\tan A\tan B\to-\tanh A\tanh B$. 1.4 Ex 15
Quadratic-in-$e^x$ trick To solve $a\sinh x+b\cosh x=c$ or $\cosh^2 x+\dots$ when no clean identity reduces it: substitute the exponential definitions, multiply by $e^x$ to clear $e^{-x}$, get a quadratic in $u=e^x$, solve, then $x=\ln u$ (real only if $u>0$). 1.4 Ex 17
Identity-substitution trick First reduce equation to one hyperbolic function using $\cosh^2-\sinh^2=1$ or $\cosh 2x=1+2\sinh^2 x=2\cosh^2 x-1$, then solve the resulting quadratic. 1.4 Ex 18, 19
Range pitfall ($\cosh x=k$) $\cosh x=k$ has two real solutions (one $+$, one $-$) when $k>1$, one solution ($x=0$) when $k=1$, no solutions when $k<1$. In contrast $\sinh x=k$ always has exactly one real solution. 1.4 Ex 19

6. Solving hyperbolic equations â€” strategy by form

Form of equation First move Then
Single $\sinh x=k$, $\cosh x=k$, $\tanh x=k$ Quote inverse log form Express $x$ as a $\ln$
Mixed linear $a\sinh x+b\cosh x=c$ (no nice identity reduces) Substitute exponential definitions, $\times e^x$ Quadratic in $e^x$, solve, $x=\ln(\text{positive root})$
$\cosh 2x$ + $\cosh x$ or $\sinh x$ terms Use $\cosh 2x=2\cosh^2 x-1$ (or $1+2\sinh^2 x$) Quadratic in $\cosh x$ (or $\sinh x$); read off, take $\ln$ via inverse
$\sinh^2 x$ + $\cosh x$ etc. Use $\sinh^2 x=\cosh^2 x-1$ Quadratic in $\cosh x$
$\sinh 2x$ + $\sinh x$ or $\cosh x$ $\sinh 2x=2\sinh x\cosh x$ Factor and split

9. Big picture (the 30-second mental model)

Hyperbolic functions are just clever combinations of $e^x$ and $e^{-x}$:

  1. $\sinh x=\dfrac{e^x-e^{-x}}{2}$, $\cosh x=\dfrac{e^x+e^{-x}}{2}$ â€” same as trig but with $i$'s removed: $\sinh x=-i\sin(ix)$, $\cosh x=\cos(ix)$.
  2. One Pythagorean identity $\cosh^2-\sinh^2=1$ generates everything else, plus addition formulas analogous to trig but with Osborn's rule flipping every $\sin\sin$ product's sign.
  3. Inverse functions turn back into logarithms via the quadratic-in-$e^x$ trick: $\operatorname{arsinh}x=\ln(x+\sqrt{x^2+1})$, etc.
  4. Solving equations: either reduce to one hyperbolic function via an identity â†’ solve as quadratic in that function â†’ invert via $\ln$, OR sub the exponential definitions and solve as a quadratic in $e^x$.

Three definitions, one identity, three log inverses, one Osborn rule â€” that's the chapter.
