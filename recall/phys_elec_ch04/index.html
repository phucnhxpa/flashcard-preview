The whole topic in one mental model. Resistance is just $R = V/I$ — voltage divided by current, *never* multiplied. A component obeys Ohm's law only while its temperature is held constant (straight $I$–$V$ line through the origin, constant gradient $1/R$). The moment temperature changes, $R$ changes — and the direction depends on the material: in a metal more heat means more lattice vibration, more electron–ion collisions, lower drift velocity, so $R$ *rises* (positive temperature coefficient, PTC). In a semiconductor / NTC thermistor more heat frees more charge carriers, so $R$ *falls* (negative temperature coefficient) — the exact opposite. Push the temperature the other way, far down, and a superconductor snaps to $\rho = 0$ exactly below its critical temperature $T_c$: zero resistance, zero heating, current that flows forever. Three materials, three different $R$–$T$ stories on one graph.

4. Big picture (last-minute recall)

  • $R = V/I$ — divide $V$ by $I$, never multiply. (Phuc's logged slip: $R = IV$ is WRONG.)
  • Ohm's law holds only at constant temperature: $I \propto V$, straight line through origin, $R$ constant. Both halves needed for the mark.
  • Non-ohmic curve: read $R = V/I$ *at the point*; never use the gradient. For a target $R$, use the graphical-intersection method (not the old fixed current).
  • Resistivity $\rho = RA/L$ is a material property in $\Omega\,$m; resistance depends on shape, $\rho$ does not. $A = \pi d^2/4$ — a 2% error in $d$ gives 4% in $\rho$.
  • Metal: $T\uparrow \Rightarrow R\uparrow$ (more lattice vibration, lower drift velocity, $n$ fixed) — PTC.
  • NTC thermistor: $T\uparrow \Rightarrow R\downarrow$ (more free carriers) — NTC. Opposite to the metal — the key trap.
  • Superconductor: below $T_c$, $\rho = 0$ *exactly*, no heating, $P = I^2R = 0$ — lossless cables, MRI magnets, maglev, particle accelerators.
  • Power: $P = IV = I^2R = V^2/R$. Fuse check: $I = P/V$ (e.g. kettle $3000/240 = 12.5$ A $< 13$ A).
  • Prefix trap: $40$ mA $= 40{\times}10^{-3}$ A, NOT $40{\times}10^{-6}$ A. m = milli = $10^{-3}$; $\mu$ = micro = $10^{-6}$.
