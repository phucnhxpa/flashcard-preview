FP2 Ch 5 — First-Order Differential Equations

The whole chapter in one mental model. Three techniques for first-order ODEs, in order of increasing scope: (1) Separation of variables when $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=f(x)g(y)$ — split, integrate both sides; (2) Integrating factor when in linear form $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+Py=Q$ — multiply by $I=e^{\int P\,dx}$, the LHS becomes $\dfrac{d}{dx}(Iy)$ exact, integrate; (3) Substitution when neither directly works — apply a given $y=xz$, $z=y^{-1}$, $u=y-x$ etc. to reduce to a separable or linear form, then back-substitute. The sketch and boundary conditions pin a particular solution.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
First-order DE A differential equation involving the first derivative $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ but no higher derivatives. The "order" is the highest derivative present. Generic shape: $F\left(x,y,\dfrac{dy}{dx}\right)=0$. 5.1
General solution The full family of solutions, with one arbitrary constant of integration $c$ (or $A=e^c$). For $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=2$ the general solution is $y=2x+c$. Each value of $c$ picks out one curve. 5.1
Particular solution A specific solution where the arbitrary constant is fixed by a boundary/initial condition. E.g. given $y=1$ at $x=0$, you pin $c$ to a number. 5.1
Family of solution curves The set of all curves $y=f(x;c)$ obtained by varying $c$ in the general solution. Sketches show several values of $c$. 5.1
Boundary / initial condition A given fact like "$y=1$ when $x=0$" used to fix the arbitrary constant. First-order needs ONE such condition (vs two for second-order). 5.1
Separable variables DE form $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=f(x)g(y)$ where $x$ and $y$ can be split apart: $\dfrac{1}{g(y)}dy=f(x)dx$. Integrate both sides independently. 5.1
Linear DE (1st order) A DE of the form $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+P(x)y=Q(x)$ — linear in $y$ and $y'$. Note $P,Q$ are functions of $x$ only (NOT $y$). 5.2
Exact derivative LHS of a DE that is already a recognisable derivative of a product, e.g. $x^3\dfrac{dy}{dx}+3x^2y=\dfrac{d}{dx}(x^3y)$. Integrating immediately gives the answer. 5.2
Integrating factor $I=e^{\int P\,dx}$ A function $I(x)$ such that multiplying the linear DE by it makes the LHS exact: $I\dfrac{dy}{dx}+IPy=\dfrac{d}{dx}(Iy)$. Derived from the exactness condition $\dfrac{dI}{dx}=IP$. 5.2
General linear DE solution formula $Iy=\int IQ\,dx+c$, i.e. $y=\dfrac{1}{I}\int IQ\,dx+\dfrac{c}{I}$. The $c/I$ term is the complementary part (homogeneous solution); the rest is the particular part. 5.2
Reducible DE A DE that's not separable or linear directly, but becomes one of those via a given substitution. The exam question hands you the substitution; you transform, solve, then back-substitute. 5.3
Bernoulli equation The form $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+P(x)y=Q(x)y^n$ for $n\neq 0,1$. Substitute $z=y^{1-n}$ (i.e. $z=y^{-(n-1)}$) → linear in $z$. 5.3 (proof in Q6)
Homogeneous DE (1st order) DE of form $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=F(y/x)$ — RHS depends only on the ratio $y/x$. Substitute $z=y/x$ (so $y=xz$, $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=z+x\dfrac{dz}{dx}$) → separable in $z,x$. 5.3
$P(x)$, $Q(x)$ Standard notation for the coefficient and forcing functions in the linear form $y'+Py=Q$. After multiplying by $I=e^{\int P}$, the equation becomes $\dfrac{d}{dx}(Iy)=IQ$. 5.2
Sketching a family Pick a few values of $c$ (e.g. $c=-2,-1,0,1,2$) and plot each curve. Often need to identify shape: lines, circles, parabolas, hyperbolas, exponentials. 5.1
"Show that $\dots$" substitution proof Given substitution $y=xz$ (etc.), differentiate using product rule (treat $z$ as a function of $x$): $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=z+x\dfrac{dz}{dx}$. Substitute into original DE. Algebraic simplification yields target form. 5.3

2. The three techniques (decision tree)

When DE looks like Technique Step
$\dfrac{dy}{dx}=f(x)g(y)$ — variables split cleanly Separation Split, integrate both sides, add $c$
$\dfrac{dy}{dx}+P(x)y=Q(x)$ — linear in $y$ Integrating factor $I=e^{\int P\,dx}$; multiply, $Iy=\int IQ\,dx+c$
Other (Bernoulli, homogeneous, etc.) Substitution (given) Apply substitution → reduce to one of the above → solve → back-substitute
Already exact LHS recognisable as $\dfrac{d}{dx}(\text{product})$ Direct integration Skip integrating factor, just integrate the exact derivative

3. Separation of variables — the recipe

Step Action
1 Rearrange $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=f(x)g(y)$
2 Separate: $\dfrac{1}{g(y)}dy=f(x)dx$
3 Integrate both sides; add ONE constant $c$ on the right
4 Solve for $y$ if asked, or leave implicit if more natural
5 (If boundary condition given) Substitute to find $c$
6 (If asked to sketch) Identify the shape (lines, circles, parabolas, etc.) and draw curves for several values of $c$

Common results table (memorise):

$\dfrac{dy}{dx}=$ Solution Family shape
$2$ $y=2x+c$ Parallel lines, gradient 2
$-x/y$ $x^2+y^2=2c$ Circles, centre origin
$-y/x$ $y=A/x$ Rectangular hyperbolas
$y/(2x)$ $y^2=4ax$ where $a=$const Family of parabolas
$\sqrt{x}$ $y=\tfrac{2}{3}x^{3/2}+c$ Stacked $x^{3/2}$ curves
$y$ $y=Ae^x$ Exponentials
$y\cot x$ $y=A\sin x$ Sine curves with varying amplitude

4. Integrating factor — the recipe

For $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+P(x)y=Q(x)$:

Step Action
1 Get into standard form: divide through if needed so the coefficient of $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ is 1
2 Identify $P(x)$
3 Compute $I(x)=e^{\int P(x)\,dx}$ — no arbitrary constant in this integral
4 Multiply DE by $I$: get $\dfrac{d}{dx}(Iy)=IQ$
5 Integrate: $Iy=\int IQ\,dx+c$
6 Solve for $y$: $y=\dfrac{1}{I}\int IQ\,dx+\dfrac{c}{I}$
7 (If boundary condition given) Sub to find $c$

Useful integrating-factor identities (memorise):

$P(x)$ $\int P\,dx$ $I=e^{\int P}$
$k$ (constant) $kx$ $e^{kx}$
$1/x$ $\ln\lvert x\rvert$ $\lvert x\rvert$ (or just $x$ if $x>0$)
$n/x$ $n\ln\lvert x\rvert$ $\lvert x\rvert^n$
$\tan x$ $\ln\lvert\sec x\rvert$ $\sec x$
$2\tan x$ $2\ln\lvert\sec x\rvert=\ln\sec^2 x$ $\sec^2 x$
$\cot x$ $\ln\lvert\sin x\rvert$ $\sin x$
$-x$ $-x^2/2$ $e^{-x^2/2}$
$\sin x$ $-\cos x$ $e^{-\cos x}$

Pro tip: if $P(x)=\dfrac{f'(x)}{f(x)}$, then $\int P\,dx=\ln\lvert f(x)\rvert$, so $I=\lvert f(x)\rvert$ — gives you $I=$ a polynomial or trig directly.

5. Substitution methods — the four standard ones

Substitution DE shape it tackles New equation
$y=xz$ (so $z=y/x$, $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=z+x\dfrac{dz}{dx}$) Homogeneous: $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=F(y/x)$ Separable in $z,x$
$z=y^{-1}$ (so $y=z^{-1}$, $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-z^{-2}\dfrac{dz}{dx}$) Bernoulli with $n=2$: $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+Py=Qy^2$ Linear in $z$
$z=y^{1-n}$ general Bernoulli Bernoulli: $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+Py=Qy^n$ Linear in $z$: $\dfrac{dz}{dx}+(1-n)Pz=(1-n)Q$
$u=y-x$ or $u=y+kx$ (so $\dfrac{du}{dx}=\dfrac{dy}{dx}\mp 1$) DE where RHS depends only on $y\pm x$ Often separable in $u,x$

Universal procedure:

  1. Write down the substitution (e.g. $y=xz$).
  2. Differentiate (use product rule!) to get $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ in terms of $\dfrac{dz}{dx},x,z$.
  3. Substitute into original DE.
  4. Simplify until separable or linear.
  5. Solve for $z$ (or $u$).
  6. Back-substitute to express in $x,y$.

6. Worked archetypes (memorise the shape)

# DE Method Solution
1 $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\dfrac{x}{y}$ Separable: $\int y\,dy=-\int x\,dx$ $x^2+y^2=2c$ — circles
2 $\dfrac{dx}{dt}=\sqrt{x}$ Separable: $\int x^{-1/2}dx=\int dt$ $x=\big(\tfrac{t+c}{2}\big)^2$ — parabolas
3 $\dfrac{dy}{dx}-4y=e^x$ Linear: $I=e^{-4x}$, $Iy=\int e^{-3x}dx$ $y=-\tfrac{1}{3}e^x+ce^{4x}$
4 $\cos x\dfrac{dy}{dx}+2y\sin x=\cos^4 x$ Divide by $\cos x$: $y'+2y\tan x=\cos^3 x$. $I=\sec^2 x$ $y=\cos^2 x(\sin x+c)$
5 $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{x^2+3y^2}{2xy}$ Sub $y=xz$: $x\dfrac{dz}{dx}=\dfrac{1+z^2}{2z}$ $y^2=Ax^3-x^2$ (so $1+z^2=Ax$)
6 $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+xy=xy^2$ Bernoulli ($n=2$): $z=y^{-1}\to z'-xz=-x$. $I=e^{-x^2/2}$ $y=\dfrac{1}{1+ce^{x^2/2}}$
7 $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{y-x+2}{y-x+3}$ Sub $u=y-x$: $\dfrac{du}{dx}=\dfrac{-1}{u+3}$ $(y-x)^2+6(y-x)+2x=2c$
8 $x^3\dfrac{dy}{dx}+3x^2y=\sin x$ LHS is exact derivative $\dfrac{d}{dx}(x^3y)$ $y=-\dfrac{\cos x}{x^3}+\dfrac{c}{x^3}$

7. Decision flow — choose the technique in 5 seconds

Spot this Do this
RHS factors as $f(x)g(y)$ Separate variables
$\dfrac{dy}{dx}+P(x)y=Q(x)$ form (no $y^2$ etc., no $y$ in $P,Q$) Integrating factor $e^{\int P\,dx}$
LHS already $\dfrac{d}{dx}(\text{product})$ Direct integration (no need to find $I$)
RHS has $y/x$ ratio, or homogeneous in $x,y$ degree Sub $y=xz$ → separable
$\dfrac{dy}{dx}+P(x)y=Q(x)y^n$, $n\neq 0,1$ Bernoulli sub $z=y^{1-n}$ → linear
$\dfrac{dy}{dx}=F(y\pm kx)$ Sub $u=y\pm kx$ → separable
Question gives substitution explicitly Use it; differentiate via product rule; substitute
Boundary condition given After finding general solution, substitute to find $c$
Asked to "describe behaviour as $x\to\infty$" Look at dominant term; e.g. if $y=ce^{4x}+\dots$ then $y\to\infty$
Asked to "sketch family" Pick 3-5 values of $c$; identify the family shape (lines, circles, exponentials, etc.)

8. Common pitfalls (and the marks they cost)

Pitfall Fix
Forgetting the constant of integration One arbitrary constant per integration; usually only one in the final answer
Splitting variables when $f$ and $g$ aren't truly separable E.g. $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=x+y$ is NOT separable — it's linear, use IF
Forgetting the $+\dfrac{c}{I}$ in IF method Always: $Iy=\int IQ\,dx+c$, so $y=\dfrac{1}{I}\int IQ\,dx+\dfrac{c}{I}$
Including arbitrary constant in $\int P\,dx$ when computing $I$ NO constant in $I$ — it's a multiplier, not a function family
Forgetting to make coefficient of $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ equal to 1 before extracting $P$ If DE is $a(x)y'+b(x)y=c(x)$, divide by $a(x)$ first, then $P=b/a$
Wrong sign on derivative when using sub $z=y^{-1}$ $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-z^{-2}\dfrac{dz}{dx}$ (not $+$); use chain rule carefully
Using $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=z\dfrac{dz}{dx}$ instead of product rule for $y=xz$ Correct: $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=z+x\dfrac{dz}{dx}$ (treat $z$ as function of $x$)
Combining two arbitrary constants into one but losing a factor Track all constants; combine at end as one constant $c$ or $A=e^c$
Not back-substituting at the end Final answer should be in original variables ($x,y$, not $u,z$)
Boundary condition: substituting $y$ before differentiating If condition is on $y'$, differentiate FIRST then substitute
Treating $\ln \lvert x\rvert$ as $\ln x$ (sign loss) If $x<0$, the absolute value matters; usually drop if domain stated $x>0$
For $I=e^{2\ln\sec x}$, simplifying wrong $e^{2\ln\sec x}=e^{\ln\sec^2 x}=\sec^2 x$
Sketching the family without varying $c$ The family is several curves, not one

9. Big picture (the 30-second mental model)

  1. First-order DEs come in three flavours, distinguished by what makes them tractable:
    • Separable $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=f(x)g(y)$ — split & integrate.
    • Linear $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+Py=Q$ — multiply by $I=e^{\int P}$, integrate.
    • Reducible — substitute (homogeneous: $y=xz$; Bernoulli: $z=y^{1-n}$; shift: $u=y\pm kx$).
  2. Each gives a general solution with ONE arbitrary constant $c$. Boundary condition pins $c$ → particular solution.
  3. Family of solution curves: vary $c$, sketch several. Identify the shape (lines, circles, exponentials, etc.).
  4. Procedure consistency:
    • Always: standard form first (coefficient of $y'$ is 1).
    • Always: identify which technique applies.
    • Always: include $+c$ on the right after integration.
    • Always: back-substitute at the end if you used a substitution.
  5. Difficulty ladder: separation < integrating factor < substitution. The further down, the more algebraic dexterity is required.

Three techniques, one philosophy: rewrite until the RHS becomes integrable, integrate, then unwind.
