P4 Ch 1 â€” Proof (by Contradiction)

The whole chapter in one mental model. Proof by contradiction: to prove statement $S$, assume $\neg S$ (the negation), apply logical steps until you derive an absurdity (a contradiction of $\neg S$ itself or of a known fact). Conclude that $\neg S$ is false, so $S$ must be true. Two staple targets: (1) "no greatest/least integer" type results, and (2) irrationality of surds (e.g. $\sqrt 2,\sqrt 3,\sqrt[3]{2}$) using the "no common factor" trick.

4. Archetype proofs to memorise

# Theorem Key contradiction step
1 No greatest odd integer Suppose $n$ is greatest; show $n+2$ is bigger and odd.
2 If $n^2$ even then $n$ even Suppose $n$ odd: $n=2k+1\Rightarrow n^2=2(2k^2+2k)+1$ is odd. Contradicts $n^2$ even.
3 If $n^2$ multiple of 3 then $n$ multiple of 3 Cases $n=3k+1$ or $n=3k+2$: each gives $n^2\not\equiv 0\pmod 3$.
4 $\sqrt 2$ is irrational Assume $\sqrt 2=a/b$ in lowest terms; squaring gives $a^2=2b^2$ â‡’ $a$ even â‡’ $a=2c$ â‡’ $b^2=2c^2$ â‡’ $b$ even. Contradicts coprime.
5 $\sqrt 3$ is irrational Same template, using "$n^2$ multiple of 3 â‡’ $n$ multiple of 3" lemma.
6 $\sqrt[3]{2}$ is irrational Assume $\sqrt[3]{2}=a/b$ in lowest terms; cube: $a^3=2b^3$. $a^3$ even â‡’ $a$ even (since cube of odd is odd). Then $b^3$ even â‡’ $b$ even. Contradicts coprime.
7 Infinitely many primes Suppose finite list $p_1,\dots,p_n$; $N=p_1\cdots p_n+1$ has a prime factor not in the list.
8 No least positive rational Suppose $n$ smallest. Then $n/2$ is smaller and positive rational. (Tip: subtraction $n-1$ may give a non-positive number â€” use $n/2$ instead.)

5. Surd irrationality â€” the canonical recipe

For "Prove $\sqrt p$ is irrational" with $p$ prime:

Step Action
1 Assume $\sqrt p=a/b$ where $a,b\in\mathbb Z$ and $\gcd(a,b)=1$ (lowest terms)
2 Square: $p=a^2/b^2$, so $a^2=p\,b^2$
3 $a^2$ is divisible by $p$ â‡’ $a$ is divisible by $p$ (the key lemma)
4 Write $a=p\,c$. Substitute: $p^2c^2=p\,b^2$, i.e. $p\,c^2=b^2$
5 So $b^2$ divisible by $p$ â‡’ $b$ divisible by $p$
6 Both $a,b$ divisible by $p$ â€” contradicts $\gcd(a,b)=1$
7 Conclude: $\sqrt p$ is irrational

For $\sqrt[n]{p}$: swap "square" for "raise to $n$th power", same logic.

7. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Negating "all are X" as "none are X" Correct negation: "at least one is not X"
Forgetting to assume lowest terms ($\gcd(a,b)=1$) in irrationality proof Always state "WLOG, $\gcd(a,b)=1$" â€” the contradiction depends on it
Using the lemma "$n^2$ divisible by $p$ â‡’ $n$ divisible by $p$" without proving it For $p=2$, prove via $n$ odd â‡’ $n^2$ odd. For $p=3$, do the case split $3k,3k+1,3k+2$
Stating contradiction without saying what it contradicts Always close with "this contradicts [specific assumption / fact]"
Skipping the conclusion sentence Always end "Therefore [original statement] is true"
Mixing up $n^3$ versus $n$ for cube cases Cube of odd is odd: $(2k+1)^3=8k^3+12k^2+6k+1=2(\dots)+1$. Cube of even is even
Confusing "$P$ implies $Q$" with "$Q$ implies $P$" Negation of "$P\Rightarrow Q$" is "$P$ and not $Q$" â€” not "not $P$ implies not $Q$"
Using "$n=2k$" without clarifying $k\in\mathbb Z$ Always state "for some integer $k$"
Forgetting that $0$ is even $0=2\cdot 0$ is even by definition

8. Big picture (30-second mental model)

Proof by contradiction = "logical reductio":

  1. Negate the statement carefully â€” most errors start with sloppy negation.
  2. Assume the negation and follow it to its logical end.
  3. Derive an absurdity â€” usually one of: a known fact violated, the assumption itself violated, two assumptions contradicting each other.
  4. Conclude the original statement.

Two killer applications: (i) "there is noâ€¦" / "infinitely manyâ€¦" structure problems; (ii) irrationality of surds via parity/divisibility on a coprime fraction.

Negate, assume, deduce, contradict, conclude â€” that's the chapter.
