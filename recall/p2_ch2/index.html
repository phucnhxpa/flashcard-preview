P2 Ch 2 — Coordinate Geometry in the $(x,y)$ Plane

The whole chapter in one mental model. A circle is the set of points equidistant ($=r$) from a fixed point. From this, Pythagoras gives the equation $(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2=r^2$. Every problem reduces to (i) writing the right circle equation, (ii) solving simultaneous equations with a line (linear sub → quadratic in $x$ → discriminant tells you 0/1/2 intersections), and (iii) using two circle theorems: (a) tangent ⊥ radius; (b) perpendicular bisector of a chord passes through the centre. The key auxiliary tools are midpoint, distance, gradient, and perpendicular bisector.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Line segment A finite portion of a straight line, with two distinct endpoints. Has a well-defined length and midpoint. (A line, by contrast, is infinite.) 2.1
Midpoint The point exactly halfway along a line segment. Coordinates: $\left(\dfrac{x_1+x_2}{2},\dfrac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)$ — the average of the endpoint coordinates. Centre of a circle = midpoint of any diameter. 2.1
Distance / length $d=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}$ — Pythagoras applied to horizontal and vertical separations. The radius is the distance from centre to any point on the circle. 2.1 / 2.2
Perpendicular bisector The unique straight line that (i) is perpendicular to a chord/line segment AND (ii) passes through its midpoint. Gradient is $-1/m_{AB}$ if $m_{AB}$ is the gradient of $AB$. 2.1
Circle (definition) The set of points $(x,y)$ at a fixed distance $r$ (the radius) from a fixed point $(a,b)$ (the centre). All such points satisfy $(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2=r^2$. 2.2
Standard form (centred) $(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2=r^2$ — exhibits centre $(a,b)$ and radius $r$ at a glance. Easiest form for reading off geometry. 2.2
Origin-centred Special case $a=b=0$: $x^2+y^2=r^2$. Always centred at $(0,0)$. 2.2
Expanded form $x^2+y^2+2fx+2gy+c=0$ — centre $(-f,-g)$, radius $\sqrt{f^2+g^2-c}$. To convert to standard form, complete the square for $x$ and $y$ separately. 2.2
Completing the square (for circles) Rewrite $x^2+px$ as $(x+\tfrac p 2)^2-(\tfrac p 2)^2$. Apply to both $x$- and $y$-groups, move constants to RHS, identify $r^2$. 2.2 Ex 7
Chord A line segment whose endpoints both lie on the circle. The longest chord is the diameter (passes through centre). 2.4
Diameter A chord through the centre. Length $2r$. The endpoints are diametrically opposite. 2.1 / 2.5
Tangent (to a circle) A line that touches the circle at exactly one point. Key property: a tangent is perpendicular to the radius at the point of tangency. So $m_{\text{tangent}}=-1/m_{\text{radius}}$. 2.4
Point of tangency The unique point where the tangent and circle meet. The radius drawn to this point is perpendicular to the tangent. 2.4
Discriminant test for line vs circle Substitute line into circle, get a quadratic in $x$. $b^2-4ac>0$ → 2 intersections; $=0$ → tangent (1 point); $<0$ → line misses the circle (0 points). 2.3
Circumcircle The unique circle passing through all three vertices of a triangle. Centre = circumcentre = intersection of two perpendicular bisectors of the sides. 2.5
Circumcentre The centre of the circumcircle. Equidistant from all three vertices (radius = distance from circumcentre to any vertex). 2.5
Angle in a semicircle theorem If a triangle has its hypotenuse as a diameter of a circle, then the angle at the third vertex is $90°$. Converse: if $\angle PRQ=90°$, then $R$ lies on the circle with diameter $PQ$. 2.5
Perpendicular-bisector-of-chord theorem The perpendicular bisector of any chord passes through the centre of the circle. (Useful: find centre by intersecting two perpendicular bisectors.) 2.4 / 2.5
Gradient × gradient = $-1$ Two lines are perpendicular iff their gradients multiply to $-1$ (assuming neither is vertical). Used everywhere: tangent–radius, perpendicular bisectors. Recall (P1 Ch5)
Sketch / diagram In coordinate geometry problems, always draw a sketch of the data. It often reveals which theorem applies. 2.1 (Problem-solving notes)

2. Core formulas — cross-table

Concept Formula When to use
Midpoint of $A(x_1,y_1)$ and $B(x_2,y_2)$ $M=\left(\dfrac{x_1+x_2}{2},\dfrac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)$ Centre = midpoint of diameter; midpoint of chord (for ⊥ bisector)
Distance / length $AB$ $d=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}$ Radius from centre to a point; chord length; verifying point lies on circle
Gradient $m=\dfrac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}$ For tangent gradient via radius; perpendicular bisector
Perpendicular gradient $m'=-\dfrac{1}{m}$ Tangent (using radius gradient); perpendicular bisector (using $AB$ gradient)
Line through point $(x_0,y_0)$ with gradient $m$ $y-y_0=m(x-x_0)$ Equation of tangent, normal, perpendicular bisector
Circle, centre $(a,b)$, radius $r$ $(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2=r^2$ Standard form

Expanded circle $x^2+y^2+2fx+2gy+c=0$ Centre $(-f,-g)$, radius $\sqrt{f^2+g^2-c}$

3. Recipe — equation of a circle from data

Given Steps
Centre $(a,b)$ and radius $r$ Plug into $(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2=r^2$ directly
Centre and a point on circle Compute $r$ = distance from centre to point. Then standard form.
Two endpoints of a diameter Centre = midpoint. $r=$ half the distance between endpoints. Then standard form.
Three points on circle (no right angle) Find perpendicular bisectors of two sides; their intersection = centre. Radius = distance from centre to any vertex.
Three points where one angle is $90°$ The hypotenuse is a diameter. Centre = midpoint of hypotenuse; $r=$ half hypotenuse length.
Expanded form $x^2+y^2+2fx+2gy+c=0$ Read centre $(-f,-g)$ and radius $\sqrt{f^2+g^2-c}$; or complete the square

4. Line ∩ Circle — the standard substitution method

Step Action
1 Take the line equation: solve for $y$ (or $x$) — get $y=mx+b$ form
2 Substitute into circle equation, replacing $y$ everywhere
3 Expand brackets, simplify to a quadratic in $x$: $Ax^2+Bx+C=0$
4 Compute discriminant $\Delta=B^2-4AC$
5a $\Delta>0$ → solve quadratic for two $x$-values; sub each into line for $y$ → two intersection points
5b $\Delta=0$ → one repeated $x$ → line is tangent; sub for $y$ → unique point of tangency
5c $\Delta<0$ → no real solutions → line misses circle
6 Always write coordinates as ordered pairs

Reverse use: when problem says "line tangent to circle" or "line meets circle at exactly one point" → set $\Delta=0$; gives equation in unknown parameter.

5. Tangent equation — standard recipe

To find the tangent to a circle at a point $P$ on the circle:

Step Action
1 Find centre $C=(a,b)$ from circle equation
2 Compute gradient of radius $CP$: $m_{\text{radius}}=\dfrac{y_P-b}{x_P-a}$
3 Tangent gradient = $-1/m_{\text{radius}}$ (perpendicular)
4 Use point-slope: $y-y_P=m_{\text{tangent}}(x-x_P)$
5 Rearrange into required form (e.g. $ax+by+c=0$)

Alternative — tangent from external point: the tangent line $y=mx+c$ meets the circle exactly once. Substitute, set $\Delta=0$, solve for $m$ — there will be two values of $m$ (two tangents from an external point).

6. Three theorems — quick recall

Theorem Statement Practical use
Tangent ⊥ radius A tangent to a circle is perpendicular to the radius at the point of tangency Find tangent gradient from radius gradient (or vice versa)
⊥ bisector of chord through centre The perpendicular bisector of any chord passes through the centre Find centre from two chords' ⊥ bisectors; show a line passes through centre
Angle in semicircle = 90° If $PQ$ is a diameter and $R$ is on the circle, $\angle PRQ=90°$. Converse: $\angle PRQ=90°\Rightarrow PQ$ is a diameter Test if three points form right triangle (then hypotenuse = diameter)

8. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Sign errors in standard form: writing $(x+a)^2$ for centre $(a,b)$ with positive $a$ Centre $(a,b)$ → $(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2$. Centre $(-3,4)$ → $(x+3)^2+(y-4)^2$
Forgetting to take square root of $r^2$ when stating radius $(x-3)^2+(y-4)^2=20$ → $r=\sqrt{20}=2\sqrt 5$, not $20$
Using midpoint formula with subtraction instead of addition Midpoint = $\left(\dfrac{x_1+x_2}{2},\dfrac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)$ — add then halve
Distance formula sign error: $(x_1-x_2)^2$ vs $(x_2-x_1)^2$ — these are equal! Doesn't matter (squared cancels). But be consistent.
Forgetting both $\sqrt{125}=5\sqrt 5$ shorthand or leaving $\sqrt{40}$ unsimplified Always extract the largest perfect square: $\sqrt{40}=2\sqrt{10}$
When completing the square, halving and squaring incorrectly: $x^2-14x\to(x-7)^2-49$, NOT $-14^2$ Halve coefficient: $\tfrac{-14}{2}=-7$, then square: $49$
In expanded form: forgetting $r=\sqrt{f^2+g^2-c}$ requires positive radicand If $f^2+g^2-c\le 0$ → not a real circle
Tangent gradient: using radius gradient instead of perpendicular $m_{\text{tangent}}=-1/m_{\text{radius}}$
Discriminant to test 0/1/2 intersections: forgetting to first reduce to quadratic in $x$ After substitution, expand fully and collect → $Ax^2+Bx+C=0$
Three-point circle: using only one ⊥ bisector You need two to get an intersection (a unique centre)
Right-angle test mixing sides: don't forget to check all three combinations of $a^2+b^2=c^2$ Try with longest side as $c$ first
Asking "does point lie on the circle?": substitute directly into LHS, compare with $r^2$ Don't try to factor; just plug in numbers
Circle "doesn't intersect $x$-axis" → discriminant of equation when $y=0$ is $<0$ Set $y=0$, get a quadratic in $x$, check $\Delta<0$
Confusing "centre lies on a line" (geometric) with "circle passes through a point" (algebraic) Different conditions: read carefully

9. Big picture (30-second mental model)

A circle is distance = radius from a centre. Algebraically: $(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2=r^2$. Three skills are universal:

  1. Convert between forms (standard ↔ expanded) using completing the square.
  2. Substitute line into circle to count intersections via discriminant.
  3. Use the three theorems: tangent ⊥ radius, ⊥ bisector of chord goes through centre, angle in semicircle = $90°$.

Plus the four building blocks: midpoint (centre of diameter), distance (radius/length), gradient (slopes for parallel/perpendicular), and point-slope line equation. Almost every problem combines 2-3 of these into a chain. Always sketch the data first — geometric intuition narrows the algebra dramatically.
