FP2 Ch 3 — Complex Numbers (Exponential Form & de Moivre)

The whole chapter in one mental model. Euler's relation $e^{i\theta}=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta$ unifies the trigonometric and exponential worlds. From it, three powerful tools fall out: (1) exponential form $z=re^{i\theta}$ — multiplication and division become arithmetic on $r$ and $\theta$; (2) de Moivre's theorem $(r e^{i\theta})^n=r^n e^{in\theta}$ — powers and roots become trivial; (3) trig identities and $n$th roots of unity via the substitution $z=e^{i\theta}$ giving $z+\tfrac1z=2\cos\theta$ and $z^n=1$ admitting $n$ regularly-spaced roots. The whole chapter is one substitution doing four jobs.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Exponential form $z=re^{i\theta}$ where $r=|z|$ and $\theta=\arg z$. Equivalent to modulus-argument form $r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)$ via Euler's relation. The exponential form makes multiplication, division, and powers behave like ordinary indices. 3.1
Euler's relation $e^{i\theta}=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta$. Derived by substituting $i\theta$ into the Maclaurin series of $e^x$ and recognising the resulting real and imaginary parts as the Maclaurin series of $\cos\theta$ and $\sin\theta$. The keystone formula of FP2 Ch 3. 3.1
Euler's identity $e^{i\pi}+1=0$. The special case $\theta=\pi$ in Euler's relation. Links five fundamental constants $0,1,\pi,e,i$. Famous for "mathematical beauty". 3.1
Modulus $r=\lvert z\rvert$ Distance from origin in the Argand diagram: $r=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ for $z=x+iy$. Always non-negative. In exponential form, the coefficient out front. 3.1
Argument $\theta=\arg z$ Angle from positive real axis to $z$, measured anticlockwise. Principal value $-\pi<\theta\leq\pi$ unless stated. Quadrant rules: Q1: $\arctan(y/x)$; Q2: $\pi-\arctan\lvert y/x\rvert$; Q3: $-\pi+\arctan\lvert y/x\rvert$; Q4: $-\arctan\lvert y/x\rvert$. 3.1
De Moivre's theorem For any integer $n$: $(r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta))^n=r^n(\cos n\theta+i\sin n\theta)$. Equivalent to $(re^{i\theta})^n=r^ne^{in\theta}$. Proved by induction on $n\geq 1$, then extended to negative integers via $z^{-n}=1/z^n$. 3.3
Multiplication / division rules $z_1z_2=r_1r_2e^{i(\theta_1+\theta_2)}$ — moduli multiply, arguments add. $\dfrac{z_1}{z_2}=\dfrac{r_1}{r_2}e^{i(\theta_1-\theta_2)}$ — moduli divide, arguments subtract. The geometric meaning: multiplying by $z$ scales by $|z|$ and rotates by $\arg z$. 3.2
The $z+1/z$ trick Setting $z=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta=e^{i\theta}$: $z+\tfrac{1}{z}=2\cos\theta$, $z-\tfrac{1}{z}=2i\sin\theta$, $z^n+\tfrac{1}{z^n}=2\cos n\theta$, $z^n-\tfrac{1}{z^n}=2i\sin n\theta$. The key to deriving identities for $\sin^n\theta$ and $\cos^n\theta$ in terms of $\cos k\theta$ and $\sin k\theta$. 3.4
Multiple-angle identity (cos $n\theta$ / sin $n\theta$) Express $\cos n\theta$ as a polynomial in $\cos\theta$ (and $\sin\theta$) — derived by binomially expanding $(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)^n$ then equating real (for $\cos n\theta$) or imaginary (for $\sin n\theta$) parts. 3.4 Ex 10
Powers-of-trig identity ($\cos^n\theta$ / $\sin^n\theta$) Express $\cos^n\theta$ (or $\sin^n\theta$) as a sum of $\cos k\theta$ (or $\sin k\theta$) terms — derived by binomially expanding $(z+1/z)^n$ or $(z-1/z)^n$ and using $z^k+1/z^k=2\cos k\theta$. Used to integrate trig powers. 3.4 Ex 11
$n$th root of a complex number A solution of $z^n=w$. Always exactly $n$ distinct roots (provided $w\neq 0$). Found via $z_k=r^{1/n}e^{i(\theta+2k\pi)/n}$ for $k=0,1,\dots,n-1$. They lie on a circle of radius $r^{1/n}$ at equal angular spacing $2\pi/n$. 3.5
$n$th root of unity Solution of $z^n=1$. There are $n$ of them: $1,\omega,\omega^2,\dots,\omega^{n-1}$ where $\omega=e^{2\pi i/n}$. They form the vertices of a regular $n$-gon centred at origin, on the unit circle. Sum is zero: $1+\omega+\omega^2+\dots+\omega^{n-1}=0$. 3.5
General argument trick $z=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)=r(\cos(\theta+2k\pi)+i\sin(\theta+2k\pi))$ for any integer $k$. Essential when solving $z^n=w$: set $n\theta=\arg w+2k\pi$ and pick $n$ consecutive values of $k$ to get all $n$ roots. 3.5
Regular $n$-gon property $n$th roots of any complex number $w$ lie on the vertices of a regular $n$-gon with centre at the origin (for unit roots, on the unit circle; for $z^n=w$, on a circle of radius $|w|^{1/n}$). Symmetric: rotate any one root by $2\pi/n$ to get the next. 3.5
Conjugate pair shortcut If a polynomial with real coefficients has $z=re^{i\theta}$ as a root, then $\bar z=re^{-i\theta}$ is also a root. Roots come in conjugate pairs. Used to factorise polynomials. Q5

Form Notation When to use

Modulus-argument $z=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)$ Visualising on Argand; rotation/scaling
Exponential $z=re^{i\theta}$ Multiplying, dividing, raising to powers

Conversion $re^{i\theta}\to x+iy$ $x=r\cos\theta$, $y=r\sin\theta$ When asked for "form $x+iy$"
Reduction to principal arg Subtract multiples of $2\pi$ until $-\pi<\theta\leq\pi$ When given $\theta>\pi$ or $\theta\leq -\pi$

3. Multiplication / division — the algebra rules

Operation Modulus rule Argument rule Exponential form
$z_1z_2$ $|z_1z_2|=|z_1||z_2|$ $\arg(z_1z_2)=\arg z_1+\arg z_2$ $r_1r_2e^{i(\theta_1+\theta_2)}$
$z_1/z_2$ $\left\lvert\dfrac{z_1}{z_2}\right\rvert=\dfrac{|z_1|}{|z_2|}$ $\arg(z_1/z_2)=\arg z_1-\arg z_2$ $\dfrac{r_1}{r_2}e^{i(\theta_1-\theta_2)}$
$z^n$ $|z^n|=|z|^n$ $\arg(z^n)=n\arg z$ (mod $2\pi$) $r^ne^{in\theta}$
$z^{-1}$ $|z^{-1}|=1/|z|$ $\arg(z^{-1})=-\arg z$ $r^{-1}e^{-i\theta}$
$\bar z$ $|\bar z|=|z|$ $\arg(\bar z)=-\arg z$ $re^{-i\theta}$

4. De Moivre's theorem and its applications

Application Method Output
Compute $z^n$ for $z=x+iy$ Convert to $re^{i\theta}$, then $z^n=r^n e^{in\theta}$, convert back if needed E.g. $(1+i\sqrt3)^7=128(\tfrac12+i\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2})$
Express $\cos n\theta$ / $\sin n\theta$ in powers of $\cos\theta$, $\sin\theta$ Expand $(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)^n$ binomially, equate real ($\cos n\theta$) and imaginary ($\sin n\theta$) parts. Use $\sin^2=1-\cos^2$ to clean up $\cos 6\theta=32\cos^6\theta-48\cos^4\theta+18\cos^2\theta-1$
Express $\cos^n\theta$ / $\sin^n\theta$ in $\cos k\theta$ / $\sin k\theta$ form Set $z=e^{i\theta}$, use $(z+1/z)^n=(2\cos\theta)^n$ or $(z-1/z)^n=(2i\sin\theta)^n$; expand binomially; group $z^k+1/z^k=2\cos k\theta$ $\cos^5\theta=\tfrac{1}{16}\cos 5\theta+\tfrac{5}{16}\cos 3\theta+\tfrac{5}{8}\cos\theta$
Integrate $\cos^n\theta$ / $\sin^n\theta$ First express in $\cos k\theta$ / $\sin k\theta$ form (linear in trig), then integrate term-by-term $\int\cos^4\theta=\tfrac{1}{8}\int(\cos4\theta+4\cos2\theta+3)d\theta$
Express $\tan n\theta$ in $\tan\theta$ $\tan n\theta=\dfrac{\sin n\theta}{\cos n\theta}$; divide both numerator and denominator by $\cos^n\theta$ $\tan 4\theta=\dfrac{4\tan\theta-4\tan^3\theta}{1-6\tan^2\theta+\tan^4\theta}$
Find roots of polynomial $a x^n+\dots=0$ Substitute $x=\cos\theta$ (or similar); reduce to $\cos n\theta=k$; solve for $\theta$, then $x=\cos\theta$ E.g. $32x^6-48x^4+18x^2-3/2=0$ → $\cos 6\theta=3/64$, six $\theta$ values

5. The $z+1/z$ trick — power-reduction formulas

Identity Use
$z+\dfrac{1}{z}=2\cos\theta$ Foundational
$z-\dfrac{1}{z}=2i\sin\theta$ Foundational
$z^n+\dfrac{1}{z^n}=2\cos n\theta$ Used after grouping
$z^n-\dfrac{1}{z^n}=2i\sin n\theta$ Used after grouping
$(z+1/z)^n=(2\cos\theta)^n$ Expand binomially → $\cos^n\theta$ identity
$(z-1/z)^n=(2i\sin\theta)^n$ Expand binomially → $\sin^n\theta$ identity

Method (for $\cos^n\theta$ as $\sum a_k\cos k\theta$):

  1. Let $z=e^{i\theta}$.
  2. Write $(2\cos\theta)^n=(z+1/z)^n$.
  3. Expand binomially.
  4. Pair up $z^k$ with $z^{-k}$ to get $z^k+z^{-k}=2\cos k\theta$.
  5. Divide by $2^n$ to isolate $\cos^n\theta$.

7. Roots of $z^n=w$ — the universal recipe

Step Action
1 Write $w$ in modulus-argument or exponential form: $w=Re^{i\phi}$
2 Use general argument: $w=Re^{i(\phi+2k\pi)}$ for integer $k$
3 Set $z=re^{i\theta}$. Then $z^n=r^ne^{in\theta}=Re^{i(\phi+2k\pi)}$
4 Compare moduli: $r=R^{1/n}$
5 Compare arguments: $n\theta=\phi+2k\pi\Rightarrow \theta=\dfrac{\phi+2k\pi}{n}$
6 Pick $n$ consecutive values of $k$ (e.g. $k=0,1,\dots,n-1$ or $k=-\lfloor n/2\rfloor,\dots,\lceil n/2\rceil-1$) so all $\theta$'s land in $(-\pi,\pi]$
7 Read off the $n$ roots: $z_k=R^{1/n}e^{i\theta_k}$
8 (Geometry check) Roots lie on a circle of radius $R^{1/n}$ at angular spacing $2\pi/n$ — vertices of a regular $n$-gon

$n$th roots of unity ($w=1$): $z_k=e^{2\pi ik/n}$. Sum is zero. Used to factorise $1+z+z^2+\dots+z^{n-1}$ etc.

10. Common pitfalls (and the marks they cost)

Pitfall Fix
Argument from $\arctan(y/x)$ without thinking quadrants Always sketch Argand; use quadrant rule (Q2: $\pi-\alpha$; Q3: $-\pi+\alpha$; Q4: $-\alpha$ where $\alpha=\arctan|y/x|$)
Argument outside $(-\pi,\pi]$ Add/subtract multiples of $2\pi$ to bring into range
For $z^n=w$, choosing $k=0,1,\dots,n-1$ blindly when some $\theta$'s exit $(-\pi,\pi]$ Use either symmetric range $k=-\lfloor n/2\rfloor,\dots$, or shift each $\theta$ by $2\pi$ as needed
Forgetting "$n$ distinct roots" — only finding 1 or 2 Always pick $n$ consecutive $k$ values
Treating de Moivre as automatic for non-integer $n$ de Moivre is for integer $n$; for fractional powers use $z^n=w$ method (multiple roots)
For $\cos n\theta$ identity: not converting all $\sin^2$ to $1-\cos^2$ Final answer must be polynomial in $\cos\theta$ alone
In $z+1/z$ trick: forgetting the $i^n$ factor for $\sin^n$ $(z-1/z)^n=(2i\sin\theta)^n=2^n i^n \sin^n\theta$ — handle $i^n=\pm 1,\pm i$
Using $|z_1+z_2|=|z_1|+|z_2|$ (false!) Triangle inequality: $|z_1+z_2|\leq |z_1|+|z_2|$, equal only if same arg
Mis-applying Euler: $e^{ix+iy}=e^{ix}+e^{iy}$ (false) Correct: $e^{ix+iy}=e^{ix}\cdot e^{iy}$
Treating $\arg(z_1+z_2)=\arg z_1+\arg z_2$ (false) $\arg$ adds for products, not sums
Forgetting that the $n$ roots of unity sum to 0 Use this to prove identities like $\cos(2\pi/5)+\cos(4\pi/5)=-1/2$
Using exponential laws for complex powers without justification "You cannot assume laws of indices work for complex numbers" — only the multiplication/division rules are guaranteed
Writing $\arg z=0$ for $z>0$ but forgetting $z<0\to\arg z=\pi$ Negative real axis: argument $\pi$ (not $-\pi$, by convention)

11. Big picture (the 30-second mental model)

  1. Euler's relation $e^{i\theta}=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta$ is the keystone — it converts trigonometric form into exponential form.
  2. Exponential form $z=re^{i\theta}$ makes multiplication and powers behave like ordinary indices: $z^n=r^ne^{in\theta}$. This is de Moivre's theorem.
  3. Trig identities flow out of de Moivre two ways:
    • Expand $(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)^n$ → identities for $\cos n\theta,\sin n\theta$ in powers of $\cos,\sin$.
    • Substitute $z=e^{i\theta}$, use $z+1/z=2\cos\theta$ etc., expand $(z+1/z)^n$ → identities for $\cos^n\theta,\sin^n\theta$ as sums of $\cos k\theta,\sin k\theta$.
  4. $n$th roots of $w$: write $w=Re^{i(\phi+2k\pi)}$, take $1/n$-th: $z_k=R^{1/n}e^{i(\phi+2k\pi)/n}$, $n$ values of $k$. Roots form a regular $n$-gon.
  5. $n$th roots of unity are the special case $w=1$: $1,\omega,\omega^2,\dots,\omega^{n-1}$ where $\omega=e^{2\pi i/n}$. Sum is zero. Used to factorise $z^n-1$ and prove trig identities.

One formula, four toolkits — that's the chapter.
