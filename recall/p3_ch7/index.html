P3 Ch 7 — Integration

The whole chapter in one mental model. P3 integration = library of standard antiderivatives (reverse the standard derivatives from Ch 6) + four manoeuvres to handle expressions that aren't already in the library: (1) $f(ax+b)$ rule, (2) trig-identity rewrite, (3) reverse chain rule for $k\dfrac{f'(x)}{f(x)}$ → $\ln|f(x)|$, (4) reverse chain rule for $kf'(x)(f(x))^n$ → $(f(x))^{n+1}$. Always work in radians for trig; always +c for indefinite integrals.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Indefinite integral An antiderivative without limits: $\int f(x)\,\mathrm{d}x = F(x) + c$ where $F'(x) = f(x)$. The "$+c$" is the constant of integration — all antiderivatives differ by a constant, so we tag one on. Forgetting $+c$ is a guaranteed mark loss. 7.1
Definite integral An integral with limits: $\int_a^b f(x)\,\mathrm{d}x = F(b)-F(a)$. The constant of integration cancels when you subtract. Geometrically: signed area between curve and $x$-axis from $a$ to $b$. 7.1 Ex 2
Integrand The function being integrated — the bit between the $\int$ and the $\mathrm{d}x$. E.g. in $\int 3x^2\,\mathrm{d}x$ the integrand is $3x^2$. 7.1
Constant of integration ($c$) Arbitrary constant added to every indefinite integral. Comes from the fact that $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}c=0$, so any constant could've been there originally. Always include for indefinite, never for definite. 7.1
Antiderivative A function $F$ whose derivative is the integrand: $F'(x)=f(x)$. Same idea as "indefinite integral" but emphasises the inverse relationship with differentiation. 7.1
Standard integral / formula booklet The 9 standard antiderivatives in §2 — quote them, don't re-derive. The booklet provides $\sec^2 x$, $\csc x\cot x$, $\csc^2 x$, $\sec x\tan x$ etc. 7.1
Reverse chain rule (linear arg) When the inside is linear in $x$ — i.e. $ax+b$: $\int f'(ax+b)\,\mathrm{d}x = \dfrac{1}{a}f(ax+b)+c$. Just integrate as if it were $f'(x)$, then divide by $a$ to compensate for the chain-rule factor. 7.2
Reverse chain rule (logarithmic) When the integrand is $k\dfrac{f'(x)}{f(x)}$ — numerator is a constant multiple of the derivative of the denominator: answer is $k\ln|f(x)|+c$ after adjusting constants. Spot it: derivative of bottom, up to a factor, lives on top. 7.4
Reverse chain rule (power) When the integrand is $kf'(x)(f(x))^n$ — derivative of inner function multiplied by a power of the inner function: answer is $\dfrac{1}{n+1}(f(x))^{n+1}+c$ after adjusting constants. 7.4
Trig-identity rewrite When the integrand has no direct standard form (e.g. $\tan^2 x$, $\sin^2 x$, $\cos^2 x$): rewrite using a Pythagorean / double-angle identity to turn it into something integrable. $\tan^2x = \sec^2x - 1$, $\sin^2x = \tfrac12(1-\cos 2x)$, $\cos^2x = \tfrac12(1+\cos 2x)$. 7.3
Modulus inside $\ln$ $\int\dfrac{1}{x}\,\mathrm{d}x = \ln|x|+c$, with bars. The bars handle negative $x$ — without them, $\ln$ is undefined for $x<0$. Always include for full marks. 7.1 Notation
"Try and adjust" method Practical recipe for reverse chain rule: guess what you'd differentiate to get this (e.g. "this looks like the derivative of $\ln|f|$"), differentiate your guess, compare with the integrand, multiply your guess by whatever constant fixes the mismatch. 7.2, 7.4
Integration by inspection Another name for "try and adjust" — recognise the structure ($f'/f$ or $f'\cdot f^n$ or $f'(ax+b)$), write down the answer, check by differentiating. Preferred over substitution at this level. 7.4
Pythagorean identities (integration uses) $\sin^2 + \cos^2 = 1$, $1+\tan^2 = \sec^2$, $1+\cot^2 = \csc^2$. Used to convert un-integrable trig powers into integrable ones. 7.3
Double-angle identities (integration uses) $\cos 2x = 1-2\sin^2 x = 2\cos^2 x - 1$. Rearranged: $\sin^2 x = \tfrac12(1-\cos 2x)$, $\cos^2 x = \tfrac12(1+\cos 2x)$. The standard trick for integrating $\sin^2$, $\cos^2$. 7.3 Ex 6
Limits in radians When using definite integrals on trig functions, the limits must be in radians — same rule as differentiation. $\sin(\pi/4)$ not $\sin 45°$. 7.1 Watch out

2. Standard antiderivatives library (memorise all)

These are the 9 from the formula booklet — the foundation. Every other technique reduces to applying these after a manoeuvre.

$f(x)$ $\int f(x)\,\mathrm{d}x$ Mirror of
$x^n$ ($n\neq -1$) $\dfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}+c$ $(x^n)' = nx^{n-1}$
$\dfrac{1}{x}$ $\ln\lvert x\rvert+c$ $(\ln x)' = \dfrac{1}{x}$
$e^x$ $e^x+c$ $(e^x)' = e^x$
$\cos x$ $\sin x+c$ $(\sin x)' = \cos x$
$\sin x$ $-\cos x+c$ $(\cos x)' = -\sin x$
$\sec^2 x$ $\tan x+c$ $(\tan x)' = \sec^2 x$
$\csc x\cot x$ $-\csc x+c$ $(\csc x)' = -\csc x\cot x$
$\csc^2 x$ $-\cot x+c$ $(\cot x)' = -\csc^2 x$
$\sec x\tan x$ $\sec x+c$ $(\sec x)' = \sec x\tan x$

Pattern: each integral is the un-derivative of the corresponding entry in the Ch 6 standard derivatives table. The minus signs flip: if differentiating gave a minus, integrating absorbs it (or adds one back).

3. The four manoeuvres — when to use which

Manoeuvre Trigger Method Result
Linear-arg (reverse chain) Inside is $ax+b$ (linear in $x$) Integrate as if it were $f'(x)$, divide by $a$ $\int f'(ax+b)\,\mathrm{d}x = \dfrac{1}{a}f(ax+b)+c$
Trig-identity rewrite Powers of trig: $\tan^2$, $\sin^2$, $\cos^2$, $\sin x\cos x$ etc. Use Pythagorean or double-angle identity to rewrite as standard E.g. $\sin^2 x \to \tfrac{1}{2}(1-\cos 2x)$
Reverse chain (log form) $k\dfrac{f'(x)}{f(x)}$ — derivative-of-bottom (up to factor) is on top Try $\ln\lvert f(x)\rvert$, differentiate, adjust constant $\int\dfrac{f'(x)}{f(x)}\,\mathrm{d}x = \ln\lvert f(x)\rvert+c$
Reverse chain (power form) $kf'(x)(f(x))^n$ — inner-derivative times power of inner Try $(f(x))^{n+1}$, differentiate, adjust constant $\int kf'(x)(f(x))^n\,\mathrm{d}x = \dfrac{k}{n+1}(f(x))^{n+1}+c$

Rule of thumb: if it's not directly in the library:

  1. Is the inside linear? → linear-arg rule (divide by $a$).
  2. Are there trig powers? → identity rewrite.
  3. Is there a fraction with derivative-on-top structure? → log form.
  4. Is there a "derivative × power-of-inner" structure? → power form.

You see… Reach for… Why

Inside is linear ($ax+b$) Linear-arg rule (÷ by $a$) Reverse chain trivially
$\tan^2 x$ $= \sec^2 x - 1$ Pyth identity
$\sin^2 x$ $= \tfrac{1}{2}(1-\cos 2x)$ Double-angle
$\cos^2 x$ $= \tfrac{1}{2}(1+\cos 2x)$ Double-angle
$\cot^2 x$ $= \csc^2 x - 1$ Pyth identity
Fraction, top is (constant ×) derivative of bottom $\ln\lvert$bottom$\rvert$ Log reverse chain
Product where one factor is derivative of inside of the other Power reverse chain $(f)^{n+1}$ Power reverse chain

$\cos x e^{\sin x}$ Try $e^{\sin x}$ ($f'(f)^n$ generalised — exponent is the inner) Power reverse chain

6. Definite integral workflow

Step Action
1 Find the indefinite integral $F(x)$ using one of the four manoeuvres
2 Drop the $+c$ (it cancels)
3 Substitute upper limit: $F(b)$
4 Substitute lower limit: $F(a)$
5 Compute $F(b) - F(a)$
6 Simplify (use log laws: $\ln a - \ln b = \ln(a/b)$; trig values: $\sin(\pi/4)=\sqrt{2}/2$ etc.)
7 Check: limits in radians for trig; answer is a number, not a function

7. Common pitfalls

Mistake What goes wrong Fix
Forgetting $+c$ on indefinite integral Loses 1 mark every time Make $+c$ a reflex
Forgetting the modulus in $\ln$ $\int\dfrac{1}{x}\,\mathrm{d}x = \ln x$ (wrong for $x<0$) Always write $\ln\lvert x\rvert + c$
Linear-arg rule applied to non-linear arg $\int\cos(2x^2+3)\,\mathrm{d}x \neq \tfrac{1}{4x}\sin(2x^2+3)$ Method only works when inside is $ax+b$ — quadratic args need substitution (not in P3)
Sign error on $\int\sin x = -\cos x$ Forgets the minus Mirror of $(\cos x)' = -\sin x$ — the minus stays
Trying $f'/f$ when numerator isn't proportional to $f'$ E.g. $\int\dfrac{1}{x^2+3}$ — here $f'=2x$ but no $x$ on top This integral needs $\arctan$ method (P4 territory)
Forgetting the chain factor when adjusting $\int \cos(2x+3)\,\mathrm{d}x = \sin(2x+3)$ instead of $\tfrac{1}{2}\sin(2x+3)$ Always divide by inner derivative
Using degrees for definite trig integrals Wrong numerical answer Radians always for calculus
Forgetting to differentiate-and-check on reverse chain Wrong constant adjustment After guess, differentiate it; compare to integrand; multiply by ratio
$\int\dfrac{1}{(2x+1)^2}\,\mathrm{d}x$ treated as $\ln$ This is $(2x+1)^{-2}$ — power form, not log form Power form: $\dfrac{1}{2}\cdot\dfrac{(2x+1)^{-1}}{-1}+c = -\dfrac{1}{2(2x+1)}+c$
Confusing $\int(2x+1)^2$ with $\int(2x+1)^{-1}$ Wrong rule chosen Positive integer power → linear-arg power form. $-1$ → $\ln$ form
Constant inside the integral missed $\int 3\sec^2 x\,\mathrm{d}x \to \tan x$ instead of $3\tan x$ Linearity: $\int kf = k\int f$. Pull constants out
Limits used in wrong order $F(a)-F(b)$ instead of $F(b)-F(a)$ Upper minus lower, always

8. Big picture (30-second mental model)

P3 integration is library + four manoeuvres:

  1. Library: 9 standard antiderivatives (§2). Memorise them — they're the reverse of the standard derivatives from Ch 6.
  2. Four manoeuvres when the integrand isn't directly in the library:
    • Inside is $ax+b$ → integrate normally, divide by $a$.
    • Trig powers → rewrite via identity, then library.
    • Fraction with $f'$ on top → $\ln\lvert f\rvert$.
    • Product $f'\cdot f^n$ → $\dfrac{f^{n+1}}{n+1}$.
  3. Always: $+c$ for indefinite, radians for trig, modulus in $\ln$, "try and differentiate to check" for reverse chain.

Decoder loop: see integrand → match a pattern → write antiderivative → differentiate to verify → adjust constant if off.

Every P3 integration question is one of these four moves (or a chain of them on linear combinations). Spot the structure in 3 seconds, the rest is mechanical.
