P1 Ch 6 — Trigonometric Ratios

The whole chapter in one mental model. For any triangle (not just right-angled), three rules cover everything: cosine rule for "two sides + included angle → 3rd side" or "three sides → angle"; sine rule for "side + opposite angle + something else"; area $=\tfrac12 ab\sin C$ for any "two sides + included angle". The sine rule has an ambiguous case ($\sin\theta=\sin(180°-\theta)$) — always check whether the obtuse partner is geometrically possible. Trig graphs ($\sin,\cos,\tan$) are periodic ($360°,360°,180°$); transformations (translate, stretch, reflect) follow the same rules as for any function $\mathrm{f}(x)$.

1. Jargon glossary

Term Meaning Where
Standard triangle labelling In $\triangle ABC$, the side opposite angle $A$ is called $a$ (lowercase letter, opposite same uppercase). So $a=BC$, $b=CA$, $c=AB$. Every formula in the chapter uses this convention; mis-labelling is the #1 source of arithmetic errors. 6.1
Cosine rule (side form) Used when you know two sides and the included angle (the angle between them). $a^2=b^2+c^2-2bc\cos A$ — a Pythagoras-with-correction. When $A=90°$, $\cos A=0$ and it reduces to Pythagoras' theorem. 6.1
Cosine rule (angle form) Algebraic rearrangement for "given all three sides, find an angle": $\cos A=\dfrac{b^2+c^2-a^2}{2bc}$. Note: $a$ is the side opposite the angle you want, $b$ and $c$ are the other two. The sign of the cosine immediately tells you whether the angle is acute or obtuse. 6.1
Sine rule (side form) Used when you know an angle and its opposite side, plus another angle: $\dfrac{a}{\sin A}=\dfrac{b}{\sin B}=\dfrac{c}{\sin C}$. Pair up "side ↔ opposite angle" — never mix. 6.2
Sine rule (angle form) The reciprocal version: $\dfrac{\sin A}{a}=\dfrac{\sin B}{b}=\dfrac{\sin C}{c}$. Used when you know two sides and the angle opposite one of them, and want the angle opposite the other. 6.2
Ambiguous case (SSA) The sine rule can produce two solutions for an unknown angle because $\sin\theta=\sin(180°-\theta)$. Triggered by "side-side-angle" data where the data could fit two distinct triangles. Resolve by: (i) is the obtuse value plausible given the other angles ($\theta_1+\theta_2<180°$)? (ii) which side is longest — opposite angle is largest. 6.2 Ex 7
Included angle The angle between two named sides (the angle whose vertex they share). Crucial for: cosine rule (side form) and area $=\tfrac12 ab\sin C$. NOT the angle opposite the unknown side. 6.1, 6.3
Area formula $\text{Area}=\tfrac12 ab\sin C$ for two sides and the included angle $C$. Letters interchangeable: $\tfrac12 ac\sin B=\tfrac12 bc\sin A$. Reduces to $\tfrac12\,\text{base}\times\text{height}$ when one of the sides is the base and $\sin C$ scales the other side to height. 6.3
Bearing A direction measured clockwise from north, written as a 3-digit angle, e.g. $060°$. Always 0–360°. Differences of bearings give included angles for triangles modelling navigation problems. 6.1 Ex 3
Angle of elevation/depression Angle measured from the horizontal up to a sighted object (elevation) or down (depression). Used in tower/building problems with the sine rule to find heights. 6.4 Q11
Periodic function A function that repeats: $\mathrm{f}(\theta+T)=\mathrm{f}(\theta)$ for all $\theta$. The smallest such $T>0$ is the period. $\sin,\cos$ have period $360°$; $\tan$ has period $180°$. 6.5
Period of $\sin\theta,\cos\theta$ $360°$ — both repeat every full revolution. Max value $+1$, min value $-1$, amplitude $1$. $\sin$ crosses zero at multiples of $180°$; $\cos$ crosses zero at $90°+180°k$. 6.5
Period of $\tan\theta$ $180°$ — $\tan$ repeats every half-revolution because the line through origin at angle $\theta$ vs $\theta+180°$ is the same line. Has vertical asymptotes at $\theta=\pm 90°,\pm 270°,\dots$ — $\tan$ is undefined there. 6.5
Asymptote A line that a curve approaches arbitrarily closely without crossing (or only crosses at infinity). $\tan\theta$ has vertical asymptotes at $\theta=90°+180°k$ where $\cos\theta=0$ in the denominator $\tan=\sin/\cos$. 6.5
Amplitude For $\sin,\cos$, half the vertical range — i.e. $1$ for $\sin\theta$, but $a$ for $a\sin\theta$. A vertical stretch by factor $a$ multiplies amplitude by $a$. ($\tan$ has no amplitude — it's unbounded.) 6.6
Translation $\mathrm{f}(x\pm a)$ $\mathrm{f}(x+a)$ shifts the graph left by $a$; $\mathrm{f}(x-a)$ shifts right by $a$ (signs feel backwards because they act inside the function). Vertical: $\mathrm{f}(x)+b$ shifts up by $b$. 6.6
Stretch $\mathrm{f}(kx)$ vs $k\mathrm{f}(x)$ $\mathrm{f}(kx)$ = horizontal stretch with scale factor $\dfrac{1}{k}$ (squeeze if $k>1$). $k\mathrm{f}(x)$ = vertical stretch with scale factor $k$. For trig: $\sin(kx)$ has period $\dfrac{360°}{k}$. 6.6
Reflections $-\mathrm{f}(x)$ = reflect in $x$-axis (flip vertically). $\mathrm{f}(-x)$ = reflect in $y$-axis (flip horizontally). For trig: $\cos(-\theta)=\cos\theta$ (cos is even); $\sin(-\theta)=-\sin\theta$ (sin is odd); $\tan(-\theta)=-\tan\theta$. 6.6 Q6
Co-function identities Translations of $90°$ link $\sin$ and $\cos$: $\cos(\theta-90°)=\sin\theta$ and $\sin(90°-\theta)=\cos\theta$. Geometrically: shift cosine right by $90°$ and you get sine. 6.6 Ex 14

2. Pick the right rule — decision table

You are given You want Use Reason
2 sides + included angle The 3rd side Cosine rule (side form) $a^2=b^2+c^2-2bc\cos A$ Only the cosine rule uses an included angle
3 sides Any angle Cosine rule (angle form) $\cos A=\dfrac{b^2+c^2-a^2}{2bc}$ No angles known → can't start sine rule
1 side + its opposite angle + 1 other angle The third side or second known angle's side Sine rule (side form) Pair side with opposite angle
2 sides + an angle opposite one of them The second angle (opposite the other side) Sine rule (angle form) — check ambiguous case One pair of (side, opp angle) lets you scale to find the other
2 sides + included angle Area Area $=\tfrac12 ab\sin C$ Direct
Right angle present Anything SOHCAHTOA + Pythagoras first Faster; only fall back to sine/cosine rule for non-right tris

Heuristic: count the data items. Cosine rule needs 3 known facts of which 2 are sides (SAS or SSS); sine rule needs an opposite pair (a side and its opposite angle) plus one more.

3. Cosine rule reference table

Goal Formula Comment
Find side $a$ $a^2=b^2+c^2-2bc\cos A$ $A$ is between $b$ and $c$
Find side $b$ $b^2=a^2+c^2-2ac\cos B$ $B$ is between $a$ and $c$
Find side $c$ $c^2=a^2+b^2-2ab\cos C$ $C$ is between $a$ and $b$
Find angle $A$ $\cos A=\dfrac{b^2+c^2-a^2}{2bc}$ $a$ is opposite $A$
Find angle $B$ $\cos B=\dfrac{a^2+c^2-b^2}{2ac}$ $b$ is opposite $B$
Find angle $C$ $\cos C=\dfrac{a^2+b^2-c^2}{2ab}$ $c$ is opposite $C$
Sign of $\cos A$ $>0\Rightarrow A<90°$; $=0\Rightarrow A=90°$ (Pythagoras); $<0\Rightarrow A>90°$ Quick acute/obtuse check

4. Sine rule + ambiguous case

Form Formula When to use
Find a missing side $\dfrac{a}{\sin A}=\dfrac{b}{\sin B}=\dfrac{c}{\sin C}$ Side on top — easier when finding side
Find a missing angle $\dfrac{\sin A}{a}=\dfrac{\sin B}{b}=\dfrac{\sin C}{c}$ Sine on top — easier when finding angle

Ambiguous case: when sine rule gives $\sin\theta=k$ with $0

Test What it tells you
Is $\theta_1+(\text{other known angle})<180°$? If yes, $\theta_1$ valid
Is $\theta_2+(\text{other known angle})<180°$? If yes, $\theta_2$ also valid → genuine ambiguity, two triangles
Is the side opposite the unknown angle the longest? Then the unknown angle is the largest — likely obtuse, choose $\theta_2$
Is the unknown angle drawn smaller than another? Then it must be acute — choose $\theta_1$
Question says "given $\angle X$ is obtuse" or "obtuse-angled triangle" Pick $\theta_2$ regardless

5. Area of a triangle — three formulas

Formula Variables you need When to use
$\tfrac12\times\text{base}\times\text{height}$ One side and the perpendicular height to it Right-angled or perpendicular height known
$\tfrac12 ab\sin C$ (or $\tfrac12 ac\sin B$, $\tfrac12 bc\sin A$) Two sides and included angle "SAS" data — most common
Heron's formula (not on syllabus, but useful) Three sides $a,b,c$; $s=\tfrac{a+b+c}{2}$ Three sides only — typically use cosine rule for an angle then area = $\tfrac12 ab\sin C$ instead

Inverse problem — given area, find an angle: $\sin C=\dfrac{2\,\text{Area}}{ab}$. Two solutions for $C$ (acute and obtuse), use context to pick. (See Ch. review Q1.)

7. Transformations of trig graphs

Transformation New equation Effect Worked example
Vertical translation by $b$ (up if $b>0$) $y=\mathrm{f}(\theta)+b$ Whole graph slides up by $b$; max becomes $\max+b$, min becomes $\min+b$ $y=-1+\sin x$: range $[-2,0]$
Horizontal translation by $a$ (right if $a>0$) $y=\mathrm{f}(\theta-a)$ Whole graph slides right by $a$; also translate asymptotes $y=\tan(\theta+45°)$: shift left $45°$, asymptotes at $45°,225°$
Vertical stretch, factor $k$ $y=k\mathrm{f}(\theta)$ Multiplies amplitude by $\lvert k\rvert$; if $k<0$ also reflects in $\theta$-axis $y=3\sin x$: max 3, min $-3$, period unchanged $360°$
Horizontal stretch, factor $\dfrac{1}{k}$ $y=\mathrm{f}(k\theta)$ Period of $\sin/\cos$ becomes $\dfrac{360°}{k}$; period of $\tan$ becomes $\dfrac{180°}{k}$ $y=\sin 2x$: period $180°$ — two waves in $[0°,360°]$
Horizontal stretch, factor $k$ ($k>1$) $y=\mathrm{f}\!\left(\dfrac{\theta}{k}\right)$ Period multiplied by $k$ $y=\cos\dfrac{\theta}{3}$: period $1080°$
Reflection in $x$-axis $y=-\mathrm{f}(\theta)$ Flip up-down (max↔min) $y=-\tan\theta$: same asymptotes, mirrored
Reflection in $y$-axis $y=\mathrm{f}(-\theta)$ Flip left-right; for even $\cos$, unchanged; for odd $\sin,\tan$, equals $-\mathrm{f}(\theta)$ $\tan(-x)=-\tan x$

Order tip: when both translation and stretch appear (e.g. $y=2\sin(3x-90°)$), apply inside operations first: shift by $90°/3=30°$, then stretch (so period $120°$), then $\times 2$ vertical.

9. Common exam pitfalls

Pitfall Fix
Mis-labelling sides — using "$a$" for a side adjacent to angle $A$ Always re-draw and re-label: opposite-side rule. $a$ is opposite $A$
Cosine rule sign error: dropping $-2bc\cos A$ → just $b^2+c^2$ Memorise the structure: "Pythagoras minus $2bc\cos A$"
Forgetting the obtuse partner in sine rule for angle Always compute both $\arcsin k$ and $180°-\arcsin k$, then test plausibility
Using sine rule when included angle is given (instead of cosine) Sine rule needs an opposite angle — if the angle given is between the two known sides, you must use cosine rule
Rounding mid-calculation, then carrying error to the next step Store intermediate values in calculator; only round the final answer to 3 s.f.
Bearings: subtracting wrongly → wrong included angle Sketch first; bearing is measured clockwise from north; the included angle of triangle is often the difference of two bearings, sometimes via a $360°-\text{bearing}$ flip
Trig graph: forgetting to translate asymptotes when shifting $\tan$ Asymptotes shift with the graph — $\tan(\theta-90°)$ has asymptotes at $90°+90°=180°$
Period miscalculation: writing period of $\sin 3x$ as $3\times 360°$ instead of $360°/3=120°$ $\mathrm{f}(kx)$ shrinks period by factor $k$
Horizontal stretch direction confusion $y=\mathrm{f}(2x)$ is narrower (squeeze, sf $\tfrac12$). $y=\mathrm{f}(\tfrac12 x)$ is wider (stretch, sf $2$)
Negative scale factor in stretch interpreted only as stretch $y=-2\sin x$ is "vertical stretch sf 2 and reflect in $x$-axis"

11. Big picture (30-second mental model)

For any triangle, three pieces of data nail it down (one of which must be a length). The rule you pick depends on which three:

  • SAS (side-angle-side, included angle) → cosine rule (side form), then sine rule for remaining angles.
  • SSS (three sides) → cosine rule (angle form), then sine rule.
  • AAS / ASA (one side + two angles) → sine rule (third angle from $180°$ sum, then ratios).
  • SSA (two sides + non-included angle) → sine rule, but check the ambiguous case ($\sin\theta=\sin(180°-\theta)$).
  • Area → $\tfrac12 ab\sin C$ when you have two sides + the angle between them; otherwise compute angle/side first.

Trig graphs $\sin,\cos,\tan$ are the clockwork of the universe — periodic, with $\sin,\cos$ bounded in $[-1,1]$ and $\tan$ shooting to $\pm\infty$ at $\cos=0$. Transformations follow the universal rules: translate inside the function = horizontal (with sign-flip), translate outside = vertical; stretch inside = horizontal (with reciprocal); stretch outside = vertical; minus signs reflect in axes.
