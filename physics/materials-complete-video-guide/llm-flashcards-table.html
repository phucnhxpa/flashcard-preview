Card 1

Starting from the elastic strain energy $$E = \frac{1}{2} F \Delta L$$, derive the expression for energy density (energy per unit volume) for a material obeying Hooke's law, showing that it equals $$\frac{1}{2} \sigma \varepsilon$$. (3 steps)

- Step 1: Energy density is energy per unit volume. Volume is $$V = A \times L$$, so divide the energy equation by volume: $$\text{Energy Density} = \frac{1}{2} \frac{F \Delta L}{A L}$$. - Step 2: Rearrange the terms to group force/area and extension/length: $$\text{Energy Density} = \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{F}{A} \right) \left( \frac{\Delta L}{L} \right)$$. - Step 3: Recognize that $$F/A$$ is stress ($$\sigma$$) and $$\Delta L / L$$ is strain ($$\varepsilon$$). Therefore, Energy Density = $$\frac{1}{2} \sigma \varepsilon$$.
Card 2

Why is elastic strain energy $$\tfrac{1}{2}Fx$$ rather than $$Fx$$ for a Hooke's-law spring? (3 steps)

- Step 1: Work done by a *constant* force is $$W = Fx$$. But during stretching, the spring force isn't constant — it starts at zero (when the spring is unstretched) and grows linearly to $$F$$. - Step 2: The *average* force during the stretch is $$(0 + F)/2 = F/2$$. So work done = average force × distance = $$\tfrac{1}{2}Fx$$. - Step 3: Equivalently, the area under the linear $$F$$-vs-$$x$$ graph from $$0$$ to $$x$$ is a triangle with base $$x$$ and height $$F$$, area $$\tfrac{1}{2}Fx$$. The factor of one-half always reflects "linearly varying from zero", whether for springs, kinetic energy ($$\tfrac{1}{2}mv^2$$), or capacitor energy ($$\tfrac{1}{2}CV^2$$).
Card 3

In a Young's modulus experiment, why is a long wire (several metres) preferred over a short wire (a few centimetres)? Explain in terms of measurement uncertainty. (2 steps)

- Step 1: For a given force, a longer wire extends more (since $$\Delta L = FL/AE$$ and $$\Delta L \propto L$$). A larger extension is easier to measure accurately and has a lower percentage uncertainty. - Step 2: If the wire is only a few centimetres long, the extension might be a fraction of a millimetre — comparable to the resolution of the measuring instrument. The percentage uncertainty in $$\Delta L$$ would be very large, making the calculated Young modulus unreliable. Longer wire → larger extension → lower percentage uncertainty → more accurate $$E$$.
Card 4

A force-extension graph for loading and unloading a metal wire in the elastic region shows the loading and unloading paths along the *same* straight line. For rubber, the paths are different (a hysteresis loop). What does this difference tell you about energy conservation in each material? (2 steps)

- Step 1: For the metal in the elastic region, the loading and unloading paths coincide. All the work done on the wire during loading is returned during unloading — energy is conserved. The material is perfectly elastic. - Step 2: For rubber, the unloading path lies below the loading path. The area between them (the hysteresis loop) represents energy that is *not* returned — it is dissipated as heat within the material. Rubber is elastic (it returns to its original shape) but not *perfectly* elastic in the energy sense. Elastic shape recovery ≠ elastic energy recovery. Rubber recovers its shape but loses energy as heat each cycle.
Card 5

Why is "stress" sometimes called the "inverse" of pressure by students, and what's wrong with this thinking?

- Step 1: This phrasing presumably comes from imagining pressure as "pushing in" on a solid and stress as "pushing out". But mathematically and dimensionally, they are the same quantity — force per unit area — with the same units (Pa). - Step 2: Tensile stress is a *pulling-apart* internal force per area in a stretched solid; compressive stress is a *pushing-together* internal force per area in a squeezed solid. Pressure (in fluids) is essentially compressive stress that acts in all directions. - Step 3: So stress is more general (it includes tensile, compressive, and shear varieties with directional information), but it is *not* the inverse of pressure — both are force/area. The corrected mental model: "Stress is like pressure, but applied internally to solids with a definite direction".
Card 6

Why is the area under a *stress–strain* graph "energy per unit volume" rather than total energy? (2 steps)

- Step 1: Area under a force–extension graph is energy: $$\int F \, dx$$ has units $$\mathrm{N\cdot m = J}$$. Dividing by the volume of the sample gives energy per unit volume. - Step 2: Stress is $$F/A$$ (force per unit area) and strain is $$\Delta L/L$$ (extension per unit length). Their product $$\sigma\varepsilon = F\Delta L/(AL) = F\Delta L/V$$ has units of $$\mathrm{J\,m^{-3}}$$. So the area under a stress–strain plot, $$\int \sigma\, d\varepsilon$$, is automatically a *volumetric* energy density. This is convenient because it makes toughness ($$\int \sigma\, d\varepsilon$$ up to fracture) a material property, not a sample property.
Card 7

Why should strain values be quoted as decimals (e.g. $$3.0\times 10^{-4}$$) and not percentages in Edexcel mark schemes?

- Step 1: Strain entering formulas like $$E = \sigma/\varepsilon$$ or $$\sigma = E\varepsilon$$ must be the dimensionless ratio. If you substitute "$$0.03\%$$" as $$0.03$$, you're off by a factor of $$100$$. - Step 2: Examiners want the decimal form to avoid that exact confusion. Writing $$\varepsilon = 3.0 \times 10^{-4}$$ is unambiguous; writing "0.03%" introduces a hidden conversion step. - Step 3: Convention: keep strain as a decimal in calculations and in final answers; only convert to a percentage if the question explicitly asks. The rule is operational: percentages multiply by an invisible factor of $$1/100$$, which causes silent errors.
Card 8

What is the conceptual difference between the *limit of proportionality* and the *elastic limit*?

- Step 1: The limit of proportionality is the point on a force–extension graph where the line stops being straight — beyond it, $$F$$ is no longer proportional to $$x$$ (Hooke's law breaks down). - Step 2: The elastic limit is the point beyond which the material no longer returns to its original shape when unloaded — permanent (plastic) deformation begins. - Step 3: Between them lies a narrow region where deformation is still *elastic* (recoverable) but *not proportional* (the curve bends but the material still snaps back). So: limit of proportionality ≤ elastic limit. They are not synonyms.
Card 9

Two identical springs each with constant $$k$$ are joined in series and a single weight is hung from the bottom. Why does the same force pass through *each* spring, and how does this make the chain "softer"? (3 steps)

- Step 1: In a vertical series chain, the bottom spring supports the weight, and the top spring supports the bottom spring (plus the weight). Since the springs themselves are taken as massless, the tension is uniform — every cross-section feels the same force $$F$$. - Step 2: Each spring therefore extends by $$x=F/k$$ individually. The total chain extension is the sum: $$x_{total}=2F/k$$. - Step 3: Effective stiffness: $$k_{eff}=F/x_{total}=k/2$$. The chain is "softer" (less stiff) because the same force produces double the extension — analogous to resistors in parallel where current finds two paths and resistance drops.
Card 10

Why is checking for *zero error* on a digital instrument just as important as on an analogue one, even though digital displays "look more reliable"?

- Step 1: A digital display has high *resolution* (small smallest-readable change), but resolution and accuracy are not the same thing. - Step 2: Internal calibration can drift due to wear, manufacturing tolerances, temperature, or battery voltage. A digital balance might read 0.02 g with nothing on it — this systematic offset contaminates every measurement. - Step 3: Checking zero (no load, no contact) and either correcting in software or subtracting from every reading is essential. The polished appearance of digital instruments tricks people into assuming they're inherently accurate; they aren't.
Card 11

Archimedes' principle states that the upthrust on an immersed object equals the weight of fluid displaced. A stone of mass 125 g is immersed in water and the spring balance reads 1.00 N. Is the upthrust equal to 1.00 N, or something else? Explain. (2 steps)

- Step 1: The weight of the stone is $$W = mg = 0.125 \times 9.81 \approx 1.23\,\text{N}$$. The spring balance reads 1.00 N, which is the *apparent weight* (weight minus upthrust). - Step 2: Upthrust = weight − apparent weight = $$1.23 - 1.00 = 0.23\,\text{N}$$. This equals the weight of water displaced. The reading on the balance is NOT the upthrust — it is the reduced weight. Spring balance reading = true weight − upthrust. To find upthrust, subtract the reading from the true weight.
Card 12

Why is taking a graphical gradient generally more accurate than computing $$k$$ from a single force-extension pair? (3 steps)

- Step 1: A single pair of readings contains both random measurement errors and any zero-error offset baked into the apparatus. - Step 2: A line of best fit through many points averages out random errors statistically. The gradient depends on differences, not absolute values, so a constant zero-error offset cancels out. - Step 3: Moreover, the gradient method visibly reveals systematic deviations: a non-zero $$y$$-intercept signals zero error or systematic offset; curvature signals departure from Hooke's law. Single-point arithmetic hides both. Graphs are diagnostic instruments, not just answer-getters.
Card 13

Explain why plotting a graph to determine Young's modulus (or spring constant) is more accurate than using a single pair of force-extension readings. (2 steps)

- Step 1: A single reading is subject to random errors in both force and extension measurements. The gradient of a best-fit line through multiple points effectively averages out these random errors — individual outliers have limited influence. - Step 2: A graph also reveals systematic errors (e.g., a non-zero intercept suggests a zero error or an initial extension) and shows whether the material obeys Hooke's law across the range (the line should be straight and pass through the origin). A graph gives you: (1) averaging of random errors, (2) detection of systematic errors, and (3) verification of the linear model.
Card 14

Why is measuring 10 sheets of paper and dividing by 10 better than measuring 1 sheet?

- Step 1: A single sheet is, say, 0.1 mm thick. A ruler with 1 mm resolution gives ~1000% percentage uncertainty — useless. - Step 2: A stack of 10 sheets is ~1 mm thick. Same ruler now gives ~100% — still poor but already much better. A micrometer (0.01 mm) on 10 sheets gives ~1%. - Step 3: Dividing by 10 at the end reduces the *measured* value while preserving the *absolute* uncertainty of the instrument. The percentage uncertainty in the final per-sheet thickness equals the percentage uncertainty in the stack measurement, which has been driven down by making the measurement larger. *Trick: enlarge the quantity to shrink the percentage uncertainty*.
Card 15

Why is the equation $$\rho=m/V$$ called a *material* property rather than just a property of one specific object?

- Step 1: For any given substance under fixed conditions (temperature, pressure, phase), packing the molecules together gives a fixed mass per unit volume. - Step 2: If you double the volume of pure water, you also double the mass — the ratio $$m/V$$ stays the same. - Step 3: So density is intensive (independent of size of sample), and depends only on what the material *is*, not how much of it you have. This is why we can quote single values like "water = 1000 kg/m³" without specifying how much water.
Card 16

A rubber band stretches very easily at first, then becomes very stiff before breaking. What's happening at the molecular level? (3 steps)

- Step 1: Natural rubber is a polymer — long tangled chains of carbon atoms held together by weak bonds between chains and stronger covalent bonds along the chain. - Step 2: Initial stretching mostly *uncoils* and aligns the chains — easy, because it doesn't stretch covalent bonds, just rearranges them. So small force produces large extension. - Step 3: Once chains are fully extended, further stretching requires stretching the actual covalent bonds along the backbone. These are stiff, so the rubber becomes very resistant — the curve bends upward sharply before breaking. This is why rubber's force-extension curve is non-linear from the start — there's no Hooke region.
Card 17

Young's modulus is defined as $$E = \sigma / \varepsilon$$. Derive the expanded form $$E = FL / (A\Delta L)$$ from the definitions of stress and strain, and explain why $$E$$ is a property of the *material* while $$k$$ is a property of the *object*. (3 steps)

- Step 1: Substitute the definitions: $$E = \frac{F/A}{\Delta L / L}$$. - Step 2: Simplify the compound fraction: $$E = \frac{F}{A} \times \frac{L}{\Delta L} = \frac{FL}{A\Delta L}$$. - Step 3: The spring constant $$k = F/\Delta L$$ depends on the object's dimensions (a longer wire extends more for the same force; a thicker wire extends less). Young's modulus divides out these dimensional effects by including $$L$$ and $$A$$, so it depends only on the material itself. Two wires of different sizes but the same material have the same $$E$$ but different $$k$$. $$k$$ describes a specific object; $$E$$ describes a class of material.
Card 18

Why is strain dimensionless, and what's the consequence for the units of Young modulus?

- Step 1: Strain is $$\varepsilon=\Delta L/L$$ — a ratio of two lengths. The metres cancel, leaving a pure number. - Step 2: Young modulus $$E=\sigma/\varepsilon$$. Since strain is dimensionless, $$E$$ inherits the units of stress. - Step 3: So $$E$$ has units of Pa (or N/m²), just like stress. Typical values are huge (steel ≈ $$2\times10^{11}\,\mathrm{Pa}$$) because strains are typically very small (e.g. $$10^{-3}$$ at modest loads), so dividing a modest stress by a tiny strain gives a giant number.
Card 19

On a stress–strain graph, what physical quantity does the *area under the curve* represent, and why?

- Step 1: Stress = force/area, strain = extension/length. So stress × strain = (F × ΔL)/(A × L) = work done/volume. - Step 2: The area under a stress–strain curve has units Pa × (dimensionless) = Pa = J/m³ — energy per unit volume. - Step 3: Physically, this is the *elastic strain energy per unit volume* stored in the material at that strain. It's a measure of *toughness*: a tough material (like copper) has a large area under the curve before fracture; a brittle material (like glass) has a small one.
Card 20

Estimate the mass of air in a 5 m × 4 m × 2.5 m room, using rough mental arithmetic. Why is this a good sanity-check exercise?

- Step 1: Volume ≈ 50 m³. Air density ≈ 1.2 kg/m³. - Step 2: Mass ≈ 50 × 1.2 = 60 kg. - Step 3: That's about the mass of one person! This surprises most people — air seems weightless, but in any sizeable enclosed space it's actually substantial. The exercise builds intuition: density values can be plugged into volume-mass conversions and immediately tested against everyday intuition (does the answer make sense?). Order-of-magnitude estimates are a powerful tool for catching arithmetic errors.
Card 21

When measuring the density of an irregular object using a Eureka can, two potential errors are: (a) air bubbles sticking to the object when submerged, and (b) water adhering to the spout instead of flowing into the measuring cylinder. For each, state whether the measured density would be too high or too low, and why. (2 steps)

- Step 1: Air bubbles on the object displace extra water, so the measured volume is *larger* than the true volume. Since $$\rho = m/V$$, a larger $$V$$ gives a *smaller* density — the measured density is too low. - Step 2: Water adhering to the spout means less water reaches the measuring cylinder, so the measured volume is *smaller* than the true volume. A smaller $$V$$ gives a *larger* density — the measured density is too high. Air bubbles → volume too large → density too low. Water loss → volume too small → density too high. They push in opposite directions.
Card 22

When a micrometer's jaws are fully closed, the thimble reads 0.03 mm instead of zero. Classify this zero error as positive or negative, state the correction rule, and give the actual reading when the observed reading is 5.47 mm. (3 steps)

- Step 1: When closed, the instrument reads 0.03 mm instead of 0. This means it reads *above* zero, so it is a **positive zero error** of $$+0.03\,\text{mm}$$. - Step 2: The correction rule is: Actual Reading = Observed Reading − Zero Error. - Step 3: Actual = $$5.47 - 0.03 = 5.44\,\text{mm}$$. Positive zero error → subtract; negative zero error → add. The mnemonic: the correction always pushes the reading back toward what it *should* have been.
Card 23

Why can't you just average the densities of copper and zinc, weighted by percentage, to get brass density when the percentages are by *mass* rather than by volume?

- Step 1: Density is mass-per-volume. If 70% of the mass is copper, that doesn't mean 70% of the volume is copper — because copper is denser, the same mass occupies less volume than the same mass of zinc. - Step 2: To find total volume from a mass-fraction split, you compute $$V = m_{Cu}/\rho_{Cu} + m_{Zn}/\rho_{Zn}$$, then $$\rho = m_{\text{total}}/V$$. - Step 3: That's a harmonic-style average, not arithmetic. So the rule is: by-volume mix → arithmetic mean weighted by volume fraction. By-mass mix → harmonic mean weighted by mass fraction. The structure of the average depends on which quantity (mass or volume) you are partitioning.
Card 24

Explain why a 0.01 mm error in radius calculation leads to a 0.03 mm error in volume for a sphere. (2 steps)

- Step 1: Volume V = (4/3)πr³ → V ∝ r³. - Step 2: A 1% radius error causes ~3% volume error (derivative dV/V = 3×dr/r via power rule).
Card 25

Distinguish *limit of proportionality*, *elastic limit*, and *yield point* on a force–extension curve.

- Step 1: Limit of proportionality: the largest extension for which $$F \propto x$$. Past this, the graph stops being a straight line — but the material may still spring back when unloaded. - Step 2: Elastic limit: the largest extension after which the material *still* returns to its original length when unloaded. Just beyond this, permanent deformation begins. - Step 3: Yield point: the load at which the material begins to deform plastically with little or no increase in force — the curve becomes nearly horizontal (or "jerky" for mild steel). Always: limit of proportionality ≤ elastic limit ≤ yield point. The three landmarks describe progressively more severe departures from ideal elastic behaviour.
Card 26

Why do you take repeated readings of a single measurement in a physics experiment, and what type of error does this address?

- Step 1: Random errors arise from unpredictable fluctuations: vibrations, slightly different reaction times, instrument noise. They scatter readings symmetrically around the true value. - Step 2: Taking many readings and averaging causes the random fluctuations to partially cancel — the mean approaches the true value as $$\sqrt{N}$$ (statistical noise drops with more samples). - Step 3: *Systematic* errors (e.g. miscalibrated instrument, zero error) are *not* reduced by averaging — they shift every reading the same way. Those require a different fix: calibration, zero correction, or improved method.
Card 27

Why is Young modulus a more useful material descriptor than the spring constant $$k$$ for comparing, say, two steel cables of different sizes?

- Step 1: The spring constant $$k=F/x$$ depends on the geometry of the object: a thicker or shorter cable has a higher $$k$$ even if made of the same steel. - Step 2: Young modulus normalises out the geometry: $$E=\frac{F/A}{\Delta L/L}=\frac{FL}{A\Delta L}$$. The cross-sectional area and original length are divided out. - Step 3: Two cables made of identical steel will have *the same* $$E$$ (≈ 200 GPa) regardless of their thickness or length. So $$E$$ is a property of the *material*; $$k$$ is a property of the *object*. This is why textbooks tabulate $$E$$ for materials, not $$k$$.
Card 28

A copper wire has area 0.30 cm² and brass has tensile strength 190 MPa. Why is the maximum *force* the wire can withstand simply $$\sigma \times A$$, and what assumption are you making? (3 steps)

- Step 1: Tensile strength = maximum stress before breaking. At the failure point, $$\sigma_{max}=F_{max}/A$$. - Step 2: Rearranging: $$F_{max}=\sigma_{max}\times A=190\times 10^6\,\mathrm{Pa}\times 0.30\times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{m^2}\approx 5700\,\mathrm{N}\approx 5.7\,\mathrm{kN}$$. - Step 3: The assumption: the area $$A$$ refers to the *cross-sectional area in the direction of pulling*. For a wire, this is the round cross-section; for a beam under tension, the rectangular face. Mistakenly using the total surface area (e.g. for a square rod) gives nonsense. *Tensile* stress is force per cross-section, not per surface.
Card 29

Why is air density (~$$1\,\mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}}$$) so much smaller than water density (~$$1000\,\mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}}$$), even though both are made of small molecules of similar mass?

- Step 1: In liquids, molecules are packed shoulder-to-shoulder, in continuous contact — molecular spacing is roughly the molecule size itself. - Step 2: In gases at atmospheric pressure, molecules are typically ~10 molecular diameters apart in each dimension — so a 1000-fold smaller density (since spacing cubed gives volume). - Step 3: Both have similar molecular *masses*, but the *volume* taken up per molecule is vastly different. Compressing a gas brings the molecules closer; once they touch, it becomes a liquid. The mass per unit volume ratio reflects how much empty space sits between molecules.
Card 30

Identify which formula is needed: a question asks for *stress* given diameter, force, original length, and Young modulus. Is the Young modulus a red herring, and why?

- Step 1: Stress = F/A. Force is given. Area: from diameter, $$A=\pi(d/2)^2$$. - Step 2: That's enough. Stress is directly $$F/(\pi d^2/4)$$. Original length and Young modulus don't enter. - Step 3: Young modulus would be needed only if the question asked for *strain* or *extension*. Sometimes exam questions deliberately give extra information to test whether you can pick the relevant subset. Always identify what's asked, then trace the minimal formula path — don't try to use every given number.
Card 31

Why measure the diameter of a wire at *several points and orientations* before computing the cross-sectional area?

- Step 1: Wires are not perfectly uniform — manufacturing leaves slight variations in thickness along the length, and the cross-section may be slightly elliptical rather than circular. - Step 2: One reading at one orientation could be unrepresentative; if it's high, $$A$$ is overestimated and $$E$$ is underestimated (and vice versa). - Step 3: Multiple readings at different positions and angles, then averaging, gives a best estimate of the typical diameter. Since $$A\propto d^2$$, any error in $$d$$ is *doubled* in $$A$$, making careful averaging especially important.
Card 32

A mild steel wire and a high-carbon steel wire have the same Young modulus (~200 GPa). A student says "they must have the same strength." Is this correct? Explain. (2 steps)

- Step 1: Young modulus measures stiffness (resistance to elastic deformation), not strength (resistance to breaking). Two materials can have the same Young modulus but different breaking stresses. - Step 2: High-carbon steel has a higher breaking stress (UTS) than mild steel — it is stronger. But mild steel has a much longer plastic region (it is tougher, absorbs more energy before breaking). High-carbon steel is more brittle — it fractures with little plastic deformation. Stiffness (Young modulus), strength (UTS), and toughness (energy to fracture) are three distinct material properties. Do not confuse them.
Card 33

What's the difference between *strength*, *stiffness*, and *toughness* as material properties?

- Step 1: Strength = maximum stress before fracture (or UTS). High-strength materials can carry large loads per unit area. - Step 2: Stiffness = resistance to deformation under stress, quantified by Young's modulus. A stiff material has small strain for a given stress. - Step 3: Toughness = total energy absorbed per unit volume before fracture (area under stress–strain curve). A tough material can absorb impacts; a brittle one cannot. + Diamond: stiff and hard but brittle (low toughness). + Rubber: low stiffness but very tough — absorbs huge energy through large strains. + Mild steel: stiff, strong, AND tough — why it's an excellent structural material. The three are independent — a material can be strong but not tough (high-carbon steel), or tough but not strong (rubber).
Card 34

What's the difference between *accuracy* and *precision*, and why can a measurement be precise but inaccurate?

- Step 1: Accuracy is closeness to the *true value*. A measurement is accurate if it's near what it ought to be. - Step 2: Precision is repeatability — how closely repeated measurements *agree with each other*, regardless of whether they're correct. - Step 3: An archer who consistently hits the same wrong spot is precise but inaccurate (a systematic error). One who scatters hits widely around the bull is accurate on average but imprecise (random error). A good experiment aims for both: low scatter (precision) *and* the right value (accuracy). Improving averaging fixes precision; calibration fixes accuracy.
Card 35

Distinguish *resolution*, *accuracy*, *precision*, and *range* of a measuring instrument — and explain why high precision does not imply high accuracy.

- Step 1: Resolution = smallest detectable change (e.g. $$0.01\,\mathrm{mm}$$ on a micrometer). Range = max − min the instrument can read. - Step 2: Accuracy = closeness of measurement to the true value. Precision = closeness of repeated measurements to *each other*. - Step 3: A faulty-calibrated instrument (e.g. micrometer with a zero error of $$+0.05\,\mathrm{mm}$$) can give very consistent readings that are all systematically wrong. That is high precision, low accuracy. + Random errors hurt precision. + Systematic errors hurt accuracy. The archer analogy: tight grouping in the wrong place = precise but inaccurate.
Card 36

Two identical springs of constant $$k$$ are joined end-to-end (in series) and a weight is hung from the bottom. Why is the combined spring constant $$k/2$$, not $$2k$$? (3 steps)

- Step 1: The same force $$F$$ passes through both springs (each spring must support the entire load). Each spring extends by $$x_1 = F/k$$. - Step 2: Total extension = $$x_1 + x_2 = 2F/k$$. So the effective spring constant is $$k_{\text{eff}} = F/(2F/k) = k/2$$. - Step 3: Physical picture: in series the springs each contribute their full extension, so the combination extends twice as much for the same force — it's *less* stiff. Series springs are like resistors in parallel: more units in line → easier to deform overall.
Card 37

Why is the area under a *stress–strain* curve called the "elastic strain energy per unit volume", and why is this preferable to the area under a force–extension curve when comparing materials? (3 steps)

- Step 1: Force–extension area gives total energy (in joules) — but this depends on the *sample size*. A long, thick steel bar stores more energy than a short, thin one, even though both are made of identical steel. - Step 2: Dividing energy by sample volume gives energy density (J/m³). Mathematically, stress × strain = (F/A)(ΔL/L) = (F × ΔL)/(A × L) = work/volume. - Step 3: So area under stress–strain curve is a *material property*: it depends only on what the material is, not how much you have. This lets you fairly compare toughness across different sample sizes and shapes.
Card 38

Why does pre-straining steel rails before laying them in cold weather prevent damage later?

- Step 1: Steel expands when heated and contracts when cooled. If rails are laid relaxed in cold weather, summer heat would make them expand and buckle (compressive stress, sideways deformation). - Step 2: Pre-straining means stretching the rails first (putting them under tension), then fixing them in place. As temperature rises, the rails *try* to expand — instead they just relieve the tension. - Step 3: If summer heat is hot enough to relieve all the tension, the rails are merely unstressed (no buckling). The engineer picks a pre-strain matched to the expected temperature rise, so the rails sit somewhere between modest tension (cool weather) and zero stress (hottest expected day), never reaching damaging compression.
Card 39

Brass is 70% copper and 30% zinc *by volume*. Without doing the arithmetic, explain why the brass density must lie between the copper and zinc densities, and closer to which one.

- Step 1: For a mixture combined by volume, total mass = $$\rho_{Cu}V_{Cu} + \rho_{Zn}V_{Zn}$$, and total volume = $$V_{Cu} + V_{Zn}$$. - Step 2: The mixture density $$\rho_{\text{brass}} = (\rho_{Cu}V_{Cu} + \rho_{Zn}V_{Zn})/V_{\text{total}}$$ is a *volume-weighted average* of the component densities. - Step 3: Any weighted average lies between the two extremes, with the result pulled toward whichever component has the larger weight. Since copper takes the larger volume share (70%), $$\rho_{\text{brass}}$$ lies closer to $$\rho_{Cu}$$. The conceptual point: by-volume mixing → volume-weighted density; by-mass mixing would give a different formula and weighting.
Card 40

Explain the difference between elastic deformation and plastic deformation. What happens at the molecular level in each case? (2 steps)

- Step 1: In **elastic deformation**, the atoms are displaced from their equilibrium positions but the bonds are only stretched/compressed, not broken. When the force is removed, the interatomic forces pull the atoms back to their original positions — the material recovers its shape completely. - Step 2: In **plastic deformation**, the applied stress is large enough that layers of atoms slide past each other. Bonds are broken and reformed in new positions. When the force is removed, there are no restoring forces to return the atoms to their original arrangement — the deformation is permanent. Elastic = bonds stretched (reversible). Plastic = bonds broken and reformed (irreversible).
Card 41

In the formula $$p = h\rho g$$ for fluid pressure, explain what each variable represents and why this formula does *not* work accurately for the Earth's atmosphere over large height differences. (2 steps)

- Step 1: $$p$$ = pressure at depth $$h$$ in a fluid of density $$\rho$$, where $$g$$ is gravitational field strength. The formula assumes the fluid density $$\rho$$ is constant throughout the column. - Step 2: For the atmosphere, air is compressible — it is denser at lower altitudes and much less dense at higher altitudes. So $$\rho$$ is not constant with height, and the formula overestimates the height of the atmosphere if you use sea-level density. You would need to use an average density, which is only an approximation. Liquids are nearly incompressible ($$\rho \approx$$ constant), so $$p = h\rho g$$ works well. Gases are compressible, so it doesn't.
Card 42

A copper wire of length $$1.52\,\mathrm{m}$$ stretches by $$3.2\,\mathrm{mm}$$ under tension. Why is it *impossible* to find the stress from this information alone?

- Step 1: With these numbers, you can compute strain: $$\varepsilon = 0.0032/1.52 \approx 2.1\times 10^{-3}$$. - Step 2: But stress is $$\sigma = F/A$$ — you need either the applied force *and* the cross-sectional area, or the Young modulus (so you could use $$\sigma = E\varepsilon$$). - Step 3: The wire's dimensions and extension alone give strain, but the relationship between strain and stress depends on the *material* (via $$E$$) and on the actual *load* — neither of which is provided. Card 48

What's the difference in the *underlying microscopic picture* between elastic deformation in a metal and elastic deformation in a polymer like rubber?

- Step 1: In a metal, elastic deformation stretches inter-atomic bonds — atoms move slightly off their equilibrium lattice positions. The restoring force comes from electron-cloud repulsion and attraction between charged ions. This is why $$E$$ is high and the relationship is linear. - Step 2: In rubber, elastic deformation is mostly *configurational* — long polymer chains uncoil and rearrange. The "restoring force" comes from entropy, not from bond-stretching: a tangled state is more probable than a stretched state, so when released the chains spontaneously return to a coiled mess. - Step 3: That's why metals have linear elasticity with high $$E$$, and rubbers have nonlinear, low-$$E$$, but enormous-strain elasticity. For an irregular object like a stone, why does Archimedes' displacement method give you the *volume* of the stone directly, and what are the dominant sources of error in this method?

- Step 1: An incompressible solid pushes water out of the way. If you mix equal masses of two liquids with densities $$\rho_1$$ and $$\rho_2$$, is the final density simply their average $$(\rho_1 + \rho_2)/2$$? Explain why or why not. (2 steps)

- Step 1: No, the final density is not the simple average. Density is mass divided by volume. Card 51

Stress and pressure both have units of pascals. What's the conceptual difference, and why don't we just call them the same thing?

- Step 1: Pressure is force per unit area applied externally by a fluid, acting perpendicular to a surface and in all directions equally (isotropic). - Step 2: Tensile/compressive stress is the *internal* force per unit area within a solid that resists deformation; it has a definite direction (along the axis of pulling/pushing) and can be tensile (pulling apart) or compressive (pushing together). - Step 3: They share units because both are force/area, but the *physical situation* differs: pressure is what a fluid exerts on a wall; stress is what particles inside a stretched solid exert on each other to resist further stretching.

On a stress–strain graph for mild steel, what physical events do these features correspond to: linear region, yield point, "necking", fracture? (4 steps)

- Step 1: Linear region — bonds between atoms behave like springs. Atoms move slightly from equilibrium positions; everything is reversible. Slope = $$E$$. - Step 2: Yield point — sudden drop or plateau in the curve. Layers of atoms begin to slip past each other along dislocations. No further force needed to keep extending. - Step 3: Strain hardening / further extension — dislocations multiply and get tangled, making further plastic flow harder, so stress rises again toward the UTS (ultimate tensile stress). - Step 4: After UTS — local cross-section narrows ("necks"), so true stress in that thin region rises rapidly until the wire fractures. Why does a balloon contain air slightly *denser* than atmospheric air, even at the same temperature?

Two identical springs of constant $$k$$ are joined in parallel supporting a single weight via a rigid bar. Why does the effective stiffness *double*?

Why do we say upthrust = weight of fluid *displaced* (not weight of fluid *above* or *around* the object)?

- Step 1: Upthrust is the net upward force from fluid pressure differences. Card 60

Why does the area under a force–extension graph give the elastic strain energy stored in a spring? (3 steps)

- Step 1: Work done by a variable force is $$W=\int F\,dx$$ — geometrically, the area under the F–x curve. - Step 2: As you stretch the spring, the force *increases* from 0 to $$F$$, so the *average* force during stretching is $$F/2$$ (for a linear Hooke region). Work done = average force × distance = $$\frac12 Fx$$. - Step 3: This work is stored in the spring as elastic potential energy (no friction, no plastic flow). For a Hooke-law spring, $$E=\frac12 Fx=\frac12 kx^2$$. If the graph is non-linear (e.g. rubber), $$\frac12 Fx$$ overestimates; you must actually count the area under the curve.
Card 61

What's the role of plotting a graph in reducing the effect of measurement errors during a Young modulus or Hooke's law experiment?

- Step 1: A single F/x pair has whatever uncertainty its individual readings carry. The graph plots many such points. - Step 2: A best-fit straight line through scattered points effectively *averages* all the data — random scatter above and below the line cancels. - Step 3: A systematic error (e.g. wrong zero) shifts all points by the same amount, but the *gradient* (which gives $$k$$ or $$E$$) is unaffected — only the intercept shifts. So the graph both reduces random error (via averaging) and reveals systematic error (via a non-zero intercept where one is expected).

- Step 1: For a given force, extension scales with original length: $$\Delta L=FL/(AE)$$. - Step 2: A longer wire produces a larger absolute extension for the same load, which means the extension reading has lower percentage uncertainty (the millimetre-scale ruling on the rule is the same, but the measurement is bigger). - Step 3: A short wire gives extensions of a fraction of a millimetre — within the noise of the measuring instrument. Why does a hot-air balloon rise when first inflated, and what changes when it reaches a stable altitude? (3 steps)

- Step 1: Heating the air inside the balloon reduces its density (same mass, larger volume). The cooler, denser surrounding air provides an upthrust equal to the weight of displaced surrounding air. - Step 2: When upthrust > total weight (balloon fabric + hot air + payload), there's a net upward force → the balloon accelerates upward. - Step 3: As the balloon rises, surrounding air gets less dense (lower atmospheric pressure), reducing upthrust. When does $$E=\frac12 kx^2$$ stop being valid for elastic energy stored, and what should you use instead? (3 steps)

- Step 1: A force-extension graph depends on the wire's dimensions: a longer wire extends more (larger $$\Delta L$$ for same $$F$$), and a thicker wire has a larger $$A$$ (so it can carry more force before reaching a given stress). Two samples of the same material — regardless of size — produce the *same* stress-strain graph. This allows direct comparison of material properties. Stress-strain = material property. Force-extension = object property.
Card 85

Why is the spring-series-parallel rule "backwards" from the resistor-series-parallel rule? (2 steps)

- Step 1: The mathematical match is by *role*, not by geometry. For resistors, voltage $$V = IR$$ relates "potential drop" to current. For springs, force $$F = kx$$ relates force to extension. The quantity that is *shared* in series is current (for resistors) and force (for springs); the quantity that *sums* is voltage drop (resistors) and extension (springs). - Step 2: So springs in geometric series behave mathematically like resistors in geometric parallel — both involve adding compliances (reciprocals of stiffness/conductance). The mapping is: stiffness ↔ conductance ($$1/R$$), compliance ↔ resistance. Spring series geometry = resistor parallel geometry in mathematical form.

A student has two identical springs with $$k = 240\,\text{N}\,\text{m}^{-1}$$ and natural length 210 mm. They are joined in series and a force of 8.0 N is applied. What is the length of *each individual spring*, and why is it wrong to add the total chain extension to just one spring? (3 steps)

- Step 1: In series, each spring carries the full 8.0 N. The extension of each spring is $$x = F/k = 8.0/240 = 0.0333\,\text{m}$$. - Step 2: The length of each individual spring = natural length + its own extension = $$0.210 + 0.0333 = 0.243\,\text{m}$$. - Step 3: A common mistake is to use the effective series stiffness ($$k_{\text{eff}} = 120\,\text{N}\,\text{m}^{-1}$$) to find the *total* extension ($$8.0/120 = 0.0667\,\text{m}$$) and then add this total to one spring's natural length. Card 87

An object falling through a viscous fluid reaches terminal velocity. Conceptually, what's happening to the upthrust and viscous drag at that moment?

- Step 1: At terminal velocity, the object stops accelerating: net force = 0. So weight = upthrust + viscous drag. - Step 2: Upthrust is constant (depends only on fluid density and submerged volume — both fixed for a given object/fluid pair). Viscous drag, $$F_D = 6\pi\eta rv$$ for a small sphere, grows with velocity. - Step 3: As the object speeds up, drag grows until weight − upthrust = drag exactly. From then on, it falls at constant velocity. The system self-regulates: drag automatically rises to match the residual weight.
Card 88

Why do we plot a *force–extension* graph (not a force–length graph) when analysing Hooke's law? (3 steps)

- Step 1: Hooke's law is $$F=kx$$ where $$x$$ is the *extension from natural length*, not the total length. - Step 2: If you plot F against total length, the line doesn't pass through the origin — it crosses the length axis at the natural length, adding a constant offset and obscuring the proportional relationship. - Step 3: Subtracting the natural length to get extension shifts the graph so it passes through the origin, making the gradient ($$k$$) easy to identify and Hooke's law visually obvious. Always: extension = current length − natural (zero-force) length.
Card 89

Why is the gradient of a stress–strain graph numerically equal to the Young modulus, while the gradient of a force–extension graph equals the spring constant $$k$$ (not $$E$$)? (3 steps)

- Step 1: On a stress–strain graph, x-axis is $$\varepsilon=\Delta L/L$$ and y-axis is $$\sigma=F/A$$. Slope = $$\sigma/\varepsilon=E$$ directly — the Young modulus. - Step 2: On a force–extension graph, x-axis is $$\Delta L$$ and y-axis is $$F$$. Slope = $$F/\Delta L=k$$ — the spring constant of this particular object. - Step 3: The two are related: $$k=EA/L$$. So $$k$$ depends on geometry (length and area), while $$E$$ doesn't. Two wires of the same steel but different sizes have different $$k$$'s but the same $$E$$. This is why physicists prefer stress–strain plots for material characterisation: the slope is universal for the material.
Card 90

Why does the maximum extension of a rubber band depend not on stiffness but on the *length of the polymer chains*?

- Step 1: At low extension, the polymer chains in rubber are tangled. Small forces untangle them without stretching the strong C-C bonds. - Step 2: As extension increases, more chains align until fully extended. The total extension at this stage depends on how long the chains *are* — longer chains can extend further before becoming fully aligned. - Step 3: Once all chains are aligned, further stretching means breaking covalent bonds (stretching them strongly). The "maximum extension" of rubber thus reflects how much *slack* exists in the molecular structure, not how stiff each bond is. This is fundamentally different from a metal, where extension comes from stretching atomic bonds throughout.
Card 91

Why does a mixture's density usually *not* equal the simple arithmetic mean of its components' densities, but a *volume-weighted* average?

- Step 1: Density is mass per volume. The total mass is the sum of component masses; the total volume is the sum of component volumes (assuming no shrinkage/expansion on mixing). - Step 2: $$\rho_{mix}=\frac{m_1+m_2}{V_1+V_2}=\frac{\rho_1 V_1+\rho_2 V_2}{V_1+V_2}$$ — this is a volume-weighted average. - Step 3: An arithmetic mean of $$\rho_1$$ and $$\rho_2$$ would only be correct if equal *volumes* were mixed. In a brass alloy that is 70% copper and 30% zinc *by volume*, the answer is closer to copper than a simple average suggests — exactly because more of the volume is copper.
Card 92

Why does adding a small initial mass to the test wire in Searle's apparatus matter before starting Young modulus measurements?

- Step 1: A new wire has small kinks, bends, and slack from manufacture and handling. - Step 2: Without an initial load, the first added weights waste their extension just straightening the wire rather than truly stretching it. This contaminates the F–x relationship. - Step 3: A small pre-load (e.g. a 100 g hanger) removes the kinks, sets a sensible "zero" for the vernier scale, and ensures all subsequent extension readings reflect actual elastic stretching. Skipping this step introduces a systematic underestimate of $$E$$.

A student is told that a suspension bridge cable has diameter 50 mm and pulls with force 4 kN. They immediately write $$A = \pi(50)^2$$. List all the errors. (3 steps)

Card 94

A force–extension graph for a non-linear spring rises sharply at first and then bends over. How do you find the elastic strain energy stored at extension $$x_0$$? (2 steps)

- Step 1: Energy stored = work done = area under the graph from $$0$$ to $$x_0$$. Do *not* use $$\tfrac{1}{2}Fx_0$$, which is only valid when the line is straight through the origin. - Step 2: For a curve, either: + Approximate by trapezia (split the area into strips and add). + Count squares under the curve and multiply by the energy per square ($$\Delta F \times \Delta x$$ for each grid square). + If the function is known analytically, integrate $$\int_0^{x_0} F(x)\, dx$$. Card 95

Young's modulus combines stress and strain. Conceptually, what does $$E$$ tell you that the spring constant $$k$$ does *not*? (3 steps)

- Step 1: Spring constant $$k$$ depends on the *object's geometry* — its length, its cross-section, its material. A long thin wire of steel and a short thick rod of steel have very different $$k$$ values. - Step 2: Young's modulus $$E = \sigma/\varepsilon$$ factors out the geometry: stress already divides force by area, and strain already divides extension by length. So $$E$$ depends only on the *material*. - Step 3: $$E$$ is a fundamental material property (like density). Steel always has $$E \approx 2\times10^{11}\,\mathrm{Pa}$$ regardless of shape; that lets engineers compare materials cleanly. $$k$$ is a sample-specific stiffness; $$E$$ is a material-specific stiffness.

Steel has $$E \approx 2\times 10^{11}\,\mathrm{Pa}$$; rubber has $$E \sim 10^{7}$$–$$10^{8}\,\mathrm{Pa}$$. By what rough factor is steel stiffer than rubber, and what does that mean for similarly-sized samples under the same load?

- Step 1: Ratio $$E_{\text{steel}}/E_{\text{rubber}} \sim 10^{11}/10^{7} = 10^{4}$$ — steel is about ten thousand times stiffer. - Step 2: For samples of the same dimensions and the same load: $$\Delta L \propto 1/E$$. So a rubber rod stretches 10,000 times as much as a steel rod under the same force. - Step 3: Equivalently, to produce the *same* extension, you'd need 10,000 times more force on the steel — which is why steel is used for structural beams and rubber is used for shock-absorbers. Card 97

A graph of F vs extension passes through the origin if Hooke's law holds. If the intercept is not at zero, what physical situation might explain it?

- Step 1: A positive y-intercept (extension at F = 0) might mean a measurement offset — for example, the ruler's zero wasn't aligned with the spring's natural length. - Step 2: A negative y-intercept could indicate the spring was pre-tensioned at "zero force" (e.g. the spring was being held compressed first) or that a small initial load was applied before measurements started. - Step 3: Either way, the slope ($$k$$) is still correct; only the offset is contaminated. Card 98

Searle's apparatus uses two wires (a "control" wire and a "test" wire). What's the role of the control wire? (3 steps)

- Step 1: Both wires hang from the same support, in the same room, so they experience identical temperature changes and any sagging of the support. - Step 2: The extension measured (via the vernier scale and spirit level) is the *difference* in length between the test wire and the control wire. Common-mode effects — temperature-driven expansion of either wire, or the support flexing — cancel out. - Step 3: What remains is only the extra extension caused by the load on the test wire. So the control wire isolates the effect of the load from environmental noise. This is a classic "differential measurement" technique — measure two similar things and subtract.
Card 99

Why doesn't the engineer just use the *highest observed temperature* to set the pre-strain (so the rail is stress-free at the hottest day)?

- Step 1: If the rail is unstressed at the hottest day, then on a cool day it contracts. Since it's fixed at both ends, contraction means tensile stress — and the colder it gets, the higher the tension. - Step 2: At the coldest expected temperature, tensile stress could exceed the breaking stress, snapping the rail. - Step 3: The engineer balances two failure modes: buckling from compression (too little pre-strain) versus snapping from tension (too much pre-strain). The pre-strain is chosen to keep stress within safe limits across the *full temperature range*, not optimised for the hot extreme alone.
Card 100

Two materials have the same Young modulus but very different breaking stresses. What does this mean physically?

- Step 1: Young modulus only describes behaviour in the *elastic* region — the initial slope of stress–strain. - Step 2: Two materials can start out equally stiff but diverge dramatically later: one might yield and stretch plastically before breaking, while the other snaps abruptly at a much lower stress. - Step 3: So $$E$$ tells you "how hard to stretch a tiny bit" but not "when does it break". Designers care about both: $$E$$ controls deflection under normal loads; breaking stress controls failure under extreme loads. Steel and aluminium have similar $$E$$/density ratios but very different breaking stresses and ductilities.
Card 101

Why does the spring constant of a single spring tell you nothing definite about the spring constants of two of them in any combination, unless you know whether they're series or parallel?

- Step 1: For two identical springs of constant $$k$$ in series, the effective stiffness is $$k/2$$ — half as stiff. - Step 2: For the same two in parallel, the effective stiffness is $$2k$$ — twice as stiff. - Step 3: That's a factor-of-4 difference between the two configurations, both made of the same components. Without specifying the arrangement, you can't predict the system's behaviour. This mirrors electrical circuits exactly: identical resistors give different totals in series vs parallel.
Card 102

Why do scientists specify *temperature and pressure* when quoting the density of a gas, but not usually for a solid or liquid?

- Step 1: Gases are highly compressible: raising the pressure squeezes molecules closer, increasing mass per unit volume. Heating expands them, decreasing density. - Step 2: Solids and liquids barely change volume with temperature or pressure — their molecules are already in contact, so density is essentially constant under lab conditions. - Step 3: For a gas, quoting "density = 1.2 kg/m³" is meaningless without conditions; this is why a balloon's air can be denser than free atmospheric air (it's compressed inside).
Card 103

Why is stress *not* the same physical thing as pressure, even though both have units of pascals?

- Step 1: Both are defined as force per unit area, so they share units. But pressure is associated with fluids pushing on a surface from outside — it's isotropic (acts equally in all directions) and is a *contact* force quantity. - Step 2: Stress refers to the *internal* state of a solid — how much force per area is being transmitted through a cross-section of the material itself. Stress can be tensile (pulling apart), compressive (pushing together), or shear (sliding along), and is generally a tensor (direction-dependent). - Step 3: A pressurised gas exerts pressure on a container wall; the container wall material has internal stresses arising from that pressure. They're related but distinct concepts: one describes the agent (fluid), one describes the response (solid). Same units, different physical role.

The elastic strain energy stored in a spring obeying Hooke's law can be written as either $$\frac{1}{2}Fx$$ or $$\frac{1}{2}kx^2$$. Show that these are equivalent, and explain why the factor of $$\frac{1}{2}$$ appears. (3 steps)

- Step 1: The factor of $$\frac{1}{2}$$ appears because the force increases from 0 to $$F$$ as the spring extends. The average force during this process is $$F/2$$, not $$F$$.
