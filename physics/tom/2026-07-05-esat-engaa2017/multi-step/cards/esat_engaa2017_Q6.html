Q6 · Energy Conservation — Useful and Wasted Energy

Question

An electric motor pulls a broken-down car slowly up a ramp onto a breakdown truck. Mass $m = 1200$ kg, vertical height $h = 1.0$ m. The total input energy to the motor is $28$ kJ and it is $75\%$ efficient. Energy is lost to the surroundings from the motor and other causes. ($g = 10$ N/kg.)

What is the total energy lost to the surroundings?

A. 7.0 kJ   B. 9.0 kJ   C. 12 kJ   D. 16 kJ   E. 21 kJ   F. 28 kJ   G. 33 kJ

[1 mark]

Solution 3 steps

STEP 1/3

Strategy. The useful output is the gravitational potential energy gained by the car. Use $E_{\text{GPE}} = mgh$ directly.

Formula (GPE)

$$E_{\text{useful}} = mgh = 1200 \times 10 \times 1.0 = 12000 \text{ J} = 12 \text{ kJ}$$
Key idea. The useful energy in any lifting/raising problem is exactly $mgh$. Only the vertical height counts — distance along the ramp does not (unless it is the vertical component). Use $g = 10$ N/kg as the paper specifies.
$E_{\text{useful}} = mgh = 12$ kJ.
STEP 2/3

Strategy. Apply conservation of energy: total input = useful + wasted. The wasted energy is simply the difference.

Formula (energy conservation)

$$E_{\text{in}} = E_{\text{useful}} + E_{\text{wasted}} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad E_{\text{wasted}} = E_{\text{in}} - E_{\text{useful}}$$
The efficiency shortcut Tom drilled. For a "how much energy is wasted?" question you often do not need the efficiency at all. If you can find the useful energy directly (it is $mgh$) and you are told the total input, then $E_{\text{wasted}} = E_{\text{input}} - E_{\text{useful}}$ in one line. All that matters is useful vs total.
$E_{\text{wasted}} = E_{\text{in}} - E_{\text{useful}}$
STEP 3/3

Strategy. Substitute: total input $= 28$ kJ, useful $= 12$ kJ. The loss is their difference.

Substitute and evaluate

$$E_{\text{wasted}} = 28 - 12 = 16 \text{ kJ}$$
Phuc's mistake (board). Phuc tried to route the whole thing through the $75\%$ efficiency figure: computing $0.75 \times 28 = 21$ kJ as the motor's useful output and $0.25 \times 28 = 7$ kJ as the motor's internal loss, then trying to reconcile that $21$ kJ against the $12$ kJ actually lifted. Tom's correction: for "total energy lost to the surroundings" you never need the $75\%$ at all. The only two numbers that matter are the useful energy ($mgh = 12$ kJ) and the total input ($28$ kJ); the loss is their difference, $16$ kJ.

Sense check. The board confirms the two-stage breakdown is self-consistent: motor loss $7$ kJ, then $21 - 12 = 9$ kJ lost after the motor, and $7 + 9 = 16$ kJ — but that longer route is exactly the "unnecessary detail about the motor" Tom told you to skip.

$\boxed{E_{\text{wasted}} = 16 \text{ kJ}}$ — Answer D
