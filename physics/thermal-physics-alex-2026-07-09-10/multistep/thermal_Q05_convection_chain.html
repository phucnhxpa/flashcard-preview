05. Convection: the ordered particle chain

Convection · fluidsAlex lessons · 9–10 July 2026Progressive worked solution
Exact question / setup
“Dyed-water loop: what happens when one corner is heated?” Explain the full convection current, including what happens in the heated region and the cooler return region. State whether a change of state is required.
The water remains liquid; the dye traces the bulk fluid motion. Suggested total: 6 marks.
9 July board: heated fluid gains energy, moves faster, spreads out, becomes less dense and rises; cooler denser fluid replaces it.
Heated side · ordered chain[3 marks]
STEP 1/2[2]

Strategy

Give the particle sequence in the required order.

Full working

Particles gain energy → move faster → spread farther apart → the heated region becomes less dense.

Sense check: Particles do not get bigger; their spacing increases.

STEP 2/2[1]

Strategy

Link lower density to bulk motion.

Full working

The less-dense heated liquid rises, carrying internal energy with it.

Sense check: Convection is bulk motion of a fluid, not vibration-passing through a fixed lattice.

Cooler side and completion[3 marks]
STEP 1/2[2]

Strategy

Describe the return path.

Full working

Cooler liquid particles move more slowly and are closer together, so that region is denser and sinks to replace the rising liquid. The continuous circulation is a convection current.

Sense check: Mass is conserved: rising fluid must be replaced.

STEP 2/2[1]

Strategy

Rule out an unnecessary state change.

Full working

The water remains liquid throughout; boiling or condensation is not required. The dye simply makes the circulating flow visible.

Sense check: A fluid can rise or sink because density changes while staying in the same state.

Physics-tightening note: This closed-loop rise-and-return flow is convection. In atmospheric examples, local buoyant rising is convection, whereas long-distance horizontal transport by wind is advection; do not label continent-scale wind transport as convection alone.
Final answer
Heating → faster particles → greater spacing → lower density → rise; cooler denser liquid sinks to replace it, forming a convection current. No state change is required.
Source-backed trap and prevention rule

Phuc said the water might “become gas” and, in the air example, suggested cooling air might become liquid. Alex’s correction was that both fluids remain in the same state; density change is sufficient.
