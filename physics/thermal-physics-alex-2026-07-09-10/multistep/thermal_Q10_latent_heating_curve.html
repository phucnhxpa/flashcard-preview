10. Latent heat and the complete heating curve

Latent heat · graph · state changeAlex lessons · 9–10 July 2026Progressive worked solution
Exact question / setup
“Sketch and explain the temperature–energy heating curve for ice heated to steam, naming which equation governs each part.” Include ice warming, melting, liquid-water warming, boiling and steam warming. Explain why temperature is constant on each plateau.
Assume a fixed mass and interpret horizontal length as energy supplied (or time at constant power). Suggested total: 8 marks.
Complete heating curve: three sloping single-state regions and two constant-temperature phase-change plateaus.
Sloping regions[3 marks]
STEP 1/2[2]

Strategy

Identify the three one-state heating stages.

Full working

Ice warming, liquid water warming and steam warming are sloping sections: temperature rises while the substance remains in one state.

Sense check: A complete “ice to steam” curve continues beyond the boiling plateau with a post-boiling steam-warming slope.

STEP 2/2[1]

Strategy

Attach the SHC equation.

Full working

\[\Delta E=mc\Delta\theta\] applies on each sloping section, using the appropriate specific heat capacity for solid, liquid or gas.

Sense check: A nonzero gradient means added energy raises average kinetic energy and temperature.

Plateaus and latent heat[5 marks]
STEP 1/3[2]

Strategy

Name both phase changes and equations.

Full working

Melting: \(\Delta E=L_f\Delta m\). Boiling: \(\Delta E=L_v\Delta m\). Both are horizontal plateaus.

Sense check: Use \(L\), not \(c\), during a state change.

STEP 2/3[2]

Strategy

Explain constant temperature microscopically.

Full working

Added energy increases molecular potential energy by loosening/overcoming intermolecular attractions rather than increasing average kinetic energy, so the temperature remains constant until the change is complete.

Sense check: Temperature tracks average random kinetic energy, not total internal energy alone.

STEP 3/3[1]

Strategy

Compare plateau sizes under stated conditions.

Full working

For equal mass and an energy axis (or constant heater power), water has \(L_v=2.26\times10^6\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}\) and \(L_f=3.34\times10^5\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}\), so the boiling plateau is about \(6.77\) times longer.

Sense check: The comparison needs equal mass and the same energy-per-horizontal-distance or constant power.

Final answer
Slopes: \(\Delta E=mc\Delta\theta\). Plateaus: \(\Delta E=L\Delta m\), with constant temperature because energy raises molecular potential energy. Include the final steam-warming slope.
Source-backed trap and prevention rule

A prior failure audit caught an earlier heating-curve figure that stopped at the boiling plateau while its text also named gas warming. The corrected guide and the diagram here now include the post-boiling steam-warming slope. The plateau-length comparison requires equal mass and an energy axis, or time at constant power.
