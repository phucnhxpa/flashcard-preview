01. Fixed-volume gas: pressure rises

Gas pressure · particle modelAlex lessons · 9–10 July 2026Progressive worked solution
Exact question / setup
“A sealed rigid container of gas is heated from \(20\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\) to \(80\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\). Explain, in particle terms, what happens to the pressure.”
Rigid means the gas volume and wall area cannot change. Suggested total: 4 marks.
Particle model: the same fixed walls receive more frequent and harder impacts.
9 July board: “fixed volume of gas” with faster particles, frequent wall collisions, force up and pressure up.
Explanation · 4 linked marking points[4 marks]
STEP 1/4[1]

Strategy

Start with the effect of heating on molecular motion.

Full working

Heating increases the particles’ average random kinetic energy, so the gas particles move faster.

Sense check: A gas moves randomly; “vibrate more” is the solid-lattice phrase.

STEP 2/4[1]

Strategy

Connect faster motion to collision rate.

Full working

The faster particles reach the container walls more often, so collisions with the walls are more frequent.

Sense check: The number of particles is unchanged; the collision frequency changes.

STEP 3/4[1]

Strategy

Connect molecular impacts to force.

Full working

Each collision produces a larger rate of change of momentum, and the total force exerted on the walls increases.

Sense check: Greater wall force is the bridge from particles to pressure.

STEP 4/4[1]

Strategy

Use the pressure definition and the fixed geometry.

Full working

Formula first: \[p=\frac{F}{A}.\] The wall area \(A\) is fixed while \(F\) increases, so \(p\) increases.

Sense check: The rigid container cannot expand; pressure, not volume, changes.

Final answer
The gas pressure increases: heating → faster particles → more frequent and harder wall collisions → greater wall force → \(p=F/A\) rises.
Source-backed trap and prevention rule

Phuc first said the gas “expands” and used “more friction into the boxes.” The exact transcript supports those corrections: the volume is fixed, and the standard phrase is “more frequent collisions with the walls.” A secondary video analysis also records “mass ÷ volume” for pressure; because that detail is absent from the exact transcript, treat the attribution as lower confidence. The physics correction remains \(p=F/A\), whereas \(m/V\) is density.
