# Thermal Physics — Jargon and Formula Recall Pack

**One mental model:** temperature differences drive energy transfer from hotter to cooler regions. At particle level, internal energy, collisions and intermolecular separation explain heating, state changes and gas pressure; at macroscopic level, the correct equation, SI units, fixed conditions and graph shape turn that model into exam marks.

**Source lock:** the audited Alex lesson guide is primary. Edexcel Year 2 Chapters 10–12, the local SaveMyExams thermal/ideal-gas packs and IAL specification points 125–132 supply examiner wording, practical detail and full IAL gas-law context. `Phuc trap` appears only where the lesson evidence directly records Phuc's response; all other warnings are `Common trap`.

## 1. Jargon glossary — foundations, radiation and transfer mechanisms

| Term | Student-friendly meaning | Exact physics meaning | Symbols/units | What it is NOT | Lesson example | Phuc/common trap |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Energy store** | A place or form in which energy is accounted for before and after a transfer. The thermal store is the one central to this chapter. | A bookkeeping category for energy possessed by a system: kinetic, gravitational potential, elastic, chemical, thermal/internal, nuclear or electromagnetic. Energy is conserved between stores. | Energy $E$ or change $\Delta E$, joule (J). | Not a route by which energy travels. | Alex separated **thermal** on the stores list from **heating** on the pathways list. | **Common trap —** mixing stores and transfer pathways in one list. |
| **Energy-transfer pathway** | The way energy moves from one store or system to another. | Energy is transferred mechanically, electrically, by heating, or by radiation/waves; the total is conserved. | Energy in J; rate of transfer is power in W. | Not an energy store contained by an object. | Heating transferred energy into the aluminium block; electrical work supplied that energy. | **Common trap —** saying an object “stores heating.” |
| **Internal energy** | The microscopic energy inside a body because its particles move randomly and interact through bonds. | The sum of the random kinetic energies and intermolecular/interatomic potential energies of all particles in the system. For an ideal gas, intermolecular potential energy is neglected, so internal energy is kinetic. | Usually $U$ or $\Delta U$, J. | Not the kinetic energy of the whole object moving through a room; not a substance called “heat.” | A stationary hot block has large internal energy even though its centre of mass is not moving. | **Common trap —** defining internal energy as kinetic energy only for every state of matter. |
| **Heating** | Energy moving because one body or region is hotter than another. | Random energy transfer from higher temperature to lower temperature through conduction, convection or radiation, requiring a temperature difference. | Energy transferred $\Delta E$ in J; rate in W. | Not energy stored inside a body and not automatically the same as temperature. | Energy moved from Phuc's warm hand to a cooler metal object until their temperatures approached equality. | **Common trap —** saying a body “contains heat”; it contains internal energy. |
| **Temperature** | A measure of how energetic the particles' random motion is on average. | A thermodynamic state quantity directly related to mean random molecular kinetic energy; thermodynamic temperature is measured from absolute zero. | $T$ in K; Celsius temperature often $\theta$ in $^\circ$C. | Not total internal energy: a small hot sample can have less total energy than a large cooler sample. | Heating a fixed-volume gas increased particle speed and therefore pressure. | **Common trap —** using Celsius in gas laws or confusing temperature with energy. |
| **Thermal equilibrium** | The state in which two bodies have no net thermal-energy transfer between them; if they are in thermal contact, they have the same temperature. | Thermal equilibrium requires no net transfer of thermal energy between the bodies and equality of temperature in thermal contact. Microscopic two-way exchange may continue. | Equal temperature; net thermal transfer rate between bodies $=0$ W. | Not defined merely by “infrared emitted $=$ infrared absorbed”: that is only the radiative special case and ignores conduction, convection, work or internal power. | Both cooling-can curves eventually level toward room temperature when each can reaches the surroundings' temperature. | **Common trap —** replacing the general no-net-transfer/equal-temperature definition with a radiation-only rate balance. |
| **Absolute zero** | The lowest possible thermodynamic temperature. | $0\,\mathrm{K}=-273.15\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$, the limiting temperature at which particles have their minimum kinetic energy; Kelvin temperatures cannot be negative. | $0\,\mathrm{K}$. | Not ordinary “cold space”; space is about $2.7\,\mathrm{K}$ and a vacuum is not a perfect heat sink. | The lesson used $0\,\mathrm{K}$ as the limiting case in which infrared emission would cease. | **Common trap —** writing $0\,\mathrm{K}=0\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$. |
| **Kelvin scale** | The absolute temperature scale used whenever a law compares temperature by ratios. | A thermodynamic scale whose zero is absolute zero and whose interval has the same size as one Celsius degree: $\Delta T/\mathrm{K}=\Delta\theta/^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$. | $T$ in K, never “degrees K.” | Not a different-sized degree from Celsius and not optional in gas-law ratios. | $15\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}=288.15\,\mathrm{K}$ in the ideal-gas example. | **Common trap —** adding 273.15 to a temperature **change**; only absolute readings require conversion. |
| **Infrared radiation** | Invisible electromagnetic radiation just beyond the red end of visible light that can transfer energy. | Ordinary thermal infrared is a broad spectrum produced by thermal motion of charged particles, including molecular rotation/vibration and lattice vibration; it needs no material medium and crosses a vacuum. | Wavelength longer and frequency lower than visible red; transfer rate in W. | Not all invisible radiation, not alpha/beta/gamma nuclear radiation, and not explained by orbital-electron drops between discrete atomic energy levels. | Herschel's thermometer just beyond red showed the largest temperature rise. | **Phuc trap —** “totally forgotten” and treated invisible light too broadly; infrared is one specific EM band just beyond red. |
| **Emission** | Giving infrared energy out from a surface. | The release of electromagnetic radiation by a body; all bodies above absolute zero emit, and hotter bodies emit at a greater rate. | Emitted power in W; intensity in $\mathrm{W\,m^{-2}}$. | Not reflection of incoming radiation and not evidence that the emitter is getting hotter. | The matt-black can cooled faster because it emitted infrared more strongly. | **Phuc trap —** predicted the stronger emitter's temperature would become higher; emitting energy cools it. |
| **Absorption** | Taking incoming radiation into a material so its internal energy can increase. | Transfer of incident electromagnetic energy to the absorber rather than transmission or reflection. Good absorbers are good emitters. | Absorbed energy in J; absorbed power in W. | Not reflection, which returns radiation without taking it into the material. | Earth absorbed incoming solar radiation before re-emitting at a longer wavelength. | **Common trap —** calling a shiny reflector a good absorber. |
| **Reflection** | Bouncing incoming radiation away from a surface. | Redirection of incident electromagnetic radiation without its energy being absorbed by that surface; shiny/light surfaces are effective infrared reflectors. | Reflectivity is dimensionless; reflected power in W. | Not emission from energy already inside the object. | Foil behind a radiator reflected infrared back into the room. | **Phuc trap —** thought the shiny can should “give more”; reflection needs incoming radiation, while the hot-water can comparison tested emission. |
| **Black/matt surface** | A dark, dull surface that exchanges infrared with its surroundings very effectively. | A relatively high-emissivity surface: a good emitter and, by Kirchhoff-style reciprocity, a good absorber; a poor reflector. | Emissivity $\varepsilon$ is dimensionless if used. | Not automatically hotter than a shiny surface at the same temperature. | The black can appeared brighter on the infrared camera and cooled more steeply. | **Common trap —** interpreting a brighter thermogram as proof of a higher actual temperature when emissivity differs. |
| **White/shiny surface** | A light or polished surface that reflects much of the infrared reaching it. | A relatively low-emissivity, low-absorptivity surface and good reflector of thermal radiation. | Reflectivity/emissivity dimensionless if used. | Not the best emitter simply because it looks bright in visible light. | A reflective layer facing the Sun reduces incoming solar absorption on spacecraft. | **Phuc trap —** linked “best reflector” with “gives off more”; reflector and emitter rankings are opposite here. |
| **Electromagnetic radiation** | Energy carried by oscillating electric and magnetic fields, including visible light and infrared. | Radiation made of photons/waves spanning radio to gamma; infrared is EM radiation and can cross a vacuum. | Frequency $f$ in Hz; wavelength $\lambda$ in m; photon energy $E=hf$ if needed elsewhere. | Not synonymous with nuclear radiation; gamma is both EM and nuclear in origin, but infrared does not come from nuclear decay. | Solar/terrestrial radiation crosses space without conduction or convection. | **Phuc trap —** called infrared “a weak type of nuclear radiation”; Alex corrected it to electromagnetic radiation. |
| **Radiation balance / greenhouse effect** | Earth warms until outgoing energy again balances incoming energy; greenhouse gases make that balance occur at a higher temperature. | Shorter-wavelength solar radiation is absorbed by Earth; Earth re-emits longer-wavelength infrared, which greenhouse gases absorb and re-radiate, reducing escape rate until a higher-temperature equilibrium is reached. | Rates in W or $\mathrm{W\,m^{-2}}$. | Not a solid “blanket” that creates energy and not nuclear radiation. | The board chain was Sun radiation $\to$ Earth absorption $\to$ longer-wavelength re-emission $\to$ atmospheric trapping $\to$ warming. | **Common trap —** omitting the longer-wavelength re-emission step or saying CO$_2$ “knocks radiation out.” |
| **Conduction** | Energy passing through neighbouring particles without the material flowing as a whole. | Thermal energy transfer down a temperature gradient: solids pass lattice vibrations to neighbours; gases transfer energy through molecular collisions and random molecular motion; metals also use mobile delocalised electrons. | Transfer rate in W; no single lesson formula required. | Not bulk movement of matter and not radiation across empty space. | The rod used lattice vibrations/free electrons; Sahara air touching hot ground was heated through molecular collisions. | **Common trap —** using the solid-lattice phrase “passing vibrations” for gas conduction; say molecular collisions/random motion in a gas. |
| **Lattice vibration** | Atoms in a solid jiggle about fixed equilibrium positions and pass energy to neighbours. | Random vibrational motion of atoms/ions in a lattice; higher-temperature regions have larger mean vibrational kinetic energy, which couples to adjacent particles. | Microscopic energy in J. | Not atoms travelling from the hot end to the cold end. | Alex's linked-arms analogy represented vibrations passed along the solid. | **Common trap —** saying the particles themselves move through the solid during conduction. |
| **Free / delocalised electron** | A mobile electron in a metal that can carry energy rapidly through the lattice. | A conduction electron not bound to one atom; it gains energy near the hot region and transfers it by collisions through the metal. | Electron charge $e$ in C if quantified; speed in $\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$. | Not the ordered electron drift of an electrical current, although the same charge carriers are involved. | Metals conducted better than non-metals because free electrons supplemented vibration transfer. | **Common trap —** forgetting free electrons in a “why are metals good thermal conductors?” explanation. |
| **Thermal conductor / conductivity** | A material that transfers internal energy quickly when a temperature difference exists. | High thermal conduction rate for a given geometry and temperature difference; metals are usually good conductors because of delocalised electrons. | Thermal conductivity would be $\mathrm{W\,m^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$, though no conductivity equation was required in the lesson. | Not necessarily a material at a higher temperature. | Both ice-demo blocks were about $21\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$; block 2 melted ice faster because it conducted energy faster. | **Phuc trap —** said the faster-melting block “must be hot”; the temperatures were the same. |
| **Thermal insulator** | A material or structure that slows thermal energy transfer. | A system with low conduction and/or suppressed convection/radiation, often using few particles, trapped gas pockets, vacuum gaps or reflective surfaces. A solid's conductivity depends on lattice structure, free electrons, scattering, density and defects. | Performance may be a transfer rate in W; no single lesson formula. | Not a perfect stopper of all three mechanisms; “weaker bonds $=$ better insulator” is not a safe universal rule. | Loft fibre trapped air; foil addressed infrared; a draught excluder blocked moving air. | **Phuc trap —** suggested very strong bonds make the best insulator; use the dependable sparse/no-particle logic for gas/vacuum and never rank solids from bond strength alone. |
| **Convection** | Energy carried because a liquid or gas itself moves. | Thermal transfer by bulk motion of a fluid: heating causes expansion, lower density and rise; cooler denser fluid sinks, forming circulation. | Fluid speed in $\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ if measured; no core lesson formula. | Not conduction between fixed particles and not possible in a vacuum. | Heated dyed water rose and circulated around the loop. | **Phuc trap —** considered water boiling/becoming gas and “dropping”; the water stayed liquid and the heated region rose. |
| **Fluid** | Any substance able to flow. | A liquid or gas that can undergo bulk motion and therefore convection. | Density $\rho$ in $\mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}}$. | Not only a liquid; gases are fluids too. | Both sea-breeze air and dyed water formed convection currents. | **Common trap —** saying convection happens in gases but not liquids, or in solids. |
| **Density** | How much mass is packed into each unit volume. | Mass per unit volume, $\rho=m/V$. A heated fluid region usually expands, so the same mass occupies more volume and density falls. | $\rho$ in $\mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}}$. | Not pressure, and particles do not need to become physically larger. | Heated air particles spread out; the region became less dense and rose. | **Phuc trap —** offered mass divided by volume when asked for pressure; that is density, not pressure. |
| **Convection current** | A circulating loop in which warm, less-dense fluid rises and cooler, denser fluid sinks. | Sustained bulk circulation driven by density differences in a gravitational field, carrying internal energy. | Flow speed in $\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ if quantified. | Not a change of state and not an electrical current. | Smoke descended one opening of the candle box while hot air rose over the flame. | **Phuc trap —** thought cooling gas must become liquid to sink; a gas can become denser while remaining gas. |
| **Advection** | Horizontal transport of warm or cool air by the wind over a large distance. | Bulk transport of a fluid property such as internal energy by the fluid's large-scale horizontal motion; unlike local buoyant convection, it does not mean the fluid rises because it became less dense. | Wind speed in $\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ if quantified. | Not the local convection that first lifts heated Sahara air and not conduction between neighbouring molecules. | Local convection raised hot Sahara air; prevailing winds advected the warm air mass toward Paris. | **Common trap —** calling the whole Sahara-to-Paris horizontal journey “convection”; the long-range transport is advection. |
| **Trapped air** | Small pockets of air that neither conduct well nor circulate freely. | Gas with low particle number density provides poor conduction; dividing it into small immobilised pockets suppresses convection currents. | No single formula; compare transfer rates. | Not the same as a large open air gap, which can permit convection. | Loft insulation, cavity fibres and curtains trapped air; double glazing trapped air or argon. | **Common trap —** giving only one role; trapped air reduces both conduction and convection. |
| **Vacuum** | A region with essentially no particles. | Absence of a material medium, preventing conduction and convection; electromagnetic radiation can still pass and carry energy. | Pressure ideally approaches $0\,\mathrm{Pa}$. | Not a perfect thermal insulator against radiation and not something that automatically cools a machine. | Space allowed radiative transfer from the Sun but no conduction through the intervening vacuum. | **Phuc trap —** suggested convection could occur if “two airs” differed; in a vacuum there is no air. |
| **Draught** | A flow of air through a gap that carries warm or cool air with it. | Bulk replacement/motion of a gas, so the relevant transfer mechanism is convection. | Air speed in $\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ if measured. | Not conduction through a stationary solid. | A draught excluder under a door stopped moving air. | **Phuc trap —** answered “conduction”; moving/replacing air is convection. |
| **Radiative cooling in space** | Rejecting waste energy from a spacecraft by emitting infrared to empty space. | In vacuum, heat must be conducted internally to a large radiator with **high thermal-infrared emissivity**; surfaces exposed to the Sun need **low solar absorptivity** so little incoming solar power is absorbed. | Radiated power in W; area in $\mathrm{m^2}$; emissivity/absorptivity dimensionless. | Not convection into “cold space”; one generic “black” or “shiny” label is not enough because thermal-IR emission and solar absorption concern different wavelength bands. | Alex's design used an effective emitting radiator plus a reflective/white sunward finish. | **Common trap —** specify both independent requirements: high thermal-IR emissivity and low solar absorptivity. |
| **Thermogram / infrared camera** | An image built from detected infrared intensity and converted into apparent temperature colours. | A calibrated detector maps received thermal radiation; readings depend on temperature and surface emissivity. | Temperature in K or $^\circ$C after calibration. | Not direct visible-light photography and not emissivity-independent proof of temperature. | The black and shiny cans at the same temperature looked different because their emission differed. | **Common trap —** assuming identical colour scales guarantee identical emissivity. |

## 2. Jargon glossary — thermal calculations, state change, gases and practicals

| Term | Student-friendly meaning | Exact physics meaning | Symbols/units | What it is NOT | Lesson example | Phuc/common trap |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Specific heat capacity (SHC)** | The energy needed per kilogram for each kelvin of temperature rise. A larger value means more energy for the same mass and rise. | Energy required to raise the temperature of $1\,\mathrm{kg}$ of a substance by $1\,\mathrm{K}$ without a change of state; $c=\Delta E/(m\Delta\theta)$. | $c$ in $\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$. | Not total heat capacity of an object and not latent heat during a phase change. | Water's reference $c=4200\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$ explains why it heats/cools slowly. | **Common trap —** using final temperature instead of temperature change, or leaving mass in grams. |
| **“Specific” quantity** | A quantity quoted per unit mass, so different sample sizes can be compared fairly. | In SHC and specific latent heat, “specific” means per kilogram. | Per kg gives factors of $\mathrm{kg^{-1}}$. | Not per area or per volume. | Alex derived $\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$ directly from $\Delta E=mc\Delta\theta$. | **Phuc trap —** treated “specific” as possibly per area/volume; in these definitions it is per kilogram. |
| **Heat capacity** | Energy needed to raise one particular object's temperature by one kelvin. | For an object of mass $m$ and material SHC $c$, heat capacity $C=mc$. | $C$ in $\mathrm{J\,K^{-1}}$. | Not specific heat capacity, which is per kilogram and is a material property. | A heavier aluminium block has greater heat capacity even though its $c$ is unchanged. | **Common trap —** giving $\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$ as the unit of whole-object heat capacity. |
| **Temperature change** | Final temperature minus initial temperature; only the size of the interval matters in SHC. | $\Delta\theta=\theta_f-\theta_i$ or $\Delta T=T_f-T_i$; one-kelvin and one-Celsius-degree intervals are equal. | K or $^\circ$C for a change. | Not the final temperature and not a ratio-scale conversion. | Cooling the avocado by $2\,\mathrm{K}$ gives $|\Delta\theta|=2\,\mathrm{K}$ for the energy magnitude. | **Common trap —** adding 273.15 to a change or allowing a negative sign to make an energy magnitude negative. |
| **Electrical SHC method** | Supply measured electrical energy to a known mass and divide by its temperature rise. | Equate electrical work $IV\Delta t$ to the block's internal-energy increase $mc\Delta\theta$, ideally after minimising/correcting losses. | $I$ A, $V$ V, $t$ s, $m$ kg, $c$ $\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$. | Not exact if heater, thermometer and surroundings absorb energy. | The aluminium block used oil for good thermal contact, lagging, a heater and thermometer. | **Common trap —** counting all supplied energy as block energy; this usually makes measured $c$ too large. |
| **Thermal contact** | Good physical coupling that lets energy pass efficiently between components. | Close contact that reduces interfacial thermal resistance and helps the heater/thermometer match the block temperature. | No core formula here. | Not thermal equilibrium by itself; equilibrium takes time. | Oil in the aluminium block's holes improved contact with heater and thermometer. | **Common trap —** leaving air gaps around sensors. |
| **Specific latent heat** | Energy per kilogram needed to change state while temperature stays constant. | Energy required to change the phase of $1\,\mathrm{kg}$ without temperature change; $L=\Delta E/\Delta m$. | $L$ in $\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$. | Not energy for raising temperature within one state. | During a heating-curve plateau, supplied energy increased potential rather than kinetic energy. | **Common trap —** omitting “without change in temperature” from the definition. |
| **Specific latent heat of fusion** | Energy per kilogram for solid–liquid change at the melting/freezing temperature. | $L_f$ is energy per unit mass for melting; the same magnitude is released on freezing by conservation of energy. | $L_f$ in $\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$; water about $3.34\times10^5$. | Not vaporisation and not a temperature-rise quantity. | Ice at $0\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ absorbed energy while melting but stayed at $0\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$. | **Common trap —** applying $mc\Delta\theta$ across the melting plateau. |
| **Specific latent heat of vaporisation** | Energy per kilogram for liquid–gas change at boiling/condensing temperature. | $L_v$ is energy per unit mass for vaporisation; the same magnitude is released in condensation. | $L_v$ in $\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$; water about $2.26\times10^6$. | Not fusion; it is usually much larger because molecules separate much further. | For the same mass and constant power, the boiling plateau is much longer than the melting plateau because $L_v\gg L_f$. | **Common trap —** comparing plateau lengths without equal mass and constant-power conditions. |
| **Change of state / phase change** | A substance changes between solid, liquid and gas while supplied/removed energy changes molecular arrangement. | During a first-order phase change at fixed pressure, energy changes intermolecular potential energy while mean kinetic energy and temperature remain constant. | Energy in J; mass changing state $\Delta m$ in kg. | Not necessarily a temperature change; sublimation is also a phase change though the IAL equations emphasise fusion/vaporisation. | Ice melting and water boiling formed flat sections of the heating curve. | **Common trap —** saying particles stop moving on a plateau. |
| **Heating curve** | A graph showing temperature as energy is supplied: slopes while solid, liquid and gas warm, with plateaus during phase changes. | Sloped sections obey $\Delta E=mc\Delta\theta$; flat sections obey $\Delta E=L\Delta m$. After boiling is complete, the gas/steam has its own rising warming section. | Vertical $T$ or $\theta$; horizontal energy, or time only when power is constant. | Not one straight line and not a curve that ends at the boiling plateau. | Ice warming $\to$ melting $\to$ liquid warming $\to$ boiling $\to$ gas/steam warming. | **Common trap —** omit gas warming, or compare plateau lengths without the same mass and constant power. |
| **Cooling curve** | A graph of temperature falling toward surroundings, usually steep initially and flattening later. | The magnitude of gradient represents cooling rate; it decreases as the temperature difference falls. A phase change creates a near-constant-temperature region. | Gradient in $\mathrm{K\,s^{-1}}$. | Not proof that the faster emitter becomes hotter. | The black can's curve was steeper than the silver can's but both approached room temperature. | **Phuc trap —** inferred strong emission should raise temperature; steeper downward gradient means faster energy loss. |
| **Pressure** | Force spread over an area; gas pressure comes from molecular impacts on container walls. | Normal force per unit area, $p=F/A$. In kinetic theory, elastic wall collisions transfer momentum and create a mean force. | $p$ in Pa, where $1\,\mathrm{Pa}=1\,\mathrm{N\,m^{-2}}$. | Not density $m/V$ and not merely collision count without momentum change. | Faster heated particles hit a rigid container's walls more often and harder, increasing pressure. | **Phuc trap —** gave mass divided by volume and said “friction into the boxes”; use force/area and wall collisions. |
| **Fixed mass of gas** | The same sealed set of particles is followed while pressure, volume or temperature changes. | Particle number $N$ and amount $n$ remain constant; gas-law comparisons require this condition unless the equation explicitly handles changing amount. | $N$ molecules or $n$ mol constant. | Not necessarily fixed volume or fixed pressure. | Boyle, Charles and pressure laws each assume a fixed mass but hold a different state variable constant. | **Common trap —** stating a gas law without “fixed mass.” |
| **Ideal gas** | A simplified gas whose particles are tiny, random-moving and interact only through elastic collisions. | Many point molecules in continuous random motion; negligible molecular volume and intermolecular forces except during collisions; collisions are perfectly elastic and brief. It obeys $pV=NkT$. | $p$ Pa, $V$ $\mathrm{m^3}$, $T$ K. | Not every real gas under all conditions; real gases approximate it best at low density/high temperature. | The IAL gas-law and kinetic-theory equations model the fixed-volume lesson gas as ideal. | **Common trap —** using ideal-gas equations without Kelvin or SI volume. |
| **Boyle's law** | Compressing a fixed amount of gas at constant temperature raises pressure so $pV$ stays constant. | For a fixed mass at constant thermodynamic temperature, $p\propto1/V$ and $p_1V_1=p_2V_2$. | $p$ Pa and $V$ $\mathrm{m^3}$ in SI, though consistent paired units cancel in a ratio. | Not valid during uncontrolled heating and not a direct $p\propto V$ relation. | The required practical plots $p$ against $1/V$ to obtain a straight line through the origin. | **Common trap —** forgetting to wait for thermal equilibrium after compression. |
| **Isothermal** | A process or curve at constant temperature. | A $p$–$V$ relation for a fixed mass of gas at one fixed thermodynamic temperature; an ideal-gas isotherm is a rectangular hyperbola. | $T$ in K, constant. | Not “insulated”; rapid compression can change temperature even if the final points later equilibrate. | Boyle's-law data were taken slowly so the gas returned to room temperature. | **Common trap —** treating “constant temperature” as automatically guaranteed. |
| **Charles's law** | At constant pressure, a warmer fixed mass of gas occupies a larger volume in direct proportion to Kelvin temperature. | For fixed mass and constant $p$, $V\propto T$ and $V_1/T_1=V_2/T_2$. | $V$ $\mathrm{m^3}$, $T$ K. | Not a Celsius proportionality and not applicable in a rigid container. | A piston can move so heating at constant pressure increases gas volume. | **Common trap —** using $^{\circ}$C in the ratio. |
| **Pressure law (Gay-Lussac)** | In a rigid container, heating raises pressure in direct proportion to Kelvin temperature. | For fixed mass and constant $V$, $p\propto T$ and $p_1/T_1=p_2/T_2$. | $p$ Pa, $T$ K. | Not expansion: volume is fixed. | Alex's opening problem: faster, more frequent, harder wall collisions raise $p$. | **Phuc trap —** said the fixed-volume gas “expands”; the pressure rises instead. |
| **Equation of state** | One equation connecting the macroscopic condition of an ideal gas. | $pV=NkT=nRT$, relating pressure, volume, absolute temperature and amount of gas at equilibrium. | $p$ Pa, $V$ $\mathrm{m^3}$, $T$ K, $N$ dimensionless, $n$ mol. | Not a transfer-rate equation and not for Celsius temperature. | The $8.3\times10^3\,\mathrm{cm^3}$ cylinder example used $pV=NkT$ to find molecule number. | **Common trap —** mixing $N$ molecules with $n$ moles. |
| **Mole** | A counting unit containing Avogadro's number of particles. | Amount of substance $n$ where one mole contains $N_A\approx6.02\times10^{23}$ entities; $N=nN_A$. | $n$ in mol. | Not the mass of one molecule or the total number $N$. | $pV=nRT$ and $pV=NkT$ are equivalent because $R=N_Ak$. | **Common trap —** substituting moles for $N$ in $pV=NkT$. |
| **Avogadro constant** | The number of particles in one mole. | $N_A\approx6.02\times10^{23}\,\mathrm{mol^{-1}}$, linking microscopic particle number to macroscopic amount. | $N_A$ in $\mathrm{mol^{-1}}$. | Not Boltzmann constant and not dimensionless when written as a constant. | $N=nN_A$ converts moles to molecules. | **Common trap —** confusing $N_A$ with $N$, the sample's actual molecule count. |
| **Boltzmann constant** | The conversion factor linking one particle's thermal energy scale to Kelvin temperature. | $k=R/N_A=1.38\times10^{-23}\,\mathrm{J\,K^{-1}}$; it appears in $pV=NkT$ and mean molecular KE. | $k$ in $\mathrm{J\,K^{-1}}$. | Not the molar gas constant $R=8.31\,\mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$. | The ideal-gas molecule calculation used the supplied $k$ value. | **Common trap —** using $R$ with molecule number or $k$ with moles. |
| **Kinetic theory** | A model that explains gas behaviour through many fast, randomly moving particles and their collisions. | Connects macroscopic $p,V,T$ to microscopic mass, momentum and mean-square speed under ideal-gas assumptions. | Uses $m$ kg per molecule, $\langle c^2\rangle$ $\mathrm{m^2\,s^{-2}}$. | Not a claim that every molecule has the same speed or that average velocity is non-zero. | Pressure rose because heated molecules transferred momentum to walls more frequently and strongly. | **Common trap —** saying pressure comes from “friction” rather than momentum-changing elastic collisions. |
| **Brownian motion** | Tiny suspended particles move in random jerks because unseen molecules strike them unevenly. | Observable random motion caused by fluctuating molecular bombardment, providing evidence that matter's molecules are in continuous random motion. | Displacement in m; time in s if measured. | Not the path of a single gas molecule and not self-propelled pollen. | Textbook evidence supports the kinetic theory assumptions used in the gas derivation. | **Common trap —** claiming molecules themselves are visible under the microscope. |
| **Mean square speed** | Square every molecular speed, then average those squared values. | $\langle c^2\rangle=(c_1^2+\cdots+c_N^2)/N$; it enters the pressure equation. | $\mathrm{m^2\,s^{-2}}$. | Not the square of the ordinary mean speed; these are close but not identical. | Speeds 310–350 $\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ give $\langle c^2\rangle=109100\,\mathrm{m^2\,s^{-2}}$. | **Common trap —** averaging first and then squaring. |
| **Root-mean-square (r.m.s.) speed** | The square root of the mean of all squared molecular speeds; a useful typical random speed. | $c_{\mathrm{rms}}=\sqrt{\langle c^2\rangle}$; for an ideal gas $c_{\mathrm{rms}}=\sqrt{3kT/m}$. | $\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$. | Not average velocity, which is zero for a stationary gas because directions cancel. | Oxygen at $297\,\mathrm{K}$ with molecular mass $5.34\times10^{-26}\,\mathrm{kg}$ has $c_{\mathrm{rms}}\approx480\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$. | **Common trap —** forgetting the square root or using molar mass where single-molecule mass is required. |
| **Mean molecular kinetic energy** | The average translational kinetic energy of one ideal-gas molecule. | $\frac12m\langle c^2\rangle=\frac32kT$; it depends only on absolute temperature, not molecular identity. | J per molecule. | Not the total KE of all $N$ molecules and not proportional to Celsius temperature. | At the same $T$, helium and oxygen have the same mean molecular KE but different r.m.s. speeds. | **Common trap —** concluding heavier molecules move faster at the same temperature. |
| **Thermistor** | A resistor whose resistance changes strongly with temperature, allowing it to be calibrated as a thermometer. | A temperature-dependent semiconductor resistance; the IAL practical records resistance against temperature while stirring and allowing equilibrium. | Resistance $R$ in $\Omega$; temperature in $^\circ$C or K. | Not a linear sensor by assumption and not self-calibrating. | Core Practical 12 plots resistance against temperature using a reference thermometer. | **Common trap —** heating too rapidly, not stirring, or letting measurement current self-heat the thermistor. |
| **Control experiment in latent-heat practical** | A second unheated ice funnel measures melting caused by the surroundings, so it can be subtracted. | With equal initial ice and time, corrected heater-melted mass is $\Delta m=m_A-m_B$. | Mass in kg after conversion. | Not an optional duplicate; it corrects environmental energy input. | The two-funnel method compares heated and unheated meltwater masses before $L=\Delta E/\Delta m$. | **Common trap —** using all melted mass as if the heater caused it. |
| **Systematic error** | A consistent bias pushing repeated results in the same direction. | An error source that shifts measurements or calculated values consistently, such as un-zeroed balance or unaccounted heat loss. | Depends on measured quantity. | Not reduced just by repeating and averaging. | Counting energy lost to surroundings as block energy makes calculated SHC systematically too large. | **Common trap —** calling every scatter source systematic. |
| **Random error** | Unpredictable variation causing repeated readings to scatter. | Measurement variation reduced by repeats, averaging, better resolution and controlled technique. | Often described by uncertainty in the measured unit. | Not a fixed calibration offset. | Repeating latent-heat mass measurements and averaging reduced random error. | **Common trap —** expecting repeats to remove a zero error. |

| **Celsius to kelvin** | $T/\mathrm{K}=\theta/^{\circ}\mathrm{C}+273.15$ | $T$ thermodynamic temperature; $\theta$ Celsius temperature. | K and $^\circ$C. | Every gas-law or molecular-KE calculation using absolute temperature. | $\theta/^{\circ}\mathrm{C}=T/\mathrm{K}-273.15$. | **Common trap —** Kelvin has no degree sign; $15^\circ$C is not $15$ K. | $15^\circ$C $=288.15$ K; $300$ K $=26.85^\circ$C. |

## 8. Final mechanism and calculation checklist

- **Worded mechanism:** name the mechanism, state the microscopic/bulk carrier, give the causal chain in the correct order, and finish with energy moving hot $\to$ cold.
- **Real scenario:** do not force one mechanism when several operate. The Sahara ground received radiation, heated adjacent air by molecular-collision conduction, local convection lifted it, and prevailing winds advected the warm air horizontally to Paris.
- **Calculation layout:** write the target symbol, write the equation, convert to SI, substitute with units, calculate, round sensibly, and check physical direction.
- **State change:** split the path into sloped $mc\Delta\theta$ stages and flat $L\Delta m$ stages; add energies.
- **Gas law:** write **fixed mass**, identify the constant variable, convert every absolute temperature to kelvin, and distinguish gauge from total pressure.
- **Space:** vacuum removes conduction and convection, not radiation; specify a radiator with high thermal-IR emissivity and a solar-facing finish with low solar absorptivity.
- **Evidence labels:** avocado Q3 is raw $3009.302325\ldots\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$, reported as $3.01\times10^3\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$ (3 s.f., classroom convention); Q4 is $\Delta T\approx4.64\,\mathrm{K}$.
