Card 1: Three-graph dictionary (gradients and areas)

What does the gradient/area tell you for s-t, v-t, and a-t graphs?

Answer

s-t: gradient = velocity (v=ds/dt), no standard area | v-t: gradient = acceleration (a=dv/dt), area = signed displacement (Δs) | a-t: gradient = jerk (not required), area = change in velocity (Δv)

Card 2: Q001 - Distance = magnitude of displacement

When is distance equal to the magnitude of displacement?

Answer

Only when the object travels directly along a straight line from start to finish without reversing direction. (Tutor concept check, Q001)

Card 3: Q002 - Out and back

An object moves away from start then returns to start. What are displacement and distance?

Answer

Displacement = 0 (final position = initial), Distance > 0 (twice the outward path length). Trap: displacement is not "how much ground was covered".

Card 4: Q003 - a-t graph with initial velocity

For a-t graph, what does area give? With u = -5 m/s, what's needed for reversal?

Answer

Area gives change in velocity (not velocity itself): v(t) = u + ∫a dt. From u = -5 m/s, cumulative signed area must reach +5 m/s to reach v=0, and exceed it to become positive. Positive acceleration alone is not enough. Trap: negative a does not mean slowing if v is also negative.

Card 5: Q004 - Total distance in 4s

v-t graph: v rises linearly 0 to 20 m/s in 0-2s, then constant 20 m/s to 4s. Total distance?

Answer

C, 60 m. Triangle = ½×2×20 = 20m, Rectangle = 2×20 = 40m. Total = 60m. (Edexcel 6PH01/01 June 2009 Q8)

Card 6: Q005 - Bouncing golf ball apex

With falling velocity positive convention, where is the first maximum height after bounce?

Answer

C. Zero crossings: A = during floor contact (deformation), B = leaving floor moving upward, C = first post-bounce apex where v=0. Trap: don't pick v=0 blindly. (OCR H157/01 May 2016 Q10)

Card 7: Q006 - Maximum velocity on s-t graph

How to find maximum velocity on s-t graph? Why s/t fails?

Answer

Draw/imagine tangents at A, B, C, D; compare slopes. Steep positive tangent = max v. Answer B. s/t from origin gives average over interval, not instantaneous velocity. Steep negative tangent is large negative v, not max positive v. (OCR H156/01 May 2018 Q1)

Card 8: Q007 - Terminal velocity

Object dropped, v-t rises and approaches constant by ~30s. What's correct?

Answer

C, acceleration = 9.81 m/s² at t=0. Decreasing gradient means a decreases. At release v=0 so drag=0, initial a≈g. As v rises drag rises; at terminal velocity, drag=weight, ΣF=0, a=0, v=constant non-zero. (OCR A topic compilation item 8)

Card 9: Q008 - Accelerometer at constant negative velocity

At Y on s-t graph, slope is approximately constant negative. Water surface in accelerometer?

Answer

A, level water. Instrument responds to acceleration not velocity. Negative but constant velocity = zero acceleration. Two-step rule: get v from s-t gradient, then check if v is changing. (Cambridge Pre-U 9792/01 May/June 2012 Q7)

Card 10: Q009 - Parachutist

Parachutist "falls freely" then opens parachute. Sign sequence of acceleration (down positive)?

Answer

B. +g during free fall, large negative on opening (parachute force opposes motion), then approaches 0 as new lower terminal speed is approached. Lesson answer: B.

Card 11: Q010 - Piecewise s-t conversion

Convert piecewise s-t (rising straight, plateau, downward parabola crossing s=0) to v-t and a-t?

Answer

- Rising straight: v=horizontal positive, a=0 - Plateau: v=0, a=0 - Concave-down parabola: v starts at 0 and decreases linearly to negative, a=constant negative - DERIVED caveat: ideal sharp join from positive s-t slope to flat plateau makes velocity jump instantly to zero; at that instant, ordinary a is not finite (impulse/undefined caveat). (Tutor deck Q1)

Card 12: Q011 - Bouncing ball

Convert bouncing ball v-t (sawtooth) to s-t and a-t? [TRANSCRIPT-SUPPORTED/DERIVED]

Answer

Choose floor s=0. Since v(0)=0, s-t begins with horizontal tangent. Constant g means each free-flight s-t arc is parabolic. Diminishing v-t areas produce lower apexes. a-t = constant -g between impacts; ideal instantaneous velocity jumps = impulse spikes (not ordinary finite a). DERIVED: post-42:30 visuals not claimed. (Tutor deck Q2)

Card 13: Signs - speeding up vs slowing

When does object speed up vs slow down (signs of v and a)?

Answer

Speeds up when v and a have same sign. Slows down when signs are opposite. Trap: positive a does not automatically mean speeding up.

Card 14: Units

What are the units and unit checks for displacement, velocity, acceleration, time?

Answer

s in m; t in s; v in m s⁻¹; a in m s⁻². Gradient units = vertical/horizontal. Area units = multiplication. Gradient of s-t = m/s = m s⁻¹ (velocity). Gradient of v-t = (m s⁻¹)/s = m s⁻² (acceleration). Area under v-t = (m s⁻¹)(s) = m. Area under a-t = (m s⁻²)(s) = m s⁻¹.

Card 15: Distance from v-t

How to find total distance from v-t graph when velocity changes sign?

Answer

Calculate magnitude of every positive and negative area, then add them. Net displacement = signed areas (cancellation allowed). Distance = absolute areas (no cancellation).

Card 16: Discontinuity/Impulse

How should an instantaneous velocity jump (e.g., ideal bounce) be represented on a-t graph? [DERIVED]

Answer

Ideal instantaneous velocity jump = impulse, not ordinary finite acceleration. Real contact time is finite (very large but finite a). In ideal graph, draw a vertical jump representing impulse/distribution, not a normal finite a value across zero time. The "plus infinity" from a=Δv/Δt as Δt→0 is a model idealisation.

Card 17: v=0 physical meaning

What are different physical meanings of v=0?

Answer

v=0 can mean: release (start of motion), a turning point (max displacement), or zero crossing during contact (e.g., during floor deformation in bounce). Don't treat all v=0 as the same.

Card 18: a=0 physical meaning

What are different physical meanings of a=0?

Answer

a=0 can mean: constant non-zero velocity, at rest, or terminal velocity (ΣF=0, drag=weight). Not "all forces are zero".

Card 19: Why constant acceleration gives parabola in s-t

Why does constant acceleration give a parabola in s-t graph?

Answer

s = s₀ + ut + ½at² (t² makes displacement quadratic). Differentiate once: v = u + at (linear v-t). Differentiate again: a = constant (horizontal a-t). If u=0, parabola starts with horizontal tangent. Trap: don't call every smooth curve a parabola; need constant a.

Card 20: Conversion algorithm

What's the reliable segment-by-segment algorithm for graph conversion?

Answer

1) Sign convention. 2) Mark breakpoints. 3) Read slope first (s-t slope=v; v-t slope=a). 4) Read area second (v-t area=Δs; a-t area=Δv). 5) Use boundary conditions (v=0 means horizontal s-t tangent). 6) Check signs. 7) Flag ideal jumps. 8) Check dimensions and physical story.
