Q003 · a–t area and reversal

Source status: Recording-derived interpretation task; completed with prompting. Drawn scale is not specified.

Recall prompt

How can an a–t graph be used to decide whether a body starting with u = −5 m s⁻¹ reverses?

Full method4 steps

STEP 1/4
STEP 1/4Name the area correctly

The area under an acceleration–time graph is change in velocity, not velocity itself: $Δv=∫a·dt$.

Why this works: The graph supplies how much velocity changes, so the starting value must be carried in.
STEP 2/4
STEP 2/4Insert the initial velocity

Use $v(t)=u+∫₀ᵗa·dt$. Here $u=-5$ m s⁻¹, so begin at −5 m s⁻¹ and add the cumulative signed area from the a–t graph.

Check: Area units are (m s⁻²)·s = m s⁻¹, the same as velocity.
STEP 3/4
STEP 3/4Interpret positive acceleration

While $v$ is negative, the body moves left under the tutor's convention. Positive acceleration points right, so it first makes velocity less negative: the body slows while still moving left.

Why this works: Speed decreases while velocity and acceleration have opposite signs.
STEP 4/4
STEP 4/4Apply the reversal threshold

The cumulative positive area must reach +5 m s⁻¹ to bring $v$ from −5 m s⁻¹ to zero. Only if it grows beyond +5 m s⁻¹ does $v$ become positive and the body turn right.

Check: At exactly +5 m s⁻¹ of area the body is instantaneously at rest; crossing the threshold establishes reversal.
Recall answer: Use $v(t)=u+∫a·dt$. From $u=-5$ m s⁻¹, reversal requires cumulative positive signed acceleration area to reach +5 m s⁻¹ and then cross it so that $v$ becomes positive.
Diagnostic / redo: If you said “positive a means reversal”, add u and track the sign of v.
