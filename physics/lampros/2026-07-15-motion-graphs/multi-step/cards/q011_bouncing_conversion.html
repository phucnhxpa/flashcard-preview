Recall promptA v–t graph has repeated straight downward flight slopes and ideal upward jumps with diminishing amplitudes. What follows for s–t and a–t?
Concept sketch: derived from the graph method on this card.

Reveal the method

STEP 1/3Integrate flight segments
A straight v–t flight slope means constant acceleration, so each displacement-time flight arc is parabolic.
STEP 2/3Use gradient and area constraints
v=0 at a flight apex gives a horizontal s–t tangent. Signed v–t areas set height changes, so diminishing rebound areas imply lower apexes.
STEP 3/3Label the impact idealisation
The upward v jump over ideal Δt→0 is impulse-like. “Infinite acceleration” is shorthand, not a finite measurable value; the simplified a–t graph omits the spikes.
Recall answer: s–t has parabolic flight arcs with lower successive rebound apexes; a–t is constant gravitational acceleration between impacts, with idealised positive impulse-like spikes only if impacts are represented.
Diagnostic / redo: If you draw straight s–t flight lines, recall that their gradients would imply constant non-zero v, not gravitational acceleration.
