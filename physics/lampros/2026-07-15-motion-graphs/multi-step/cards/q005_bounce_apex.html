Q005 · Bounce graph: apex versus contact

Source status: Source-controlled OCR question; 1 mark. Secure answer, not a claim that a mark scheme is present.

Original question

A golf ball is dropped from rest onto a hard floor. The graph shows how the velocity of the ball varies with time as it bounces, from the time of release. At which point does the ball reach its maximum height after the first bounce?

Full method4 steps

STEP 1/4
STEP 1/4Fix the sign convention

In the displayed graph, falling velocity is positive and rising velocity is negative. Keep that convention throughout; the physical sequence, not sign alone, identifies each labelled point.

Why this works: A zero crossing can be floor contact or a flight turning point, so its context matters.
STEP 2/4
STEP 2/4Trace the bounce

The ball is released at $v=0$, falls and speeds up, then meets the floor. During contact it deforms, slows through $v=0$, reverses direction, and leaves with upward velocity.

Check: With rising negative, the post-contact velocity lies below the axis and returns toward zero during upward flight.
STEP 3/4
STEP 3/4Correct the tempting zero

The student knew ‘maximum height means $v=0$’ and chose C, but admitted the choice was partly lucky and initially misread A. The tutor corrected this: A is the zero-velocity crossing during finite floor contact, while the deformed ball is reversing, not a post-bounce apex.

Why this works: Use the turning-point rule at the zero reached after upward flight, not at every zero on the graph.
STEP 4/4
STEP 4/4Select the flight apex

Follow the graph after the ball leaves the floor. The next point where rising velocity returns to zero is C, so C is the first maximum height after the bounce.

Check: A realistic contact reversal is sloped because it takes finite time; an ideal vertical jump changes that contact detail, not the answer C.
Recall answer: C. Point A is the zero-velocity crossing during floor contact, not the maximum height after the bounce.
Diagnostic / redo: If every v=0 point became “maximum height”, state the physical event either side of the point.
