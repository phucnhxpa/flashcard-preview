1. What Edexcel expects

The specification requires you to draw and interpret displacement-time, velocity-time and acceleration-time graphs, and to obtain physical quantities from their slopes and areas, including non-uniform acceleration. The lesson directly trained those skills.

Interpret

  • State what the sign and gradient mean physically.
  • Distinguish velocity from speed and displacement from distance.
  • Use the initial condition: an area gives a change, not necessarily the quantity itself.

Convert

  • Convert one graph segment at a time.
  • Match gradients at boundaries where the motion is continuous.
  • Show turning points where velocity is zero.
  • State idealisations at discontinuities.
Exam habit: read both axes and units before touching the graph. Underline decisive modelling words such as from rest, falls freely, constant velocity and uniform acceleration.

3. Signs, units and areas

Signs

Velocity says which way the object moves. Acceleration says which way velocity changes. The object speeds up when velocity and acceleration have the same sign, and slows when their signs are opposite.

Units

s in m; t in s; v in m s-1; a in m s-2. Gradient units divide vertical by horizontal. Area units multiply.

Zero is contextual

v = 0 can mean release, a turning point, or a zero crossing during contact. a = 0 can mean constant non-zero velocity, rest, or terminal velocity.

OperationUnit checkResult
Gradient of s-tm / sm s-1 (velocity)

Distance from a v-t graph: calculate the magnitude of every positive and negative area, then add. Net displacement instead adds signed areas, so cancellation is allowed.

Solved

Q002 - RECORDING-DERIVED CONCEPT CHECK

Move away, then return to the start

Solved
Your attempt: displacement is zero.
Tutor confirmation: the final position equals the initial position, so the displacement vector is zero; the distance is twice the outward path length.
Answer: displacement = 0, but distance > 0.

Trap: displacement is not "how much ground was covered". It depends only on initial and final position.

Q003 - ACCELERATION-TIME INTERPRETATION

What can an a-t graph tell you when the initial velocity changes?

Completed with prompting

Given: acceleration begins positive, decreases through zero and later becomes negative. Compare u = 0 with u = −5 m s-1. No numerical scale was certified.

Your attempt: you asked for repetition and later reasoned that, in the tutor's illustrated negative-velocity case, the object could slow, stop and turn right.
Tutor correction: area under a-t gives change in velocity, not velocity itself. Positive acceleration while v is negative makes the velocity less negative, so the object slows. Reversal occurs only when the accumulated positive area is large enough to bring v to zero and then positive.
v(t) = u + ∫0t a(τ) dτ
Condition for reversal from u = −5 m s-1: the cumulative signed acceleration area must reach +5 m s-1 to reach v = 0, and exceed it to become positive. Positive acceleration alone is not enough.
Sign trap: negative acceleration does not automatically mean slowing down. If v is also negative, the object speeds up in the negative direction.

Evidence limitation: the drawn curve had no certified scale, so this guide states the reversal condition rather than claiming that every such curve must cause reversal.

6. Q007-Q009: drag, terminal velocity and sensors

Q007 - OCR A TOPIC COMPILATION, ITEM 8 - 1 MARK IN COMPILATION

Object approaching terminal velocity

PromptedSitting unresolved

Given: an object is dropped from rest; its v-t curve rises from zero and approaches a constant by about 30 s. Which statement is correct?

Your attempt: you considered constant acceleration, then mixed up zero resultant force with zero drag. In the vacuum extension you briefly suggested the velocity might remain zero.
Tutor correction: the decreasing gradient means acceleration decreases. At release, v = 0 so drag is zero and the initial acceleration is approximately g. As speed rises, drag rises; at terminal velocity, drag equals weight, resultant force is zero, acceleration is zero, and velocity is constant but non-zero.
At terminal velocity: ΣF = 0   ⇒   drag = weight   and   a = 0
Answer: C, acceleration is 9.81 m s-2 at t = 0.

Vacuum extension: near Earth with no air resistance, weight still acts. Acceleration remains constant at g and the v-t graph is a straight line (for a fixed sign convention).

Source status: exact content matched to an OCR A topic compilation, "Motion with non-uniform acceleration", item 8. The original paper code/session was not proven from the cropped lesson slide.

7. Q010: piecewise graph conversion

Q010 - TUTOR-AUTHORED/COMPILED DECK, QUESTION 1

Convert a piecewise displacement-time graph to v-t and a-t

PromptedMarks/source not shown

Visible prompt: describe the motion, then sketch the velocity-time and acceleration-time graphs. The given s-t graph has a straight positive-slope segment, a horizontal plateau, then a downward parabolic segment crossing s = 0.

Your attempt: correctly identified positive motion and rest, but initially thought a curved s-t section could not produce a straight v-t section. You later mixed "constant velocity" with "velocity increasing at a constant rate".
Tutor method: translate segment by segment. Straight positive s-t means constant positive v. Flat s-t means v = 0. For the final parabolic interval, v starts at 0 and then decreases linearly into negative values; a is constant negative.

8. Q011: bouncing-ball graph conversion

Q011 - SAME TUTOR DECK, QUESTION 2

Convert a bouncing-ball v-t graph to s-t and a-t

PromptedQualified evidence

Visible pre-boundary prompt: the v-t graph consists of repeated straight downward-sloping flight segments separated by ideal instantaneous upward jumps; the amplitudes decrease. Sketch s-t and a-t.

Your attempt: chose a positive release height and the floor as zero. You initially proposed a straight s-t descent, then chose a parabola by intuition but needed prompting to justify its initial horizontal tangent and the lower rebound peaks.
Tutor method: choose floor s = 0. Since v(0) = 0, the s-t graph begins with a horizontal tangent. Constant gravitational acceleration makes each free-flight s-t arc parabolic. Areas under v-t determine height changes, so diminishing areas produce lower rebound apexes. Parallel v-t flight slopes mean the same constant gravitational acceleration between impacts.
slope of s-t = v    |    area under v-t = Δs    |    slope of v-t = a
Secure result: s-t has repeated parabolic down/up arcs touching the floor, with successively lower apexes. a-t is constant gravitational acceleration between impacts; ideal instantaneous velocity jumps correspond to impulse-like acceleration spikes.
Idealisation: the tutor described the instantaneous-bounce spike as "plus infinity" from a = Δv/Δt as Δt tends to zero. In a real bounce, contact time is finite and acceleration is very large but finite. In an ideal graph, the jump is represented by an impulse/distribution, not an ordinary measurable infinite acceleration.

9. Parabolas and graph conversion

Derived expansion: this section generalises the lesson's conversion method. It is supporting teaching, not a claim about a particular hidden slide.

Why constant acceleration gives a parabola in s-t

s = s0 + ut + 1/2 at2    ⇒    v = u + at    ⇒    a = constant
  • The t2 term makes displacement quadratic in time, so the s-t graph is parabolic.
  • Differentiating once gives linear v-t.
  • Differentiating again gives horizontal a-t.
  • The initial s-t tangent is u. If u = 0, the parabola starts with a horizontal tangent.
Appearance trap: do not call every smooth curved s-t line a parabola. You may infer quadratic behaviour when constant acceleration is stated, established by the v-t gradient, or otherwise justified.

Reliable segment-by-segment conversion algorithm

  1. Write the sign convention. For vertical motion, decide whether upward or downward is positive.
  2. Mark breakpoints. Treat each straight/curved/flat interval separately.
  3. Read slope first. s-t slope gives v; v-t slope gives a.
  4. Read area second. v-t area sets displacement change; a-t area sets velocity change.
  5. Use boundary conditions. v = 0 means a horizontal s-t tangent; the graph itself should remain continuous unless position genuinely jumps, which physical motion normally forbids.
  6. Check signs. Below-axis v means displacement decreases; it does not make distance decrease.
  7. Flag ideal jumps. A vertical v-t jump means an impulsive acceleration, not a normal finite a-t level.
  8. Check dimensions and physical story. State moving direction, speeding/slowing, rest, contact or terminal motion.

10. Worked examples

Derived examples: these are new practice expansions using the lesson rules. They are not additional recovered lesson questions.

Example A - signed area versus distance

A v-t graph is +4 m s-1 for 3 s, then −2 m s-1 for 5 s.

  1. Positive displacement = 4 × 3 = +12 m.
  2. Negative displacement = (−2) × 5 = −10 m.
  3. Net displacement = +12 − 10 = +2 m.
  4. Total distance = 12 + 10 = 22 m.

Check: displacement may cancel; distance cannot.

Example B - acceleration area and reversal

An object starts with u = −6 m s-1. It experiences a = +2 m s-2 for 4 s.

Δv = area = at = (+2)(4) = +8 m s-1;   v = u + Δv = −6 + 8 = +2 m s-1

It reaches v = 0 after 3 s, then reverses. The positive acceleration did not instantly mean positive motion; it first reduced the negative velocity.

Example C - tangent and curvature

An s-t graph is increasing but becoming less steep.

  • v is positive because the slope is positive.
  • Speed is decreasing because the slope magnitude decreases.
  • a is negative because velocity is decreasing.

Example D - terminal velocity statement

At terminal velocity, write: "The upward resistive force equals the downward weight, so resultant force and acceleration are zero. The object continues at constant non-zero velocity." Do not write "all forces are zero".

Graph checklist
  • Axes and units?
  • Initial value?
  • Gradient sign/magnitude?
  • Area signed or absolute?
  • Continuous curve or ideal jump?
  • Physical story?
Language checklist
  • "Constant velocity" = horizontal v-t.
  • "Velocity increases at a constant rate" = straight sloping v-t.
  • "At rest" means v = 0, not necessarily s = 0.
  • "Zero net force" does not mean zero individual forces.
