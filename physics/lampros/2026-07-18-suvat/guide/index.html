2. One-page exam summary

The four SUVAT equations

v = u + at
s = ½(u + v)t
s = ut + ½at²
v² = u² + 2as

Condition: acceleration must be constant over the modeled interval.

If you remember nothing else:
  1. Choose one positive direction and write signs before selecting an equation.
  2. “Between two times” usually means s(t₂) − s(t₁), not “put Δt into SUVAT”.
  3. The nth second is the interval n−1 → n.
  4. Direct proportionality requires a straight line through the origin.
  5. For a vertical projection with up positive, a = −g throughout ascent and descent.
  6. For “time above a height”, solve for both crossing times and subtract larger minus smaller—but only after checking that two relevant crossings exist.

3. SUVAT foundations and equation choice

3.1 The conditions come before the formula

SUVAT is not a collection of universal motion equations. It models one-dimensional motion with constant acceleration. For free fall near Earth's surface, use g = 9.81 m s−2 when the lesson or question does so and neglect air resistance unless told otherwise.

  • Displacement is signed. It depends on your chosen positive direction.
  • Distance is scalar. In a motion that reverses, total distance is not automatically the magnitude of one net displacement.
  • Time is an elapsed duration. If you restart the clock at an interval boundary, then the interval's own initial velocity must also be reset correctly.

3.2 The five-line setup

  1. Sketch the motion and mark the interval or endpoints.
  2. Declare the positive direction: e.g. “up is positive”.
  3. Write s, u, v, a, t with units and signs.
  4. Choose the equation that excludes the variable you do not know and do not need.
  5. Substitute, solve, then check direction, magnitude, units and precision.
Textbook-backed habit: write down the known SUVAT quantities first, then select the equation. This prevents equation-hunting by appearance.

4. Q01 — proportional graph and free fall

Official control OCR A Level Physics A H556/01, June 2017, Q4, 1 mark. Official answer: A. The recording begins during the explanation, so the very first setup is not preserved.

Prompt

A ball is dropped from rest. Air resistance is negligible. Its speed is v after falling distance h. Select the correct graph.

Lesson attempt: the learner chose A from the qualitative idea that greater fallen distance corresponds to greater speed.
Lampros's correction / strengthening: “increasing” is not enough to prove the graph's exact form. A straight line alone also does not prove direct proportionality; it must pass through the origin.

Controlled derivation

Use the no-time equation because the graph connects speed and distance:

v² = u² + 2as
u = 0, a = g, s = h
v² = 2gh

Therefore v² ∝ h. A graph of v² against h is a straight line through the origin with gradient 2g. Equivalently, v = √(2gh), so a graph of v against h is a square-root curve, not a straight line.

Do not confuse the straight-line graph of v² against h with the curved graph of v against h.
Exam trap: a line such as y = kx + c with c ≠ 0 is linear but not directly proportional. A negative-gradient line through the origin, e.g. y = −kx, can still be a proportional relationship.

5. Q02 — displacement in a time interval

Official control OCR H556/01, June 2017, Q12, 1 mark. Official answer: B, 0.25 m.

Prompt

An object is released from rest at t = 0. Air resistance is negligible. Find the distance traveled between t = 0.20 s and t = 0.30 s.

Lesson attempt: the learner first focused on Δt = 0.10 s and considered v = Δs/Δt.
Lampros's correction: velocity is changing, so a constant-velocity distance formula over 0.10 s is invalid. If you restart the clock at 0.20 s, you would need the non-zero velocity at that instant.

Reliable endpoint method

  1. Use one clock from release and calculate the cumulative displacement at each absolute time.
  2. With downward positive, u = 0, a = +9.81 m s−2, and s(t) = ½gt².
  3. Subtract the earlier cumulative displacement from the later one.
Δs = s(0.30) − s(0.20)
= ½(9.81)[(0.30)² − (0.20)²]
= 0.2452500 m ≈ 0.25 m

Independent calculation The full arithmetic above is reconstructed from the controlled values; the lesson emphasized the method more than every calculator line.

General interval formula from rest

Δs = ½a(t₂² − t₁²) = ½a(t₂ − t₁)(t₂ + t₁)

This explains why equal-duration intervals get longer under positive acceleration: the factor t₂ + t₁ grows as time passes.

6. Q03 — “second second” and “fourth second”

Official control OCR Physics A H156/01 Sample Assessment Materials, Q9, 1 mark. Official answer: A, 28 m.

Translation rule: the nth second means the one-second interval from t = n−1 to t = n. So the second second is 1→2 s; the fourth second is 3→4 s.
Prompt

A car accelerates uniformly from rest and travels 12 m during the second second. How far does it travel during the fourth second?

Lesson attempt: the learner was initially unsure whether “second second” meant 0→2 s. Later, after finding s(4)=64 m, that cumulative value was momentarily treated as the answer.
Lampros's correction: label the intervals on a timeline. The answer must be the change from 3 s to 4 s, not total displacement from 0 s to 4 s.

Step 1: use the second-second condition to find acceleration

Since the car starts from rest, s(t)=½at².

s(2) − s(1) = 12
½a(2²) − ½a(1²) = 12
2a − ½a = 12
a = 8 m s−2

Step 2: calculate the fourth-second displacement

s(4)=½(8)(4²)=64 m
s(3)=½(8)(3²)=36 m
s(4)−s(3)=28 m

Reusable nth-second formula

Let each interval have duration τ = 1 s. For constant acceleration, displacement during the nth interval is:

Δsn = s(nτ) − s[(n−1)τ]
= uτ + ½a(2n−1)τ²
with τ = 1 s

When u and a are entered as SI numerical values and each interval is exactly one second, this is conventionally shortened to sn = u + ½a(2n−1). For this car, u=0. The second-second distance is ½a(3)=12, giving a=8; the fourth-second distance is ½(8)(7)=28 m. Use the shortcut as a check, not as a substitute for understanding the interval or tracking units.

7. Q04 — time above a height

Tutor / worksheet source Exact source unknown; marks unknown. The lesson presented a general derivation rather than an independently written learner solution.

General problem

A ball is projected vertically upward from s=0 with initial speed u. How long is it above a height h₀?

Choose upward as positive. Gravity remains downward for the entire flight:

s = ut + ½at², with a = −g
h₀ = ut − ½gt²
½gt² − ut + h₀ = 0

The two roots are the upward and downward crossings:

t₁ = [u − √(u²−2gh₀)]/g
t₂ = [u + √(u²−2gh₀)]/g
time above h₀ = t₂ − t₁ = 2√(u²−2gh₀)/g

Independent extension The compact root-difference formula is derived from the lesson's quadratic method; it is not presented as a verbatim board result.

Conditions: when do two relevant roots exist?

  • The maximum height is hmax = u²/(2g).
  • If 0 < h₀ < hmax, there are two positive crossing times after launch, so subtract them.
  • If h₀ = hmax, the two roots coincide and the ball is above that exact height for zero duration.
  • If h₀ > hmax, the discriminant is negative: the ball never reaches that height.
  • If the requested height is below the launch level, one algebraic root may occur before t=0. Restrict the answer to the physical time domain stated in the question rather than subtracting roots blindly.
Do not split ascent and descent unless it helps. One equation with a=−g already describes both. At the top, velocity is zero only instantaneously; acceleration is still −g.

8. Q05 — vertical projection at 10 m s−1

Lesson attempt: the learner correctly identified v=0 at maximum height and selected the equation without time.

0 = 10² + 2(−9.81)s

Q13(a) — braking distance scaling [3 marks]

Controlled task statement

A road train has braking distance 70 m at 15 m s−1. Assuming the same braking force at all speeds, show that its braking distance at 25 m s−1 is about 190 m.

Method

The same braking force on the same vehicle mass gives the same magnitude of deceleration. During braking, use v²=u²+2as with final velocity v=0:

0 = u² + 2as ⇒ s = −u²/(2a)
For fixed deceleration magnitude, s ∝ u².
s₂ = 70(25/15)² = 194.444444… m
s₂ ≈ 194 m ≈ 190 m

Official result 194 m; 194 m or 195 m accepted in the official control.

Mark-winning structure

  1. Relate braking distance to speed squared using constant deceleration.
  2. Use the ratio (25/15)².
  3. Obtain about 190 m with units.

Q13(b) — length of a road train [3 marks]

Controlled task statement

A car accelerates uniformly at 3.7 m s−2 while passing a stationary road train. Its initial speed is 30 m s−1, and it reaches the 130 km h−1 speed limit as it passes the front of the cab. Calculate the road train's length.

Method

Convert the speed limit before using SUVAT:

v = 130/3.6 = 36.111111… m s−1
v² = u² + 2as
s = [36.111111…² − 30²]/[2(3.7)]
s = 54.596262… m ≈ 55 m

Official result 55 m; the controlled worked value is 54.6 m.

Mark-winning structure

  1. Convert km h−1 to m s−1.
  2. Select the no-time equation and rearrange for s.
  3. Substitute unrounded speed, then round the final length to 55 m.

10. Formula, unit, sign and rounding traps

Trap 1: using Δt alone

If acceleration acts before the interval begins, the velocity at the interval start is not the original u. Prefer s(t₂)−s(t₁) unless you correctly reset the full state.

Trap 2: cumulative vs interval

s(4) is displacement from 0 to 4 s. The fourth-second displacement is s(4)−s(3).

Trap 3: “straight line”

Linear does not mean directly proportional. Direct proportion requires zero intercept.

Trap 4: top of flight

At maximum height, v=0 but a=−g, not zero.

Trap 5: double negative

Use s=ut+½at² with a=−g, or s=ut−½gt². Never write a minus sign and then substitute negative g.

Trap 6: root order

Labels do not matter. Sort roots physically and calculate later minus earlier.

Trap 7: km h−1

Divide by 3.6 to convert to m s−1. Do this before squaring.

Trap 8: early rounding

Keep calculator precision through intermediate steps. Show the unrounded result, then the exam-appropriate rounded answer.

Dimensional checks

ExpressionUnit check
ut(m s−1)(s) = m
½at²(m s−2)(s²) = m
2as(m s−2)(m) = m² s−2, matching velocity squared
Δv/a(m s−1)/(m s−2) = s

11. Exam checklists and next actions

Before calculating

  • Is acceleration constant over the modeled interval?
  • Have I sketched the motion and marked the actual time interval?
  • Have I declared a positive direction?
  • Are all speeds in m s−1, distances in m and times in s?
  • Have I listed s, u, v, a, t with signs?

After calculating

  • Did I answer interval displacement rather than cumulative displacement?
  • If there are two roots, do they correspond to two physical crossings in the allowed time domain?
  • Is the larger crossing time minus the smaller one positive?
  • Does the direction/sign of the result make physical sense?
  • Did I keep full precision until the final line, then include units and suitable rounding?

12. Self-test

  1. What extra feature turns a straight-line graph into evidence of direct proportionality?
  2. A body starts from rest with constant acceleration 4.0 m s−2. Find its displacement between 2.0 s and 3.0 s.
  3. Translate “distance during the sixth second” into a time interval.
  4. An object starts from rest and moves 7.5 m in its third second. Find its acceleration.
  5. A ball is projected upward. What are its velocity and acceleration at maximum height if up is positive?
  6. For a launch speed u, state the condition on h₀ for two positive crossing times of height h₀.
  7. Explain why inserting only 0.10 s into s=ut+½at² can fail for the interval 0.20→0.30 s.
  8. Convert 108 km h−1 to m s−1.

Answers

1. It must pass through the origin, so the relation has the form y=kx.
2. ½(4.0)(3.0²−2.0²)=10 m.
3. t=5 s to t=6 s.
4. Third-second distance =½a(2×3−1)=2.5a=7.5, so a=3.0 m s−2.
5. v=0 instantaneously; a=−g≈−9.81 m s−2.
6. For launch from s=0, require 0<h₀<u²/(2g).
7. At 0.20 s the object already has non-zero velocity; restarting time without resetting u loses that state. Use endpoint positions or calculate the interval-start velocity.
8. 108/3.6=30 m s−1.
