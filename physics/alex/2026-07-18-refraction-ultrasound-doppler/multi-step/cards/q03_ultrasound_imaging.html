Q03 · Explain how ultrasound can form an image

Lesson-grounded recall prompt

Explain how a pulse of ultrasound can be used to form an image of structures inside the body.

Source: Loom recording, full video analysis and transcript. Student-attempt language is included only where recorded.

Tutor method and corrections3 steps

STEP 1/3Send and partly reflect

A transducer sends a short ultrasound pulse into the body. At each boundary between tissues, part of the wave reflects while the rest continues to deeper boundaries.

Why this method: Partial reflection is essential: total reflection would hide everything deeper. Alex linked this directly to the boundary ideas from reflection and refraction.
Check / transfer: Use “transducer sends”; the body or baby does not create the ultrasound.
STEP 2/3Detect and locate boundaries

The transducer detects the returning echoes. The machine measures the delay from transmission to reception. With known sound speed, a longer return time means a deeper boundary; the one-way depth follows $d=vt/2$.

Why this method: The recorded delay covers travel to the boundary and back, so the path is twice the depth.
Check / transfer: An earlier echo means a nearer boundary because the path is shorter, not because that echo travelled faster.
STEP 3/3Build the image

The echo strength and time are processed: time locates a boundary and echo amplitude can set brightness. Repeating pulses along neighbouring directions builds a cross-sectional image, such as a fetal scan.

Why this method: One trace gives depths along one line; repeated lines supply spatial coverage.
Check / transfer: Complete answer: pulse → partial reflections → timed echoes → depth/brightness → image.
