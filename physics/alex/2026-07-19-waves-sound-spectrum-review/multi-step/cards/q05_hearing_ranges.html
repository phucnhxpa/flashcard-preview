Q05 · Hearing ranges: test every statement

Source: Loom recording (full video analysis) + transcript.

Question

Hearing ranges: dog 70–45 000 Hz; bat 2000–110 000 Hz; elephant 10–12 000 Hz. Human range used in the lesson: 20–20 000 Hz.

Test: (1) the highest human pitch cannot be heard by a dog; (2) the lowest elephant pitch can be heard by a human; (3) 500 Hz can be heard by humans, dogs, bats and elephants; (4) elephants hear the shortest wavelength.

Full tutor method3 steps

STEP 1/3Check statements 1 and 2 against endpoints

Check statements 1 and 2 against endpoints

Statement 1 is false: $20{,}000$ Hz lies within the dog range $70$–$45{,}000$ Hz. Statement 2 is false: the elephant minimum is 10 Hz, below the human minimum of 20 Hz.

Method: translate each verbal claim into an inequality and compare with both endpoints. “Highest” and “lowest” usually point directly to table boundaries.
STEP 2/3Find the missed bat constraint

Find the missed bat constraint

Phuc initially ticked statement 3, reasoning that 500 Hz worked, but the revealed solution and discussion caught the bat row. The bat minimum is 2000 Hz, so 500 Hz is too low for a bat even though it lies within the human, dog and elephant ranges.

Alex acknowledged the missed detail: “Oh, bats... cannot be heard by bats.”

Universal-claim check: words such as “all” require every named range to pass; one failure makes the statement false.
STEP 3/3Link shortest wavelength to highest frequency

Link shortest wavelength to highest frequency

Phuc correctly said shortest wavelength means highest frequency. At a common wave speed, $\lambda=v/f$, so increasing $f$ decreases $\lambda$. The largest upper frequency belongs to bats ($110{,}000$ Hz), not elephants ($12{,}000$ Hz). Statement 4 is false.

Final answer: none of the four statements is correct.
