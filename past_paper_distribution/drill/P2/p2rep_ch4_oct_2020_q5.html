P2 (WMA12/01) · Q5 · Oct 2020

Arithmetic Sequences with Real-World Constraints

Question

Ben and Lina each have a savings account. They each start putting money into their account in January 2015. Ben puts £100 into his account in January 2015. He then puts an extra £5 each month so that he puts £105 in February 2015, £110 in March 2015, and so on, so that the monthly amounts form an arithmetic sequence.

(a) Find the amount Ben puts into his account in the 40th month. [2]

(b) Find the total amount Ben puts into his account in the first 60 months. [2]

Lina puts £600 into her account in January 2015. She then puts £10 less each month than the previous month so that she puts £590 in February 2015, £580 in March 2015, and so on, so that the monthly amounts form an arithmetic sequence.

(c) Given that Lina puts a total of £18,200 into her account in the first $n$ months, show that $$n^2 - 121n + 3640 = 0$$ [3]

(d) Solve the equation in part (c). [2]

(e) State, giving a reason, which of your solutions to part (d) is not sensible in this context. [2]

[11 marks]

Part (a) — Amount Ben saves in month 40 [2 marks] · 2 steps

Ben: $a = 100$, $d = 5$
STEP 1/2 M1

Formula: nth term of AP: $u_n = a + (n-1)d$

$$u_{40} = 100 + (40-1)(5)$$

$$u_{40} = 100 + 39 \times 5$$

Common error: Using $n=40$ instead of $(n-1)=39$ in the formula.
$\boxed{u_{40} = 100 + 195}$
Carry-in from previous step$u_{40} = 100 + 195$
STEP 2/2 A1

Evaluate:

$$u_{40} = 100 + 195 = 295$$

Always include units (£) in context questions.
$\boxed{£295}$

Part (b) — Total Ben saves in 60 months [2 marks] · 2 steps

Ben: $a = 100$, $d = 5$, $n = 60$
STEP 1/2 M1

Formula: Sum of AP: $S_n = \frac{n}{2}(2a + (n-1)d)$

$$S_{60} = \frac{60}{2}(2(100) + (60-1)(5))$$

$$S_{60} = 30(200 + 295)$$

Alternative formula: $S_n = \frac{n}{2}(a + l)$ where $l$ is the last term.
$\boxed{S_{60} = 30 \times 495}$
Carry-in from previous step$S_{60} = 30 \times 495$
STEP 2/2 A1

Evaluate:

$$S_{60} = 30 \times 495 = 14850$$

Check: $30 \times 500 = 15000$, so $30 \times 495 = 15000 - 150 = 14850$ ✓
$\boxed{£14850}$

Part (c) — Forming equation for Lina [3 marks] · 3 steps

Lina: $a = 600$, $d = -10$, $S_n = 18200$
STEP 1/3 M1

Formula: Substitute into sum formula

$$S_n = \frac{n}{2}(2a + (n-1)d) = 18200$$

$$\frac{n}{2}(2(600) + (n-1)(-10)) = 18200$$

Critical: $d = -10$ (she saves £10 LESS each month). Using $d = +10$ is a common error.
$\boxed{\frac{n}{2}(1200 - 10n + 10) = 18200}$
Carry-in from previous step$\frac{n}{2}(1200 - 10n + 10) = 18200$
STEP 2/3 M1

Simplify and expand:

$$\frac{n}{2}(1210 - 10n) = 18200$$

Multiply both sides by 2:

$$n(1210 - 10n) = 36400$$

$$1210n - 10n^2 = 36400$$

Keep track of signs carefully when expanding brackets.
$\boxed{-10n^2 + 1210n - 36400 = 0}$
Carry-in from previous step$-10n^2 + 1210n - 36400 = 0$
STEP 3/3 A1

Divide by $-10$:

$$n^2 - 121n + 3640 = 0$$

When dividing by a negative, all signs change. Check: $-10 \div -10 = 1$, $1210 \div -10 = -121$, $-36400 \div -10 = 3640$ ✓
$\boxed{n^2 - 121n + 3640 = 0}$

Part (d) — Solve the quadratic [2 marks] · 2 steps

$n^2 - 121n + 3640 = 0$
STEP 1/2 M1

Factorise: Find two numbers that multiply to $3640$ and add to $121$

Testing factor pairs of $3640$:

$56 \times 65 = 3640$ and $56 + 65 = 121$ ✓

$$(n - 56)(n - 65) = 0$$

Alternative: Use quadratic formula $n = \frac{121 \pm \sqrt{121^2 - 4(3640)}}{2} = \frac{121 \pm 9}{2}$
$\boxed{(n - 56)(n - 65) = 0}$
Carry-in from previous step$(n - 56)(n - 65) = 0$
STEP 2/2 A1

Solve:

$$n - 56 = 0 \quad \text{or} \quad n - 65 = 0$$

$$n = 56 \quad \text{or} \quad n = 65$$

Both solutions are mathematically valid — context determines which applies.
$\boxed{n = 56 \text{ or } n = 65}$

Part (e) — Rejecting a solution [2 marks] · 2 steps

$n = 56$ or $n = 65$ from part (d)
STEP 1/2 M1

Check when savings become zero or negative:

Lina's monthly saving: $u_n = 600 + (n-1)(-10)$

Setting $u_n = 0$:

$$600 - 10(n-1) = 0$$

$$600 = 10(n-1)$$

$$n - 1 = 60 \Rightarrow n = 61$$

From month 62 onwards, Lina would have negative savings (withdrawing money).
$\boxed{u_n = 0 \text{ when } n = 61}$
Carry-in from previous step$u_n = 0$ when $n = 61$
STEP 2/2 A1

Conclusion:

When $n = 65$: $u_{65} = 600 + 64(-10) = 600 - 640 = -40$

$n = 65$ is not sensible because from month 62 onwards, Lina's monthly savings would be negative, meaning she would be withdrawing money rather than saving.

Must give a clear reason — stating 'negative savings' or 'model breaks down' is essential for full marks.
$\boxed{n = 65 \text{ rejected; } n = 56 \text{ is the answer}}$
