Geometric Series — Logarithms & Convergence

Question

A geometric sequence has first term 4 and common ratio 6.

(i) Find the smallest value of n for which the nth term of this sequence exceeds 10100. [4]

A different geometric sequence has second term −6 and sum to infinity 25.

(ii)(a) Show that 25r² − 25r − 6 = 0, where r is the common ratio. [3]

(ii)(b) Hence find the two possible values of r. [2]

(ii)(c) Given that the sum to infinity exists, state which value of r is valid, giving a reason. [1]

(ii)(d) For the valid value of r, find the exact value of S4. [3]

[13 marks]

Part (i) — Find minimum n [4 marks] · 4 steps

$a = 4$, $r = 6$
STEP 1/4 M1

Set up inequality with nth term formula

$$u_n = ar^{n-1} > 10^{100}$$

$$4 \times 6^{n-1} > 10^{100}$$

Pitfall: Using $r^n$ instead of $r^{n-1}$ gives an off-by-one error.
$\boxed{4 \times 6^{n-1} > 10^{100}}$
Carry-in from previous step$4 \times 6^{n-1} > 10^{100}$
STEP 2/4 M1

Take logarithms of both sides

$$\log 4 + (n-1)\log 6 > 100$$

$$(n-1) > \frac{100 - \log 4}{\log 6}$$

Pitfall: Ensure you use consistent log base (base 10 here).
$\boxed{(n-1) > \frac{100 - \log 4}{\log 6}}$
Carry-in from previous step$(n-1) > \frac{100 - \log 4}{\log 6}$
STEP 3/4 A1

Evaluate the expression

$$n - 1 > \frac{100 - 0.60206...}{0.77815...} = \frac{99.398...}{0.77815...} = 127.734...$$

$\boxed{n - 1 > 127.734}$
Carry-in from previous step$n - 1 > 127.734$
STEP 4/4 A1

Round up to next integer

$$n - 1 \geq 128 \implies n \geq 129$$

Pitfall: Must round UP — n must be a whole number satisfying the strict inequality.
$\boxed{n = 129}$

Part (ii)(a) — Show quadratic in r [3 marks] · 3 steps

Second term $= -6$, $S_\infty = 25$
STEP 1/3 M1

Form equations from given information

Second term: $ar = -6$

Sum to infinity: $\frac{a}{1-r} = 25$

$\boxed{ar = -6, \quad \frac{a}{1-r} = 25}$
Carry-in from previous step$ar = -6$, $\frac{a}{1-r} = 25$
STEP 2/3 M1

Substitute $a = \frac{-6}{r}$ into sum formula

$$\frac{-6/r}{1-r} = 25$$

$$\frac{-6}{r(1-r)} = 25$$

Pitfall: Sign errors here — keep track of the negative carefully.
$\boxed{-6 = 25r(1-r)}$
Carry-in from previous step$-6 = 25r(1-r)$
STEP 3/3 A1

Expand and rearrange to standard form

$$-6 = 25r - 25r^2$$

$$25r^2 - 25r - 6 = 0 \quad \square$$

$\boxed{25r^2 - 25r - 6 = 0}$

Part (ii)(b) — Solve for r [2 marks] · 2 steps

$25r^2 - 25r - 6 = 0$
STEP 1/2 M1

Factorise or use quadratic formula

$$25r^2 - 25r - 6 = (5r - 6)(5r + 1) = 0$$

$\boxed{(5r - 6)(5r + 1) = 0}$
Carry-in from previous step$(5r - 6)(5r + 1) = 0$
STEP 2/2 A1

Solve each factor

$$5r - 6 = 0 \implies r = \frac{6}{5}$$

$$5r + 1 = 0 \implies r = -\frac{1}{5}$$

$\boxed{r = \frac{6}{5} \text{ or } r = -\frac{1}{5}}$

Part (ii)(c) — Identify valid r [1 marks] · 1 step

$r = \frac{6}{5}$ or $r = -\frac{1}{5}$
STEP 1/1 B1

Apply convergence condition

For $S_\infty$ to exist, we require $|r| < 1$.

$$\left|\frac{6}{5}\right| = 1.2 > 1 \quad \text{(invalid)}$$

$$\left|-\frac{1}{5}\right| = 0.2 < 1 \quad \text{(valid)}$$

Pitfall: Choosing $r = \frac{6}{5}$ fails the convergence condition — the series would diverge.
$\boxed{r = -\frac{1}{5}}$

Part (ii)(d) — Find S₄ [3 marks] · 3 steps

$r = -\frac{1}{5}$, $ar = -6$
STEP 1/3 M1

Find first term a

From $ar = -6$:

$$a = \frac{-6}{r} = \frac{-6}{-1/5} = 30$$

Pitfall: Sign error — both numerator and denominator are negative, so $a$ is positive.
$\boxed{a = 30}$
Carry-in from previous step$a = 30$, $r = -\frac{1}{5}$
STEP 2/3 M1

Apply sum formula

$$S_n = \frac{a(1 - r^n)}{1 - r}$$

$$S_4 = \frac{30\left(1 - \left(-\frac{1}{5}\right)^4\right)}{1 - \left(-\frac{1}{5}\right)}$$

$\boxed{S_4 = \frac{30\left(1 - \frac{1}{625}\right)}{\frac{6}{5}}}$
Carry-in from previous step$S_4 = \frac{30\left(1 - \frac{1}{625}\right)}{\frac{6}{5}}$
STEP 3/3 A1

Simplify to exact value

$$S_4 = \frac{30 \times \frac{624}{625}}{\frac{6}{5}} = 30 \times \frac{624}{625} \times \frac{5}{6} = \frac{624}{25}$$

$\boxed{S_4 = \frac{624}{25} = 24.96}$
