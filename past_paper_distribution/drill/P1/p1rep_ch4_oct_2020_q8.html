Q8 — Show dy/dx; tangent at x=6; parallel tangent at x=α

Question

The curve $C$ has equation $y = (x-2)(x-4)^2$

(a) Show that $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x} = 3x^2 - 20x + 32$   [4]

The line $\ell_1$ is the tangent to $C$ at the point where $x = 6$.

(b) Find the equation of $\ell_1$ in the form $y = mx + c$.   [4]

The line $\ell_2$ is the tangent to $C$ at the point where $x = \alpha$. Given that $\ell_1$ and $\ell_2$ are parallel and distinct:

(c) Find the value of $\alpha$.   [3]

[11 marks]

Solution [11 marks] · 7 steps

STEP 1/7 M1

First expand \((x-4)^2 = x^2 - 8x + 16\).

Then multiply by \((x-2)\):

\( y = (x-2)(x^2 - 8x + 16) \)

\( = x^3 - 8x^2 + 16x - 2x^2 + 16x - 32 \)

\( = x^3 - 10x^2 + 32x - 32 \)

Pitfall: Sign errors when expanding — systematically distribute each term and combine like terms carefully.
\(\boxed{y = x^3 - 10x^2 + 32x - 32}\)
↳ from STEP 1:y = x^3 - 10x^2 + 32x - 32
STEP 2/7 M1

Apply the power rule term by term:

\( \frac{d}{dx}(x^3) = 3x^2 \)

\( \frac{d}{dx}(-10x^2) = -20x \)

\( \frac{d}{dx}(32x) = 32 \)

\( \frac{d}{dx}(-32) = 0 \)

Therefore \( \frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 - 20x + 32 \) as required.

Pitfall: Don't forget the constant term differentiates to zero — it disappears from the derivative.
\(\boxed{\frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 - 20x + 32}\)
↳ from STEP 2:y = (x-2)(x-4)^2
STEP 3/7 M1

Substitute \( x = 6 \) into the original equation:

\( y = (6-2)(6-4)^2 \)

\( = (4)(2)^2 \)

\( = 4 \times 4 = 16 \)

Pitfall: Use the factored form — it's quicker and less error-prone than substituting into the expanded polynomial.
\(\boxed{y = 16 \text{ at } x = 6}\)
↳ from STEP 3:\(\frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 - 20x + 32\)
STEP 4/7 M1

Substitute \( x = 6 \) into the derivative:

\( \frac{dy}{dx} = 3(6)^2 - 20(6) + 32 \)

\( = 3(36) - 120 + 32 \)

\( = 108 - 120 + 32 \)

\( = 20 \)

Pitfall: Evaluate powers before multiplying — compute \(6^2 = 36\) first, then multiply by 3.
\(\boxed{m = 20}\)
↳ from STEP 4:\(\text{Point } (6, 16), \text{ gradient } m = 20\)
STEP 5/7 M1

Using point-gradient form with \((6, 16)\) and \(m = 20\):

\( y - 16 = 20(x - 6) \)

\( y - 16 = 20x - 120 \)

\( y = 20x - 104 \)

Pitfall: When rearranging to \(y = mx + c\), ensure you add 16 to both sides: \(-120 + 16 = -104\).
\(\boxed{y = 20x - 104}\)
↳ from STEP 5:\(l_1 \text{ has gradient } 20\)
STEP 6/7 M1

Parallel lines have equal gradients, so the gradient at \( x = \alpha \) must equal 20:

\( 3\alpha^2 - 20\alpha + 32 = 20 \)

\( 3\alpha^2 - 20\alpha + 32 - 20 = 0 \)

\( 3\alpha^2 - 20\alpha + 12 = 0 \)

Pitfall: Remember to subtract 20 from both sides — don't leave the equation as \(3\alpha^2 - 20\alpha + 32 = 20\).
\(\boxed{3\alpha^2 - 20\alpha + 12 = 0}\)
↳ from STEP 6:\(3\alpha^2 - 20\alpha + 12 = 0\)
STEP 7/7 M1

Factorise the quadratic:

\( 3\alpha^2 - 20\alpha + 12 = (3\alpha - 2)(\alpha - 6) = 0 \)

So \( \alpha = \frac{2}{3} \) or \( \alpha = 6 \).

Since \( l_1 \) is the tangent at \( x = 6 \), and \( l_1, l_2 \) are distinct:

\( \alpha = 6 \) gives the same tangent \( l_1 \), so we reject it.

Therefore \( \alpha = \frac{2}{3} \).

Pitfall: Both roots satisfy "parallel" but only \(\alpha = \frac{2}{3}\) gives a distinct line — always check the "distinct" condition.
\(\boxed{\alpha = \frac{2}{3}}\)
