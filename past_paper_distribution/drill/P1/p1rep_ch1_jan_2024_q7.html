Q7 — Line $y = 3x + k$ and curve $y = \frac{4}{x}$ intersection conditions

Question

The line $L$ has equation $y = 3x + k$, where $k$ is a constant.

The curve $C$ has equation $y = \frac{4}{x}$, where $x \neq 0$.

(a) Show that the $x$-coordinates of any points of intersection of $L$ and $C$ satisfy the equation

$3x^2 + kx - 4 = 0$

(b) Hence find the range of values of $k$ for which $L$ and $C$ do not intersect.

[9 marks]

Part (a) — Deriving the quadratic equation [[3] marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3 M1

Strategy: Equate the expressions for $y$ to find intersection points.

Principle: At intersection, both equations are satisfied simultaneously.

Line $L$: $y = 3x + k$

Curve $C$: $y = \frac{4}{x}$

Setting equal: $3x + k = \frac{4}{x}$

Watch: Remember $x \neq 0$ for the curve, so we can multiply both sides by $x$.
$\boxed{3x + k = \frac{4}{x}}$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $3x + k = \frac{4}{x}$
STEP 2/3 M1

Strategy: Eliminate the fraction by multiplying both sides by $x$.

Method: Multiply each term by $x$:

$x(3x + k) = x \cdot \frac{4}{x}$

$3x^2 + kx = 4$

Rearranging to standard form (all terms on one side):

Watch: Ensure the equation equals zero for standard quadratic form.
$\boxed{3x^2 + kx - 4 = 0}$
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: $3x^2 + kx - 4 = 0$
STEP 3/3 A1

Conclusion: We have derived the required equation.

Starting from $y = 3x + k$ and $y = \frac{4}{x}$

Through equating and algebraic manipulation

We obtain: $3x^2 + kx - 4 = 0$

This is the equation whose solutions give $x$-coordinates of intersection points.

Note: This is shown as required (QED).
$\boxed{3x^2 + kx - 4 = 0 \text{ (as required)}}$

Part (b) — Finding values of $k$ for no intersection [[6] marks] · 6 steps

From (a): $3x^2 + kx - 4 = 0$. Now find when $L$ and $C$ do not intersect.
STEP 1/6 M1

Strategy: Use the discriminant to determine when the quadratic has no real solutions.

Formula (Discriminant): For $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$, discriminant $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$

No real roots when $\Delta < 0$

Here: $a = 3$, $b = k$, $c = -4$

For no intersection, we need no real $x$-coordinates.

Watch: Carefully identify $a$, $b$, $c$ — note $c = -4$ (negative).
$\boxed{\text{Need } b^2 - 4ac < 0}$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $a = 3$, $b = k$, $c = -4$
STEP 2/6 A1

Strategy: Substitute values into discriminant formula.

Calculation:

$\Delta = k^2 - 4(3)(-4)$

$\Delta = k^2 - (-48)$

$\Delta = k^2 + 48$

Watch: Two negatives multiply to give positive: $-4 \times 3 \times (-4) = +48$.
$\boxed{\Delta = k^2 + 48}$
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: $\Delta = k^2 + 48$
STEP 3/6 M1

Strategy: Apply the condition $\Delta < 0$ for no real roots.

Inequality:

$k^2 + 48 < 0$

We need to solve this inequality for $k$.

Consider: $k^2 \geq 0$ for all real values of $k$.

Key insight: Any real number squared is non-negative.
$\boxed{k^2 + 48 < 0}$
↳ from STEP 3:From STEP 3: Need $k^2 + 48 < 0$
STEP 4/6 M1

Strategy: Determine if any real value of $k$ satisfies the inequality.

Analysis:

Since $k^2 \geq 0$ for all real $k$

The minimum value of $k^2$ is $0$ (when $k = 0$)

Therefore minimum value of $k^2 + 48$ is $0 + 48 = 48$

Critical observation: $k^2 + 48 \geq 48 > 0$ always.
$\boxed{k^2 + 48 \geq 48 > 0 \text{ for all real } k}$
↳ from STEP 4:From STEP 4: $k^2 + 48 > 0$ always
STEP 5/6 A1

Strategy: Draw conclusion about when the line and curve don't intersect.

Reasoning:

We required $k^2 + 48 < 0$ for no intersection.

But $k^2 + 48 > 0$ for ALL real values of $k$.

Therefore the discriminant is always positive.

Interpretation: Positive discriminant means two distinct real roots always exist.
$\boxed{\Delta > 0 \text{ for all real } k}$
↳ from STEP 5:From STEP 5: Discriminant always positive
STEP 6/6 A1

Conclusion: State the range of values of $k$.

Since the discriminant $k^2 + 48$ is always positive:

The quadratic $3x^2 + kx - 4 = 0$ always has two distinct real roots.

Therefore $L$ and $C$ always intersect (at two points) for any real value of $k$.

There are no values of $k$ for which $L$ and $C$ do not intersect.

Geometric interpretation: The line $y = 3x + k$ always crosses the rectangular hyperbola $y = \frac{4}{x}$ twice, regardless of the value of $k$.
$\boxed{\text{No values of } k \text{ (or } k \in \emptyset \text{)}}$
