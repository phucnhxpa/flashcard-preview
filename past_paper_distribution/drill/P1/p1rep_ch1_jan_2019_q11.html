Completing the Square, Discriminant, and Line-Curve Intersection

Question

The function $f$ is defined by $f(x) = x^2 - kx + 9$ where $k$ is a positive constant.

(a) Find the range of values of $k$ for which $f(x) > 0$ for all real values of $x$.

(b) For the value of $k$ at the upper limit of this range, express $f(x)$ in completed square form and hence state the coordinates of the vertex of the curve $y = f(x)$.

(c) The line $y = kx - 5$ meets the curve $y = x^2 + 4$ at two distinct points. Find the range of values of $k$.

[[12] marks]

(a) [4 marks] · 4 steps

STEP 1/4 M1

For $f(x) = x^2 - kx + 9 > 0$ for all real $x$, the quadratic must have no real roots.

This requires the discriminant to be negative: $b^2 - 4ac < 0$

$\text{Discriminant} < 0$
↳ from STEP 1:Using $a = 1$, $b = -k$, $c = 9$:
STEP 2/4 A1

$\Delta = (-k)^2 - 4(1)(9)$

$\Delta = k^2 - 36$

$\Delta = k^2 - 36$
↳ from STEP 2:Setting $k^2 - 36 < 0$:
STEP 3/4Solve the inequality $k^2 - 36 < 0$. M1

$k^2 < 36$

$-6 < k < 6$

$-6 < k < 6$
↳ from STEP 3:Since $k$ is positive and $-6 < k < 6$:
STEP 4/4 A1

Combining with $k > 0$:

$0 < k < 6$

$\boxed{0 < k < 6}$

(b) [4 marks] · 4 steps

STEP 1/4 B1

The upper limit of the range $0 < k < 6$ is $k = 6$.

Therefore $f(x) = x^2 - 6x + 9$

$f(x) = x^2 - 6x + 9$
↳ from STEP 1:Completing the square:
STEP 2/4 M1

$f(x) = x^2 - 6x + 9$

$f(x) = (x - 3)^2 - 9 + 9$

$f(x) = (x - 3)^2$

$\boxed{f(x) = (x - 3)^2}$
↳ from STEP 2:From $f(x) = (x - 3)^2 + 0$:
STEP 3/4 A1

The vertex form $y = (x - h)^2 + k$ has vertex at $(h, k)$.

Here $h = 3$ and the constant term is $0$.

$\text{Vertex at } (3, 0)$
↳ from STEP 3:Vertex at $(3, 0)$
STEP 4/4 A1

The minimum value of $(x-3)^2$ is $0$, occurring when $x = 3$.

$\boxed{\text{Vertex} = (3, 0)}$
