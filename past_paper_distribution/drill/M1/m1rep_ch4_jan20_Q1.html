Jan 2020 Q1 — Particles P, Q collide head-on; both reverse direction [8m]

Question

Particles P (mass $m_1$) and Q (mass $m_2$) move on a smooth horizontal plane toward each other, each with speed $u$. Both reverse direction after the collision. After the collision, Q has speed $\tfrac{1}{3}u$.

(a) Find the magnitude of the impulse exerted by P on Q. [3]

(b) Find the speed of P after the collision, in terms of $m_1$, $m_2$, $u$. [3]

(c) Hence show that $m_2 > \tfrac{3}{4}m_1$. [2]

[8 marks]

Part (a) — Impulse on Q [3 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1

Strategy: define a positive direction first, then apply impulse-momentum. Take P’s initial direction as positive.

Initial velocities: $u_P = +u$ (P goes rightward), $u_Q = -u$ (Q goes leftward = opposite direction).

After collision (both reversed):

• Q was going $-u$; reverses to $+\tfrac{1}{3}u$. So $v_Q = +\tfrac{u}{3}$.

Formula (Impulse-Momentum Theorem):

$$J_{P \to Q} = m_2 v_Q - m_2 u_Q$$

Substitute:

$$J_{P \to Q} = m_2 \cdot \frac{u}{3} - m_2 \cdot (-u) = \frac{m_2 u}{3} + m_2 u = \frac{4m_2 u}{3}$$

$$\boxed{\;|J| = \frac{4m_2 u}{3}\;}$$

Sign trap: $u_Q = -u$ (negative!) so subtracting it gives $+u_Q$ contribution. A head-on reversal changes Q's momentum by $m_2 u_3 - (-m_2 u) = m_2(u/3+u) = 4m_2u/3$. This is larger than if Q had been stationary initially.
$|J_{P\to Q}| = \dfrac{4m_2 u}{3}$

Part (b) — Speed of P after collision [3 marks] · 1 step

From (a): $v_Q = +u/3$ (Q now moves in P’s original direction).
STEP 1/1

Formula (Conservation of Linear Momentum):

$$m_1 u_P + m_2 u_Q = m_1 v_P + m_2 v_Q$$

P reverses direction: final velocity $v_P = -v_P'$ where $v_P' > 0$ is P’s final speed.

Substitute $u_P = +u$, $u_Q = -u$, $v_Q = +u/3$, $v_P = -v_P'$:

$$m_1 u + m_2(-u) = m_1(-v_P') + m_2 \cdot \frac{u}{3}$$

$$(m_1 - m_2)u = -m_1 v_P' + \frac{m_2 u}{3}$$

$$m_1 v_P' = \frac{m_2 u}{3} - (m_1 - m_2)u = \frac{m_2 u}{3} + m_2 u - m_1 u$$

$$m_1 v_P' = \frac{4m_2 u}{3} - m_1 u$$

$$\boxed{\;v_P' = \frac{u(4m_2 - 3m_1)}{3m_1}\;}$$

Alternative check (N3L): impulse on P from Q = $-$impulse on Q from P $= -\frac{4m_2 u}{3}$. So $m_1 v_P - m_1 u = -\frac{4m_2 u}{3}$, giving same answer ✓.
$v_P' = \dfrac{u(4m_2 - 3m_1)}{3m_1}$

Part (c) — Show $m_2 > \tfrac{3}{4}m_1$ [2 marks] · 1 step

From (b): $v_P' = \dfrac{u(4m_2 - 3m_1)}{3m_1}$.
STEP 1/1

Key insight: $v_P'$ is a speed, which must be strictly positive (P actually reverses, so it cannot be zero or move in its original direction).

Constraint: $v_P' > 0$:

$$\frac{u(4m_2 - 3m_1)}{3m_1} > 0$$

Since $u > 0$ and $m_1 > 0$, the numerator must be positive:

$$4m_2 - 3m_1 > 0 \implies m_2 > \frac{3m_1}{4} = \frac{3}{4}m_1$$

$$\boxed{\;m_2 > \frac{3}{4}m_1 \quad \blacksquare\;}$$

Physical meaning: Q must be heavy enough relative to P that the collision actually reverses P. If $m_2 \leq \frac{3}{4}m_1$, P would not reverse (the formula would give $v_P'\leq 0$, contradiction). This is the kinematic condition for a genuine reversal of both particles.
$v_P' > 0 \Rightarrow 4m_2 - 3m_1 > 0 \Rightarrow \boxed{m_2 > \tfrac{3}{4}m_1}$
