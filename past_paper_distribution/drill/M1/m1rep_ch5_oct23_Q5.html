Oct 2023 Q5 — Particle on rough vertical rod; string at angle; find $F$ and range of $T$ [12m]

Question

A particle (mass $m = 0.2$ kg) rests on a rough vertical rod. A string makes angle $\theta$ with the vertical where $\sin\theta = \tfrac{12}{13}$, $\cos\theta = \tfrac{5}{13}$. Coefficient of friction $\mu = \tfrac{1}{4}$. ($g = 9.8$ m/s².)

(a) When $T = 10$ N, find the frictional force $F$. [3]

(b) State the direction of $F$ and explain. [1]

(c) Find the range of values of $T$ for equilibrium. [8]

[12 marks]

Parts (a) & (b) — Friction force when $T = 10$ N [4 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1

Setup: particle on vertical rod. String at angle $\theta$ from vertical. Forces: weight $mg$ down, tension $T$ at angle $\theta$ from vertical (has horizontal component away from rod), normal reaction $R$ horizontal from rod, friction $F$ along rod (up or down).

Resolve horizontally (perpendicular to rod, away from rod = positive):

$$T\sin\theta - R = 0 \implies R = T\sin\theta = 10 \cdot \frac{12}{13} = \frac{120}{13}\;\text{N}$$

Resolve vertically (upward = positive, assume $F$ acts downward initially):

$$T\cos\theta - mg - F = 0$$

$$F = T\cos\theta - mg = 10 \cdot \frac{5}{13} - 0.2 \times 9.8 = \frac{50}{13} - 1.96 \approx 3.846 - 1.96 = 1.886\;\text{N}$$

$$\boxed{\;F \approx 1.89\;\text{N}\;}$$

Part (b) direction: $F > 0$ confirms our assumption: $F$ acts downward (the tension’s vertical component $T\cos\theta \approx 3.85$ N exceeds weight $1.96$ N, so particle tends to slide UP, and friction opposes this by acting DOWN).

Key angle insight: $\theta$ is from the VERTICAL, so $T\cos\theta$ is the vertical component and $T\sin\theta$ is the horizontal component. If $\theta$ were from the horizontal, cos and sin would swap. Always draw and label the angle clearly.
$F \approx 1.89$ N downward

Part (c) — Range of $T$ for equilibrium [8 marks] · 2 steps

From (a)/(b): $R = \frac{12T}{13}$; limiting friction $F_{\max} = \mu R = \frac{3T}{13}$.
STEP 1/2

Strategy: at $T_{\max}$, limiting friction opposes upward slide → $F = \mu R$ downward.

Vertical equilibrium with $F$ downward:

$$T\cos\theta - \mu R - mg = 0$$

Substitute $R = \frac{12T}{13}$, $\mu = \frac{1}{4}$, $\cos\theta = \frac{5}{13}$:

$$T \cdot \frac{5}{13} - \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{12T}{13} - mg = 0$$

$$\frac{5T}{13} - \frac{3T}{13} = mg$$

$$\frac{2T}{13} = mg = 0.2 \times 9.8 = 1.96$$

$$T_{\max} = \frac{13 \times 1.96}{2} = \frac{25.48}{2} = 12.74\;\text{N}$$

$T_{\max} = 12.74$ N
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $T_{\max} = 12.74$ N.
STEP 2/2

Strategy: at $T_{\min}$, the tension’s upward component is small; particle tends to slide DOWN; friction opposes this by acting UP.

Vertical equilibrium with $F$ upward:

$$T\cos\theta + \mu R - mg = 0$$

$$\frac{5T}{13} + \frac{3T}{13} = mg$$

$$\frac{8T}{13} = 1.96$$

$$T_{\min} = \frac{13 \times 1.96}{8} = \frac{25.48}{8} = 3.185\;\text{N}$$

$$\boxed{\;3.185\;\text{N} \leq T \leq 12.74\;\text{N}\;}$$

Friction direction reversal is the key: at $T_{\min}$ friction acts UPWARD (to prevent downward slide). At $T_{\max}$ friction acts DOWNWARD (to prevent upward slide). Both use $F_{\max} = \mu R$ with $R = \frac{12T}{13}$, but the sign flips. The two inequalities bracket the equilibrium range.
$\boxed{3.185\,\text{N} \leq T \leq 12.74\,\text{N}}$
