Uniform Rod Against Smooth Wall — Equilibrium with Friction at Ground

Question

A uniform rod $AB$ of length $4$ m and mass $3$ kg rests with end $A$ on rough horizontal ground and end $B$ against a smooth vertical wall. The rod makes an angle of $60°$ with the horizontal.

Take $g = 9.8$ m s$^{-2}$.

(a) Taking moments about $A$, find the reaction $R_B$ from the wall.

(b) Find the normal reaction $N_A$ and friction $F_A$ at the ground.

(c) Find the minimum coefficient of friction $\mu$ for the rod to remain in equilibrium.

[B1 marks]

(a) 2 steps

STEP 1/2 B1

Formula: For moments about $A$: $\sum M_A = 0$ (equilibrium)

Perpendicular distances from $A$:

  • To $R_B$ (horizontal at $B$): vertical height = $4\sin 60° = 4 \times \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = 2\sqrt{3}$ m
  • To $W$ (vertical at midpoint $M$): horizontal distance = $2\cos 60° = 2 \times \frac{1}{2} = 1$ m
Pitfall: The perpendicular distance is NOT along the rod — it's the shortest distance from the pivot to the line of action of the force.
Perpendicular distance to $R_B$: $2\sqrt{3}$ m; to $W$: $1$ m
↳ from STEP 1:Perpendicular distances: $d_{R_B} = 2\sqrt{3}$ m, $d_W = 1$ m
STEP 2/2 B1

Formula: Clockwise moments = Anticlockwise moments about $A$

Calculation:

$R_B \times 2\sqrt{3} = W \times 1 = 3g \times 1$

$R_B = \frac{3g}{2\sqrt{3}} = \frac{3g}{2\sqrt{3}} \times \frac{\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{3g\sqrt{3}}{6} = \frac{g\sqrt{3}}{2}$

$R_B = \frac{9.8 \times 1.732}{2} \approx 8.49$ N

Pitfall: $R_B$ acts horizontally (wall is smooth), perpendicular to the wall surface — NOT along the rod.
$R_B = \frac{g\sqrt{3}}{2} \approx 8.49$ N (horizontal, away from wall)

(b) 1 step

From (a): $R_B = \frac{g\sqrt{3}}{2} \approx 8.49$ N (horizontal, away from wall)
STEP 1/1 B1

Formula: Resolve forces: $\sum F_x = 0$, $\sum F_y = 0$

Vertical equilibrium ($\uparrow$ positive):

$N_A - W = 0$

$N_A = W = 3g = 3 \times 9.8 = 29.4$ N

Horizontal equilibrium ($\rightarrow$ positive):

$F_A - R_B = 0$

$F_A = R_B = \frac{g\sqrt{3}}{2} \approx 8.49$ N

Pitfall: Friction $F_A$ acts towards the wall (preventing the rod from sliding outward at $A$) — same direction as the rod's horizontal component would push.
$N_A = 3g \approx 29.4$ N; $F_A = \frac{g\sqrt{3}}{2} \approx 8.49$ N

(c) 1 step

From (b): $N_A = 3g \approx 29.4$ N; $F_A = \frac{g\sqrt{3}}{2} \approx 8.49$ N
STEP 1/1 B1

Formula: For equilibrium: $F_A \leq \mu N_A$, so $\mu_{\text{min}} = \frac{F_A}{N_A}$

Calculation:

$\mu_{\text{min}} = \frac{F_A}{N_A} = \frac{\frac{g\sqrt{3}}{2}}{3g} = \frac{g\sqrt{3}}{2} \times \frac{1}{3g} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{6}$

$\mu_{\text{min}} = \frac{1.732}{6} \approx 0.289$

Pitfall: At limiting equilibrium, friction equals its maximum value $\mu N$. The minimum $\mu$ is when the friction required equals the maximum available.
$\mu_{\text{min}} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{6} \approx 0.289$
