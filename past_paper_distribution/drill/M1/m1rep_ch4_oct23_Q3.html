Oct 2023 Q3 — Hammer hits tent peg; find common velocity, impulse, resistance [10m]

Question

A hammer of mass $1.8$ kg strikes a tent peg (mass $0.2$ kg at rest) and they move together after impact (perfectly inelastic). The hammer has speed $10$ m/s just before impact. After impact, the peg is driven $12$ cm into the ground and comes to rest.

(a) Find the common velocity immediately after impact. [2]

(b) Find the magnitude of the impulse exerted by the hammer on the peg. [2]

(c) Find the constant resistive force $R$ exerted by the ground on the combined system. [6]

[10 marks]

Part (a) — Common velocity $v$ [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1

Assumption: collision is perfectly inelastic → hammer and peg coalesce into one combined body of mass $1.8 + 0.2 = 2.0$ kg.

Formula (CLM, downward = positive):

$$m_H u_H + m_P u_P = (m_H + m_P) v$$

Substitute ($m_H=1.8$, $u_H=10$, $m_P=0.2$, $u_P=0$):

$$(1.8)(10) + (0.2)(0) = (2.0)v$$

$$18 = 2.0\,v$$

$$\boxed{\;v = 9\;\text{m/s (downward)}\;}$$

Energy check: KE before $= \frac{1}{2}(1.8)(100) = 90$ J; KE after $= \frac{1}{2}(2.0)(81) = 81$ J. Loss $= 9$ J. Energy is NOT conserved in an inelastic collision – the 9 J goes into deforming the peg/ground. This is expected and consistent.
$v = 9$ m/s (downward)

Part (b) — Impulse on peg [2 marks] · 1 step

From (a): $v = 9$ m/s immediately after impact.
STEP 1/1

Formula (Impulse = change in momentum of peg):

$$J = m_P v - m_P u_P$$

Substitute ($m_P = 0.2$, $v = 9$, $u_P = 0$):

$$J = (0.2)(9) - (0.2)(0) = 1.8\ \text{Ns}$$

$$\boxed{\;|J| = 1.8\;\text{Ns}\;}$$

Cross-check via hammer: Impulse on hammer from peg $= 1.8(9 - 10) = 1.8(-1) = -1.8$ Ns. By Newton’s 3rd Law, impulse on peg from hammer $= +1.8$ Ns ✓. Consistent.
$|J| = 1.8$ Ns

Part (c) — Resistive force $R$ [6 marks] · 2 steps

From (a): combined system (mass $2.0$ kg) enters ground at $9$ m/s, decelerates to rest over $s = 0.12$ m.
STEP 1/2

Formula (SUVAT): $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$

Known: $u = 9$ m/s (entering ground), $v = 0$ (at rest), $s = 0.12$ m.

$$0 = (9)^2 + 2a(0.12)$$

$$0 = 81 + 0.24a$$

$$a = -\frac{81}{0.24} = -337.5\ \text{m/s}^2$$

The magnitude of deceleration is $337.5$ m/s² (large because $s$ is very small!).

Unit consistency: $s = 12$ cm $= 0.12$ m. If you forget to convert cm to m you get $a = -81/24 = -3.375$ m/s² — 100× too small! Always convert at the start.
$a = -337.5$ m/s² (decelerating downward)
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $a = -337.5$ m/s² (net deceleration, i.e. $337.5$ m/s² upward).
STEP 2/2

Formula (Newton’s 2nd Law, downward = positive):

$$\Sigma F = ma$$

Forces on combined system (mass $M = 2.0$ kg):

• Weight $Mg = 2.0 \times 9.8 = 19.6$ N downward ($+$)

• Ground resistive force $R$ upward ($-$)

$$Mg - R = Ma = 2.0 \times (-337.5)$$

$$19.6 - R = -675$$

$$R = 19.6 + 675 = 694.6\ \text{N} \approx 695\ \text{N}$$

$$\boxed{\;R \approx 695\;\text{N}\;}$$

Why is R so much larger than weight? $R = 695$ N vs $Mg = 19.6$ N. The deceleration is $337.5/9.8 \approx 34.4\,g$ — over 34 times gravitational acceleration! This is why pile-drivers are effective: the enormous resistive force over a tiny distance does the work.
$\boxed{\,R \approx 695\,\text{N}\,}$
