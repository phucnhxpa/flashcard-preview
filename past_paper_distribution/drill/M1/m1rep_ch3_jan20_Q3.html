Q3 — P Projected Upward at $U$; Q Projected at $\tfrac{U}{2}$ When P at Max Height — Find Collision

Question

Particle P projected vertically upward at speed $U$ from fixed point O.

At the instant P reaches greatest height $H$ above O, particle Q is projected vertically upward from O at speed $\tfrac{1}{2}U$.

(a) Find $H$ in terms of $U$ and $g$. [2]

(b) Find, in terms of $U$ and $g$, the time from Q's projection to the collision. [6]

(c) Find where the particles collide. [3]

[📐 Diagram]

[11 marks]

Part (a) — Maximum height $H$ [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Strategy: At maximum height, velocity = 0. Use $v^2=u^2+2as$ with upward positive.

Formula (SUVAT): $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$

Recall: $v=0$ at max height; $u=U$; $a=-g$; $s=H$.

$0 = U^2 + 2(-g)H$

$0 = U^2 - 2gH$

$H = \dfrac{U^2}{2g}$

Pitfall: Using $a=+g$ (forgetting upward is positive so gravity is $-g$), giving $H=-U^2/(2g)$ (negative — impossible). Convention: take upward as positive throughout, so $a=-g$.
$\boxed{H = \dfrac{U^2}{2g}}$

Part (b) — Time from Q's projection to collision [6 marks] · 2 steps

From (a): $H=U^2/(2g)$. Set $t=0$ at moment Q is projected. P is at height $H$ with velocity 0.
STEP 1/2 M1 A1 A1

Strategy: Write height equations for P and Q from the same reference time ($t=0$ = Q's projection). Set them equal to find collision time.

P's height above O (at $t=0$: P is at $H$ with $v_P=0$):

$s_P(t) = H + 0\cdot t + \tfrac{1}{2}(-g)t^2 = \dfrac{U^2}{2g} - \dfrac{g}{2}t^2$

Q's height above O (at $t=0$: Q at O with $v_Q=U/2$ upward):

$s_Q(t) = 0 + \dfrac{U}{2}t + \tfrac{1}{2}(-g)t^2 = \dfrac{U}{2}t - \dfrac{g}{2}t^2$

$s_P=U^2/(2g) - gt^2/2$; $s_Q=Ut/2 - gt^2/2$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $s_P=U^2/(2g)-gt^2/2$, $s_Q=Ut/2-gt^2/2$
STEP 2/2 M1 A1 A1

Strategy: Set $s_P = s_Q$ and solve. Notice the $-gt^2/2$ terms cancel.

$\dfrac{U^2}{2g} - \dfrac{g}{2}t^2 = \dfrac{U}{2}t - \dfrac{g}{2}t^2$

$\dfrac{U^2}{2g} = \dfrac{U}{2}t \quad$ (the $-gt^2/2$ cancels both sides)

$t = \dfrac{U^2}{2g} \cdot \dfrac{2}{U} = \dfrac{U}{g}$

Pitfall: Not noticing the $-gt^2/2$ cancels. This is a key insight — when both particles are under gravity, the $\frac{1}{2}at^2$ term is identical and cancels. What remains is a simple linear equation, not quadratic. This elimination is a hallmark of collision problems under gravity.
$\boxed{t = \dfrac{U}{g}}$

Part (c) — Where do they collide? [3 marks] · 1 step

From (b): Collision at $t=U/g$ after Q projected.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1 A1

Strategy: Substitute $t=U/g$ into either height equation. Use Q's (simpler).

$s_Q\!\left(\dfrac{U}{g}\right) = \dfrac{U}{2}\cdot\dfrac{U}{g} - \dfrac{g}{2}\cdot\left(\dfrac{U}{g}\right)^2$

$= \dfrac{U^2}{2g} - \dfrac{g}{2}\cdot\dfrac{U^2}{g^2}$

$= \dfrac{U^2}{2g} - \dfrac{U^2}{2g} = 0$

The collision occurs at height $= 0$, i.e. at point O (the launch point).

Verify with P: $s_P(U/g) = U^2/(2g) - g/2 \cdot U^2/g^2 = U^2/(2g)-U^2/(2g)=0$ ✓

Pitfall: Expecting a non-trivial height and assuming a calculation error. The surprising-but-correct answer: P falls all the way back to O in time $U/g$, and Q (projected at $U/2$) rises to $U^2/(8g)$ then falls back to O in the same time $U/g$ (symmetric motion). They both arrive at O simultaneously. The collision is at O.
$\boxed{\text{Collision at point O, height 0 above O}}$
