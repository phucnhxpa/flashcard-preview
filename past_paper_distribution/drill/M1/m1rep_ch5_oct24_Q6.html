Particle $P$ of mass $5\text{ kg}$ on rough plane inclined at $\alpha$ to horizontal where $\tan\alpha = \frac{3}{4}$. Coefficient of friction $\mu = \frac{1}{4}$. Horizontal force $H$ N keeps particle in equilibrium.

Question

A particle $P$ of mass $5\text{ kg}$ lies on the surface of a rough plane.

The plane is inclined at an angle $\alpha$ to the horizontal, where $\tan\alpha = \dfrac{3}{4}$

The particle is held in equilibrium by a horizontal force of magnitude $H$ newtons, acting in a vertical plane containing a line of greatest slope of the inclined plane.

The coefficient of friction between the particle and the plane is $\dfrac{1}{4}$

(a) Find the smallest possible value of $H$.

The horizontal force is now removed, and $P$ starts to slide down the slope.

In the first $T$ seconds after $P$ is released from rest, $P$ slides $1.5\text{ m}$ down the slope.

(b) Find the value of $T$.

[12 marks]

(a) [6 marks] · 4 steps

STEP 1/4 1

Extract angle values from $\tan\alpha = \frac{3}{4}$:

Using a 3-4-5 right triangle:

  • $\sin\alpha = \dfrac{3}{5}$
  • $\cos\alpha = \dfrac{4}{5}$

Identify forces on particle:

  • Weight $5g$ acting vertically downward
  • Normal reaction $R$ perpendicular to plane
  • Friction $F$ along the plane
  • Horizontal force $H$ (has components both parallel and perpendicular to plane)
Common pitfall: forgetting that horizontal force H has both parallel ($H\cos\alpha$) and perpendicular ($H\sin\alpha$) components to the slope.
$\sin\alpha = \frac{3}{5}$, $\cos\alpha = \frac{4}{5}$
↳ from STEP 1:From Step 1: $\sin\alpha = \frac{3}{5}$, $\cos\alpha = \frac{4}{5}$
STEP 2/4 2

Resolve perpendicular to plane:

For equilibrium perpendicular to the inclined surface:

$$R = 5g\cos\alpha + H\sin\alpha$$

Substituting values:

$$R = 5g \times \frac{4}{5} + H \times \frac{3}{5}$$ $$R = 4g + \frac{3H}{5}$$

Determine friction direction:

Without $H$: Component down slope $= 5g\sin\alpha = 3g$

Max friction $= \mu \times 5g\cos\alpha = \frac{1}{4} \times 4g = g$

Since $3g > g$, particle would slide DOWN. For minimum $H$, friction acts UP the slope (limiting equilibrium).

Common pitfall: not checking whether particle tends to slide up or down to determine friction direction.
$R = 4g + \frac{3H}{5}$, friction acts UP slope
↳ from STEP 2:From Step 2: $R = 4g + \frac{3H}{5}$, friction acts up slope
STEP 3/4 2

Resolve parallel to plane (up slope positive):

For equilibrium along the slope:

$$H\cos\alpha + F = 5g\sin\alpha$$

Using $F = \mu R$ at limiting equilibrium:

$$H\cos\alpha + \mu R = 5g\sin\alpha$$

Substituting $\cos\alpha = \frac{4}{5}$, $\sin\alpha = \frac{3}{5}$, $\mu = \frac{1}{4}$:

$$\frac{4H}{5} + \frac{1}{4}\left(4g + \frac{3H}{5}\right) = 3g$$ $$\frac{4H}{5} + g + \frac{3H}{20} = 3g$$
Common pitfall: sign errors when resolving — always draw a clear diagram showing positive direction.
$\frac{4H}{5} + g + \frac{3H}{20} = 3g$
↳ from STEP 3:From Step 3: $\frac{4H}{5} + g + \frac{3H}{20} = 3g$
STEP 4/4 1

Solve for $H$:

$$\frac{4H}{5} + \frac{3H}{20} = 3g - g = 2g$$

Finding common denominator (20):

$$\frac{16H}{20} + \frac{3H}{20} = 2g$$ $$\frac{19H}{20} = 2g$$ $$H = \frac{40g}{19}$$

Substituting $g = 9.8$:

$$H = \frac{40 \times 9.8}{19} = \frac{392}{19} \approx 20.6\text{ N}$$
Common pitfall: arithmetic errors when combining fractions — always find a common denominator carefully.
$H = \frac{40g}{19} \approx 20.6$ N

(b) [6 marks] · 3 steps

From (a): With $H$ applied, particle was in equilibrium.
STEP 1/3 2

When $H$ is removed, find new normal reaction and friction:

Resolve perpendicular to plane (no $H$ component now):

$$R = 5g\cos\alpha = 5g \times \frac{4}{5} = 4g$$

Since particle slides down, friction acts UP the slope:

$$F = \mu R = \frac{1}{4} \times 4g = g$$
Common pitfall: using the old value of $R$ from part (a) — when $H$ is removed, $R$ changes because there's no longer a perpendicular component from $H$.
$R = 4g$, $F = g$
↳ from STEP 1:From Step 1: $R = 4g$, $F = g$
STEP 2/3 2

Apply Newton's Second Law parallel to plane:

Taking down the slope as positive:

$$ma = 5g\sin\alpha - F$$ $$5a = 5g \times \frac{3}{5} - g$$ $$5a = 3g - g = 2g$$ $$a = \frac{2g}{5}$$

Substituting $g = 9.8$:

$$a = \frac{2 \times 9.8}{5} = \frac{19.6}{5} = 3.92\text{ m s}^{-2}$$
Common pitfall: forgetting to subtract friction force — friction always opposes motion (here acting up slope while particle moves down).
$a = \frac{2g}{5} = 3.92$ m s$^{-2}$
↳ from STEP 2:From Step 2: $a = \frac{2g}{5} = 3.92$ m s$^{-2}$
STEP 3/3 2

Use SUVAT to find time $T$:

Given: $u = 0$ (starts from rest), $s = 1.5$ m, $a = \frac{2g}{5}$

Using $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$:

$$1.5 = 0 + \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{2g}{5} \times T^2$$ $$1.5 = \frac{g}{5} \times T^2$$ $$T^2 = \frac{1.5 \times 5}{g} = \frac{7.5}{9.8}$$ $$T^2 = 0.7653...$$ $$T = \sqrt{0.7653} = 0.875\text{ s (3 s.f.)}$$
Common pitfall: forgetting that the particle starts from rest, so $u = 0$ in SUVAT equations.
$T = \sqrt{\frac{7.5}{g}} \approx 0.875$ s
