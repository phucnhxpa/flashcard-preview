Q6 — Particle P Under Two Forces: Find $p,q$; Angle of $\mathbf{a}$; Time to Move in Given Direction

Question

Particle P of mass 2 kg, forces $(p\mathbf{i}+q\mathbf{j})$ N and $(2q\mathbf{i}+p\mathbf{j})$ N. Acceleration $\mathbf{a}=(\mathbf{i}-\mathbf{j})$ m s$^{-2}$.

(a) Find $p$ and $q$. [5]

(b) Angle between direction of acceleration and vector $\mathbf{j}$. [2]

At $t=0$: $\mathbf{u}=(3\mathbf{i}-4\mathbf{j})$ m s$^{-1}$. At $t=T$: P moves in direction $(11\mathbf{i}-13\mathbf{j})$.

(c) Find the value of $T$. [5]

[12 marks]

Part (a) — Find $p$ and $q$ [5 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2 M1 A1

Strategy: Apply Newton's Second Law $\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}}=m\mathbf{a}$. Add the two forces, equate to $m\mathbf{a}$, then match $\mathbf{i}$ and $\mathbf{j}$ components separately.

Formula (Newton's 2nd Law): $\sum \mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$

$\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}} = (p\mathbf{i}+q\mathbf{j})+(2q\mathbf{i}+p\mathbf{j})$

$= (p+2q)\mathbf{i}+(p+q)\mathbf{j}$

$m\mathbf{a} = 2(\mathbf{i}-\mathbf{j}) = 2\mathbf{i}-2\mathbf{j}$

Match components:

$\mathbf{i}$: $p+2q=2 \quad (1)$

$\mathbf{j}$: $p+q=-2 \quad (2)$

System: $p+2q=2$; $p+q=-2$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $p+2q=2$, $p+q=-2$
STEP 2/2 A1 A1 A1

Strategy: Solve the simultaneous equations by elimination.

$(1)-(2)$: $(p+2q)-(p+q) = 2-(-2)$

$q = 4$

Substitute $q=4$ into $(2)$: $p+4=-2$

$p = -6$

Verify (1): $(-6)+2(4)=-6+8=2$ ✓

Pitfall: Setting $p+2q=m\cdot a_i$ but forgetting $m=2$, writing $p+2q=1$ instead of $p+2q=2$. Always multiply both $\mathbf{i}$ and $\mathbf{j}$ components of $\mathbf{a}$ by the mass $m$.
$\boxed{p=-6, \quad q=4}$

Part (b) — Angle between acceleration and j [2 marks] · 1 step

From (a): $\mathbf{a}=(\mathbf{i}-\mathbf{j})$ m s$^{-2}$.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Strategy: Use dot product to find angle between $\mathbf{a}=(\mathbf{i}-\mathbf{j})$ and $\mathbf{j}=(0\mathbf{i}+1\mathbf{j})$.

Formula (Dot Product Angle): $\cos\theta = \dfrac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{j}}{|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{j}|}$

$\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{j} = (1)(0)+(-1)(1) = -1$

$|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{1^2+(-1)^2}=\sqrt{2}$; $|\mathbf{j}|=1$

$\cos\theta = \dfrac{-1}{\sqrt{2}} \Rightarrow \theta = \arccos\!\left(-\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) = 135^\circ$

Pitfall: Reporting $45°$ instead of $135°$. $\arccos(-1/\sqrt{2})=135°$, not $45°$. The negative dot product means the vectors point in "opposite-ish" directions. $\mathbf{a}=(\mathbf{i}-\mathbf{j})$ points SE while $\mathbf{j}$ points N — they're $135°$ apart.
$\boxed{\theta = 135^\circ}$

Part (c) — Find $T$ for direction $(11\mathbf{i}-13\mathbf{j})$ [5 marks] · 2 steps

From (a): $\mathbf{a}=(\mathbf{i}-\mathbf{j})$; $\mathbf{u}=(3\mathbf{i}-4\mathbf{j})$ at $t=0$.
STEP 1/2 M1 A1

Strategy: Write $\mathbf{v}(T) = \mathbf{u}+T\mathbf{a}$. "Moving in direction $(11\mathbf{i}-13\mathbf{j})$" means $\mathbf{v}(T)$ is parallel to $(11\mathbf{i}-13\mathbf{j})$.

Formula (SUVAT Vector): $\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{u}+t\mathbf{a}$

$\mathbf{v}(T) = (3\mathbf{i}-4\mathbf{j}) + T(\mathbf{i}-\mathbf{j})$

$= (3+T)\mathbf{i}+(-4-T)\mathbf{j}$

$\mathbf{v}(T) = (3+T)\mathbf{i}-(4+T)\mathbf{j}$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $\mathbf{v}(T)=(3+T)\mathbf{i}-(4+T)\mathbf{j}$
STEP 2/2 M1 A1 A1

Strategy: Parallel vectors have equal ratios of components. Set $\dfrac{3+T}{11} = \dfrac{-(4+T)}{-13}$.

$\dfrac{3+T}{11} = \dfrac{4+T}{13}$

$13(3+T) = 11(4+T)$

$39+13T = 44+11T$

$2T = 5$

$T = 2.5$ s

Verify: $\mathbf{v}(2.5) = 5.5\mathbf{i}-6.5\mathbf{j}$. Ratio to $(11,-13)$: $5.5/11=0.5$, $6.5/13=0.5$ ✓

Pitfall: Cross-multiplying and getting sign errors on $-(4+T)$. Write the $\mathbf{j}$ component of direction as $-13$ (negative) matching $-(4+T)$ (also negative), so the negatives cancel in the ratio. Alternatively, use $(-4-T)/(-13)=(4+T)/13$.
$\boxed{T = 2.5 \text{ s}}$
