Q8 — Boats P and Q with Constant Velocities: Bearings, Relative Position & Distance

Question

i and j are horizontal unit vectors directed due east and due north. Position vectors relative to fixed origin O.

Boat P: velocity $\mathbf{v}_P = 15\mathbf{i}$ m s$^{-1}$.

Boat Q: velocity $\mathbf{v}_Q = (20\mathbf{i} - 20\mathbf{j})$ m s$^{-1}$.

(a) Find the bearing of Q's direction of travel. [2]

At $t=0$: P at origin O, Q at $200\mathbf{j}$ m.

(b) Show that $\vec{PQ} = [5t\mathbf{i}+(200-20t)\mathbf{j}]$ m. [5]

(c) Bearing of P from Q when $t=10$. [2]

(d) Distance PQ when Q is north-east of P. [5]

(e) Times when P and Q are 200 m apart. [3]

[17 marks]

Part (a) — Bearing of Q [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Strategy: The velocity vector gives the direction of travel. Bearing is measured clockwise from North. Decompose the velocity into east (i) and south/north (j) components to find the angle from North.

Formula (Bearing from velocity vector): For $\mathbf{v} = v_E\mathbf{i} + v_N\mathbf{j}$, $\text{bearing} = \arctan\!\left(\dfrac{v_E}{v_N}\right)$ measured clockwise from N, adjusting for quadrant.

Recall: positive $\mathbf{j}$ = North; negative $\mathbf{j}$ = South;

$\mathbf{v}_Q = 20\mathbf{i} - 20\mathbf{j}$ means 20 East, 20 South $\Rightarrow$ South-East quadrant.

Angle east of south: $\tan\theta = \dfrac{20}{20} = 1 \Rightarrow \theta = 45^\circ$

Bearing from N going clockwise: $90^\circ + 45^\circ = 135^\circ$

Pitfall: Students write $\arctan(20/20) = 45°$ and report 45° as the bearing. But bearing is from North, not East. SE direction = 90° + 45° = 135°, not 45°. Always ask: "how far clockwise from N?" not "what angle from E?"
$\boxed{\text{Bearing} = 135^\circ}$

Part (b) — Show $\vec{PQ} = [5t\mathbf{i}+(200-20t)\mathbf{j}]$ m [5 marks] · 2 steps

From (a): Q travels on bearing 135°. Now find positions at time $t$.
STEP 1/2 M1

Strategy: Use position vector formula for constant velocity motion. Write each boat's position $\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}t$, then subtract.

Formula (Constant Velocity Position): $\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}t$

Recall: P starts at origin $\mathbf{0}$; Q starts at $200\mathbf{j}$.

Position of P: $\mathbf{p} = \mathbf{0} + 15\mathbf{i}\cdot t = 15t\mathbf{i}$

Position of Q: $\mathbf{q} = 200\mathbf{j} + (20\mathbf{i}-20\mathbf{j})t$

$\mathbf{q} = 20t\mathbf{i} + (200-20t)\mathbf{j}$

$\mathbf{p} = 15t\mathbf{i}$; $\quad \mathbf{q} = 20t\mathbf{i}+(200-20t)\mathbf{j}$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $\mathbf{p}=15t\mathbf{i}$, $\mathbf{q}=20t\mathbf{i}+(200-20t)\mathbf{j}$
STEP 2/2 A1 A1

Strategy: The vector from P to Q is $\vec{PQ} = \mathbf{q} - \mathbf{p}$. Subtract component by component.

$\vec{PQ} = \mathbf{q} - \mathbf{p}$

$= [20t\mathbf{i}+(200-20t)\mathbf{j}] - 15t\mathbf{i}$

$\mathbf{i}$-component: $20t - 15t = 5t$

$\mathbf{j}$-component: $(200-20t) - 0 = (200-20t)$

$\vec{PQ} = 5t\mathbf{i} + (200-20t)\mathbf{j}$ m $\quad \checkmark$ (shown)

Pitfall: Writing $\vec{PQ} = \mathbf{p}-\mathbf{q}$ (backwards). Remember $\vec{PQ}$ goes from P to Q so it equals $\mathbf{q}-\mathbf{p}$. The letter Q comes last $\Rightarrow$ Q vector minus P vector.
$\boxed{\vec{PQ} = 5t\mathbf{i}+(200-20t)\mathbf{j}}$ m (shown)

Part (c) — Bearing of P from Q at $t=10$ [2 marks] · 1 step

From (b): $\vec{PQ} = 5t\mathbf{i}+(200-20t)\mathbf{j}$. Now find bearing of P from Q at $t=10$.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Strategy: Substitute $t=10$ into $\vec{PQ}$, then reverse to get $\vec{QP}$. Convert the direction of $\vec{QP}$ to a bearing.

At $t=10$: $\vec{PQ} = 5(10)\mathbf{i}+(200-20(10))\mathbf{j}$

$= 50\mathbf{i} + (200-200)\mathbf{j}$

$= 50\mathbf{i}$ (i.e. P is due East of Q)

We want bearing of P from Q, i.e. direction of $\vec{QP} = -\vec{PQ} = -50\mathbf{i}$

$-50\mathbf{i}$ points due West $= $ bearing $270^\circ$

Pitfall: Reporting bearing 090° (East) instead of 270° (West). "Bearing of P from Q" means standing at Q, looking toward P. P is at $50\mathbf{i}$ relative to Q, so P is East of Q — meaning from Q you look East. But $\vec{QP} = -50\mathbf{i}$ is West, meaning Q is East of P, so from Q, P is West = 270°. Re-read: $\vec{QP}$ points from Q toward P = $-50\mathbf{i}$ = West.
$\boxed{\text{Bearing of P from Q} = 270^\circ}$

Part (d) — Distance when Q is NE of P [5 marks] · 2 steps

From (b): $\vec{PQ}=5t\mathbf{i}+(200-20t)\mathbf{j}$. Use the NE condition to find $t$, then $|\vec{PQ}|$.
STEP 1/2 M1 A1

Strategy: "Q is NE of P" means Q lies on a bearing of 045° from P, so $\vec{PQ}$ has equal positive i and j components.

NE condition: $\mathbf{i}$-component $=$ $\mathbf{j}$-component, both positive.

Set $5t = 200-20t$:

$25t = 200$

$t = 8$ s

Check positivity: $5(8)=40>0$ ✓, $200-20(8)=40>0$ ✓

$t = 8$ s
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $t=8$ s so $\vec{PQ}=40\mathbf{i}+40\mathbf{j}$
STEP 2/2 M1 A1 A1

Strategy: Substitute $t=8$ into $\vec{PQ}$ and find its magnitude.

Formula (Magnitude of vector): $|\mathbf{v}|=\sqrt{v_i^2+v_j^2}$

$\vec{PQ}\big|_{t=8} = 40\mathbf{i}+40\mathbf{j}$

$|\vec{PQ}| = \sqrt{40^2+40^2} = \sqrt{1600+1600} = \sqrt{3200}$

$= \sqrt{1600\times 2} = 40\sqrt{2}$ m

Pitfall: Writing $\sqrt{40+40}=\sqrt{80}$ instead of $\sqrt{40^2+40^2}=\sqrt{3200}$. Magnitudes use Pythagoras: square each component. $\sqrt{3200}=40\sqrt{2}\approx 56.6$ m, not $\sqrt{80}\approx 8.9$ m.
$\boxed{|\vec{PQ}| = 40\sqrt{2} \approx 56.6 \text{ m}}$

Part (e) — Times when $|\vec{PQ}|=200$ m [3 marks] · 1 step

From (b): $\vec{PQ}=5t\mathbf{i}+(200-20t)\mathbf{j}$. Set $|\vec{PQ}|=200$ and solve.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1 A1

Strategy: Set up $|\vec{PQ}|^2 = 200^2$, expand and solve the quadratic.

Formula: $|\vec{PQ}|^2 = (5t)^2 + (200-20t)^2 = 40000$

$25t^2 + 40000 - 8000t + 400t^2 = 40000$

$425t^2 - 8000t = 0$

$t(425t - 8000) = 0$

$t = 0$ or $t = \dfrac{8000}{425} = \dfrac{320}{17}\approx 18.8$ s

At $t=0$: $|\vec{PQ}|=|200\mathbf{j}|=200$ m ✓ (initial separation)

Pitfall: Forgetting $t=0$ as a solution, or cancelling the $t$ factor without noting $t=0$. This is a quadratic; always factorise fully. The $t=0$ solution is physically meaningful (initial distance = 200 m).
$\boxed{t = 0 \text{ s or } t = \tfrac{320}{17} \approx 18.8 \text{ s}}$
