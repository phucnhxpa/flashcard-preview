Stem Lengths of 120 Tulips — Histogram Areas & Quartiles

Question

The stem lengths of a sample of 120 tulips are recorded in the grouped frequency table below.

Stem length (cm)$40\leqslant x<42$$42\leqslant x<45$$45\leqslant x<50$$50\leqslant x<55$$55\leqslant x<58$$58\leqslant x<60$
Frequency12182335248

A histogram is drawn to represent these data. The area of the bar representing the $40\leqslant x<42$ class is 16.5 cm².

(a) Calculate the exact area of the bar representing the $42\leqslant x<45$ class. [2]

The height of the tallest bar in the histogram is 10 cm.

(b) Find the exact height of the second tallest bar. [3]

$Q_1$ for these data is 45 cm.

(c) Use linear interpolation to find an estimate for (i) $Q_2$; (ii) the interquartile range. [4]

One measure of skewness is given by $\dfrac{Q_3 - Q_2 - (Q_2 - Q_1)}{Q_3 - Q_1}$.

(d) By calculating this measure, describe the skewness of these data. [2]

[11 marks]

(a) Area of 42–45 bar [2 marks · 1 step]

Key Concept — Bar Area in a Histogram is Proportional to Frequency
In a histogram, the area of each bar (height × width) is proportional to the frequency of that class. This is the defining property: \[\text{Area of bar} \propto \text{Frequency}\] or equivalently: \[\frac{\text{Area of bar A}}{\text{Area of bar B}} = \frac{\text{Frequency of class A}}{\text{Frequency of class B}}\] Why? The bar's height equals frequency density \(= \tfrac{\text{freq}}{\text{width}}\), so area \(= \text{fd} \times \text{width} = \text{frequency}\). Because a single scale factor converts frequency densities to actual heights in cm (determined by the physical drawing), the scale factor cancels in ratios, making area directly proportional to frequency.
STEP 1

What we're doing: Use the proportionality of area to frequency. We know that the 40–42 class has area 16.5 cm² and frequency 12. We need the area for the 42–45 class with frequency 18.

Setting up the proportion: \[\frac{\text{Area}_{42\text{–}45}}{\text{Area}_{40\text{–}42}} = \frac{\text{Freq}_{42\text{–}45}}{\text{Freq}_{40\text{–}42}}\]

Substituting known values: \[\frac{\text{Area}_{42\text{–}45}}{16.5} = \frac{18}{12} = \frac{3}{2}\]

Solving for the unknown area: \[\text{Area}_{42\text{–}45} = 16.5 \times \frac{3}{2} = \frac{33}{2} \times \frac{3}{2} = \frac{99}{4} = 24.75 \text{ cm}^2\]

The scale factor converting frequency density to bar height in cm is fixed for the whole histogram. This scale factor cancels in the ratio, leaving areas directly proportional to frequencies. We do not need to know the actual scale factor — the ratio is enough.
\(\boxed{\text{Area} = \dfrac{99}{4} = 24.75 \text{ cm}^2}\)

(b) Height of second tallest bar [3 marks · 2 steps]

Key Concept — Bar Height = Frequency Density; Heights Proportional to fd
The height of a histogram bar is proportional to the bar's frequency density: \[\text{fd} = \frac{\text{Frequency}}{\text{Class Width}}\] The tallest bar has the largest frequency density. Since the tallest bar is given a physical height of 10 cm, we find all other heights by ratio: \[\text{Height of bar} = \frac{\text{fd of that bar}}{\text{fd of tallest bar}} \times 10 \text{ cm}\]

Tallest bar: 55–58 (fd = 8.0). Second tallest: 50–55 (fd = 7.0).

Even though 50–55 has more tulips (35 vs 24), the 55–58 class spans only 3 cm while 50–55 spans 5 cm. Packing 24 tulips into 3 cm gives a higher density (8 per cm) than 35 tulips spread across 5 cm (7 per cm). This is why we always use frequency density — not frequency — to determine bar height in a histogram.
STEP 2

What we're doing: Use the ratio of frequency densities to find the physical height of the 50–55 bar. The tallest bar (fd = 8) has height = 10 cm:

\[\text{Height of 50–55 bar} = \frac{\text{fd}_{50\text{–}55}}{\text{fd}_{55\text{–}58}} \times 10 = \frac{7}{8} \times 10 = \frac{70}{8} = \frac{35}{4} \text{ cm}\]
Bar heights are directly proportional to frequency densities (same scale factor applies to all bars). So the 50–55 bar's height is \(\tfrac{7}{8}\) of the tallest bar's height of 10 cm, giving \(8.75\) cm exactly.
\(\boxed{\text{Height} = \dfrac{35}{4} = 8.75 \text{ cm}}\)

(c) Q₂ and IQR by interpolation [4 marks · 2 steps]

Given \(Q_1 = 45\) cm
Key Concept — Median (\(Q_2\)) and Upper Quartile (\(Q_3\)) by Linear Interpolation
For \(n = 120\) data points, the quartile positions are: \[Q_2 \text{ (median): } \frac{n}{2} = \frac{120}{2} = 60\text{th value}\] \[Q_3 \text{ (upper quartile): } \frac{3n}{4} = \frac{3 \times 120}{4} = 90\text{th value}\] Once we identify which class contains the required value, we use the linear interpolation formula: \[Q = L + \frac{\text{(required position)} - F}{f} \times w\] where \(L\) = lower class boundary; \(F\) = cumulative frequency before this class; \(f\) = frequency of this class; \(w\) = class width.
This formula assumes data is uniformly spread within each class.
STEP 1

What we're doing: Find \(Q_2\) = 60th value. Build cumulative frequencies:

After 40–42: \(F = 12\)
After 42–45: \(F = 12 + 18 = 30\)
After 45–50: \(F = 30 + 23 = 53\)
After 50–55: \(F = 53 + 35 = 88\)

The 60th value lies in the 50–55 class (since \(53 < 60 \leq 88\)). Here \(L = 50\), \(F = 53\), \(f = 35\), \(w = 5\).

\[Q_2 = 50 + \frac{60 - 53}{35} \times 5 = 50 + \frac{7}{35} \times 5 = 50 + \frac{35}{35} = 50 + 1 = 51\]
After the 45–50 class we have 53 values. We need \(60 - 53 = 7\) more to reach the median. The 50–55 class has 35 values spread across 5 cm, so we travel \(\tfrac{7}{35} = \tfrac{1}{5}\) of the way through the 5 cm width: \(\tfrac{1}{5} \times 5 = 1\) cm beyond the lower boundary of 50.
\(\boxed{Q_2 = 51 \text{ cm}}\)
STEP 2

What we're doing: Find \(Q_3\) = 90th value, then compute IQR.

From cumulative frequencies: after 50–55, \(F = 88\). The 90th value lies in the 55–58 class (since \(88 < 90 \leq 88 + 24 = 112\)). Here \(L = 55\), \(F = 88\), \(f = 24\), \(w = 3\).

\[Q_3 = 55 + \frac{90 - 88}{24} \times 3 = 55 + \frac{2}{24} \times 3 = 55 + \frac{6}{24} = 55 + \frac{1}{4} = 55.25\]

Now compute IQR using \(Q_1 = 45\) (given) and \(Q_3 = 55.25\): \[\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1 = 55.25 - 45 = 10.25 \text{ cm}\]

After 88 values, we need the \(90 - 88 = 2\)nd value from the 55–58 class. This class has 24 values spread across 3 cm, so we travel \(\tfrac{2}{24}\) of the way through: \(\tfrac{2}{24} \times 3 = 0.25\) cm above the lower boundary of 55. The IQR = 10.25 cm means the central 50% of tulip stem lengths span about 10 cm.
\(\boxed{Q_3 = 55.25 \text{ cm},\quad \text{IQR} = 10.25 \text{ cm}}\)

(d) Skewness measure [2 marks · 1 step]

Key Concept — Quartile-Based Skewness Measure
The formula \(\dfrac{Q_3 - Q_2 - (Q_2 - Q_1)}{Q_3 - Q_1}\) measures asymmetry using quartiles:
  • \(Q_3 - Q_2\) = upper spread (distance from median to upper quartile)
  • \(Q_2 - Q_1\) = lower spread (distance from median to lower quartile)
  • Numerator = upper spread minus lower spread: positive if the upper half is more spread out, negative if the lower half is more spread out.
  • Denominator \(Q_3 - Q_1 = \text{IQR}\) normalises the result.
\[\text{Skewness} > 0 \Rightarrow \text{positive (right) skew};\quad \text{Skewness} < 0 \Rightarrow \text{negative (left) skew}\]
STEP 1

What we're doing: Substitute \(Q_1 = 45\), \(Q_2 = 51\), \(Q_3 = 55.25\) into the skewness formula and interpret the sign.

Compute each component:
Upper spread: \(Q_3 - Q_2 = 55.25 - 51 = 4.25\)
Lower spread: \(Q_2 - Q_1 = 51 - 45 = 6\)
Numerator: \(4.25 - 6 = -1.75\)
Denominator (IQR): \(Q_3 - Q_1 = 55.25 - 45 = 10.25\)

\[\text{Skewness} = \frac{-1.75}{10.25} \approx -0.171\]

Since skewness \(< 0\), the distribution is negatively skewed (left-skewed).

The lower spread (6 cm from \(Q_1\) to \(Q_2\)) is larger than the upper spread (4.25 cm from \(Q_2\) to \(Q_3\)). This means the bottom half of the data (below the median) is more spread out than the top half — the tail extends further to the left. A negative numerator confirms negative (left) skew.
\(\boxed{\text{Skewness} \approx -0.171 \Rightarrow \text{negatively skewed (left-skewed)}}\)
