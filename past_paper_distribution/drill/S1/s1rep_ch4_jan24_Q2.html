Kolkata Temperatures — PMCC & Linear Coding

Question

The average minimum monthly temperature, $x$°F, and average maximum monthly temperature, $y$°F, in Kolkata were recorded for 12 months.

Summary statistics: $\Sigma x = 862$, $\Sigma x^2 = 62802$, $S_{yy} = 413.67$, $S_{xy} = 512.67$, $n = 12$

Conversion formula: $c = \frac{5}{9}(f - 32)$

(a) (i) Find $\bar{x}$    (ii) Show $\sigma = 8.57$°F    (b) Find PMCC $r$    (c) Find mean and SD in °C    (d) State PMCC between $u$ and $v$ (°C) with reason

[12 marks]

(a) [3 marks] · 2 steps

Mean and Standard Deviation from Summary Statistics
The mean is: \[\bar{x} = \frac{\Sigma x}{n}\] The corrected sum of squares is: \[S_{xx} = \Sigma x^2 - \frac{(\Sigma x)^2}{n}\] This is equivalent to \(S_{xx} = \Sigma(x - \bar{x})^2\) — the total squared deviation from the mean.
The population standard deviation (when all values are recorded, not a sample) is: \[\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{S_{xx}}{n}}\] Note: Use \(\sigma = \sqrt{S_{xx}/n}\) (population SD), not \(\sqrt{S_{xx}/(n-1)}\) (sample SD), when the question gives the complete dataset (all 12 months).
STEP 1

Find \(\bar{x}\)

\[\bar{x} = \frac{\Sigma x}{n} = \frac{862}{12} = 71.8\overline{3}\text{°F}\]
Exam pitfall: Do not round \(\bar{x}\) prematurely — keep the full recurring decimal \(71.8\overline{3}\) for use in subsequent steps. Rounding early can cause errors in SD calculation.
$\boxed{\bar{x} = 71.8\overline{3}\text{°F}}$
From STEP 1:$\bar{x} = 71.8\overline{3}$°F
STEP 2

Calculate \(S_{xx}\), then show \(\sigma = 8.57\)°F

\[S_{xx} = \Sigma x^2 - \frac{(\Sigma x)^2}{n} = 62802 - \frac{862^2}{12}\] \[= 62802 - \frac{743044}{12} = 62802 - 61920.\overline{3} = 881.6\overline{7}\]

Now find the population standard deviation:

\[\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{S_{xx}}{n}} = \sqrt{\frac{881.6\overline{7}}{12}} = \sqrt{73.47\overline{2}} = 8.571\ldots \approx 8.57\text{°F} \checkmark\]
Why population SD, not sample SD? The 12 months recorded constitute the complete dataset (not a sample from a larger population). So we divide by \(n=12\), not \(n-1=11\).
$S_{xx} = 881.6\overline{7};\quad \sigma = 8.57$°F ✓ (shown)

(b) [3 marks] · 2 steps

Product Moment Correlation Coefficient (PMCC)
The PMCC measures the strength and direction of linear association between two variables: \[r = \frac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx} \cdot S_{yy}}} \qquad \text{where} -1 \leq r \leq 1\]
  • \(r\) close to \(+1\): strong positive linear correlation
  • \(r\) close to \(-1\): strong negative linear correlation
  • \(r\) close to \(0\): weak/no linear correlation
Always use \(S_{xy}\), \(S_{xx}\), \(S_{yy}\) (the corrected sums), not the raw \(\Sigma xy\), \(\Sigma x^2\), \(\Sigma y^2\).
From (a): $S_{xx} = 881.6\overline{7}$; given: $S_{yy} = 413.67$, $S_{xy} = 512.67$
STEP 1

Set up the PMCC formula with the known values

\[r = \frac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx} \cdot S_{yy}}} = \frac{512.67}{\sqrt{881.6\overline{7} \times 413.67}}\]

Compute the product under the square root:

\[881.6\overline{7} \times 413.67 = 364{,}897.7\ldots\]
Exam pitfall: Make sure you use \(S_{xy}\) (not \(\Sigma xy\)) and both \(S_{xx}\), \(S_{yy}\) — not raw \(\Sigma x^2\), \(\Sigma y^2\). The \(S\)-quantities have the mean correction already applied.
Numerator $= 512.67$; denominator $= \sqrt{881.67 \times 413.67}$
From STEP 1:denominator $= \sqrt{364{,}897.7\ldots}$
STEP 2

Complete the calculation

\[\sqrt{364{,}897.7} \approx 604.1\] \[r = \frac{512.67}{604.1} = 0.8486\ldots \approx 0.849\]
Interpretation: \(r = 0.849\) indicates a strong positive linear correlation between Kolkata's minimum and maximum monthly temperatures — as the minimum temperature rises, the maximum tends to rise too, and quite strongly.
$\boxed{r = 0.849}$ (3 s.f.) — strong positive linear correlation

(c) [4 marks] · 2 steps

Linear Transformations of Mean and Standard Deviation
If \(c = af + b\) (a linear transformation), then: \[E(c) = \bar{c} = a\bar{f} + b \qquad \text{(the mean transforms the same way)}\] \[\text{SD}(c) = |a| \cdot \text{SD}(f) \qquad \text{(additive constant } b \text{ has NO effect on spread)}\] Intuition: Shifting all values by a constant \(b\) moves the whole distribution but doesn't change how spread out it is. Only the multiplicative scale factor \(a\) changes the spread.
The conversion from Fahrenheit to Celsius is \(c = \frac{5}{9}(f - 32) = \frac{5}{9}f - \frac{160}{9}\), so \(a = \frac{5}{9}\), \(b = -\frac{160}{9}\).
From (a): $\bar{x} = 71.8\overline{3}$°F, $\sigma_x = 8.571$°F; conversion: $c = \frac{5}{9}(f - 32)$
STEP 1

Find the mean temperature in °C

Apply the linear transformation to the mean:

\[\bar{c} = \frac{5}{9}(\bar{f} - 32) = \frac{5}{9}(71.8\overline{3} - 32) = \frac{5}{9}(39.8\overline{3})\] \[= \frac{5 \times 39.8\overline{3}}{9} = \frac{199.1\overline{6}}{9} = 22.13\ldots\text{°C}\]
Why subtract 32 first? The transformation is \(c = \frac{5}{9}(f-32)\). We must apply the entire formula to the mean — not just multiply \(\bar{f}\) by \(\frac{5}{9}\).
$\boxed{\bar{c} \approx 22.1\text{°C}}$
From STEP 1:$\bar{c} \approx 22.1$°C
STEP 2

Find the standard deviation in °C

The transformation is \(c = af + b\) where \(a = \frac{5}{9}\) and \(b = -\frac{160}{9}\).

Only the multiplicative factor scales the SD:

\[\sigma_c = |a| \times \sigma_f = \frac{5}{9} \times 8.571 = 4.762\text{°C}\]
Key principle: The constant shift \(-32\) (or equivalently \(b = -\frac{160}{9}\)) shifts every value by the same amount. This means the distances between values are unchanged — so the SD is unaffected by \(b\). Only \(a = \frac{5}{9}\) scales the spread.
$\boxed{\sigma_c \approx 4.76\text{°C}}$

(d) [2 marks] · 1 step

PMCC is Invariant Under Linear Coding
If \(u = af + b\) and \(v = cy + d\) are linear transformations of \(x\) and \(y\), then: \[r_{uv} = r_{xy} \quad \text{if } a \text{ and } c \text{ have the same sign}\] \[r_{uv} = -r_{xy} \quad \text{if } a \text{ and } c \text{ have opposite signs}\] Intuition: The PMCC measures the pattern of linear association between the variables. A linear rescaling/shift doesn't change the pattern — only a reflection (negative scale factor) would flip the direction.
Here: \(u = \frac{5}{9}(x-32)\) and \(v = \frac{5}{9}(y-32)\), both with scale factor \(\frac{5}{9} > 0\) — so the PMCC is unchanged.
From (b): $r = 0.849$ between $x$°F and $y$°F; $u = \frac{5}{9}(x-32)$, $v = \frac{5}{9}(y-32)$
STEP 1

State the PMCC between \(u\) and \(v\) and give a reason

Both \(u = \frac{5}{9}(x-32)\) and \(v = \frac{5}{9}(y-32)\) are linear transformations with positive scale factor \(a = +\frac{5}{9}\).

The PMCC is invariant under linear coding (provided scale factors have the same sign).

\[r_{uv} = r_{xy} = 0.849\]
Exam pitfall: If either scale factor were negative, the PMCC would flip sign. For example, if \(u = -x\), then \(r_{uv} = -0.849\). Here both scale factors are \(+\frac{5}{9} > 0\), so the sign is preserved.
$\boxed{r_{uv} = 0.849}$ — PMCC is invariant under linear coding with positive scale factors
