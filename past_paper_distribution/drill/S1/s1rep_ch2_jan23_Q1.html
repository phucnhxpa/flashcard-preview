Histogram — Times to Swim 500 m (100 People)

Question

The histogram shows the times taken, $t$ minutes, by each of 100 people to swim 500 metres.

(a) Use the histogram to complete the frequency table. [1]

Time ($t$ min)5 – 1010 – 1414 – 1818 – 2525 – 40
Frequency101624  

(b) Estimate the number of people who took less than 16 minutes to swim 500 metres. [2]

(c) Find an estimate for the mean time taken to swim 500 metres. [2]

Given that $\sum ft^2 = 41\,033$

(d) find an estimate for the standard deviation of the times taken to swim 500 metres. [2]

Given that $Q_3 = 23$

(e) use linear interpolation to estimate the interquartile range of the times taken to swim 500 metres. [3]

[10 marks]

(a) Complete the frequency table [1 mark · 1 step]

Key Concept — Frequency Density in Histograms
When class widths are unequal, a histogram uses frequency density (not frequency) on the vertical axis. This ensures that the area of each bar equals the frequency — otherwise a wider bar would look artificially taller even if it contained fewer people. \[\text{Frequency Density} = \frac{\text{Frequency}}{\text{Class Width}} \qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad \text{Frequency} = \text{Frequency Density} \times \text{Class Width}\] To find a missing frequency: read the bar's frequency density from the histogram, then multiply by the class width.
STEP 1

What we're doing: Three frequencies are given: \(10 + 16 + 24 = 50\). Since total \(n = 100\), the remaining two classes account for \(100 - 50 = 50\) people. We recover each missing frequency by reading the bar's frequency density from the histogram and multiplying by the class width.

Class 18–25 (width \(= 25 - 18 = 7\)): frequency density read from histogram \(= 4\), so: \[\text{Frequency} = 4 \times 7 = 28\]

Class 25–40 (width \(= 40 - 25 = 15\)): remaining people \(= 50 - 28 = 22\). Verify via histogram fd \(\approx 1.47\): \(1.47 \times 15 \approx 22\).

Check: \(10 + 16 + 24 + 28 + 22 = 100\) ✓

We use frequency density — not frequency — on the vertical axis because the class widths differ. The 25–40 class spans 15 minutes; without the density adjustment, its bar would be disproportionately tall relative to its actual share of people. Using fd = freq/width ensures area = frequency for every bar, making areas directly comparable.
\(\boxed{18\text{–}25: 28 \text{ people},\quad 25\text{–}40: 22 \text{ people}}\)

(b) Estimate people with t < 16 min [2 marks · 1 step]

Key Concept — Proportional Estimation Within a Class
When a boundary (here \(t = 16\)) falls inside a class (here 14–18), we cannot know exactly how many people are on each side. We assume data is uniformly (evenly) distributed across the whole class interval. So the fraction of the class below \(x\) is: \[\text{Estimated frequency below } x = \frac{x - \text{lower boundary}}{\text{class width}} \times \text{class frequency}\] Classes that lie entirely below 16 contribute their full frequency; the class straddling 16 contributes a proportional part.
STEP 1

What we're doing: Identify which classes are entirely below 16 (take full frequency), which straddle 16 (take proportional part), and which are entirely above 16 (ignore).

• 5–10: entirely below 16. Contributes all \(10\) people.

• 10–14: entirely below 16. Contributes all \(16\) people.

• 14–18 (frequency \(= 24\), width \(= 4\)): straddles 16. We want the sub-interval 14 to 16, which has width \(16 - 14 = 2\): \[\text{Estimate} = \frac{16 - 14}{18 - 14} \times 24 = \frac{2}{4} \times 24 = 12 \text{ people}\]

• 18–25 and 25–40: entirely above 16. Contribute 0.

Total: \[10 + 16 + 12 = 38\]

We assume the 24 people in the 14–18 class are spread uniformly across the 4-minute interval. The sub-interval 14–16 is half of the full 4-minute span, so we estimate half of 24 = 12 people lie within it. This is an approximation — the true number could differ slightly.
\(\boxed{38 \text{ people}}\)

(c) Estimate mean time [2 marks · 1 step]

Key Concept — Mean of Grouped Data Using Midpoints
For grouped data, the exact value of each observation is unknown — we only know its class. We estimate by assuming all observations in a class take the class midpoint \(m = \tfrac{\text{lower} + \text{upper}}{2}\). The estimated mean is then: \[\bar{t} = \frac{\sum f \cdot m}{\sum f} = \frac{\sum f \cdot m}{n}\] where \(f\) is the frequency of each class and \(n\) is the total number of observations.
STEP 1

What we're doing: Calculate each class midpoint, multiply by frequency to get \(f \cdot m\) for each class, sum all products, then divide by \(n = 100\).

Class midpoints: \[m_1 = \tfrac{5+10}{2} = 7.5,\quad m_2 = \tfrac{10+14}{2} = 12,\quad m_3 = \tfrac{14+18}{2} = 16,\quad m_4 = \tfrac{18+25}{2} = 21.5,\quad m_5 = \tfrac{25+40}{2} = 32.5\]

Compute \(\sum f \cdot m\): \[\sum f \cdot m = 10(7.5) + 16(12) + 24(16) + 28(21.5) + 22(32.5)\] \[= 75 + 192 + 384 + 602 + 715 = 1968\]

Mean: \[\bar{t} = \frac{1968}{100} = 19.68\]

Using the midpoint is the fairest single estimate for every value in a class — it is equidistant from both boundaries. If data were truly uniform within each class, the midpoint would be exactly the class average. Any deviation from uniformity makes this an approximation.
\(\boxed{\bar{t} \approx 19.68 \text{ minutes}}\)

(d) Estimate standard deviation [2 marks · 1 step]

\(\sum ft^2 = 41\,033,\quad n = 100,\quad \bar{t} = 19.68\) (from part c)
Key Concept — Standard Deviation of Grouped Data
The standard deviation \(\sigma\) measures how far values typically lie from the mean. For grouped data, using midpoints for \(t\): \[\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\sum f \cdot t^2}{n} - \bar{t}^2}\] This comes from the variance identity: \(\text{Var}(t) = E(t^2) - [E(t)]^2\). Here \(\dfrac{\sum f \cdot t^2}{n}\) is the mean of \(t^2\) (average of squared midpoints weighted by frequency), and \(\bar{t}^2\) is the square of the mean. Taking the square root gives units back in minutes.
STEP 1

What we're doing: Substitute the given values into the standard deviation formula. \(\sum ft^2 = 41\,033\) is provided by the question (it has been pre-calculated using the midpoints squared times frequencies). We use \(n = 100\) and \(\bar{t} = 19.68\) from part (c).

\[\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\sum ft^2}{n} - \bar{t}^2} = \sqrt{\frac{41\,033}{100} - (19.68)^2}\] \[= \sqrt{410.33 - 387.3024}\] \[= \sqrt{23.0276}\] \[\approx 4.80\]
The term \(\tfrac{\sum ft^2}{n} = 410.33\) is the average of the squared swim times. Subtracting \(\bar{t}^2 = 387.30\) removes the contribution of the mean, leaving only the spread around it. The square root restores the units to minutes, giving a measure of typical deviation from the mean of about 4.8 minutes.
\(\boxed{\sigma \approx 4.80 \text{ minutes}}\)

(e) Estimate IQR by linear interpolation [3 marks · 2 steps]

\(Q_3 = 23\) minutes (given)
Key Concept — Quartiles and Linear Interpolation
The lower quartile \(Q_1\) is the value below which 25% of data falls — it is at position \(\tfrac{n}{4}\) in the ordered dataset. For \(n = 100\): \(Q_1\) is at the 25th value.
The interquartile range \(\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1\) measures the spread of the middle 50% of the data.

Since data is grouped, we use linear interpolation to estimate \(Q_1\): \[Q_1 = L + \frac{\tfrac{n}{4} - F}{f} \times w\] where: \(L\) = lower class boundary of the class containing \(Q_1\); \(F\) = cumulative frequency before that class; \(f\) = frequency of that class; \(w\) = class width.
Intuition: we travel \(\tfrac{\text{how many more values we need}}{\text{how many values the class contains}}\) of the way through the class width.
STEP 1

What we're doing: Locate the 25th value using cumulative frequencies, then apply linear interpolation to estimate \(Q_1\).

Cumulative frequencies:
After 5–10: \(F = 10\) (covers positions 1–10)
After 10–14: \(F = 10 + 16 = 26\) (covers positions 11–26)

The 25th value lies in the 10–14 class (since 10 < 25 ≤ 26). We have \(L = 10\), \(F = 10\) (before this class), \(f = 16\), \(w = 4\).

Applying the formula: \[Q_1 = 10 + \frac{25 - 10}{16} \times 4 = 10 + \frac{15}{16} \times 4 = 10 + \frac{60}{16} = 10 + 3.75 = 13.75\]

After the 5–10 class we have 10 values. We still need \(25 - 10 = 15\) more to reach the 25th. The 10–14 class contains 16 values spread across 4 minutes, so we travel \(\tfrac{15}{16}\) of the way through its 4-minute width: \(\tfrac{15}{16} \times 4 = 3.75\) minutes into the class from its lower boundary of 10.
\(\boxed{Q_1 = 13.75 \text{ minutes}}\)
STEP 2

What we're doing: Subtract \(Q_1\) from the given \(Q_3\) to get the interquartile range.

\[\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1 = 23 - 13.75 = 9.25\]
The IQR is the distance between the 75th and 25th percentiles — the range that contains the middle half of the dataset. An IQR of 9.25 minutes means the central 50% of swimmers' times span roughly 9.25 minutes, indicating moderate spread in swim times.
\(\boxed{\text{IQR} = 9.25 \text{ minutes}}\)
