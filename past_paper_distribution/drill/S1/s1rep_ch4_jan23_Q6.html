Sugar Dissolving — Regression & Correlation

Question

A research student is investigating the maximum weight, $y$ grams, of sugar that will dissolve in 100 grams of water at various temperatures, $x\,^{\circ}\text{C}$, where $10 \leq x \leq 80$.

The research student calculated the regression line of $y$ on $x$:

$\hat{y} = 151.2 + 2.72x$

(a) Give an interpretation of the gradient of the regression line. [1]

(b) Use the regression line to estimate the maximum weight of sugar that will dissolve in 100 grams of water when the temperature is 90°C. [2]

(c) Comment on the reliability of your estimate, giving a reason for your answer. [2]

Using the regression line of $y$ on $x$ and the following summary statistics:

$\Sigma y = 3119,\quad \Sigma y^2 = 851093,\quad \Sigma x^2 = 24500,\quad n = 12$

(d) Show that the product moment correlation coefficient for these data is $0.988$ to 3 decimal places. [7]

(e) With reference to both the scatter diagram and the correlation coefficient, discuss the suitability of a linear regression model to describe the relationship between $x$ and $y$. [2]

[15 marks]

(a) [1 mark] · 1 step

Regression Line Formula
The regression line of \(y\) on \(x\) has the form: \[\hat{y} = a + bx\] where the gradient \(b = \dfrac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}}\) tells you: for every 1 unit increase in \(x\), the predicted value of \(y\) changes by \(b\) units.
The intercept \(a\) is the predicted value when \(x = 0\) (only meaningful if \(x=0\) is within the data range).
When interpreting the gradient: always refer to the context of the variables — state the units and direction.
STEP 1

The regression line is \(\hat{y} = 151.2 + 2.72x\), so the gradient is \(b = 2.72\).

This gradient means: for each 1°C increase in temperature, the maximum weight of sugar that will dissolve in 100 g of water increases by 2.72 grams on average.

Why this phrasing? The gradient is the rate of change of \(y\) per unit of \(x\). A positive gradient means \(y\) increases as \(x\) increases. Always say "on average" or "predicted" since it's a model.
Gradient interpretation: +2.72 grams per 1°C increase in temperature

(b) [2 marks] · 1 step

Using the Regression Line for Prediction
To estimate \(y\) for a given value of \(x\): substitute the \(x\) value directly into the regression equation \(\hat{y} = a + bx\).
The result \(\hat{y}\) is the predicted (estimated) value. It is not exact — it is the value the model gives.
Regression line: $\hat{y} = 151.2 + 2.72x$; estimate at $x = 90$°C
STEP 1

Substitute \(x = 90\) into the regression equation:

\[\hat{y} = 151.2 + 2.72 \times 90\] \[= 151.2 + 244.8\] \[= 396 \text{ grams}\]
Why substitute directly? The regression equation is a formula that maps any \(x\) value to a predicted \(y\) value. We just plug in \(x = 90\).
$\hat{y} = \boxed{396 \text{ grams}}$

(c) [2 marks] · 1 step

Interpolation vs Extrapolation
Interpolation: predicting \(y\) for an \(x\) value within the range of the original data. Generally reliable — the linear model was fitted to this region.
Extrapolation: predicting \(y\) for an \(x\) value outside the range of the original data. Generally unreliable — we cannot assume the linear relationship continues beyond the observed range.
Always state: (1) whether inside or outside range, (2) therefore reliable or unreliable, (3) the reason.
Data range: $10 \leq x \leq 80$; estimate at $x = 90$°C
STEP 1

The data was collected for temperatures in the range \(10 \leq x \leq 80\)°C.

The estimate is at \(x = 90\)°C, which is outside this range.

Therefore we are extrapolating — the linear relationship observed between 10°C and 80°C may not continue to hold at 90°C.

Why is extrapolation unreliable? The regression line was fitted only to data in [10, 80]. We have no evidence that the linear pattern extends to 90°C — the true relationship could curve, plateau, or behave differently outside the observed region.
Unreliable — extrapolation beyond the observed range $[10, 80]$°C. The linear relationship may not hold at $x = 90$°C.

(d) [7 marks] · 5 steps

Summary Statistics & PMCC Formula
The three key summary statistics are: \[S_{xx} = \Sigma x^2 - \frac{(\Sigma x)^2}{n}, \quad S_{yy} = \Sigma y^2 - \frac{(\Sigma y)^2}{n}, \quad S_{xy} = \Sigma xy - \frac{(\Sigma x)(\Sigma y)}{n}\] Each one measures "spread" or "co-spread" after correcting for the mean. Note: \(S_{xx} = \Sigma x^2 - n\bar{x}^2\) is equivalent.
The Product Moment Correlation Coefficient (PMCC) is: \[r = \frac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx} \cdot S_{yy}}} \qquad (-1 \leq r \leq 1)\] Key trick here: We aren't given \(\Sigma x\) or \(\Sigma xy\) directly, but we can recover them from the regression line equation using \(\bar{x}\) and \(\bar{y}\), and from the gradient \(b = S_{xy}/S_{xx}\).
$\Sigma y = 3119,\;\Sigma y^2 = 851093,\;\Sigma x^2 = 24500,\;n = 12,\;\hat{y} = 151.2 + 2.72x$
STEP 1

Find \(\bar{y}\) and then \(\bar{x}\) from the regression line

\[\bar{y} = \frac{\Sigma y}{n} = \frac{3119}{12} = 259.91\overline{6}\]

Both regression lines pass through \((\bar{x}, \bar{y})\), so substituting into \(\hat{y} = 151.2 + 2.72x\):

\[\bar{y} = 151.2 + 2.72\bar{x}\] \[\bar{x} = \frac{\bar{y} - 151.2}{2.72} = \frac{259.91\overline{6} - 151.2}{2.72} = \frac{108.71\overline{6}}{2.72} \approx 39.97\]
Why can we substitute \(\bar{x}, \bar{y}\)? Every regression line of \(y\) on \(x\) passes through the mean point \((\bar{x}, \bar{y})\). This is a fundamental property — the line is anchored at the means.
$\bar{y} \approx 259.917,\quad \bar{x} \approx 39.97$
From STEP 1:$\bar{x} \approx 39.97,\;\bar{y} \approx 259.917$
STEP 2

Calculate \(S_{xx}\)

Using \(\Sigma x = n\bar{x} = 12 \times 39.97 \approx 479.6\overline{3}\) (exact: derived from regression line):

\[S_{xx} = \Sigma x^2 - \frac{(\Sigma x)^2}{n} = 24500 - \frac{(479.6\overline{3})^2}{12}\] \[= 24500 - \frac{230049.0\overline{6}}{12} = 24500 - 19170.75 \approx 5329.4\]
Why this formula? \(S_{xx} = \Sigma x^2 - n\bar{x}^2\) measures the total squared deviation of the \(x\)-values from their mean. It's the "corrected sum of squares" for \(x\).
$S_{xx} \approx 5329.4$
From STEP 1:$\bar{y} \approx 259.917,\;\Sigma y^2 = 851093,\;\Sigma y = 3119$
STEP 3

Calculate \(S_{yy}\)

\[S_{yy} = \Sigma y^2 - \frac{(\Sigma y)^2}{n} = 851093 - \frac{3119^2}{12}\] \[= 851093 - \frac{9728161}{12} = 851093 - 810680.08\overline{3}\] \[= 40412.91\overline{6} \approx 40412.92\]
Same structure as \(S_{xx}\) but using \(y\) values. This measures total squared deviation of \(y\) from \(\bar{y}\).
$S_{yy} \approx 40412.92$
From STEPs 1–2:$S_{xx} \approx 5329.4$; gradient $b = 2.72$
STEP 4

Find \(S_{xy}\) using the regression gradient

The gradient of the regression line satisfies:

\[b = \frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}} \implies S_{xy} = b \times S_{xx}\] \[S_{xy} = 2.72 \times 5329.4 = 14495.97\]
Why this works: The regression coefficient \(b\) is defined as \(b = S_{xy}/S_{xx}\). Rearranging gives \(S_{xy} = b \cdot S_{xx}\). We use the known gradient \(b = 2.72\) to recover \(S_{xy}\) even though \(\Sigma xy\) was not given.
$S_{xy} \approx 14495.97$
From STEPs 2–4:$S_{xx} \approx 5329.4,\;S_{yy} \approx 40412.92,\;S_{xy} \approx 14495.97$
STEP 5

Apply the PMCC formula

\[r = \frac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx} \cdot S_{yy}}} = \frac{14495.97}{\sqrt{5329.4 \times 40412.92}}\] \[= \frac{14495.97}{\sqrt{215{,}408{,}015}} = \frac{14495.97}{14677.5}\] \[\approx 0.9876\ldots \approx 0.988 \checkmark\]
Check your answer: \(r\) must satisfy \(-1 \leq r \leq 1\). A value of \(r = 0.988\) is close to 1, indicating very strong positive linear correlation — consistent with the data.
$r = \boxed{0.988}$ (3 d.p.) $\checkmark$

(e) [2 marks] · 1 step

Interpreting the PMCC
The PMCC \(r\) measures the strength and direction of linear association:
  • \(r \approx +1\): strong positive linear correlation — as \(x\) increases, \(y\) increases closely along a line
  • \(r \approx -1\): strong negative linear correlation
  • \(r \approx 0\): little or no linear correlation
To assess model suitability, use both: (1) the value of \(r\), and (2) the pattern in the scatter diagram (no obvious curve, points close to the line).
$r = 0.988$; scatter diagram shows points close to the regression line
STEP 1

Assess suitability of a linear model using both pieces of evidence:

Evidence 1 — PMCC: \(r = 0.988 \approx 1\), which indicates a very strong positive linear correlation between temperature and maximum weight of dissolved sugar.

Evidence 2 — Scatter diagram: The data points lie close to the regression line with no obvious curved pattern, confirming the linear model fits well.

Why cite both? The question asks you to refer to both the scatter diagram AND the correlation coefficient. A high \(|r|\) alone is necessary but not sufficient — the scatter diagram confirms there is no systematic non-linear pattern (like a curve).
Linear model is suitable/appropriate — both \(r = 0.988\) (very strong linear correlation) and the scatter diagram (points close to line, no curve) support this conclusion.
