Q6 · Regression and Correlation

Question

The variables $x$ and $y$ have regression equations based on the same 12 observations:

  • $y$ on $x$: $y = 1.4x + 1.5$
  • $x$ on $y$: $x = 1.2 + 0.2y$

(a)(i) Find the point of intersection of these lines.

  (ii) Hence show that $\bar{x} = \frac{25}{12}$

Given that $\Sigma xy = \frac{6961}{60}$

(b) Find $S_{xy}$

(c) Find the product moment correlation coefficient between $x$ and $y$

[12 marks]

(a) [4 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2

Formula: Rearrange $x = 1.2 + 0.2y$ to express $y$ in terms of $x$, then substitute into the other equation.

Working:

From $x = 1.2 + 0.2y$:

$$0.2y = x - 1.2$$

$$y = 5x - 6$$

Substitute into $y = 1.4x + 1.5$:

$$5x - 6 = 1.4x + 1.5$$

$$5x - 1.4x = 1.5 + 6$$

$$3.6x = 7.5$$

$$x = \frac{7.5}{3.6} = \frac{75}{36} = \frac{25}{12}$$

Substitute back: $y = 5 \times \frac{25}{12} - 6 = \frac{125}{12} - \frac{72}{12} = \frac{53}{12}$

Why? The intersection point satisfies both regression equations simultaneously.

$\boxed{\left(\frac{25}{12}, \frac{53}{12}\right)}$
Carry-in from previous stepIntersection point $= \left(\frac{25}{12}, \frac{53}{12}\right)$
STEP 2/2

Key property: Both regression lines pass through the point $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$.

Working:

Since the intersection point is $(\frac{25}{12}, \frac{53}{12})$ and both regression lines pass through $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$:

$$\bar{x} = \frac{25}{12}, \quad \bar{y} = \frac{53}{12}$$

Why? The regression line of $y$ on $x$ and the regression line of $x$ on $y$ always intersect at the mean point $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$.

$\boxed{\bar{x} = \frac{25}{12}}$ as required

(b) [4 marks] · 1 step

$\Sigma xy = \frac{6961}{60}$
STEP 1/1

Formula: $S_{xy} = \Sigma xy - \frac{(\Sigma x)(\Sigma y)}{n}$

Working:

First find $\Sigma x$ and $\Sigma y$:

$$\Sigma x = n\bar{x} = 12 \times \frac{25}{12} = 25$$

$$\Sigma y = n\bar{y} = 12 \times \frac{53}{12} = 53$$

Now apply the formula:

$$S_{xy} = \frac{6961}{60} - \frac{25 \times 53}{12}$$

$$S_{xy} = \frac{6961}{60} - \frac{1325}{12}$$

$$S_{xy} = \frac{6961}{60} - \frac{6625}{60}$$

$$S_{xy} = \frac{336}{60} = 5.6$$

Why? $S_{xy}$ measures the covariance sum, calculated by subtracting the correction factor from $\Sigma xy$.

$\boxed{S_{xy} = 5.6}$

(c) [4 marks] · 3 steps

$S_{xy} = 5.6$
STEP 1/3

Formula: $b_{yx} = \frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}}$ where $b_{yx}$ is the gradient of the $y$ on $x$ line.

Working:

From $y = 1.4x + 1.5$, we have $b_{yx} = 1.4$

$$1.4 = \frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}} = \frac{5.6}{S_{xx}}$$

$$S_{xx} = \frac{5.6}{1.4} = 4$$

Why? The regression coefficient $b_{yx}$ equals the ratio $S_{xy}/S_{xx}$.

$\boxed{S_{xx} = 4}$
Carry-in from previous step$S_{xx} = 4$
STEP 2/3

Formula: $b_{xy} = \frac{S_{xy}}{S_{yy}}$ where $b_{xy}$ is the gradient of the $x$ on $y$ line.

Working:

From $x = 1.2 + 0.2y$, we have $b_{xy} = 0.2$

$$0.2 = \frac{S_{xy}}{S_{yy}} = \frac{5.6}{S_{yy}}$$

$$S_{yy} = \frac{5.6}{0.2} = 28$$

Why? The regression coefficient $b_{xy}$ equals the ratio $S_{xy}/S_{yy}$.

$\boxed{S_{yy} = 28}$
Carry-in from previous step$S_{xx} = 4$, $S_{yy} = 28$, $S_{xy} = 5.6$
STEP 3/3

Formula: $r = \frac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx} \times S_{yy}}}$

Working:

$$r = \frac{5.6}{\sqrt{4 \times 28}}$$

$$r = \frac{5.6}{\sqrt{112}}$$

$$r = \frac{5.6}{10.583...}$$

$$r = 0.529...$$

Why? The PMCC measures linear correlation strength. Here $r > 0$ confirms positive correlation, consistent with both regression coefficients being positive.

$\boxed{r = 0.529}$ (3 s.f.)
