Q6 · Regression and Correlation

Question

The variables $x$ and $y$ have regression equations based on the same 12 observations:

  • $y$ on $x$: $y = 1.4x + 1.5$
  • $x$ on $y$: $x = 1.2 + 0.2y$

(a)(i) Find the point of intersection of these lines.

  (ii) Hence show that $\bar{x} = \frac{25}{12}$

Given that $\Sigma xy = \frac{6961}{60}$

(b) Find $S_{xy}$

(c) Find the product moment correlation coefficient between $x$ and $y$

[12 marks]

(a) [4 marks] · 2 steps

Key Property: Both Regression Lines Pass Through $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$
There are two regression lines:
  • The regression line of \(y\) on \(x\): minimises vertical distances from points to the line. Used to predict \(y\) from \(x\). Gradient \(= \dfrac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}}\).
  • The regression line of \(x\) on \(y\): minimises horizontal distances. Used to predict \(x\) from \(y\). Gradient \(= \dfrac{S_{xy}}{S_{yy}}\).
Fundamental property: Both regression lines pass through the mean point \((\bar{x}, \bar{y})\). This means their intersection gives the means directly.
STEP 1

Find the intersection point by solving simultaneously

From \(x = 1.2 + 0.2y\), rearrange to get \(y\) in terms of \(x\):

\[0.2y = x - 1.2 \implies y = 5x - 6\]

Substitute into \(y = 1.4x + 1.5\):

\[5x - 6 = 1.4x + 1.5\] \[5x - 1.4x = 1.5 + 6\] \[3.6x = 7.5\] \[x = \frac{7.5}{3.6} = \frac{75}{36} = \frac{25}{12}\]

Substitute back: \(y = 5 \times \frac{25}{12} - 6 = \frac{125}{12} - \frac{72}{12} = \frac{53}{12}\)

Why solve simultaneously? The intersection point is the unique \((x, y)\) pair that satisfies both equations at once. We substitute one equation into the other to eliminate one variable.
Intersection point $= \left(\dfrac{25}{12},\, \dfrac{53}{12}\right)$
From STEP 1:Intersection $= \left(\frac{25}{12}, \frac{53}{12}\right)$
STEP 2

Use the mean-point property to identify \(\bar{x}\) and \(\bar{y}\)

Since both regression lines pass through \((\bar{x}, \bar{y})\), and their intersection is \(\left(\frac{25}{12}, \frac{53}{12}\right)\):

\[\bar{x} = \frac{25}{12}, \qquad \bar{y} = \frac{53}{12}\]
Why does this work? The regression line of \(y\) on \(x\) is constructed so it passes through \((\bar{x}, \bar{y})\) — this is a consequence of the least-squares method. The same is true for the \(x\) on \(y\) line. Since both pass through the same point, their intersection must be \((\bar{x}, \bar{y})\).
$\boxed{\bar{x} = \dfrac{25}{12}}$ as required $\checkmark$, and $\bar{y} = \dfrac{53}{12}$

(b) [4 marks] · 1 step

Sxy — The Corrected Sum of Cross-Products
\[S_{xy} = \Sigma xy - \frac{(\Sigma x)(\Sigma y)}{n}\] This measures the total "co-deviation" of \(x\) and \(y\) from their respective means. It is equivalent to \(S_{xy} = \Sigma(x - \bar{x})(y - \bar{y})\), but computed more efficiently from summary statistics.
Strategy here: We're given \(\Sigma xy\), and we can find \(\Sigma x = n\bar{x}\) and \(\Sigma y = n\bar{y}\) from the means already found.
$\Sigma xy = \frac{6961}{60}$; from (a): $\bar{x} = \frac{25}{12}$, $\bar{y} = \frac{53}{12}$, $n = 12$
STEP 1

Compute \(\Sigma x\) and \(\Sigma y\), then apply the formula

\[\Sigma x = n\bar{x} = 12 \times \frac{25}{12} = 25\] \[\Sigma y = n\bar{y} = 12 \times \frac{53}{12} = 53\]

Now apply the formula:

\[S_{xy} = \Sigma xy - \frac{(\Sigma x)(\Sigma y)}{n} = \frac{6961}{60} - \frac{25 \times 53}{12}\] \[= \frac{6961}{60} - \frac{1325}{12} = \frac{6961}{60} - \frac{6625}{60} = \frac{336}{60} = 5.6\]
Why convert to the same denominator? To subtract fractions, they need a common denominator: \(\frac{1325}{12} = \frac{6625}{60}\). Then \(\frac{6961 - 6625}{60} = \frac{336}{60} = 5.6\).
$\boxed{S_{xy} = 5.6}$

(c) [4 marks] · 3 steps

Finding Sxx and Syy from the Regression Gradients
The gradient of the regression line of \(y\) on \(x\) is: \[b_{yx} = \frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}} \implies S_{xx} = \frac{S_{xy}}{b_{yx}}\] The gradient of the regression line of \(x\) on \(y\) is: \[b_{xy} = \frac{S_{xy}}{S_{yy}} \implies S_{yy} = \frac{S_{xy}}{b_{xy}}\] Once we have \(S_{xx}\), \(S_{yy}\), \(S_{xy}\), we can compute: \[r = \frac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx} \cdot S_{yy}}} \qquad \text{or equivalently: } r^2 = b_{yx} \times b_{xy}\]
$S_{xy} = 5.6$; $y$ on $x$ gradient $= 1.4$; $x$ on $y$ gradient $= 0.2$
STEP 1

Find \(S_{xx}\) from the \(y\) on \(x\) gradient

From \(y = 1.4x + 1.5\), the gradient is \(b_{yx} = 1.4\).

\[b_{yx} = \frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}} \implies 1.4 = \frac{5.6}{S_{xx}}\] \[S_{xx} = \frac{5.6}{1.4} = 4\]
Why? The regression gradient of \(y\) on \(x\) is defined as \(b_{yx} = S_{xy}/S_{xx}\). This is the ratio that minimises the sum of squared vertical residuals. Rearranging gives \(S_{xx}\).
$\boxed{S_{xx} = 4}$
From STEP 1:$S_{xx} = 4$
STEP 2

Find \(S_{yy}\) from the \(x\) on \(y\) gradient

From \(x = 1.2 + 0.2y\), the gradient is \(b_{xy} = 0.2\).

\[b_{xy} = \frac{S_{xy}}{S_{yy}} \implies 0.2 = \frac{5.6}{S_{yy}}\] \[S_{yy} = \frac{5.6}{0.2} = 28\]
Symmetry: Just as \(b_{yx} = S_{xy}/S_{xx}\), the gradient of \(x\) on \(y\) is \(b_{xy} = S_{xy}/S_{yy}\) — the roles of \(x\) and \(y\) are swapped.
$\boxed{S_{yy} = 28}$
From STEPs 1–2:$S_{xx} = 4$, $S_{yy} = 28$, $S_{xy} = 5.6$
STEP 3

Compute the PMCC

\[r = \frac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx} \times S_{yy}}} = \frac{5.6}{\sqrt{4 \times 28}} = \frac{5.6}{\sqrt{112}}\] \[= \frac{5.6}{10.583\ldots} = 0.529\ldots \approx 0.529\]

Quick check: Also, \(r^2 = b_{yx} \times b_{xy} = 1.4 \times 0.2 = 0.28\), so \(r = \sqrt{0.28} = 0.529\) ✓ (positive since both gradients are positive).

Interpretation: \(r = 0.529\) indicates a moderate positive linear correlation between \(x\) and \(y\). The relationship is positively associated but not very tightly linear.
$\boxed{r = 0.529}$ (3 s.f.) — moderate positive linear correlation
