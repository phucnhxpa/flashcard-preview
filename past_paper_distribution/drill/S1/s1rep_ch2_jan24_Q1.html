Histogram — Heights of 408 Plants

Question

The histogram below shows the distribution of the heights, to the nearest cm, of 408 plants.

(a) Use the histogram to complete the following table. [2]

Frequency32152  120

(b) Use interpolation to estimate the median. [2]

The mean height of these plants is 13.2 cm correct to one decimal place.

(c) Describe the skew of these data. Give a reason for your answer. [1]

Two of these plants are chosen at random.

(d) Estimate the probability that both of their heights are between 8 cm and 14 cm. [3]

[8 marks]

(a) Complete the table [2 marks · 1 step]

Key Concept — Reading Frequencies from a Histogram
In a histogram with unequal class widths, the vertical axis shows frequency density (fd), not frequency. This ensures each bar's area represents frequency: \[\text{Frequency Density} = \frac{\text{Frequency}}{\text{Class Width}} \qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad \text{Frequency} = \text{fd} \times \text{Class Width}\] Alternatively, since the total \(n = 408\) is known and three frequencies are given, we can find the remaining two by: (1) reading their heights (fd) from the histogram and multiplying by class width, or (2) noting the two unknown classes share equal bars on the histogram.
STEP 1

What we're doing: Three frequencies total \(32 + 152 + 120 = 304\), leaving \(408 - 304 = 104\) plants in the two unknown classes (13–15 and 15–17).

From the histogram, the bars for 13–15 and 15–17 are drawn at equal height (equal frequency density). Since both classes also have equal width (\(w = 2\) cm each), equal fd means equal frequency. So both classes share the 104 plants equally: \[\text{Each frequency} = \frac{104}{2} = 52\]

Verification: \(32 + 152 + 52 + 52 + 120 = 408\) ✓

Both bars have the same height (frequency density) and the same width (2 cm), so their areas — which represent their frequencies — must be equal. Equal areas means equal frequencies, giving each class 52 plants.
\(\boxed{13\text{–}15: 52 \text{ plants},\quad 15\text{–}17: 52 \text{ plants}}\)

(b) Estimate median [2 marks · 1 step]

Key Concept — Median by Linear Interpolation
The median is the middle value. For \(n\) data points, the median is at position \(\tfrac{n}{2}\) — for \(n = 408\), this is the 204th value when all data are ordered.

Since data is grouped, we cannot list all values. Instead we use linear interpolation, assuming data is uniformly spread within the class: \[\text{Median} = L + \frac{\tfrac{n}{2} - F}{f} \times w\] where \(L\) = lower boundary of the class containing the median; \(F\) = cumulative frequency before that class; \(f\) = frequency of that class; \(w\) = class width.
STEP 1

What we're doing: Find the 204th value using cumulative frequencies to locate the median class, then apply linear interpolation.

Cumulative frequencies:
After 5–9: \(F = 32\) (positions 1–32)
After 9–13: \(F = 32 + 152 = 184\) (positions 33–184)
After 13–15: \(F = 184 + 52 = 236\) (positions 185–236)

The 204th value falls in the 13–15 class (since 184 < 204 ≤ 236). Here \(L = 13\), \(F = 184\), \(f = 52\), \(w = 2\).

Applying the formula: \[\text{Median} = 13 + \frac{204 - 184}{52} \times 2 = 13 + \frac{20}{52} \times 2 = 13 + \frac{40}{52} = 13 + 0.769 \approx 13.8\]

We have already accounted for 184 plants in the first two classes. We need \(204 - 184 = 20\) more plants to reach the median. The 13–15 class has 52 plants spread across 2 cm, so we travel \(\tfrac{20}{52}\) of the way through the 2 cm width: \(\tfrac{20}{52} \times 2 \approx 0.77\) cm beyond the lower boundary of 13 cm.
\(\boxed{\text{Median} \approx 13.8 \text{ cm}}\)

(c) Describe skew [1 mark · 1 step]

Mean \(= 13.2\) cm (given); Median \(\approx 13.8\) cm (from part b)
Key Concept — Skewness from Mean and Median
Skewness describes the asymmetry of a distribution:
  • Positive skew (right-skewed): a long tail pulls the mean to the right of the median. Mean > Median.
  • Negative skew (left-skewed): a long tail pulls the mean to the left of the median. Mean < Median.
  • Symmetric: mean ≈ median.
Intuition: the mean is sensitive to extreme values (outliers), while the median is not. A few very small values drag the mean left (below the median), giving a left/negative skew.
STEP 1

What we're doing: Compare mean and median to identify the direction of skew.

\[\text{Mean} = 13.2 \text{ cm} \quad < \quad \text{Median} \approx 13.8 \text{ cm}\]

Since mean < median, the distribution is negatively skewed (left-skewed).

A few unusually short plants (very small heights) pull the mean downward — below the median. The median is unaffected by these extreme low values, so it remains higher. The tail of the distribution extends to the left (toward smaller heights), which is the defining feature of negative skew.
\(\boxed{\text{Negatively skewed} \text{ — because mean (13.2) < median (13.8)}}\)

(d) P(both heights in 8–14 cm) [3 marks · 2 steps]

Key Concept — Estimating Counts in a Sub-Interval & Probability Without Replacement
The interval 8–14 cm straddles several class boundaries. We estimate the count of plants with height in [8, 14) by summing contributions from each class, assuming uniform spread within each class: \[\text{Plants in sub-interval} = \frac{\text{sub-interval width}}{\text{class width}} \times \text{class frequency}\] Since two plants are chosen at random without replacement (one plant cannot be chosen twice), the probability is: \[P(\text{both in range}) = \frac{\text{count in range}}{n} \times \frac{\text{count in range} - 1}{n - 1}\]
STEP 1

What we're doing: Estimate how many of the 408 plants have heights in [8, 14). We split the range 8–14 across the classes it touches.

• 5 ≤ h < 9 (freq = 32, width = 4): we want 8 to 9, sub-interval width = 1: \[\frac{1}{4} \times 32 = 8 \text{ plants}\]

• 9 ≤ h < 13 (freq = 152, width = 4): entirely within 8–14, take all: \[152 \text{ plants}\]

• 13 ≤ h < 15 (freq = 52, width = 2): we want 13 to 14, sub-interval width = 1: \[\frac{1}{2} \times 52 = 26 \text{ plants}\]

Total in 8–14: \[8 + 152 + 26 = 186 \text{ plants}\]

Each class's plants are assumed to be uniformly spread across its width. So the fraction of plants in a sub-interval equals the fraction of the class width that sub-interval occupies.
STEP 2

What we're doing: Use the without-replacement probability formula. There are 186 plants in range out of 408. The first plant is chosen (186 in range out of 408 total), then the second is chosen from the remaining 407 plants (185 in range out of 407).

\[P(\text{both in 8–14}) = \frac{186}{408} \times \frac{185}{407}\] \[= \frac{186 \times 185}{408 \times 407} = \frac{34\,410}{166\,056} \approx 0.207\]
We multiply because the two selections are sequential independent events (given the first result). After removing one plant in range, only 185 remain in range out of 407 total — this is the "without replacement" adjustment that prevents counting the same plant twice.
\(\boxed{P \approx 0.207}\)
