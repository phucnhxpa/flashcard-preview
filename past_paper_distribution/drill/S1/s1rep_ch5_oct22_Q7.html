Discrete RV \(X\): E(X), Var(X), Linear Transforms, Two Independent Observations

Question

Adana selects one number at random from the distribution of $X$ which has the following probability distribution.

The discrete random variable $X$ takes values 0, 5, 10 with probabilities:

$x$0510
$P(X=x)$0.10.20.7

(a) Given that the number selected by Adana is not 5, write down the probability it is 0. [1]

$X_1$ and $X_2$ are independent observations of $X$. Bruno and Charlie each independently select one number at random from the distribution of $X$.
(b) Show that $E(X^2) = 75$. [1]   (c) Find $\text{Var}(X)$. [3]   (d) Find $\text{Var}(4-3X)$. [2]
(e) Find the probability that the number Bruno selects is greater than the number Charlie selects. [3]   (f) Determine the probability distribution of $D = \text{Bruno's number} \times \text{Charlie's number}$. [4]

[15 marks]

(a) Conditional Probability [1 mark · 1 step]

Conditional Probability
\[P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}\] Here: "Given \(X \neq 5\)" means we restrict to the outcomes \(\{X=0, X=10\}\). Their combined probability is \(P(X \neq 5) = 0.1 + 0.7 = 0.8\).
The conditional probability that \(X = 0\) given this restriction is the probability of 0 as a fraction of the surviving probability mass.
STEP 1

Apply the conditional probability formula

Given \(X \neq 5\), the remaining outcomes are \(X = 0\) (probability 0.1) and \(X = 10\) (probability 0.7).

\[P(X \neq 5) = 0.1 + 0.7 = 0.8\] \[P(X=0 \mid X \neq 5) = \frac{P(X=0)}{P(X \neq 5)} = \frac{0.1}{0.8} = \frac{1}{8}\]
Why just \(P(X=0)\) in the numerator? Since \(\{X=0\} \subseteq \{X \neq 5\}\), we have \(P(X=0 \cap X\neq 5) = P(X=0) = 0.1\). The event "X=0 AND X≠5" is just "X=0".
$P(X=0 \mid X\neq 5) = \boxed{\dfrac{1}{8}}$

(b) Show \(E(X^2) = 75\) [1 mark · 1 step]

Computing E(X²)
\[E(X^2) = \sum_x x^2 \cdot P(X = x)\] Method: Square each possible value of \(X\), multiply by its probability, then sum all terms.
Do not confuse \(E(X^2)\) with \([E(X)]^2\) — these are generally different and serve different roles.
STEP 1

Substitute each value into \(E(X^2) = \sum x^2 P(X=x)\)

\[E(X^2) = 0^2 \times 0.1 + 5^2 \times 0.2 + 10^2 \times 0.7\] \[= 0 \times 0.1 + 25 \times 0.2 + 100 \times 0.7\] \[= 0 + 5 + 70 = 75 \checkmark\]
Arithmetic check: \(0^2 = 0\), \(5^2 = 25\), \(10^2 = 100\). Multiply: \(25 \times 0.2 = 5\), \(100 \times 0.7 = 70\). Sum: \(0 + 5 + 70 = 75\) ✓
$E(X^2) = 0 + 5 + 70 = \boxed{75}$ ✓ (shown)

(c) Find \(\text{Var}(X)\) [3 marks · 1 step]

Variance Formula
\[\text{Var}(X) = E(X^2) - [E(X)]^2\] Derivation: \(\text{Var}(X) = E[(X-\mu)^2]\). Expanding: \(= E[X^2 - 2\mu X + \mu^2] = E(X^2) - 2\mu \cdot E(X) + \mu^2 = E(X^2) - \mu^2\).
Step 1: First compute \(E(X) = \sum x \cdot P(X=x)\) from the probability table.
Step 2: Square \(E(X)\) to get \([E(X)]^2\).
Step 3: Subtract from \(E(X^2)\).
From part (b):$E(X^2) = 75$
STEP 1

Compute \(E(X)\), then apply \(\text{Var}(X) = E(X^2) - [E(X)]^2\)

\[E(X) = \sum x \cdot P(X=x) = 0(0.1) + 5(0.2) + 10(0.7)\] \[= 0 + 1 + 7 = 8\]

Now apply the variance formula:

\[\text{Var}(X) = E(X^2) - [E(X)]^2 = 75 - 8^2 = 75 - 64 = 11\]
Common mistake: Writing \([E(X)]^2 = E(X) = 8\) instead of squaring: \([E(X)]^2 = 8^2 = 64\). Always square \(E(X)\) before subtracting.
$E(X) = 8;\quad \boxed{\text{Var}(X) = 11}$

(d) Find \(\text{Var}(4-3X)\) [2 marks · 1 step]

Variance of a Linear Transform
For constants \(a\) and \(b\): \[\text{Var}(aX + b) = a^2 \cdot \text{Var}(X)\] Key points:
  • The constant \(b\) has no effect on variance — shifting every outcome by \(b\) doesn't change the spread
  • The coefficient \(a\) is squared — even if \(a\) is negative, \(a^2 > 0\), so variance is always non-negative
Here: \(4 - 3X = -3X + 4\), so \(a = -3\), \(b = 4\), giving \(\text{Var}(4-3X) = (-3)^2 \text{Var}(X) = 9\text{Var}(X)\).
$\text{Var}(X) = 11$ (from part (c))
From part (c):$\text{Var}(X) = 11$
STEP 1

Apply \(\text{Var}(aX + b) = a^2\,\text{Var}(X)\)

Identify \(a = -3\) and \(b = +4\) in the expression \(4 - 3X = -3X + 4\):

\[\text{Var}(4-3X) = (-3)^2 \times \text{Var}(X) = 9 \times 11 = 99\]
Why does \(+4\) vanish? Adding 4 to every outcome shifts the whole distribution right by 4. But variance measures spread — how far values are from the mean. The mean also shifts by 4, so deviations from the mean are unchanged. Therefore the constant \(b = 4\) contributes nothing to variance.
$\boxed{\text{Var}(4-3X) = 99}$

(e) \(P(X_1 > X_2)\) where \(X_1, X_2\) are independent [3 marks · 1 step]

P(X₁ > X₂) Using Symmetry
When \(X_1\) and \(X_2\) are independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.), there are three mutually exclusive cases: \[P(X_1 > X_2) + P(X_1 = X_2) + P(X_1 < X_2) = 1\] By symmetry: \(P(X_1 > X_2) = P(X_1 < X_2)\). So: \[P(X_1 > X_2) = \frac{1 - P(X_1 = X_2)}{2}\] To find \(P(X_1 = X_2)\): since \(X_1\) and \(X_2\) are independent, \(P(X_1 = x \text{ and } X_2 = x) = [P(X=x)]^2\), so: \[P(X_1 = X_2) = \sum_x [P(X=x)]^2\]
$P(X=0)=0.1,\; P(X=5)=0.2,\; P(X=10)=0.7$; $X_1, X_2$ i.i.d.
From the probability table:$P(X=0)=0.1,\ P(X=5)=0.2,\ P(X=10)=0.7$
STEP 1

Find \(P(X_1 = X_2)\), then use symmetry

\[P(X_1 = X_2) = [P(X=0)]^2 + [P(X=5)]^2 + [P(X=10)]^2\] \[= 0.1^2 + 0.2^2 + 0.7^2 = 0.01 + 0.04 + 0.49 = 0.54\]

By symmetry (\(X_1\) and \(X_2\) have the same distribution):

\[P(X_1 > X_2) = \frac{1 - P(X_1 = X_2)}{2} = \frac{1 - 0.54}{2} = \frac{0.46}{2} = 0.23\]
Why symmetry works: Since \(X_1\) and \(X_2\) are i.i.d., the situation "\(X_1 > X_2\)" is just as likely as "\(X_2 > X_1\)" (which is "\(X_1 < X_2\)"). So both halves are equal, and we split the remaining probability equally.
$\boxed{P(X_1 > X_2) = 0.23}$

(f) Distribution of \(D = X_1 X_2\) [4 marks · 1 step]

Distribution of a Product of Two Independent RVs
When \(X_1\) and \(X_2\) are independent, \(P(X_1 = a \text{ and } X_2 = b) = P(X_1 = a) \times P(X_2 = b)\).
To find the distribution of \(D = X_1 X_2\):
  1. Identify all possible products \(d = x_1 \times x_2\)
  2. For each value of \(d\), find all combinations of \((x_1, x_2)\) giving that product
  3. Sum the probabilities of those combinations
Possible products: \(X\) takes values 0, 5, 10. Products: \(0\times\text{anything} = 0\), \(5\times 5=25\), \(5\times 10 = 10\times 5=50\), \(10\times 10=100\). So \(D \in \{0, 25, 50, 100\}\).
$X_1, X_2$ independent copies of $X$; $D = X_1 X_2$
From the probability table:$P(X=0)=0.1,\ P(X=5)=0.2,\ P(X=10)=0.7$
STEP 1

Find \(P(D = d)\) for each possible product value

\(P(D = 0)\): This happens whenever at least one of \(X_1, X_2\) equals 0.

\[P(D=0) = 1 - P(\text{neither is 0}) = 1 - P(X_1 \neq 0) \times P(X_2 \neq 0) = 1 - 0.9^2 = 1 - 0.81 = 0.19\]

\(P(D = 25)\): Requires both \(X_1 = 5\) and \(X_2 = 5\).

\[P(D=25) = P(X_1=5) \times P(X_2=5) = 0.2 \times 0.2 = 0.04\]

\(P(D = 50)\): Either \((X_1=5, X_2=10)\) or \((X_1=10, X_2=5)\).

\[P(D=50) = 2 \times P(X=5) \times P(X=10) = 2 \times 0.2 \times 0.7 = 0.28\]

\(P(D = 100)\): Requires both \(X_1 = 10\) and \(X_2 = 10\).

\[P(D=100) = P(X=10)^2 = 0.7^2 = 0.49\]

Check: \(0.19 + 0.04 + 0.28 + 0.49 = 1.00\) ✓

Why the factor of 2 for \(D=50\)? There are two distinct ordered pairs: \((X_1=5, X_2=10)\) and \((X_1=10, X_2=5)\) — both give \(D = 50\). Add both contributions. For \(D=25\) and \(D=100\), there's only one pair each.
Distribution of \(D\):
$d$: 0 (prob 0.19), 25 (prob 0.04), 50 (prob 0.28), 100 (prob 0.49)
