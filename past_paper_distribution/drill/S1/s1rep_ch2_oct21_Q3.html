Question

The stem and leaf diagram shows the ages of the 35 male passengers on a cruise.

Age              Key: 1 | 3 represents 13 years

(a) Find the median age of the male passengers. [1]

(b) Show that the interquartile range (IQR) of these ages is 16. [2]

An outlier is defined as a value that is more than 1.5 × IQR above the upper quartile or 1.5 × IQR below the lower quartile.

(c) Show that there are 3 outliers amongst these ages. [3]

(d) On the grid, draw a box plot for the ages of the male passengers on the cruise. [4]

Anja, along with her 2 daughters and a granddaughter, joins the cruise. Anja's granddaughter is younger than both daughters. Anja had her 23rd birthday on the day her eldest daughter was born. When their 4 ages are included with the other female passengers, the box plot does not change.

(f) State, giving reasons, what you can say about (i) the granddaughter's age; (ii) Anja's age. [3]

(a) Find the median [1 mark · 1 step]

Key Concept — Median from a Stem & Leaf Diagram
The median is the middle value when all data are arranged in order. For \(n\) data points: \[\text{Median position} = \frac{n+1}{2}\text{th value}\] For \(n = 35\): median = \(\tfrac{35+1}{2} = 18\)th value in ascending order.

A stem and leaf diagram already lists values in order (leaves within each stem are listed smallest to largest). We count through the stems/leaves until we reach the 18th value.
STEP 1

What we're doing: Count to the 18th value in order. The key says "1|3 represents 13 years", so stem 5, leaf 3 represents 53 years old.

Cumulative count through each stem:
Stem 1: 1 value → running total: 1 (positions 1)
Stem 2: 2 values → running total: 3 (positions 2–3)
Stem 3: 4 values → running total: 7 (positions 4–7)
Stem 4: 7 values → running total: 14 (positions 8–14)
Stem 5: 10 values → running total: 24 (positions 15–24)

The 18th value falls in stem 5. Listing the leaves for stem 5 in order: 2, 2, 3, 3, 4, 4, 5, 6, 6, 8.

Positions within stem 5: 15th=52, 16th=52, 17th=53, 18th=53.

Since \(n = 35\) is odd, there is a unique middle value at position \(\tfrac{35+1}{2} = 18\). We count through the ordered stem-and-leaf: after stems 1–4 we have 14 values, so the 18th lies in stem 5, and specifically it is the 4th leaf of stem 5 (positions 15, 16, 17, 18 correspond to leaves 2, 2, 3, 3 — so the 4th leaf is 3, giving age 53).
\(\boxed{\text{Median} = 53 \text{ years}}\)

(b) Show IQR = 16 [2 marks · 2 steps]

Key Concept — Quartiles for Discrete Data
The lower quartile \(Q_1\) is the value below which 25% of the data lies. The upper quartile \(Q_3\) is the value below which 75% lies. For \(n = 35\): \[Q_1 \text{ position} = \frac{n+1}{4} = \frac{36}{4} = 9\text{th value}\] \[Q_3 \text{ position} = \frac{3(n+1)}{4} = \frac{108}{4} = 27\text{th value}\] The interquartile range (IQR) is then: \[\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1\] It measures the spread of the central 50% of the data — the range between the 25th and 75th percentiles.
STEP 1

What we're doing: Find \(Q_1\) = 9th value. Using the cumulative counts from part (a):

After stem 3: 7 values. Stem 4 provides positions 8–14.
8th = 45, 9th = 45.

The 9th value falls in stem 4 (the 40s). Stem 4's leaves in order are: 5, 5, 6, 7, 8, 8, 9. The 8th overall is 45, and the 9th overall is also 45 (second leaf of 5).
\(\boxed{Q_1 = 45 \text{ years}}\)
STEP 2

What we're doing: Find \(Q_3\) = 27th value and compute IQR.

After stem 5: 24 values. Stem 6 provides positions 25–31.
Stem 6 leaves in order: 0, 1, 1, 4, 4, 4, 7.
25th=60, 26th=61, 27th=61. So \(Q_3 = 61\).

\[\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1 = 61 - 45 = 16 \checkmark\]
The 27th value falls in stem 6. After the first 24 values (through stem 5), we need the 3rd value in stem 6: leaves 0, 1, 1 → 3rd is 61. IQR = 61 − 45 = 16, confirming the required result.
\(\boxed{Q_1 = 45,\quad Q_3 = 61,\quad \text{IQR} = 16}\) ✓

(c) Show 3 outliers [3 marks · 2 steps]

Key Concept — Outlier Fences
An outlier is a value that lies unusually far from the bulk of the data. The question defines outliers as values beyond the outlier fences: \[\text{Lower fence} = Q_1 - 1.5 \times \text{IQR}\] \[\text{Upper fence} = Q_3 + 1.5 \times \text{IQR}\] Any data value below the lower fence or above the upper fence is an outlier. The factor 1.5 is a standard choice — values more than 1.5 IQRs from the quartiles are considered unusually extreme.
STEP 1

What we're doing: Calculate both fences using \(Q_1 = 45\), \(Q_3 = 61\), \(\text{IQR} = 16\).

\[1.5 \times \text{IQR} = 1.5 \times 16 = 24\] \[\text{Lower fence} = Q_1 - 24 = 45 - 24 = 21\] \[\text{Upper fence} = Q_3 + 24 = 61 + 24 = 85\]
Any value below 21 or above 85 is flagged as an outlier — it lies more than 1.5 IQRs outside the interquartile range, suggesting it is atypically extreme compared to the central half of the data.
STEP 2

What we're doing: Scan the stem-and-leaf diagram for any values outside the fences (\({<}21\) or \({>}85\)).

Below lower fence (21): Stem 1: age 13. That is \(13 < 21\) → outlier. Ages 27, 29 (stem 2) are above 21 → not outliers.

Above upper fence (85): Stem 8: ages 87, 88. Both \(> 85\) → outliers. Ages 73, 76 (stem 7) are below 85 → not outliers.

Total outliers: 3 (ages 13, 87, 88) ✓

We check every value in the diagram against the fences. Values at stems 1 and 8 are the candidates, but only age 13 breaches the lower fence and only ages 87 and 88 breach the upper fence, giving exactly 3 outliers.
\(\boxed{\text{Outliers: } 13,\ 87,\ 88 \text{ — total of 3}}\) ✓

(d) Box plot [4 marks · 1 step]

Min non-outlier = 27, \(Q_1\) = 45, Median = 53, \(Q_3\) = 61, Max non-outlier = 76; Outliers: 13, 87, 88
Key Concept — Box Plot Construction
A box plot (box-and-whisker diagram) displays five key statistics and any outliers:
  • Box: drawn from \(Q_1\) to \(Q_3\), with a line at the median inside.
  • Whiskers: extend from the box to the most extreme non-outlier values (not to the fences themselves).
  • Outliers: plotted individually as crosses (×) or dots beyond the whiskers.
The left whisker ends at the smallest value that is NOT an outlier (i.e. \(\geq\) lower fence); the right whisker ends at the largest value that is NOT an outlier (i.e. \(\leq\) upper fence).
STEP 1

What we're doing: Identify all five key values and the outlier positions, then draw the box plot.

• Minimum non-outlier: lowest age ≥ 21 → age 27 (stem 2, first leaf).
• \(Q_1 = 45\)
• Median \(= 53\)
• \(Q_3 = 61\)
• Maximum non-outlier: highest age ≤ 85 → age 76 (stem 7, second leaf).
• Outliers: ×13, ×87, ×88

Draw: a horizontal axis; box from 45 to 61 with vertical line at 53; left whisker to 27; right whisker to 76; mark outliers with × at 13, 87, 88.

The whiskers do not extend to the fences (21 and 85) — they extend to the actual data values nearest the fences. This ensures the whiskers represent real data, not theoretical boundaries.
\(\boxed{\text{Box: [45, 61], median at 53, whiskers to 27 and 76, outliers at 13, 87, 88}}\)

(e) Compare distributions [1 mark · 1 step]

Key Concept — Comparing Box Plots
When comparing two distributions using box plots, comment on:
  • Location (centre): compare medians — which group has higher typical values?
  • Spread (variability): compare IQRs or overall ranges — which group is more spread out?
Always quote the specific numerical values from each box plot to support your comparison.
STEP 1

What we're doing: Read the female median from Figure 1's box plot and compare with the male median of 53 years. Also compare IQRs.

From Figure 1 (female box plot — values must be read from the actual diagram in the exam): suppose female median ≈ 46 years and female IQR ≈ 20 years.

Comparison: "The male passengers have a higher median age (53 years) than the female passengers (46 years), suggesting males are typically older. The male IQR (16 years) is smaller than the female IQR (20 years), suggesting male ages are less spread out / more consistent around the median."

We compare medians for location (typical value) and IQRs for spread (how much variability exists in the middle 50%). Always quote both statistics with their numerical values — a vague statement like "males are older" without statistics scores no marks.

(f) Granddaughter and Anja ages [3 marks · 1 step]

Key Concept — When Adding Data Doesn't Change a Box Plot
A box plot is determined by five values: minimum (non-outlier), \(Q_1\), median, \(Q_3\), maximum (non-outlier), plus the positions of outliers. Adding new data to a dataset does not change the box plot if and only if all new values fall within the existing range of non-outliers and don't shift the quartiles. Specifically:
  • Values ≤ existing minimum non-outlier may create new outliers or extend the left whisker — if the box plot doesn't change, they must be ≥ existing minimum.
  • Values ≥ existing maximum non-outlier similarly may extend the right whisker.
  • New quartile values won't change if new data is positioned inside the existing box.
STEP 1

What we're doing: Use the constraint "the box plot does not change" to bound each person's age, then use the age relationship to further constrain Anja's age.

Context recap: Let the existing female box plot have minimum non-outlier \(= m_{\min}\) and lower fence \(= L_f\) (read from Figure 1). Let the female upper fence \(= U_f\).

(i) Granddaughter's age: The granddaughter is younger than both daughters, so she has the smallest age of the four new additions. For the female box plot to remain unchanged, this smallest value must not become a new minimum (i.e., must not be below the existing female minimum non-outlier). Therefore: \[\text{granddaughter's age} \geqslant \text{existing female minimum (non-outlier)}\] If the existing female minimum is, say, 14 (read from the diagram): the granddaughter must be aged at least 14.

(ii) Anja's age: Anja had her 23rd birthday on the day her eldest daughter was born, so Anja is exactly 23 years older than her eldest daughter. For the box plot to remain unchanged, Anja's age must not exceed the existing female maximum non-outlier (otherwise the right whisker would extend): \[\text{Anja's age} = \text{eldest daughter's age} + 23 \leqslant \text{existing female maximum (non-outlier)}\] So Anja is at most [existing female max] years old. (Quote the specific value from Figure 1.)

(i) If the granddaughter were younger than the existing female minimum, the left whisker would extend leftward (or she'd become a new outlier), changing the box plot. So she must be at least as old as the existing minimum. (ii) If Anja were older than the existing female maximum non-outlier, the right whisker would extend rightward, or she'd become a new outlier — either change would alter the box plot. So Anja cannot exceed the existing maximum.
\(\boxed{(i)\ \text{Granddaughter age} \geqslant \text{female min};\quad (ii)\ \text{Anja age} = \text{eldest daughter} + 23 \leqslant \text{female max non-outlier}}\)
