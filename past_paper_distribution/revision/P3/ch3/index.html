Logarithmic transformation / linearising an exponential model (from graph)
#1 most important
3×
5-7m

A graph of $\log y$ against $x$ (or $\log y$ against $\log x$) is given as a straight line. You must find the equation of the line and convert it back to the original model $y = ab^x$ or $y = ax^n$. Two data points may be given.

Triggersstraight linelog ylog xgraphmodelgradientinterceptexponential model
Assumptions
  • $\log$ usually means $\log_{10}$ unless $\ln$ is explicitly stated — check the axis labels
  • The straight-line form $\log y = (\log b)x + \log a$ corresponds to $y = ab^x$
  • The straight-line form $\log y = n \log x + \log a$ corresponds to $y = ax^n$
  • Constants $a$ and $b$ should be found to appropriate accuracy (usually 3 s.f.) unless exact values are clear
Approaches
A: Find line equation then equate coefficients
When: When two points on the log graph are given or gradient/intercept can be read off
  1. Identify the axes: is it $\log y$ vs $x$, or $\log y$ vs $\log x$?
  2. Find the gradient $m$ using $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$ (using the log-scale coordinates)
  3. Find the $y$-intercept $c$ using $\log y = mx + c$ and one of the points
  4. Write the full straight-line equation: $\log y = mx + c$
  5. Compare with the theoretical form: e.g. for $y = ab^x$, $\log y = x \log b + \log a$
  6. Equate: gradient $m = \log b \Rightarrow b = 10^m$; intercept $c = \log a \Rightarrow a = 10^c$
  7. Write the final model $y = ab^x$ with calculated values
+ Pros: Methodical and clearly shows working for full marks
– Cons: Requires careful identification of which log form corresponds to which model
B: Substitute two data points directly into the exponential model
When: When you prefer to work directly with the original model and solve simultaneous equations
  1. Write $y = ab^x$ and substitute each data point to get two equations
  2. Divide one equation by the other to eliminate $a$
  3. Solve for $b$, then back-substitute to find $a$
+ Pros: Avoids logarithm manipulation entirely if data points are in original (non-log) form
– Cons: If data is given in log form, you must first convert back, which adds steps
Formulas
$y = ab^x \Rightarrow \log y = x\log b + \log a$ — linear in $x$ (MEMORIZE)
$y = ax^n \Rightarrow \log y = n\log x + \log a$ — linear in $\log x$ (MEMORIZE)
$\log(AB) = \log A + \log B$ (MEMORIZE)
$\log(A^n) = n\log A$ (MEMORIZE)
Gradient $= \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Confusing $\log(ab^t)$ with $(\log a)(\log b)^t$ — must use $\log a + t\log b$
  • Reading $\log_{10}$ values from the graph but then using $\ln$ in calculations (or vice versa)
  • Premature rounding of $a$ and $b$ before using them in subsequent parts of the question
  • Writing $\log(a + b)$ instead of $\log a + \log b$ — the addition rule applies to products, not sums
  • Forgetting that $4 \neq \log_{10} 4$; a constant on a log axis must be converted: $c = \log a \Rightarrow a = 10^c$

Triggersmodellogshow thatfind the value ofconstants a and bexponential form
Assumptions
  • $\log_{10} P = mt + c$ converts to $P = 10^{mt+c} = 10^c \cdot (10^m)^t$
  • Round constants only at the final step unless exact values are obtainable
  • When interpreting constants: the initial value is usually $a = 10^c$ (when $t=0$)
Approaches
A: Use definition of logarithm to convert
When: Always — this is the standard approach
  1. Start from $\log_{10} y = mt + c$
  2. Rewrite using definition: $y = 10^{mt + c}$
  3. Split using index law: $y = 10^c \cdot (10^m)^t$
  4. Identify $a = 10^c$ and $b = 10^m$
  5. Calculate numerical values of $a$ and $b$
  6. If asked to substitute, plug in the given value of $t$ (or $y$) and solve
+ Pros: Direct and efficient
– Cons: Requires confident use of index laws
Formulas
$\log_a x = y \iff x = a^y$ (MEMORIZE)
$a^{m+n} = a^m \cdot a^n$ (MEMORIZE)
$a^{mn} = (a^m)^n$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Confusing $10^{a+b}$ with $10^a + 10^b$
  • Premature rounding of $10^c$ or $10^m$ — keep full precision until the final answer
  • Incorrectly splitting: $10^{0.4t + 2.1} \neq 10^{0.4t} + 10^{2.1}$

Solve exponential equation via hidden quadratic substitution

3×

An equation involving terms like $a^{2x}$, $a^x$, and a constant. The exponents suggest a quadratic structure in disguise, e.g. $3^{2x} + 5 \cdot 3^x - 6 = 0$ or $e^{2x} - 7e^x + 12 = 0$.

Triggersexponential equationsolvee^{2x}3^{2x}quadraticexact form
Assumptions
  • Exponential function $a^x > 0$ for all real $x$, so any substitution variable $y = a^x$ must be positive — reject $y \le 0$ solutions
  • If the question says 'exact form', leave answer in terms of $\ln$ or $\log$ — do not use a calculator decimal
  • Assume real solutions only unless stated otherwise
Approaches
A: Substitution to form quadratic
When: When you spot $a^{2x} = (a^x)^2$ and the equation has terms in $a^{2x}$, $a^x$, and constants
  1. Rewrite all exponential terms in terms of a single base, e.g. recognise $9^x = (3^x)^2$ or $e^{2x} = (e^x)^2$, and rewrite $a^{2x+1} = a \cdot (a^x)^2$
  2. Let $y = a^x$ (or the appropriate base expression)
  3. Rewrite the equation as a quadratic in $y$: $Ay^2 + By + C = 0$
  4. Solve the quadratic by factorisation or the quadratic formula
  5. Reject any solution where $y \le 0$ (since $a^x > 0$)
  6. For each valid $y$, solve $a^x = y$ by taking logarithms: $x = \frac{\ln y}{\ln a}$
  7. Give answer in exact form unless told otherwise
+ Pros: Systematic and reduces to familiar quadratic techniques
– Cons: Easy to forget to substitute back or to reject negative $y$ values
B: Direct logarithmic manipulation (simple cases only)
When: When the equation simplifies to $a^x = k$ after straightforward algebra without needing a quadratic
  1. Isolate the exponential term on one side
  2. Take $\ln$ (or $\log_{10}$) of both sides
  3. Apply power rule: $\ln(a^x) = x \ln a$
  4. Solve for $x$
+ Pros: Faster when no quadratic structure is present
– Cons: Not applicable when two different exponential terms are present
Formulas
$a^{2x} = (a^x)^2$ (MEMORIZE — index law for substitution)
$a^{mx+n} = a^n \cdot (a^x)^m$ (MEMORIZE — splitting exponents)
$x = \frac{\ln y}{\ln a}$ when $a^x = y$ (MEMORIZE)
Quadratic formula: $y = \frac{-B \pm \sqrt{B^2 - 4AC}}{2A}$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting that $a^{2x+1} = a \cdot (a^x)^2$, not $(a^x)^{2x+1}$
  • Accepting a negative value for $y = a^x$ and getting an extraneous solution
  • Forgetting to substitute back from $y$ to $x$ after solving the quadratic
  • Giving a decimal answer when 'exact form' involving $\ln$ is required

Triggerssolveexponentialdifferent basesgive answer tologarithms
Assumptions
  • You may use $\ln$ or $\log_{10}$ — either is valid unless the question specifies one
  • Power rule of logarithms applies: $\log(a^n) = n \log a$
  • The exponent expression must be treated as a complete bracket when applying the power rule
Approaches
A: Take logarithms of both sides and solve linearly
When: When the equation has the form $a^{f(x)} = b^{g(x)}$ with linear functions $f$ and $g$
  1. Take $\ln$ (or $\log_{10}$) of both sides of the equation
  2. Apply the power rule: $\ln(a^{f(x)}) = f(x) \ln a$, being careful to bracket the entire exponent
  3. Expand both sides so that terms involving $x$ are visible
  4. Collect all $x$ terms on one side and constants on the other
  5. Factor out $x$ and solve: $x = \frac{\text{constants}}{\text{coefficient of } x}$
  6. Evaluate using a calculator if a decimal is required
+ Pros: Direct and always works for equations of this type
– Cons: Algebraic errors are common when expanding and collecting terms with $\ln$ coefficients
Formulas
$\log(a^n) = n \log a$ — power rule (formula booklet)
$\ln(a^n) = n \ln a$ — power rule with natural log (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting brackets around the full exponent: $\ln(a^{3x-1}) = (3x-1)\ln a$, NOT $3x \ln a - 1$
  • Sign errors when collecting $x$ terms, especially when $\ln$ values are decimals
  • Using an incorrect log rule such as $\log(a^x) = x + \log a$

A function involving products of polynomials and exponentials, e.g. $y = (2x+3)e^{-4x}$. You differentiate using the product rule (and chain rule for $e^{kx}$), set the derivative to zero, and find the stationary point.

Triggersdifferentiatestationary pointproduct rulee^{kx}turning pointmaximumminimum
Assumptions
  • $e^{kx} > 0$ for all real $x$, so $e^{kx} \neq 0$ — it can be cancelled/divided when solving $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$
  • Product rule is required when the function is a product of two distinct functions
  • Chain rule: $\frac{d}{dx}[e^{kx}] = ke^{kx}$
Approaches
A: Product rule then set equal to zero
When: When $y$ is a product of two functions, one of which involves $e^{kx}$
  1. Identify $u$ and $v$ in $y = uv$ (e.g., $u = 2x+3$, $v = e^{-4x}$)
  2. Find $\frac{du}{dx}$ and $\frac{dv}{dx}$ (use chain rule for $e^{kx}$: $\frac{dv}{dx} = ke^{kx}$)
  3. Apply product rule: $\frac{dy}{dx} = u\frac{dv}{dx} + v\frac{du}{dx}$
  4. Factor out common terms (usually $e^{kx}$)
  5. Set $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$
  6. Since $e^{kx} \neq 0$, divide it out and solve the remaining algebraic equation
  7. Find the $y$-coordinate by substituting back into the original equation
  8. Determine nature (max/min) if required, using second derivative or sign changes
+ Pros: Standard and reliable method
– Cons: Multi-step with many opportunities for sign and coefficient errors
B: Logarithmic differentiation (alternative for products)
When: When the function has multiple factors and you want to avoid the product rule directly
  1. Take $\ln$ of both sides: $\ln y = \ln u + \ln v$
  2. Differentiate implicitly: $\frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{u'}{u} + \frac{v'}{v}$
  3. Multiply through by $y$ to get $\frac{dy}{dx}$
  4. Set equal to zero and solve
+ Pros: Can simplify complicated products
– Cons: Less standard at this level; product rule is expected and more familiar
Formulas
$\frac{d}{dx}[e^{kx}] = ke^{kx}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{d}{dx}[a^x] = a^x \ln a$ (formula booklet)
Product rule: $\frac{d}{dx}[uv] = u\frac{dv}{dx} + v\frac{du}{dx}$ (formula booklet)
Chain rule: $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting the factor of $k$ when differentiating $e^{kx}$: the derivative is $ke^{kx}$, not $e^{kx}$
  • Misapplying the product rule: $\frac{d}{dx}(uv) \neq u'v'$
  • Trying to solve $e^{kx} = 0$ instead of recognising it is never zero
  • Sign errors when $k$ is negative, e.g., $\frac{d}{dx}[e^{-4x}] = -4e^{-4x}$

Assumptions
  • Arguments of logarithms must be positive — always check domain at the end
  • $e^{\ln(A)} = A$ and $10^{\log_{10}(A)} = A$ — inverse operations
  • If the question asks for exact answers, leave in terms of $e$ or surds
Approaches
A: Combine logs, then exponentiate
When: When the equation has multiple log terms that can be combined into a single log equal to a constant
  1. Use log laws to combine: $\ln A - \ln B = \ln\frac{A}{B}$, $\ln A + \ln B = \ln(AB)$
  2. Obtain a single logarithm on one side: $\ln(\text{expression}) = k$
  3. Exponentiate both sides: $\text{expression} = e^k$ (or $10^k$ for $\log_{10}$)
  4. Solve the resulting algebraic equation for $x$
  5. CHECK: substitute back to verify all original log arguments are positive — reject extraneous solutions
+ Pros: Clean and systematic; works for all standard log equations
– Cons: Students often forget the domain check, losing the final mark
B: Convert each log term to exponential form separately
When: When the equation has isolated log terms that can be individually converted
  1. If $\log_a(f(x)) = g(x)$, rewrite as $f(x) = a^{g(x)}$
  2. Solve the resulting equation
  3. Check domain
+ Pros: Simpler when there is only one log term
– Cons: Not efficient when multiple log terms need combining first
Formulas
$\ln A + \ln B = \ln(AB)$ (MEMORIZE)
$\ln A - \ln B = \ln\frac{A}{B}$ (MEMORIZE)
$n \ln A = \ln(A^n)$ (MEMORIZE)
$e^{\ln A} = A$ (MEMORIZE)
$\log_a b = c \iff b = a^c$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Misapplying subtraction rule: $\ln(A - B) \neq \ln A - \ln B$
  • Exponentiating incorrectly: $e^{\ln A - \ln B} = \frac{A}{B}$, not $A - B$
  • Forgetting to check that all arguments of $\ln$ are positive in the final answer
  • Confusing $e^{a+b}$ with $e^a + e^b$

Must memorize

  • $\log(AB) = \log A + \log B$ — product rule for logarithms
  • $\log\frac{A}{B} = \log A - \log B$ — quotient rule for logarithms
  • $\log(A^n) = n\log A$ — power rule for logarithms
  • $\log_a b = c \iff b = a^c$ — definition of logarithm
  • $e^{\ln x} = x$ and $\ln(e^x) = x$ — inverse relationship
  • $y = ab^x \Rightarrow \log y = x\log b + \log a$ — linearisation for exponential models
  • $y = ax^n \Rightarrow \log y = n\log x + \log a$ — linearisation for power models
  • $\frac{d}{dx}[e^{kx}] = ke^{kx}$ — chain rule result for exponentials
  • $\frac{d}{dx}[a^x] = a^x \ln a$ — general exponential derivative (formula booklet)
  • $a^{m+n} = a^m \cdot a^n$ and $(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$ — index laws

Global strategy

  • Identify the question type immediately: is it (1) solving an equation, (2) transforming a model/graph, (3) differentiating? This determines your opening move.
  • For any equation with $a^{2x}$ and $a^x$: always think 'hidden quadratic' — write $y = a^x$ and check if a quadratic forms.
  • For graph-based questions: read the axis labels carefully to determine if it's $\log y$ vs $x$ (exponential model) or $\log y$ vs $\log x$ (power model).
  • Master the three core log laws (product, quotient, power) — they underpin nearly every question in this chapter.
  • Never round intermediate values: keep full calculator precision for $a$, $b$, $p$, $q$ etc. until the final answer.
  • Always check the domain for logarithmic equations: arguments of $\ln$ or $\log$ must be strictly positive.
  • When taking logs of both sides, bracket the entire exponent: $\ln(a^{3x-1}) = (3x-1)\ln a$, not $3x\ln a - 1$.
  • For differentiation questions: factor out $e^{kx}$ from the derivative before setting equal to zero — since $e^{kx} > 0$ always, the zero comes from the other factor.

Exam tips

  1. If a question says 'show that' or 'hence find', you must use the given/derived result — marks are for the method using that result, not an independent approach.
  2. When the question specifies 'give your answer in the form $p + q\ln r$' or similar, match that exact structure — rearrange before writing your final answer.
  3. For 'exact answer' questions: do NOT use a calculator to evaluate $\ln 5$ etc. Leave it symbolic.
  4. In log-graph questions, always state the straight-line equation explicitly ($Y = mX + c$ with the log variables) — this is usually worth a mark.
  5. Double-check: $\log(A + B) \neq \log A + \log B$. This is the single most common error in the chapter.
  6. When solving quadratics from substitution, always write both roots and explicitly reject the invalid one with a reason (e.g., '$y = a^x > 0$ so $y = -3$ is rejected').
  7. Label each step in modelling questions: write 'gradient = ...', 'intercept = ...', '$a = ...$', '$b = ...$' clearly — examiners award method marks for these.
  8. Use $\ln$ for natural-log-based questions and $\log_{10}$ for common-log-based questions — do not mix them within the same working unless you apply the change-of-base formula.
