Worked solution

A curve $C$ has equation $3x^2 - 2xy + y^2 = 3$.

(a) Find $\frac{dy}{dx}$ in terms of $x$ and $y$.

*Thought Process*:
We recognize this as a problem requiring *implicit differentiation* because the equation of the curve $C$ has $x$ and $y$ terms mixed together, and $y$ is not explicitly defined as a function of $x$. We need to differentiate each term with respect to $x$. When differentiating a term involving $y$, we treat $y$ as a function of $x$ and apply the chain rule, which means we differentiate $y$ with respect to $y$ and then multiply by $\frac{dy}{dx}$. For the $xy$ term, we must apply the product rule. Finally, we will rearrange the resulting equation to make $\frac{dy}{dx}$ the subject.

*Working*:
The equation of the curve $C$ is $3x^2 - 2xy + y^2 = 3$.
Differentiate each term with respect to $x$:
$$ \frac{d}{dx}(3x^2) - \frac{d}{dx}(2xy) + \frac{d}{dx}(y^2) = \frac{d}{dx}(3) $$

  1. For $3x^2$: $$\frac{d}{dx}(3x^2) = 6x$$
  1. For $2xy$: This is a product of functions $u=2x$ and $v=y$. We apply the product rule, $\frac{d}{dx}(uv) = u'v + uv'$:
$u = 2x \implies u' = \frac{d}{dx}(2x) = 2$ $v = y \implies v' = \frac{d}{dx}(y) = \frac{dy}{dx}$ So, $$\frac{d}{dx}(2xy) = (2)(y) + (2x)\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right) = 2y + 2x\frac{dy}{dx}$$
  1. For $y^2$: Use the chain rule. Differentiate with respect to $y$ and multiply by $\frac{dy}{dx}$:
$$ \frac{d}{dx}(y^2) = \frac{d}{dy}(y^2) \times \frac{dy}{dx} = 2y\frac{dy}{dx} $$
  1. For $3$: The derivative of a constant is 0:
$$ \frac{d}{dx}(3) = 0 $$

Substitute these derivatives back into the main equation:
$$ 6x - (2y + 2x\frac{dy}{dx}) + 2y\frac{dy}{dx} = 0 $$
$$ 6x - 2y - 2x\frac{dy}{dx} + 2y\frac{dy}{dx} = 0 $$
Rearrange to group terms containing $\frac{dy}{dx}$ on one side and other terms on the other side:
$$ (2y - 2x)\frac{dy}{dx} = 2y - 6x $$
Isolate $\frac{dy}{dx}$:
$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2y - 6x}{2y - 2x} $$
Simplify by dividing the numerator and denominator by 2:
$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{y - 3x}{y - x} $$

(b) The point $P(1, 2)$ lies on $C$. Find the equation of the normal to $C$ at $P$, giving your answer in the form $ax + by + c = 0$, where $a, b$ and $c$ are integers.

*Thought Process*:
First, we need to find the gradient of the tangent at point $P(1,2)$. This is done by substituting the coordinates of $P$ into the expression for $\frac{dy}{dx}$ found in part (a). The normal to the curve is perpendicular to the tangent, so its gradient will be the negative reciprocal of the tangent's gradient. Finally, we use the point-gradient form of a straight line, $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$, and then rearrange it into the specified form $ax + by + c = 0$.

*Working*:
Find the gradient of the tangent at $P(1, 2)$ by substituting $x=1$ and $y=2$ into $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{y - 3x}{y - x}$:
$$ \frac{dy}{dx} \Big|_{(1,2)} = \frac{2 - 3(1)}{2 - 1} = \frac{2 - 3}{1} = \frac{-1}{1} = -1 $$
The gradient of the tangent at $P$ is $m_t = -1$.

The normal to the curve at $P$ is perpendicular to the tangent at $P$. Therefore, the gradient of the normal, $m_n$, is the negative reciprocal of $m_t$:
$$ m_n = -\frac{1}{m_t} = -\frac{1}{-1} = 1 $$
Now use the point-gradient form $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ with point $P(x_1, y_1) = (1, 2)$ and gradient $m=1$:
$$ y - 2 = 1(x - 1) $$
$$ y - 2 = x - 1 $$
Rearrange into the form $ax + by + c = 0$:
$$ x - y + 2 - 1 = 0 $$
$$ x - y + 1 = 0 $$

Answer:
(a) $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{y - 3x}{y - x}$
(b) $x - y + 1 = 0$

Key techniques

  • differentiate implicitly term by term
  • product rule for $xy$ terms
  • find gradient at a specific point
  • calculate perpendicular gradient
  • use $y-y_1=m(x-x_1)$

Common pitfalls

  • Forgetting to apply the product rule correctly when differentiating terms involving $xy$. A common error is to differentiate $2xy$ as $2y + 2x$ instead of $2y + 2x\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  • Omitting the $\frac{dy}{dx}$ factor when differentiating terms involving $y$, e.g., differentiating $y^2$ as $2y$ instead of $2y\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  • Algebraic errors when collecting terms and making $\frac{dy}{dx}$ the subject, particularly sign errors or incorrect factorisation.
  • Incorrectly finding the gradient of the normal by not taking the negative reciprocal of the tangent's gradient.
