Implicit Differentiation & Equation of Tangent/Normal
#1 most important

A curve is defined by an equation mixing x and y terms (e.g. $x^2 + 3xy + y^3 = 7$). You are asked to find $\frac{dy}{dx}$, then the equation of the tangent or normal at a given point.

Assumptions
  • The point given lies on the curve — verify by substituting if unsure.
  • When differentiating with respect to x, every y-term picks up a factor $\frac{dy}{dx}$ via the chain rule.
  • Product rule must be applied to any mixed $xy$ term — it is NOT simply $x \frac{dy}{dx}$.
  • Gradient of normal = $-1 / (\text{gradient of tangent})$.
Approaches
A: Standard implicit differentiation
When: Always the default for implicit equations
  1. Differentiate every term with respect to $x$.
  2. For $x$-only terms, differentiate normally.
  3. For $y^n$ terms, use chain rule: $\frac{d}{dx}(y^n) = ny^{n-1}\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  4. For mixed $xy$ terms, apply product rule: $\frac{d}{dx}(xy) = y + x\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  5. For $f(y)$ terms like $\ln y$, $e^{2y}$, $\sin y$: differentiate with respect to $y$ then multiply by $\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  6. Collect all $\frac{dy}{dx}$ terms on one side, factor out $\frac{dy}{dx}$, and solve.
  7. Substitute the given point $(x_0, y_0)$ to get numerical gradient $m$.
  8. Use $y - y_0 = m(x - x_0)$ for tangent, or $y - y_0 = -\frac{1}{m}(x - x_0)$ for normal.
+ Pros: Systematic and always works; examiners expect this approach.
– Cons: Algebra can get messy with many terms — careful bookkeeping required.
B: Differentiate as $x = f(y)$ then invert
When: When the equation is simpler to differentiate with respect to $y$ (e.g. $x = \sin^2(2y)$, $x = ye^{2y}$)
  1. Rearrange (if helpful) so $x$ is expressed in terms of $y$.
  2. Differentiate to find $\frac{dx}{dy}$.
  3. Take reciprocal: $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{dx/dy}$.
  4. Substitute the point to find the gradient.
  5. Proceed with tangent/normal equation as usual.
+ Pros: Avoids messy implicit differentiation when $x = f(y)$ is clean.
– Cons: Must remember to invert; need to convert final answer back to terms of $x$ if required.
Formulas
$\frac{d}{dx}(y^n) = ny^{n-1}\frac{dy}{dx}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{d}{dx}(xy) = y + x\frac{dy}{dx}$ — product rule (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{d}{dx}(e^{ky}) = ke^{ky}\frac{dy}{dx}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{d}{dx}(\ln y) = \frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx}$ (MEMORIZE)
Tangent: $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ (MEMORIZE)
Normal gradient: $m_N = -\frac{1}{m_T}$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to apply the product rule when differentiating $xy$ or $x^2 y$ terms — the most common error across all sessions.
  • Omitting the $\frac{dy}{dx}$ factor when differentiating any term involving $y$.
  • Sign errors in the product rule, especially with negative coefficients like $-3xy$.
  • Forgetting the chain rule factor when differentiating $e^{-2x}y$ or $\ln y$.
  • Using tangent gradient instead of normal gradient (or vice versa).
  • Arithmetic errors when substituting coordinates into a complicated $\frac{dy}{dx}$ expression.

Implicit Differentiation for Stationary Points (dy/dx = 0)

Given an implicit curve equation, find the stationary points. After implicit differentiation, set $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$ which means setting the numerator of $\frac{dy}{dx}$ to zero, then solve simultaneously with the original curve equation.

Assumptions
  • At a stationary point, $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$, which means the numerator of the $\frac{dy}{dx}$ expression equals zero (provided denominator $\neq 0$).
  • The stationary point must satisfy the original curve equation — substitute back to solve.
  • There may be multiple stationary points; find all of them.
Approaches
A: Implicit differentiation → set numerator = 0 → simultaneous equations
When: Always the approach for implicit stationary point problems
  1. Differentiate the curve equation implicitly to find $\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  2. Write $\frac{dy}{dx}$ as a fraction $\frac{N}{D}$ where $N$ and $D$ are expressions in $x$ and $y$.
  3. Set $N = 0$ — this gives a relationship between $x$ and $y$.
  4. Substitute this relationship into the original curve equation.
  5. Solve the resulting equation for $x$ (or $y$).
  6. Find corresponding $y$ (or $x$) values.
  7. State the coordinates of all stationary points.
+ Pros: Systematic and complete.
– Cons: Simultaneous equations can be algebraically demanding.
B: Set $dx/dy = 0$ for vertical tangents (if asked)
When: When the question asks for points where the tangent is vertical (rare but possible)
  1. Find $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{N}{D}$.
  2. For vertical tangent, set $D = 0$.
  3. Substitute into original equation and solve.
+ Pros: Uses the same differentiation work; just sets denominator to zero instead.
– Cons: Rarely asked but worth knowing.
Formulas
All implicit differentiation rules (see Type 1 above) (MEMORIZE)
At stationary point: $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0 \Rightarrow$ numerator $= 0$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Setting the entire $\frac{dy}{dx}$ expression (including denominator) to zero instead of just the numerator.
  • Forgetting to substitute back into the ORIGINAL equation (not the derivative) to find coordinates.
  • Losing solutions by dividing by a variable that could be zero.
  • Forgetting to apply product rule on $xy$ terms during differentiation (same as Type 1).

Assumptions
  • $e^0 = 1$ — always used when substituting $t = 0$.
  • As $t \to \infty$, $e^{-kt} \to 0$ for $k > 0$ — this gives the limiting value.
  • The model is valid only for $t \geq 0$ unless stated otherwise.
  • Rates of change are found by differentiating the model with respect to $t$.
Approaches
A: Substitute-and-solve sequentially
When: Always — this is the standard approach for modelling questions
  1. Substitute $t = 0$ and the initial value to find the first unknown constant.
  2. Substitute a second data point to find remaining constants.
  3. To find when a quantity reaches a certain value, substitute and solve for $t$ using logarithms.
  4. For limiting value, evaluate the limit as $t \to \infty$ by noting $e^{-kt} \to 0$.
  5. For rate of change, differentiate the model with respect to $t$ and evaluate at the given time.
  6. Interpret the result in context (units, meaning).
+ Pros: Methodical and covers all typical sub-parts.
– Cons: Must be careful with algebraic rearrangement before taking logarithms.
B: Logarithmic linearisation
When: When the model is $y = ab^t$ or $y = ae^{kt}$ and you need to find $a$ and $k$ from two data points
  1. Take natural log: $\ln y = \ln a + kt$.
  2. Use two data points to form simultaneous equations in $\ln a$ and $k$.
  3. Solve for $k$ and $a$.
+ Pros: Clean when the model is purely exponential.
– Cons: Doesn't apply when the model has additive constants (e.g. $A - Be^{-kt}$).
Formulas
$\frac{d}{dx}(e^{kx}) = ke^{kx}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{d}{dx}(a^x) = a^x \ln a$ (formula booklet)
$\ln(e^x) = x$ and $e^{\ln x} = x$ (MEMORIZE)
$\ln\left(\frac{1}{a}\right) = -\ln a$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting $e^0 = 1$ when substituting $t = 0$.
  • Algebraic errors when rearranging to isolate the exponential term before taking $\ln$.
  • Applying $\ln$ incorrectly: $\ln(A - Be^{-kt}) \neq \ln A - \ln(Be^{-kt})$.
  • Forgetting the chain rule factor $k$ when differentiating $e^{kt}$.
  • Omitting $\ln a$ when differentiating $a^t$ (writing $\frac{d}{dt}(a^t) = a^t$ instead of $a^t \ln a$).
  • Treating $10^{A-Bt}$ as $10^A - 10^{Bt}$ — index laws don't work like that.
  • Incorrectly reducing fractions before taking logarithms.

Assumptions
  • $e^{kx} > 0$ for all real $x$ — so $e^{kx}$ is never zero and can be divided out when solving $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$.
  • When factorising the derivative, extract ALL common factors before setting equal to zero.
  • Check domain restrictions if any are given.
Approaches
A: Product rule then factorise
When: Standard approach for $y = u(x) \cdot v(x)$
  1. Identify $u$ and $v$; find $u'$ and $v'$ (using chain rule where needed).
  2. Apply $\frac{dy}{dx} = u'v + uv'$.
  3. Factorise the derivative fully — extract common factors (exponentials, powers).
  4. Set $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$ and solve the remaining algebraic factor.
  5. Substitute $x$-values back into original to find $y$-coordinates if required.
  6. For increasing/decreasing: solve the inequality $\frac{dy}{dx} > 0$ or $< 0$.
+ Pros: Direct and systematic; factorised form makes solving for stationary points straightforward.
– Cons: Factorising can be tricky if common factors are not spotted.
B: Logarithmic differentiation
When: When the function is a product of several complicated factors, or involves $x^x$ type expressions
  1. Take $\ln$ of both sides: $\ln y = \ln u + \ln v$.
  2. Differentiate both sides with respect to $x$: $\frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{u'}{u} + \frac{v'}{v}$.
  3. Multiply through by $y$ to get $\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  4. Substitute back $y = uv$ if needed.
+ Pros: Useful for products of many factors or variable-base-variable-exponent functions.
– Cons: Rarely needed at IAL P3 level; can introduce unnecessary complexity for simple products.
Formulas
$\frac{d}{dx}(uv) = u'v + uv'$ — Product Rule (formula booklet)
$\frac{d}{dx}(e^{kx}) = ke^{kx}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{d}{dx}((ax+b)^n) = na(ax+b)^{n-1}$ — Chain Rule (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting the chain rule factor when differentiating $e^{-2x}$ — the derivative is $-2e^{-2x}$, NOT $e^{-2x}$.
  • Forgetting the inner derivative when differentiating $(2x+5)^2$ or $(4x+9)^{1/2}$ — must multiply by the coefficient of $x$.
  • Not factorising the derivative fully before setting it to zero — leads to unnecessarily complicated equations.
  • Failing to combine product rule terms over a common denominator when fractional powers are involved.
  • Dividing by $e^{kx}$ without stating that $e^{kx} \neq 0$ (minor but good practice).

Assumptions
  • The denominator is non-zero at the point of interest.
  • $\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1$ is frequently needed to simplify the numerator after applying the quotient rule.
  • When solving trig equations from $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$, only the numerator of the quotient rule result needs to equal zero.
Approaches
A: Quotient rule directly
When: When the function is naturally a fraction $y = \frac{u}{v}$
  1. Identify $u$ (numerator) and $v$ (denominator).
  2. Find $u'$ and $v'$.
  3. Apply $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{u'v - uv'}{v^2}$.
  4. Simplify the numerator using identities (e.g. $\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1$).
  5. For stationary points, set numerator = 0 and solve.
  6. Use R-formula if the trig equation is of the form $a\sin x + b\cos x = c$.
+ Pros: Directly applicable; formula is in the booklet.
– Cons: Easy to make sign errors; numerator can be complex before simplification.
B: Rewrite as product and use product rule
When: When the denominator is simple (e.g. single term) so $v^{-1}$ is easy to differentiate
  1. Rewrite $y = u \cdot v^{-1}$.
  2. Apply product rule with chain rule on $v^{-1}$.
  3. Simplify.
+ Pros: Avoids remembering the quotient rule formula; some find it more natural.
– Cons: Can produce messier intermediate expressions; negative exponents add complexity.
Formulas
$\frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{u}{v}\right) = \frac{u'v - uv'}{v^2}$ — Quotient Rule (formula booklet)
$\frac{d}{dx}(\sin x) = \cos x$, $\frac{d}{dx}(\cos x) = -\sin x$ (formula booklet)
$\frac{d}{dx}(\sec x) = \sec x \tan x$ (formula booklet)
$\frac{d}{dx}(\tan x) = \sec^2 x$ (formula booklet)
$\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1$ (MEMORIZE)
$R\sin(x + \alpha) = a\sin x + b\cos x$ where $R = \sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Swapping the order in the quotient rule numerator: it's $u'v - uv'$, NOT $uv' - u'v$.
  • Sign error when differentiating $\cos x$ — derivative is $-\sin x$.
  • Misidentifying numerator and denominator when the function has been rewritten.
  • Forgetting to use $\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1$ to simplify — leaving the answer unsimplified.
  • Not checking solutions against the given domain when solving trig equations.

Assumptions
  • The function is continuous on the interval tested — sign change then guarantees a root (Intermediate Value Theorem).
  • Calculator must be in radians mode for any trigonometric evaluations.
  • Full calculator precision must be used between iterations — do not round intermediate values.
Approaches
A: Sign change then iterate
When: Standard approach when asked to verify a root exists and find it numerically
  1. Evaluate $f(a)$ and $f(b)$ where the root lies in $[a, b]$.
  2. State: sign change and $f$ is continuous, so root exists in $(a, b)$.
  3. Use the given iterative formula: substitute $x_0$ (starting value).
  4. Compute $x_1, x_2, x_3, \ldots$ keeping full precision.
  5. Continue until the required number of decimal places stabilises.
  6. State final answer to the required accuracy.
+ Pros: Straightforward if you follow the method carefully.
– Cons: Calculator errors or rounding too early can give wrong answers.
Formulas
Sign change rule: if $f(a)$ and $f(b)$ have opposite signs and $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$, then $f$ has a root in $(a,b)$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to use radians mode when evaluating trig functions.
  • Rounding intermediate iterates — store full precision and only round the final answer.
  • Not stating the conclusion clearly: 'sign change therefore root exists in (a, b)'.
  • Incorrectly rearranging the equation to form the iterative formula.

Assumptions
  • Angles are in radians unless stated otherwise.
  • Domain restrictions on inverse trig functions determine sign of square roots (e.g. $\cos(\arcsin x) = \sqrt{1-x^2}$ is positive).
  • $\sec^2 \theta = 1 + \tan^2 \theta$ is frequently needed.
  • When finding $\frac{dy}{dx}$ via $\frac{dx}{dy}$, you must invert: $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{dx/dy}$.
Approaches
A: Direct chain rule application
When: When $y = f(g(x))$ and both $f$ and $g$ are straightforward to differentiate
  1. Identify the outer function $f$ and inner function $g(x)$.
  2. Differentiate: $\frac{dy}{dx} = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$.
  3. Simplify using trig identities if needed.
  4. Substitute specific values if finding gradient at a point.
+ Pros: Direct and efficient.
– Cons: Must remember derivatives of all trig functions and their chain rule forms.
B: Differentiate $x = f(y)$ then invert
When: For inverse trig functions or when the curve is given as $x = f(y)$
  1. Write $x = f(y)$ (e.g. $x = \sin(2y)$ if $y = \frac{1}{2}\arcsin x$).
  2. Find $\frac{dx}{dy}$.
  3. Apply trig identities to simplify (e.g. $\cos(2y) = 1 - 2\sin^2 y$).
  4. Use the original equation to substitute back in terms of $x$.
  5. Invert: $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{dx/dy}$.
+ Pros: Avoids memorising inverse trig derivatives directly; often cleaner algebra.
– Cons: Must remember to invert at the end; converting back to $x$ can be tricky.
Formulas
$\frac{d}{dx}(\sec x) = \sec x \tan x$ (formula booklet)
$\frac{d}{dx}(\cot x) = -\csc^2 x$ (formula booklet)
$\frac{d}{dx}(\csc x) = -\csc x \cot x$ (formula booklet)
$\frac{d}{dx}(\arcsin x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}$ (formula booklet)
$\frac{d}{dx}(\arctan x) = \frac{1}{1+x^2}$ (formula booklet)
$\sec^2 \theta = 1 + \tan^2 \theta$ (MEMORIZE)
$\sin 2\theta = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting the chain rule factor when differentiating $\sec(2x)$ — derivative is $2\sec(2x)\tan(2x)$, not $\sec(2x)\tan(2x)$.
  • Incorrect chain rule for $(\sin(2y))^2$: differentiating as $2\sin(2y)$ instead of $2\sin(2y) \cdot 2\cos(2y)$.
  • Taking the wrong sign for $\tan(2x)$ — must consider which quadrant $2x$ lies in.
  • Forgetting to justify positive square root when using $\cos(\arcsin x) = \sqrt{1-x^2}$.
  • Using degrees instead of radians in calculator.

Assumptions
  • $\log_{10} x = \frac{\ln x}{\ln 10}$ — change of base is essential.
  • $y = \log_{10} x \Leftrightarrow x = 10^y$.
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(a^x) = a^x \ln a$ for any constant $a > 0$.
Approaches
A: Convert to exponential form and differentiate $x = f(y)$
When: When $y = \log_a x$, convert to $x = a^y$ and find $\frac{dx}{dy}$ then invert
  1. Write $x = a^y$.
  2. Differentiate: $\frac{dx}{dy} = a^y \ln a$.
  3. Invert: $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{a^y \ln a}$.
  4. Substitute $a^y = x$ to get $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{x \ln a}$.
+ Pros: Avoids needing to remember the log derivative formula directly.
– Cons: Extra step of inverting and substituting back.
B: Direct differentiation using known formula
When: When you know the derivative of $\log_a x$ and $\ln(f(x))$
  1. Use $\frac{d}{dx}(\log_a x) = \frac{1}{x \ln a}$.
  2. For composite functions, apply chain rule: $\frac{d}{dx}(\ln(f(x))) = \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)}$.
  3. Simplify the result.
+ Pros: Quick and direct.
– Cons: Must remember the formula accurately.
Formulas
$\frac{d}{dx}(\ln x) = \frac{1}{x}$ (formula booklet)
$\frac{d}{dx}(\ln(f(x))) = \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)}$ (MEMORIZE — chain rule application)
$\frac{d}{dx}(a^x) = a^x \ln a$ (formula booklet)
$\frac{d}{dx}(e^{f(x)}) = f'(x)e^{f(x)}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\log_a x = \frac{\ln x}{\ln a}$ — change of base (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Using $e^y$ instead of $10^y$ when converting $\log_{10}$.
  • Forgetting the $\ln a$ factor when differentiating $a^x$ or $a^y$.
  • Forgetting the chain rule factor $\frac{dy}{dx}$ when differentiating $\ln y$ implicitly.

  • Reciprocal derivative: $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{dx/dy}$
  • $e^{kx} > 0$ for all $x$ — never zero, safe to divide
  • Gradient of normal $= -\frac{1}{\text{gradient of tangent}}$
  • Tangent: $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$
  • $\sec^2\theta = 1 + \tan^2\theta$, $\csc^2\theta = 1 + \cot^2\theta$
  • As $t \to \infty$, $e^{-kt} \to 0$ for $k > 0$

Global strategy

  • Implicit differentiation dominates this chapter — practise it until the product rule on $xy$ terms and the chain rule $\frac{dy}{dx}$ factor on $y$ terms are automatic.
  • Always identify the differentiation rule needed BEFORE starting: product rule, quotient rule, chain rule, or implicit. Many errors come from applying the wrong rule or forgetting to combine rules.
  • When differentiating implicitly, write $\frac{d}{dx}(\text{term})$ next to each term as you go — this prevents you from forgetting the $\frac{dy}{dx}$ factor.
  • For 'show that' questions, work towards the given answer and don't panic if intermediate steps look different — algebraic manipulation will usually match.
  • Exponential modelling questions follow a predictable pattern: (a) find constants using $t=0$, (b) solve for $t$ using logs, (c) find limiting value, (d) differentiate for rate of change.
  • Factorise derivatives fully before solving $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$ — extract exponentials (which are never zero) and common polynomial factors.
  • Keep exact values throughout — do not convert to decimals until the final step (or never, if exact form is requested).
  • Check your answer dimensions: if $\frac{dy}{dx}$ should be a pure number at a point, verify it is — this catches many algebraic errors.

Exam tips

  1. Before differentiating, spend 10 seconds identifying which rules are needed — write 'PR' for product rule, 'QR' for quotient rule, 'CR' for chain rule next to the question.
  2. In implicit differentiation, underline every $y$-term before you start — each one needs a $\frac{dy}{dx}$ factor.
  3. For product rule on $xy$ terms: always write $\frac{d}{dx}(xy) = 1 \cdot y + x \cdot \frac{dy}{dx}$ in full — never skip steps.
  4. In 'show that' questions, if your answer differs by a sign or factor, re-check chain rule multipliers — the factor of 2 from $(2x+5)$ or $\sec(2x)$ is the most commonly missed.
  5. When solving $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$ from an implicit equation, clearly separate: (1) numerator = 0 gives a relation, (2) substitute into original equation to find points.
  6. In exponential modelling, always start by substituting $t = 0$ — this almost always simplifies because $e^0 = 1$.
  7. For iterative methods, write down ALL iterates to full calculator precision — examiners check intermediate values.
  8. Set your calculator to radians at the start of the exam and leave it there.
  9. If a 'show that' derivative looks wrong, try multiplying top and bottom of your fraction by a common factor or using $e^{2y} = x/y$ type substitutions from the original equation.
  10. Time management: implicit differentiation questions are procedural — don't spend more than 1 minute per mark. If stuck on algebra, move on and return later.
