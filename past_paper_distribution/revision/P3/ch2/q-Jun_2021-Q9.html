Worked solution

Thought process: This question requires the standard $R\sin(x - \alpha)$ transformation, then applying it to a related function to find extrema, and finally determining the range of a function involving a squared trig expression.

---

(a) Express $12\sin x - 5\cos x$ in the form $R\sin(x - \alpha)$

Thought process: Expand $R\sin(x-\alpha)$ using the compound angle formula and match coefficients.

$$R\sin(x - \alpha) = R\cos\alpha\sin x - R\sin\alpha\cos x$$

Comparing with $12\sin x - 5\cos x$:
$$R\cos\alpha = 12 \quad (1), \qquad R\sin\alpha = 5 \quad (2)$$

Squaring and adding: $R^2 = 144 + 25 = 169$, so $R = 13$ (exact).

Dividing (2) by (1): $\tan\alpha = \frac{5}{12}$, so $\alpha = \arctan\!\left(\frac{5}{12}\right) \approx 0.395$ radians (3 d.p.).

Since both $R\cos\alpha > 0$ and $R\sin\alpha > 0$, $\alpha$ is in the first quadrant ✓.

Answer: $12\sin x - 5\cos x = 13\sin(x - 0.395)$, where $R = 13$ and $\alpha = 0.395$.

---

(b) $g(\theta) = 10 + 12\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\theta - \frac{\pi}{6}\right) - 5\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\theta - \frac{\pi}{6}\right)$, $\theta > 0$

Thought process: Let $X = \frac{\pi}{2}\theta - \frac{\pi}{6}$. Then the trig part is $12\sin X - 5\cos X = 13\sin(X - \alpha)$ from part (a).

So $g(\theta) = 10 + 13\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\theta - \frac{\pi}{6} - \alpha\right)$.

(i) Since $-1 \le \sin(\cdot) \le 1$, the minimum of $g$ is $10 - 13 = \boxed{-3}$.

(ii) The minimum occurs when $\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\theta - \frac{\pi}{6} - \alpha\right) = -1$, i.e.

$$\frac{\pi}{2}\theta - \frac{\pi}{6} - \alpha = -\frac{\pi}{2} + 2k\pi$$

Key insight: We need the *smallest* $\theta > 0$, so try $k = 0$ first:

$$\frac{\pi}{2}\theta = \frac{\pi}{6} + \alpha - \frac{\pi}{2} = \alpha - \frac{\pi}{3}$$

Since $\alpha \approx 0.395$ and $\frac{\pi}{3} \approx 1.047$, this gives $\theta < 0$. So take $k = 1$:

$$\frac{\pi}{2}\theta = \alpha - \frac{\pi}{3} + 2\pi = \alpha + \frac{5\pi}{3}$$

$$\theta = \frac{2}{\pi}\!\left(\alpha + \frac{5\pi}{3}\right) = \frac{2\alpha}{\pi} + \frac{10}{3}$$

Substituting $\alpha = \arctan\!\left(\frac{5}{12}\right) \approx 0.39479$:

$$\theta \approx \frac{2(0.39479)}{\pi} + \frac{10}{3} \approx 0.25133 + 3.33333 \approx 3.585$$

Answer: (i) Minimum value is $-3$. (ii) Smallest $\theta \approx 3.585$ (3 d.p.).

---

(c) Find the range of $h(\beta) = 10 - (12\sin\beta - 5\cos\beta)^2$

Thought process: From part (a), $12\sin\beta - 5\cos\beta = 13\sin(\beta - \alpha)$, so

$$h(\beta) = 10 - 169\sin^2(\beta - \alpha)$$

Since $0 \le \sin^2(\beta - \alpha) \le 1$:


  • Maximum of $h$: when $\sin^2 = 0$, giving $h = 10$.

  • Minimum of $h$: when $\sin^2 = 1$, giving $h = 10 - 169 = -159$.

Answer: The range of $h$ is $-159 \le h(\beta) \le 10$.

Key techniques

  • Expand R-form using compound angle formula
  • Compare coefficients to find R and alpha
  • Determine min/max values of Rsin(angle) expressions
  • Solve basic trigonometric equations for specific solutions
  • Analyze the range of squared trigonometric expressions

Common pitfalls

  • Forgetting to try both $k=0$ and $k=1$ (or $-\pi/2$ vs $3\pi/2$) when solving $\sin(\cdot)=-1$ and checking $\theta>0$
  • In part (c), forgetting to square $R$ — $(13)^2 = 169$, not $13$
  • Mixing up $\sin(A-B)$ expansion signs, leading to wrong sign on $\alpha$
  • Giving $R$ as a decimal instead of the exact value $13$
