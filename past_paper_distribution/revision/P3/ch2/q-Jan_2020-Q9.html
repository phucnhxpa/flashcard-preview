Worked solution

(a) Express $f(\theta)$ in the form $R\cos(\theta + \alpha)$

  • Thought process: Expand $R\cos(\theta+\alpha)$ using the addition formula, then match coefficients of $\cos\theta$ and $\sin\theta$ with $5\cos\theta - 4\sin\theta$. Use Pythagoras to find $R$ and $\tan\alpha$ to find $\alpha$, checking the quadrant.
  • Working:
$$R\cos(\theta + \alpha) = R\cos\theta\cos\alpha - R\sin\theta\sin\alpha$$ Comparing with $5\cos\theta - 4\sin\theta$: $$R\cos\alpha = 5 \quad (1)$$ $$R\sin\alpha = 4 \quad (2)$$ (Note: the coefficient of $-\sin\theta$ is $4$, giving $R\sin\alpha = 4$.)

Square and add:
$$R^2 = 25 + 16 = 41 \implies R = \sqrt{41}$$

Divide (2) by (1):
$$\tan\alpha = \frac{4}{5}$$

Since $R\cos\alpha > 0$ and $R\sin\alpha > 0$, $\alpha$ is in the first quadrant ($0 < \alpha < \frac{\pi}{2}$) ✓
$$\alpha = \arctan\!\left(\frac{4}{5}\right) = 0.675 \text{ radians (3 d.p.)}$$

Answer: $f(\theta) = \sqrt{41}\cos(\theta + 0.675)$

(b) Describe the two transformations

  • Thought process: We go from $y = \cos\theta$ to $y = \sqrt{41}\cos(\theta + 0.675)$. The question specifies the stretch comes first, then the translation. The vertical stretch changes the amplitude; the horizontal translation shifts the argument.
  • (i) From $y = \cos\theta$ to $y = \sqrt{41}\cos\theta$:

Answer (i): Stretch parallel to the $y$-axis, scale factor $\sqrt{41}$.

  • (ii) From $y = \sqrt{41}\cos\theta$ to $y = \sqrt{41}\cos(\theta + 0.675)$:

Answer (ii): Translation by the vector $\begin{pmatrix} -0.675 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$ (i.e. $0.675$ units in the negative $\theta$-direction).

(c) Find the range of $g(\theta) = \dfrac{2}{90 + 4f(\theta)}$

  • Thought process: Note carefully that the denominator in the question is $90 + 4f(\theta)$, not $90 - 4f(\theta)$ (reading the OCR: the expression is $\frac{2}{90+4f(\theta)}$). We find the range of $f(\theta)$ first, then track through the algebra.

Actually, re-reading the question stem more carefully, the formatting shows `g(θ) = 2 / (90 + 4f(θ))` based on the layout `90` and `4` with `f(θ)` and `+` sign. However, alternative readings of the OCR could give $90 - 4f(\theta)$. I will present the working for $g(\theta) = \frac{2}{90 + 4f(\theta)}$ as this matches the visible `+` sign.

  • Working:
From part (a): $-\sqrt{41} \le f(\theta) \le \sqrt{41}$

Multiply by 4: $-4\sqrt{41} \le 4f(\theta) \le 4\sqrt{41}$

Add 90: $90 - 4\sqrt{41} \le 90 + 4f(\theta) \le 90 + 4\sqrt{41}$

Since $4\sqrt{41} \approx 25.6$, the denominator is always positive (minimum $\approx 64.4$).

Taking the reciprocal (positive values, so inequality reverses), then multiplying by 2:
$$\frac{2}{90 + 4\sqrt{41}} \le g(\theta) \le \frac{2}{90 - 4\sqrt{41}}$$

Answer: The range of $g$ is $\left[\dfrac{2}{90 + 4\sqrt{41}},\; \dfrac{2}{90 - 4\sqrt{41}}\right]$.

Key techniques

  • Expand $R\cos(\theta+\alpha)$ and equate coefficients
  • Calculate $R$ using $R = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$
  • Calculate $\alpha$ using $\tan\alpha = b/a$ and quadrant analysis
  • Identify vertical stretch and horizontal translation from function form
  • Determine range by tracking transformations on the range of $f$

Common pitfalls

  • Incorrect quadrant for $\alpha$ — both $R\cos\alpha$ and $R\sin\alpha$ are positive so $\alpha$ is in Q1
  • Forgetting that taking reciprocals reverses inequality direction
  • Sign error when reading the coefficient of $\sin\theta$: $-R\sin\alpha = -4$ gives $R\sin\alpha = 4$
  • Misreading the denominator of $g(\theta)$ — check whether it is $90 + 4f$ or $90 - 4f$ from the paper
  • Order of transformations matters: stretch first then translate as specified in the question
