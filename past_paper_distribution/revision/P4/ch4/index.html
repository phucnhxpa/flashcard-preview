Using binomial expansion for numerical/surd approximation
#1 most important
5×
2-4m (usually the final part of a larger question)

After expanding, you are told to substitute a specific value of $x$ to approximate a surd or cube root, e.g., 'Hence find an approximation to $\sqrt{3.96}$' or 'show that $\sqrt[3]{7.7} \approx \frac{788}{400}$'. You must identify the correct $x$ value.

Assumptions
  • The value of $x$ substituted must lie within the range of validity of the expansion
  • Only the first few terms of the expansion are used (as directed by the question)
  • The approximation improves with more terms
Approaches
A: Match the target expression to the expanded function
When: Always — this is the standard method
  1. Identify what the original function $(a+bx)^n$ becomes for a specific numerical argument.
  2. Set $a + bx = $ (the number under the root/power) and solve for $x$.
  3. Check that $x$ is within the range of validity.
  4. Substitute $x$ into your truncated series expansion.
  5. Simplify the arithmetic carefully (keep fractions if asked for exact form, or compute to required d.p.).
+ Pros: Direct and always works if the expansion has been correctly found.
– Cons: Arithmetic can be tedious; must carefully match the target to the expansion.
Formulas
The truncated binomial expansion from the earlier part of the question (derived, not from booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Choosing the wrong value of $x$ — e.g., for $(4-5x)^{1/2}$ to approximate $\sqrt{3}$, set $4-5x = 3$ giving $x = 0.2$, not $x = 3$
  • Substituting $x$ outside the range of validity, leading to a poor or invalid approximation
  • Arithmetic errors in the substitution — work with fractions where possible to avoid rounding errors
  • Forgetting to multiply by the factored-out constant $a^n$ when substituting

Assumptions
  • Expand the binomial part first, then multiply by the polynomial factor
  • Only terms up to the required power of $x$ need to be kept during multiplication
  • The validity condition comes from the binomial part only (the polynomial factor does not affect convergence)
Approaches
A: Expand binomial first, then multiply and collect terms
When: Always for these compound expressions
  1. Expand $(a+bx)^n$ as a series up to one power higher than required (to be safe when multiplying).
  2. Multiply each term of the polynomial by each term of the binomial expansion.
  3. Collect like powers of $x$ and simplify.
  4. If coefficient matching is required, equate the resulting coefficients to the given values and solve for unknowns.
  5. State the final expansion clearly.
+ Pros: Systematic; handles any polynomial prefactor.
– Cons: Easy to miss cross-terms when collecting like powers.
Formulas
$(1+X)^n = 1 + nX + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}X^2 + \ldots$ (formula booklet)
When multiplying $(p + qx)(1 + Ax + Bx^2 + Cx^3 + \ldots)$: coefficient of $x^r$ is $pC_r + qC_{r-1}$ where $C_r$ is the coefficient of $x^r$ in the expansion (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Only expanding the binomial to the exact required power and then missing the cross-term contribution from the polynomial
  • Forgetting that $(2+kx) \times (\text{coeff of } x^2) $ contributes to the $x^2$ term AND the $x^3$ term
  • Not factoring out the constant from the binomial before expanding
  • Misreading which coefficient is given or needs to be found

Factorising and solving quadratic equations
#3
2×
4-7m

A standard quadratic equation $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ to factorise and solve. May appear as a sub-step within a binomial expansion problem (e.g., solving for unknowns $a$ and $n$) or as a standalone question in the chapter.

Assumptions
  • The quadratic has rational roots if factorisation is requested
  • Both roots should be stated unless context restricts solutions
Approaches
A: Factorisation by inspection
When: When integer/simple rational roots exist and the quadratic is straightforward
  1. For $x^2 + bx + c$: find two numbers that multiply to $c$ and add to $b$.
  2. For $ax^2 + bx + c$: find factors of $ac$ that add to $b$, split the middle term, and group.
  3. Write as product of two linear factors.
  4. Set each factor to zero and solve.
+ Pros: Quick when it works; no formula needed.
– Cons: Fails if roots are irrational or complex.
B: Quadratic formula
When: When factorisation is not obvious or when asked for exact solutions
  1. Identify $a$, $b$, $c$ from $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$.
  2. Compute discriminant $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$.
  3. Apply $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{\Delta}}{2a}$.
  4. Simplify the surd if possible.
+ Pros: Always works for any quadratic.
– Cons: Slower; more room for arithmetic error.
Formulas
$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Sign errors when identifying $p$ and $q$ in $(x-p)(x+q)$ — double-check by expanding
  • Forgetting to divide by the leading coefficient when solving $(mx + n) = 0 \Rightarrow x = -n/m$
  • Not checking factorisation by expanding back

Finding unknowns by comparing coefficients in binomial expansions
#4
4×
7-9m

You are given $(1+ax)^n$ or $(a+bx)^n$ and told that certain coefficients in the expansion equal specific values, or that the coefficient of $x^2$ is some multiple of the coefficient of $x$. You form simultaneous equations to find $a$ and $n$.

Assumptions
  • $n$ is rational (not necessarily a positive integer)
  • Two unknowns require two equations — typically from the $x$ and $x^2$ (or $x^3$) coefficients
  • The resulting equations may involve quadratics or simultaneous equations
Approaches
A: Expand and equate coefficients systematically
When: When you are given numerical values of specific coefficients and need to find parameters
  1. Write out the general binomial expansion to the required number of terms.
  2. Express each coefficient in terms of the unknowns ($a$, $n$, etc.).
  3. Set up equations by equating your expressions to the given coefficient values.
  4. Solve the simultaneous equations — often divide one equation by another to eliminate a variable.
  5. Check your values by substituting back into a third coefficient if available.
+ Pros: Systematic and works for all configurations of unknowns.
– Cons: Algebra can become messy; easy to make fraction errors.
B: Use ratio of consecutive coefficients
When: When both $a$ and $n$ are unknown — dividing the $x^2$ coefficient equation by the $x$ coefficient equation often eliminates one unknown cleanly
  1. Write coefficient of $x$: $C_1 = na$.
  2. Write coefficient of $x^2$: $C_2 = \frac{n(n-1)}{2}a^2$.
  3. Form the ratio $\frac{C_2}{C_1} = \frac{(n-1)a}{2}$.
  4. Use the given information about $C_1$ and $C_2$ to find $a$ in terms of $n$ (or vice versa).
  5. Substitute back to find both unknowns.
+ Pros: Cleaner algebra; avoids messy simultaneous equations.
– Cons: Only useful when consecutive coefficient values are both known.
Formulas
Coefficient of $x^r$ in $(1+ax)^n$: $\frac{n(n-1)(n-2)\cdots(n-r+1)}{r!} \cdot a^r$ (MEMORIZE)
$(1+X)^n = 1 + nX + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}X^2 + \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}X^3 + \ldots$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to include $a^r$ when computing the coefficient of $x^r$ — the coefficient is not just $\binom{n}{r}$ but $\binom{n}{r}a^r$
  • Division by zero or losing solutions if $a=0$ is not excluded
  • Errors in solving the simultaneous equations, especially with negative and fractional values
  • Not checking solutions against all given conditions

#5
4×
6-8m

Assumptions
  • The expansion is valid only for $|bx| < 1$, i.e., $|x| < \frac{1}{|b|}$
  • The exponent $n$ is not a positive integer, so the series is infinite
  • Terms beyond the requested power are ignored (truncated series)
Approaches
A: Direct application of generalised binomial formula
When: When the expression is already in the form $(1 + X)^n$
  1. Identify $X$ (the 'small' term) and $n$ (the exponent).
  2. Write out the formula: $(1+X)^n = 1 + nX + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}X^2 + \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}X^3 + \ldots$
  3. Substitute $X = bx$ and compute each coefficient carefully, noting that $X^k = (bx)^k = b^k x^k$.
  4. Simplify each fractional coefficient fully (e.g., $\frac{n(n-1)}{2}$).
  5. State the range of validity: $|bx| < 1 \Rightarrow |x| < \frac{1}{|b|}$.
+ Pros: Straightforward when already in $(1+X)^n$ form; minimal algebra.
– Cons: Easy to make sign/fraction errors in the descending product $n(n-1)(n-2)\ldots$
Formulas
$(1+X)^n = 1 + nX + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}X^2 + \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}X^3 + \ldots$ where $n \in \mathbb{Q}$, $|X|<1$ (formula booklet)
Range of validity: $|X| < 1$ where $X$ is the entire expression inside the bracket minus 1 (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to raise the entire term $bx$ to the appropriate power — e.g., $(2x)^2 = 4x^2$, not $2x^2$
  • Sign errors when $n$ is negative: $n(n-1) = (-2)(-3) = +6$, not $-6$
  • Sign errors when $X$ is negative: $(-3x)^2 = 9x^2$ (positive), $(-3x)^3 = -27x^3$ (negative)
  • Not simplifying fractional coefficients fully

Assumptions
  • Must rewrite $(a+bx)^n = a^n\left(1 + \frac{b}{a}x\right)^n$ before expanding
  • Validity condition is $\left|\frac{b}{a}x\right| < 1$, i.e., $|x| < \left|\frac{a}{b}\right|$
  • The constant factor $a^n$ multiplies every term of the expansion
Approaches
A: Factor out constant, then expand (1 + kx)^n
When: Always use this when $a \neq 1$
  1. Rewrite: $(a+bx)^n = a^n\left(1 + \frac{b}{a}x\right)^n$.
  2. Let $X = \frac{b}{a}x$ and apply the generalised binomial expansion to $(1+X)^n$.
  3. Expand up to the required power of $x$.
  4. Multiply every term by $a^n$ (don't forget this!).
  5. Simplify all coefficients.
  6. State validity: $|x| < \left|\frac{a}{b}\right|$.
+ Pros: Systematic and reliable; works for all $(a+bx)^n$ forms.
– Cons: Extra step of factoring and multiplying by $a^n$ introduces more places for error.
Formulas
$(a+bx)^n = a^n\left(1 + \frac{b}{a}x\right)^n$ (MEMORIZE — key rearrangement step)
$(1+X)^n = 1 + nX + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}X^2 + \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}X^3 + \ldots$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to factor out the constant $a$ entirely — applying the formula directly to $(a+bx)^n$ as if it were $(1+bx)^n$
  • Forgetting to multiply back by $a^n$ after expanding $(1+X)^n$
  • Incorrectly computing $a^n$ for fractional $n$, e.g., $4^{-1/2} = \frac{1}{2}$, $8^{1/3} = 2$
  • Getting the sign of $\frac{b}{a}$ wrong when $b$ is negative

Must memorize

  • $(1+X)^n = 1 + nX + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}X^2 + \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}X^3 + \ldots$ valid for $|X| < 1$, $n \in \mathbb{Q}$ (formula booklet)
  • $(a+bx)^n = a^n\left(1 + \frac{b}{a}x\right)^n$ — the essential rearrangement step (MEMORIZE)
  • Validity condition: $\left|\frac{b}{a}x\right| < 1 \Rightarrow |x| < \left|\frac{a}{b}\right|$ (MEMORIZE)
  • Coefficient of $x^r$: $\frac{n(n-1)(n-2)\cdots(n-r+1)}{r!} \cdot \left(\frac{b}{a}\right)^r \cdot a^n$ (MEMORIZE — derived from the formula)
  • Key fractional powers: $4^{1/2}=2$, $4^{-1/2}=\frac{1}{2}$, $8^{1/3}=2$, $8^{-1/3}=\frac{1}{2}$, $27^{1/3}=3$ (MEMORIZE)

Global strategy

  • ALWAYS rewrite $(a+bx)^n$ as $a^n(1+\frac{b}{a}x)^n$ before applying the binomial formula — this is the single most common source of errors.
  • Be meticulous with signs: when $n$ is negative or fractional, the descending product $n(n-1)(n-2)\ldots$ alternates signs in unexpected ways. Write out each factor explicitly.
  • When raising $(kx)$ to a power, remember $(kx)^r = k^r x^r$ — do NOT forget to raise $k$ as well as $x$.
  • For coefficient matching problems, write out the general coefficients symbolically before substituting — this prevents errors and makes simultaneous equations clearer.
  • Always state the range of validity $|x| < \frac{|a|}{|b|}$ as a final step — it is almost always worth a mark.
  • For approximation parts, identify the correct $x$ by setting the expression inside the bracket equal to the target number, then check $x$ is in the valid range.
  • In compound expansion problems (polynomial × binomial), expand one more term than you think you need to catch cross-term contributions.
  • Simplify fractional coefficients fully at each stage — leaving unsimplified fractions compounds errors in later terms.

Exam tips

  1. Write out the descending product $n(n-1)(n-2)$ EXPLICITLY with brackets — do not try to do it in your head. E.g., $\frac{1}{3}\left(\frac{1}{3}-1\right)\left(\frac{1}{3}-2\right) = \frac{1}{3} \cdot (-\frac{2}{3}) \cdot (-\frac{5}{3})$.
  2. Circle or underline the 'first 4 terms' instruction — students often expand too few or too many terms.
  3. For approximation questions, ALWAYS verify your $x$-value is within the valid range before substituting — state this explicitly for the mark.
  4. If a 'show that' asks you to reach a specific answer, work towards it and present your algebra clearly — if your answer doesn't match, recheck signs and powers.
  5. Use fractions throughout rather than decimals — this avoids premature rounding and keeps answers exact.
  6. When multiplying a polynomial by a series, set up a clear grid or table to track which products contribute to each power of $x$.
  7. Check your expansion by substituting $x = 0$: the result should equal $a^n$ (or 1 if already in $(1+X)^n$ form).
  8. In coefficient-matching problems, if you get two possible solutions, check which one is consistent with any stated conditions (e.g., $n$ being negative, $a > 0$, etc.).
