Binomial expansion of (1+bx)^n for fractional/negative n
4× 6-8m

A: Direct application of generalised binomial formula: When the expression is already in the form $(1 + X)^n$
$(1+X)^n = 1 + nX + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}X^2 + \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}X^3 + \ldots$ where $n \in \mathbb{Q}$, $|X|<1$ (formula booklet)
Range of validity: $|X| < 1$ where $X$ is the entire expression inside the bracket minus 1 (MEMORIZE)
  • Forgetting to raise the entire term $bx$ to the appropriate power — e.g., $(2x)^2 = 4x^2$, not $2x^2$
  • Sign errors when $n$ is negative: $n(n-1) = (-2)(-3) = +6$, not $-6$
  • Sign errors when $X$ is negative: $(-3x)^2 = 9x^2$ (positive), $(-3x)^3 = -27x^3$ (negative)
  • Not simplifying fractional coefficients fully

Implicit differentiation & equation of tangent
7× 6-10m
An implicit curve equation (e.g. $x^2 + 3xy + y^3 = 17$) is given along with a point on the curve. You are asked to find $\frac{dy}{dx}$ and then the equation of the tangent at that point.
Jan 2022 Q1Jun 2021 Q5Jun 2022 Q3Jun 2024 Q3Oct 2023 Q5

A: Standard implicit differentiation: Default approach for any implicit curve
$\frac{d}{dx}[f(y)] = f'(y)\frac{dy}{dx}$ — chain rule for $y$ terms (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{d}{dx}[u \cdot v] = u\frac{dv}{dx} + v\frac{du}{dx}$ — product rule (MEMORIZE)
Equation of tangent: $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ (MEMORIZE)
  • Forgetting to multiply by $\frac{dy}{dx}$ when differentiating any term involving $y$
  • Forgetting to apply the product rule on mixed $xy$ terms — treating $3xy$ as if $y$ is a constant
  • Sign errors when collecting $\frac{dy}{dx}$ terms to one side
  • Substituting the point coordinates incorrectly or into the wrong expression

3× 7-10m

A: Implicit differentiation then set numerator = 0: Standard approach when the curve is given implicitly
If $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{N(x,y)}{D(x,y)}$, then stationary points occur where $N(x,y) = 0$ and $D(x,y) \neq 0$ (MEMORIZE)
Product rule: $\frac{d}{dx}[uv] = u'v + uv'$ (MEMORIZE)
  • Setting the entire $\frac{dy}{dx}$ expression to zero but forgetting that only the numerator needs to be zero
  • Forgetting to substitute back into the original equation — finding $y$ in terms of $x$ (or vice versa) is not enough
  • Missing solutions when the substitution back produces a quadratic
  • Forgetting the product rule on the $xy$ term
