Assumptions
  • The normal is perpendicular to the tangent: $m_{\text{normal}} = -\frac{1}{m_{\text{tangent}}}$
  • The gradient of the tangent must be non-zero for the normal to exist as a non-vertical line
  • If finding axis intercepts, set $y=0$ or $x=0$ respectively in the normal equation
Approaches
A: Implicit differentiation then normal gradient
When: Default for all implicit-curve normal questions
  1. Differentiate the implicit equation term by term with respect to $x$.
  2. Apply the product rule for mixed terms ($xy$, $x^2y$, $xy^2$, $ye^{-2x}$, $x^2\ln y$).
  3. Apply the chain rule for all $y$ terms: always attach $\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  4. Rearrange to isolate $\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  5. Substitute the given point to find $m_T$ (gradient of tangent).
  6. Compute $m_N = -\frac{1}{m_T}$.
  7. Write the normal equation: $y - y_1 = m_N(x - x_1)$.
  8. If asked for intercepts, set $x=0$ or $y=0$ and solve.
+ Pros: Straightforward extension of tangent method
– Cons: Extra step of taking negative reciprocal introduces sign-error risk
Formulas
$m_{\text{normal}} = -\frac{1}{m_{\text{tangent}}}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{d}{dx}[x^2 y] = 2xy + x^2\frac{dy}{dx}$ — product rule example (MEMORIZE pattern)
$\frac{d}{dx}[\ln y] = \frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{d}{dx}[e^{f(x)}] = f'(x)e^{f(x)}$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Using $m_T$ instead of $m_N$ in the line equation — read the question carefully
  • Forgetting the negative sign: $m_N = -1/m_T$, not $1/m_T$
  • Incorrectly applying the product rule to $x^2\ln(y)$: must differentiate $\ln(y)$ as $\frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx}$
  • Arithmetic errors when finding where the normal crosses an axis

Triggerstangentimplicitequation of the curveshow that the point lies ongradient
Assumptions
  • The point given lies on the curve — verify by substitution if asked or if unsure
  • Both $x$ and $y$ are treated as functions when differentiating, but $y$ depends on $x$
  • The tangent line is a straight line: use $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$
Approaches
A: Standard implicit differentiation
When: Default approach for any implicit curve
  1. Differentiate every term with respect to $x$, treating $y$ as a function of $x$.
  2. For pure $x$ terms (e.g. $x^3$), differentiate normally.
  3. For pure $y$ terms (e.g. $y^3$), use the chain rule: $\frac{d}{dx}(y^3) = 3y^2 \frac{dy}{dx}$.
  4. For mixed $xy$ terms (e.g. $3xy$), apply the product rule: $\frac{d}{dx}(3xy) = 3y + 3x\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  5. For terms like $x^2\ln y$ or $ye^{-2x}$, apply the product rule with chain rule on the $y$ part.
  6. Collect all $\frac{dy}{dx}$ terms on one side, factor out $\frac{dy}{dx}$, and solve.
  7. Substitute the given point $(x_1, y_1)$ to get the numerical gradient $m$.
  8. Write the tangent equation: $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$.
+ Pros: Systematic, works for all implicit curves
– Cons: Algebra can get heavy with exponential or logarithmic terms
Formulas
$\frac{d}{dx}[f(y)] = f'(y)\frac{dy}{dx}$ — chain rule for $y$ terms (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{d}{dx}[u \cdot v] = u\frac{dv}{dx} + v\frac{du}{dx}$ — product rule (MEMORIZE)
Equation of tangent: $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to multiply by $\frac{dy}{dx}$ when differentiating any term involving $y$
  • Forgetting to apply the product rule on mixed $xy$ terms — treating $3xy$ as if $y$ is a constant
  • Sign errors when collecting $\frac{dy}{dx}$ terms to one side
  • Substituting the point coordinates incorrectly or into the wrong expression

Triggersrate of changeincreasing at a ratedecreasing at a ratecm per seconddV/dtdA/dtdh/dt
Assumptions
  • The chain rule connects rates: $\frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{dA}{dx} \cdot \frac{dx}{dt}$
  • Geometric formulas for volume/area must be known or derived
  • All quantities are positive unless stated otherwise; check direction of change (increasing vs decreasing)
Approaches
A: Chain rule linking rates
When: When you know one rate (e.g. $\frac{dV}{dt}$) and need another (e.g. $\frac{dh}{dt}$)
  1. Identify the given rate and the required rate.
  2. Write the chain rule connection: e.g. $\frac{dh}{dt} = \frac{dh}{dV} \cdot \frac{dV}{dt}$.
  3. Find the linking derivative (e.g. $\frac{dV}{dh}$) from the geometric formula.
  4. Differentiate the formula with respect to the appropriate variable.
  5. Substitute all known values (including the specific value of the variable at that instant).
  6. Compute the required rate; include units and state whether increasing/decreasing.
+ Pros: Very structured; just identify the chain and differentiate
– Cons: Must know or derive the correct geometric formula
B: Differentiate composite expression directly
When: When volume or area is given as a function of a single variable already
  1. Write $V$ (or $A$) explicitly as a function of one variable, expanding if necessary.
  2. Differentiate directly with respect to that variable.
  3. Apply the chain rule: $\frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{dV}{dx} \cdot \frac{dx}{dt}$.
  4. Substitute known values and solve for the unknown rate.
+ Pros: Avoids intermediate linking variables
– Cons: Expanding polynomials before differentiation can be error-prone
Formulas
Chain rule for rates: $\frac{dy}{dt} = \frac{dy}{dx} \cdot \frac{dx}{dt}$ (MEMORIZE)
Volume of cylinder: $V = \pi r^2 h$ (MEMORIZE)
Volume of cone: $V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$ (MEMORIZE)
Area of equilateral triangle with side $a$: $A = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}a^2$ (MEMORIZE)
Volume of prism: $V = A_{\text{cross-section}} \times \text{length}$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Inverting the chain rule: using $\frac{dV}{dh}$ when you need $\frac{dh}{dV} = \frac{1}{dV/dh}$
  • Forgetting to expand before differentiating, leading to incorrect derivatives
  • Using the wrong geometric formula (e.g. hemisphere vs sphere, prism vs pyramid)
  • Forgetting to substitute the specific value of the variable at the given instant
  • Sign errors: a decreasing quantity should give a negative rate

Triggersstationary pointdy/dx = 0turning pointmaximumminimumimplicit
Assumptions
  • At a stationary point $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$, which means the numerator of $\frac{dy}{dx}$ equals zero
  • The denominator of $\frac{dy}{dx}$ must be non-zero at the stationary point
  • Coordinates must satisfy both $\frac{dy}{dx}=0$ and the original curve equation simultaneously
Approaches
A: Implicit differentiation then set numerator = 0
When: Standard approach when the curve is given implicitly
  1. Differentiate the curve implicitly to find $\frac{dy}{dx}$ as a fraction $\frac{N(x,y)}{D(x,y)}$.
  2. Set the numerator $N(x,y) = 0$ (this gives $\frac{dy}{dx}=0$).
  3. From $N(x,y)=0$, express one variable in terms of the other (e.g. $y = f(x)$).
  4. Substitute this relationship back into the original curve equation.
  5. Solve the resulting equation to find the coordinates of the stationary point(s).
  6. Check that the denominator $D(x,y) \neq 0$ at each solution.
+ Pros: Systematic and reliable
– Cons: Substitution back into the original equation can produce difficult algebra
Formulas
If $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{N(x,y)}{D(x,y)}$, then stationary points occur where $N(x,y) = 0$ and $D(x,y) \neq 0$ (MEMORIZE)
Product rule: $\frac{d}{dx}[uv] = u'v + uv'$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Setting the entire $\frac{dy}{dx}$ expression to zero but forgetting that only the numerator needs to be zero
  • Forgetting to substitute back into the original equation — finding $y$ in terms of $x$ (or vice versa) is not enough
  • Missing solutions when the substitution back produces a quadratic
  • Forgetting the product rule on the $xy$ term

Triggersparametricparameter $t$eliminate the parameterCartesian equationcompleting the squarerange
Assumptions
  • $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}$ provided $\frac{dx}{dt} \neq 0$
  • Trigonometric identities may be needed to eliminate the parameter (e.g. $\sec^2 t = 1 + \tan^2 t$)
  • The domain of $t$ restricts the range of $x$ and $y$ on the Cartesian curve
Approaches
A: Chain rule for dy/dx, then eliminate parameter
When: Standard approach for parametric curves
  1. Differentiate $x$ with respect to $t$: find $\frac{dx}{dt}$.
  2. Differentiate $y$ with respect to $t$: find $\frac{dy}{dt}$.
  3. Compute $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}$.
  4. If a gradient at a specific point is needed, find the value of $t$ at that point and substitute.
  5. To find the Cartesian equation, express $t$ in terms of $x$ from one equation.
  6. Substitute into the other equation to eliminate $t$.
  7. Alternatively, use a trig identity to relate $x$ and $y$ directly.
  8. Complete the square or simplify if required. State the range if asked.
+ Pros: Very systematic; each step is straightforward
– Cons: Eliminating the parameter can be tricky for non-standard functions
Formulas
$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{d}{dt}[\tan t] = \sec^2 t$ (formula booklet)
$\frac{d}{dt}[\sec t] = \sec t \tan t$ (formula booklet)
$\sec^2 t = 1 + \tan^2 t$ (formula booklet)
Completing the square: $ax^2 + bx + c = a(x + \frac{b}{2a})^2 + c - \frac{b^2}{4a}$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting the chain rule when differentiating $\sec^2 t$: $\frac{d}{dt}[\sec^2 t] = 2\sec^2 t \tan t$
  • Choosing the wrong value of $t$ (e.g. $t = \frac{3\pi}{4}$ instead of $t = -\frac{\pi}{4}$) when $\tan t = -1$
  • Losing track of domain restrictions when converting to Cartesian form
  • Errors in completing the square

Triggerstangent to the curvehorizontal tangentdiscriminantexactly one pointvalues of p and qtouches
Assumptions
  • A line is tangent to a curve when they intersect at exactly one point (for quadratics, $\Delta = 0$)
  • The equation must be rearrangable into a quadratic in one variable
  • Both solutions for $y$ (or the parameter) should be checked for validity
Approaches
A: Rearrange to quadratic and use $\Delta = 0$
When: When substituting $y = k$ into the curve gives a quadratic in $x$ (or vice versa)
  1. Substitute $y = k$ (or the line equation) into the curve equation.
  2. Rearrange into standard quadratic form $Ax^2 + Bx + C = 0$ where $A$, $B$, $C$ depend on $k$.
  3. Set the discriminant $B^2 - 4AC = 0$.
  4. Solve the resulting equation in $k$ (often a quadratic itself).
  5. State the required values of $p$ and $q$.
+ Pros: Elegant and avoids calculus entirely
– Cons: Only works when the substitution yields a quadratic
B: Implicit differentiation with $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$
When: When the question asks specifically for horizontal tangents and the discriminant approach is not obvious
  1. Differentiate implicitly and set $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$.
  2. From the numerator = 0 condition, express $x$ in terms of $y$ (or vice versa).
  3. Substitute back into the original equation to find the $y$-values.
  4. These $y$-values give the horizontal tangent lines $y = p$ and $y = q$.
+ Pros: Uses standard calculus techniques; more general
– Cons: May involve harder algebra than the discriminant method
Formulas
Discriminant: $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$; tangency requires $\Delta = 0$ (MEMORIZE)
Quadratic formula: $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Using $\Delta > 0$ or $\Delta < 0$ instead of $\Delta = 0$ for tangency
  • Algebraic errors when identifying $A$, $B$, $C$ from the rearranged quadratic
  • Forgetting that $A$, $B$, $C$ depend on $y$ (or $k$) — they are not constants
  • Not checking that the resulting quadratic in $k$ has valid (real) solutions

Triggerslogarithmic differentiationtake logarithms$y = x^{x}$$y = (f(x))^{g(x)}$stationary points
Assumptions
  • $y > 0$ is required for $\ln y$ to be defined (or consider $|y|$ if needed)
  • Log laws: $\ln(a^b) = b\ln a$, $\ln(ab) = \ln a + \ln b$ (MEMORIZE)
  • After finding $\frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx}$, multiply both sides by $y$ to get $\frac{dy}{dx}$
Approaches
A: Take ln, differentiate implicitly
When: When $y = [f(x)]^{g(x)}$ or the function is a complicated product/quotient
  1. Take $\ln$ of both sides: $\ln y = g(x) \ln[f(x)]$.
  2. Apply log laws to simplify the right-hand side.
  3. Differentiate both sides with respect to $x$: LHS gives $\frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  4. On the RHS, apply the product rule if needed (e.g. $g(x) \cdot \ln[f(x)]$).
  5. Multiply both sides by $y$ to isolate $\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  6. Substitute back the original expression for $y$ if needed.
  7. For stationary points, set $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$ — since $y \neq 0$, set the bracket to zero.
+ Pros: Handles variable bases and variable exponents that no other technique can
– Cons: Requires comfort with log laws and implicit differentiation combined
Formulas
$\frac{d}{dx}[\ln y] = \frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\ln(a^b) = b\ln a$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{d}{dx}[\ln f(x)] = \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)}$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to multiply by $y$ (or substitute $y$ back) at the end
  • Failing to use the product rule when differentiating $g(x)\ln[f(x)]$
  • Forgetting the chain rule on $\ln y$: writing $\frac{1}{y}$ instead of $\frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx}$
  • For stationary points: since $y \neq 0$ generally, only the other factor needs to be zero

Triggersshow thatverifyprove that$\frac{dy}{dx} =$implicit
Assumptions
  • Every $y$ term requires $\frac{dy}{dx}$ via the chain rule
  • Mixed terms require the product rule
  • The answer is given — your working must lead to it exactly
Approaches
A: Term-by-term implicit differentiation
When: Always — this is the only approach
  1. Differentiate each term of the equation with respect to $x$.
  2. For $x$-only terms: standard differentiation.
  3. For $y$-only terms: chain rule — multiply derivative by $\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  4. For mixed $xy$ terms: product rule, with chain rule on $y$ parts.
  5. Collect all $\frac{dy}{dx}$ terms on one side.
  6. Factor out $\frac{dy}{dx}$ and divide to isolate it.
  7. Simplify to match the given expression exactly.
+ Pros: Straightforward when careful
– Cons: No alternative method; errors must be found by re-checking
Formulas
$\frac{d}{dx}[y^n] = ny^{n-1}\frac{dy}{dx}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{d}{dx}[e^y] = e^y \frac{dy}{dx}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{d}{dx}[\sin y] = \cos y \cdot \frac{dy}{dx}$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting $\frac{dy}{dx}$ on $y$ terms — the single most common error across all sessions
  • Incorrectly applying the product rule — especially when one factor involves $y$
  • Algebraic slips when rearranging to match the given form
  • Not showing enough working in a 'show that' question — every step must be visible

Must memorize

  • $\frac{d}{dx}[f(y)] = f'(y)\frac{dy}{dx}$ — chain rule for implicit differentiation
  • Product rule: $\frac{d}{dx}[uv] = u'v + uv'$
  • Gradient of normal: $m_N = -\frac{1}{m_T}$
  • Equation of a line: $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$
  • Chain rule for rates: $\frac{dy}{dt} = \frac{dy}{dx} \cdot \frac{dx}{dt}$
  • Parametric derivative: $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}[\ln y] = \frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx}$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}[e^y] = e^y\frac{dy}{dx}$
  • Discriminant for tangency: $b^2 - 4ac = 0$
  • Log law for powers: $\ln(a^b) = b\ln a$

Global strategy

  • Implicit differentiation dominates this chapter (~80% of questions): master the product rule + chain rule combination for $xy$ terms until it is automatic.
  • Always attach $\frac{dy}{dx}$ every time you differentiate a $y$ term — practise this reflex relentlessly.
  • After finding $\frac{dy}{dx}$, read the question: tangent uses $m_T$ directly; normal uses $m_N = -1/m_T$. Many marks are lost by mixing these up.
  • For stationary point questions, set the numerator of $\frac{dy}{dx}$ to zero and substitute back into the original equation — both conditions must be satisfied.
  • For related rates, always write the chain rule connection explicitly before differentiating: $\frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{dA}{dx} \cdot \frac{dx}{dt}$.
  • Show all working in 'show that' questions. If the answer is given, the marks are for the method.
  • Check your final answer by verifying the given point satisfies the tangent/normal equation.
  • Practise product rule on exotic combinations: $ye^{-2x}$, $x^2\ln y$, $xy^2$, $6ye^{-2x}$ — these appear repeatedly.

Exam tips

  1. Circle every $y$ in the equation before you start differentiating — this reminds you to attach $\frac{dy}{dx}$ each time.
  2. When the question says 'find the equation of the normal', underline the word 'normal' to avoid accidentally giving the tangent.
  3. For related rates questions, write the chain explicitly: $\frac{d?}{dt} = \frac{d?}{d?} \times \frac{d?}{dt}$ with blanks filled in before computing anything.
  4. If your $\frac{dy}{dx}$ expression looks nothing like the expected form, re-check the product rule on the $xy$ term first — this is where 90% of errors occur.
  5. In 'show that' questions, do NOT skip algebraic steps. Each simplification earns method marks even if the final answer is given.
  6. After finding the tangent/normal equation, quickly verify: does the given point satisfy your line equation? This 5-second check catches many errors.
  7. For discriminant questions, clearly identify $A$, $B$, $C$ and write $\Delta = B^2 - 4AC = 0$ — label them to avoid mixing up terms.
  8. Time management: implicit differentiation tangent/normal questions are worth 6-9 marks and should take 8-12 minutes. If stuck on algebra for more than 3 minutes, move on and return.
