Assumptions
  • The substitution is usually provided — use it exactly as given, do not invent your own.
  • All parts of the integrand (including dx) must be expressed in terms of u and du before integrating.
  • Limits must be converted from x-values to u-values using the substitution equation.
Approaches
A: Direct substitution (standard method)
When: Always — this is the expected method when a substitution is given.
  1. Write u = f(x) and differentiate to find du/dx.
  2. Rearrange to express dx in terms of du: dx = du / f'(x).
  3. If the integrand contains x terms that don't cancel, rearrange u = f(x) to express x in terms of u.
  4. Substitute everything into the integral so no x or dx remains — only u and du.
  5. Change the limits: substitute the original x-limits into u = f(x) to get new u-limits.
  6. Simplify the integrand algebraically (expand, cancel, split fractions).
  7. Integrate with respect to u.
  8. Evaluate using the new limits.
+ Pros: Systematic and reliable; follows directly from given substitution.
– Cons: Heavy algebra when expressing x in terms of u, especially for rational substitutions like u = (x+a)/(x+b).
B: Split the fraction after substitution
When: When the substituted integrand is an improper or complex rational function in u.
  1. After substitution, if the integrand is a rational function, perform polynomial long division if the degree of the numerator ≥ degree of denominator.
  2. Split into simpler terms (e.g., A + B/u + C/u²).
  3. Integrate each term separately.
  4. Evaluate at the limits.
+ Pros: Avoids partial fractions when the fraction simplifies by simple division.
– Cons: Only works when the rational function is amenable to splitting.
Formulas
$\int u^n\,du = \frac{u^{n+1}}{n+1} + C$ for $n \neq -1$ (MEMORIZE)
$\int \frac{1}{u}\,du = \ln|u| + C$ (MEMORIZE)
$\int \frac{1}{(au+b)}\,du = \frac{1}{a}\ln|au+b| + C$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to change the limits of integration from x-values to u-values.
  • Leaving x terms in the integrand after substitution — every x must be converted to u.
  • Forgetting to replace dx with the expression in du (e.g., leaving dx in the integral).
  • Algebraic errors when rearranging u = f(x) to get x = g(u), especially with rational substitutions.
  • Sign errors when the substitution reverses the limits (if u decreases when x increases, limits swap).
  • Errors in expanding (u-1)² or similar expressions after substitution.

Assumptions
  • Use LIATE ordering (Logarithmic, Inverse trig, Algebraic, Trigonometric, Exponential) to choose u.
  • When the integral recurs (e.g., ∫eˣ sin x dx), set I = the integral and solve the resulting equation for I.
  • For definite integrals, apply limits only at the final step after all integration is complete.
Approaches
A: Standard integration by parts (LIATE rule)
When: When the integrand is a product of two functions from different categories.
  1. Identify u and dv/dx using LIATE: u = the function that simplifies when differentiated (e.g., ln x, xⁿ).
  2. Differentiate u to get du/dx; integrate dv/dx to get v.
  3. Apply the formula: $\int u \frac{dv}{dx}\,dx = uv - \int v \frac{du}{dx}\,dx$.
  4. Simplify the resulting integral.
  5. If the new integral still requires parts, repeat (e.g., x² requires two rounds).
  6. For definite integrals, apply limits to the [uv] term and the final integral.
+ Pros: Universally applicable; systematic with LIATE.
– Cons: Can be lengthy with repeated application; easy to make sign errors.
B: Tabular method (for repeated parts with polynomial × exponential/trig)
When: When one factor is a polynomial (differentiates to zero eventually) and the other is easily integrated repeatedly.
  1. Create a table: Column 1 = successive derivatives of the polynomial; Column 2 = successive integrals of the other function.
  2. Multiply diagonally with alternating signs: +, -, +, -, ...
  3. Sum all diagonal products to get the answer.
  4. Add + C for indefinite integrals.
+ Pros: Much faster and less error-prone for polynomial × exp or polynomial × trig.
– Cons: Does not work for recurring integrals like ∫eˣ sin x dx.
C: Recurring integral method
When: When the original integral reappears after two rounds of parts (e.g., ∫eˣ sin x dx or ∫eˣ cos x dx).
  1. Let I = ∫f(x)g(x)dx.
  2. Apply integration by parts once.
  3. Apply integration by parts a second time to the new integral.
  4. Recognise that I appears on the right-hand side.
  5. Collect I terms: 2I = expression, so I = expression/2.
  6. Add + C.
+ Pros: Elegant solution for exponential × trigonometric products.
– Cons: Students often forget to solve for I or make sign errors in the two rounds.
Formulas
$\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$ (formula booklet)
$\int e^{ax}\,dx = \frac{1}{a}e^{ax} + C$ (MEMORIZE)
$\int \ln x\,dx = x\ln x - x + C$ (MEMORIZE — derived by parts with u=ln x, dv=dx)
Pitfalls
  • Choosing u and dv/dx the wrong way round — leads to a harder integral.
  • Sign errors, especially the minus sign in the IBP formula and when applying it twice.
  • Forgetting the 1/a factor when integrating e^(ax): ∫e^(3x)dx = (1/3)e^(3x), not e^(3x).
  • For recurring integrals, inconsistent choice of u and dv in the second application — must keep the same type as u both times.
  • Algebraic errors when solving 2I = ... for I.
  • Forgetting to evaluate the [uv] boundary term in definite integrals.

Assumptions
  • The DE is separable: can be written as h(y) dy = k(x) dx.
  • Initial/boundary conditions are used to find the arbitrary constant after integration.
  • For limiting behaviour, let t → ∞ and use the fact that exponential decay terms → 0.
Approaches
A: Separate, decompose with partial fractions, integrate
When: When the y-side (or x-side) of the separated equation has a product of linear factors in the denominator.
  1. Rearrange the DE into the form h(y) dy = k(x) dx.
  2. If h(y) is a rational function, decompose into partial fractions (check for repeated factors or irreducible quadratics).
  3. Integrate both sides separately.
  4. Apply the initial condition to find C.
  5. Rearrange to express the dependent variable explicitly if required.
  6. For limiting behaviour, let the independent variable → ∞ and interpret.
+ Pros: Handles a wide variety of DEs; partial fractions always work for rational integrands.
– Cons: Multi-step process with many places for algebraic errors.
B: Separate and use standard integral results directly
When: When the integrals on one or both sides are standard forms (e.g., ∫1/y dy, ∫cos²x dx, ∫cosec y dy).
  1. Separate variables.
  2. Recognise standard forms: ∫1/y dy = ln|y|, ∫cosec y dy = -ln|cosec y + cot y|, etc.
  3. Integrate both sides.
  4. Apply initial conditions.
  5. Rearrange.
+ Pros: Faster when standard results are applicable.
– Cons: Requires memorisation of standard integrals like ∫cosec x dx.
Formulas
$\frac{1}{(y-a)(y-b)} = \frac{1}{a-b}\left(\frac{1}{y-a} - \frac{1}{y-b}\right)$ — partial fractions (MEMORIZE pattern)
$\int \frac{1}{ax+b}\,dx = \frac{1}{a}\ln|ax+b| + C$ (MEMORIZE)
$\int \cot x\,dx = \ln|\sin x| + C$ (formula booklet)
$\int \csc x\,dx = -\ln|\csc x + \cot x| + C$ (formula booklet)
$\ln a - \ln b = \ln\frac{a}{b}$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Incorrect separation: writing (H-5)(H+3) dH = dt instead of 1/[(H-5)(H+3)] dH = dt.
  • Forgetting the negative sign in the DE when separating variables.
  • Forgetting the constant of integration C after integrating both sides.
  • Sign errors when rearranging expressions like -1/A = -3/(8t⁴) + C.
  • Incorrect integration of y² (writing 2y instead of y³/3).
  • Forgetting the 1/a factor in ∫1/(ax+b) dx = (1/a)ln|ax+b|.
  • Errors in log manipulation when combining ln terms.

Triggersvolume of revolutionrotated 2πrotated through 360°solid of revolutionexact volumeV = π∫
Assumptions
  • Rotation is about the x-axis unless stated otherwise; use $V = \pi\int y^2\,dx$.
  • For parametric curves, convert using $V = \pi\int y^2 \frac{dx}{d\theta}\,d\theta$ with θ-limits.
  • For regions between two curves (washer method), use $V = \pi\int(y_{\text{outer}}^2 - y_{\text{inner}}^2)\,dx$.
  • All volumes are positive — take absolute value if integration gives negative.
Approaches
A: Direct integration of πy²
When: When y is given explicitly as a function of x.
  1. Write V = π∫ₐᵇ y² dx.
  2. Square y carefully — expand all brackets (do NOT assume (A-B)² = A²-B²).
  3. Integrate term by term.
  4. Evaluate at the limits.
  5. Include the factor of π in the final answer.
+ Pros: Straightforward for simple functions.
– Cons: Squaring can produce complicated expressions, especially with exponentials or surds.
B: Parametric volume of revolution
When: When the curve is given parametrically as x(t), y(t).
  1. Find dx/dt (or dx/dθ).
  2. Write V = π∫ y² · (dx/dt) dt.
  3. Convert x-limits to t-limits (or θ-limits) by solving x(t) = boundary values.
  4. Expand y² and multiply by dx/dt.
  5. Simplify using trigonometric identities if needed (e.g., double angle formulas).
  6. Integrate and evaluate.
+ Pros: Avoids the need to convert to Cartesian form.
– Cons: Requires careful limit conversion and can involve heavy trig manipulation.
C: Washer method (region between two curves)
When: When the region is bounded by two curves and both contribute to the volume.
  1. Identify the outer curve (further from axis) and inner curve.
  2. Write V = π∫ₐᵇ (y_outer² - y_inner²) dx.
  3. Determine limits from intersection points.
  4. Expand, integrate, and evaluate.
+ Pros: Handles hollow solids correctly.
– Cons: Must correctly identify which curve is outer/inner; easy to subtract the wrong way.
Formulas
$V = \pi\int_a^b y^2\,dx$ — rotation about x-axis (formula booklet)
$V = \pi\int_a^b x^2\,dy$ — rotation about y-axis (formula booklet)
$V = \pi\int_{t_1}^{t_2} y^2 \frac{dx}{dt}\,dt$ — parametric form (MEMORIZE — derived from above)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting the π factor in the volume formula.
  • Not squaring y before integrating — integrating y instead of y².
  • Incorrectly expanding (e^(x/2) - 2)²: must use (A-B)² = A² - 2AB + B², giving e^x - 4e^(x/2) + 4.
  • Incorrectly squaring exponentials: (e^(x/2))² = e^x, NOT e^(x²/4).
  • Forgetting to change limits when using parametric form.
  • Subtracting curves in the wrong order in washer method.

Triggerspartial fractionsexpresshence findirreducible quadraticrepeated factor∫ rational function
Assumptions
  • The degree of the numerator must be less than the degree of the denominator; if not, perform polynomial long division first.
  • For repeated linear factor (ax+b)², include both A/(ax+b) and B/(ax+b)² terms.
  • For irreducible quadratic factor (x²+a²), use numerator (Bx+C), not just a constant.
Approaches
A: Decompose then integrate each term
When: Standard approach for all partial fraction integration.
  1. Factorise the denominator completely.
  2. Write the correct partial fraction form (A/(linear), A/(linear) + B/(linear)², (Bx+C)/(quadratic)).
  3. Find constants by substituting convenient values of x or comparing coefficients.
  4. Integrate each fraction: A/(ax+b) → (A/a)ln|ax+b|; B/(ax+b)² → -B/[a(ax+b)]; (Bx+C)/(x²+a²) → split into Bx/(x²+a²) + C/(x²+a²).
  5. For Bx/(x²+a²): use ln; for C/(x²+a²): use arctan.
  6. Combine and simplify using log laws.
+ Pros: Systematic and always works for rational functions.
– Cons: Finding constants can be tedious; easy to forget the Bx+C form for quadratics.
Formulas
$\int \frac{1}{ax+b}\,dx = \frac{1}{a}\ln|ax+b| + C$ (MEMORIZE)
$\int \frac{1}{(ax+b)^2}\,dx = -\frac{1}{a(ax+b)} + C$ (MEMORIZE)
$\int \frac{1}{x^2+a^2}\,dx = \frac{1}{a}\arctan\frac{x}{a} + C$ (formula booklet)
$\int \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)}\,dx = \ln|f(x)| + C$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting the Bx+C numerator form for the irreducible quadratic factor — using just B is wrong.
  • Not splitting (−2x+2)/(x²+1) into −2x/(x²+1) + 2/(x²+1) before integrating.
  • Incorrect number of partial fraction terms for repeated factors.
  • Forgetting the 1/a factor in the arctan result.
  • Log arithmetic errors when evaluating definite integrals.

Triggersparametricareax = f(t)y = g(t)show that the areaCartesian equation
Assumptions
  • Area formula: $A = \int y \frac{dx}{dt}\,dt$ with appropriate t-limits.
  • Limits must correspond to the x-boundaries of the region, converted to t-values.
  • If dx/dt is negative over the interval, the integral gives a negative value — take the absolute value or reverse limits.
Approaches
A: Parametric area formula directly
When: When the curve is given parametrically and converting to Cartesian is difficult.
  1. Find dx/dt by differentiating x(t).
  2. Determine t-limits by solving x(t) = boundary x-values.
  3. Write A = ∫ y(t) · (dx/dt) dt with the t-limits.
  4. Simplify the integrand using algebraic or trigonometric identities.
  5. Integrate and evaluate.
  6. Ensure the answer is positive (take |A| if needed).
+ Pros: Avoids messy Cartesian conversion.
– Cons: Must be very careful with limit direction and sign of dx/dt.
B: Convert to Cartesian, then integrate
When: When the parametric equations are simple enough to eliminate t easily.
  1. Eliminate the parameter to get y = f(x).
  2. Determine the x-domain from the parameter range.
  3. Integrate y with respect to x directly.
  4. Evaluate the definite integral.
+ Pros: Conceptually simpler if Cartesian form is neat.
– Cons: Often impossible or very messy for trigonometric parametric equations.
Formulas
$A = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} y\,\frac{dx}{dt}\,dt$ (MEMORIZE — not always in formula booklet for this form)
$\cos 2t = 1 - 2\sin^2 t = 2\cos^2 t - 1$ (MEMORIZE)
$\sin 2t = 2\sin t \cos t$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting the negative sign from dx/dt, giving the wrong sign for area.
  • Incorrect limits: not tracing which t-values correspond to the boundary of R.
  • Forgetting to flip integration limits when converting from x to t and the mapping reverses direction.
  • Not cancelling terms properly when substituting u = 81 - 16t² or similar.

Triggerssubstitution x = a sinθsubstitution x = a tanθtrigonometric substitutionsin²θ cos²θexact value
Assumptions
  • The substitution converts the integrand into a purely trigonometric expression.
  • Standard identities (double angle, Pythagorean) will be needed to simplify.
  • Limits change from x-values to θ-values.
Approaches
A: Apply given trig substitution
When: When the question specifies x = a sinθ or x = a tanθ.
  1. Write x = a sinθ (or tanθ), so dx = a cosθ dθ (or a sec²θ dθ).
  2. Substitute into the integrand, replacing all x and dx.
  3. Simplify using Pythagorean identity: 1 - sin²θ = cos²θ or 1 + tan²θ = sec²θ.
  4. Change limits: solve for θ at each x-boundary.
  5. Simplify the trigonometric integrand using double angle formulas if needed.
  6. Integrate and evaluate.
+ Pros: Eliminates square roots and produces tractable trig integrals.
– Cons: Requires fluent knowledge of trig identities; limits conversion needs care.
Formulas
$\sin^2\theta = \frac{1 - \cos 2\theta}{2}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\cos^2\theta = \frac{1 + \cos 2\theta}{2}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\sin 2\theta = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta$ (MEMORIZE)
$1 + \tan^2\theta = \sec^2\theta$ (MEMORIZE)
$\int \sec^2\theta\,d\theta = \tan\theta + C$ (MEMORIZE)
$\int \tan\theta\,d\theta = -\ln|\cos\theta| + C$ or $\ln|\sec\theta| + C$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to change limits from x to θ — evaluating at x-values with a θ-expression.
  • Errors in trig identities, especially when simplifying sin²θ cos²θ = (1/4)sin²2θ.
  • Forgetting the scalar factor (1/a) when integrating trig functions of (aθ).
  • Misreading √3 as 3 from poorly printed questions.

Assumptions
  • The differentiation result is used in a subsequent integration — get it right or the rest fails.
  • Exact algebraic form must match what's requested (fully factorised, simplified).
Approaches
A: Product rule + chain rule, then reverse for integration
When: When asked to differentiate a product and then use the result to find an integral.
  1. Identify the two parts for the product rule: u and v.
  2. Differentiate each using chain rule where needed.
  3. Apply product rule: d/dx(uv) = u(dv/dx) + v(du/dx).
  4. Simplify and factorise to match the required form.
  5. For the 'hence integrate' part, rearrange the derivative result to isolate the target integral.
+ Pros: Connects differentiation and integration cleanly.
– Cons: Factorisation can be tricky with fractional powers.
Formulas
$\frac{d}{dx}[uv] = u\frac{dv}{dx} + v\frac{du}{dx}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = nx^{n-1}$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Failing to apply the chain rule correctly when differentiating composite functions with fractional powers.
  • Errors in algebraic simplification, especially when factorising terms with fractional exponents.
  • Not recognising that d/dx[F(x)] = f(x) implies ∫f(x)dx = F(x) + C.

Must memorize

  • $\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$ — integration by parts formula (formula booklet)
  • $V = \pi\int_a^b y^2\,dx$ — volume of revolution about x-axis (formula booklet)
  • $A = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} y\,\frac{dx}{dt}\,dt$ — area under parametric curve (MEMORIZE)
  • $\int \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)}\,dx = \ln|f(x)| + C$ (MEMORIZE)
  • $\int \frac{1}{x^2+a^2}\,dx = \frac{1}{a}\arctan\frac{x}{a} + C$ (formula booklet)
  • $\sin^2\theta = \frac{1-\cos 2\theta}{2}$, $\cos^2\theta = \frac{1+\cos 2\theta}{2}$ (MEMORIZE)
  • $\int \sec^2 x\,dx = \tan x + C$, $\int \csc x\,dx = -\ln|\csc x + \cot x| + C$ (formula booklet)
  • $\int \tan x\,dx = \ln|\sec x| + C$, $\int \cot x\,dx = \ln|\sin x| + C$ (formula booklet)
  • Partial fraction forms: $\frac{A}{ax+b}$ for linear, $\frac{A}{ax+b}+\frac{B}{(ax+b)^2}$ for repeated, $\frac{Bx+C}{ax^2+bx+c}$ for irreducible quadratic (MEMORIZE pattern)

Global strategy

  • Identify the integration technique FIRST: scan for substitution (given or implied), product of two types (→ parts), rational function (→ partial fractions), or parametric setup.
  • For substitution problems: immediately write u = ..., find du/dx, convert dx, convert all x terms, and change limits — do this mechanically before attempting to simplify.
  • For integration by parts: always use LIATE to choose u. If the integral recurs, call it I and solve algebraically.
  • For volume of revolution: always square y first, then integrate. Never forget π. For parametric, replace dx with (dx/dt)dt.
  • For differential equations: separate variables completely before integrating. Check the separated form by multiplying back.
  • Show-that questions: if your answer doesn't match, check sign errors and algebraic manipulation — work backwards from the given answer if stuck.
  • Always simplify logarithmic answers using log laws: ln a − ln b = ln(a/b), n ln a = ln(aⁿ).
  • Practice changing limits for substitution — this accounts for the most common mark loss across all sessions.

Exam tips

  1. Read the question for a given substitution before trying to invent your own — if they say 'use u = ...', use it.
  2. When changing limits for substitution, write old → new limits explicitly in the margin to avoid mix-ups.
  3. In 'show that' questions, if your algebra doesn't match, try working backwards from the given answer to find where you diverged.
  4. For integration by parts, write u, du/dx, v, dv/dx in a small table before applying the formula — reduces sign errors dramatically.
  5. When squaring y for volume of revolution, expand fully and carefully: (A ± B)² = A² ± 2AB + B², never A² ± B².
  6. Check units/dimensions of your answer: volumes should be positive and contain π; areas should be positive.
  7. For separable DEs, after separating, sanity-check by mentally multiplying both sides back together — you should recover the original DE.
  8. Use exact values throughout (ln, π, surds) — do not convert to decimals unless the question specifically asks for a decimal answer.
  9. If an integral looks impossible, check whether you need a trig identity first (especially double angle formulas to reduce powers).
  10. Time management: substitution and parts questions average 8-10 minutes each. If stuck after 3 minutes, move on and return later.
