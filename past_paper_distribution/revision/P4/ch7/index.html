Assumptions
  • The angle between two lines is always taken as the acute angle ($0 \le \theta \le 90°$)
  • Use direction vectors of lines (not position vectors) when finding angles between lines
  • If the dot product gives a negative cosine, the acute angle is $180° - \theta$
Approaches
A: Scalar (dot) product formula
When: Standard method for finding angle between any two vectors
  1. Identify the two relevant direction vectors $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$
  2. Calculate the dot product $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + a_3 b_3$
  3. Calculate $|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{a_1^2 + a_2^2 + a_3^2}$ and $|\mathbf{b}| = \sqrt{b_1^2 + b_2^2 + b_3^2}$
  4. Apply $\cos\theta = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|}$
  5. If the question asks for the acute angle and $\cos\theta < 0$, take $\theta = 180° - \cos^{-1}(\text{result})$ or equivalently use $|\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}|$
  6. State the angle in degrees (or radians as required)
+ Pros: Direct and formulaic; always applicable
– Cons: Must remember to ensure acute angle for lines; arithmetic errors in magnitudes
B: Using the correct pair of vectors at a vertex
When: When finding the angle at a specific point (e.g. angle ABC in a triangle or parallelogram)
  1. Identify the vertex where the angle is required (e.g. B)
  2. Form vectors from that vertex to the other two points: $\vec{BA}$ and $\vec{BC}$
  3. Apply the dot product formula: $\cos(\angle ABC) = \frac{\vec{BA} \cdot \vec{BC}}{|\vec{BA}||\vec{BC}|}$
  4. Compute $\theta = \cos^{-1}(\text{result})$
+ Pros: Gives the exact angle at the vertex without ambiguity about acute/obtuse
– Cons: Must be careful about vector direction — both vectors must originate from the vertex
Formulas
$\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + a_3 b_3$ (MEMORIZE)
$\cos\theta = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|}$ (formula booklet)
$|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{a_1^2 + a_2^2 + a_3^2}$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to take the acute angle when the question asks for the angle between two lines and the dot product is negative
  • Using $\vec{AB} \cdot \vec{BC}$ instead of $\vec{BA} \cdot \vec{BC}$ for the angle at vertex B
  • Using position vectors instead of direction vectors for angle between lines
  • Forgetting to take $\cos^{-1}$ and leaving $\cos\theta$ as the final answer
  • Calculation errors in the dot product or magnitudes

Triggersshortest distanceperpendicularfoot of perpendicularclosest pointminimum distance
Assumptions
  • The shortest distance from a point to a line is the perpendicular distance
  • At the foot of the perpendicular, the vector from the foot to the point is perpendicular to the direction vector of the line
  • The perpendicularity condition gives a dot product equal to zero
Approaches
A: Dot product perpendicularity condition
When: Standard method — always applicable
  1. Let a general point on the line be $Q = \mathbf{a} + \lambda\mathbf{b}$
  2. Form the vector $\vec{QP} = \mathbf{p} - (\mathbf{a} + \lambda\mathbf{b})$
  3. Set $\vec{QP} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 0$ (perpendicularity condition)
  4. Solve for $\lambda$
  5. Substitute $\lambda$ back into the line equation to find the position vector of the foot of the perpendicular
  6. Calculate $|\vec{QP}|$ for the shortest distance
+ Pros: Direct and systematic; finds both the foot and the distance
– Cons: Can involve messy fractions; arithmetic errors in expanding dot product
B: Using the formula $d = \frac{|\vec{AP} \times \mathbf{b}|}{|\mathbf{b}|}$
When: When only the distance is needed (not the foot), and cross product is known
  1. Let $A$ be any known point on the line and $\mathbf{b}$ the direction vector
  2. Calculate $\vec{AP} = \mathbf{p} - \mathbf{a}$
  3. Compute the cross product $\vec{AP} \times \mathbf{b}$
  4. Find $|\vec{AP} \times \mathbf{b}|$
  5. Divide by $|\mathbf{b}|$ to get the perpendicular distance
+ Pros: Gives the distance in one calculation without finding $\lambda$
– Cons: Does not give the foot of the perpendicular; requires knowledge of cross product (not always in P4 syllabus)
Formulas
Perpendicularity condition: $\vec{QP} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 0$ (MEMORIZE)
$|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + v_3^2}$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Arithmetic errors when substituting fractional $\lambda$ values back into the line equation
  • Forgetting to actually compute the magnitude after finding the foot of perpendicular
  • Sign errors in forming $\vec{QP}$ (subtracting in wrong order)

Triggersareaparallelogramtrianglecross productsin
Assumptions
  • Area of parallelogram = $|\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}|$ or $|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta$
  • Area of triangle = $\frac{1}{2}|\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}|$
  • The angle $\theta$ is the angle between the two side vectors at a common vertex
Approaches
A: Use $|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta$ after finding $\theta$ from dot product
When: When the angle has already been found or is easy to find
  1. Find the two side vectors from a common vertex
  2. Calculate $|\mathbf{a}|$ and $|\mathbf{b}|$
  3. Find $\cos\theta$ using the dot product formula
  4. Calculate $\sin\theta$ using $\sin\theta = \sqrt{1 - \cos^2\theta}$
  5. Area of parallelogram $= |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta$; for triangle, halve it
+ Pros: Uses dot product which is always in the syllabus
– Cons: Extra step to convert from $\cos\theta$ to $\sin\theta$
B: Cross product method
When: When cross product is in the syllabus or you want a direct calculation
  1. Find the two side vectors $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ from a common vertex
  2. Compute $\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \\ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \end{vmatrix}$
  3. Find $|\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}|$
  4. Area of parallelogram $= |\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}|$; for triangle, halve it
+ Pros: Direct — no need to find the angle first
– Cons: Cross product computation can be error-prone; may not be explicitly in P4 syllabus
Formulas
Area of parallelogram $= |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta$ (MEMORIZE)
Area of triangle $= \frac{1}{2}|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta$ (MEMORIZE)
$\sin\theta = \sqrt{1 - \cos^2\theta}$ for $0 < \theta < 180°$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Using the wrong pair of vectors (must share a common vertex for the area formula)
  • Forgetting to halve for triangle area
  • Errors in computing $\sin\theta$ from $\cos\theta$

Triggersvector equation of the lineunit vectordirection vectorwrite downequation of line through
Assumptions
  • The direction vector can be any scalar multiple — simplify if convenient but not required
  • A unit vector has magnitude 1
  • Both $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ and $-\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ are valid unit vectors unless a specific direction is stated
Approaches
A: Subtract position vectors for direction, then form line equation
When: When two points on the line are given
  1. Find direction vector $\mathbf{b} = \vec{AB} = \mathbf{b}_{\text{pos}} - \mathbf{a}_{\text{pos}}$
  2. Write the line equation as $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a}_{\text{pos}} + \lambda\mathbf{b}$
  3. For a unit vector: divide $\mathbf{b}$ by $|\mathbf{b}|$
+ Pros: Quick and direct
– Cons: None — straightforward
Formulas
$\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + \lambda(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a})$ for line through points $A$ and $B$ (MEMORIZE)
$\hat{\mathbf{b}} = \frac{\mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{b}|}$ — unit vector (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Subtracting position vectors in the wrong order (gives opposite direction — acceptable for line but may matter for unit vector sign)
  • Forgetting to consider $\pm$ for unit vector when both directions are valid
  • Not simplifying direction vector when the question expects integer components

Triggersdistance fromfind the points on the linegiven distancequadratic
Assumptions
  • There may be 0, 1, or 2 solutions depending on whether the distance is less than, equal to, or greater than the perpendicular distance
  • The magnitude equation leads to a quadratic in the line parameter
  • Both solutions of the quadratic (if they exist) are valid unless context excludes one
Approaches
A: Set up distance equation and solve quadratic
When: Always — the standard method for this question type
  1. Let a general point on the line be $Q = \mathbf{a} + \lambda\mathbf{b}$
  2. Form the vector $\vec{PQ} = (\mathbf{a} + \lambda\mathbf{b}) - \mathbf{p}$
  3. Write $|\vec{PQ}|^2 = d^2$ where $d$ is the given distance
  4. Expand the left side: sum of squares of each component (each a linear function of $\lambda$)
  5. Simplify to a quadratic equation in $\lambda$
  6. Solve using the quadratic formula or factorisation
  7. Substitute each value of $\lambda$ back into the line equation to find the coordinates
+ Pros: Straightforward setup; always yields the answer
– Cons: Expanding squared terms is error-prone; quadratic may involve surds
Formulas
$|\vec{PQ}|^2 = (q_1-p_1)^2 + (q_2-p_2)^2 + (q_3-p_3)^2$ (MEMORIZE)
Quadratic formula: $\lambda = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Errors in expanding squared terms, especially $(-\lambda - 2)^2$ — must get $\lambda^2 + 4\lambda + 4$
  • Algebraic mistakes when simplifying the quadratic equation
  • Forgetting to substitute both values of $\lambda$ back to find both points

Triggersskewprovedo not intersectnot parallel
Assumptions
  • Skew lines exist only in 3D (not in 2D)
  • Two conditions must BOTH be shown: (1) lines are not parallel, (2) lines do not intersect
  • Direction vectors that are not scalar multiples of each other confirm non-parallelism
Approaches
A: Two-part proof (non-parallel + non-intersecting)
When: Always — this is the only valid method for proving lines are skew
  1. Identify the direction vectors $\mathbf{b}_1$ and $\mathbf{b}_2$ of the two lines
  2. Show that $\mathbf{b}_1 \neq k\mathbf{b}_2$ for any scalar $k$ (not parallel)
  3. Equate the parametric forms of the two lines component-wise to get three equations
  4. Solve two of the three equations simultaneously for the two parameters
  5. Substitute into the third equation and show it is NOT satisfied (contradiction)
  6. Conclude: lines are not parallel and do not intersect, therefore they are skew
+ Pros: Complete and rigorous proof
– Cons: Must remember BOTH parts; proving only non-intersection is incomplete
Formulas
Lines are parallel if $\mathbf{b}_1 = k\mathbf{b}_2$ for some scalar $k$ (MEMORIZE)
Lines are skew if they are not parallel AND do not intersect (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to prove the lines are not parallel (only showing non-intersection is not enough for 'skew')
  • Using only two equations without checking the third — the inconsistency must be shown via the third equation
  • Concluding 'skew' without explicitly stating both conditions have been verified

Triggersposition vectordisplacement vectorscalar productfind the value ofperpendicular
Assumptions
  • $\vec{AB} = \mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a}$ (position vector of B minus position vector of A)
  • If $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 0$ then $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ are perpendicular
  • Dot product is commutative: $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{b} \cdot \mathbf{a}$
Approaches
A: Direct computation
When: Always — compute vectors then apply dot product
  1. Find displacement vectors by subtracting position vectors in the correct order
  2. Compute the dot product by summing products of corresponding components
  3. If an unknown is present, set up an equation (e.g. dot product = 0 for perpendicularity) and solve
  4. If a quadratic results, solve using factorisation or the quadratic formula
+ Pros: Straightforward and mechanical
– Cons: Easy to make arithmetic errors with surds or negatives
Formulas
$\vec{AB} = \mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + a_3 b_3$ (MEMORIZE)
$\mathbf{a} \perp \mathbf{b} \iff \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 0$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Incorrect order for vector subtraction: $\vec{BC} = \mathbf{c} - \mathbf{b}$, NOT $\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{c}$
  • Errors in calculating the dot product, such as forgetting to sum all three component products
  • Sign errors when components are negative or involve surds

Triggersintersectiontwo linesvector equationpoint of intersectionshow that the lines meetfind the coordinates
Assumptions
  • Lines are in 3D space (three component equations to match)
  • Parameters on different lines must use different letters (e.g. $\lambda$ and $\mu$), not the same parameter
  • All three component equations must be satisfied simultaneously for intersection
Approaches
A: Equate components and solve simultaneous equations
When: Standard method; always works for checking intersection
  1. Write both lines in parametric form: $\mathbf{r}_1 = \mathbf{a} + \lambda\mathbf{b}$, $\mathbf{r}_2 = \mathbf{c} + \mu\mathbf{d}$
  2. Equate the $i$, $j$, and $k$ components to get three simultaneous equations in $\lambda$ and $\mu$
  3. Solve any two of the three equations simultaneously for $\lambda$ and $\mu$
  4. Substitute $\lambda$ and $\mu$ into the THIRD equation to check consistency
  5. If consistent, substitute the found parameter into either line to get the position vector of the intersection point
  6. If inconsistent, the lines do not intersect (then check parallelism to determine if skew or parallel)
+ Pros: Systematic and reliable; works for all cases
– Cons: Arithmetic-heavy with three equations; easy to make sign errors
Formulas
$\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + \lambda\mathbf{b}$ — vector equation of a line through point $\mathbf{a}$ with direction $\mathbf{b}$ (MEMORIZE)
For intersection: equate $\mathbf{a} + \lambda\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{c} + \mu\mathbf{d}$ component-wise (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Using the same parameter ($\lambda$) for both lines — they are independent parameters
  • Solving only two equations and not checking the third for consistency
  • Algebraic sign errors when forming the simultaneous equations from equating components
  • Substituting the parameter back into the wrong line

Must memorize

  • $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + \lambda\mathbf{b}$ — vector equation of a line (MEMORIZE)
  • $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + a_3 b_3$ — scalar (dot) product (MEMORIZE)
  • $\cos\theta = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|}$ — angle formula (formula booklet)
  • $|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{a_1^2 + a_2^2 + a_3^2}$ — magnitude (MEMORIZE)
  • $\hat{\mathbf{a}} = \frac{\mathbf{a}}{|\mathbf{a}|}$ — unit vector (MEMORIZE)
  • $\mathbf{a} \perp \mathbf{b} \iff \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 0$ (MEMORIZE)
  • Lines are parallel $\iff \mathbf{b}_1 = k\mathbf{b}_2$; skew $\iff$ not parallel AND not intersecting (MEMORIZE)
  • Area of parallelogram $= |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta$; triangle $= \frac{1}{2}|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta$ (MEMORIZE)
  • $\vec{AB} = \mathbf{b}_{\text{pos}} - \mathbf{a}_{\text{pos}}$ — displacement vector (MEMORIZE)

Global strategy

  • Always use different parameter letters ($\lambda$, $\mu$) for different lines — never use the same parameter for two lines.
  • When checking intersection of two 3D lines, solve TWO equations then VERIFY with the THIRD — this verification step earns marks and catches errors.
  • For angles between lines, always give the ACUTE angle unless the question specifies otherwise (use $|\cos\theta|$ if needed).
  • For angles at a vertex (e.g. $\angle ABC$), both vectors must originate FROM that vertex: use $\vec{BA}$ and $\vec{BC}$, not $\vec{AB}$ and $\vec{BC}$.
  • Draw a quick sketch labelling points and vectors — this prevents direction errors in vector subtraction.
  • When a question leads to a quadratic in a parameter, check the discriminant and consider whether both roots are geometrically valid.
  • Show all working for dot products and magnitudes — marks are awarded for intermediate steps, not just the final answer.
  • Always double-check vector arithmetic by verifying one component independently.

Exam tips

  1. Write out the three component equations clearly and label them (1), (2), (3) — this helps you track which two you solve and which one you use for verification.
  2. When the dot product gives a negative value for $\cos\theta$, the angle is obtuse; if the question asks for the acute angle between lines, subtract from $180°$.
  3. If you get a non-integer $\lambda$ or $\mu$ (e.g. $\lambda = -\frac{31}{18}$), don't panic — substitute carefully using fractions, not decimals, to avoid rounding errors.
  4. For 'show that' questions (e.g. show lines are skew), you must explicitly state both conclusions: (i) not parallel because direction vectors are not multiples, (ii) not intersecting because the third equation is inconsistent.
  5. Check your final coordinates are sensible by substituting back into both line equations if time permits.
  6. In area questions, ensure you use $\sin\theta$ not $\cos\theta$ — a common slip under pressure.
  7. If asked for exact answers, leave surds in your answer; do not convert to decimals unless instructed.
