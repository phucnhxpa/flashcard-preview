Worked solution

**Part (a): Sketch the curve with equation $y = -\frac{k}{x}$ ($k>0$)

*Thought Process:* We recognize this as a standard reciprocal function, $y = A/x$. Since $k>0$, the term $-k$ means $A<0$. This places the branches in the second and fourth quadrants. The asymptotes for $y=A/x$ are always the x and y axes.

*Working:*
The equation is $y = -\frac{k}{x}$, where $k>0$.
The vertical asymptote is $x=0$ (the y-axis).
The horizontal asymptote is $y=0$ (the x-axis).
Since $k>0$, the expression $-\frac{k}{x}$ will be negative when $x$ is positive (e.g., if $x=1$, $y=-k$) and positive when $x$ is negative (e.g., if $x=-1$, $y=k$).
This means the graph lies in the second quadrant ($x<0, y>0$) and the fourth quadrant ($x>0, y<0$).
(Sketch description): Show two smooth branches: one in the second quadrant, and one in the fourth quadrant. Both branches approach the x-axis and y-axis as asymptotes. Neither branch should touch the axes.

**Part (b): Sketch the curve with equation $y = -\frac{k}{x} + k$ ($k>0$), stating the coordinates of the point of intersection with the x-axis and, in terms of k, the equation of the horizontal asymptote.

*Thought Process:* This equation is a transformation of the graph from part (a). Adding $k$ to the entire function shifts the graph vertically upwards by $k$ units. This will directly affect the horizontal asymptote. To find the x-intercept, we set $y=0$ and solve for $x$.

*Working:*
The equation is $y = -\frac{k}{x} + k$, where $k>0$.
This represents a vertical translation of the curve $y = -\frac{k}{x}$ by $k$ units upwards.
The vertical asymptote remains $x=0$.
The horizontal asymptote of $y = -\frac{k}{x}$ was $y=0$. After shifting upwards by $k$ units, the new horizontal asymptote is $y=k$.
To find the x-intercept, we set $y=0$:
$$0 = -\frac{k}{x} + k$$
Add $\frac{k}{x}$ to both sides:
$$\frac{k}{x} = k$$
Since $k>0$, we can divide both sides by $k$:
$$\frac{1}{x} = 1$$
$$x = 1$$
So, the coordinates of the x-intercept are $(1,0)$.
(Sketch description): Show two smooth branches, identical in shape to part (a) but translated upwards. The horizontal asymptote should be drawn as a dashed line at $y=k$. The curve in the upper-right region must pass through the point $(1,0)$.

**Part (c): Find the range of possible values of k for which the curve with equation $y = -\frac{k}{x} + k$ does not touch or intersect the line with equation $y = 3x + 4$.

*Thought Process:* When a curve and a line do not touch or intersect, it means there are no real solutions to their simultaneous equations. This mathematical condition is typically handled using the discriminant of the resulting quadratic equation. If there are no real solutions, the discriminant must be negative ($\Delta < 0$). First, we equate the two $y$ expressions, then rearrange to form a quadratic equation in $x$. Finally, we apply the discriminant condition and solve the resulting quadratic inequality for $k$.

*Working:*
Equate the two equations:
$$-\frac{k}{x} + k = 3x + 4$$
To clear the fraction, multiply every term by $x$. Note that $x \ne 0$ as it is a vertical asymptote for the curve:
$$-k + kx = 3x^2 + 4x$$
Rearrange the equation into the standard quadratic form $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$:
$$0 = 3x^2 + 4x - kx + k$$
$$3x^2 + (4-k)x + k = 0$$
For this quadratic equation, we have $a=3$, $b=(4-k)$, and $c=k$.
For the curve and the line not to touch or intersect, there must be no real solutions for $x$. This means the discriminant, $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$, must be less than zero:
$$\Delta = (4-k)^2 - 4(3)(k) < 0$$
Expand and simplify the expression:
$$(16 - 8k + k^2) - 12k < 0$$
$$k^2 - 20k + 16 < 0$$
To solve this quadratic inequality for $k$, we first find the roots of the corresponding quadratic equation $k^2 - 20k + 16 = 0$. Using the quadratic formula $k = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$:
$$k = \frac{-(-20) \pm \sqrt{(-20)^2 - 4(1)(16)}}{2(1)}$$
$$k = \frac{20 \pm \sqrt{400 - 64}}{2}$$
$$k = \frac{20 \pm \sqrt{336}}{2}$$
Simplify the surd $\sqrt{336}$: $\sqrt{336} = \sqrt{16 \times 21} = 4\sqrt{21}$.
$$k = \frac{20 \pm 4\sqrt{21}}{2}$$
$$k = 10 \pm 2\sqrt{21}$$
The two roots are $k_1 = 10 - 2\sqrt{21}$ and $k_2 = 10 + 2\sqrt{21}$.
Since the inequality is $k^2 - 20k + 16 < 0$ and the coefficient of $k^2$ is positive (meaning the parabola opens upwards), the values of $k$ that satisfy the inequality must lie between the two roots.
The problem also states that $k>0$. We check that $10 - 2\sqrt{21} \approx 10 - 2(4.5826) = 10 - 9.1652 = 0.8348$, which is indeed greater than 0.

Answer: $10 - 2\sqrt{21}
< k < 10 + 2\sqrt{21}$

Key techniques

  • Recognize standard reciprocal function shape
  • Apply vertical translation
  • Determine asymptotes and intercepts from equation
  • Equate functions to find intersection points
  • Form a quadratic equation in x
  • Use the discriminant $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$ to determine number of real roots
  • Solve quadratic inequalities for parameter values

Common pitfalls

  • Incorrectly interpreting the sign of $k$ in part (a) leading to an incorrect base sketch.
  • Algebraic errors when setting up the quadratic equation in part (c), especially when clearing fractions or collecting terms.
  • Misinterpreting the discriminant condition: using $\Delta > 0$ or $\Delta = 0$ instead of $\Delta < 0$ for 'does not touch or intersect'.
  • Errors in solving the quadratic inequality for $k$, particularly finding the correct range (e.g., selecting outside the roots instead of between them).
