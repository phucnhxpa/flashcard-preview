Worked solution

This question tests your understanding of quadratic functions, specifically completing the square to find the maximum point, and then applying differentiation to find the equation of a tangent line parallel to a given line.

Part (a)(i): Completing the square

Thought Process: The question asks us to rewrite the quadratic equation $y = 4 + 12x - 3x^2$ in the form $a + b(x+c)^2$. This is the standard 'completed square' form, which is useful for identifying the turning point of a parabola. Since the coefficient of $x^2$ is negative ($-3$), we expect $b$ to be negative, indicating a maximum turning point. The key is to factor out the coefficient of $x^2$ from the $x^2$ and $x$ terms first.

Working:
Let the equation be $y = 4 + 12x - 3x^2$. We rearrange it to group the $x$ terms:
$$y = -3x^2 + 12x + 4$$
Now, factor out the coefficient of $x^2$, which is $-3$, from the terms involving $x$:
$$y = -3(x^2 - 4x) + 4$$
Next, complete the square for the expression inside the parenthesis, $x^2 - 4x$. To do this, we take half of the coefficient of $x$ (which is $-4/2 = -2$) and square it (which is $(-2)^2 = 4$). We add and subtract this value inside the parenthesis:
$$y = -3((x^2 - 4x + 4) - 4) + 4$$
Now, we can write $x^2 - 4x + 4$ as a perfect square $(x-2)^2$:
$$y = -3((x-2)^2 - 4) + 4$$
Distribute the $-3$ back into the parenthesis:
$$y = -3(x-2)^2 + (-3)(-4) + 4$$
$$y = -3(x-2)^2 + 12 + 4$$
Finally, combine the constant terms:
$$y = 16 - 3(x-2)^2$$
Comparing this to the form $a + b(x+c)^2$, we have $a=16$, $b=-3$, and $c=-2$.

Part (a)(ii): Stating the coordinates of M

Thought Process: The completed square form $y = a + b(x+c)^2$ directly provides the coordinates of the turning point. For a quadratic of the form $y = k - B(x-h)^2$ (where $B>0$), the maximum value of $y$ occurs when $(x-h)^2 = 0$, because this is the smallest possible value for a squared term. This happens when $x=h$, and the maximum $y$ value is $k$. Our equation is $y = 16 - 3(x-2)^2$.

Working:
The maximum value of $y$ occurs when $(x-2)^2 = 0$, which means $x=2$.
Substitute $x=2$ into the completed square form:
$$y = 16 - 3(2-2)^2$$
$$y = 16 - 3(0)^2$$
$$y = 16$$
So, the coordinates of the maximum turning point M are $(2, 16)$.

Answer: M $(2, 16)$

Part (b): Finding an equation for l2

Thought Process: We need to find the equation of line $l_2$. We are given two crucial pieces of information:


  1. $l_2$ touches the curve C (it's a tangent).

  2. $l_2$ is parallel to line $l_1$.

First, we need to find the gradient of $l_1$. Line $l_1$ passes through the origin O$(0,0)$ and the maximum point M$(2,16)$. The gradient of a line passing through $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$ is $(y_2 - y_1) / (x_2 - x_1)$.

Since $l_2$ is parallel to $l_1$, it will have the same gradient. For $l_2$ to be a tangent to curve C, its gradient must be equal to the derivative of the curve at the point of tangency. So, we will differentiate the curve's equation, set it equal to the gradient of $l_1$, and solve for the $x$-coordinate of the tangency point. Then find the corresponding $y$-coordinate and use the point-gradient form to get the equation of $l_2$.

Working:


  1. Find the gradient of $l_1$ (line OM):


Line $l_1$ passes through O$(0,0)$ and M$(2,16)$.
Gradient of $l_1$, $m_1 = \frac{16 - 0}{2 - 0} = \frac{16}{2} = 8$.

  1. Determine the gradient of $l_2$:
Since $l_2$ is parallel to $l_1$, its gradient $m_2$ is also 8.
  1. Find the point of tangency on C for $l_2$:
The equation of the curve C is $y = 4 + 12x - 3x^2$. Differentiate $y$ with respect to $x$ to find the gradient function, $dy/dx$: $$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{d}{dx}(4 + 12x - 3x^2) = 12 - 6x$$ At the point where $l_2$ touches C, the gradient of the curve must be equal to the gradient of $l_2$. So, set $dy/dx = 8$: $$12 - 6x = 8$$ $$6x = 12 - 8$$ $$6x = 4$$ $$x = \frac{4}{6} = \frac{2}{3}$$ Now, substitute $x = 2/3$ back into the original curve equation $y = 4 + 12x - 3x^2$ to find the $y$-coordinate of the point of tangency: $$y = 4 + 12\left(\frac{2}{3}\right) - 3\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^2$$ $$y = 4 + 8 - 3\left(\frac{4}{9}\right)$$ $$y = 12 - \frac{12}{9}$$ $$y = 12 - \frac{4}{3}$$ To subtract, find a common denominator: $$y = \frac{36}{3} - \frac{4}{3} = \frac{32}{3}$$ The point of tangency is $\left(\frac{2}{3}, \frac{32}{3}\right)$.
  1. Find the equation of $l_2$:
Use the point-gradient form of a straight line equation: $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$. We have $m = 8$ and $(x_1, y_1) = \left(\frac{2}{3}, \frac{32}{3}\right)$: $$y - \frac{32}{3} = 8\left(x - \frac{2}{3}\right)$$ $$y - \frac{32}{3} = 8x - \frac{16}{3}$$ Add $32/3$ to both sides: $$y = 8x - \frac{16}{3} + \frac{32}{3}$$ $$y = 8x + \frac{16}{3}$$
Answer: $y = 8x + \frac{16}{3}$

Key techniques

  • Factor out coefficient to complete square
  • Identify turning point from vertex form
  • Differentiate polynomial
  • Find gradient of line
  • Set dy/dx equal to gradient to find tangent point
  • Use point-gradient formula

Common pitfalls

  • Sign errors when completing the square, especially when factoring out a negative coefficient.
  • Using the differentiated equation to find the y-coordinate of the point of tangency instead of the original curve equation.
  • Algebraic mistakes when substituting fractions or simplifying the final equation of the line.
