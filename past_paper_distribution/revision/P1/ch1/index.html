Quadratic Functions: Analysis & Sketching
10×
6-10m

A question asking you to 'complete the square' for a quadratic $ax^2+bx+c$. You'll then be asked to state the coordinates of the 'minimum/maximum point' (vertex) and 'sketch' the curve, showing all axis intercepts and the turning point.

Core strategy
  1. 1. To complete the square for $ax^2+bx+c$, first factor out 'a' from the first two terms: $a[x^2 + (b/a)x] + c$.
  2. 2. Inside the bracket, take half the coefficient of x (which is b/2a), square it, and add and subtract it: $a[(x + b/2a)^2 - (b/2a)^2] + c$.
  3. 3. Multiply back by 'a' and simplify to get the form $a(x-p)^2+q$.
  4. 4. Identify the vertex from this form. The coordinates are $(p, q)$. Remember to flip the sign for the x-coordinate from the bracket.
  5. 5. Find the y-intercept by setting $x=0$ in the original equation. It is $(0, c)$.
  6. 6. Find the x-intercepts (roots) by setting $y=0$ and solving the quadratic, often easiest from the completed square form or using the quadratic formula.
  7. 7. Plot the vertex, y-intercept, and x-intercepts. Draw a smooth parabola (U-shape if $a>0$, n-shape if $a<0$) through these points, labelling all coordinates.
Must-know formulas
Completed Square (Vertex) Form: $y = a(x-p)^2 + q$ has vertex at $(p, q)$ (MEMORIZE)
Quadratic Formula: $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$ (formula booklet)
Common pitfalls
  • Sign errors when extracting the vertex from $(x+p)^2$; the x-coordinate is $-p$, not $+p$.
  • Arithmetic mistakes when completing the square, especially when 'a' is not 1 or is negative.
  • Forgetting to label the coordinates of intercepts and the vertex on the final sketch.

Line-Curve Intersections & The Discriminant
10×
6-9m

You are given equations for a line and a curve, where one contains an unknown constant 'k'. Keywords are 'tangent', 'touches at one point', 'two distinct points of intersection', or 'do not intersect'. The goal is to find the value(s) of 'k'.

Core strategy
  1. 1. Set the equations for the line and curve equal to each other to eliminate one variable (usually y).
  2. 2. Rearrange the resulting equation into the standard quadratic form $Ax^2+Bx+C=0$. Be meticulous with algebra, as the coefficients A, B, and C will often involve 'k'.
  3. 3. Identify the condition from the question and select the correct discriminant test.
  4. 4. Apply the discriminant: for two distinct points, use $B^2-4AC > 0$; for a tangent (one point), use $B^2-4AC = 0$; for no intersection, use $B^2-4AC < 0$.
  5. 5. This creates a new equation or inequality involving 'k'. Solve it to find the required values.
  6. 6. If you get a quadratic inequality in 'k', find the critical values and use a sketch or number line to determine the correct range(s).
Must-know formulas
The discriminant, $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$ (formula booklet)
Discriminant Conditions: $b^2-4ac > 0 \implies$ 2 distinct real roots; $b^2-4ac = 0 \implies$ 1 repeated real root; $b^2-4ac < 0 \implies$ no real roots (MEMORIZE)
Common pitfalls
  • Making algebraic errors when expanding brackets or collecting terms to form the $Ax^2+Bx+C=0$ equation.
  • Incorrectly identifying the coefficients A, B, and C, especially when they are complex expressions with 'k'.
  • Solving the final quadratic inequality for 'k' incorrectly, for example, choosing the region between the roots when it should be the outer regions.

Graph Transformations
9×
6-8m

You are given a sketch of $y=f(x)$ with key points marked. You'll be asked to sketch a transformed graph, like $y=f(x+2)$ or $y = -f(2x)$, showing the new coordinates of the key points. Alternatively, you might be asked to describe the transformations that map one function to another.

Core strategy
  1. 1. Deconstruct the transformation: changes inside the function bracket $f(...)$ are horizontal; changes outside are vertical.
  2. 2. Remember the rules: $f(x)+a$ is a vertical shift up by a. $f(x+a)$ is a horizontal shift LEFT by a. $af(x)$ is a vertical stretch by factor a. $f(ax)$ is a horizontal stretch by factor 1/a.
  3. 3. Pick a key point $(x, y)$ from the original graph.
  4. 4. Apply horizontal transformations to the x-coordinate. Remember they are often counter-intuitive (e.g., for $f(2x-6)$, you solve $2x'-6=x$ for $x'$, implying stretch by 1/2 then shift right by 3).
  5. 5. Apply vertical transformations to the y-coordinate.
  6. 6. Repeat for all key points and any asymptotes.
  7. 7. Sketch the new curve through the new points, ensuring the shape is preserved.
Must-know formulas
$y=f(x)+a$: Translation by vector $\begin{pmatrix}0\\a\end{pmatrix}$ (MEMORIZE)
$y=f(x+a)$: Translation by vector $\begin{pmatrix}-a\\0\end{pmatrix}$ (MEMORIZE)
$y=af(x)$: Vertical stretch, scale factor $a$, parallel to y-axis (MEMORIZE)
$y=f(ax)$: Horizontal stretch, scale factor $1/a$, parallel to x-axis (MEMORIZE)
Common pitfalls
  • Confusing $f(x+a)$ as a shift to the right instead of to the left.
  • Applying a horizontal stretch for $f(ax)$ with a scale factor of 'a' instead of '1/a'.
  • Incorrectly transforming asymptotes, e.g., applying a vertical shift to a vertical asymptote.

Indices & Surds
9×
3-7m

A question asking you to 'simplify' an expression with fractional or negative indices, or to 'express in the form $a+b\sqrt{c}$' or 'rationalise the denominator'. These are pure algebraic manipulation tasks.

Core strategy
  1. 1. For indices problems, ensure every term is written as a power of its prime factors (e.g., $9^x = (3^2)^x = 3^{2x}$).
  2. 2. Systematically apply the laws of indices to combine terms. Work step-by-step to avoid errors.
  3. 3. For surd problems, simplify any surds first by finding square factors (e.g., $\sqrt{75} = \sqrt{25 \times 3} = 5\sqrt{3}$).
  4. 4. To rationalise a denominator of the form $a \pm \sqrt{b}$, multiply the numerator and denominator by its conjugate, $a \mp \sqrt{b}$.
  5. 5. Use the difference of two squares, $(x-y)(x+y)=x^2-y^2$, to simplify the new denominator.
  6. 6. Expand the numerator and collect like terms to get the final answer in the required format.
Must-know formulas
Laws of Indices: $a^m a^n=a^{m+n}$, $(a^m)^n=a^{mn}$, $a^{-n}=1/a^n$, $a^{1/n}=\sqrt[n]{a}$ (MEMORIZE)
Rationalising with a conjugate: To rationalise $\frac{c}{a+\sqrt{b}}$, multiply by $\frac{a-\sqrt{b}}{a-\sqrt{b}}$ (MEMORIZE)
Common pitfalls
  • Forgetting to apply a power to the numerical coefficient, e.g., $(3x^2)^3 = 27x^6$, not $3x^6$.
  • Making sign errors when expanding brackets, especially when rationalising using a conjugate.
  • Incorrectly adding or subtracting indices instead of multiplying them, e.g., $(x^3)^2 = x^5$ instead of $x^6$.

Solving Equations in Disguise (Substitution)
6×
5-7m

An equation that isn't a standard quadratic, but has a repeated element that can be substituted. Common examples are exponential equations (e.g., $9^x - 10(3^x) + 9 = 0$) or polynomials with related powers (e.g., $x^4 - 5x^2 + 4 = 0$).

Core strategy
  1. 1. Use laws of indices or algebra to rewrite the equation so the underlying structure is clear. E.g., rewrite $9^x$ as $(3^x)^2$.
  2. 2. Identify the repeated term (e.g., $3^x$ or $x^2$) and substitute it with a new variable, like 'u'. This transforms the equation into a standard quadratic (e.g., $u^2 - 10u + 9 = 0$).
  3. 3. Solve this new quadratic for 'u' by factorising or using the formula.
  4. 4. For each valid solution for 'u', substitute back to find the original variable 'x'. (e.g., $3^x = 9 \implies x=2$ and $3^x = 1 \implies x=0$).
  5. 5. Check for invalid solutions. For example, if $u=2^x$, then 'u' must be positive. If $u=x^2$, 'u' cannot be negative. Discard any solutions for 'u' that don't fit these constraints.
Must-know formulas
Laws of Indices are essential for the setup (MEMORIZE)
Quadratic Formula: $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$ (formula booklet)
Common pitfalls
  • Stopping after solving for the substituted variable 'u' and forgetting to solve for 'x'.
  • Incorrectly applying index laws, such as writing $3^{x+1}$ as $3^x + 3^1$ instead of $3^x \times 3^1$.
  • Failing to reject invalid solutions for 'u' (e.g., accepting a negative value for $2^x$).

Regions, Lines & Curve Intersections
14×
7-10m

A multi-part question that involves finding points of intersection between a line and a curve, and/or defining a shaded region 'R' on a graph using inequalities.

Core strategy
  1. 1. To find intersection points, use substitution to solve the equations simultaneously. This usually leads to a quadratic equation.
  2. 2. Solve the quadratic for x (by factorising, formula, or completing the square).
  3. 3. Substitute each x-value back into the *linear* equation to find the corresponding y-value for each intersection point.
  4. 4. To define a region 'R', first find the equation of each boundary line and/or curve.
  5. 5. For each boundary, choose a test point well inside 'R'. Substitute its coordinates into the boundary's equation.
  6. 6. Determine if the region is where y is 'greater than' or 'less than' the boundary line/curve. Use $>$ or $<$ for dotted lines and $\ge$ or $\le$ for solid lines.
  7. 7. Write down the set of inequalities that define the region.
Must-know formulas
Equation of a straight line: $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ (formula booklet)
Gradient of a line: $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$ (formula booklet)
Common pitfalls
  • Algebraic errors when rearranging the simultaneous equations into a quadratic.
  • Forgetting to find the y-coordinates after finding the x-coordinates of intersection points.
  • Choosing the wrong inequality sign (e.g., $>$ instead of $<$) or using $\le$ for a dotted boundary line.

Must memorize

  • Discriminant Conditions: $b^2-4ac > 0$ (two distinct real roots), $b^2-4ac = 0$ (one repeated real root), $b^2-4ac < 0$ (no real roots).
  • Completed Square (Vertex) Form: A quadratic $y = a(x-p)^2+q$ has its vertex at $(p, q)$.
  • Graph Transformation Rules: $f(x)+a$ (vertical shift), $f(x+a)$ (horizontal shift), $af(x)$ (vertical stretch), $f(ax)$ (horizontal stretch). Know the direction and scale factor for each.
  • Laws of Indices: $a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n}$, $a^m \div a^n = a^{m-n}$, $(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$, $a^{-n} = 1/a^n$, $a^{m/n} = (\sqrt[n]{a})^m$.
  • Rationalising with the Conjugate: To rationalise a denominator of the form $a+\sqrt{b}$, multiply the numerator and denominator by $a-\sqrt{b}$.

Global strategy

  • Master Quadratic Theory: Fluency in completing the square, using the quadratic formula, and applying the discriminant is the single most important skill set for this chapter. It is the core of at least four major question types.
  • Prioritise Algebraic Accuracy: The vast majority of dropped marks come from simple mistakes in expanding brackets, collecting terms with negative signs, or errors in index laws. Always double-check your algebraic manipulation.
  • Sketch to Visualise: Even when not required, a quick, simple sketch of the graphs can provide valuable insight into the problem, helping you understand intersections, regions, and transformations.
  • Translate Keywords to Maths: Train yourself to instantly connect key phrases to mathematical techniques. 'Tangent' means $b^2-4ac=0$. 'Does not intersect' means $b^2-4ac<0$. 'Minimum point' means complete the square. 'Exact form' means leave your answer with surds or fractions, not decimals.
  • Build from the Basics: Ensure you have a rock-solid foundation in index laws and surd manipulation. These skills are often the first step in a longer, more complex problem.

Exam tips

  1. If a question involves finding a range of values for a constant 'k' for a certain number of intersections, your final step will almost always be solving a quadratic inequality in 'k'.
  2. When asked to 'Show that...' a particular equation can be formed, you must show every single step of your algebraic rearrangement. Do not skip steps, as marks are awarded for a clear, logical progression.
  3. After solving simultaneous equations, quickly substitute your final $(x, y)$ coordinate pairs back into both of the original equations to verify your solution. It's a fast way to catch errors.
  4. Pay close attention to the distinction between strict ($<, >$) and non-strict ($\le, \ge$) inequalities. This is often linked to whether a boundary line is dotted or solid.
  5. If you are stuck on a multi-part question, check if you can use the 'given' result from a previous part to attempt the next part, even if you couldn't prove it yourself.
