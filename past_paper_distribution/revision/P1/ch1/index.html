Triggerscomplete the squarevertexminimum pointmaximum pointline of symmetrysketch the curvein the form $A(x+B)^2+C$
Assumptions
  • The graph of a quadratic is a symmetrical parabola.
  • The vertex represents the global minimum if the $x^2$ coefficient is positive, and the global maximum if it is negative.
Approaches
A: Completing the Square
When: When asked explicitly or when finding the vertex/turning point is the primary goal.
  1. Start with $f(x) = ax^2+bx+c$.
  2. Factor out the coefficient 'a' from the first two terms: $a[x^2 + (b/a)x] + c$.
  3. Inside the bracket, take half the coefficient of x, square it, and add and subtract it: $a[x^2 + (b/a)x + (b/2a)^2 - (b/2a)^2] + c$.
  4. The first three terms inside the bracket now form a perfect square: $a[(x+b/2a)^2 - (b/2a)^2] + c$.
  5. Expand the outer bracket and simplify: $a(x+b/2a)^2 - a(b^2/4a^2) + c = a(x+b/2a)^2 + (c - b^2/4a)$.
  6. Identify the vertex from the form $A(x-h)^2+k$ as $(h,k)$.
+ Pros: Guaranteed method to find the exact coordinates of the vertex and the line of symmetry.
– Cons: Can be tricky with fractions or when 'a' is not 1 or -1.
B: Using Calculus ($dy/dx=0$)
When: An alternative for finding the x-coordinate of the vertex, especially if differentiation is more comfortable for the student.
  1. Find the derivative: $f'(x) = 2ax + b$.
  2. Set the derivative to zero to find the stationary point: $2ax+b=0$.
  3. Solve for x: $x = -b/2a$. This is the x-coordinate of the vertex and the equation of the line of symmetry.
  4. Substitute this x-value back into the original function $f(x)$ to find the y-coordinate of the vertex.
+ Pros: Often quicker and less prone to algebraic slips than completing the square.
– Cons: Doesn't directly give the completed square form if the question specifically asks for it.
Formulas
$y=a(x-h)^2+k$ means vertex is at $(h,k)$ (MEMORIZE)
Line of symmetry is at $x = -b/2a$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Errors when factoring out the coefficient 'a', especially if it's negative.
  • Sign errors when identifying the vertex coordinates from the form $a(x+p)^2+q$. The vertex is at $(-p, q)$.
  • Forgetting to multiply the constant term by 'a' when taking it out of the bracket during completing the square.
  • When sketching, incorrectly identifying the y-intercept (found by setting x=0) or x-intercepts (found by setting y=0).

Triggerssolvehence or otherwiseuse the substitution y = ...disguised quadratic
Assumptions
  • The substitution must be mathematically valid (e.g., if $u=2^x$, then $u$ must be positive).
  • All solutions for the original variable must be found after solving for the substituted variable.
Approaches
A: Substitution Method
When: Standard method for all 'disguised quadratic' problems.
  1. Use index laws or algebraic rules to rewrite the equation so the repeated term is obvious. (e.g., rewrite $4^x$ as $(2^x)^2$).
  2. Define a substitution, e.g., 'Let $u = 2^x$'.
  3. Rewrite the entire equation in terms of 'u', which should now be a standard quadratic: $au^2+bu+c=0$.
  4. Solve the quadratic for 'u' by factorising or using the formula.
  5. For each valid solution for 'u', substitute back to find the original variable. (e.g., $2^x = u_1$ and $2^x = u_2$).
  6. Reject any invalid solutions (e.g., $2^x = -1$ has no real solution).
+ Pros: Transforms a complex problem into a standard, solvable quadratic.
– Cons: Requires strong skills in index laws and recognizing the underlying structure.
Formulas
$a^{m+n} = a^m a^n$ (MEMORIZE)
$a^{mn} = (a^m)^n$ (MEMORIZE)
$a^{-m} = 1/a^m$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Incorrect application of index laws, such as writing $2^{2x+1}$ as $2^{2x} + 2^1$ instead of $2^{2x} \times 2^1$.
  • Forgetting to solve for the original variable after finding the values for 'u'.
  • Failing to reject invalid solutions, such as negative results for an exponential substitution like $u=a^x$.
  • Algebraic errors when factorizing the resulting quadratic.

Triggersintersectiontangenttouch the curverepeated roottwo distinct real rootsno real rootsshow that the line and curve do not meet
Assumptions
  • The number of real solutions to the combined quadratic equation equals the number of intersection points.
  • The discriminant determines the nature of the roots of a quadratic equation.
Approaches
A: Finding Points of Intersection
When: When asked for the coordinates of intersection points.
  1. Set the two equations equal to each other (e.g., $y_{line} = y_{curve}$).
  2. Rearrange the resulting equation into the standard quadratic form $Ax^2+Bx+C=0$.
  3. Solve the quadratic for $x$ using factorisation, the quadratic formula, or completing the square.
  4. Substitute each $x$-value back into one of the original equations (usually the linear one is easier) to find the corresponding $y$-coordinate.
  5. State the coordinates of the intersection points clearly.
+ Pros: Directly finds the exact points where the line and curve meet.
– Cons: Can involve messy algebra and surds if the quadratic does not factorize nicely.
B: Using the Discriminant for Conditions of Intersection
When: When asked to find a value or range of values for a constant 'k' so that the line and curve have a specific number of intersections (e.g., are tangent, do not meet).
  1. Set the two equations equal to each other.
  2. Rearrange into the standard quadratic form $Ax^2+Bx+C=0$, where the coefficients A, B, and C may contain the constant 'k'.
  3. Identify the correct condition for the discriminant, $\Delta = b^2-4ac$:
  4. - Two distinct intersections $\implies$ Two distinct real roots $\implies b^2-4ac > 0$.
  5. - One intersection (tangent) $\implies$ One repeated real root $\implies b^2-4ac = 0$.
  6. - No intersections $\implies$ No real roots $\implies b^2-4ac < 0$.
  7. Substitute A, B, C into the chosen inequality/equation and solve for 'k'. This often results in a quadratic inequality.
+ Pros: Powerful way to find conditions without solving for the actual intersection points.
– Cons: Very prone to algebraic errors, especially when expanding brackets with 'k' or identifying coefficients A, B, and C.
Formulas
$b^2-4ac > 0 \implies$ 2 distinct real roots (MEMORIZE)
$b^2-4ac = 0 \implies$ 1 repeated real root (tangent) (MEMORIZE)
$b^2-4ac < 0 \implies$ no real roots (MEMORIZE)
$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}$ (Quadratic Formula) (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Algebraic errors when collecting terms to form $Ax^2+Bx+C=0$, especially with negative signs.
  • Incorrectly identifying the coefficients A, B, and C when they involve an unknown constant 'k'.
  • Using the wrong discriminant condition (e.g., using = 0 when the question asks for 'no intersections').
  • Mistakes when solving the final quadratic inequality for 'k', such as finding incorrect critical values or selecting the wrong region.

Triggerssimplifyindicessurdsrationalise the denominatorexpress in the form a + b\sqrt{c}without using a calculator
Assumptions
  • The standard laws of indices and surds apply.
  • Answers must be given in their simplest, exact form unless otherwise stated.
Approaches
A: Applying Index Laws
When: For expressions involving integer, fractional, or negative powers.
  1. Convert any surds to fractional powers (e.g., $\sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}$).
  2. Apply the power to any numerical coefficients within brackets.
  3. Use the laws of indices to combine terms with the same base (add powers for multiplication, subtract for division).
  4. Simplify any numerical coefficients.
  5. Express the final answer with positive indices if required.
+ Pros: A systematic application of rules.
– Cons: Easy to make mistakes with signs or when adding/subtracting fractions in the powers.
B: Rationalising the Denominator
When: When a fraction has a surd in the denominator, especially of the form $a \pm \sqrt{b}$ or $\sqrt{a} \pm \sqrt{b}$.
  1. Identify the conjugate of the denominator. The conjugate of $a+\sqrt{b}$ is $a-\sqrt{b}$.
  2. Multiply both the numerator and the denominator by this conjugate.
  3. Expand the numerator by multiplying out the brackets.
  4. Expand the denominator using the difference of two squares: $(x+y)(x-y) = x^2 - y^2$. This will eliminate the surd.
  5. Simplify the resulting fraction by collecting like terms and cancelling common factors.
+ Pros: A guaranteed method to remove surds from the denominator.
– Cons: Prone to arithmetic errors during bracket expansion, especially with negative signs.
Formulas
$a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n}$ (MEMORIZE)
$(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$ (MEMORIZE)
$a^{m/n} = \sqrt[n]{a^m}$ (MEMORIZE)
$(a+b)(a-b) = a^2-b^2$ (Difference of Squares) (MEMORIZE)
$\sqrt{ab} = \sqrt{a}\sqrt{b}$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Incorrectly applying index laws, e.g., $(x^2)^3 = x^5$ instead of $x^6$.
  • Forgetting to apply a power to the numerical coefficient, e.g., $(2x^3)^2 = 2x^6$ instead of $4x^6$.
  • Errors in expanding brackets when rationalising, especially with signs, e.g., $(3-\sqrt{2})^2 = 9-2$ instead of $9 - 6\sqrt{2} + 2$.
  • Not fully simplifying surds, e.g., leaving $\sqrt{12}$ instead of writing $2\sqrt{3}$.

Triggerstransformationtranslationstretchreflectionsketchthe curve C has equation y=f(x)...
Assumptions
  • Key features like vertices, intercepts, and asymptotes transform predictably.
  • The order of transformations matters (stretches/reflections before translations is a safe convention).
Approaches
A: Transform Key Points/Features
When: Always a reliable method, especially when the original graph has clear turning points, intercepts, or asymptotes.
  1. Identify key features on the original graph $y=f(x)$: coordinates of turning points, x-intercepts, y-intercepts, and equations of any asymptotes.
  2. Deconstruct the transformation. For $y=af(k(x+b))+c$, identify the shifts, stretches, and reflections.
  3. Apply the transformations to the x and y components of the key features in the correct order (horizontal transformations to x, vertical to y).
  4. - For $x$: apply stretch/reflection first ($x o x/k$), then translation ($x o x-b$).
  5. - For $y$: apply stretch/reflection first ($y o ay$), then translation ($y o y+c$).
  6. Plot the new transformed points and asymptotes, and sketch the new curve, ensuring it has the correct shape.
+ Pros: Systematic and reduces the chance of error by focusing on specific points.
– Cons: Can be slow if there are many points to transform.
Formulas
$f(x)+a$: Vertical translation by vector $\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ a \end{pmatrix}$ (MEMORIZE)
$f(x+a)$: Horizontal translation by vector $\begin{pmatrix} -a \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$ (MEMORIZE)
$af(x)$: Vertical stretch, scale factor $a$, parallel to the y-axis (MEMORIZE)
$f(ax)$: Horizontal stretch, scale factor $1/a$, parallel to the x-axis (MEMORIZE)
$-f(x)$: Reflection in the x-axis (MEMORIZE)
$f(-x)$: Reflection in the y-axis (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Confusing $f(x+a)$ as a shift to the right. It is a shift to the LEFT by 'a'.
  • Confusing $f(ax)$ as a stretch by factor 'a'. It is a stretch by factor $1/a$.
  • Incorrectly transforming asymptotes. Vertical asymptotes $x=k$ are affected by horizontal transformations. Horizontal asymptotes $y=c$ are affected by vertical transformations.
  • Applying transformations in the wrong order.

Triggersregion Rinequalitiesshaded regionsatisfy the inequalities
Assumptions
  • A solid line implies an inclusive inequality ($\le$ or $\ge$).
  • A dashed or dotted line implies a strict inequality ($<$ or $>$).
  • The region is defined by being 'above' ($y > ...$) or 'below' ($y < ...$) a boundary.
Approaches
A: Boundary and Test Point Method
When: This is the standard and most reliable method.
  1. For each boundary, determine its equation. Use $y=mx+c$, $y-y_1 = m(x-x_1)$, or quadratic properties (vertex, roots) as appropriate.
  2. Once you have the equation (e.g., $y = 2x+1$), determine if the line is solid or dashed to choose between $(\le, \ge)$ and $(<, >)$.
  3. Choose a test point that is clearly inside the shaded region (e.g., (0,0) if it is in the region and not on a boundary).
  4. Substitute the coordinates of the test point into your equation. For example, if (1,5) is in the region and the line is $y=2x+1$, we test: Is $5$ related to $2(1)+1=3$? Since $5 > 3$, the inequality is $y > 2x+1$ (or $y \ge 2x+1$ if solid).
  5. Repeat for all boundaries and list the final set of inequalities.
+ Pros: A logical, step-by-step process that minimizes guesswork.
– Cons: Requires care in finding the correct boundary equations and can be tedious if the boundaries are complex.
Formulas
$m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$ (Gradient formula) (formula booklet)
$y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ (Point-gradient form) (formula booklet)
$y = a(x-p)(x-q)$ (Quadratic from roots) (MEMORIZE)
$y = a(x-h)^2+k$ (Quadratic from vertex) (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Choosing the wrong inequality sign ($<$ vs $>$), especially for vertical lines ($x>k$ vs $x
  • Using $\le$ when the line is dashed or $<$ when the line is solid.
  • Making algebraic errors when deriving the equations of the boundary lines or curves.
  • For a quadratic boundary $y=ax^2+...$, if the region is 'inside' a U-shaped parabola, the inequality is $y < ax^2+...$. If it is 'outside', the inequality is $y > ax^2+...$ (and vice versa for n-shaped parabolas).

A: Substitute and solve simultaneously
When: Always — this is the standard fit-from-data method.

  • Not interpreting the constants in context (e.g. a is the limiting value as t→infinity).

Must memorize

  • The Discriminant Conditions: $b^2-4ac > 0$ (2 distinct real roots), $b^2-4ac = 0$ (1 repeated real root), $b^2-4ac < 0$ (no real roots).
  • Completed Square (Vertex) Form: For $y = a(x-h)^2+k$, the vertex is at $(h, k)$. Note the sign change for the x-coordinate.
  • Laws of Indices: $a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n}$, $a^m \div a^n = a^{m-n}$, $(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$, $a^{-m} = 1/a^m$, $a^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a}$.
  • Graph Transformation Rules: Especially the counter-intuitive horizontal transformations $f(x+a)$ (shift LEFT) and $f(ax)$ (stretch by $1/a$).
  • Difference of Two Squares: $(A-B)(A+B) = A^2 - B^2$. Essential for rationalising surds.

Global strategy

  • Master the Discriminant: The conditions $b^2-4ac > 0, = 0, < 0$ are tested constantly. Know what they mean graphically (intersections, tangent, no intersection) and be able to apply them flawlessly, especially when coefficients involve 'k'.
  • Completing the Square is a Swiss Army Knife: It's not just for finding the vertex. It can be used to solve quadratic equations, sketch graphs, and prove expressions are always positive/negative.
  • Always Rearrange to Standard Form: For almost any problem involving quadratics (solving, discriminant, intersection), your first step should be to manipulate the equation into the form $ax^2+bx+c=0$.
  • Visualize with a Sketch: Even if not explicitly asked, a quick sketch of the functions can help you understand the problem, check if your answer is reasonable (e.g., intersection points are in the correct quadrant), and solve inequality problems.
  • Be Fluent with Index Laws: Questions involving indices and substitutions to solve exponential equations are common. You must be able to manipulate terms like $a^{2x+1}$ into $a \times (a^x)^2$ quickly and accurately.
  • Don't Forget the 'Back-Substitution': In substitution questions (e.g. let $u=x^2$), solving for 'u' is only half the job. Always remember to substitute back to find the values of the original variable 'x', and check for invalid solutions.
  • Check for Both Coordinates: When finding points of intersection, a common mistake is to only find the x-values. Read the question carefully; if it asks for coordinates, you must substitute back to find the corresponding y-values.

Exam tips

  1. Read the Keywords: Pay close attention to words like 'tangent', 'distinct', 'exact value', 'region R'. They are triggers for specific techniques (discriminant, surds, inequalities).
  2. Show Your Working for Quadratics: Even if your calculator can solve quadratic equations, you must show a valid method (factorisation, formula, or completing the square) to get the marks.
  3. Bracket Negative Numbers: When substituting negative values or expanding expressions involving unknowns (like $(2k-1)^2$), always use brackets to avoid sign errors.
  4. Check the Plausibility of Graphical Answers: After sketching a transformed graph or finding an intersection point, look at it. Does it make sense? Is the vertex in the right quadrant? Does the y-intercept look correct?
  5. Use the Linear Equation to find 'y': When finding intersection coordinates, after solving the quadratic for 'x', substitute back into the linear equation, not the quadratic. It's much less work and less prone to error.
