Worked solution

Let's break this question down into its parts. We're given a base curve `C1` as $y = 3\sin x$ and asked to identify key points on it, then we deal with a transformed curve `C2`.

Part (a)(i): Coordinates of P

Thought Process: We recognize $y = 3\sin x$ as a sine wave with amplitude 3. The point P is clearly the first maximum point shown on the graph. We know that the standard $\sin x$ graph has its first maximum at $x = 90^\circ$ where $\sin x = 1$. Since the amplitude is 3, the maximum y-value will be $3 imes 1 = 3$.

Working:
For the curve $C_1$ with equation $y = 3\sin x$, the maximum value of $\sin x$ is 1. This occurs when $x = 90^\circ$.
Substituting $\sin x = 1$ into the equation:
$y = 3(1)$
$y = 3$
So, the coordinates of P are $(90^\circ, 3)$.

Part (a)(ii): Coordinates of Q

Thought Process: The point Q is an x-intercept, meaning $y=0$. We know that $\sin x = 0$ at multiples of $180^\circ$ ($0^\circ, 180^\circ, 360^\circ, 540^\circ, \dots$). Looking at Figure 3, Q is the third x-intercept after the origin, so it's not $180^\circ$ or $360^\circ$. It must be the next one in the sequence.

Working:
For $y = 3\sin x$ to be zero, we must have $\sin x = 0$.
The general solutions for $\sin x = 0$ are $x = n \cdot 180^\circ$ for integer values of $n$.
The x-intercepts, in increasing order, are $0^\circ, 180^\circ, 360^\circ, 540^\circ, \dots$.
From Figure 3, Q is the third positive x-intercept.
Thus, $x = 540^\circ$.
So, the coordinates of Q are $(540^\circ, 0)$.

Part (b): Coordinates of R

Thought Process: Curve $C_2$ has equation $y = 3\sin x + k$. This is a vertical translation of $C_1$ by $k$ units. We are given that its maximum y-value is 10. The maximum y-value of $3\sin x$ is 3. So, for $C_2$, the maximum y-value is $3 + k$. We can use this to find $k$. Once $k$ is found, we know the full equation for $C_2$.

Then, we need to find the minimum point R on $C_2$ with the smallest positive x-coordinate. For $y = 3\sin x$, the minimum value of $\sin x$ is -1, which occurs at $x = 270^\circ$ (first positive minimum). The minimum y-value for $C_2$ will be $3(-1) + k$.

Working:
For the curve $C_2$ with equation $y = 3\sin x + k$, the maximum value of $\sin x$ is 1.
So, the maximum y-value of $C_2$ is $3(1) + k = 3 + k$.
We are given that the maximum y-value of $C_2$ is 10.
Therefore, $3 + k = 10 \implies k = 7$.

The equation for $C_2$ is $y = 3\sin x + 7$.

Now we need to find the minimum point R. The minimum value of $\sin x$ is -1.
This occurs at $x = 270^\circ$ for the smallest positive x-coordinate.
Substituting $\sin x = -1$ into the equation for $C_2$:
$y = 3(-1) + 7$
$y = -3 + 7$
$y = 4$
So, the coordinates of R are $(270^\circ, 4)$.

Answer:
(a)(i) $\mathbf{P = (90^\circ, 3)}$
(a)(ii) $\mathbf{Q = (540^\circ, 0)}$
(b) $\mathbf{R = (270^\circ, 4)}$

Key techniques

  • recall properties of y=sin x graph
  • identify maximum/minimum points of y=A sin x
  • understand vertical translation y=f(x)+k
  • find specific points (roots, turning points) on trig graphs

Common pitfalls

  • Mixing up degrees and radians: The question specifies degrees, so ensure answers are in degrees. While the mark scheme condones radians, it's best practice to stick to the given units.
  • Incorrectly identifying the maximum/minimum x-values for sin x (e.g., thinking a minimum occurs at $90^\circ$).
  • Calculation errors when finding the constant k or the new minimum y-value after the vertical translation.
