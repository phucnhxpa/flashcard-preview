Area & Perimeter of Composite Shapes involving Sectors
15×
8-14m

A diagram showing a shape made from sectors, triangles, and straight lines, often presented as a 'logo' or 'pendant' design. The question asks for perimeter, area, or a specific length/angle, with angles almost always in radians.

Triggerssectorarc lengthperimeterareasegmentradiansshow that
Assumptions
  • Angles are given in radians unless specified otherwise.
  • Diagrams are not drawn to scale, but geometric properties (e.g., right angles, parallel lines) can be assumed if stated or marked.
  • Use the calculator's value for pi unless an exact answer in terms of pi is requested.
Approaches
Decomposition Method
When: This is the standard method for all composite shape problems.
  1. Identify all the basic shapes (sectors, triangles, segments) that make up the composite figure.
  2. List the required formulas: Arc Length, Sector Area, Triangle Area (1/2 ab sinC), Cosine Rule, Sine Rule.
  3. Extract all given information (radii, side lengths, angles) and label them clearly on the diagram.
  4. Calculate any missing angles or lengths needed for the formulas. This may involve using SOH CAH TOA, Sine/Cosine Rule, or angle properties (e.g., angles on a straight line = $\pi$).
  5. Calculate the area or perimeter of each individual component shape. Ensure your calculator is in RADIAN mode.
  6. Combine the components: Add areas/lengths for the total, or subtract to find a shaded region.
  7. Check the final answer for rounding instructions (e.g., '3 significant figures'). Do not round intermediate steps.
+ Pros: Systematic and breaks a complex problem into manageable parts.
– Cons: Requires remembering multiple formulas and can involve many intermediate calculations, increasing the chance of error.
Formulas
$s = r\theta$ (Arc Length, $\theta$ in radians) (formula booklet)
$A = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta$ (Area of Sector, $\theta$ in radians) (formula booklet)
$A = \frac{1}{2}ab\sin C$ (Area of Triangle) (formula booklet)
$a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc\cos A$ (Cosine Rule) (formula booklet)
$\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C}$ (Sine Rule) (formula booklet)
Area of Segment = Area of Sector - Area of Triangle = $\frac{1}{2}r^2(\theta - \sin \theta)$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Using degrees instead of radians for sector/arc formulas (CHECK CALCULATOR MODE!).
  • Prematurely rounding intermediate values, which leads to inaccuracies in the final answer.
  • Forgetting to add straight-line lengths when calculating the perimeter of a shape that includes arcs.
  • Using the minor sector angle when the major sector is required (e.g. using $\theta$ instead of $2\pi - \theta$).

Trigonometric Graphs: Transformations and Solutions
12×
4-8m

An equation like $y = A \sin(Bx + C) + D$ is given, or a sketch of such a graph. Questions ask for coordinates of max/min points, intercepts, period, or values of parameters A, B, C, D. It may also ask for the number of solutions to an equation by considering intersections.

Triggerssketch the graphstate the coordinatesmaximum valueminimum pointperiodtransformationnumber of solutions
Assumptions
  • The domain is given in degrees or radians – pay close attention to the units specified.
  • Standard sine/cosine graphs start at (0,0) and (0,1) respectively before transformations.
  • An equation of the form $y=f(x-a)$ represents a horizontal translation by vector $\begin{pmatrix} a \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$ (a shift to the right for $a>0$).
Approaches
A: Parameter Analysis
When: When given an equation $y = A f(Bx+C) + D$ and asked to find features or sketch the graph.
  1. Identify the parent function (sin, cos, or tan) and the parameters: A (Amplitude/Vertical Stretch), B (Period), C (Phase Shift), D (Vertical Shift).
  2. Determine the key features: Amplitude = $|A|$. Period = $360^\circ/B$ or $2\pi/B$. Principal Axis is the line $y=D$.
  3. Calculate the range of the function: $[D - |A|, D + |A|]$.
  4. To sketch: Draw the principal axis $y=D$. Mark the max ($D+|A|$) and min ($D-|A|$) lines.
  5. Determine the new starting point and key points (maxima, minima, intercepts with principal axis) over one full period, then extend over the required domain.
+ Pros: Methodical and directly links equation parameters to graph features.
– Cons: Can be confusing with multiple transformations, especially the horizontal shift.
B: Graphical Intersection Method
When: When asked for the 'number of solutions' to an equation like `trig(x) = linear(x)`.
  1. Separate the equation into two functions, e.g., $y_1 = A \sin(Bx) + D$ and $y_2 = kx + c$.
  2. Carefully sketch the graph of the trigonometric function $y_1$ over the given domain, labelling key points.
  3. On the same axes, sketch the graph of the second function $y_2$ (often a straight line).
  4. Count the number of points where the two graphs intersect. This is the number of solutions.
+ Pros: Visual and intuitive way to find the number of roots without solving algebraically.
– Cons: Requires an accurate sketch. An inaccurate sketch can lead to the wrong number of solutions.
Formulas
For $y = A \sin(B\theta) + D$ or $y = A \cos(B\theta) + D$:
Amplitude = $|A|$ (MEMORIZE)
Period = $\frac{2\pi}{B}$ (radians) or $\frac{360^\circ}{B}$ (degrees) (MEMORIZE)
Principal Axis (Vertical Shift) is the line $y = D$ (MEMORIZE)
Range = $[D - |A|, D + |A|]$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Incorrectly calculating the period (e.g., using $2\pi \times B$ instead of $2\pi / B$).
  • Confusing amplitude (A) with the maximum value (D+A).
  • For $A < 0$, forgetting the graph is reflected in the principal axis (e.g., a negative cosine graph starts at a minimum).
  • Inaccurately sketching the tangent graph, especially missing or misplacing asymptotes.

Solving Triangles with Sine & Cosine Rules
3×
5-7m

A question about a single triangle (or a parallelogram). You are given some side lengths and/or angles and asked to find a missing one. Often involves the 'ambiguous case' of the sine rule.

Triggerstrianglefind the lengthcalculate the angleobtuseacutelongest sidearea of the triangle
Assumptions
  • Angles in a triangle sum to $180^\circ$ (or $\pi$ radians).
  • The side opposite an angle is denoted by the corresponding lowercase letter.
  • An obtuse angle is between $90^\circ$ and $180^\circ$.
Approaches
Rule Selection Method
When: Standard approach for all non-right-angled triangle problems.
  1. Draw the triangle and label all known sides and angles.
  2. Choose the correct rule: Use Cosine Rule for 'Side-Angle-Side' (SAS) or 'Side-Side-Side' (SSS). Use Sine Rule for 'Angle-Side-Angle' (ASA) or 'Side-Side-Angle' (SSA).
  3. Apply the chosen formula carefully, substituting the correct values.
  4. **Beware the Ambiguous Case**: When using Sine Rule to find an angle (SSA case), your calculator gives an acute angle $\theta$. The obtuse solution is $180^\circ - \theta$. Use information in the question (e.g., 'angle is obtuse', 'longest side') to decide which angle is correct.
+ Pros: A clear flowchart for which formula to apply based on the given information.
– Cons: The ambiguous case of the sine rule is a very common source of error if not considered.
Formulas
$\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C}$ (Sine Rule) (formula booklet)
$a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc\cos A$ (Cosine Rule) (formula booklet)
$A = \frac{1}{2}ab\sin C$ (Area of Triangle) (formula booklet)
Ambiguous case: If $\sin \theta = k$, then potential solutions are $\theta_1 = \arcsin(k)$ and $\theta_2 = 180^\circ - \theta_1$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to check for the ambiguous case (obtuse angle) when using the Sine Rule to find an angle.
  • Applying the Cosine Rule with the wrong angle (it must be the angle *between* the two known sides).
  • Mixing up side-angle pairings in the Sine Rule (e.g., `a` with `sin B`).
  • Calculator in the wrong mode (degrees vs. radians).

Solving Equations using Trigonometric Identities
1×
5-7m

An equation involving different trig functions (e.g., `sin x` and `cos^2 x`) or powers of trig functions, which must be solved for `x` in a given interval.

Triggerssolvefor $0 \le x < 360^\circ$give your answers to 1 decimal place
Assumptions
  • The solution interval is crucial; answers outside are incorrect.
  • The identity $\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x \equiv 1$ is usually the key to simplifying the equation.
  • If a quadratic doesn't factorize, the quadratic formula is needed.
Approaches
Identity, Substitute, Solve, CAST
When: For any equation with a mix of sin and cos, or with a squared trig term.
  1. **Identity**: Use $\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x \equiv 1$ to convert the equation into one involving only ONE trigonometric function (e.g., all `cos x`).
  2. **Substitute**: Rearrange the equation into a standard quadratic form $ay^2 + by + c = 0$, where `y` is your chosen trig function (e.g., $y = \cos x$).
  3. **Solve**: Solve the quadratic for `y` by factorizing or using the quadratic formula. This gives up to two numerical values.
  4. **Reject Invalid Solutions**: Discard any solutions where $|y| > 1$ for $\sin x$ or $\cos x$.
  5. **CAST/Graph**: For each valid solution, find all angles `x` within the given interval. First, find the principal value (PV) from your calculator. Then, use a CAST diagram or a graph sketch to find all other related angles in the correct quadrants.
  6. List all solutions in order, to the required accuracy.
+ Pros: A robust, repeatable algorithm for a common exam question type.
– Cons: Multiple steps mean multiple places for algebraic errors, especially with negative signs.
Formulas
$\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x \equiv 1$ (MEMORIZE)
$\tan x \equiv \frac{\sin x}{\cos x}$ (MEMORIZE)
Quadratic Formula: $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to find all solutions in the given range (e.g., only giving the principal value).
  • Making sign errors when rearranging the identity, e.g., writing $\cos^2 x = 1 + \sin^2 x$.
  • Incorrectly factorizing the quadratic or errors in the quadratic formula.
  • Dividing by a trig function (e.g., `sin x`) which could be zero, thus losing potential solutions.

Must memorize

  • $\sin^2 \theta + \cos^2 \theta \equiv 1$ (The Pythagorean Identity)
  • $\tan \theta \equiv \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta}$
  • Area of Segment = $\frac{1}{2}r^2(\theta - \sin \theta)$ (for $\theta$ in radians)
  • Period of $\sin(B\theta)$ or $\cos(B\theta)$ is $\frac{2\pi}{B}$ (rad) or $\frac{360^\circ}{B}$ (deg)
  • Period of $\tan(B\theta)$ is $\frac{\pi}{B}$ (rad) or $\frac{180^\circ}{B}$ (deg)
  • Exact values for 30°, 45°, 60° (and their radian equivalents $\frac{\pi}{6}, \frac{\pi}{4}, \frac{\pi}{3}$) for sin, cos, and tan.

Global strategy

  • **Check Calculator Mode First!** Before reading, check if you need Degrees or Radians. Radians are the default for any question with arc length or sector area. Degrees are common for graphs and equations unless $\pi$ is in the range.
  • **Master the Formulas.** The sector/arc/triangle formulas are your bread and butter. Know them perfectly, especially the ones you must memorize like the area of a segment.
  • **Draw and Annotate.** For any geometry problem, redraw the diagram. Label all given lengths and angles. This makes relationships obvious.
  • **Don't Round Prematurely.** Keep intermediate answers stored in your calculator's memory or write them down to 4-5 significant figures. Only round your final answer.
  • **Always Check the Range/Domain.** For solving equations or graph questions, the specified interval (e.g., $0 \le x < 360^\circ$) is critical. Don't lose marks by giving answers outside the range or missing solutions within it.
  • **Remember the Ambiguous Case.** If you use the Sine Rule to find an angle, always ask: 'Could it be the obtuse angle $180^\circ - \theta$?' The question will contain a clue.
  • **Understand Graph Transformations.** Don't just memorize A, B, D. Understand what each parameter *does*: A stretches vertically, B squashes horizontally, D shifts vertically.

Exam tips

  1. For 'Show that...' questions in geometry, you MUST show every step of your calculation clearly. Your final answer should match the target exactly or be rounded to it as the very last step.
  2. When sketching a trig graph, label the coordinates of all key points: intercepts with the axes, and any maximum or minimum points within the domain.
  3. If you solve a quadratic trig equation and get, for example, $\sin x = 1.5$, write it down and explicitly state 'no solutions' since $-1 \le \sin x \le 1$. This shows understanding.
  4. Use a CAST diagram or a quick sketch of the graph to find all solutions to a trig equation. This is more reliable than trying to memorize formulas like $180-x$, $180+x$, $360-x$.
  5. On composite area questions, break your working down with clear subheadings (e.g., 'Area of Sector AOB', 'Area of Triangle BOC') to keep your logic clear.
