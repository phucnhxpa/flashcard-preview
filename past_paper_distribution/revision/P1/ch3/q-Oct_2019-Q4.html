Calculate obtuse angle and diagonal length of a parallelogram using area formula and cosine rule.

Question
PMT

10

 
 
 

4. A parallelogram ABCD has area 40 cm2
 
Given that AB has length 10 cm, BC has length 6 cm and angle DAB is obtuse, find
 
(a) the size of angle DAB, in degrees, to 2 decimal places,
(3)
 
(b) the length of diagonal BD, in cm, to one decimal place.
(2)

PMT

11

 
 
 
 

Question 4 continued
Worked solution

Part (a)

Thought Process:
We are given the area of a parallelogram (40 cm²), the lengths of two adjacent sides (AB = 10 cm, BC = 6 cm), and that angle DAB is obtuse. We need to find the size of angle DAB.

We recognize that the area of a parallelogram can be calculated using the formula $Area = ab \sin \theta$, where $a$ and $b$ are the lengths of two adjacent sides and $\theta$ is the included angle between them. In this case, the sides are AB and AD. Since it's a parallelogram, AD = BC = 6 cm.

So, we'll set up the equation using the given area and side lengths, then solve for $\sin(\angle DAB)$. When finding an angle from its sine, there are usually two possible values within $0^{\circ}$ to $180^{\circ}$ (i.e., $\theta$ and $180^{\circ} - \theta$). The question explicitly states that angle DAB is *obtuse*, which means it must be greater than $90^{\circ}$ and less than $180^{\circ}$. This guides our choice of the angle.

Working:
Let $\theta = \angle DAB$.
The area of parallelogram ABCD is given by $AB \times AD \times \sin(\angle DAB)$.
Since AD = BC = 6 cm (opposite sides of a parallelogram are equal):
$$40 = 10 \times 6 \times \sin(\theta)$$
$$40 = 60 \sin(\theta)$$
Divide both sides by 60:
$$\sin(\theta) = \frac{40}{60}$$
$$\sin(\theta) = \frac{2}{3}$$

Now, we find the principal value for $\theta$:
$$\theta_1 = \arcsin\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)$$
$$\theta_1 \approx 41.8103^{\circ}$$

Since angle DAB is obtuse, we must use the alternative solution for $\sin(\theta) = \frac{2}{3}$ in the range $0^{\circ} < \theta < 180^{\circ}$:
$$\theta = 180^{\circ} - \theta_1$$
$$\theta = 180^{\circ} - 41.8103^{\circ}$$
$$\theta = 138.1897^{\circ}$$
Rounding to 2 decimal places:
$$\angle DAB = 138.19^{\circ}$$

Part (b)

Thought Process:
We need to find the length of the diagonal BD. We can consider triangle ABD. We know the lengths of sides AB (10 cm) and AD (6 cm), and we just found the included angle DAB ($138.19^{\circ}$). This is a perfect scenario for applying the Cosine Rule, which states $c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab \cos C$.

In triangle ABD, we are looking for BD. So, $BD^2 = AB^2 + AD^2 - 2(AB)(AD) \cos(\angle DAB)$.

Working:
Applying the Cosine Rule to triangle ABD:
$$BD^2 = AB^2 + AD^2 - 2(AB)(AD) \cos(\angle DAB)$$
Substitute the known values (using the unrounded value for DAB for better accuracy, or the value to 2dp as per the mark scheme):
$$BD^2 = 10^2 + 6^2 - 2(10)(6) \cos(138.19^{\circ})$$
$$BD^2 = 100 + 36 - 120 \cos(138.19^{\circ})$$
$$BD^2 = 136 - 120(-0.7455...)$$
$$BD^2 = 136 + 89.46...$$
$$BD^2 = 225.46...$$
Take the square root to find BD:
$$BD = \sqrt{225.46...}$$
$$BD = 15.015...$$
Rounding to one decimal place:
$$BD = 15.0 \text{ cm}$$

Answer:
(a) $\angle DAB = 138.19^{\circ}$
(b) $BD = 15.0 \text{ cm}$

Key techniques

  • using area formula $Area = ab \sin C$
  • identifying obtuse angles for sine inverse
  • applying the cosine rule $c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab \cos C$

Common pitfalls

  • Forgetting to choose the obtuse angle in part (a) (e.g., giving $41.81^{\circ}$ instead of $138.19^{\circ}$).
  • Using the wrong angle in the cosine rule in part (b). For instance, using the acute angle if part (a) was incorrect, or misidentifying the included angle.
  • Calculation errors, especially with signs when using cosine of an obtuse angle (e.g. $\cos(138.19^{\circ})$ is negative, leading to addition in the cosine rule).
