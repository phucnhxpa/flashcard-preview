Worked solution

Part (a): Express $2x^2 - 12x + 14$ in the form $a(x-b)^2 + c$.

Thought Process:
We recognise this as a "completing the square" problem. The general form required is $a(x-b)^2 + c$. The first step is to factor out the coefficient of $x^2$ (which is $2$) from the $x^2$ and $x$ terms. Then, we complete the square for the quadratic expression inside the bracket. Finally, we expand and simplify to get the desired form.

Working:
Given the expression $2x^2 - 12x + 14$.
First, factor out the coefficient of $x^2$ from the terms involving $x$:
$$2(x^2 - 6x) + 14$$
Next, complete the square for the expression inside the bracket, $x^2 - 6x$. We know that $(x-k)^2 = x^2 - 2kx + k^2$. Comparing this to $x^2 - 6x$, we have $-2k = -6$, so $k=3$.
Therefore, $x^2 - 6x = (x-3)^2 - 3^2 = (x-3)^2 - 9$.
Substitute this back into the expression:
$$2((x-3)^2 - 9) + 14$$
Now, expand the bracket and simplify:
$$2(x-3)^2 - 18 + 14$$
$$2(x-3)^2 - 4$$
This is in the form $a(x-b)^2 + c$, with $a=2$, $b=3$, and $c=-4$.

Part (b): Write down the coordinates of the minimum point of $C$.

Thought Process:
For a quadratic function in the form $y = a(x-b)^2 + c$, if $a > 0$, the parabola opens upwards, and the minimum value occurs when $(x-b)^2 = 0$, which means $x=b$. The minimum $y$-value is then $c$. So the minimum point is $(b, c)$. Since $a=2$ (which is positive), this curve has a minimum point.

Working:
From part (a), the equation of the curve $C$ is $y = 2(x-3)^2 - 4$.
The minimum value of $y$ occurs when the squared term $(x-3)^2$ is at its minimum, which is $0$. This happens when $x-3=0$, so $x=3$.
Substitute $x=3$ into the equation:
$$y = 2(3-3)^2 - 4$$
$$y = 2(0)^2 - 4$$
$$y = -4$$
Therefore, the coordinates of the minimum point of $C$ are $(3, -4)$.

Part (c): Find an equation for the line $l$, giving your answer in the form $y = mx + c$.

Thought Process:
We need to find the equation of a straight line, $l$, that passes through two given points: the minimum point of $C$ (found in part b) and the point $(-1, 28)$.
The first step is to calculate the gradient ($m$) of the line using the formula $m = rac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$.
Once the gradient is found, we can use the point-gradient form of a straight line equation, $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$, with either of the two points.
Finally, rearrange the equation into the desired $y = mx + c$ form.

Working:
The line $l$ passes through the minimum point $(3, -4)$ and the point $(-1, 28)$.
Let $(x_1, y_1) = (3, -4)$ and $(x_2, y_2) = (-1, 28)$.
Calculate the gradient $m$:
$$m = rac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} = rac{28 - (-4)}{-1 - 3}$$
$$m = rac{28 + 4}{-4} = rac{32}{-4}$$
$$m = -8$$
Now, use the point-gradient form $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ with $m=-8$ and the point $(3, -4)$:
$$y - (-4) = -8(x - 3)$$
$$y + 4 = -8x + 24$$
Rearrange to the form $y = mx + c$:
$$y = -8x + 24 - 4$$
$$y = -8x + 20$$

Part (d): Define the region $R$ by writing down all necessary inequalities.

Thought Process:
We need to define the finite region $R$ bounded by the curve $C$, the line $l$, and the positive $x$ and $y$ axes. This means we need to set up a system of inequalities.


  1. The region is "above" the curve $C$: $y \ge 2x^2 - 12x + 14$.

  2. The region is "below" the line $l$: $y \le -8x + 20$.

  3. The region is to the "right" of the positive $y$-axis: $x \ge 0$.

  4. The region is "above" the positive $x$-axis: $y \ge 0$.


We use "$\ge$" or "$\le$" because the boundaries themselves are part of the region.

Working:
The region $R$ is bounded by:


  • The curve $C$: $y = 2x^2 - 12x + 14$. The region lies on or above the curve, so $y \ge 2x^2 - 12x + 14$.

  • The line $l$: $y = -8x + 20$. The region lies on or below the line, so $y \le -8x + 20$.

  • The positive $x$-axis: This implies that the $y$-coordinates in the region must be non-negative, so $y \ge 0$.

  • The positive $y$-axis: This implies that the $x$-coordinates in the region must be non-negative, so $x \ge 0$.

Combining these, the inequalities that define the region $R$ are:
$$y \ge 2x^2 - 12x + 14$$
$$y \le -8x + 20$$
$$x \ge 0$$
$$y \ge 0$$

Answer:
(a) $2(x-3)^2 - 4$
(b) $(3, -4)$
(c) $y = -8x + 20$
(d) $y \ge 2x^2 - 12x + 14$, $y \le -8x + 20$, $x \ge 0$, $y \ge 0$

Key techniques

  • Completing the square
  • Finding minimum point of a quadratic
  • Calculating gradient from two points
  • Equation of a line (point-gradient form)
  • Formulating inequalities for bounded regions

Common pitfalls

  • Sign errors when completing the square, especially when expanding $a(x-b)^2 - k^2$ or calculating gradients.
  • Incorrectly identifying the coordinates of the minimum point from the completed square form, e.g., using $(b, -c)$ or $(-b, c)$.
  • Confusing the direction of inequalities for defining the region, e.g., using $y < ext{function}$ instead of $y \le ext{function}$ or getting 'above'/'below' incorrect.
  • Forgetting to include the $x \ge 0$ and $y \ge 0$ conditions when the region is bounded by the 'positive x and y axes'.
