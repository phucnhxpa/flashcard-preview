Worked solution

Part (a): Find $\frac{dy}{dx}$

Thought Process:
We are asked to differentiate $y = x^3 + 6x - \frac{4}{\sqrt{x}} - 15$. This is a standard differentiation problem involving terms with powers of $x$. To make differentiation easier, we should first rewrite the term $\frac{4}{\sqrt{x}}$ using negative and fractional exponents, and remember that the derivative of a constant (like $-15$) is zero.

Working:
First, rewrite the equation in a form suitable for differentiation:
$$y = x^3 + 6x - 4x^{-\frac{1}{2}} - 15$$
Now, we differentiate term by term using the power rule $\frac{d}{dx}(ax^n) = anx^{n-1}$:
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{d}{dx}(x^3) + \frac{d}{dx}(6x) - \frac{d}{dx}(4x^{-\frac{1}{2}}) - \frac{d}{dx}(15)$$
Applying the power rule:
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^{3-1} + 6x^{1-1} - 4\left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)x^{-\frac{1}{2}-1} - 0$$
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 + 6x^0 + 2x^{-\frac{3}{2}}$$
Simplify the terms:
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 + 6 + \frac{2}{x^{\frac{3}{2}}}$$
Alternatively, it can be written as:
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 + 6 + \frac{2}{x\sqrt{x}}$$

Answer: $\frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 + 6 + \frac{2}{x^{\frac{3}{2}}}$

Part (b): Find the equation of the normal to the curve at P$(4, \frac{11}{3})$

Thought Process:
To find the equation of the normal, we first need its gradient. The normal is perpendicular to the tangent at point P. So, we'll calculate the gradient of the tangent at P by substituting $x=4$ into our $\frac{dy}{dx}$ from Part (a). Then, we'll use the relationship $m_{\text{normal}} = -\frac{1}{m_{\text{tangent}}}$. Finally, with the gradient of the normal and the point P$(4, \frac{11}{3})$, we can use the point-gradient form of a straight line, $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$, and rearrange it into the required form $ax + by + c = 0$.

Working:


  1. Find the gradient of the tangent at P:


Substitute $x=4$ into $\frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 + 6 + \frac{2}{x^{\frac{3}{2}}}$:
$$m_{\text{tangent}} = 3(4)^2 + 6 + \frac{2}{(4)^{\frac{3}{2}}}$$
$$m_{\text{tangent}} = 3(16) + 6 + \frac{2}{(\sqrt{4})^3}$$
$$m_{\text{tangent}} = 48 + 6 + \frac{2}{(2)^3}$$
$$m_{\text{tangent}} = 54 + \frac{2}{8}$$
$$m_{\text{tangent}} = 54 + \frac{1}{4}$$
$$m_{\text{tangent}} = \frac{216}{4} + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{217}{4}$$

  1. Find the gradient of the normal:
The gradient of the normal is the negative reciprocal of the tangent's gradient: $$m_{\text{normal}} = -\frac{1}{m_{\text{tangent}}} = -\frac{1}{\frac{217}{4}} = -\frac{4}{217}$$
  1. Find the equation of the normal:
Using the point-gradient form $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ with $P(4, \frac{11}{3})$ and $m_{\text{normal}} = -\frac{4}{217}$: $$y - \frac{11}{3} = -\frac{4}{217}(x - 4)$$ To eliminate the fractions, multiply the entire equation by the lowest common multiple of the denominators, which is $3 \times 217 = 651$: $$651\left(y - \frac{11}{3}\right) = 651\left(-\frac{4}{217}(x - 4)\right)$$ $$651y - 217(11) = -3(4)(x - 4)$$ $$651y - 2387 = -12(x - 4)$$ $$651y - 2387 = -12x + 48$$ Rearrange to the form $ax + by + c = 0$ by moving all terms to one side: $$12x + 651y - 2387 - 48 = 0$$ $$12x + 651y - 2435 = 0$$

Key techniques

  • rewrite terms with negative/fractional powers for differentiation
  • differentiate $ax^n$ to $anx^{n-1}$
  • evaluate derivative at a specific point
  • find negative reciprocal for normal's gradient
  • use point-gradient formula for straight line

Common pitfalls

  • Incorrectly rewriting terms like $\frac{1}{\sqrt{x}}$ or $4\sqrt{x}$ using negative or fractional powers before differentiation.
  • Making algebraic errors when substituting the $x$-value into $\frac{dy}{dx}$ or when calculating the numerical value of the gradient.
  • Forgetting to use the negative reciprocal to find the gradient of the normal, or making sign errors when doing so.
  • Algebraic mistakes when rearranging the equation of the line into the form $ax + by + c = 0$, especially with fractions or signs.
