Worked solution

(a) Find $\frac{dy}{dx}$


  • Thought Process: We need to differentiate the given polynomial function $y$ with respect to $x$. This involves applying the power rule for differentiation ($ax^n \rightarrow anx^{n-1}$) to each term. The derivative of a constant term, $k$, is $0$.

  • Working:


Given $y = \frac{2}{7}x^3 + \frac{1}{7}x^2 - \frac{5}{2}x + k$
Differentiating term by term:
$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{2}{7}x^3\right) + \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{1}{7}x^2\right) - \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{5}{2}x\right) + \frac{d}{dx}(k)$
$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2}{7}(3x^{3-1}) + \frac{1}{7}(2x^{2-1}) - \frac{5}{2}(1x^{1-1}) + 0$
$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{6}{7}x^2 + \frac{2}{7}x - \frac{5}{2}$
Answer: $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{6}{7}x^2 + \frac{2}{7}x - \frac{5}{2}$

(b) Show that the x coordinate of the point B is a solution of the equation $12x^2 + 4x - 33 = 0$


  • Thought Process: The problem states that line $l$ is the normal to curve C at point A (with $x = -\frac{7}{2}$) and also a tangent to C at point B. This implies two key pieces of information about gradients:



  1. The gradient of line $l$ is the negative reciprocal of the gradient of the curve C at A.

  2. The gradient of curve C at B is equal to the gradient of line $l$.


Our strategy is to first find the gradient of the curve C at A. Then, calculate the gradient of the normal line $l$. Finally, set the general derivative expression $\frac{dy}{dx}$ equal to the gradient of line $l$ to find the x-coordinate(s) of the point(s) where C has this gradient, and rearrange to match the given equation.

  • Working:



  1. Find the gradient of the curve C at point A, where $x = -\frac{7}{2}$.


Substitute $x = -\frac{7}{2}$ into the derivative $\frac{dy}{dx}$ found in part (a):
$\left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_{x = -7/2} = \frac{6}{7}\left(-\frac{7}{2}\right)^2 + \frac{2}{7}\left(-\frac{7}{2}\right) - \frac{5}{2}$
$= \frac{6}{7}\left(\frac{49}{4}\right) + \frac{2}{7}\left(-\frac{7}{2}\right) - \frac{5}{2}$
$= \frac{3 \times 7}{2} - 1 - \frac{5}{2}$
$= \frac{21}{2} - \frac{2}{2} - \frac{5}{2}$
$= \frac{21 - 2 - 5}{2} = \frac{14}{2} = 7$
So, the gradient of the tangent to C at A is $7$.

  1. Find the gradient of the line $l$. Since $l$ is the normal to C at A, its gradient $m_l$ is the negative reciprocal of the tangent gradient at A.
$m_l = -\frac{1}{7}$
  1. The line $l$ is also a tangent to C at point B. This means the gradient of the curve C at point B must be equal to the gradient of line $l$.
So, at point B, $\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{1}{7}$. Set the general derivative from part (a) equal to this value: $\frac{6}{7}x^2 + \frac{2}{7}x - \frac{5}{2} = -\frac{1}{7}$
  1. Rearrange the equation to match the desired quadratic form. To eliminate the fractions, multiply the entire equation by the lowest common multiple (LCM) of the denominators (7, 2), which is 14:
$14 \left( \frac{6}{7}x^2 \right) + 14 \left( \frac{2}{7}x \right) - 14 \left( \frac{5}{2} \right) = 14 \left( -\frac{1}{7} \right)$ $2(6x^2) + 2(2x) - 7(5) = 2(-1)$ $12x^2 + 4x - 35 = -2$ $12x^2 + 4x - 35 + 2 = 0$ $12x^2 + 4x - 33 = 0$ This matches the given equation.

(c) Hence find the x coordinate of B, justifying your answer.


  • Thought Process: We need to solve the quadratic equation $12x^2 + 4x - 33 = 0$ for $x$. The quadratic formula is a general and reliable method for this. Since a sketch (Figure 5) is provided, we should use it to justify which of the two solutions corresponds to point B.

  • Working:


Solve $12x^2 + 4x - 33 = 0$ using the quadratic formula $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$.
Here, $a=12$, $b=4$, $c=-33$.
$x = \frac{-4 \pm \sqrt{(4)^2 - 4(12)(-33)}}{2(12)}$
$x = \frac{-4 \pm \sqrt{16 + 1584}}{24}$
$x = \frac{-4 \pm \sqrt{1600}}{24}$
$x = \frac{-4 \pm 40}{24}$
The two possible solutions for $x$ are:
$x_1 = \frac{-4 + 40}{24} = \frac{36}{24} = \frac{3}{2}$
$x_2 = \frac{-4 - 40}{24} = \frac{-44}{24} = -\frac{11}{6}$
Referring to Figure 5, point B is clearly located in the region where $x > 0$. The value $x = -\frac{11}{6}$ is negative. Therefore, the x coordinate of point B must be the positive solution.
Answer: $x_B = \frac{3}{2}$

(d) Find the value of k.


  • Thought Process: We have the gradient of line $l$ from part (b) ($m_l = -\frac{1}{7}$) and its y-intercept (given as $-1$). This is enough to write the complete equation of line $l$. We know point A (with $x_A = -\frac{7}{2}$) lies on line $l$, so we can use the equation of $l$ to find its corresponding y-coordinate, $y_A$. Since point A also lies on the curve C, its coordinates $(x_A, y_A)$ must satisfy the curve's equation. Substituting these coordinates into the equation of C will allow us to solve for the unknown constant $k$.

  • Working:



  1. Determine the equation of the line $l$.


From part (b), the gradient of line $l$ is $m_l = -\frac{1}{7}$.
We are given that the y-intercept of $l$ is $-1$.
So, the equation of line $l$ is $y = -\frac{1}{7}x - 1$.

  1. Find the y-coordinate of point A. We know point A has $x_A = -\frac{7}{2}$ and lies on line $l$.
Substitute $x_A = -\frac{7}{2}$ into the equation of $l$: $y_A = -\frac{1}{7}\left(-\frac{7}{2}\right) - 1$ $y_A = \frac{1}{2} - 1$ $y_A = -\frac{1}{2}$ So, the coordinates of point A are $\left(-\frac{7}{2}, -\frac{1}{2}\right)$.
  1. Substitute the coordinates of point A into the equation of curve C to find $k$.
The equation of curve C is $y = \frac{2}{7}x^3 + \frac{1}{7}x^2 - \frac{5}{2}x + k$. Substitute $x = -\frac{7}{2}$ and $y = -\frac{1}{2}$ into the curve's equation: $-\frac{1}{2} = \frac{2}{7}\left(-\frac{7}{2}\right)^3 + \frac{1}{7}\left(-\frac{7}{2}\right)^2 - \frac{5}{2}\left(-\frac{7}{2}\right) + k$ $-\frac{1}{2} = \frac{2}{7}\left(-\frac{343}{8} ight) + \frac{1}{7}\left(\frac{49}{4} ight) + \frac{35}{4} + k$ $-\frac{1}{2} = -\frac{49}{4} + \frac{7}{4} + \frac{35}{4} + k$ Combine the fractions on the right-hand side: $-\frac{1}{2} = \frac{-49 + 7 + 35}{4} + k$ $-\frac{1}{2} = \frac{-7}{4} + k$ Isolate $k$: $k = -\frac{1}{2} + \frac{7}{4}$ To add these fractions, find a common denominator (4): $k = -\frac{2}{4} + \frac{7}{4}$ $k = \frac{5}{4}$
Answer: $k = \frac{5}{4}$

Key techniques

  • Differentiate a polynomial function
  • Calculate the gradient of a normal
  • Form the equation of a straight line (y=mx+c)
  • Substitute coordinates into a curve equation
  • Solve quadratic equations

Common pitfalls

  • Forgetting to take the negative reciprocal when calculating the gradient of the normal line.
  • Making algebraic errors when substituting fractions or solving the quadratic equation.
  • Incorrectly selecting the x-coordinate for B from the two solutions of the quadratic equation, by not using the information from the diagram.
  • Confusing the equation of the curve with the equation of the line, or incorrectly applying points to the wrong equation.
