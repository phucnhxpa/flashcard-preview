Worked solution

Part (a): Show that $\frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 - 20x + 32$

Thought Process:
We are given the equation of the curve in a factored form, $y = (x-2)(x-4)^2$. To find $\frac{dy}{dx}$, we need to differentiate this expression. The most straightforward method for a polynomial like this is to first expand the brackets completely to get a polynomial in the form $ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d$, and then differentiate each term using the power rule $(\frac{d}{dx}(x^n) = nx^{n-1})$. Alternatively, one could use the product rule, treating it as $u = (x-2)$ and $v = (x-4)^2$, but expanding first is often less error-prone for 'show that' questions with polynomials.

Working:
First, expand the $(x-4)^2$ term:
$$ (x-4)^2 = (x-4)(x-4) = x^2 - 4x - 4x + 16 = x^2 - 8x + 16 $$
Now, substitute this back into the equation for $y$:
$$ y = (x-2)(x^2 - 8x + 16) $$
Expand these two brackets:
$$ y = x(x^2 - 8x + 16) - 2(x^2 - 8x + 16) $$
$$ y = x^3 - 8x^2 + 16x - 2x^2 + 16x - 32 $$
Collect like terms:
$$ y = x^3 - (8x^2 + 2x^2) + (16x + 16x) - 32 $$
$$ y = x^3 - 10x^2 + 32x - 32 $$
Now, differentiate $y$ with respect to $x$ term by term using the power rule:
$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{d}{dx}(x^3) - \frac{d}{dx}(10x^2) + \frac{d}{dx}(32x) - \frac{d}{dx}(32) $$
$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^{3-1} - 10(2x^{2-1}) + 32(1x^{1-1}) - 0 $$
$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 - 20x + 32 $$
This matches the expression we needed to show.

Part (b): Find the equation of $l_1$, giving your answer in the form $y = mx + c$

Thought Process:
To find the equation of a straight line, we need two pieces of information: a point $(x_1, y_1)$ that the line passes through and its gradient $m$. We are given that $l_1$ is the tangent to $C$ at the point where $x=6$. So, $x_1 = 6$. We can find $y_1$ by substituting $x=6$ into the original equation for $C$. We can find the gradient $m$ by substituting $x=6$ into the $\frac{dy}{dx}$ expression found in part (a). Once we have the point and the gradient, we can use the formula $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$.

Working:
First, find the $y$-coordinate of the point on $C$ where $x=6$. Substitute $x=6$ into the equation $y = (x-2)(x-4)^2$:
$$ y = (6-2)(6-4)^2 $$
$$ y = (4)(2)^2 $$
$$ y = 4 \times 4 $$
$$ y = 16 $$
So, the point is $(6, 16)$.

Next, find the gradient of the tangent at $x=6$. Substitute $x=6$ into the $\frac{dy}{dx}$ expression from part (a):
$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = 3(6)^2 - 20(6) + 32 $$
$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = 3(36) - 120 + 32 $$
$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = 108 - 120 + 32 $$
$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = -12 + 32 $$
$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = 20 $$
So, the gradient of $l_1$ is $m=20$.

Now, use the point $(x_1, y_1) = (6, 16)$ and gradient $m=20$ in the formula $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$:
$$ y - 16 = 20(x - 6) $$
Expand the right side:
$$ y - 16 = 20x - 120 $$
Add 16 to both sides to get the equation in the form $y = mx + c$:
$$ y = 20x - 120 + 16 $$
$$ y = 20x - 104 $$

Answer: $y = 20x - 104$

Part (c): Find the value of $\alpha$

Thought Process:
We are given that line $l_2$ is the tangent to $C$ at the point where $x = \alpha$. We are also told that $l_1$ and $l_2$ are parallel and distinct. Parallel lines have the same gradient. From part (b), we know the gradient of $l_1$ is $20$. Therefore, the gradient of $l_2$ must also be $20$. The gradient of the tangent at any point $x$ is given by $\frac{dy}{dx}$. So, we need to set $\frac{dy}{dx} = 20$ and solve for $x$. One of the solutions will be $x=6$ (for $l_1$). The other solution will be the value of $\alpha$ for $l_2$, since $l_1$ and $l_2$ are distinct.

Working:
Set the derivative equal to the gradient of $l_1$ (which is $20$):
$$ 3x^2 - 20x + 32 = 20 $$
Rearrange this into a standard quadratic equation ($ax^2 + bx + c = 0$):
$$ 3x^2 - 20x + 32 - 20 = 0 $$
$$ 3x^2 - 20x + 12 = 0 $$
Now, solve this quadratic equation. We can try to factorise it or use the quadratic formula. For factorisation, we look for two numbers that multiply to $3 \times 12 = 36$ and add to $-20$. These numbers are $-2$ and $-18$.
$$ 3x^2 - 18x - 2x + 12 = 0 $$
Factor by grouping:
$$ 3x(x - 6) - 2(x - 6) = 0 $$
$$ (3x - 2)(x - 6) = 0 $$
This gives two possible values for $x$:
$$ 3x - 2 = 0 \implies 3x = 2 \implies x = \frac{2}{3} $$
or
$$ x - 6 = 0 \implies x = 6 $$
We know that $x=6$ corresponds to line $l_1$. Since $l_1$ and $l_2$ are distinct tangents, $\alpha$ must be the other value.
$$ \alpha = \frac{2}{3} $$

Answer: $\alpha = \frac{2}{3}$

Key techniques

  • Expand and differentiate a polynomial function
  • Calculate y-coordinate for a given x-value on a curve
  • Calculate gradient of tangent using dy/dx
  • Form the equation of a tangent line
  • Equate gradients for parallel lines
  • Solve a quadratic equation to find x-values

Common pitfalls

  • Algebraic errors when expanding brackets or collecting terms in part (a), leading to an incorrect $\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  • Calculation mistakes when substituting $x=6$ into $y$ or $\frac{dy}{dx}$ for part (b).
  • Forgetting that parallel lines have equal gradients, or failing to set $\frac{dy}{dx}$ equal to the gradient for part (c). Misinterpreting the 'distinct' condition and giving $x=6$ as the final answer for $\alpha$.
