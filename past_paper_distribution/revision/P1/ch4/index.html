Equations of Tangents and Normals
8×
6-11m

The question asks to 'find the equation of the tangent' or 'find the equation of the normal' to a given curve at a specific point or x-value.

Core strategy
  1. 1. Identify the point $(x_1, y_1)$. If you are only given $x_1$, substitute it into the original curve equation $y=f(x)$ to find $y_1$.
  2. 2. Rewrite the curve's equation $y=f(x)$ into differentiable form, with each term as $ax^n$. Pay close attention to roots and fractions (e.g., $\frac{k}{x^p} = kx^{-p}$).
  3. 3. Differentiate to find the gradient function, $\frac{dy}{dx} = f'(x)$, using the power rule.
  4. 4. Calculate the gradient of the tangent, $m_T$, by substituting the x-coordinate $x_1$ into your expression for $\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  5. 5. Determine the required gradient. For a TANGENT, use $m = m_T$. For a NORMAL, you must calculate the perpendicular gradient $m = m_N = -1/m_T$.
  6. 6. Substitute the point $(x_1, y_1)$ and your calculated gradient $m$ into the equation of a line, $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$.
  7. 7. Rearrange the resulting equation into the specific format requested by the question (e.g., $ax+by+c=0$ or $y=mx+c$).
Must-know formulas
$y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ (formula booklet)
For a normal, the gradient is $m_N = -\frac{1}{m_T}$ (MEMORIZE)
Common pitfalls
  • Incorrectly calculating the normal's gradient (e.g., forgetting the negative sign, or not taking the reciprocal).
  • Substituting the x-coordinate into the original function $y$ to find the gradient, instead of into the derivative $\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  • Errors converting terms like $\frac{1}{\sqrt{x}}$ into index form, leading to an incorrect derivative.

Stationary Points and Gradients
7×
5-9m

Key phrases are 'find the coordinates of the stationary points', 'turning points', 'local maximum/minimum', or 'find the points where the gradient is k'.

Core strategy
  1. 1. Rewrite the curve's equation $y=f(x)$ into differentiable $ax^n$ terms.
  2. 2. Differentiate to find the gradient function, $\frac{dy}{dx} = f'(x)$.
  3. 3. Set up the correct equation based on the question. For stationary points, set $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$. For a specific gradient $k$, set $\frac{dy}{dx} = k$.
  4. 4. Solve the resulting equation to find the x-coordinate(s). This is often a quadratic or an equation with fractional/negative powers that can be simplified.
  5. 5. For each x-value found, substitute it back into the ORIGINAL equation $y=f(x)$ to find the corresponding y-coordinate.
  6. 6. (If asked to determine the nature) Find the second derivative, $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$. Substitute the x-coordinate of the stationary point. If $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} > 0$, it's a local minimum. If $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} < 0$, it's a local maximum.
Must-know formulas
For stationary points, $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$ (MEMORIZE)
For a local minimum, $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} > 0$ (MEMORIZE)
For a local maximum, $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} < 0$ (MEMORIZE)
Common pitfalls
  • Solving for x but then forgetting to substitute back into the original equation to find the y-coordinates.
  • Algebraic errors solving the equation, particularly with fractional powers (e.g., treating $x^{1/2}=4$ as $x=2$ instead of $x=16$).
  • Differentiating incorrectly, especially negative or fractional powers (e.g., $\frac{d}{dx}(x^{-1}) = -x^{-2}$, not $x^{-2}$ or $-x^0$).

Curve Analysis and Applications
5×
10-12m

A multi-part question, usually on a cubic function, that combines differentiation with other topics like sketching, roots, inequalities, or transformations.

Core strategy
  1. 1. Prepare the function: Expand any brackets to get the polynomial in the form $y = ax^3+bx^2+cx+d$.
  2. 2. Find key features for sketching: Find the y-intercept by setting $x=0$. Find the x-intercepts (roots) by setting $y=0$. Note that a repeated root means the curve touches, not crosses, the x-axis.
  3. 3. Differentiate to find $\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  4. 4. Find stationary points by setting $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$, solving for x, and finding the corresponding y-values. Use these to accurately place turning points on your sketch.
  5. 5. Synthesize and sketch: Use the roots, y-intercept, and turning points to draw a smooth curve. Check the overall shape (e.g., a positive cubic starts low, ends high).
  6. 6. Address the specific question parts, which might involve finding a tangent at a point, solving an inequality $f(x)>0$ by looking at your sketch, or describing a transformation.
Must-know formulas
A repeated root (e.g. from a factor $(x-a)^2$) corresponds to a point where the curve touches the x-axis.
Common pitfalls
  • Algebraic errors when expanding brackets, which then lead to an incorrect derivative and wrong stationary points.
  • Confusing a 'touch' (repeated root) with a 'cut' (single root) when sketching.
  • Sign errors with horizontal translations, commonly thinking $f(x+a)$ is a shift to the right instead of to the left.

Differentiation and Integration
5×
6-10m

A question asking you to both differentiate and integrate a function, or one that provides the derivative $f'(x)$ and asks you to find the original function $f(x)$ given a point on the curve.

Core strategy
  1. 1. Rewrite the given function(s) into $ax^n$ form, ready for calculus.
  2. 2. Identify the process: Differentiating is finding $\frac{dy}{dx}$ from $y$. Integrating is finding $y$ from $\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  3. 3. Apply the correct power rule: For differentiation, multiply by the power, then subtract one from the power. For integration, add one to the power, then divide by the new power.
  4. 4. When integrating to find $y = f(x)$, ALWAYS include the constant of integration: '`+ C`'.
  5. 5. If a point $(x_1, y_1)$ on the curve is provided, substitute these values into your integrated equation to form an equation in C.
  6. 6. Solve for C and write down the final, complete equation for the curve.
Must-know formulas
$\frac{d}{dx}(ax^n) = anx^{n-1}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\int ax^n dx = \frac{ax^{n+1}}{n+1} + C$ (for $n \neq -1$) (formula booklet)
Common pitfalls
  • Forgetting the constant of integration '+C' is the most common and easily avoided error.
  • Mixing up the rules for differentiation and integration (e.g., adding 1 to the power when differentiating).
  • Arithmetic errors with fractions or negative indices, e.g., $-2+1 = -1$, not $-3$.

Differentiation from First Principles
2×
6m

The question will explicitly use the phrase 'from first principles' to find the derivative of a simple polynomial, usually a quadratic.

Core strategy
  1. 1. Write down the definition of the derivative: $f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$.
  2. 2. Find the expression for $f(x+h)$ by carefully replacing every $x$ in $f(x)$ with $(x+h)$.
  3. 3. Expand any brackets in your $f(x+h)$ expression. Be very careful with terms like $(x+h)^2 = x^2 + 2xh + h^2$.
  4. 4. Substitute your expanded $f(x+h)$ and the original $f(x)$ into the numerator of the formula. All terms that don't contain $h$ must cancel out. If they don't, you have made a mistake.
  5. 5. Factor out $h$ from every term remaining in the numerator.
  6. 6. Cancel the $h$ in the numerator with the $h$ in the denominator.
  7. 7. Evaluate the limit by substituting $h=0$ into the simplified expression to get the final derivative, $f'(x)$.
Must-know formulas
$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$ (formula booklet)
Common pitfalls
  • Errors expanding $(x+h)^2$, typically by forgetting the middle $2xh$ term.
  • Sign errors when subtracting $f(x)$, especially if $f(x)$ itself contains negative terms (e.g., $-(x^2 - 5x) = -x^2 + 5x$).
  • Trying to cancel $h$ before all non-h terms in the numerator have cancelled out.

Finding Unknown Constants in the Function
2×
5-7m

The curve's equation contains an unknown constant (e.g., $y = x^2 + kx + 3$). You are given information about the gradient at a specific point (e.g., 'the tangent at $x=4$ is parallel to the line $y=2x$').

Core strategy
  1. 1. Write down the function $y=f(x)$ which includes the unknown constant, say 'k'.
  2. 2. Differentiate the function term-by-term to find $\frac{dy}{dx}$. Remember to treat 'k' as a coefficient (e.g., the derivative of $kx$ is $k$).
  3. 3. Translate the given information into a mathematical statement about the derivative. For example, 'the gradient at $x=a$ is $m$' means $\frac{dy}{dx} = m$ when $x=a$.
  4. 4. Substitute the given x-value into your expression for $\frac{dy}{dx}$ and set the result equal to the given gradient.
  5. 5. Solve the resulting equation, which is usually linear, to find the value of the unknown constant 'k'.
Must-know formulas
The derivative of a constant term is zero, e.g., $\frac{d}{dx}(k) = 0$.
The derivative of a term with a constant coefficient is $\frac{d}{dx}(kf(x)) = k f'(x)$.
Common pitfalls
  • Incorrectly differentiating the term with the constant, for example differentiating $kx$ to $0$ instead of $k$.
  • Substituting the given x-value into the original function $y=f(x)$ instead of the derivative $\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  • Arithmetic errors when solving the final equation for the constant.

Must memorize

  • The Power Rule: $\frac{d}{dx}(ax^n) = anx^{n-1}$
  • Condition for Stationary Points: $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$
  • Gradient of a Normal: $m_N = -\frac{1}{m_T}$
  • Second Derivative Test: For a stationary point, if $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} > 0$ it is a local minimum; if $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} < 0$ it is a local maximum.

Global strategy

  • 1. **Rewrite First, Differentiate Second.** Your first step in almost every question should be to rewrite the function into a 'calculus-ready' form where every term is $ax^n$. This single habit prevents the most common errors.
  • 2. **Master the Power Rule.** The rule $\frac{d}{dx}(ax^n) = anx^{n-1}$ is the foundation of this chapter. Be fluent in applying it to positive, negative, and fractional powers without hesitation.
  • 3. **Remember: $\frac{dy}{dx}$ IS the Gradient.** The central concept is that the derivative gives you a formula for the gradient of the tangent at any point on the curve. All tangent, normal, and stationary point problems rely on this fact.
  • 4. **Stationary Points mean $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$.** This is a non-negotiable trigger. The moment you see 'stationary point', 'turning point', 'maximum', or 'minimum', your first thought should be 'find the derivative and set it to zero'.
  • 5. **Distinguish $y$ from $\frac{dy}{dx}$.** To find a coordinate on the curve, use the original equation $y=f(x)$. To find the gradient at that point, use the derivative $\frac{dy}{dx}=f'(x)$. Mixing these up is a frequent source of lost marks.
  • 6. **Check Your Algebra.** Calculus steps are often simple, but they are embedded in algebra. Errors in expanding brackets, solving equations with indices, or working with fractions are the main reason students lose marks.

Exam tips

  1. For tangent/normal questions, create a mini-checklist on your paper: Point $(x_1, y_1)$? Gradient $m$? This ensures you find both the y-coordinate and the correct gradient before writing the line equation.
  2. If a question has a 'Show that...' part for a derivative or equation, you MUST use that given result for the following parts, even if you couldn't derive it yourself. This firewalls your working and allows you to score marks on the rest of the question.
  3. Always rewrite the function in a clean, differentiable format on a new line ($y = ...$) before you write $\frac{dy}{dx} = ...$. This separation reduces careless errors.
  4. In 'First Principles' questions, remember the goal is to cancel an 'h' from the denominator. If your algebra doesn't lead to a state where you can factor out 'h' from the numerator, re-check your expansion of $f(x+h)$ immediately.
