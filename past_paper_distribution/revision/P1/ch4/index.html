Finding Equations of Tangents and Normals
#1 most important
11×
7-11m

You are given a curve's equation, $y=f(x)$, and a point $P$ (or just its x-coordinate). You're asked to find the equation of the line that is either the tangent or the normal to the curve at point $P$. The question may require the final equation in a specific format like $ax+by+c=0$.

Assumptions
  • The curve is smooth and continuous at the point of interest.
  • The point P lies on the curve, so its coordinates satisfy the curve's equation.
Approaches
The Standard 5-Step Method
When: Always. This is the universal procedure for these questions.
  1. Step 1 (Point): If not given the full coordinate of P, substitute the given x-coordinate into the original equation $y=f(x)$ to find the y-coordinate.
  2. Step 2 (Differentiate): Rewrite $f(x)$ into index form ($ax^n$) and find the derivative, $\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  3. Step 3 (Gradient): Substitute the x-coordinate of P into $\frac{dy}{dx}$ to find the numerical gradient of the tangent, $m_T$.
  4. Step 4 (Target Gradient): If finding a tangent, use $m = m_T$. If finding a normal, calculate the normal's gradient: $m_N = -\frac{1}{m_T}$.
  5. Step 5 (Line Equation): Use the point from Step 1 and the gradient from Step 4 in the line equation formula $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ and rearrange as required.
+ Pros: A reliable, step-by-step process that works every time.
– Cons: Requires careful execution at each step; an error in an early step cascades through the problem.
Formulas
$\frac{d}{dx}(ax^n) = anx^{n-1}$ (MEMORIZE)
$m_{normal} = -\frac{1}{m_{tangent}}$ (MEMORIZE)
$y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Incorrectly converting roots/fractions to index form, e.g., $\frac{1}{x\sqrt{x}} = x^{-3/2}$, not $x^{-1.5}$ or other variants.
  • Confusing the normal and tangent gradients. A common mistake is using $m_T$ when asked for the normal.
  • Substituting the x-coordinate into the original function $y=f(x)$ to find the gradient, instead of into the derivative $\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  • Algebraic errors when rearranging the final line equation into the required form, especially with fractions and signs.

Assumptions
  • You must use the provided limit formula and cannot just write down the answer using the power rule.
Approaches
The Limit Definition Method
When: Only when explicitly asked to use 'first principles'.
  1. Step 1: Write down the formula for the definition of the derivative: $f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$.
  2. Step 2: Substitute the specific function $f(x)$ into the formula. This involves calculating $f(x+h)$ by replacing every $x$ in the function with $(x+h)$.
  3. Step 3: Expand all brackets in the $f(x+h)$ term and simplify the numerator. All terms from the original $f(x)$ should cancel out.
  4. Step 4: Every remaining term in the numerator must have a factor of $h$. Factor out $h$ from the numerator.
  5. Step 5: Cancel the $h$ in the numerator with the $h$ in the denominator.
  6. Step 6: Evaluate the limit by setting the remaining $h$ to 0. The result is the derivative, $f'(x)$.
+ Pros: Guaranteed marks if the algebraic procedure is followed correctly.
– Cons: Very prone to algebraic errors, especially when expanding brackets like $(x+h)^2$ or $(x+h)^3$.
Formulas
$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Incorrectly expanding $f(x+h)$, for example writing $(x+h)^2$ as $x^2+h^2$ instead of $x^2+2xh+h^2$.
  • Sign errors when subtracting $f(x)$ in the numerator.
  • Failing to cancel the $h$ from the denominator. If you cannot cancel it, you have made an algebraic mistake in the numerator.

Approaches
A: Differentiate twice + solve
When: When asked to find f''(x) and then solve f''(x) = 0 or f''(x) > 0.
  1. Differentiate f(x) once to get f'(x).
  2. Differentiate again to get f''(x).
  3. Set up and solve the equation/inequality involving f''(x).
  4. Interpret in context (max/min/inflection).
B: Second-derivative test for stationary points
When: When you've found stationary points and need to classify them.
  1. At each stationary point x = a, evaluate f''(a).
  2. If f''(a) > 0 → local minimum.
  3. If f''(a) < 0 → local maximum.
  4. If f''(a) = 0 → inconclusive, check sign change of f'.
Formulas
$\frac{d^2 y}{dx^2}$ = derivative of $\frac{dy}{dx}$
Point of inflection: $f''(x) = 0$ and $f''$ changes sign
Min: $f'(a)=0, f''(a)>0$; Max: $f'(a)=0, f''(a)<0$
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to differentiate the second time (i.e., reporting f'(x) as f''(x)).
  • Sign/coefficient errors when applying power rule on negative exponents (e.g., x^{-1} → -x^{-2} → 2x^{-3}).
  • Concluding 'point of inflection' from f''=0 alone without confirming a sign change.

Assumptions
  • A repeated root in a polynomial corresponds to a point where the curve touches (is tangent to) the x-axis.
  • A single root corresponds to a point where the curve crosses the x-axis.
Approaches
Combined Analysis Method
When: For complex, multi-part questions involving a single function.
  1. Step 1 (Analysis): From the factored form, identify the roots (where $y=0$) and determine if they are single (crosses axis) or repeated (touches axis). Find the y-intercept by setting $x=0$.
  2. Step 2 (Expansion): Expand the factored form to get the full polynomial, e.g., $ax^3+bx^2+cx+d$. Check your algebra carefully.
  3. Step 3 (Differentiation): Differentiate the expanded polynomial to find $\frac{dy}{dx}$ and $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$.
  4. Step 4 (Stationary Points): Use the methods from the 'Stationary Points' question type (set $\frac{dy}{dx}=0$, solve, find coordinates) to locate turning points.
  5. Step 5 (Sketching): Use the roots, y-intercept, and stationary points to draw a clear sketch of the curve.
  6. Step 6 (Transformation): If asked about a transformation like $g(x)=f(x+a)$, describe the geometric transformation (e.g., translation by vector $\begin{pmatrix} -a \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$) and apply it to key features like the stationary points.
+ Pros: Integrates multiple P1 skills, showing a deep understanding of functions.
– Cons: Very high risk of error propagation; a mistake in the expansion in Step 2 will make all subsequent calculus incorrect.
Formulas
Transformation $y=f(x+a)$ is a horizontal translation by vector $\begin{pmatrix} -a \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$ (MEMORIZE)
Transformation $y=f(x)+a$ is a vertical translation by vector $\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ a \end{pmatrix}$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Algebraic errors when expanding multiple brackets, especially with negative signs.
  • Misinterpreting a repeated root as a crossing point instead of a touching point on a sketch.
  • Getting the direction of a horizontal translation wrong, e.g., thinking $f(x+2)$ is a shift 2 units to the right (it is to the left).

Assumptions
  • A solution exists; there is at least one point on the curve that satisfies the given gradient condition.
Approaches
Differentiate, Equate, and Solve
When: Used for any problem asking for a point with a specific gradient property.
  1. Step 1: Find the derivative of the curve's equation, $\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  2. Step 2: Determine the target gradient, $m$. If the tangent is parallel to $y=mx+c$, the target gradient is $m$. If given a numerical value, use that.
  3. Step 3: Set up the equation by equating the derivative to the target gradient: $\frac{dy}{dx} = m$.
  4. Step 4: Solve the resulting equation for $x$. This may be linear, quadratic, or a 'disguised quadratic' requiring substitution (e.g., let $u = \sqrt{x}$).
  5. Step 5: If required, substitute the found x-value(s) back into the original equation $y=f(x)$ to find the full coordinates.
+ Pros: Direct and logical extension of basic differentiation.
– Cons: The solving step (Step 4) can involve tricky algebra, especially with fractional/negative powers or disguised quadratics.
Formulas
Parallel lines have equal gradients: $m_1 = m_2$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Making algebraic errors when rearranging and solving the equation set up in Step 3.
  • Incorrectly dealing with disguised quadratics, e.g., mistakes in substitution or failing to solve for the original variable $x$ after finding the substitute variable $u$.
  • Forgetting to consider domain restrictions (e.g., $x>0$ for functions involving $\sqrt{x}$ or $\log x$) which may invalidate a solution.

Finding and Classifying Stationary Points
#6
4×
6-9m

Assumptions
  • The function is differentiable over the domain of interest.
  • The question is asking for local maxima/minima, not global ones.
Approaches
The Second Derivative Test
When: This is the standard and most efficient method in P1.
  1. Step 1: Find the first derivative, $\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  2. Step 2: For stationary points, set the first derivative to zero: $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$.
  3. Step 3: Solve the resulting equation for $x$ to find the x-coordinates of the stationary points.
  4. Step 4: Find the second derivative, $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$.
  5. Step 5: Substitute each stationary x-coordinate into $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ to determine its nature:
  6. - If $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} > 0$, the point is a local minimum.
  7. - If $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} < 0$, the point is a local maximum.
  8. - (If $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 0$, the test is inconclusive - this is rare in P1).
  9. Step 6: Substitute the x-coordinates back into the original equation $y=f(x)$ to find the corresponding y-coordinates.
+ Pros: A definitive and structured method to find and classify points.
– Cons: Requires an extra differentiation step; algebraic errors in solving $\frac{dy}{dx}=0$ are common.
Formulas
For stationary points: $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$ (MEMORIZE)
Second Derivative Test: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} > 0 \implies$ Minimum; $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} < 0 \implies$ Maximum (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Errors differentiating negative or fractional powers.
  • Algebraic mistakes when solving the equation $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$, which is often a quadratic or involves fractional powers.
  • Forgetting to find the y-coordinate of the stationary point after finding x.
  • Calculating $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ incorrectly or making a sign error when evaluating it.

Must memorize

  • The Power Rule: $\frac{d}{dx}(ax^n) = anx^{n-1}$
  • Derivative of a constant: $\frac{d}{dx}(c) = 0$
  • Derivative of a linear term: $\frac{d}{dx}(ax) = a$
  • Condition for stationary points: $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$
  • Gradient of a Normal: $m_N = -\frac{1}{m_T}$ where $m_T$ is the tangent gradient.
  • The Second Derivative Test: If $\frac{dy}{dx}=0$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} > 0 \implies$ Local Minimum. $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} < 0 \implies$ Local Maximum.

Global strategy

  • 1. **Rewrite First, Differentiate Second:** Before you do any calculus, convert all terms into the standard index form $ax^n$. This is the most common source of early errors. Master conversions like $\frac{5}{x^3} = 5x^{-3}$ and $4\sqrt{x} = 4x^{1/2}$.
  • 2. **The Power Rule is Everything:** Be fluent in applying $\frac{d}{dx}(ax^n) = anx^{n-1}$. Pay meticulous attention to signs when $n$ is negative (e.g., differentiating $x^{-2}$ gives $-2x^{-3}$) and to fractions.
  • 3. **Distinguish the Function and its Derivative:** Know when to use $y=f(x)$ and when to use $\frac{dy}{dx}$. To find a point's y-coordinate, use $y=f(x)$. To find a curve's gradient, use $\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  • 4. **Read the Final Instruction Carefully:** Does the question ask for an x-coordinate, full coordinates, the equation of a line, or the nature of a point? Give exactly what is requested.
  • 5. **Check Your Algebra:** Differentiation in P1 is procedurally simple. Most lost marks come from algebraic slips: incorrect expansion, errors in solving quadratics, or sign mistakes. Build in time to check your working.
  • 6. **Tangent vs. Normal:** Always double-check if you need the tangent or the normal. If it's the normal, write down $m_T$ first, then explicitly write $m_N = -1/m_T$ before proceeding. This simple habit prevents forgetting the negative reciprocal.
  • 7. **Show Full Working:** Lay out your steps logically. Write `dy/dx = ...`, show the substitution of the x-value, state the gradient you are using, and show the line equation formula. This helps you track your logic and can earn method marks even if a calculation error occurs.

Exam tips

  1. Before starting, quickly scan the question for keywords like 'tangent', 'normal', 'stationary', or 'first principles' to immediately activate the correct mental playbook.
  2. When a question requires the equation of a line in the form $ax+by+c=0$, make sure your final answer has integer values for a, b, and c. Multiply the entire equation by a common denominator if you have fractions.
  3. For 'First Principles' questions, the goal is always to cancel the $h$ in the denominator. If you expand the numerator and you can't factor out an $h$ from every term, you have made an error. Go back and check your expansion.
  4. If you solve $\frac{dy}{dx}=0$ and get two x-values, remember to find the corresponding y-values for both points and check the nature of both points, unless the question specifies only one.
  5. Be extra careful with negative powers. Remember that subtracting 1 from a negative power makes it 'more negative'. E.g., the power of $x^{-3}$ becomes $-4$, not $-2$.
