A problem about a sequence with a common ratio. Information is given about terms, sums, or the sum to infinity, and you need to find 'a', 'r', or 'n'. Often involves solving quadratic equations or inequalities with logarithms.

Triggersgeometric sequencegeometric seriescommon ratiosum to infinitypercentage increase/decrease
Assumptions
  • The common ratio 'r' is non-zero and constant throughout the sequence.
  • For a sum to infinity ($S_\infty$) to exist, the condition $|r| < 1$ must be met.
Approaches
A: Formulate and Solve (Non-Linear) Equations
When: The standard method for all GP problems.
  1. Translate the given information (e.g., $u_2=12, u_5=324$) into equations using GP formulas.
  2. If given two terms, form two equations ($ar=12$, $ar^4=324$) and divide one by the other to eliminate 'a' and find 'r': $\frac{ar^4}{ar} = \frac{324}{12} \implies r^3 = 27$.
  3. Substitute 'r' back into one of the equations to find 'a'.
  4. If given sums or a mix of terms and sums, substitute to form a single equation, which might be a quadratic in 'r' or 'a'.
  5. If $S_\infty$ is used, you MUST state the condition $|r| < 1$ and use it to reject any invalid solutions for 'r'.
+ Pros: Dividing term equations is a very efficient way to find 'r'.
– Cons: The algebra can be more complex than for APs, involving indices, quadratics, and sometimes logarithms.
Formulas
$u_n = ar^{n-1}$ (n-th term) (formula booklet)
$S_n = \frac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r} = \frac{a(r^n-1)}{r-1}$ (sum of n terms) (formula booklet)
$S_\infty = \frac{a}{1-r}$ (sum to infinity) (formula booklet)
Condition for $S_\infty$: $|r| < 1$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Using $ar^n$ instead of $ar^{n-1}$ for the n-th term.
  • Forgetting to state and check the condition $|r|<1$ when using the sum to infinity formula.
  • In percentage problems, a 4% increase means $r=1.04$, not $r=0.04$. A 4% decrease means $r=0.96$.
  • Algebraic errors with indices when dividing equations.

Assumptions
  • The power 'n' is a positive integer.
  • In approximation questions, the value of 'x' is small enough for higher powers to be negligible.
Approaches
A: Direct Application of Formula
When: This is the standard and universal method for all binomial expansion questions in P2.
  1. Identify the components of your expression: 'a', 'b', and 'n' from $(a+b)^n$. Be careful: 'b' might be something like '-kx/2'.
  2. Write down the formula from the booklet: $(a+b)^n = a^n + \binom{n}{1}a^{n-1}b + \binom{n}{2}a^{n-2}b^2 + ...$
  3. Substitute 'a', 'b', and 'n' into the formula. Use brackets liberally, especially around the 'b' term: e.g., $\binom{8}{2}(2)^6(-\frac{x}{4})^2$.
  4. Calculate each binomial coefficient, $\binom{n}{r}$.
  5. Simplify each term, paying close attention to signs and powers of coefficients: $(-\frac{x}{4})^2$ becomes $+\frac{x^2}{16}$.
  6. If finding a coefficient from a product, e.g., $(1+x)(2-x)^5$, identify all pairs of terms that multiply to give the desired power of x and sum their coefficients.
+ Pros: Systematic and guarantees the correct answer if algebra is done carefully.
– Cons: Prone to arithmetic errors from coefficients, signs, or powers if rushed.
Formulas
$(a+b)^n = a^n + \binom{n}{1}a^{n-1}b + \binom{n}{2}a^{n-2}b^2 + ... + \binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r + ... + b^n$ (formula booklet)
$\binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to apply the power to the entire 'b' term, e.g., writing $3x^2$ instead of $(3x)^2=9x^2$.
  • Sign errors when the 'b' term is negative, especially on odd vs. even powers.
  • When multiplying two expansions, missing one of the combinations of terms that produces the required power.
  • Errors in calculating the binomial coefficient $\binom{n}{r}$ on the calculator or by hand.

Triggersarithmetic progressiongeometric progression(both mentioned in the same question)
Assumptions
  • All standard assumptions for both AP and GP apply.
Approaches
A: Systematic Equation Building and Substitution
When: Always. This is the only method for these integrated problems.
  1. Define your variables clearly and separately. Use 'a' and 'd' for the AP, and different letters like 'b' and 'r' for the GP to avoid confusion (or be extremely careful if using 'a' for both).
  2. Translate each statement in the problem into an equation using the correct formula for the sequence type it refers to.
  3. Establish the link equations. E.g., if 'the 3rd term of AP equals the 2nd term of GP', write $a+2d = br$.
  4. This creates a system of simultaneous equations. The key is often to express everything in terms of just two variables (e.g., 'a' and 'd') and then use the GP common ratio property ($u_2/u_1 = u_3/u_2$) to form a single solvable equation.
  5. Solve the system, which will often result in a quadratic equation.
  6. Check all conditions (like $|r|<1$ if needed) and answer the specific question asked.
+ Pros: A good test of systematic working and algebraic skill, often with high marks available.
– Cons: Very easy to mix up formulas or variables. The algebra can become complicated quickly.
Formulas
All AP and GP formulas are required.
The common ratio property: $\frac{u_{n+1}}{u_n} = r$ (constant) is very useful. (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Using an AP formula for a GP term, or vice-versa. Write the formulas down before you start!
  • Using the same variable 'a' for the first term of both sequences and getting confused.
  • Making an algebraic slip when expanding brackets or simplifying, leading to an incorrect quadratic equation.

A word problem or abstract question describing a sequence with a common difference. You are given information about specific terms (e.g., 'the 5th term is 21') and/or sums (e.g., 'the sum of the first 10 terms is 180') and asked to find 'a', 'd', or 'n'.

Triggersarithmetic sequencearithmetic seriescommon differencesum of the first n termssalary increases by a fixed amountsavings plan
Assumptions
  • The common difference 'd' is constant throughout the sequence.
Approaches
A: Formulate and Solve Simultaneous Equations
When: This is the primary method for almost all AP problems with two unknown variables (usually 'a' and 'd').
  1. Identify and write down the given information, e.g., $u_5=21$, $S_{10}=180$.
  2. Translate each piece of information into an equation using the AP formulas: $u_n = a+(n-1)d$ and $S_n = \frac{n}{2}(2a+(n-1)d)$.
  3. This will create a system of two equations, usually linear, in terms of 'a' and 'd'. For example: $a+4d=21$ and $5(2a+9d)=180$.
  4. Solve the simultaneous equations using substitution or elimination.
  5. If the $S_n$ formula leads to a quadratic equation in 'n', solve it and discard any non-positive integer solutions.
  6. Reread the question to ensure you answer what is asked (e.g., finding $u_{20}$, not just 'a' and 'd').
+ Pros: A direct, structured method that breaks the problem down into pure algebra.
– Cons: High potential for algebraic errors when solving the equations.
Formulas
$u_n = a + (n-1)d$ (n-th term) (formula booklet)
$S_n = \frac{n}{2}(2a + (n-1)d)$ (sum of n terms) (formula booklet)
$S_n = \frac{n}{2}(a + l)$ where $l$ is the last term, $u_n$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Using 'nd' instead of '(n-1)d' in the formulas. This is a very common slip.
  • Sign errors when 'd' is negative.
  • Making a mistake when rearranging or solving the simultaneous equations.
  • When solving a quadratic for 'n', forgetting that 'n' must be a positive integer.

Triggersrecurrence relationiterative formula$u_{n+1}$$u_n$limitconvergesproof by induction
Assumptions
  • If a sequence converges to a limit L, then as $n \to \infty$, both $u_n \to L$ and $u_{n+1} \to L$.
Approaches
A: Term-by-Term Iteration
When: To find the first few terms of the sequence (e.g., $u_2, u_3, u_4$) or to calculate a sum of a small number of terms.
  1. Start with the given initial value, e.g., $u_1$.
  2. Substitute this value into the recurrence relation to find the next term, $u_2$.
  3. Repeat the process, substituting the newest term to find the next one.
  4. Be meticulous with calculations, especially with negative numbers or fractions.
+ Pros: Straightforward and requires no advanced theory.
– Cons: Impractical for finding distant terms or general properties.
B: Finding the Limit
When: When the question explicitly asks for the 'limit' or the value the sequence 'approaches'.
  1. Assume the sequence converges to a limit, L.
  2. In the recurrence relation, replace every occurrence of $u_{n+1}$ and $u_n$ with L.
  3. For example, $u_{n+1} = 0.8u_n + 10$ becomes $L = 0.8L + 10$.
  4. Solve this algebraic equation for L.
+ Pros: Very fast and efficient.
– Cons: Only works if the sequence is convergent (which is usually a given if the question asks for a limit).
C: Proof by Induction
When: When asked to PROVE that a given closed-form formula (e.g., $u_n = 3^n - 1$) is the general term for a sequence defined by a recurrence relation.
  1. **Base Case:** Show the given formula works for n=1. Calculate $u_1$ from the formula and check it matches the given $u_1$.
  2. **Inductive Hypothesis:** Assume the formula is true for n=k. Write down 'Assume $u_k = ...$'.
  3. **Inductive Step:** Use the recurrence relation to find $u_{k+1}$ in terms of $u_k$. Substitute your assumption for $u_k$ and manipulate the algebra to show that the result matches the given formula for n=k+1.
  4. **Conclusion:** Write a concluding sentence stating that the formula is true for all positive integers n by the principle of mathematical induction.
+ Pros: The only rigorous way to prove a general formula of this type.
– Cons: Requires a very clear logical structure and can be algebraically demanding.
Formulas
Limit of $u_{n+1} = f(u_n)$ is found by solving $L=f(L)$. (MEMORIZE TECHNIQUE)
Pitfalls
  • Simple arithmetic errors when iterating the sequence.
  • When finding a limit, forgetting to replace BOTH $u_{n+1}$ and $u_n$ with L.
  • In proof by induction, failing to clearly state the assumption or the conclusion, or making an algebraic error in the inductive step.

TriggersProve that the sum...$\sum$summation notation
Assumptions
  • Standard summation rules, such as splitting terms, apply.
Approaches
A: Proof by Pairing (for AP Sum)
When: When asked to prove the AP sum formula.
  1. Write $S_n$ forwards: $S_n = a + (a+d) + \dots + (a+(n-1)d)$.
  2. Write $S_n$ backwards: $S_n = (a+(n-1)d) + (a+(n-2)d) + \dots + a$.
  3. Add the two equations vertically, term by term.
  4. Note that each pair of terms sums to the same value: $2a+(n-1)d$.
  5. Recognise there are 'n' such pairs, so $2S_n = n[2a+(n-1)d]$.
  6. Divide by 2 to get the required formula.
+ Pros: The classic, elegant proof that is expected.
– Cons: Can be confusing if you haven't seen it before; easy to miscount terms.
B: Splitting the Summation
When: When the term inside a $\Sigma$ is a sum or difference of other terms, e.g., $\sum_{r=1}^{15} (3r + (-1)^r)$.
  1. Use the property $\sum (A_r + B_r) = \sum A_r + \sum B_r$.
  2. Identify each separate summation. One part might be an AP (like $\sum 3r$), while another might be a GP or a simple pattern (like $\sum (-1)^r$).
  3. Evaluate each summation using the appropriate formula or by direct calculation if it's a simple pattern.
  4. Combine the results.
+ Pros: Reduces a complicated problem into manageable, standard pieces.
– Cons: Requires you to recognise the different types of series within the expression.
Formulas
$\sum_{r=1}^{n} (A_r \pm B_r) = \sum_{r=1}^{n} A_r \pm \sum_{r=1}^{n} B_r$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • In the AP proof, making an error in the expression for the last term or the number of terms.
  • When splitting a sum, using the wrong formula for one of the parts or miscalculating the number of terms.

Must memorize

  • Condition for convergence of a GP for $S_\infty$: $|r| < 1$. This is not given in the formula booklet and is required for full marks.
  • A percentage increase of p% means the common ratio $r = 1 + \frac{p}{100}$.
  • A percentage decrease of p% means the common ratio $r = 1 - \frac{p}{100}$.
  • The common ratio property of a GP: if $x, y, z$ are consecutive terms, then $\frac{y}{x} = \frac{z}{y}$. This is essential for solving many mixed AP/GP problems.
  • The structure of a proof by induction: Base Case, Inductive Hypothesis, Inductive Step, Conclusion.

Global strategy

  • **1. Identify Sequence Type First:** Before writing anything else, read the question and decide: is it Arithmetic (AP), Geometric (GP), a Recurrence Relation, or Binomial? This single decision dictates every formula you will use.
  • **2. Write Down Relevant Formulas:** Once identified, immediately write the key formulas ($u_n$, $S_n$, $S_\infty$) on your page. This prevents you from using an AP formula in a GP context under pressure.
  • **3. Translate Words to Algebra Systematically:** Convert every clause of the question into a mathematical equation. 'The third term is 12' becomes $u_3=12$. This turns a word problem into a pure algebra problem.
  • **4. Master Simultaneous Equations:** A huge proportion of questions in this chapter reduce to solving a pair of simultaneous equations. Be fluent in solving linear pairs and non-linear pairs (especially by dividing GP term equations).
  • **5. State and Check Conditions:** For any question involving $S_\infty$, you must write down and use the condition $|r|<1$. If you solve a quadratic for 'r', you must check which solution is valid. If you solve for 'n', it must be a positive integer.
  • **6. Use Brackets in Binomial Expansion:** When expanding $(a+bx)^n$, always write the terms as, for example, $\binom{n}{r}(a)^{n-r}(bx)^r$. The bracket around 'bx' is crucial and prevents errors like writing $2x^3$ instead of $(2x)^3=8x^3$.
  • **7. Don't Confuse Variables in Mixed Problems:** In mixed AP/GP questions, use different letters for the parameters (e.g., $a_A, d$ for the AP and $a_G, r$ for the GP) to keep your work clear and avoid errors.

Exam tips

  1. When a question asks for a sum or term 'in terms of k', don't panic. Just carry the letter 'k' through your normal calculations as if it were a number.
  2. For binomial expansion, calculate the three parts of each term separately: the coefficient $\binom{n}{r}$, the power of 'a', and the power of 'b'. Then multiply them together at the end. This isolates potential errors.
  3. If you solve for 'r' and get two answers (e.g., $r=2$ and $r=1/2$), re-read the question for clues. If it mentions a 'sum to infinity', you know you must choose $r=1/2$.
  4. Always re-read the very last line of the question before you stop. Have you actually answered what was asked for? (e.g., Did they want the common difference, or the 20th term?).
  5. Don't round intermediate calculations. Keep values exact (as fractions or in your calculator's memory) until the final answer.
  6. When you get an answer, do a quick 'sanity check'. Does it make sense? If a sequence is decreasing, 'd' or 'r' should be negative or less than 1 respectively. If the sum is large, 'n' should probably be large.
