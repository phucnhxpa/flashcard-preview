Triggersperpendicular bisectormidpointgradientintersectionarea of triangle
Assumptions
  • Lines are straight and extend infinitely.
  • Standard Euclidean geometry rules apply.
Approaches
A: Step-by-step Formula Application
When: This is the standard procedure for all problems of this type.
  1. For a Perpendicular Bisector: 1. Find the midpoint of the segment. 2. Find the gradient of the segment. 3. Calculate the negative reciprocal gradient. 4. Use the midpoint and perpendicular gradient in $y-y_1 = m(x-x_1)$.
  2. For Intersection: 1. Set up the two line equations as a simultaneous system. 2. Use substitution or elimination to solve for one variable. 3. Substitute the result back into an original equation to find the second variable.
  3. For Area of a Triangle: 1. Find the coordinates of all three vertices. 2. Use the 'base and height' method or the Shoelace formula: $Area = \frac{1}{2} |x_1(y_2 - y_3) + x_2(y_3 - y_1) + x_3(y_1 - y_2)|$.
+ Pros: Follows a clear, logical sequence of applying basic, well-known formulas.
– Cons: Multiple steps can increase the chance of a single calculation error derailing the whole question.
Formulas
Gradient: $m = \frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}$ (formula booklet)
Midpoint: $M = \left(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)$ (formula booklet)
Perpendicular gradients: $m_1 m_2 = -1$ (MEMORIZE)
Area of Triangle (Shoelace): $Area = \frac{1}{2} |x_1(y_2 - y_3) + x_2(y_3 - y_1) + x_3(y_1 - y_2)|$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Calculation errors with midpoint or gradient formulas, especially with negative coordinates.
  • Incorrectly finding the perpendicular gradient (e.g., using the reciprocal but not changing the sign).
  • Algebraic errors when solving simultaneous equations for intersection points.

Triggerscirclecentreradiusdiameterchordequation of the circle
Assumptions
  • The equation given represents a valid circle, which implies the radius squared term ($r^2$) will be positive.
Approaches
A: Completing the Square
When: When given the general form of the circle's equation ($x^2 + y^2 + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0$).
  1. Group x-terms and y-terms: $(x^2 + 2gx) + (y^2 + 2fy) + c = 0$.
  2. Complete the square for x: $(x+g)^2 - g^2$.
  3. Complete the square for y: $(y+f)^2 - f^2$.
  4. Rewrite the equation: $(x+g)^2 - g^2 + (y+f)^2 - f^2 + c = 0$.
  5. Rearrange to standard form: $(x+g)^2 + (y+f)^2 = g^2 + f^2 - c$.
  6. Identify Centre $(-g, -f)$ and Radius $r = \sqrt{g^2 + f^2 - c}$.
+ Pros: Systematic and always works. Directly provides the centre and radius.
– Cons: Prone to sign errors, especially when identifying the centre coordinates from the brackets.
B: Using Midpoint and Distance Formulae
When: When given the coordinates of two points that form a diameter.
  1. Find the midpoint of the diameter using $M = \left(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)$. This gives the circle's centre $(a, b)$.
  2. Find the radius by calculating the distance from the centre to one of the endpoints using $r = \sqrt{(x_1-a)^2 + (y_1-b)^2}$.
  3. Substitute the centre $(a, b)$ and radius $r$ into the standard equation $(x-a)^2 + (y-b)^2 = r^2$.
+ Pros: More direct than completing the square if diameter endpoints are known.
– Cons: Only applicable in the specific scenario where diameter endpoints are given.
Formulas
Standard form: $(x-a)^2 + (y-b)^2 = r^2$ for centre $(a, b)$ and radius $r$. (formula booklet)
General form: $x^2 + y^2 + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0$ for centre $(-g, -f)$ and radius $\sqrt{g^2+f^2-c}$. (MEMORIZE)
Midpoint: $M = \left(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)$ (formula booklet)
Distance: $d = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2}$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Sign errors identifying the centre, e.g., centre of $(x+3)^2 + (y-2)^2 = 16$ is $(-3, 2)$, not $(3, -2)$.
  • Forgetting to square the radius for the equation, or forgetting to square root the constant term to find the radius.
  • Arithmetic errors when completing the square, especially with the constant terms.
  • Forgetting that $r^2$ must be positive for a real circle to exist.

Triggerstangentnormaltouches the circleat the point P
Assumptions
  • A tangent touches the circle at exactly one point.
  • The radius to the point of tangency is perpendicular to the tangent line.
Approaches
A: Geometric Property (Perpendicular Radius)
When: This is the most direct and reliable method for circles.
  1. Find the coordinates of the circle's centre, C.
  2. Calculate the gradient of the radius from the centre C to the point of tangency P. Let this be $m_{radius}$.
  3. The gradient of the tangent is the negative reciprocal: $m_{tangent} = -1/m_{radius}$.
  4. The gradient of the normal is the same as the radius: $m_{normal} = m_{radius}$.
  5. Use the point-gradient form $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ with point P and the appropriate gradient.
+ Pros: Conceptually straightforward, relying on core coordinate geometry rules.
– Cons: Requires finding the circle's centre first, which may be an extra step.
B: Implicit Differentiation
When: A powerful calculus-based alternative, and good practice for general curves.
  1. Differentiate the circle's equation with respect to x, using the chain rule for y-terms: e.g., $\frac{d}{dx}((y-b)^2) = 2(y-b)\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  2. Rearrange the resulting equation to find an expression for the gradient, $\frac{dy}{dx}$.
  3. Substitute the coordinates of the point of tangency P $(x_1, y_1)$ into the expression for $\frac{dy}{dx}$ to find the numerical gradient of the tangent.
  4. Use the point-gradient form $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ to find the tangent's equation.
+ Pros: A general method that works for any differentiable curve, not just circles.
– Cons: Requires knowledge of implicit differentiation and can be more prone to algebraic errors.
Formulas
Perpendicular gradients: $m_1 m_2 = -1$ (MEMORIZE)
Point-gradient form: $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ (formula booklet)
Implicit Differentiation of $y^n$: $\frac{d}{dx}(y^n) = ny^{n-1}\frac{dy}{dx}$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Mixing up the tangent and normal gradients. Remember: Normal = Radius.
  • Errors in calculating the negative reciprocal (e.g., forgetting the sign change or inverting incorrectly).
  • Mistakes applying the chain rule during implicit differentiation.

Triggerscurveequation of the tangentequation of the normalgradientstationary point
Assumptions
  • The curve is differentiable at the point of interest.
Approaches
A: Standard Differentiation
When: Always for non-circular curves defined by an explicit function $y=f(x)$.
  1. Differentiate the function to find the gradient function, $\frac{dy}{dx} = f'(x)$.
  2. Substitute the x-coordinate of the given point into $f'(x)$ to find the gradient of the tangent, $m_{tangent}$.
  3. If required, find the gradient of the normal: $m_{normal} = -1/m_{tangent}$.
  4. Use the given point and the calculated gradient in the point-gradient form $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$.
  5. If asked for intercepts, set x=0 or y=0 in the line's equation and solve.
+ Pros: Universal method for finding gradients of any explicit function.
– Cons: Entirely dependent on correct application of differentiation rules.
Formulas
Power rule: $\frac{d}{dx}(ax^n) = anx^{n-1}$ (formula booklet)
Perpendicular gradients: $m_1 m_2 = -1$ (MEMORIZE)
Point-gradient form: $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ (formula booklet)
Area of a triangle: $Area = \frac{1}{2} \times base \times height$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Arithmetic errors when substituting the x-coordinate to find the y-coordinate or the gradient.
  • Confusing the gradient of the normal with the tangent (i.e., forgetting the negative reciprocal step).
  • Errors in differentiation, such as with negative or fractional powers.

Assumptions
  • The discriminant of the resulting quadratic determines the nature of the intersection: $b^2-4ac > 0$ for 2 points, $b^2-4ac = 0$ for a tangent (1 point), $b^2-4ac < 0$ for no intersection.
Approaches
A: Substitution and Solving a Quadratic
When: This is the only standard algebraic method for this problem.
  1. Rearrange the linear equation to make y or x the subject (e.g., $y = mx+c$).
  2. Substitute this expression into the equation of the circle.
  3. Expand all brackets and simplify the equation into the standard quadratic form $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$.
  4. Solve the quadratic equation for the first variable (e.g., x) by factorising or using the formula.
  5. Substitute the x-value(s) back into the original *linear* equation to find the corresponding y-value(s).
+ Pros: A guaranteed algebraic method to find the exact points of intersection.
– Cons: Can lead to messy algebra and arithmetic, especially when expanding brackets or using the quadratic formula.
Formulas
Quadratic Formula: $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}$ (formula booklet)
Discriminant for nature of roots: $b^2 - 4ac$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Errors when expanding squared brackets, for example $(x-2)^2 \neq x^2 - 4$.
  • Forgetting to find the corresponding y-coordinates after solving for x.
  • Arithmetic errors when simplifying the quadratic or using the quadratic formula.
  • Substituting the x-values back into the more complex circle equation instead of the simpler linear one.

Must memorize

  • Perpendicular Gradients: $m_1 m_2 = -1$. This is not explicitly in the formula booklet and is absolutely essential. (MEMORIZE)
  • General Circle Equation: $x^2 + y^2 + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0$ has Centre $(-g, -f)$ and Radius $\sqrt{g^2+f^2-c}$. Memorizing this saves time over completing the square, but be careful with signs. (MEMORIZE)
  • Point-gradient form: $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$. The fastest way to write a line's equation. (formula booklet, but memorize for speed)
  • Midpoint Formula: $M = \left(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)$. (formula booklet, but memorize for speed)

Global strategy

  • Always draw a quick sketch. Visualising the problem helps to spot geometric relationships (like perpendicular lines) and prevent errors.
  • Master the circle equation. Be flawless at converting from the general form to the standard $(x-a)^2 + (y-b)^2 = r^2$ form by completing the square. Watch for sign flips in the centre coordinates.
  • The rule $m_1 m_2 = -1$ for perpendicular gradients is the most important concept in this chapter. It's the key to tangents on circles and perpendicular bisectors.
  • Use geometry for circles, calculus for curves. For tangents to circles, the perpendicular radius property is usually faster. For any other curve, differentiation is the way.
  • For any intersection problem, the method is always simultaneous equations. For a line and a circle, this means substituting the linear equation into the circular one.
  • Read the question carefully for the required format of the final answer (e.g., $ax+by+c=0$ vs $y=mx+c$).
  • When asked for 'coordinates' or a 'point', always give both the x and y values in your final answer.

Exam tips

  1. For 'Show that...' questions, you must demonstrate every logical step. If showing a point lies on a line, substitute the coordinates and show that LHS = RHS.
  2. When finding a perpendicular bisector, explicitly state that you are finding the 'midpoint' and the 'perpendicular gradient'. This signals to the examiner that you know the method.
  3. If you solve a quadratic and get a complex surd, quickly double-check your algebraic manipulation. While possible, it's often a sign of an earlier mistake.
  4. Be extra careful with signs when completing the square. $x^2 - 8x$ becomes $(x-4)^2 - 16$, and the x-coordinate of the centre is $+4$.
  5. Always use the simpler (linear) equation to find the second coordinate after solving a system of simultaneous equations to reduce the chance of errors.
