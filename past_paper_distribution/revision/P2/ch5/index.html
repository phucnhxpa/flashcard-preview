Solve exponential equation by quadratic substitution
#1 most important
8×
5-8m

An equation involving terms like $a^{2x}$, $a^{x}$, and a constant. The student must recognise a hidden quadratic in $a^x$ (or $e^x$). Often uses bases 2, 3, or $e$. May include terms like $2^{2x+1}$, $e^{2x}$, $4^x$, or $9^x$ that need rewriting.

Triggerssolveexponential equation2^{2x}e^{2x}4^xgive your answer in the formexact valuelnlog
Assumptions
  • $a^x > 0$ for all real $x$ — so after substitution $y = a^x$, only positive values of $y$ are valid
  • When $a^{m+n}$ appears, it must be split as $a^m \cdot a^n$, NOT $a^m + a^n$
  • Solutions should be exact (logarithmic form) unless told otherwise
Approaches
A: Substitution to quadratic
When: When the equation can be written entirely in terms of $(a^x)^2$, $a^x$, and constants
  1. Rewrite all exponential terms to the same base, e.g. $4^x = (2^2)^x = (2^x)^2$, $2^{2x+1} = 2 \cdot (2^x)^2$
  2. Let $y = a^x$ (or $y = e^x$) and substitute to obtain a quadratic in $y$
  3. Solve the quadratic by factorisation or the quadratic formula
  4. Discard any solution where $y \leq 0$ (since $a^x > 0$)
  5. Convert back: $a^x = y \Rightarrow x = \log_a y$ (or $x = \ln y$ for base $e$)
  6. Give exact answers unless otherwise instructed
+ Pros: Systematic and reliable for all hidden-quadratic exponential equations
– Cons: Requires careful index manipulation before substitution; easy to make algebraic slips
B: Rearrange to common base and equate indices
When: When both sides can be expressed as powers of the same base without needing a quadratic (simpler cases)
  1. Express both sides as powers of the same base
  2. Use $a^m = a^n \Rightarrow m = n$ to equate exponents
  3. Solve the resulting linear equation for $x$
+ Pros: Very fast for simpler equations with matching bases
– Cons: Only works when the equation reduces to a single exponential on each side, not for quadratic-type
Formulas
$a^{m+n} = a^m \cdot a^n$ (MEMORIZE)
$a^{mn} = (a^m)^n$ (MEMORIZE)
$a^x = b \iff x = \log_a b$ (MEMORIZE)
$e^x = b \iff x = \ln b$ (MEMORIZE)
Quadratic formula: $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Writing $2^{2x+1} = (2^{2x}) + (2^1)$ instead of $2 \cdot (2^x)^2$
  • Writing $(2^2)^{x-6} = 2^{2x-6}$ instead of $2^{2(x-6)} = 2^{2x-12}$
  • Forgetting to reject negative solutions for $y = a^x$
  • Only presenting solutions for $y$ and forgetting to convert back to $x$
  • Premature rounding — give exact logarithmic answers

Assumptions
  • The model is valid for $t \geq 0$ (time is non-negative)
  • Parameters such as $\lambda$ or $k$ are positive for decay models
  • The asymptote represents the long-run steady state (e.g. room temperature)
Approaches
A: Substitute known values, then use logarithms to solve
When: Standard approach for modelling questions
  1. Use initial conditions ($t = 0$) to find one unknown parameter
  2. Substitute a second data point to form an equation in the remaining unknown
  3. Isolate the exponential term
  4. Take natural logarithms of both sides to solve for the parameter in the exponent
  5. For 'find the time when…': substitute the target value and solve using logarithms
  6. For 'sketch': identify asymptote, intercept, and shape (growth/decay)
+ Pros: Directly follows from the model; each step is clear
– Cons: Premature rounding in intermediate steps causes significant errors in later parts
Formulas
$\ln(e^{kx}) = kx$ (MEMORIZE)
$e^{\ln a} = a$ (MEMORIZE)
For $T = Ae^{-\lambda t} + c$: as $t \to \infty$, $T \to c$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Rounding $\lambda$ or $k$ prematurely — keep at least 4 significant figures until the final answer
  • Forgetting to subtract the constant $c$ before taking logarithms (e.g. $\ln(T-c)$ not $\ln T$)
  • Not labelling the graph sketch with the initial value and asymptote
  • Misinterpreting 'rate of decrease at time $t$' — this requires differentiation, not just substitution

Solve exponential equations using logarithms (non-quadratic)
#3
2×
5-7m

An equation like $a^x = b$ or $a^{f(x)} = b^{g(x)}$ where taking logs of both sides and using the power rule is the main technique. Not reducible to a quadratic. May ask for answers in a specific form like $\frac{\ln a}{\ln b}$.

Triggerssolvegiving your answer in the form3 significant figuresexactlnlog
Assumptions
  • Both sides must be positive before taking logarithms
  • Any base of logarithm can be used, but $\ln$ or $\log_{10}$ are standard
  • Exact answers should be left in logarithmic form unless stated otherwise
Approaches
A: Take logarithms of both sides
When: When bases cannot be made equal and the equation is not quadratic
  1. Take $\ln$ (or $\log$) of both sides
  2. Apply power rule: $\ln(a^x) = x \ln a$
  3. Expand and collect all terms involving $x$ on one side
  4. Factor out $x$ and solve
  5. Simplify or give to required accuracy
+ Pros: Universal method; works for any exponential equation
– Cons: Can produce messy expressions; students must be comfortable with algebraic manipulation of logs
B: Convert to same base if possible
When: When both sides are powers of the same base (e.g. $8^x = 32$ → base 2)
  1. Express both sides as powers of the same base
  2. Equate exponents
  3. Solve the resulting equation
+ Pros: Avoids logarithms entirely; fast and clean
– Cons: Only works when a common base exists
Formulas
$\ln(a^n) = n\ln a$ (MEMORIZE)
$\log_a b = \frac{\ln b}{\ln a}$ — change of base (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Taking $\log$ of individual terms in a sum: $\log(A+B) \neq \log A + \log B$
  • Errors in collecting $x$-terms when both sides have $x$ in the exponent
  • Giving a decimal when exact form is required

Solve logarithmic equation (single variable, leading to polynomial)
#4
3×
5-10m

An equation involving multiple $\log$ terms (often $\log_a(\text{expression})$) that must be combined using log laws, then converted to index form. The resulting equation is often quadratic or cubic in $x$. May include a constant that must first be written as a logarithm.

Assumptions
  • The argument of every logarithm must be positive — always check domain at the end
  • All logarithms in the equation share the same base (or must be converted to the same base)
  • A constant $k$ can be written as $k \cdot \log_a a = \log_a(a^k)$
Approaches
A: Combine logs then convert to index form
When: Standard approach for most log equations
  1. Use power rule: $k\log_a B = \log_a(B^k)$
  2. Use product rule: $\log_a A + \log_a B = \log_a(AB)$
  3. Use quotient rule: $\log_a A - \log_a B = \log_a(A/B)$
  4. Convert any standalone constant $c$ to $\log_a(a^c)$
  5. Combine to get $\log_a(\text{expression}_1) = \log_a(\text{expression}_2)$
  6. Equate arguments: $\text{expression}_1 = \text{expression}_2$
  7. Solve the resulting polynomial (quadratic or cubic)
  8. Check all solutions satisfy domain: arguments of all original logs must be positive
  9. Reject any extraneous solutions
+ Pros: Works for all standard log equations; systematic
– Cons: Domain checking step is essential and easily forgotten
B: Convert to exponential form directly
When: When there is a single log term equal to a constant, e.g. $\log_a(f(x)) = k$
  1. Rewrite as $f(x) = a^k$
  2. Solve for $x$
  3. Check domain
+ Pros: Very quick for simple single-log equations
– Cons: Not applicable when multiple log terms need combining first
Formulas
$\log_a(xy) = \log_a x + \log_a y$ (MEMORIZE)
$\log_a(x/y) = \log_a x - \log_a y$ (MEMORIZE)
$\log_a(x^k) = k\log_a x$ (MEMORIZE)
$\log_a a = 1$ (MEMORIZE)
$\log_a 1 = 0$ (MEMORIZE)
$\log_a N = P \iff N = a^P$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Failing to check the domain: including solutions where an argument of a log is negative or zero
  • Confusing $\log(A+B)$ with $\log A + \log B$ — the product rule applies to multiplication, not addition
  • Confusing $\log A / \log B$ with $\log(A/B)$
  • Forgetting to convert the constant to log form before combining
  • Incorrectly expanding cubic expressions like $(2x-1)^3$

Assumptions
  • For $y = a^x$ with $a > 1$: graph passes through $(0,1)$, is always positive, and has asymptote $y = 0$
  • Transformations follow standard rules: $f(x-a)$ shifts right by $a$, $f(x) + b$ shifts up by $b$
  • The asymptote transforms with vertical translations but NOT horizontal translations
Approaches
A: Plot key features systematically
When: For any exponential graph sketch question
  1. Identify the base function $y = a^x$ and any transformations applied
  2. Find the $y$-intercept by setting $x = 0$
  3. Determine the horizontal asymptote (transform $y = 0$ accordingly)
  4. Determine the general shape: increasing if $a > 1$, decreasing if $0 < a < 1$
  5. Mark at least one additional point if requested
  6. Draw a smooth curve approaching the asymptote, labelling all features
+ Pros: Ensures all mark-scoring features are included
– Cons: Students must remember to label everything; rushing leads to missing marks
Formulas
$y = a^x$ passes through $(0,1)$ with asymptote $y = 0$ (MEMORIZE)
$y = a^{x-b} + c$ has asymptote $y = c$ and passes through $(b, 1+c)$ (MEMORIZE)
$y = A \cdot a^{-x} + c$ is a reflection and vertical stretch with asymptote $y = c$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Drawing the curve crossing the asymptote or touching the $x$-axis when it shouldn't
  • Confusing $f(x-a)$ (shift right) with $f(x+a)$ (shift left)
  • Forgetting to label the asymptote, intercept, or axes
  • Not showing the correct end behaviour (curve flattening towards asymptote)

Triggerssimplifyshow thatsumserieslogarithmΣarithmetic
Assumptions
  • $\log_a a = 1$ always
  • When summing logs, the product rule converts the sum to a single log of the product of arguments
  • The resulting product or expression inside the log may telescope or simplify
Approaches
A: Convert sum of logs to log of product, then simplify
When: When the question involves $\sum \log_a(\cdot)$
  1. Write out the first few terms to spot the pattern
  2. Apply $\sum \log_a(f(r)) = \log_a\left(\prod f(r)\right)$
  3. Simplify the product (look for telescoping or known products)
  4. Use power rule to simplify further if needed
  5. Express the final answer in the required form
+ Pros: Elegant and efficient once the product is identified
– Cons: Requires careful algebraic manipulation of the product
B: Use log laws term-by-term to identify arithmetic structure
When: When each log term simplifies to a linear expression in $r$, forming an AP
  1. Simplify each $\log_a f(r)$ using quotient/power rules
  2. Recognise the resulting expression as an arithmetic series
  3. Apply $S_n = \frac{n}{2}(a + l)$ or $S_n = \frac{n}{2}(2a + (n-1)d)$
  4. Simplify to get the final answer
+ Pros: Good when the log simplification yields a clean arithmetic sequence
– Cons: Only works when the structure is arithmetic after simplification
Formulas
$\log_a(x/y) = \log_a x - \log_a y$ (MEMORIZE)
$\log_a(x^k) = k\log_a x$ (MEMORIZE)
$\log_a a = 1$ (MEMORIZE)
$S_n = \frac{n}{2}(2a + (n-1)d)$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Confusing $\frac{\log A}{\log B}$ with $\log(A/B)$
  • Forgetting that $\log_a a = 1$, not $0$
  • Errors in summing the arithmetic series (wrong $n$, $a$, or $d$)

Triggerssimultaneouslogsolvetwo equationsx and y
Assumptions
  • Both $x$ and $y$ must be positive (domain of logarithm)
  • Logs are to the same base in each equation
Approaches
A: Combine logs, convert to index form, then substitute
When: Standard approach for simultaneous log equations
  1. Use log product rule: $\log x + \log y = \log(xy)$
  2. Convert to index form: $\log_a(xy) = k \Rightarrow xy = a^k$
  3. From the second equation, express one variable in terms of the other
  4. Substitute into $xy = a^k$ to form a quadratic
  5. Solve the quadratic using factorisation or the formula
  6. Find corresponding values of the other variable
  7. Check both values are positive (valid for logs)
+ Pros: Clean and systematic
– Cons: Quadratic formula may give messy answers; must check domain
Formulas
$\log_a x + \log_a y = \log_a(xy)$ (MEMORIZE)
$\log_a N = P \iff N = a^P$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Writing $\log a + \log b = \log(a+b)$ instead of $\log(ab)$
  • Algebraic errors in the substitution step
  • Forgetting that both variables must be positive

Jun 2022 Q4QPMS

Must memorize

  • $\log_a(xy) = \log_a x + \log_a y$
  • $\log_a(x/y) = \log_a x - \log_a y$
  • $\log_a(x^k) = k \log_a x$
  • $\log_a a = 1$ and $\log_a 1 = 0$
  • $\log_a b = \frac{\log_c b}{\log_c a}$ (change of base — in formula booklet but must know how to use)
  • $a^{m+n} = a^m \cdot a^n$, $a^{m-n} = a^m / a^n$, $(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$
  • $e^{\ln x} = x$ and $\ln(e^x) = x$
  • $a^x = b \iff x = \log_a b$
  • $y = a^x$ has $y$-intercept $(0,1)$ and asymptote $y = 0$ for $a > 0, a \neq 1$

Global strategy

  • Master the three log laws (product, quotient, power) and be able to apply them in BOTH directions — combining and expanding
  • For any exponential equation, first check: can I make bases equal? If not, can I spot a hidden quadratic via substitution? If not, take logs of both sides
  • Always write $a^{m+n} = a^m \cdot a^n$, never $a^m + a^n$ — index law errors are the #1 source of lost marks
  • After solving any log equation, CHECK DOMAIN: every argument of every original log must be positive with your solution
  • After quadratic substitution in exponential equations, remember $a^x > 0$ always — reject non-positive roots for $y$
  • In modelling questions, keep parameters to at least 4 s.f. through intermediate steps; only round at the very end
  • For graph sketches, always label: y-intercept (with coordinates), horizontal asymptote (with equation), general shape and direction
  • Read the required answer form carefully: 'exact value', 'in terms of ln 2', '3 significant figures' — present accordingly

Exam tips

  1. When you see $a^{2x}$, immediately think $(a^x)^2$ — this is almost always a quadratic substitution signal
  2. Write your substitution (e.g. 'Let $y = 2^x$') explicitly on paper — examiners award method marks for this
  3. After finding solutions, always write a brief domain check: 'Since $\log$ requires positive argument, $x = -3$ is rejected'
  4. In sketch questions, a quick table of 2-3 $x$-values confirms the shape — examiners look for the correct curvature
  5. If the question says 'show that' or 'hence', you MUST use the given result or method — don't start from scratch
  6. Present logarithmic answers in the exact form the question specifies — e.g. $\frac{\ln 5}{\ln 2}$ not $\log_2 5$ unless asked
  7. Use brackets carefully: $\ln(3x+1)$ not $\ln 3x + 1$ which could be misread as $(\ln 3x) + 1$
  8. In multi-part questions, later parts often use earlier results — if stuck, use the 'show that' result from part (a) even if you couldn't prove it
