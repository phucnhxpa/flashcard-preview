Worked solution

Part (a): Finding the coordinates of point B

Thought process: The points where two curves intersect are found by setting their equations equal to each other, as their $y$-values must be the same at these points. This will give us an equation in terms of $x$. Since we're dealing with a cubic and a quadratic, the resulting equation will be a cubic. We are given one intersection point, A $(1,4)$, which means $x=1$ is a root of this cubic equation. This allows us to factorize the cubic and find the other roots, which correspond to the $x$-coordinates of other intersection points.

Working:
Set $C_1 = C_2$ to find the intersection points:
$$x^3 - 6x + 9 = -2x^2 + 7x - 1$$
Rearrange the equation to form a cubic polynomial equal to zero:
$$x^3 + 2x^2 - 13x + 10 = 0$$
We are given that point A is $(1,4)$, so $x=1$ is a root of this equation. This means $(x-1)$ is a factor of the cubic. We can use polynomial division or inspection to find the other factors.

By inspection (or synthetic division):
$$(x-1)(x^2 + 3x - 10) = 0$$
Now, factorize the quadratic term:
$$(x-1)(x+5)(x-2) = 0$$
The possible $x$-coordinates for the intersection points are $x=1$, $x=-5$, and $x=2$.

The problem states $x \ge 0$, so we disregard $x=-5$. The given point A has $x=1$. Therefore, point B must have $x=2$.

To find the $y$-coordinate of B, substitute $x=2$ into either $C_1$ or $C_2$. Using $C_1$:
$$y = (2)^3 - 6(2) + 9$$
$$y = 8 - 12 + 9$$
$$y = 5$$
(Check with $C_2$: $y = -2(2)^2 + 7(2) - 1 = -2(4) + 14 - 1 = -8 + 14 - 1 = 5$. Both give the same $y$-value, confirming our $x$-value is correct.)

Answer: The coordinates of point B are $(2, 5)$.

Part (b): Finding the exact area of R

Thought process: The area of the finite region R bounded by two curves is found by integrating the difference between the upper curve and the lower curve with respect to $x$, between the $x$-coordinates of their intersection points. From Figure 1, curve $C_2$ is above curve $C_1$ in the region R, which spans from $x=1$ (point A) to $x=2$ (point B). So we will integrate $C_2 - C_1$ from $x=1$ to $x=2$.

Working:
First, define the integrand, which is the difference between the upper curve ($C_2$) and the lower curve ($C_1$):
$$C_2 - C_1 = (-2x^2 + 7x - 1) - (x^3 - 6x + 9)$$
$$C_2 - C_1 = -x^3 - 2x^2 + 7x + 6x - 1 - 9$$
$$C_2 - C_1 = -x^3 - 2x^2 + 13x - 10$$
The area of region R is given by the definite integral:
$$Area = \int_{1}^{2} (-x^3 - 2x^2 + 13x - 10) dx$$
Now, perform the integration term by term:
$$Area = \left[ -\frac{x^4}{4} - \frac{2x^3}{3} + \frac{13x^2}{2} - 10x \right]_{1}^{2}$$
Evaluate the integral at the upper limit ($x=2$) and subtract its value at the lower limit ($x=1$):

At $x=2$:
$$- \frac{(2)^4}{4} - \frac{2(2)^3}{3} + \frac{13(2)^2}{2} - 10(2)$$
$$= - \frac{16}{4} - \frac{2(8)}{3} + \frac{13(4)}{2} - 20$$
$$= -4 - \frac{16}{3} + 26 - 20$$
$$= (26 - 4 - 20) - \frac{16}{3} = 2 - \frac{16}{3} = \frac{6}{3} - \frac{16}{3} = -\frac{10}{3}$$

At $x=1$:
$$- \frac{(1)^4}{4} - \frac{2(1)^3}{3} + \frac{13(1)^2}{2} - 10(1)$$
$$= - \frac{1}{4} - \frac{2}{3} + \frac{13}{2} - 10$$
To combine these, find a common denominator, which is 12:
$$= - \frac{3}{12} - \frac{8}{12} + \frac{78}{12} - \frac{120}{12}$$
$$= \frac{-3 - 8 + 78 - 120}{12} = \frac{-131 + 78}{12} = -\frac{53}{12}$$

Now, subtract the lower limit value from the upper limit value:
$$Area = \left( -\frac{10}{3} \right) - \left( -\frac{53}{12} \right)$$
$$Area = -\frac{10}{3} + \frac{53}{12}$$
Convert $-10/3$ to twelfths:
$$Area = -\frac{40}{12} + \frac{53}{12}$$
$$Area = \frac{13}{12}$$

Answer: The exact area of R is $\frac{13}{12}$.

Key techniques

  • equating functions to find intersection points
  • factorizing cubic equations using a known root
  • integrating polynomials
  • evaluating definite integrals

Common pitfalls

  • Algebraic errors when solving the cubic equation or factorizing it.
  • Incorrectly identifying which curve is 'upper' and which is 'lower' when setting up the integral, leading to a negative area (and needing to take the absolute value).
  • Arithmetic mistakes when evaluating the definite integral, especially with fractions.
