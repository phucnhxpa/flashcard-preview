Worked solution

Part (a): Sketching Speed-Time Graphs

Thought Process: We need to visualise the motion of both vehicles on the same speed-time graph. The car travels at a constant speed, so its graph will be a horizontal line. The motorcyclist starts from rest and accelerates in two distinct phases, so their graph will consist of two straight line segments with different positive gradients, starting from the origin.

Working:

  1. Car's Motion: The car travels at a constant speed of $40 \text{ m s}^{-1}$. On a speed-time graph, this is represented by a horizontal line at $v=40$.
  2. Motorcyclist's Motion:
  • Starts from rest at $t=0$, so the graph begins at $(0,0)$.
  • Accelerates at $4 \text{ m s}^{-2}$ for $15 \text{ s}$. This is a straight line segment with a gradient of $4$.
  • Speed at $t=15$: $v = u + at = 0 + 4(15) = 60 \text{ m s}^{-1}$. So, this segment connects $(0,0)$ to $(15,60)$.
  • Then accelerates at $1 \text{ m s}^{-2}$ for a further $T$ seconds until catching up. This is another straight line segment with a gradient of $1$, starting from $(15,60)$.
  • The total time when the motorcyclist catches up is $15+T$.
  • Both graphs should end at the same total time, $t=15+T$, and the area under both graphs (representing distance) must be equal.

Sketch Description:

  • A horizontal line for the car at $v=40$.
  • A line for the motorcyclist starting from $(0,0)$, increasing with a steep positive gradient to $(15,60)$.
  • From $(15,60)$, the motorcyclist's graph continues with a shallower positive gradient until it reaches a point $(15+T, V_{final})$, where $V_{final}$ is the motorcyclist's final speed.
  • The car's horizontal line and the motorcyclist's second segment will intersect at some point $t_1$ (where their speeds are equal).
  • Label the $y$-axis 'Speed (m s$^{-1}$)' and $x$-axis 'Time (s)'. Mark $40$ and $60$ on the $y$-axis, and $15$ and $15+T$ on the $x$-axis.

Part (b): Finding $t_1$

Thought Process: The time $t_1$ is when the car and motorcyclist are moving at the same speed. We need to find the expressions for their speeds and set them equal. The car has a constant speed. The motorcyclist's speed changes, so we need to determine which phase of the motorcyclist's motion $t_1$ falls into. Given the motorcyclist accelerates for $15 \text{ s}$ to $60 \text{ m s}^{-1}$, and the car's speed is $40 \text{ m s}^{-1}$, $t_1$ must occur during the first acceleration phase ($t < 15 \text{ s}$).

Working:

  • Car's speed, $V_{car} = 40 \text{ m s}^{-1}$ (constant).
  • Motorcyclist's speed during the first phase ($0 \le t \le 15 \text{ s}$), using $v = u + at$:
$V_{moto} = 0 + 4t = 4t$.
  • At $t=t_1$, their speeds are equal:
$4t_1 = 40$ $t_1 = \frac{40}{4}$ $t_1 = 10 \text{ s}$
Answer: $t_1 = 10 \text{ s}$

Part (c): Showing $T^2 + kT - 300 = 0$

Thought Process: When the motorcyclist catches up with the car, they have both travelled the same total distance from $t=0$. We need to calculate the total distance travelled by each vehicle up to the time $t = 15+T$. The distance travelled is the area under the speed-time graph.

Working:

1. Distance travelled by the car:
The car travels at a constant speed of $40 \text{ m s}^{-1}$ for a total time of $(15+T)$ seconds.
Distance $S_{car} = \text{speed} \times \text{time}$
$S_{car} = 40(15+T)$
$S_{car} = 600 + 40T \quad (1)$

2. Distance travelled by the motorcyclist:
We divide the motorcyclist's motion into two phases.

  • Phase 1 ($0 \le t \le 15 \text{ s}$):
Initial speed $u_1 = 0 \text{ m s}^{-1}$. Acceleration $a_1 = 4 \text{ m s}^{-2}$. Time $t_1 = 15 \text{ s}$. Speed at $t=15 \text{ s}$: $v_1 = u_1 + a_1t_1 = 0 + 4(15) = 60 \text{ m s}^{-1}$. Distance $S_1 = u_1t_1 + \frac{1}{2}a_1t_1^2 = 0(15) + \frac{1}{2}(4)(15^2) = 2(225) = 450 \text{ m}$. (Alternatively, area of triangle = $\frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} = \frac{1}{2} \times 15 \times 60 = 450 \text{ m}$.)
  • Phase 2 ($15 < t \le 15+T \text{ s}$):
Initial speed $u_2 = 60 \text{ m s}^{-1}$ (this is $v_1$ from the end of Phase 1). Acceleration $a_2 = 1 \text{ m s}^{-2}$. Duration of this phase is $T \text{ s}$. Distance $S_2 = u_2T + \frac{1}{2}a_2T^2 = 60T + \frac{1}{2}(1)T^2 = 60T + \frac{1}{2}T^2$. (Alternatively, speed at $t=15+T$ is $v_2 = 60+T$. Area of trapezium = $\frac{1}{2}(u_2 + v_2)T = \frac{1}{2}(60 + (60+T))T = \frac{1}{2}(120+T)T = 60T + \frac{1}{2}T^2$.)

Total distance travelled by the motorcyclist $S_{moto} = S_1 + S_2$:
$S_{moto} = 450 + 60T + \frac{1}{2}T^2 \quad (2)$

3. Equating distances:
Since the motorcyclist catches up with the car, their total distances travelled are equal.
$S_{car} = S_{moto}$
$600 + 40T = 450 + 60T + \frac{1}{2}T^2$

4. Rearranging into the required form:
Multiply the entire equation by 2 to eliminate the fraction:
$2(600 + 40T) = 2(450 + 60T + \frac{1}{2}T^2)$
$1200 + 80T = 900 + 120T + T^2$
Move all terms to one side to get a quadratic equation in $T$:
$0 = T^2 + 120T - 80T + 900 - 1200$
$0 = T^2 + 40T - 300$
So, $T^2 + 40T - 300 = 0$.

Comparing this with $T^2 + kT - 300 = 0$, we find that $k=40$.

Answer: $T^2 + 40T - 300 = 0$, where $k=40$.

Final Answer:
Answer:
(a) See walkthrough description for sketch details.
(b) $t_1 = 10 \text{ s}$
(c) $T^2 + 40T - 300 = 0$, where $k=40$.

Key techniques

  • Sketching speed-time graphs for different phases of motion
  • Calculating distance as area under speed-time graph
  • Applying constant acceleration formulae ($v=u+at$)
  • Formulating and solving equations based on problem conditions

Common pitfalls

  • Misinterpreting the variable 'T' – it's the *duration* of the second phase, not the total time.
  • Errors in calculating the area under the speed-time graph, especially for the trapezium (second phase of motorcyclist's motion).
  • Failing to use the correct initial speed for the second phase of the motorcyclist's motion (i.e., using $u=0$ instead of $u=60 \text{ m s}^{-1}$).
  • Algebraic mistakes when rearranging the equation to the required form.
