5.	 A small ball is projected vertically upwards with speed 29.4 m s–1 from a point A which is 
19.6 m above horizontal ground.
	
The ball is modelled as a particle moving freely under gravity until it hits the ground. It is 
assumed that the ball does not rebound.
	
(a)	 Find the distance travelled by the ball while its speed is less than 14.7 m s–1
(3)

(3)
	
(c)	 Sketch a speed-time graph for the motion of the ball from the instant when it is 
projected from A to the instant when it hits the ground. Show clearly where your graph

Worked solution

Part (a): Find the distance travelled by the ball while its speed is less than $14.7 \text{ m s}^{-1}$

Thought Process:
We recognize this as a `suvat` problem where the acceleration is constant ($g = 9.8 \text{ m s}^{-2}$). The ball is projected upwards, its speed decreases to zero at the maximum height, and then increases as it falls. The condition "speed is less than $14.7 \text{ m s}^{-1}$" means we are interested in the motion around the peak of the trajectory, where its upward speed has dropped below $14.7 \text{ m s}^{-1}$ or its downward speed is still less than $14.7 \text{ m s}^{-1}$. Due to symmetry, the path segments where speed is less than $14.7 \text{ m s}^{-1}$ will be symmetric around the maximum height.

Let's set the origin at point A and the upward direction as positive. So, initial velocity $u = 29.4 \text{ m s}^{-1}$ and acceleration $a = -9.8 \text{ m s}^{-2}$.

  1. First, we find the maximum height ($s_{max}$) reached by the ball above A. At maximum height, the final velocity $v = 0$.
  2. Next, we find the height ($s_{14.7}$) above A where the ball's speed is $14.7 \text{ m s}^{-1}$ during its upward journey. Its velocity at this point is $v = 14.7 \text{ m s}^{-1}$.
  3. The distance travelled *upwards* while the speed is less than $14.7 \text{ m s}^{-1}$ is the difference between these two heights: $s_{max} - s_{14.7}$.
  4. The ball then falls back down. From the peak, it reaches a speed of $14.7 \text{ m s}^{-1}$ (downwards) at a height $s_{14.7}$ above A. The distance travelled *downwards* from the peak, where speed is less than $14.7 \text{ m s}^{-1}$, is also $s_{max} - s_{14.7}$.
  5. The total distance travelled where the speed is less than $14.7 \text{ m s}^{-1}$ is the sum of these two distances.

Working:
Let upward be positive, origin at A.
$u = 29.4 \text{ m s}^{-1}$, $a = -9.8 \text{ m s}^{-2}$.

  1. Find the maximum height ($s_{max}$): At maximum height, $v=0$.
Using $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$: $$0^2 = (29.4)^2 + 2(-9.8)s_{max}$$ $$0 = 864.36 - 19.6s_{max}$$ $$19.6s_{max} = 864.36$$ $$s_{max} = \frac{864.36}{19.6} = 44.1 \text{ m}$$
  1. Find the height ($s_{14.7}$) when speed is $14.7 \text{ m s}^{-1}$ (upwards): $v=14.7 \text{ m s}^{-1}$.
Using $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$: $$(14.7)^2 = (29.4)^2 + 2(-9.8)s_{14.7}$$ $$216.09 = 864.36 - 19.6s_{14.7}$$ $$19.6s_{14.7} = 864.36 - 216.09$$ $$19.6s_{14.7} = 648.27$$ $$s_{14.7} = \frac{648.27}{19.6} = 33.075 \text{ m}$$
  1. Calculate the total distance:
Distance for which speed is less than $14.7 \text{ m s}^{-1}$ is $2 \times (s_{max} - s_{14.7})$. $$ = 2 \times (44.1 - 33.075)$$ $$ = 2 \times 11.025$$ $$ = 22.05 \text{ m}$$
Answer: $22.05 \text{ m}$

Part (b): Find the time for which the ball is moving with a speed of more than $29.4 \text{ m s}^{-1}$

Thought Process:
The ball is projected with an initial speed of $29.4 \text{ m s}^{-1}$. Its speed decreases to $0$ at the peak, then increases as it falls. Its speed will only exceed $29.4 \text{ m s}^{-1}$ *after* it has passed its initial projection point A on its way down and continues to fall below A. At point A on the way down, its speed is exactly $29.4 \text{ m s}^{-1}$. So we need to find the time it takes for the ball to travel from point A (when moving downwards) to the ground.

Let's set the origin at point A and the positive direction downwards for this part of the motion.
Initial velocity (at point A, moving downwards) $u = 29.4 \text{ m s}^{-1}$.
Displacement to the ground $s = 19.6 \text{ m}$.
Acceleration $a = g = 9.8 \text{ m s}^{-2}$.
We need to find the time $t$.

Working:
Using $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$:
$$19.6 = (29.4)t + \frac{1}{2}(9.8)t^2$$
$$19.6 = 29.4t + 4.9t^2$$
Rearrange into a quadratic equation:
$$4.9t^2 + 29.4t - 19.6 = 0$$
Divide by $4.9$ to simplify:
$$t^2 + 6t - 4 = 0$$
Use the quadratic formula $t = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$:
$$t = \frac{-6 \pm \sqrt{6^2 - 4(1)(-4)}}{2(1)}$$
$$t = \frac{-6 \pm \sqrt{36 + 16}}{2}$$
$$t = \frac{-6 \pm \sqrt{52}}{2}$$
$$t = \frac{-6 \pm 2\sqrt{13}}{2}$$
$$t = -3 \pm \sqrt{13}$$
Since time $t$ must be positive:
$$t = -3 + \sqrt{13}$$
$$t \approx -3 + 3.60555$$
$$t \approx 0.60555 \text{ s}$$
Rounding to 3 significant figures, $t = 0.606 \text{ s}$.

Answer: $0.606 \text{ s}$

Part (c): Sketch a speed-time graph

Thought Process:
A speed-time graph plots the magnitude of velocity against time. Speed is always non-negative. For vertical motion under gravity, the velocity changes linearly with time ($v = u + at$). The acceleration is constant ($a = -9.8 \text{ m s}^{-2}$ if upward is positive). Therefore, the speed-time graph will consist of straight-line segments.

  1. Initial point: At $t=0$, the speed is $29.4 \text{ m s}^{-1}$. This is the point $(0, 29.4)$.
  2. Point of zero speed (maximum height): The ball's speed decreases until it reaches its maximum height, where its velocity (and thus speed) is $0$. This occurs when $v=u+at \implies 0 = 29.4 + (-9.8)t \implies t = \frac{29.4}{9.8} = 3 \text{ s}$. This is the point $(3, 0)$.
  3. Final point (hits the ground): After reaching maximum height, the ball falls, and its speed increases linearly. We need to find the total time of flight and the speed when it hits the ground.
From Part (b)'s working (or using $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$ with origin at A, upward positive, $s=-19.6$), the total time to hit the ground is $t = 3 + \sqrt{13} \approx 6.61 \text{ s}$. To find the velocity at this time, $v = u + at = 29.4 + (-9.8)(3 + \sqrt{13}) = 29.4 - 29.4 - 9.8\sqrt{13} = -9.8\sqrt{13} \approx -35.33 \text{ m s}^{-1}$. The speed is the magnitude of this velocity, so final speed $\approx 35.33 \text{ m s}^{-1}$. This is the point $(6.61, 35.33)$.

Sketch Description:
The graph is a V-shape. It starts at $(0, 29.4)$ on the speed axis. It decreases linearly to $(3, 0)$ on the time axis (the minimum speed, at the peak of trajectory). It then increases linearly from $(3, 0)$ to approximately $(6.61, 35.33)$ when it hits the ground. The gradient of the initial decreasing segment is $-9.8 \text{ m s}^{-2}$ (if we consider velocity), but for speed, it's just a segment with negative slope whose magnitude is $9.8$. The increasing segment has a slope of $9.8 \text{ m s}^{-2}$. The graph clearly shows $t=3$ on the time axis and speed $=29.4$ on the speed axis.

Answer:
A sketch of a speed-time graph:


  • The vertical axis is labelled 'Speed (m/s)' and the horizontal axis is labelled 'Time (s)'.

  • The graph starts at $(0, 29.4)$.

  • It forms a straight line segment from $(0, 29.4)$ to $(3, 0)$.

  • It then forms another straight line segment from $(3, 0)$ to approximately $(6.61, 35.3)$.

  • The graph has a clear 'V' shape, touching the time axis at $t=3$ s.

  • The point $(0, 29.4)$ and the point $(3, 0)$ are clearly marked or implied by the shape and labels.

  • The final speed (at $t \approx 6.61$ s) is greater than $29.4 \text{ m s}^{-1}$.

Key techniques

  • applying suvat equations with correct sign conventions
  • interpreting 'speed' correctly in kinematics problems
  • solving quadratic equations for time
  • sketching speed-time graphs for projectile motion

Common pitfalls

  • Confusing speed with velocity: Using negative values for speed, or drawing a velocity-time graph when a speed-time graph is requested.
  • Incorrect sign conventions for 'suvat': Failing to consistently define positive direction for displacement, velocity, and acceleration.
  • Misinterpreting time intervals: Forgetting to consider the specific conditions for 'speed less than X' or 'speed more than Y' (e.g., in part b, not realising the speed only exceeds 29.4 m/s *after* passing A on the way down).
