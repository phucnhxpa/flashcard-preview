Triggerssketch the speed-time graphaccelerates uniformlyconstant speeddeceleratestotal distancetotal time
Assumptions
  • Acceleration/deceleration is uniform within each stage.
  • Changes between stages of motion are instantaneous.
  • The object is treated as a particle (a point mass with no dimensions).
Approaches
A: Graphical Method (Area & Gradient)
When: Almost always, especially when finding total distance or when a sketch is explicitly requested.
  1. Sketch the speed-time graph based on the description. The shape is typically a trapezium, or a combination of triangles and rectangles.
  2. Label all known times, initial/final/max speeds on the axes. Use variables (e.g., T) for unknowns.
  3. Recall that Gradient = Acceleration ($a = \Delta v / \Delta t$). Use this to find unknown accelerations or times.
  4. Recall that Area under graph = Distance travelled. The area of a trapezium, $\frac{1}{2}(a+b)h$, is often the most efficient formula.
  5. Form an equation by equating the total area to the given total distance, then solve for the unknown variable.
+ Pros: Visually intuitive, often faster for total distance problems than multiple SUVAT equations.
– Cons: Requires a neat and accurate sketch; prone to arithmetic errors in area calculations.
B: Segmented SUVAT Equations
When: As a primary method if no graph is needed, or as a check for the graphical method. Good for finding specific velocities or times within a single stage.
  1. Break the journey into distinct stages (e.g., Stage 1: acceleration, Stage 2: constant speed).
  2. For each stage, list the known SUVAT variables (s, u, v, a, t).
  3. Crucially, the final velocity (`v`) of one stage becomes the initial velocity (`u`) of the next stage.
  4. Apply the appropriate SUVAT equation to find the required unknown in each stage.
  5. Sum the distances (`s`) or times (`t`) from all stages to find the total.
+ Pros: Systematic and precise, less reliance on a visual diagram.
– Cons: Can be slower and more laborious; easy to mix up variables between stages.
Formulas
Distance = Area under speed-time graph (MEMORIZE)
Acceleration = Gradient of speed-time graph (MEMORIZE)
Area of Trapezium = $\frac{1}{2}(a+b)h$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Confusing the total time of the journey with the duration of a specific segment (e.g., the constant speed part).
  • Incorrectly identifying the parallel sides for the area of a trapezium formula.
  • Sketching a distance-time graph (curves for acceleration) instead of a speed-time graph (straight lines for constant acceleration).

Triggersprojected vertically upwardsdropped from restfrom a height ofunder gravitygreatest heighttime of flight
Assumptions
  • Acceleration due to gravity is constant ($g = 9.8 \, \text{ms}^{-2}$).
  • Air resistance is negligible and can be ignored.
  • The object is treated as a particle.
Approaches
A: Single Application of SUVAT
When: For most standard problems involving one particle moving between two points in time.
  1. Define an origin and a positive direction (e.g., upwards from the ground is positive). THIS IS THE MOST IMPORTANT STEP.
  2. List the five SUVAT variables: s, u, v, a, t.
  3. Populate the list: `a` will be either $+9.8$ or $-9.8$ depending on your choice of positive direction. `s` is the displacement (final position - initial position), not distance. `u` is the initial velocity.
  4. Key facts: at the greatest height, velocity `v = 0`. If an object is 'dropped', `u = 0`.
  5. Choose the SUVAT equation that connects your three knowns with your one desired unknown.
  6. Solve. If you get a quadratic for `t`, interpret the two roots physically (e.g., time passing a point on the way up vs. on the way down).
+ Pros: Direct, efficient, and versatile for all vertical motion problems.
– Cons: Extremely prone to sign errors if the positive direction is not defined and applied consistently.
B: Splitting the Motion (Up and Down)
When: When finding total time of flight or final speed for a particle that goes up and returns down. It can feel more intuitive for some students.
  1. Phase 1 (Upwards): Analyse the motion to the highest point. Define up as positive. `u` is given, `v=0`, `a=-g`. Find time to the top ($t_{up}$) and max height ($s_{up}$).
  2. Phase 2 (Downwards): Analyse the motion from the highest point to the final position. Define down as positive. `u=0` (at the top), `a=+g`. Find the time to fall ($t_{down}$).
  3. Combine results: Total Time = $t_{up} + t_{down}$. Final speed is the value of `v` from Phase 2.
+ Pros: Can make sign conventions feel simpler as motion in each phase is one-directional.
– Cons: More steps and takes longer; less efficient for finding displacement directly over the whole journey.
Formulas
$v = u + at$ (formula booklet)
$s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$ (formula booklet)
$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ (formula booklet)
At greatest height, vertical velocity $v=0$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Inconsistent sign convention: e.g., taking initial velocity `u` as positive upwards but acceleration `a` as `+9.8`.
  • Confusing displacement `s` (a vector) with distance travelled (a scalar).
  • Incorrectly stating speed (a scalar) is negative. If you find `v = -15`, the velocity is -15 m/s, but the speed is 15 m/s.

Triggerstravels X m in T sthen travels Y m in the next T sdistance travelled in the nth secondtwo stages of motion
Assumptions
  • Acceleration is constant within each defined stage or throughout the motion.
  • The object is treated as a particle.
Approaches
A: Formulate and Solve SUVAT Equations
When: This is the standard and only reliable method for these problems.
  1. Identify the distinct stages of motion or time intervals described in the problem.
  2. For each stage/interval, write down a SUVAT equation using the information given. This will create equations with unknowns (usually `u` and `a`).
  3. For 'distance in the nth second': Calculate this as `displacement at n seconds` minus `displacement at (n-1) seconds`. Use $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$ for both calculations.
  4. For multi-stage problems: Remember that the final velocity `v` of stage 1 is the initial velocity `u` of stage 2.
  5. You will now have a system of two (usually linear) simultaneous equations. Solve them for the unknowns.
+ Pros: A systematic algebraic approach that works for any problem of this type.
– Cons: Requires careful and accurate algebraic manipulation to avoid simple errors.
Formulas
$s_{nth} = s(t=n) - s(t=n-1)$ (MEMORIZE CONCEPT)
The five standard SUVAT equations (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Misinterpreting 'in the 5th second' to mean 'in the first 5 seconds' (i.e., using t=5). It means the interval between t=4 and t=5.
  • Making algebraic errors when solving the simultaneous equations.
  • Forgetting to link stages: failing to set the `v` from stage 1 as the `u` for stage 2.

Triggersmassforcethrustresistancefrictionfind the distance/time
Assumptions
  • Resistance forces are constant unless otherwise stated.
  • The object is a particle.
  • Newton's Second Law ($F_{net}=ma$) applies.
Approaches
A: Two-Step (Dynamics then Kinematics)
When: This is the universal method for these problems.
  1. Step 1 (Dynamics): Draw a force diagram, showing all forces acting on the object.
  2. Step 2: Establish a positive direction of motion and apply Newton's Second Law in that direction: Resultant Force = mass × acceleration.
  3. Step 3: Write the equation: `Driving Forces - Resistive Forces = ma`.
  4. Step 4: Solve this equation to find the acceleration, `a`.
  5. Step 5 (Kinematics): Treat the problem as a standard kinematics question where `a` is now a known value.
  6. Step 6: List the SUVAT variables, select the appropriate equation, and solve for the required kinematic quantity (s, u, v, or t).
+ Pros: Clear separation of two key mechanics concepts (Forces and Kinematics), very structured.
– Cons: An error in the initial F=ma calculation will make all subsequent kinematic answers incorrect.
Formulas
$F_{net} = ma$ (Newton's Second Law) (MEMORIZE)
The five standard SUVAT equations (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Getting the signs wrong in the F=ma equation (e.g., adding resistance instead of subtracting it from the driving force).
  • Forgetting to include the component of weight ($mg \sin \theta$) if the motion is on an inclined plane.
  • Using `a=g` in a vertical motion problem when other forces (like engine thrust or air resistance) are present.

Triggersvelocity $v(t) = ...$acceleration $a(t) = ...$find an expression forinstantaneously at restinitial displacement
Assumptions
  • Standard rules of differentiation and integration apply.
  • Initial conditions (at t=0) are required to find the constant of integration.
Approaches
A: Differentiation and Integration
When: This is the only method for variable acceleration problems.
  1. To move from displacement to velocity to acceleration, you Differentiate with respect to time: $s \xrightarrow{d/dt} v \xrightarrow{d/dt} a$.
  2. To move from acceleration to velocity to displacement, you Integrate with respect to time: $a \xrightarrow{\int dt} v \xrightarrow{\int dt} s$.
  3. When integrating, you MUST include the constant of integration, `+ C`.
  4. Use the initial conditions given (e.g., "at t=0, the particle is at the origin" means s=0 when t=0) to find the value of `C`.
  5. To find when a particle is 'at rest', set $v(t) = 0$ and solve for `t`.
  6. For 'total distance' in an interval, check if `v` changes sign in that interval. If so, integrate over the separate parts and add the magnitudes of the displacements.
+ Pros: The only valid method for this question type.
– Cons: Very easy to forget the constant of integration `+ C`, or to mix up differentiation and integration.
Formulas
$v = \frac{ds}{dt}$ (MEMORIZE)
$a = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2s}{dt^2}$ (MEMORIZE)
$s = \int v \, dt$ (MEMORIZE)
$v = \int a \, dt$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting the constant of integration `+ C` after integrating.
  • Applying SUVAT equations to a problem where acceleration is clearly not constant.
  • Calculating displacement (`s(final) - s(initial)`) when asked for total distance travelled, and failing to account for a change in direction.

Must memorize

  • The 5 SUVAT Equations (Constant Acceleration Only):
  • $v = u + at$ (formula booklet)
  • $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$ (formula booklet)
  • $s = vt - \frac{1}{2}at^2$ (formula booklet)
  • $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ (formula booklet)
  • $s = \frac{1}{2}(u+v)t$ (formula booklet)
  • The Calculus Relationships (Variable Acceleration):
  • $a = \frac{dv}{dt}$ and $v = \frac{ds}{dt}$ (MEMORIZE)
  • $v = \int a \, dt$ and $s = \int v \, dt$ (MEMORIZE)
  • Speed-Time Graph Relationships:
  • Area under graph = Distance (MEMORIZE)
  • Gradient = Acceleration (MEMORIZE)

Global strategy

  • 1. Constant or Variable 'a'? First, decide if acceleration is constant (use SUVAT/Graphs) or a function of time (use Calculus). This is the most important decision.
  • 2. Draw a Diagram: Always sketch a simple diagram of the motion or a speed-time graph. It helps to visualize stages, directions, and key points.
  • 3. Define a Positive Direction: For any problem with vectors (s, u, v, a), state clearly at the start which direction is positive and stick to it for all variables.
  • 4. List Your Variables (SUVAT): For constant acceleration problems, write down s, u, v, a, t and fill in the known values from the question. This makes selecting the correct equation straightforward.
  • 5. Distinguish Scalars and Vectors: Be precise. Speed is a scalar (always >= 0), velocity is a vector. Distance is a scalar, displacement is a vector.
  • 6. Check for Key Phrases: 'From rest' ($u=0$), 'dropped' ($u=0$), 'returns to start' ($s=0$), 'greatest height' ($v=0$), 'instantaneously at rest' ($v=0$).
  • 7. Sanity Check Your Answer: Does the result make physical sense? A negative time is impossible. A very large speed for a car is unlikely. This can help you spot a mistake.

Exam tips

  1. If a question asks you to 'show' or 'prove' a result, you must show every step of your working clearly. Do not skip steps.
  2. When solving a quadratic equation for time `t`, always consider both roots. Reject any negative solutions as time cannot be negative, but a second positive solution may be physically valid.
  3. Use your calculator's simultaneous equation solver to check your answers, but you must show the algebraic method to get full marks.
  4. If you make a mistake in an early part of a question (e.g., calculating `a`), you can still earn 'error carried forward' (ECF) marks in later parts if you use your incorrect value correctly.
  5. Pay attention to units. If a speed is given in km/h, convert it to m/s ($1 \text{ km/h} = \frac{1000}{3600} \text{ m/s}$) before using it with `g=9.8`.
