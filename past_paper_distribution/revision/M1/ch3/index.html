Speed-Time Graph Kinematics
8×
10-14m

A description of a journey in multiple stages (e.g., accelerates from rest, travels at a constant speed, then decelerates to a stop). You are asked to sketch a speed-time graph, find accelerations, total distance, or total time.

Core strategy
  1. 1. Sketch the axes: speed (or velocity) on the y-axis, time on the x-axis. Label units.
  2. 2. Draw the journey as connected line segments: an upward slope for constant acceleration, a horizontal line for constant speed, and a downward slope for constant deceleration. Label key coordinates (time, speed) at the start and end of each segment.
  3. 3. Remember two key principles: Gradient = Acceleration, and Area under the graph = Distance travelled.
  4. 4. For acceleration/deceleration, calculate the gradient of the relevant slope ($\Delta v / \Delta t$).
  5. 5. For total distance, split the area under the graph into simpler shapes (triangles, rectangles, or one large trapezium) and sum their areas. The area of a trapezium is often the most efficient method.
  6. 6. Formulate equations based on the information given (e.g., `Total Area = Total Distance`) and solve for the unknown variable, which could be time ($T$) or maximum speed ($V$).
Must-know formulas
Gradient = $a = \frac{v-u}{t}$ (MEMORIZE)
Area of Trapezium = $\frac{1}{2}(a+b)h$ (MEMORIZE)
$s = \frac{u+v}{2}t$ (formula booklet)
Common pitfalls
  • Confusing the total time of the journey with the duration of just one segment (e.g., the constant speed phase).
  • Incorrectly calculating the area of the trapezium, especially when one side is a complex expression like '(T-30)'.
  • Making sign errors when calculating deceleration from a gradient (it should be negative).
  • Mistaking the final velocity of one stage for the final velocity of the entire journey.

Vertical Motion Under Gravity (SUVAT)
5×
6-12m

A particle (ball, stone) is projected vertically upwards or dropped from a height. Questions involve finding the maximum height, time to reach the ground, or speed at a certain point.

Core strategy
  1. 1. Establish a clear sign convention immediately. A common choice is 'upwards is positive'.
  2. 2. Write down the SUVAT variables: $s, u, v, a, t$.
  3. 3. Populate the variables based on the question and your sign convention. If 'up' is positive: $a = -9.8 \text{ ms}^{-2}$. Initial velocity $u$ is positive if projected up, negative if projected down. Displacement $s$ is the final position relative to the start (negative if it ends up below the start point).
  4. 4. At the maximum height, the instantaneous velocity $v$ is always 0.
  5. 5. Select the SUVAT equation that connects your known variables with the one you need to find.
  6. 6. Solve the equation. If you get a quadratic for time, the positive solution is usually the one required. A negative solution may represent a time before the motion started.
  7. 7. Distinguish between 'velocity' (can be negative) and 'speed' (always positive).
Must-know formulas
$v = u + at$ (formula booklet)
$s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$ (formula booklet)
$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ (formula booklet)
$s = \frac{u+v}{2}t$ (formula booklet)
Common pitfalls
  • Inconsistent sign convention: e.g., using $u=15$ (up) and $a=9.8$ instead of $a=-9.8$.
  • Confusing displacement with distance: if a ball is thrown up and caught at the same height, its displacement is 0, but the distance travelled is non-zero.
  • Incorrectly identifying displacement, e.g., if an object is thrown from a cliff 20m high and lands in the sea, the final displacement is $s=-20$.
  • Using $v=0$ at points other than the maximum height.

Multi-Stage & Complex SUVAT Problems
4×
7-10m

A particle undergoes several consecutive periods of constant acceleration. Or, you are given information about displacement in different time intervals (e.g., 'in the fifth second') and asked to find initial velocity $u$ and acceleration $a$.

Core strategy
  1. 1. Break the motion into distinct stages. Each stage has its own set of SUVAT variables.
  2. 2. For each stage, list what you know: $s, u, v, a, t$.
  3. 3. Crucially, the final velocity ($v$) of one stage is the initial velocity ($u$) of the next stage.
  4. 4. If dealing with 'displacement in the nth second', understand this means $s_{\text{total}}(n) - s_{\text{total}}(n-1)$.
  5. 5. Formulate separate SUVAT equations for each stage or piece of information provided.
  6. 6. This will often lead to a system of simultaneous equations. Solve them to find the required unknowns like $u$ and $a$.
Must-know formulas
The 5 SUVAT equations (see above) (formula booklet)
$s_{\text{nth}} = s(t=n) - s(t=n-1)$ (MEMORIZE CONCEPT)
Common pitfalls
  • Using a single SUVAT equation across multiple stages of motion with different accelerations.
  • Misinterpreting 'in the fifth second' to mean 'at t=5' or 'in the first 5 seconds'.
  • Sign errors with deceleration; remember to use a negative value for acceleration.
  • Algebraic errors when solving the simultaneous equations.

Kinematics with Dynamics (F=ma)
3×
8-14m

A problem that describes the forces acting on a particle (e.g., driving force, resistance) and also asks for kinematic quantities like distance, time, or velocity. You need to first calculate acceleration using Newton's Second Law.

Core strategy
  1. 1. Draw a clear force diagram, showing all forces acting on the particle in the direction of motion.
  2. 2. Apply Newton's Second Law: Resultant Force = mass × acceleration ($F_{net} = ma$).
  3. 3. The Resultant Force is 'Driving Forces - Resistive Forces'. Solve for acceleration, $a$.
  4. 4. Once acceleration is found, the problem becomes a standard kinematics problem. List your SUVAT variables.
  5. 5. Use the calculated value of $a$ and the other information in the question to select and solve the appropriate SUVAT equation.
  6. 6. Be aware that if the forces change (e.g., engine cuts out), the acceleration changes. This creates a multi-stage kinematics problem.
Must-know formulas
$F_{net} = ma$ (Newton's Second Law) (formula booklet)
The 5 SUVAT equations (see above) (formula booklet)
Common pitfalls
  • Using a value for acceleration from a previous part of the question after the forces have changed.
  • Forgetting to include all forces (e.g., resistance) when calculating the resultant force.
  • Sign errors in the force equation, e.g., adding resistance instead of subtracting it.
  • Using $a=g$ in horizontal motion or when other vertical forces are present besides weight.

Collision / Relative Motion (Kinematics)
1×
11m

Two particles are moving along the same straight line, starting at different points or times. The question asks when and where they collide or meet.

Core strategy
  1. 1. Define a single origin and a positive direction for both particles. Be consistent.
  2. 2. Establish a common timescale. Let $t=0$ be the moment the first particle starts moving. If a second particle starts `T` seconds later, its time of travel is `(t-T)`.
  3. 3. For each particle (A and B), write down a displacement equation in terms of $t$, usually using $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$. For example, $s_A(t)$ and $s_B(t)$.
  4. 4. 'Collision' or 'meeting' occurs when the particles are at the same position at the same time. Set their displacement equations equal to each other: $s_A(t) = s_B(t)$.
  5. 5. Solve the resulting equation (often quadratic) for $t$.
  6. 6. Substitute the valid time $t$ back into either displacement equation to find the collision position.
Must-know formulas
$s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$ (formula booklet)
Common pitfalls
  • Inconsistent time frames: using 't' for both particles when one starts later. The second particle's time must be expressed relative to the first (e.g., t-2).
  • Sign errors: failing to use a consistent positive direction for displacements, velocities, and accelerations for both particles.
  • Mixing up starting positions: Ensure the initial displacement ($s_0$) is correctly included in the equations for $s(t)$.

Variable Acceleration (Calculus)
1×
13m

Velocity or acceleration is given as a function of time, $t$ (e.g., $v = 3t^2 - 5t + 2$). Questions ask for acceleration, velocity or displacement at a certain time.

Core strategy
  1. 1. Remember the relationship: Displacement ($s$), Velocity ($v$), Acceleration ($a$). Differentiate to go down the list ($s \rightarrow v \rightarrow a$), integrate to go up ($a \rightarrow v \rightarrow s$).
  2. 2. To find acceleration from velocity, differentiate $v(t)$ with respect to $t$: $a = \frac{dv}{dt}$.
  3. 3. To find velocity from acceleration, integrate $a(t)$ with respect to $t$: $v = \int a(t) dt$. Don't forget the constant of integration, '+C'.
  4. 4. To find displacement from velocity, integrate $v(t)$ with respect to $t$: $s = \int v(t) dt$. Again, don't forget the constant of integration, '+C'.
  5. 5. Use the initial conditions given in the question (e.g., 'at t=0, the particle is at the origin' means $s=0$ when $t=0$) to find the value of 'C'.
  6. 6. To find total distance when velocity changes sign, find the time(s) when $v=0$. Calculate the displacement in each interval separately and add the absolute values (magnitudes) of the displacements.
Must-know formulas
$v = \frac{ds}{dt}$ (formula booklet)
$a = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2s}{dt^2}$ (formula booklet)
$v = \int a\ dt$ (MEMORIZE)
$s = \int v\ dt$ (MEMORIZE)
Common pitfalls
  • Forgetting the constant of integration '+C' after integrating.
  • Using incorrect initial conditions to find 'C'.
  • Confusing displacement with total distance travelled. If the particle turns back, displacement and distance are different.

Collision Dynamics (Momentum & Impulse)
1×
11m

Two particles collide directly. You are given masses, initial velocities, and the coefficient of restitution ($e$). You are asked to find final velocities or the impulse. NOTE: This is a Chapter 4 (Collisions) topic, but appeared on one paper listed.

Core strategy
  1. 1. Define a positive direction and draw 'before' and 'after' diagrams.
  2. 2. Apply the Principle of Conservation of Linear Momentum (CLM): $m_1u_1 + m_2u_2 = m_1v_1 + m_2v_2$. Be careful with signs for velocities in the negative direction.
  3. 3. Apply Newton's Law of Restitution (NLOR): $v_2 - v_1 = -e(u_2 - u_1)$. This is the 'separation speed = e * approach speed' law.
  4. 4. You now have two simultaneous equations with two unknown velocities ($v_1, v_2$). Solve them.
  5. 5. Calculate impulse on one particle using the Impulse-Momentum Theorem: Impulse = change in momentum = $m(v-u)$. Remember impulse is a vector.
Must-know formulas
Conservation of Momentum: $m_1u_1 + m_2u_2 = m_1v_1 + m_2v_2$ (MEMORIZE)
Newton's Law of Restitution: $v_2 - v_1 = -e(u_2 - u_1)$ (MEMORIZE)
Impulse = $m v - m u$ (MEMORIZE)
Common pitfalls
  • Sign errors in the CLM and NLOR equations, especially for particles moving towards each other.
  • Mixing up particles or velocities (e.g., using $v_1 - v_2$ in the restitution formula).
  • Calculating impulse as $m(u-v)$ instead of $m(v-u)$.

Oct 2024 Q7

Must memorize

  • The five SUVAT (Constant Acceleration) Equations. (formula booklet)
  • Gradient of a speed-time graph represents acceleration. (MEMORIZE)
  • Area under a speed-time graph represents distance/displacement. (MEMORIZE)
  • For vertical motion under gravity, acceleration is $g \approx 9.8 \text{ ms}^{-2}$ and acts downwards. (MEMORIZE CONSTANT)
  • Displacement in the 'nth' second is $s(n) - s(n-1)$. (MEMORIZE CONCEPT)
  • Calculus relationships: $a = dv/dt$, $v = ds/dt$; and $v = \int a\ dt$, $s = \int v\ dt$. (partially in formula booklet)

Global strategy

  • 1. Sign Convention is King: Before writing any equations, define a positive direction (e.g., 'Up is +ve' or 'Right is +ve') and apply it consistently to all vector quantities: displacement ($s$), initial velocity ($u$), final velocity ($v$), and acceleration ($a$). This is the single biggest source of error.
  • 2. Sketch a Diagram: Always draw a simple diagram. For vertical motion, show the path and key points. For multi-stage journeys, a speed-time graph is invaluable, even if not explicitly asked for. It organizes information and prevents mistakes.
  • 3. List SUVAT Variables: For any constant acceleration problem, write down 'S, U, V, A, T' and fill in the values you know from the question. This makes it easy to see which of the five equations you need to use.
  • 4. Break Down Multi-Stage Problems: Never apply a single SUVAT equation across a journey with different accelerations. Treat each stage (e.g., accelerates, then constant velocity) as a separate problem. The final velocity of one stage is the initial velocity of the next.
  • 5. Distinguish Speed from Velocity: Velocity has a direction and can be negative. Speed is the magnitude of velocity and is always non-negative. Pay close attention to which one the question asks for.
  • 6. Check Your Answer Makes Sense: Once you have a solution, do a quick sanity check. Time cannot be negative. The speed of a train is unlikely to be 3000 m/s. This can help you spot a calculation error.
  • 7. Identify the Acceleration Type: Is acceleration constant? If yes, use SUVAT. Is acceleration a function of time? If yes, use calculus (integration/differentiation). Are forces involved? If yes, use $F=ma$ first to find $a$.

Exam tips

  1. Kinematics questions are often near the start of the M1 paper. They are a good opportunity to secure marks and build confidence. Be methodical and don't rush.
  2. If a question involves a speed-time graph, think 'gradient for acceleration' and 'area for distance' before you do anything else.
  3. When solving a quadratic equation for time ($t$), if you get two positive solutions, re-read the question carefully to determine which one is physically relevant for the scenario described.
  4. Always check units are consistent before you begin calculations. Convert everything to SI units (metres, seconds, kilograms) if necessary.
  5. If you're stuck on a multi-part question, don't give up. The parts are often linked. Sometimes reading part (c) can give you a clue for part (b). If you can't do part (a), you may still be able to use a symbolic answer (e.g., 'a') to solve a later part and get method marks.
