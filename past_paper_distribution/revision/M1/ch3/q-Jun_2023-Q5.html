Worked solution

(a) Find an expression, in terms of $T$ and $V$, for the total distance travelled by the car.

*Thought process:* We recognize that the total distance travelled by an object is represented by the area under its speed-time graph. For the car, the graph in Figure 2 forms a trapezium. The formula for the area of a trapezium is $\frac{1}{2}(a+b)h$, where $a$ and $b$ are the lengths of the parallel sides and $h$ is the perpendicular height.

*Working:*
From Figure 2, the car's motion is represented by a trapezium.
The parallel sides correspond to the total time $T$ (the full base of the trapezium) and the duration of constant speed, which is $(T - 30)$ seconds (the top base of the trapezium).
The perpendicular height of the trapezium is the constant speed $V$.

$$ \text{Distance}_{\text{car}} = \frac{1}{2} (\text{sum of parallel sides}) \times \text{height} $$
$$ \text{Distance}_{\text{car}} = \frac{1}{2} (T + (T-30)) V $$
$$ \text{Distance}_{\text{car}} = \frac{1}{2} (2T - 30) V $$
$$ \text{Distance}_{\text{car}} = (T - 15) V $$

Answer: $(T-15)V$

(b) Sketch, on the axes in Figure 2, a speed-time graph for the motorbike.

*Thought process:* We need to draw a speed-time graph for the motorbike based on its described motion. It starts from rest, accelerates uniformly, moves at a constant speed, and then decelerates uniformly to rest. The maximum speed is given as $4/3 V$.

*Working:*


  1. Starts from rest: The graph begins at the origin, point $(0,0)$.

  2. Accelerates for 10s: The speed increases uniformly from $0$ to $4/3 V$ over 10 seconds. This is represented by a straight line segment from $(0,0)$ to $(10, 4/3 V)$.

  3. Constant speed for 50s: The motorbike maintains the speed $4/3 V$ for 50 seconds. This is a horizontal line segment from $(10, 4/3 V)$ to $(10+50, 4/3 V)$, which is $(60, 4/3 V)$.

  4. Decelerates to rest: The speed decreases uniformly from $4/3 V$ to $0$ over the remaining time $(T-60)$ seconds. This is a straight line segment from $(60, 4/3 V)$ to $(T, 0)$.

The sketch should include:


  • A line segment connecting $(0,0)$ and $(10, 4/3 V)$.

  • A line segment connecting $(10, 4/3 V)$ and $(60, 4/3 V)$.

  • A line segment connecting $(60, 4/3 V)$ and $(T, 0)$.

  • The horizontal axis should be labelled with time points $0, 10, 60, T$.

  • The vertical axis should be labelled with speed $4/3 V$ at the peak.

(c) Given that the maximum speed reached by the motorbike is 40 m/s, find the value of $V$.

*Thought process:* From part (b), we established that the maximum speed reached by the motorbike is $4/3 V$. The question provides a specific value for this maximum speed, allowing us to form a simple equation to find $V$.

*Working:*
From the description in part (b), the maximum speed reached by the motorbike is $4/3 V$.
We are given that this maximum speed is 40 m/s.
$$ \frac{4}{3} V = 40 $$
To solve for $V$, multiply both sides of the equation by $\frac{3}{4}$:
$$ V = 40 \times \frac{3}{4} $$
$$ V = 10 \times 3 $$
$$ V = 30 $$

Answer: $V = 30$ m/s

(d) Given that the total distance travelled by the car is the same as the total distance travelled by the motorbike, find the value of $T$.

*Thought process:* We need to equate the total distance travelled by the car (from part a, using $V=30$) and the total distance travelled by the motorbike (calculated as the area under its graph from part b, also using $V=30$). This will give us an equation in terms of $T$, which we can then solve.

*Working:*
1. Total distance travelled by the car:
Using the expression from part (a) and substituting the value $V=30$ from part (c):
$$ \text{Distance}_{\text{car}} = (T - 15) V $$
$$ \text{Distance}_{\text{car}} = (T - 15) (30) $$
$$ \text{Distance}_{\text{car}} = 30T - 450 $$

2. Total distance travelled by the motorbike:
The motorbike's speed-time graph (from part b) is a trapezium. The maximum speed it reaches is $4/3 V = 4/3 \times 30 = 40$ m/s.
We calculate the area under the motorbike's graph by dividing it into three sections:


  • Section 1 (Acceleration phase): A triangle from $t=0$ to $t=10$ with height 40 m/s.


$$ \text{Area}_1 = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} = \frac{1}{2} \times 10 \times 40 = 200 \text{ m} $$

  • Section 2 (Constant speed phase): A rectangle from $t=10$ to $t=60$ with height 40 m/s.


$$ \text{Area}_2 = \text{width} \times \text{height} = (60 - 10) \times 40 = 50 \times 40 = 2000 \text{ m} $$

  • Section 3 (Deceleration phase): A triangle from $t=60$ to $t=T$ with height 40 m/s.


$$ \text{Area}_3 = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} = \frac{1}{2} \times (T - 60) \times 40 = 20(T - 60) $$
$$ \text{Area}_3 = 20T - 1200 \text{ m} $$
Total distance for the motorbike is the sum of these areas:
$$ \text{Distance}_{\text{motorbike}} = \text{Area}_1 + \text{Area}_2 + \text{Area}_3 $$
$$ \text{Distance}_{\text{motorbike}} = 200 + 2000 + (20T - 1200) $$
$$ \text{Distance}_{\text{motorbike}} = 2200 + 20T - 1200 $$
$$ \text{Distance}_{\text{motorbike}} = 1000 + 20T $$

3. Equate the distances and solve for $T$:
We are given that the total distance travelled by the car is the same as the total distance travelled by the motorbike.
$$ \text{Distance}_{\text{car}} = \text{Distance}_{\text{motorbike}} $$
$$ 30T - 450 = 1000 + 20T $$
Subtract $20T$ from both sides:
$$ 10T - 450 = 1000 $$
Add $450$ to both sides:
$$ 10T = 1450 $$
Divide by $10$:
$$ T = 145 $$

Answer: $T = 145$ s

Key techniques

  • interpret speed-time graphs
  • calculate area of trapezium/triangle
  • formulate distance equations
  • solve linear equations

Common pitfalls

  • Misinterpreting the shape of the car's graph (e.g., seeing it as a rectangle and a triangle instead of a single trapezium with correct parallel sides).
  • Errors in calculating the area under the motorbike's graph, especially for the deceleration phase if the time duration is incorrectly calculated (e.g., using T instead of T-60).
  • Algebraic mistakes when solving the linear equation for T after equating the distances.
