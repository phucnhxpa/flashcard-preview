Worked solution

Part (a): Find H in terms of U and g.

Thought Process: We recognize this as a standard vertical projectile motion problem. We need to find the maximum height `H` reached by particle P. At the greatest height, the particle's instantaneous velocity is 0. We know the initial speed `U`, and the acceleration due to gravity `g` (acting downwards). The suvat equation that relates these quantities without time is $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$.

Working:
Let the origin O be the starting point. Let the positive direction be vertically upwards.
For particle P:
Initial velocity, $u = U$
Final velocity at greatest height, $v = 0$
Acceleration, $a = -g$ (since `g` acts downwards)
Displacement, $s = H$

Applying the suvat equation $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$:
$$0^2 = U^2 + 2(-g)H$$
$$0 = U^2 - 2gH$$
$$2gH = U^2$$
$$H = \frac{U^2}{2g}$$

Answer: $H = \frac{U^2}{2g}$

Part (b): Find the time between Q projected and collision.

Thought Process: This is a collision problem involving two particles projected at different times. The crucial step is to define a consistent time frame and origin for both particles. The question asks for the time *after Q is projected*, so it's simplest to set $t=0$ at the instant particle Q is projected. We need to find displacement equations for both particles from the same origin (O) and in the same direction (upwards).

For particle P: When Q is projected ($t=0$ for our new timeline), P is at its greatest height `H` (which we found in part a) and momentarily at rest. So, for P, its initial velocity at $t=0$ (in this timeline) is $0$, and its initial displacement from O is $H$. It's moving under gravity.
For particle Q: It is projected from O with speed $U/2$ at $t=0$. Its motion is also under gravity.
Collision occurs when their displacements from O are equal, i.e., $s_P = s_Q$.

Working:
Let the origin O be the starting point. Let the positive direction be vertically upwards. Let $t=0$ be the instant when particle Q is projected.

For particle P:
Initial displacement from O at $t=0$: $s_0 = H = \frac{U^2}{2g}$
Initial velocity at $t=0$: $u_P = 0$ (since it's at its greatest height)
Acceleration: $a_P = -g$
Using $s = s_0 + ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$:
$$s_P(t) = H + (0)t + \frac{1}{2}(-g)t^2$$
$$s_P(t) = \frac{U^2}{2g} - \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \quad (1)$$

For particle Q:
Initial displacement from O at $t=0$: $s_0 = 0$
Initial velocity at $t=0$: $u_Q = \frac{1}{2}U$ (projected upwards)
Acceleration: $a_Q = -g$
Using $s = s_0 + ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$:
$$s_Q(t) = 0 + \left(\frac{1}{2}U\right)t + \frac{1}{2}(-g)t^2$$
$$s_Q(t) = \frac{1}{2}Ut - \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \quad (2)$$

Collision occurs when $s_P(t) = s_Q(t)$:
$$\frac{U^2}{2g} - \frac{1}{2}gt^2 = \frac{1}{2}Ut - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$$
Notice that the $-\frac{1}{2}gt^2$ terms cancel out on both sides. This is a common simplification in collision problems when both particles are under constant gravitational acceleration and the time is referenced consistently.
$$\frac{U^2}{2g} = \frac{1}{2}Ut$$
To solve for $t$, we can multiply both sides by $2g$ and divide by $U$ (assuming $U \neq 0$):
$$U^2 = Ugt$$
$$t = \frac{U^2}{Ug}$$
$$t = \frac{U}{g}$$

Answer: $t = \frac{U}{g}$

Part (c): Find where the two particles collide.

Thought Process: To find the collision point, we simply need to substitute the time of collision, $t = U/g$, into either of the displacement equations ($s_P(t)$ or $s_Q(t)$). Using $s_Q(t)$ is generally simpler as it starts from $s_0=0$.

Working:
Substitute $t = \frac{U}{g}$ into equation (2) for $s_Q(t)$:
$$s_Q(t) = \frac{1}{2}Ut - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$$
$$s_Q\left(\frac{U}{g}\right) = \frac{1}{2}U\left(\frac{U}{g}\right) - \frac{1}{2}g\left(\frac{U}{g}\right)^2$$
$$s_Q = \frac{U^2}{2g} - \frac{1}{2}g\left(\frac{U^2}{g^2}\right)$$
$$s_Q = \frac{U^2}{2g} - \frac{U^2}{2g}$$
$$s_Q = \frac{U^2}{2g} - \frac{U^2}{2g}$$
$$s_Q = 0$$

This result implies the collision happens at the origin O. Let's double check with $s_P(t)$:
$$s_P(t) = \frac{U^2}{2g} - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$$
$$s_P\left(\frac{U}{g}\right) = \frac{U^2}{2g} - \frac{1}{2}g\left(\frac{U}{g}\right)^2$$
$$s_P = \frac{U^2}{2g} - \frac{1}{2}g\frac{U^2}{g^2}$$
$$s_P = \frac{U^2}{2g} - \frac{U^2}{2g}$$
$$s_P = 0$$

Both equations yield a displacement of 0. This means the particles collide at point O.

Answer: The particles collide at O.

Key techniques

  • applying suvat equations to vertical motion
  • defining consistent origin and positive direction
  • setting up displacement equations for multiple particles with different starting times
  • solving simultaneous equations to find collision time/position

Common pitfalls

  • Inconsistent time frames: Not defining a clear $t=0$ for all particles or using the same $t$ value for particles starting at different times.
  • Sign errors for acceleration: Forgetting that gravity acts downwards, or using an inconsistent positive direction for displacement and acceleration.
  • Incorrect initial conditions: Forgetting that P is at rest at height H when Q is projected, or miscalculating P's initial height.
