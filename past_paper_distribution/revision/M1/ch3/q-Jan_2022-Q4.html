Worked solution

Part (a): Find the value of T

Thought Process:
We recognize this as a standard kinematics problem involving motion under constant acceleration, which is gravity. We're given initial height, maximum height (or height above projection point where velocity is zero), and need to find the total time until the ball hits the ground. It's usually best to define a positive direction, typically upwards, which means acceleration due to gravity ($g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$) will be negative. We can break the motion into segments or consider the entire motion from projection to ground.

The most efficient approach is often to first find the initial projection velocity ($u$) using the information about the ball reaching instantaneous rest at point B. Then, use this $u$ to analyze the total motion from point A to the ground.

Working:

  1. Define positive direction: Let's take upwards as the positive direction. Therefore, acceleration $a = -g = -9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$.
  1. Find the initial velocity ($u$) from A to B:
At point B, the ball comes to instantaneous rest, so its final velocity $v = 0$. The displacement from A to B is $s = 19.6 \text{ m}$. Using the SUVAT equation $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$: $$0^2 = u^2 + 2(-9.8)(19.6)$$ $$0 = u^2 - 38.416$$ $$u^2 = 38.416$$ $$u^2 = 2 \times 9.8 \times 19.6$$ We can notice that $2 \times 9.8 = 19.6$, so $u^2 = 19.6 \times 19.6 = 19.6^2$. Therefore, $u = 19.6 \text{ m/s}$ (we take the positive root as the initial velocity is upwards).
  1. Find the total time ($T$) from A to the ground:
From point A, the initial velocity is $u = 19.6 \text{ m/s}$. The acceleration is $a = -9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. The ball starts at a height of $24.5 \text{ m}$ above the ground and hits the ground, so its final displacement relative to A is $s = -24.5 \text{ m}$ (since it ends up $24.5 \text{ m}$ below its starting point). Using the SUVAT equation $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$: $$-24.5 = (19.6)T + \frac{1}{2}(-9.8)T^2$$ $$-24.5 = 19.6T - 4.9T^2$$ Rearrange into a quadratic equation by moving all terms to one side: $$4.9T^2 - 19.6T - 24.5 = 0$$ To simplify, divide the entire equation by $4.9$: $$\frac{4.9T^2}{4.9} - \frac{19.6T}{4.9} - \frac{24.5}{4.9} = 0$$ $$T^2 - 4T - 5 = 0$$ Factorize the quadratic equation: $$(T - 5)(T + 1) = 0$$ This gives two possible solutions for $T$: $T = 5$ or $T = -1$. Since time $T$ must be positive, we discard $T = -1$.
Answer: $T = 5$

Part (b): Sketch a speed-time graph for the motion

Thought Process:
A speed-time graph shows the magnitude of velocity. Since the acceleration is constant, the velocity-time graph would be a straight line with a constant negative gradient (due to gravity). The speed-time graph, however, will show the absolute value of this velocity. It will always be non-negative.

  1. Initial speed: At $t=0$, the speed is the initial velocity $u = 19.6 \text{ m/s}$.
  2. Time to reach B: The ball momentarily stops at B, meaning its speed is zero. We can calculate this time ($t_{AB}$) using $v = u + at$: $0 = 19.6 + (-9.8)t_{AB} \implies t_{AB} = 19.6/9.8 = 2 \text{ s}$.
  3. Shape of the graph: From $t=0$ to $t=2 \text{ s}$, the ball is moving upwards, its velocity is positive, and its speed decreases linearly from $19.6 \text{ m/s}$ to $0 \text{ m/s}$. The graph will be a straight line sloping downwards.
  4. From $t=2 \text{ s}$ to $t=T=5 \text{ s}$, the ball is falling downwards. Its velocity is negative and increasing in magnitude. Its speed increases linearly from $0 \text{ m/s}$. The graph will be a straight line sloping upwards.
  5. Gradients: The gradient of a speed-time graph represents the magnitude of acceleration. Since gravity is constant ($|a|=9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$), the magnitude of the gradient will be constant throughout the motion. This means the two straight line segments of the graph will have equal (magnitude) slopes, forming a 'V' shape with approximately equal angles to the t-axis.
  6. Relative lengths: The first segment lasts for $2 \text{ s}$. The second segment lasts for $T - t_{AB} = 5 - 2 = 3 \text{ s}$. Since $3 \text{ s} > 2 \text{ s}$, the second line segment (where speed is increasing) should be horizontally longer than the first.
  7. Labels: Mark $O$ at the origin, $T$ on the horizontal axis (time), and 'speed' and 't' on the axes.

Working (Description of Sketch):

The sketch will be a V-shaped graph.


  • It starts at $(0, 19.6)$ on the speed axis.

  • It goes downwards in a straight line to $(2, 0)$ on the time axis.

  • From $(2, 0)$, it goes upwards in a straight line to $(5, V_{final})$, where $V_{final}$ is the speed when it hits the ground. (No calculation of $V_{final}$ is explicitly required for the sketch, but it helps to ensure the graph goes up).

  • The point where the speed is zero (the tip of the 'V') should be at $t=2$.

  • The final point on the graph should be at $t=T=5$.

  • The two straight line segments forming the 'V' shape should have gradients of equal magnitude, so they make roughly equal angles with the t-axis.

  • The second segment (from $t=2$ to $t=5$) should be horizontally longer than the first segment (from $t=0$ to $t=2$).

Answer: (See description above; no ASCII art allowed). The graph will be a V-shape, starting at a positive speed on the y-axis, decreasing linearly to zero at $t=2$s, then increasing linearly until $t=5$s. The segment from $t=2$s to $t=5$s will be horizontally longer than the segment from $t=0$s to $t=2$s, and both segments will have gradients of equal magnitude (thus forming roughly equal angles with the t-axis). The point $T$ (at $t=5$) should be clearly marked on the time axis.

Key techniques

  • Select and apply appropriate SUVAT equations
  • Solve quadratic equations for time
  • Distinguish between velocity and speed for graphing
  • Sketch speed-time graphs for constant acceleration
