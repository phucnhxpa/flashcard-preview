Worked solution

Let's assume the question describes a particle $P$ of mass $250 \text{ kg}$ released from rest, falling freely for $5 \text{ s}$. At $t=5 \text{ s}$, a parachute opens, creating an upward resistive force of $3200 \text{ N}$. The total distance fallen is $520 \text{ m}$. We use $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$.

Part (a): Find the distance fallen by $P$ in the first $5 \text{ s}$ of its motion.

Thought process: We recognize this as a free-fall problem with constant acceleration due to gravity. The particle starts from rest, so its initial velocity is 0. We need to find displacement given initial velocity, acceleration, and time. This points to using a suitable SUVAT equation.

Working:
For the first phase (free fall):
$u = 0 \text{ m/s}$ (released from rest)
$a = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$ (acceleration due to gravity)
$t = 5 \text{ s}$
We want to find $s$.
Using the SUVAT equation $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$:
$$s = (0)(5) + \frac{1}{2}(9.8)(5)^2$$
$$s = 0 + \frac{1}{2}(9.8)(25)$$
$$s = 4.9 \times 25$$
$$s = 122.5 \text{ m}$$

Part (b): Find the speed of $P$ when it hits the ground.

Thought process: This is a two-phase problem. First, we need the velocity at the end of the free-fall phase, which becomes the initial velocity for the second phase. Next, we must determine the new constant acceleration for the second phase by applying Newton's Second Law ($F=ma$) considering the weight and the resistive force. We'll then find the distance covered in the second phase and finally use SUVAT to find the final velocity.

Working:


  1. Velocity at the end of Phase 1 (when parachute opens):


Using $v = u + at$ for Phase 1:
$$v_{open} = 0 + (9.8)(5)$$
$$v_{open} = 49 \text{ m/s}$$
This speed is the initial velocity for the second phase ($u_2 = 49 \text{ m/s}$).

  1. Acceleration during Phase 2 (parachute open):
The forces acting on the particle are its weight downwards ($mg$) and the resistive force upwards ($R$). Weight $W = mg = 250 \times 9.8 = 2450 \text{ N}$ (downwards). Resistive force $R = 3200 \text{ N}$ (upwards). Let's take downwards as the positive direction. The net force $F_{net}$ is: $$F_{net} = W - R = 2450 - 3200 = -750 \text{ N}$$ Using Newton's Second Law, $F_{net} = ma$: $$-750 = 250a$$ $$a = \frac{-750}{250} = -3 \text{ m/s}^2$$ The negative sign indicates deceleration (acceleration is upwards, opposite to the direction of motion).
  1. Displacement during Phase 2:
The total distance fallen is $520 \text{ m}$. The distance fallen in Phase 1 was $122.5 \text{ m}$. So, the distance fallen in Phase 2 ($s_2$) is: $$s_2 = 520 - 122.5 = 397.5 \text{ m}$$
  1. Final velocity at landing (end of Phase 2):
For Phase 2: $u_2 = 49 \text{ m/s}$ $a_2 = -3 \text{ m/s}^2$ $s_2 = 397.5 \text{ m}$ We want to find $v_2$. Using the SUVAT equation $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$: $$v_2^2 = (49)^2 + 2(-3)(397.5)$$ $$v_2^2 = 2401 - 6(397.5)$$ $$v_2^2 = 2401 - 2385$$ $$v_2^2 = 16$$ $$v_2 = \sqrt{16} = 4 \text{ m/s}$$ (Speed is positive, as it's still moving downwards).
Answer: The speed of $P$ when it hits the ground is $4 \text{ m/s}$.

Part (c): Find the total time taken for the motion of $P$.

Thought process: We already know the time for Phase 1 (5 s). We need to find the time for Phase 2 using the parameters we've calculated (initial velocity, acceleration, final velocity). Then we add the two times.

Working:
For Phase 2:
$u_2 = 49 \text{ m/s}$
$v_2 = 4 \text{ m/s}$ (from part b)
$a_2 = -3 \text{ m/s}^2$
We want to find $t_2$.
Using the SUVAT equation $v = u + at$:
$$4 = 49 + (-3)t_2$$
$$4 - 49 = -3t_2$$
$$-45 = -3t_2$$
$$t_2 = \frac{-45}{-3} = 15 \text{ s}$$
Total time for the motion is the sum of times for Phase 1 and Phase 2:
$$T_{total} = t_1 + t_2 = 5 + 15 = 20 \text{ s}$$

Answer: The total time taken for the motion of $P$ is $20 \text{ s}$.

Part (d): Sketch a speed-time graph for the motion of $P$.

Thought process: A speed-time graph for constant acceleration is a straight line. We have two phases, each with constant acceleration, so the graph will consist of two straight line segments. We need to identify the key points in time and corresponding speeds.

Working:


  • Phase 1 (0 to 5 s): Starts from rest, so speed is 0 at $t=0$. Ends at $t=5 \text{ s}$ with a speed of $49 \text{ m/s}$. The acceleration is positive ($9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$), so the graph is a straight line with a positive gradient from $(0,0)$ to $(5,49)$.

  • Phase 2 (5 s to 20 s): Starts at $t=5 \text{ s}$ with a speed of $49 \text{ m/s}$. Ends at $t=20 \text{ s}$ (total time) with a speed of $4 \text{ m/s}$. The acceleration is negative ($-3 \text{ m/s}^2$), so the graph is a straight line with a negative gradient from $(5,49)$ to $(20,4)$.

The graph would be a trapezium shape (not hitting the t-axis at the end).

Answer: The speed-time graph starts at $(0,0)$, increases linearly to $(5,49)$, and then decreases linearly to $(20,4)$.

Key techniques

  • Apply suvat equations for constant acceleration
  • Calculate acceleration from forces (F=ma)
  • Break complex motion into distinct phases
  • Ensure consistency of velocity and displacement between phases
  • Sketch and interpret speed-time graphs

Common pitfalls

  • Failing to break the motion into distinct phases with different accelerations.
  • Incorrectly calculating the new acceleration for the second phase by misapplying Newton's Second Law or using an incorrect sign convention.
  • Forgetting to add the times from each phase for the total time, or incorrectly using the total distance/time in a single SUVAT equation for the multi-phase motion.
  • Incorrectly drawing the speed-time graph, e.g., using curved lines, not indicating correct coordinates for key points, or incorrectly ending the graph at the t-axis if the final velocity is non-zero.
