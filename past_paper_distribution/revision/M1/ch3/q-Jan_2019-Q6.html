Question 6 continued
Worked solution

This question involves a multi-stage journey, making a speed-time graph the most effective tool to visualise and solve it. We will use the fact that the area under a speed-time graph represents distance, and the gradient represents acceleration.

(a) Sketch below a speed‑time graph for the journey of the train between the two stations A and B.

Thought Process: We need to represent three distinct phases of motion: constant acceleration from rest, constant speed, and constant deceleration to rest. This will naturally form a trapezium shape. We must label the key times and the maximum speed $V$.

Working:
Draw a graph with time ($t$) on the x-axis and speed ($v$) on the y-axis.


  1. The train starts from rest at A ($v=0$ at $t=0$).

  2. It accelerates constantly for 60 s to a speed of $V$ m s$^{-1}$. This is a straight line segment from $(0,0)$ to $(60,V)$.

  3. It then travels at constant speed $V$ m s$^{-1}$. This is a horizontal line segment starting from $(60,V)$.

  4. It decelerates for 30 s, coming to rest at B. Since the total journey is 270 s, the deceleration phase starts at $t = 270 - 30 = 240$ s. This is a straight line segment from $(240,V)$ to $(270,0)$.

The graph will be a trapezium with vertices at $(0,0)$, $(60,V)$, $(240,V)$, and $(270,0)$.

(b) find the value of V,

Thought Process: The total distance between the stations is given as 4.5 km, which is 4500 m. For a speed-time graph, the total distance travelled is equal to the total area under the graph. We can calculate this area by splitting the trapezium into a triangle (acceleration), a rectangle (constant speed), and another triangle (deceleration).

Working:
Let $D$ be the total distance.
$$D = \text{Area under graph}$$
The total area can be calculated as the sum of the areas of the first triangle, the rectangle, and the second triangle:
$$\text{Area}_1 = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base}_1 \times \text{height} = \frac{1}{2} \times 60 \times V = 30V$$
$$\text{Area}_2 = \text{base}_2 \times \text{height} = (240 - 60) \times V = 180V$$
$$\text{Area}_3 = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base}_3 \times \text{height} = \frac{1}{2} \times 30 \times V = 15V$$
Total distance $D = \text{Area}_1 + \text{Area}_2 + \text{Area}_3 = 30V + 180V + 15V = 225V$.
Given $D = 4500$ m:
$$225V = 4500$$
$$V = \frac{4500}{225}$$
$$V = 20$$

Answer: $V = 20$ m s$^{-1}$

(c) find how long it takes the train to travel from station A to the point that is exactly halfway between the two stations.

Thought Process: The total distance is 4500 m, so halfway is $4500/2 = 2250$ m. We need to determine in which phase of the journey this halfway point occurs. We'll calculate the distance covered in the first phase and then in the second phase until the cumulative distance reaches 2250 m.

Working:
We know $V=20$ m s$^{-1}$.
Distance covered during the first 60 s (acceleration phase):
$$D_{0-60} = \frac{1}{2} \times 60 \times V = \frac{1}{2} \times 60 \times 20 = 600 \text{ m}$$
The halfway point is 2250 m. Since 600 m < 2250 m, the halfway point is reached during the constant speed phase (i.e., between $t=60$ s and $t=240$ s).
Distance remaining to reach the halfway point after the first 60 s:
$$D_{\text{remaining}} = 2250 - 600 = 1650 \text{ m}$$
During the constant speed phase, the train travels at $V=20$ m s$^{-1}$. Let $t'$ be the time spent in this phase to cover $D_{\text{remaining}}$.
$$t' = \frac{D_{\text{remaining}}}{V} = \frac{1650}{20} = 82.5 \text{ s}$$
The total time $T$ to reach the halfway point is the sum of the time for the acceleration phase and the time $t'$ spent in the constant speed phase:
$$T = 60 + 82.5 = 142.5 \text{ s}$$

Answer: $T = 142.5$ s

(d) Find the value of T1 and the value of T2

Thought Process: The train is travelling at speed $\frac{1}{4}V$. With $V=20$, this speed is $\frac{1}{4} \times 20 = 5$ m s$^{-1}$. We need to find two times, $T_1$ and $T_2$, when the speed is 5 m s$^{-1}$. This speed occurs once during acceleration and once during deceleration.

Working:
Let the target speed be $v_s = \frac{1}{4}V = \frac{1}{4}(20) = 5$ m s$^{-1}$.

For $T_1$ (during acceleration phase: $0 \le t \le 60$):
In this phase, the speed increases linearly from 0 to $V$ (20 m s$^{-1}$) over 60 s. The acceleration is constant:
$$a = \frac{\text{change in speed}}{\text{change in time}} = \frac{V - 0}{60 - 0} = \frac{20}{60} = \frac{1}{3} \text{ m s}^{-2}$$
Using the kinematic equation $v = u + at$, with $u=0$:
$$v_s = 0 + a T_1$$
$$5 = \frac{1}{3} T_1$$
$$T_1 = 5 \times 3 = 15 \text{ s}$$

For $T_2$ (during deceleration phase: $240 \le t \le 270$):
In this phase, the speed decreases linearly from $V$ (20 m s$^{-1}$) to 0 over 30 s. The deceleration is constant:
$$a' = \frac{\text{change in speed}}{\text{change in time}} = \frac{0 - V}{270 - 240} = \frac{0 - 20}{30} = -\frac{20}{30} = -\frac{2}{3} \text{ m s}^{-2}$$
Let $t_{dec}$ be the time elapsed since the start of the deceleration phase (i.e., $t_{dec} = T_2 - 240$). Using $v = u + a' t_{dec}$, where $u=V=20$ m s$^{-1}$ is the speed at $t=240$ s:
$$v_s = V + a' t_{dec}$$
$$5 = 20 + \left(-\frac{2}{3}\right) t_{dec}$$
$$5 - 20 = -\frac{2}{3} t_{dec}$$
$$-15 = -\frac{2}{3} t_{dec}$$
$$t_{dec} = -15 \times \left(-\frac{3}{2}\right) = \frac{45}{2} = 22.5 \text{ s}$$
Now, find the actual time $T_2$ from the start of the journey:
$$T_2 = 240 + t_{dec} = 240 + 22.5 = 262.5 \text{ s}$$

Answer: $T_1 = 15$ s and $T_2 = 262.5$ s

Key techniques

  • Sketching speed-time graphs from description
  • Calculating distance as area under speed-time graph
  • Applying SUVAT equations or proportionality for constant acceleration/deceleration
  • Solving for specific times/distances within segments of motion

Common pitfalls

  • Misinterpreting graph segments (e.g., using acceleration for constant speed section).
  • Calculation errors when finding areas or solving equations, especially with fractions or negative signs for deceleration.
  • Forgetting to add the initial time periods when calculating total time for later phases (e.g., in part (c) and for $T_2$ in part (d)).
