7.	 Two small children, Ajaz and Beth, are running a 100 m race along a straight horizontal track.
	
They both start from rest, leaving the start line at the same time.
	
Ajaz accelerates at 0.8 m s–2 up to a speed of 4 m s–1 and then maintains this speed until he 
crosses the finish line.
	
Beth accelerates at 1 m s–2 for T seconds and then maintains a constant speed until she 
crosses the finish line.
	
Ajaz and Beth cross the finish line at the same time.
	
(a)	 Sketch, on the same axes, a speed‑time graph for each child, from the instant when they 
leave the start line to the instant when they cross the finish line.

Worked solution

(a) Sketch, on the same axes, a speed‑time graph for each child, from the instant when they leave the start line to the instant when they cross the finish line.

Thought Process: We recognize this as a standard kinematics problem requiring a visual representation of motion. A speed-time graph is the most appropriate tool here, as its gradient represents acceleration and its area represents distance travelled. For each child, the motion consists of two distinct phases: initial acceleration (straight line from the origin) followed by constant speed (horizontal line).

Working:


  • Ajaz: Starts at $(0,0)$. Accelerates at $0.8 \text{ m/s}^2$ up to a speed of $4 \text{ m/s}$. This phase is represented by a straight line from the origin with a gradient of $0.8$. The time taken to reach $4 \text{ m/s}$ is $t_1 = \frac{4}{0.8} = 5 \text{ s}$. After $t_1=5 \text{ s}$, he maintains a constant speed of $4 \text{ m/s}$, so the graph becomes a horizontal line at $v=4$.

  • Beth: Starts at $(0,0)$. Accelerates at $1 \text{ m/s}^2$ for $T$ seconds. This phase is represented by a straight line from the origin with a gradient of $1$. At time $T$, her speed will be $v_B = 1 \times T = T \text{ m/s}$. After time $T$, she maintains a constant speed of $T \text{ m/s}$, so the graph becomes a horizontal line at $v=T$.

  • Both graphs must originate from $(0,0)$ and terminate at the same final time on the horizontal axis, as they finish the race at the same instant. Beth's initial gradient (1) is steeper than Ajaz's (0.8).

  • The speed '4' should be labelled on the vertical axis. The time 'T' should be marked on the horizontal axis corresponding to the end of Beth's acceleration, and 'T' should also be labelled on the vertical axis for Beth's constant speed.

Answer: *A sketch showing a linearly increasing speed for Ajaz up to $4 \text{ m/s}$ (at $t=5s$) then constant at $4 \text{ m/s}$. For Beth, a linearly increasing speed up to $T \text{ m/s}$ (at $t=T \text{ s}$) then constant at $T \text{ m/s}$. Beth's initial slope is steeper than Ajaz's. Both graphs start at the origin and end at the same time on the horizontal axis. Speed '$4$' and time '$T$' are labelled.*

(b) Find the time taken by Ajaz to complete the race.

Thought Process: Ajaz's motion covers two distinct phases: constant acceleration followed by constant velocity. To find the total time to cover the $100 \text{ m}$ distance, we will calculate the time and distance for each phase separately, using SUVAT equations for acceleration and the distance formula for constant velocity.

Working:


  • Phase 1 (Acceleration): Ajaz accelerates from rest ($u=0$) at $a=0.8 \text{ m/s}^2$ to a speed of $v=4 \text{ m/s}$.

  • Using $v = u + at_1$ to find the time $t_1$ for this phase:


$$4 = 0 + 0.8t_1 \implies t_1 = \frac{4}{0.8} = 5 \text{ s}$$

  • Using $s_1 = ut_1 + \frac{1}{2}at_1^2$ to find the distance $s_1$ covered in this phase:


$$s_1 = 0(5) + \frac{1}{2}(0.8)(5^2) = 0.4 \times 25 = 10 \text{ m}$$

  • Phase 2 (Constant Speed): Ajaz travels the remaining distance at a constant speed of $4 \text{ m/s}$.

  • Remaining distance $s_2 = 100 - s_1 = 100 - 10 = 90 \text{ m}$.

  • Time $t_2$ for this phase:


$$t_2 = \frac{\text{distance}}{\text{speed}} = \frac{s_2}{v} = \frac{90}{4} = 22.5 \text{ s}$$

  • Total time for Ajaz: The total time $t_A$ is the sum of the times for the two phases.


$$t_A = t_1 + t_2 = 5 + 22.5 = 27.5 \text{ s}$$

Answer: $
Ajaz's total time: $27.5 \text{ s}$.

(c) Find the value of T

Thought Process: We know Beth finishes the race in the same total time as Ajaz, which is $27.5 \text{ s}$. Similar to Ajaz, Beth's motion has two phases: acceleration for $T$ seconds, then constant speed. We need to express her total distance ($100 \text{ m}$) in terms of $T$ and equate it to $100 \text{ m}$. This will lead to a quadratic equation for $T$.

Working:


  • Total time for Beth = $27.5 \text{ s}$.

  • Phase 1 (Acceleration): Beth accelerates from rest ($u=0$) at $a=1 \text{ m/s}^2$ for time $T$.

  • Speed reached at time $T$: $v_B = u + aT \implies v_B = 0 + 1(T) = T \text{ m/s}$.

  • Distance $s_{B1}$ covered in this phase:


$$s_{B1} = ut + \frac{1}{2}aT^2 \implies s_{B1} = 0(T) + \frac{1}{2}(1)(T^2) = \frac{1}{2}T^2 \text{ m}$$

  • Phase 2 (Constant Speed): Beth travels the remaining distance at a constant speed of $v_B = T \text{ m/s}$.

  • Time for this phase = total time - acceleration time = $27.5 - T \text{ s}$.

  • Distance $s_{B2}$ covered in this phase:


$$s_{B2} = \text{speed} \times \text{time} = T(27.5 - T) \text{ m}$$

  • Total distance for Beth: The sum of distances for Beth must be $100 \text{ m}$.


$$s_B = s_{B1} + s_{B2} = 100$$
$$\frac{1}{2}T^2 + T(27.5 - T) = 100$$
$$\frac{1}{2}T^2 + 27.5T - T^2 = 100$$
$$27.5T - \frac{1}{2}T^2 = 100$$

  • Multiply the entire equation by 2 to eliminate the fraction:


$$55T - T^2 = 200$$

  • Rearrange into a standard quadratic equation $aT^2 + bT + c = 0$:


$$T^2 - 55T + 200 = 0$$

  • Solve the quadratic equation: Using the quadratic formula $T = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$:


$$T = \frac{-(-55) \pm \sqrt{(-55)^2 - 4(1)(200)}}{2(1)}$$
$$T = \frac{55 \pm \sqrt{3025 - 800}}{2}$$
$$T = \frac{55 \pm \sqrt{2225}}{2}$$
$$T = \frac{55 \pm 47.1699...}{2}$$

  • This yields two possible values for $T$:

  • $T_1 = \frac{55 + 47.1699...}{2} = \frac{102.1699...}{2} \approx 51.08 \text{ s}$.

  • $T_2 = \frac{55 - 47.1699...}{2} = \frac{7.8301...}{2} \approx 3.9150 \text{ s}$.

  • Physical Interpretation: $T$ represents the duration of Beth's acceleration phase. This time cannot be greater than the total race time ($27.5 \text{ s}$). Therefore, $T_1 = 51.08 \text{ s}$ is not a physically possible solution. We select the smaller value.

Answer: $
T = 3.92 \text{ s}$ (3 s.f.).

(d) Find the difference in the speeds of the two children as they cross the finish line.

Thought Process: At the moment they cross the finish line, Ajaz is moving at his constant speed of $4 \text{ m/s}$. Beth has also completed her acceleration phase and is moving at her constant speed, which is $T \text{ m/s}$. We need to calculate the absolute difference between these two speeds.

Working:**


  • Ajaz's speed at the finish line = $4 \text{ m/s}$.

  • Beth's speed at the finish line = $T \text{ m/s} \approx 3.9150... \text{ m/s}$ (using the more precise value of $T$ from part (c)).

  • Difference in speeds = $|4 - T| = |4 - 3.9150...| = 0.0849... \text{ m/s}$.

Answer: $
Difference in speeds: $0.085 \text{ m/s}$ (2 s.f.).

Key techniques

  • Sketching speed-time graphs (linear acceleration, constant velocity)
  • Relating area under speed-time graph to distance travelled
  • Applying SUVAT formulae for constant acceleration
  • Solving quadratic equations
  • Interpreting physical solutions from mathematical models

Common pitfalls

  • Not rejecting the physically impossible root when solving the quadratic equation for T, as the acceleration phase time cannot exceed the total race time.
  • Incorrectly calculating the area under the speed-time graph, especially forgetting the $\frac{1}{2}$ for the triangular acceleration phase or miscalculating the time duration for the constant speed phase.
  • Confusing the time 'T' with Beth's constant speed, which also happens to be 'T' m/s, or misinterpreting the speeds at the finish line.
