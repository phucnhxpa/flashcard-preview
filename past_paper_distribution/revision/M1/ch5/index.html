Equilibrium on a Rough Horizontal Surface
5×
9-12m

A particle (box, block, ring) on a flat rough horizontal surface, acted on by one or more forces at angles. Questions ask for the magnitude of friction, the coefficient of friction (mu), or the range of an applied force for equilibrium to be maintained.

Triggersin equilibriumon the point of slippinglimiting frictionrough horizontal planecoefficient of friction
Assumptions
  • The object can be modelled as a particle, meaning it's a point mass with no dimensions, so we don't consider rotation.
  • The surface is uniformly rough, meaning the coefficient of friction mu is constant across the entire surface.
  • Air resistance is negligible and can be ignored.
  • An applied force is a pull (tension) unless described as a push or thrust.
Approaches
A: Resolve Horizontally and Vertically
When: Always. This is the standard and most reliable method for these problems.
  1. Draw a clear free-body diagram showing all forces: Weight (W=mg), Normal Reaction (R), Applied Force(s) (P), and Friction (F).
  2. Resolve all angled forces into their horizontal (x) and vertical (y) components.
  3. Apply Newton's First Law: sum of forces in any direction is zero.
  4. Set up the vertical equilibrium equation: Sum of upward forces = Sum of downward forces. This allows you to solve for R. Remember that R is not always mg if there are vertical components from other forces.
  5. Set up the horizontal equilibrium equation: Sum of leftward forces = Sum of rightward forces. This relates the applied force to the friction.
  6. Use the correct friction law. If not slipping, F <= mu*R. If on the point of slipping (limiting equilibrium), use the specific formula F = mu*R.
  7. Solve the resulting equations, often simultaneously, for the unknowns.
+ Pros: Systematic, universally applicable, and less prone to conceptual errors.
– Cons: Can lead to more algebra than other methods if non-standard axes were used.
Formulas
Resultant Force = 0 (for equilibrium)
F <= mu*R (Friction Law for equilibrium)
F = mu*R (Limiting Friction, for when an object is on the point of moving)
W = mg
Pitfalls
  • Assuming friction is limiting (F = mu*R) when the particle is just in equilibrium but not stated to be on the point of slipping.
  • Incorrectly resolving applied force components (e.g., mixing up sin and cos).
  • Forgetting to include the vertical component of an angled applied force when calculating the normal reaction R. R is not always equal to mg.

Equilibrium on a Rough Inclined Plane
3×
8-12m

A particle on a rough inclined plane, held in equilibrium by an external force (often acting horizontally or parallel to the plane). The question usually involves 'impending motion' either up or down the plane.

Triggersinclined planeangle of inclinationrough surfaceon the point of moving up the planeon the point of sliding downsmallest force to prevent slipping
Assumptions
  • The particle is a point mass.
  • The plane's surface is uniformly rough.
  • Weight (mg) acts vertically downwards, NOT perpendicular to the plane.
  • The normal reaction (R) acts perpendicular to the plane, NOT vertically upwards.
Approaches
A: Resolve Parallel and Perpendicular to the Plane
When: Almost always the most efficient method for inclined plane problems.
  1. Draw a clear free-body diagram. Label Weight (W=mg), Normal Reaction (R), Friction (F), and any applied force (P).
  2. Crucially, determine the direction of impending motion to set the direction of friction. Friction always opposes this tendency to move.
  3. Resolve all forces into components parallel and perpendicular to the inclined plane. The most common force to resolve is weight: W_parallel = mg*sin(theta), W_perpendicular = mg*cos(theta).
  4. Apply equilibrium perpendicular to the plane: Sum of forces = 0. Solve this equation for R.
  5. Apply equilibrium parallel to the plane: Sum of forces = 0.
  6. Use the limiting friction formula F = mu*R, as these questions almost always involve impending motion.
  7. Substitute your expressions for R and F into the parallel equation and solve for the unknown.
+ Pros: Massively simplifies equations as Normal Reaction and Friction don't need resolving.
– Cons: Requires careful resolving of weight and any other forces not aligned with the plane.
Formulas
Component of weight parallel to plane = mg*sin(theta)
Component of weight perpendicular to plane = mg*cos(theta)
F = mu*R (Limiting Friction)
Pitfalls
  • Incorrectly identifying the direction of friction. For the 'smallest' force to prevent slipping down, friction acts UP the plane. For motion UP the plane, friction acts DOWN the plane.
  • Swapping sin and cos when resolving weight. A good mnemonic is 'sine slides', as mg*sin(theta) is the component that causes sliding down the slope.
  • Resolving a horizontal force (P) incorrectly into components. Parallel to plane is P*cos(theta), perpendicular is P*sin(theta).

Equilibrium with Multiple Forces (Smooth/No Friction)
3×
6-10m

A particle (e.g., a knot, a ring) held in place by two or more forces, typically tensions from strings. The geometry of the setup (angles, lengths) is usually given. There is no friction.

Triggersin equilibriumlight inextensible stringsmooth pulleytensionsmooth
Assumptions
  • The particle is a point mass.
  • Strings are 'light' (massless) and 'inextensible' (do not stretch).
  • Pulleys or pegs are 'smooth', which means the tension is the same on both sides of the pulley/peg.
Approaches
A: Resolve Horizontally and Vertically
When: The standard, most reliable method for any number of forces.
  1. Draw a large, clear free-body diagram of the particle, showing all forces radiating outwards with their angles relative to the horizontal or vertical.
  2. Use geometry (e.g., Z-angles, F-angles, SOHCAHTOA) to find all necessary angles.
  3. Resolve every force into its horizontal (x) and vertical (y) components.
  4. Apply equilibrium conditions: Sum of all horizontal components = 0.
  5. Apply equilibrium conditions: Sum of all vertical components = 0.
  6. Solve the two resulting simultaneous equations for the unknowns (usually tensions or weights).
+ Pros: Systematic and works for any number of forces, making it universally applicable.
– Cons: Can involve tedious algebra if the angles are complex.
B: Triangle of Forces / Lami's Theorem
When: Only when there are exactly THREE forces acting on the particle in equilibrium.
  1. Confirm there are only three forces acting on the particle.
  2. Draw a vector triangle by placing the force vectors tip-to-tail. Since the particle is in equilibrium, the triangle must close (resultant is zero).
  3. Use the Sine Rule on this triangle of forces (this is Lami's Theorem): P/sin(alpha) = Q/sin(beta) = R/sin(gamma), where alpha is the angle in the triangle opposite the side representing force P, etc.
  4. Alternatively, if the triangle is right-angled, use SOHCAHTOA to solve.
+ Pros: Can be much quicker and more elegant than resolving if the geometry is simple.
– Cons: Only works for three forces; it's easy to miscalculate the internal angles of the force triangle.
Formulas
Sum Fx = 0, Sum Fy = 0
Lami's Theorem: P/sin(alpha) = Q/sin(beta) = R/sin(gamma) (for 3 forces only)
Pitfalls
  • Incorrectly calculating the angles used for resolving, often mixing up angles with the horizontal vs. vertical.
  • Making algebraic errors when solving the simultaneous equations.
  • Attempting to use a triangle of forces when more than three forces are acting.

Equilibrium using Force Vectors
1×
10m

Forces are given explicitly as vectors in i and j component form, e.g., F1 = (3i - 4j) N. The particle is stated to be in equilibrium, and you need to find unknown constants or magnitudes.

Triggersin equilibriumforces F1, F2, F3vectorsi and j components
Assumptions
  • The i and j vectors represent perpendicular unit vectors, typically horizontal and vertical respectively.
  • The particle is a point mass.
Approaches
A: Vector Summation
When: This is the only method when forces are given in vector form.
  1. Write down the condition for equilibrium in vector form: F1 + F2 + F3 + ... = 0 (the zero vector, 0i + 0j).
  2. Sum the i components of all force vectors and set the total equal to 0.
  3. Sum the j components of all force vectors and set the total equal to 0.
  4. This creates two independent linear equations.
  5. Solve these equations for any unknown constants.
  6. If asked for the magnitude of a force P = ai + bj, use Pythagoras: |P| = sqrt(a^2 + b^2).
  7. If asked for direction, use trigonometry: tan(theta) = |b/a| and use a diagram to find the angle from a specified axis.
+ Pros: Very direct and completely avoids geometry/trigonometry with angles.
– Cons: Relies on careful vector addition and avoiding sign errors.
Formulas
Sum of Fi = 0 (vector sum)
Magnitude |F| = sqrt(Fx^2 + Fy^2)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting that equilibrium means the resultant force is the zero vector (0i + 0j).
  • Making sign errors when adding or subtracting the vector components.
  • Confusing magnitude (a scalar quantity) with the force vector itself.

Hybrid Statics & Dynamics Problems
2×
12m

A two-part question. Part (a) is a standard statics problem (e.g., find mu for equilibrium). Part (b) describes a change in the system (e.g., a string is cut, a force is removed) and asks for the subsequent acceleration.

Triggersis held in equilibriumthen it is releasedfind the accelerationafter the string is cut
Assumptions
  • Assumptions from the relevant statics type (e.g., inclined plane) apply in part (a).
  • After release, friction is no longer governed by equilibrium but by F=ma, though its maximum value F=mu*R is usually assumed to be the same.
Approaches
A: Sequential Analysis (Statics then Dynamics)
When: This is the required structure for these problems.
  1. Part (a) - Statics: Treat this as a standard equilibrium problem. Draw the FBD for the equilibrium situation and use Sum of Forces = 0 to find the required value (often mu or a tension).
  2. Part (b) - Dynamics: Read the change carefully. Redraw the free-body diagram for the new situation. Erase any forces that have been removed. The direction of friction might reverse.
  3. Determine the direction of acceleration, a. Define this as the positive direction.
  4. Apply Newton's Second Law: Resultant Force = m*a. The resultant force is the sum of all forces in the direction of motion.
  5. The normal reaction R might need to be recalculated if vertical forces have changed.
  6. Friction is now kinetic. In M1, we assume the value of limiting friction F = mu*R is maintained, using the value of mu found in part (a).
  7. Solve the F=ma equation for the acceleration, a.
+ Pros: Breaks a complex problem down into two manageable, familiar parts.
– Cons: High risk of 'knock-on' errors; a mistake in part (a) will lead to an incorrect answer in part (b).
Formulas
Sum of F = 0 (for equilibrium part)
Resultant Force = m*a (for dynamics part)
F = mu*R (for friction in both parts)
Pitfalls
  • Using a force from the equilibrium part (a) in the dynamics calculation for part (b) after it has been removed.
  • Forgetting to reverse the direction of the friction force if the direction of motion is opposite to the impending motion in part (a).
  • Using the numerical value of friction from part (a) instead of recalculating F=mu*R with a potentially new value of R in part (b).

Resultant of Two Forces (magnitude & bearing)
1×
5-7m

Two forces given in vector or magnitude-direction form. Find the magnitude and direction (often as a bearing) of their resultant. Pure force-resolution, NOT equilibrium.

Triggersresultantmagnitude and directionmagnitude and bearingtwo forcesfind the resultant
Approaches
A: Component method
When: When forces are given in i-j vector form or as magnitude+angle. Resolve each into x,y components, sum, then take magnitude and bearing.
  1. Resolve F1 and F2 into i, j components.
  2. Sum: R = F1 + F2.
  3. Magnitude |R| = sqrt(Rx^2 + Ry^2).
  4. Bearing = atan2 of components, converted to compass bearing (clockwise from N).
B: Triangle of forces (geometric)
When: When forces are given as vectors with a known angle between them. Use cosine rule for magnitude, sine rule for direction.
  1. Draw the head-to-tail triangle.
  2. Apply cosine rule: |R|^2 = F1^2 + F2^2 + 2*F1*F2*cos(angle between).
  3. Apply sine rule for direction angle.
Formulas
$|\mathbf{R}| = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2}$
Bearing $\theta = $ angle measured clockwise from North
Cosine rule: $|\mathbf{R}|^2 = F_1^2 + F_2^2 + 2 F_1 F_2 \cos\alpha$
Pitfalls
  • Confusing bearing (clockwise from N) with standard math angle (anti-clockwise from x-axis).
  • Sign errors when resolving forces: a force at angle alpha below horizontal has component -F sin(alpha) in y.
  • Reporting bearing in the wrong quadrant (must be 0-360°).

Must memorize

  • Condition for Equilibrium: The vector sum of all forces is zero. This means the sum of components in any chosen direction is zero. (MEMORIZE)
  • Friction Law: F <= mu*R. This is the general law for a particle in equilibrium on a rough surface. (MEMORIZE)
  • Limiting Friction: F = mu*R. This is used ONLY when an object is on the point of moving (limiting equilibrium). (MEMORIZE)
  • Weight Components on an Inclined Plane at angle theta to horizontal: mg*sin(theta) acting parallel down the plane, and mg*cos(theta) acting perpendicular into the plane. (MEMORIZE)
  • Weight W = mg, where g = 9.8 m/s^2 unless specified otherwise. (formula booklet)

Global strategy

  • Always Draw the Diagram First: A large, clear Free-Body Diagram (FBD) is the single most important step. Label every force (Weight, Reaction, Tension, Friction, Applied). Don't draw on the question paper's tiny diagram.
  • Choose Your Axes Wisely: For horizontal/vertical problems, resolve horizontally and vertically. For inclined plane problems, ALWAYS resolve parallel and perpendicular to the plane. This simplifies the equations dramatically.
  • State Your Equations Clearly: Before substituting numbers, write down the principle you are using, e.g., 'Resolve // plane: P - F - mg*sin(theta) = 0'. This helps you think clearly and allows the examiner to award method marks.
  • Master Friction's Rules: Understand the difference between F <= mu*R (the general rule for equilibrium) and F = mu*R (the specific rule for *limiting* equilibrium, i.e., on the point of moving). Don't use the latter unless the question states or implies it.
  • Check Friction's Direction: Friction always opposes the direction of *impending* (or actual) motion. Read the question carefully: 'smallest force to prevent slipping down' means impending motion is DOWN, so friction acts UP. 'Force to pull it up' means impending motion is UP, so friction acts DOWN.
  • Resolve, Don't Rotate: M1 Statics is about particles, not rigid bodies. This means all forces act through a single point and you only need to resolve forces (net force is zero). You do not need to take moments.
  • Be Meticulous with Angles: Use SOHCAHTOA and basic geometry (Z-angles, alternate angles) to find the correct angles for resolving. A common mistake is using theta when you should be using 90 - theta or resolving a horizontal force on a slope incorrectly.

Exam tips

  1. Give non-exact answers to 2 or 3 significant figures, as stated on the exam paper cover. However, use the full calculator value in intermediate steps to avoid rounding errors.
  2. If you calculate a value for mu that is greater than 1, double-check your working. It's mathematically possible but rare in exam questions and might indicate an error.
  3. For two-part questions where you must find mu first, label it clearly. You will almost certainly need to use this exact value in the second part of the question.
  4. The Normal Reaction force 'R' is NOT always equal to 'mg'. It is the total force perpendicular to the surface, which counteracts the perpendicular components of ALL other forces (like weight and angled pulls/pushes).
  5. If a question seems too simple for the marks allocated, you have probably missed something. Re-read it carefully. Did you miss a force? Is friction acting? Is the force a push or a pull?
