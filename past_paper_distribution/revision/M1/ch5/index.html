Equilibrium on a Rough Horizontal Surface
5×
9-12m

A particle is on a 'rough horizontal' surface, pulled or pushed by a force at an angle. The question will involve 'equilibrium', 'limiting equilibrium', or being 'on the point of moving'.

Core strategy
  1. 1. Draw a large, clear free-body diagram showing all forces: Weight (W=mg) down, Normal Reaction (R) up, Applied Force (P) at its angle, and Friction (F) opposing the horizontal component of P.
  2. 2. Resolve the angled force P into horizontal ($P \cos \theta$) and vertical ($P \sin \theta$) components.
  3. 3. Apply Newton's First Law ($"\sum F = 0"$) in two directions.
  4. 4. Vertically ($\uparrow$): Sum of upward forces equals sum of downward forces. This gives an equation for R. e.g., $R + P \sin \theta - mg = 0$. Note: R is rarely just mg.
  5. 5. Horizontally ($\rightarrow$): Sum of forces to the right equals sum of forces to the left. e.g., $P \cos \theta - F = 0$.
  6. 6. Check if friction is limiting. If the particle is 'on the point of moving', 'in limiting equilibrium', or you're finding a max/min force, apply the formula $F = \mu R$.
  7. 7. Substitute your expressions for F and R into $F = \mu R$ and solve the resulting equation.
Must-know formulas
$\sum \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$ (MEMORIZE)
$F \le \mu R$ (General friction law) (MEMORIZE)
$F_{max} = \mu R$ (Used for limiting equilibrium) (MEMORIZE)
Common pitfalls
  • Assuming R = mg. If the applied force has a vertical component, R will be different. R is the 'result' of the vertical equilibrium equation.
  • Using $F = \mu R$ when not in limiting equilibrium. If the particle is just in equilibrium, F is simply the force required to balance other horizontal forces.
  • Incorrectly resolving the applied force, for example using sine for the horizontal component instead of cosine.

Equilibrium on a Rough Inclined Plane
3×
8-12m

A particle on a 'rough' slope (inclined plane), held in equilibrium by a force. The question often asks for the 'range of values', or the 'greatest and least' value of a force, which hints at two scenarios for friction.

Core strategy
  1. 1. Draw a large, clear free-body diagram. Label: Weight (mg) acting vertically down, Normal Reaction (R) perpendicular to the plane, the applied Force (P), and Friction (F) parallel to the plane.
  2. 2. Determine the direction of impending motion to set the friction direction. For a 'greatest' pulling force, motion is imminent UP the slope, so friction acts DOWN. For a 'least' force, motion is imminent DOWN the slope, so friction acts UP.
  3. 3. Resolve all forces PARALLEL (//) and PERPENDICULAR (⊥) to the plane. This is much easier than horizontal/vertical.
  4. 4. Weight (mg) resolves into $mg \sin \theta$ (parallel, down the slope) and $mg \cos \theta$ (perpendicular, into the slope).
  5. 5. Apply equilibrium ($"\sum F = 0"$). Resolve ⊥ to the plane to find R. Resolve // to the plane to create an equation involving F.
  6. 6. Use the limiting friction formula $F = \mu R$.
  7. 7. Substitute the expression for R (from step 5) into $F = \mu R$, then substitute this F into your parallel equation to solve.
Must-know formulas
$F_{max} = \mu R$ (MEMORIZE)
Weight components on slope: $W_\parallel = mg \sin\theta$, $W_\perp = mg \cos\theta$ (MEMORIZE or derive quickly)
Common pitfalls
  • Incorrect direction for friction. It MUST oppose impending motion. Read the question carefully for 'greatest' or 'least' force clues.
  • Mixing up $\sin\theta$ and $\cos\theta$ for weight components. Remember 'sin slides' (the parallel component makes it slide), 'cos crushes' (the perpendicular component crushes it into the plane).
  • Incorrectly resolving a horizontal force on an inclined plane. A horizontal force P has components $P \cos \theta$ parallel and $P \sin \theta$ perpendicular to the plane.

Equilibrium of a Particle (No Friction)
3×
6-10m

A particle (like a ring or knot) held in equilibrium by several forces, typically from strings or light rods. There is no mention of 'rough' surfaces or friction.

Core strategy
  1. 1. Draw a free-body diagram of the particle, with all force arrows originating from a single point.
  2. 2. Use the geometry of the setup (e.g., Z-angles, F-angles, SOH CAH TOA) to find the angles each force makes with the horizontal or vertical.
  3. 3. Set up a standard horizontal (x) and vertical (y) coordinate system.
  4. 4. Resolve every force into its x and y components. A force T at angle $\theta$ to the horizontal is $T \cos \theta$ in x-direction and $T \sin \theta$ in y-direction.
  5. 5. Apply the equilibrium conditions: $\sum F_x = 0$ (all forces right = all forces left) and $\sum F_y = 0$ (all forces up = all forces down).
  6. 6. This creates a pair of simultaneous equations. Solve them to find the two unknown quantities (e.g., two tensions, or a tension and an angle).
Must-know formulas
$\sum F_x = 0$ and $\sum F_y = 0$ (MEMORIZE)
Common pitfalls
  • Incorrectly calculating angles from the diagram, especially mixing up angles to the horizontal with angles to the vertical.
  • Sign errors when setting up equilibrium equations. Be consistent with a positive direction (e.g., right is positive, up is positive).
  • Algebraic errors when solving the simultaneous equations.

Equilibrium with Forces as Vectors
1×
10m

Forces are explicitly given in i-j notation or as column vectors. The word 'equilibrium' is used.

Core strategy
  1. 1. Identify all force vectors acting on the particle: $\mathbf{F}_1, \mathbf{F}_2, ...$
  2. 2. The condition for equilibrium is that the resultant force is the zero vector: $\sum \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$.
  3. 3. Add all the given force vectors together and set the sum equal to the zero vector, $0\mathbf{i} + 0\mathbf{j}$.
  4. 4. Separate this single vector equation into two scalar equations by equating the $\mathbf{i}$ components and the $\mathbf{j}$ components to zero independently.
  5. 5. Solve the resulting simple simultaneous equations for any unknowns.
  6. 6. If asked for the magnitude of a force $\mathbf{F} = a\mathbf{i} + b\mathbf{j}$, calculate $|\mathbf{F}| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$.
  7. 7. If asked for direction, calculate the angle using $\tan\theta = |b/a|$ and use a sketch to find the correct bearing or angle with an axis.
Must-know formulas
$\sum \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$ (MEMORIZE)
Magnitude: $|a\mathbf{i} + b\mathbf{j}| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$ (formula booklet)
Common pitfalls
  • Forgetting that equilibrium means the *sum* of forces is zero, not that individual forces are equal.
  • Making sign errors when adding or subtracting vector components.
  • Providing only the magnitude of a vector when a full vector answer is required, or vice versa.

Must memorize

  • The condition for equilibrium: The resultant force is zero. $\sum \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$. This can be split into components: $\sum F_x = 0$ and $\sum F_y = 0$. (MEMORIZE)
  • The law of friction: The friction force $F$ opposes impending motion and satisfies $F \le \mu R$. In limiting equilibrium (on the point of moving), $F = \mu R$. (MEMORIZE)

Global strategy

  • Always start with a large, clear, fully-labelled free-body diagram. This is the single most important step and helps prevent most common errors.
  • The core principle is Newton's First Law: If a particle is in equilibrium, the net force is zero ($"\sum F = 0"$). This always splits into two independent equations by resolving forces in two perpendicular directions.
  • For inclined planes, ALMOST ALWAYS resolve parallel and perpendicular to the plane, not horizontally and vertically. It simplifies the equations immensely.
  • Understand the friction law deeply. $F \le \mu R$ is the general rule. The equality $F = \mu R$ ONLY applies when the object is on the verge of sliding (limiting equilibrium).
  • The Normal Reaction force, R, is not a fixed formula. It is the result of applying $"\sum F = 0"$ perpendicular to the surface of contact. It is not always equal to mg.
  • Be meticulous with your signs. Choose a positive direction for each of your resolution axes and stick to it throughout the problem.
  • Use geometry and trigonometry confidently to find angles before you start resolving forces.

Exam tips

  1. In any question involving friction, explicitly write down $F \le \mu R$ or $F = \mu R$ to show the examiner you know the principle you are applying. This is often required for a method mark.
  2. For 'greatest' and 'least' force questions on a rough slope, this means you must solve the problem twice: once with friction acting down the slope (for the greatest force) and once with friction acting up the slope (for the least force).
  3. Unless specified, use $g = 9.8 \, \text{m s}^{-2}$. Do not round intermediate calculations. Keep values in the calculator or use exact fractions/surds to maintain precision.
  4. Read the question carefully to distinguish between a particle on a 'smooth' plane (no friction) and a 'rough' plane (friction must be included).
