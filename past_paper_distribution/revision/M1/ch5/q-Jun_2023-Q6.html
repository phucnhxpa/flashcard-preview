Worked solution

We recognize this as a statics problem involving a uniform beam in equilibrium. The key to solving such problems is to apply the conditions for equilibrium: the net force acting on the beam must be zero (translational equilibrium), and the net moment about any point must be zero (rotational equilibrium).

We need to draw a clear force diagram to visualize all forces and their distances from a reference point. Given two unknown tensions and one unknown position, along with a relationship between the tensions, we will set up two equations: one by resolving forces vertically and one by taking moments. This will allow us to solve for the unknown position. Choosing the point for taking moments is crucial; taking moments about one of the support points eliminates that force from the moment equation, simplifying the algebra.

Reconstructed Question (based on mark scheme for Q4(a)):
A uniform rod AB has length $2 \text{ m}$ and mass $M$. The rod is supported in a horizontal position by two vertical strings attached at points C and D on the rod. Point C is $0.2 \text{ m}$ from A. Point D is $1.6 \text{ m}$ from A. A particle of mass $M$ is placed on the rod at a distance $p \text{ m}$ from A. The tension in the string at D is 4 times the tension in the string at C. Find the value of $p$.

Working:
Let the rod be AB, with A as the origin (position $0 \text{ m}$). The rod has length $2 \text{ m}$ and is uniform, so its weight $Mg$ acts downwards at its midpoint, $1 \text{ m}$ from A.
String C is at $0.2 \text{ m}$ from A, providing an upward tension $T_C$.
String D is at $1.6 \text{ m}$ from A, providing an upward tension $T_D$.
A particle of mass $M$ is placed at $p \text{ m}$ from A, its weight is $Mg$ acting downwards.
We are given the relationship: $T_D = 4T_C$.

  1. Resolve forces vertically (upward positive):
The sum of upward forces must balance the sum of downward forces for equilibrium. $$T_C + T_D - Mg (\text{beam}) - Mg (\text{particle}) = 0$$ $$T_C + T_D = 2Mg$$ Substitute the given relationship $T_D = 4T_C$ into this equation: $$T_C + 4T_C = 2Mg$$ $$5T_C = 2Mg$$ $$T_C = \frac{2}{5}Mg$$ From this, $T_D = 4 \times \frac{2}{5}Mg = \frac{8}{5}Mg$.
  1. Take moments about a convenient point:
To simplify the equation, we take moments about point C, as this eliminates $T_C$ from the moment equation. We will consider clockwise moments positive (or sum of clockwise moments = sum of anti-clockwise moments). Distances are measured from C ($0.2 \text{ m}$ from A):
  • Weight of beam $Mg$: Acts at $1 \text{ m}$ from A. Distance from C is $(1 - 0.2) = 0.8 \text{ m}$. This causes a clockwise moment.
  • Weight of particle $Mg$: Acts at $p \text{ m}$ from A. Distance from C is $(p - 0.2) \text{ m}$. We assume $p > 0.2$, so this causes a clockwise moment.
  • Tension $T_D$: Acts at $1.6 \text{ m}$ from A. Distance from C is $(1.6 - 0.2) = 1.4 \text{ m}$. This causes an anti-clockwise moment.

Sum of moments about C = 0:
$$Mg(0.8) + Mg(p - 0.2) - T_D(1.4) = 0$$
$$Mg(0.8) + Mg(p - 0.2) = T_D(1.4)$$
Divide the entire equation by $g$ (assuming $g \neq 0$):
$$M(0.8) + M(p - 0.2) = \frac{T_D}{g}(1.4)$$
Now substitute the expression for $T_D = \frac{8}{5}Mg$:
$$0.8M + Mp - 0.2M = \left(\frac{8}{5}M\right)(1.4)$$
Combine terms on the left side:
$$0.6M + Mp = \frac{8 \times 1.4}{5}M$$
Divide the entire equation by $M$ (since $M \neq 0$):
$$0.6 + p = \frac{11.2}{5}$$
$$0.6 + p = 2.24$$
Solve for $p$:
$$p = 2.24 - 0.6$$
$$p = 1.64$$

Answer: $p = 1.64 \text{ m}$

Key techniques

  • Draw a clear force diagram
  • Resolve forces vertically (sum = 0)
  • Take moments about a convenient point (sum = 0)
  • Solve simultaneous equations

Common pitfalls

  • Failing to draw a clear force diagram, leading to missed forces or incorrect distances.
  • Errors in calculating perpendicular distances for moments, especially when taking moments about a point other than an end of the beam.
  • Incorrectly solving the simultaneous equations for forces and moments, or making algebraic slips when substituting.
