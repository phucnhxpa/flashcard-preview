Calculate unknown force components and a constant using vector forces, equilibrium, and Newton's second law.

3.	 [In this question i and j are perpendicular horizontal unit vectors.]
	
Three forces, F1, F2 and F3, are given by
F1 = (5i + 2j) N F2 = (–3i + j) N F3 = (ai + bj) N
	
where a and b are constants.
	
The forces F1, F2 and F3 act on a particle P of mass 4 kg.  
	
Given that P rests in equilibrium on a smooth horizontal surface under the action of these 
three forces,
	
(a)	 find the size of the angle between the direction of F3 and the direction of – j.
(4)
	
The force F3 is now removed and replaced by the force F4 given by F4 = λ (i + 3j) N, 
where λ is a positive constant.
	
When the three forces F1, F2 and F4 act on P,  the acceleration of P has magnitude 
3.25 m s –2 
	
(b)	 Find the value of  λ.
(5)

Worked solution

Part (a): Finding the angle of F3 in equilibrium

Thought Process:
We recognize that when a particle is in equilibrium, the net (resultant) force acting on it is zero. This means the sum of all force vectors must be the zero vector. We'll sum the i-components and j-components separately and set each sum to zero to find the unknown constants $a$ and $b$ for $\mathbf{F_3}$. Once $\mathbf{F_3}$ is fully defined, we need to find the angle it makes with the direction of $-\mathbf{j}$. The direction of $-\mathbf{j}$ is along the negative y-axis, or the vector $(0, -1)$. A robust way to find the angle between two vectors is using the dot product formula: $\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B} = |\mathbf{A}| |\mathbf{B}| \cos \theta$.

Working:


  1. Calculate the resultant force $\mathbf{F_R}$:


The sum of the forces is $\mathbf{F_1} + \mathbf{F_2} + \mathbf{F_3}$.
$$ \mathbf{F_R} = (5\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j}) + (-3\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j}) + (a\mathbf{i} + b\mathbf{j}) $$
$$ \mathbf{F_R} = (5 - 3 + a)\mathbf{i} + (2 + 1 + b)\mathbf{j} $$
$$ \mathbf{F_R} = (2 + a)\mathbf{i} + (3 + b)\mathbf{j} $$

  1. Apply equilibrium condition:
Since the particle is in equilibrium, $\mathbf{F_R} = \mathbf{0}$. Equating i-components: $2 + a = 0 \Rightarrow a = -2$ Equating j-components: $3 + b = 0 \Rightarrow b = -3$ So, the force $\mathbf{F_3}$ is $\mathbf{F_3} = (-2\mathbf{i} - 3\mathbf{j})$ N.
  1. Find the angle between $\mathbf{F_3}$ and $-\mathbf{j}$:
Let $\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{F_3} = -2\mathbf{i} - 3\mathbf{j}$ and $\mathbf{B} = -\mathbf{j} = 0\mathbf{i} - 1\mathbf{j}$. Calculate the dot product $\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B}$: $$ \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B} = (-2)(0) + (-3)(-1) = 0 + 3 = 3 $$ Calculate the magnitudes $|\mathbf{A}|$ and $|\mathbf{B}|$: $$ |\mathbf{A}| = \sqrt{(-2)^2 + (-3)^2} = \sqrt{4 + 9} = \sqrt{13} $$ $$ |\mathbf{B}| = \sqrt{(0)^2 + (-1)^2} = \sqrt{0 + 1} = 1 $$ Using the dot product formula $\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B} = |\mathbf{A}| |\mathbf{B}| \cos \theta$: $$ 3 = \sqrt{13} \cdot 1 \cdot \cos \theta $$ $$ \cos \theta = \frac{3}{\sqrt{13}} $$ $$ \theta = \arccos\left(\frac{3}{\sqrt{13}}\right) \approx 33.6900...^\circ $$ Rounding to 1 decimal place: $$ \theta \approx 33.7^\circ $$
Answer: The size of the angle between $\mathbf{F_3}$ and $-\mathbf{j}$ is $33.7^\circ$ (1 d.p.).

Part (b): Finding the value of $\lambda$ using Newton's Second Law

Thought Process:
When $\mathbf{F_3}$ is replaced by $\mathbf{F_4}$, the particle is no longer in equilibrium, and it accelerates. This requires applying Newton's Second Law, $\mathbf{F_{net}} = m\mathbf{a}$. We'll find the new resultant force $\mathbf{F_{net}}$ in terms of $\lambda$, then calculate its magnitude. We are given the magnitude of acceleration, $a = 3.25 \text{ m s}^{-2}$, and the mass $m = 4 \text{ kg}$. So, we can find the magnitude of the resultant force, $|\mathbf{F_{net}}| = ma$. Equating this to the calculated magnitude of the resultant force (in terms of $\lambda$) will allow us to solve for $\lambda$. We must remember that $\lambda$ is a positive constant.

Working:


  1. Calculate the new resultant force $\mathbf{F_{net}}$:


The new forces are $\mathbf{F_1}$, $\mathbf{F_2}$, and $\mathbf{F_4}$.
$$ \mathbf{F_{net}} = \mathbf{F_1} + \mathbf{F_2} + \mathbf{F_4} $$
$$ \mathbf{F_{net}} = (5\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j}) + (-3\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j}) + \lambda(\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j}) $$
$$ \mathbf{F_{net}} = (5 - 3 + \lambda)\mathbf{i} + (2 + 1 + 3\lambda)\mathbf{j} $$
$$ \mathbf{F_{net}} = (2 + \lambda)\mathbf{i} + (3 + 3\lambda)\mathbf{j} $$

  1. Apply Newton's Second Law:
We know that $\mathbf{F_{net}} = m\mathbf{a}$. The magnitude of the acceleration is $a = 3.25 \text{ m s}^{-2}$ and the mass is $m = 4 \text{ kg}$. So, the magnitude of the resultant force is: $$ |\mathbf{F_{net}}| = ma = 4 \times 3.25 = 13 \text{ N} $$ Now, express the magnitude of $\mathbf{F_{net}}$ in terms of $\lambda$: $$ |\mathbf{F_{net}}| = \sqrt{(2 + \lambda)^2 + (3 + 3\lambda)^2} $$ Equating the two expressions for $|\mathbf{F_{net}}|$: $$ \sqrt{(2 + \lambda)^2 + (3 + 3\lambda)^2} = 13 $$ Square both sides to eliminate the square root: $$ (2 + \lambda)^2 + (3 + 3\lambda)^2 = 13^2 $$ $$ (4 + 4\lambda + \lambda^2) + (9 + 18\lambda + 9\lambda^2) = 169 $$ Combine like terms: $$ 10\lambda^2 + 22\lambda + 13 = 169 $$ Rearrange into a quadratic equation: $$ 10\lambda^2 + 22\lambda - 156 = 0 $$ Divide by 2 to simplify: $$ 5\lambda^2 + 11\lambda - 78 = 0 $$
  1. Solve the quadratic equation for $\lambda$:
Using the quadratic formula $\lambda = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$: $$ \lambda = \frac{-11 \pm \sqrt{11^2 - 4(5)(-78)}}{2(5)} $$ $$ \lambda = \frac{-11 \pm \sqrt{121 + 1560}}{10} $$ $$ \lambda = \frac{-11 \pm \sqrt{1681}}{10} $$ $$ \lambda = \frac{-11 \pm 41}{10} $$ Two possible values for $\lambda$: $$ \lambda_1 = \frac{-11 + 41}{10} = \frac{30}{10} = 3 $$ $$ \lambda_2 = \frac{-11 - 41}{10} = \frac{-52}{10} = -5.2 $$ The question states that $\lambda$ is a positive constant. Therefore, $\lambda = 3$.
Answer: The value of $\lambda$ is $3$.

Key techniques

  • Sum vector components
  • Apply $\sum \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$
  • Apply $\sum \mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$
  • Calculate vector magnitude
  • Dot product for angle between vectors

Common pitfalls

  • Errors in vector addition, especially with signs, or mixing i and j components.
  • Algebraic mistakes when expanding squared brackets, e.g., $(A+B)^2 = A^2+B^2$ instead of $A^2+2AB+B^2$.
  • Forgetting to apply the condition (e.g., $\lambda > 0$) when solving equations that yield multiple solutions.
