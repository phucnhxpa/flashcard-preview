Worked solution

Thought Process

We start by identifying that this is an equilibrium problem involving a particle on a rough horizontal surface. This immediately tells us we'll need to resolve forces horizontally and vertically and use the limiting friction equation, $F = \mu R$. The key to solving for $T$ is to correctly determine the direction of friction.

The question asks for the *smallest* value of $T$ for which equilibrium is maintained. This implies that if $T$ were any smaller, the parcel would start to move to the left, under the influence of the 200 N force's horizontal component, which is acting to the left. Therefore, to *prevent* this motion, the friction force must be acting to the *right*, opposing the impending motion. Since we are finding the *smallest* $T$ to *maintain* equilibrium, the parcel is on the point of slipping, meaning friction is at its maximum value, $F_{max} = \mu R$.

Working

  1. Identify and Sketch Forces: The forces acting on the parcel are:
  • Weight ($mg$) acting vertically downwards.
  • Normal reaction ($R$) acting vertically upwards from the floor.
  • 200 N force acting 15° *below* the horizontal (to the left).
  • $T$ N force acting 25° *above* the horizontal (to the right).
  • Friction ($F$) acting horizontally. As discussed, for the smallest $T$ to prevent motion to the left, friction acts to the *right*.
  1. Resolve forces vertically for equilibrium (R$\uparrow$ = R$\downarrow$):
We sum the vertical components of forces. Upwards forces balance downwards forces. The vertical component of $T$ is $T \sin(25^\circ)$ (upwards). The vertical component of the 200 N force is $200 \sin(15^\circ)$ (downwards). The weight is $mg = 20(9.8) = 196$ N (downwards). $$R + T \sin(25^\circ) = mg + 200 \sin(15^\circ)$$ $$R + T \sin(25^\circ) = 196 + 200 \sin(15^\circ)$$ Rearranging to express $R$: $$R = 196 + 200 \sin(15^\circ) - T \sin(25^\circ) \quad (1)$$
  1. Resolve forces horizontally for equilibrium (F$_{right}$ = F$_{left}$):
We sum the horizontal components of forces. Rightward forces balance leftward forces. The horizontal component of $T$ is $T \cos(25^\circ)$ (rightwards). The horizontal component of the 200 N force is $200 \cos(15^\circ)$ (leftwards). The friction force $F$ acts to the right, and since the parcel is on the point of slipping, $F = \mu R$. $$T \cos(25^\circ) + F = 200 \cos(15^\circ)$$ Substitute $F = \mu R$: $$T \cos(25^\circ) + \mu R = 200 \cos(15^\circ) \quad (2)$$
  1. Substitute equation (1) into equation (2):
Substitute the expression for $R$ from (1) into (2). Given $\mu = 0.3$. $$T \cos(25^\circ) + 0.3 (196 + 200 \sin(15^\circ) - T \sin(25^\circ)) = 200 \cos(15^\circ)$$
  1. Expand and collect terms involving $T$:
$$T \cos(25^\circ) + 0.3(196) + 0.3(200 \sin(15^\circ)) - 0.3 T \sin(25^\circ) = 200 \cos(15^\circ)$$ Group terms with $T$ on one side and constants on the other: $$T (\cos(25^\circ) - 0.3 \sin(25^\circ)) = 200 \cos(15^\circ) - 0.3(196) - 0.3(200 \sin(15^\circ))$$ $$T (\cos(25^\circ) - 0.3 \sin(25^\circ)) = 200 \cos(15^\circ) - 58.8 - 60 \sin(15^\circ)$$
  1. Calculate the numerical values:
Using a calculator for the trigonometric functions: Left-hand side coefficient for $T$: $\cos(25^\circ) - 0.3 \sin(25^\circ) \approx 0.9063 - 0.3(0.4226) = 0.9063 - 0.1268 = 0.7795$ Right-hand side: $200 \cos(15^\circ) - 58.8 - 60 \sin(15^\circ) \approx 200(0.9659) - 58.8 - 60(0.2588)$ $\approx 193.18 - 58.8 - 15.53 = 118.85$

So, we have:
$$T (0.7795) = 118.85$$
$$T = \frac{118.85}{0.7795} \approx 152.47$$

  1. State the final answer to an appropriate precision (3 significant figures):
$T \approx 152 \text{ N}$
Answer: $T = 152 \text{ N}$

Key techniques

  • draw force diagram
  • resolve forces horizontally and vertically
  • apply $F=\mu R$
  • solve simultaneous equations

Common pitfalls

  • Incorrectly determining the direction of the friction force (e.g., if 'smallest T' means preventing motion to the left, friction acts right).
  • Errors in resolving forces, such as using sine for the horizontal component or cosine for the vertical component.
  • Sign errors when setting up the equilibrium equations, especially for forces acting downwards or leftwards.
