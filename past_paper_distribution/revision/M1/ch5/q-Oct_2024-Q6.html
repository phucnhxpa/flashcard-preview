Worked solution

Hypothetical Question: A particle $P$ of mass $2 \text{ kg}$ rests on a rough plane inclined at $15^\circ$ to the horizontal. The coefficient of friction between $P$ and the plane is $0.3$. A light inextensible string is attached to $P$. The string lies in a line of greatest slope of the plane.

(a) Find the magnitude of the normal reaction between $P$ and the plane. (3 marks)
(b) The string is slack. Show that $P$ remains at rest on the plane. (4 marks)
(c) Find the range of possible values for the tension in the string for which $P$ remains in equilibrium. (5 marks)

---

Thought Process:
This question involves a particle on a rough inclined plane, which immediately signals we'll need to use principles of forces in equilibrium and friction. It's crucial to visualize all forces acting on the particle: its weight, the normal reaction from the plane, the tension from the string (if present), and the friction force.

For an inclined plane, it's always best to resolve forces perpendicular and parallel to the plane. This simplifies the components of the normal reaction and friction, and often the tension. The weight of the particle ($mg$) always acts vertically downwards, so its components will be $mg \cos\theta$ perpendicular to the plane and $mg \sin\theta$ parallel to the plane, where $\theta$ is the angle of inclination.

Friction is key: it always opposes *impending* motion. If the particle tends to slide down, friction acts up the plane. If the particle tends to slide up, friction acts down the plane. At the point of *limiting equilibrium* (when the particle is on the point of moving), the friction force is at its maximum value, $F_{max} = \mu R$, where $\mu$ is the coefficient of friction and $R$ is the normal reaction.

Part (a): Normal Reaction
We need to find the normal reaction, $R$. The normal reaction is perpendicular to the plane. Therefore, we should resolve forces perpendicular to the plane. The forces perpendicular to the plane are the normal reaction $R$ (outwards from the plane) and the component of the weight $mg\cos\theta$ (into the plane). Since the particle is in equilibrium, these forces must balance.

Working for Part (a):
Let $m = 2\text{ kg}$, $g = 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$, $\theta = 15^\circ$.

Resolving forces perpendicular to the plane (taking upwards from the plane as positive):
$$R - mg\cos\theta = 0$$
$$R = mg\cos\theta$$
Substitute the given values:
$$R = (2)(9.8)\cos 15^\circ$$
$$R = 19.6 \cos 15^\circ$$
$$R \approx 18.9324\dots \text{ N}$$
Rounding to 3 significant figures:
$$R = 18.9\text{ N}$$

Answer (a): The magnitude of the normal reaction is $18.9\text{ N}$.

---

Thought Process for Part (b): String Slack, Show it Stays at Rest
"String is slack" means the tension $T=0$. To show the particle remains at rest, we need to compare the component of weight trying to pull it down the plane with the maximum static friction available to prevent it from sliding down. If the downward component of weight is less than or equal to the maximum friction, the particle will remain at rest.

Working for Part (b):
When the string is slack ($T=0$), the only forces acting parallel to the plane are the component of weight acting down the plane, $mg\sin\theta$, and the friction force $F$ acting up the plane (opposing the tendency to slide down).

Calculate the component of weight acting down the plane:
$$W_\text{down} = mg\sin\theta = (2)(9.8)\sin 15^\circ$$
$$W_\text{down} = 19.6 \sin 15^\circ$$
$$W_\text{down} \approx 5.0782\dots \text{ N}$$

Calculate the maximum possible static friction, $F_{max}$, using the normal reaction from part (a) and the coefficient of friction $\mu = 0.3$.
$$F_{max} = \mu R = 0.3 \times (18.9324\dots)$$
$$F_{max} \approx 5.6797\dots \text{ N}$$

Now, compare $W_\text{down}$ with $F_{max}$:
Since $W_\text{down} (5.08\text{ N}) < F_{max} (5.68\text{ N})$, the component of the weight down the plane is less than the maximum friction force that the plane can provide to oppose this motion. Therefore, the particle will not slide down and will remain at rest.

Answer (b): Since $mg\sin\theta < \mu R$, the particle remains at rest on the plane.

---

Thought Process for Part (c): Range of Tension for Equilibrium
For the particle to remain in equilibrium, the tension must be within a certain range. This range is bounded by two limiting cases:


  1. The particle is on the point of moving *up* the plane. In this case, friction acts *down* the plane.

  2. The particle is on the point of moving *down* the plane. In this case, friction acts *up* the plane.

We will calculate the tension for each of these limiting cases. Let $T_{up}$ be the tension when it's about to move up, and $T_{down}$ be the tension when it's about to move down.

Working for Part (c):
We use the values calculated in parts (a) and (b): $R \approx 18.9324\text{ N}$, $mg\sin\theta \approx 5.0782\text{ N}$, $F_{max} \approx 5.6797\text{ N}$.

Case 1: Particle on the point of moving UP the plane.
Friction acts DOWN the plane, opposing the upward motion. Resolving forces parallel to the plane (taking up the plane as positive):
$$T_{up} - mg\sin\theta - F_{max} = 0$$
$$T_{up} = mg\sin\theta + F_{max}$$
$$T_{up} = 5.0782\dots + 5.6797\dots$$
$$T_{up} \approx 10.7579\dots \text{ N}$$

Case 2: Particle on the point of moving DOWN the plane.
Friction acts UP the plane, opposing the downward motion. Resolving forces parallel to the plane (taking up the plane as positive):
$$T_{down} - mg\sin\theta + F_{max} = 0$$
$$T_{down} = mg\sin\theta - F_{max}$$
$$T_{down} = 5.0782\dots - 5.6797\dots$$
$$T_{down} \approx -0.6015\dots \text{ N}$$

A negative tension means that the string would need to push the particle down the plane. Since a string can only pull (i.e., tension must be non-negative), this indicates that even with zero tension, the particle would not slide down because the component of weight down the plane ($mg\sin\theta$) is less than the maximum static friction ($F_{max}$). Therefore, the minimum tension required for equilibrium is $0\text{ N}$.

So the range of tension is from $0\text{ N}$ up to $T_{up}$.

Rounding to 3 significant figures:
$T_{up} = 10.8\text{ N}$.

The range of possible values for the tension is $0 \le T \le 10.8\text{ N}$.

Answer (c): The range of possible values for the tension in the string is $[0, 10.8]\text{ N}$.

Key techniques

  • draw force diagram
  • resolve perpendicular to plane
  • resolve parallel to plane
  • apply limiting friction $F = \mu R$
  • identify direction of friction

Common pitfalls

  • Incorrectly resolving the weight vector ($mg$) into components ($mg\sin\theta$ and $mg\cos\theta$). A common error is swapping sine and cosine.
  • Incorrectly identifying the direction of the friction force. Friction always opposes the *tendency* of motion.
  • Forgetting to consider both limiting cases (impending motion up and impending motion down) when asked for a *range* of forces for equilibrium, or misinterpreting a negative tension value as a physical push from the string rather than indicating zero tension is sufficient.
