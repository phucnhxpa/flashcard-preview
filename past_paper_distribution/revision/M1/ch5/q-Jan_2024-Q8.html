Worked solution

Let's break this down into two parts, focusing on the thought process and then the working.

Part (a): Find the coefficient of friction $\mu$

Thought Process:
We are given a system in equilibrium, where particle P is on an inclined plane and connected to particle Q hanging freely. P is on the point of moving *up* the plane. This tells us a few things:


  1. Since the system is in equilibrium, the net force on both P and Q is zero.

  2. We need to find the coefficient of friction, $\mu$. The formula relating friction, normal reaction, and $\mu$ is $F = \mu R$.

  3. To find $\mu$, we first need to find the maximum friction force $F$ and the normal reaction force $R$.

  4. The normal reaction $R$ is found by resolving forces perpendicular to the inclined plane on particle P, as there is no acceleration in this direction.

  5. The friction force $F$ is found by resolving forces parallel to the inclined plane on particle P. We must remember that since P is on the point of moving *up* the plane, the friction will oppose this tendency, meaning friction acts *down* the plane.

  6. The tension $T$ in the string connects P and Q. We can find $T$ by first analyzing particle Q, which is simpler as it's only under gravity and tension.

  7. We are given $\sin \alpha = \frac{5}{13}$. We'll need $\cos \alpha$ as well, which can be found using the identity $\sin^2 \alpha + \cos^2 \alpha = 1$.

Working:
Let $m$ be the mass of P. The mass of Q is $0.5m$.

First, find $\cos \alpha$:
$$\cos \alpha = \sqrt{1 - \sin^2 \alpha} = \sqrt{1 - \left(\frac{5}{13}\right)^2} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{25}{169}} = \sqrt{\frac{144}{169}} = \frac{12}{13}$$

For particle Q (hanging freely):
Forces are tension $T$ upwards and weight $0.5mg$ downwards. Since Q is in equilibrium:
$$T - 0.5mg = 0$$
$$T = 0.5mg$$

For particle P (on the inclined plane):
Forces acting on P are: weight $mg$, normal reaction $R$, tension $T$, and friction $F$. We resolve the weight into components parallel ($mg \sin \alpha$) and perpendicular ($mg \cos \alpha$) to the plane.

Resolve forces perpendicular to the plane (no acceleration):
$$R - mg \cos \alpha = 0$$
$$R = mg \cos \alpha = mg \left(\frac{12}{13}\right)$$

Resolve forces parallel to the plane. Since P is on the point of moving *up* the plane, friction $F$ acts *down* the plane, opposing the upward tendency. Taking the upward direction along the plane as positive:
$$T - mg \sin \alpha - F = 0$$
$$F = T - mg \sin \alpha$$

Substitute $T = 0.5mg$ and $\sin \alpha = \frac{5}{13}$:
$$F = 0.5mg - mg \left(\frac{5}{13}\right)$$
$$F = mg \left(0.5 - \frac{5}{13}\right) = mg \left(\frac{1}{2} - \frac{5}{13}\right) = mg \left(\frac{13-10}{26}\right) = mg \left(\frac{3}{26}\right)$$

Now, use the friction law $F = \mu R$:
$$\mu R = mg \left(\frac{3}{26}\right)$$
Substitute $R = mg \left(\frac{12}{13}\right)$:
$$\mu \left(mg \frac{12}{13}\right) = mg \left(\frac{3}{26}\right)$$
Divide by $mg$ (since $m \neq 0$ and $g \neq 0$):
$$\mu \frac{12}{13} = \frac{3}{26}$$
$$\mu = \frac{3}{26} \times \frac{13}{12} = \frac{3}{2 \times 12} = \frac{3}{24} = \frac{1}{8}$$

Part (b): Find the speed of P after sliding 0.8 m

Thought Process:
Particle Q is removed, so there is no tension in the string. Particle P is now free to slide down the plane. We need to find its speed after a certain distance, which suggests using SUVAT equations. To use SUVAT, we first need to find the acceleration of P.


  1. We'll use Newton's second law, $F=ma$, for particle P parallel to the plane.

  2. The forces acting parallel to the plane are the component of weight $mg \sin \alpha$ (down the plane) and friction $F$. Since P is now sliding *down* the plane, friction will oppose this motion, so $F$ acts *up* the plane.

  3. Once the acceleration $a$ is found, we can use an appropriate SUVAT equation, specifically $V^2 = u^2 + 2as$, as we know the initial velocity, displacement, and acceleration.

Working:
With Q removed, P accelerates down the plane. The tension $T=0$.

Normal reaction $R$ remains the same as in part (a):
$$R = mg \cos \alpha = mg \left(\frac{12}{13}\right)$$

Friction force $F = \mu R$. Since P is moving down the plane, friction acts *up* the plane:
$$F = \frac{1}{8} R = \frac{1}{8} \left(mg \frac{12}{13}\right) = mg \frac{12}{104} = mg \frac{3}{26}$$

Now apply Newton's second law ($F=ma$) for particle P parallel to the plane. Taking the downward direction along the plane as positive:
$$mg \sin \alpha - F = ma$$
Substitute values for $\sin \alpha$ and $F$:
$$mg \left(\frac{5}{13}\right) - mg \left(\frac{3}{26}\right) = ma$$
Divide by $m$:
$$g \left(\frac{5}{13}\right) - g \left(\frac{3}{26}\right) = a$$
$$a = g \left(\frac{10}{26} - \frac{3}{26}\right) = g \left(\frac{7}{26}\right)$$

Now, use SUVAT equations to find the final speed $V$. We have:
$u = 0$ (starts from rest)
$a = \frac{7g}{26}$
$s = 0.8$ m
$V = ?$

Using $V^2 = u^2 + 2as$:
$$V^2 = (0)^2 + 2 \left(\frac{7g}{26}\right) (0.8)$$
$$V^2 = \frac{14g}{26} (0.8) = \frac{7g}{13} (0.8)$$
Substitute $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$:
$$V^2 = \frac{7 \times 9.8}{13} \times 0.8 = \frac{54.88}{13} \approx 4.221538$$
$$V = \sqrt{4.221538} \approx 2.0546 \text{ m/s}$$

Rounding to 3 significant figures:

Answer:
(a) $\mu = \frac{1}{8}$ (or 0.125)
(b) $V = 2.05 \text{ m/s}$

Key techniques

  • draw force diagrams (mental or sketch)
  • resolve forces parallel and perpendicular to an inclined plane
  • apply $F = \mu R$ for limiting friction
  • apply Newton's second law ($F=ma$)
  • select and apply appropriate SUVAT equations

Common pitfalls

  • Incorrect direction of friction: Always ensure friction opposes the direction of *imminent* or *actual* motion. For 'on the point of moving up', friction is down; for 'sliding down', friction is up.
  • Sign errors when resolving forces: Be consistent with the chosen positive direction (e.g., up the slope as positive, or down the slope as positive).
  • Mixing up $\sin \alpha$ and $\cos \alpha$ components: Remember that $mg \sin \alpha$ is parallel to the slope and $mg \cos \alpha$ is perpendicular to the slope for a mass on an incline.
