Constant Velocity: Relative Position & Bearings
6× 10-13m
Two particles, A and B, move with constant velocities. You're given initial positions and velocities. The question asks for the position of one particle relative to another at a certain time, or the t

A: General Position Vector & Relative Vector Method: This is the standard and most reliable method for all such problems.
Position vector at time t: $\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}t$ (MEMORIZE)
Relative position of B from A: $\vec{AB} = \mathbf{r}_B - \mathbf{r}_A$ (MEMORIZE)
Magnitude (for speed/distance): $|x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j}| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$ (MEMORIZE)
  • Incorrectly converting a vector's angle to a bearing (e.g., measuring anti-clockwise from the positive i-axis instead of clockwise from the North (j-axis)).
  • Misinterpreting relative position conditions, e.g., for 'B is south-west of A', setting $\vec{AB} = (-k, -k)$ but forgetting to check that k must be positive.
  • Confusing $\vec{AB}$ with $\vec{BA}$. Remember $\vec{AB}$ means 'the position of B relative to A'.
Minimum Distance Between Moving Particles
2× 9-12m
Two particles move with constant velocities. You are asked to find the shortest distance between them during their motion, and sometimes the time when this occurs.
Jun 2023 Q8Jan 2021 Q6Jan 2024 Q7

A: Minimise Distance-Squared using Differentiation: This is a robust and universally applicable calculus method.
B: Minimise Distance-Squared by Completing the Square: A good alternative, especially if the quadratic for $D^2$ has simple coefficients.
Position vector: $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}t$ (MEMORIZE)
Relative position: $\vec{AB} = \mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a}$ (MEMORIZE)
Condition for minimum of quadratic $ax^2+bx+c$: $x = -b/2a$ (MEMORIZE)
  • Forgetting to take the square root at the end to report the distance $D$, not $D^2$.
  • Making algebraic errors when expanding the brackets for the magnitude squared, e.g. $(p - qt)^2$.
  • Confusing the time of minimum distance with the minimum distance itself.
Collision / Interception Problems
2× 13-15m
Two particles move with given velocities. The question asks if they collide, or asks you to find an unknown component of a velocity or position vector for a collision to occur.
Oct 2023 Q6Jun 2024 Q7

A: Equate Position Vectors: This is the only valid method for determining a collision.
Collision condition: $\mathbf{r}_A(t) = \mathbf{r}_B(t)$ for some $t > 0$ (MEMORIZE)
Position vector: $\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}t$ (MEMORIZE)
  • Assuming that if the paths cross, the particles collide. You must show they are at the crossing point at the same time.
  • Incorrectly forming the initial position vector, e.g., missing out $\mathbf{r}_0$.
  • Making sign errors when rearranging the vector equations to solve for $t$.
Constant Acceleration Kinematics (Vector SUVAT)
2× 6-9m
A particle moves with a constant acceleration, which is given as a vector. You'll be given initial velocity and asked to find the velocity or position at a later time, or vice-versa.
Jun 2021 Q5Jun 2022 Q6Jan 2022 Q6Jan 2023 Q3Jun 2023 Q8Oct 2021 Q8Oct 2023 Q4Oct 2024 Q3
 constant accelerationinitial velocityfinal velocityspeed
A: Apply SUVAT Equations in Vector Form: Standard method for any problem involving constant acceleration in 2D.
$\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{a}t$ (MEMORIZE vector form)
$\mathbf{s} = \mathbf{u}t + \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{a}t^2$ (MEMORIZE vector form)
$\mathbf{s} = \frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v})t$ (MEMORIZE vector form)
  • Confusing velocity (a vector) with speed (a scalar magnitude). Always calculate the magnitude at the end if speed is requested.
  • Mistaking a direction vector for a velocity vector. If a particle is 'moving in the direction of $2\mathbf{i} - \mathbf{j}$', its velocity is $\mathbf{v} = k(2\mathbf{i} - \mathbf{j})$ for some scalar $k > 0$.
Dynamics with Vectors (F=ma)
2× 8-12m
One or more forces, given in vector form, act on a particle of a given mass. You'll need to find the resultant force, the acceleration, and then often use vector kinematics to find subsequent velocity
Jan 2024 Q4Jan 2022 Q6Jan 2020 Q6Jan 2021 Q6Jun 2024 Q2
 forcemassresultant forceacts on a particleacceleration
A: Resultant Force & Newton's Second Law: The standard approach when forces and motion are described by vectors.
Newton's Second Law (vector form): $\mathbf{F}_{resultant} = m\mathbf{a}$ (formula booklet)
Vector Kinematics: $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{a}t$ (MEMORIZE vector form)
  • Using the resultant force $\mathbf{R}$ in a kinematics equation instead of the acceleration $\mathbf{a}$. Always remember to divide by mass.
  • Making errors in vector addition, especially with negative components.
  • If a force has unknown magnitude 'P' but known direction, write it as a magnitude multiplied by a unit vector, or resolve it into components involving P.

A: Graphical Method (Area & Gradient): Almost always, especially when finding total distance or when a sketch is explicitly requested.
B: Segmented SUVAT Equations: As a primary method if no graph is needed, or as a check for the graphical method. Good for finding specific velocities or times within a single stage.
Distance = Area under speed-time graph (MEMORIZE)
Acceleration = Gradient of speed-time graph (MEMORIZE)
Area of Trapezium = $\frac{1}{2}(a+b)h$ (MEMORIZE)

  • Incorrectly identifying the parallel sides for the area of a trapezium formula.
  • Sketching a distance-time graph (curves for acceleration) instead of a speed-time graph (straight lines for constant acceleration).

5× 6-12m
A particle (ball, stone) is thrown, projected, or dropped vertically. Questions ask for maximum height, time to hit the ground, speed at a certain point, or when/where two particles meet.

A: Single Application of SUVAT: For most standard problems involving one particle moving between two points in time.
B: Splitting the Motion (Up and Down): When finding total time of flight or final speed for a particle that goes up and returns down. It can feel more intuitive for some students.
$v = u + at$ (formula booklet)
$s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$ (formula booklet)
$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ (formula booklet)
At greatest height, vertical velocity $v=0$ (MEMORIZE)
  • Inconsistent sign convention: e.g., taking initial velocity `u` as positive upwards but acceleration `a` as `+9.8`.
  • Confusing displacement `s` (a vector) with distance travelled (a scalar).
  • Incorrectly stating speed (a scalar) is negative. If you find `v = -15`, the velocity is -15 m/s, but the speed is 15 m/s.

Kinematics with Forces (F=ma → SUVAT)
3× 8-14m
A problem describing forces acting on a massive object (e.g., engine thrust, friction, resistance) and then asking kinematic questions (distance, time, speed). The acceleration is not given directly.
Jun 2023 Q3Jun 2024 Q5Jan 2024 Q3Jan 2023 Q5

A: Two-Step (Dynamics then Kinematics): This is the universal method for these problems.
$F_{net} = ma$ (Newton's Second Law) (MEMORIZE)
The five standard SUVAT equations (formula booklet)
  • Getting the signs wrong in the F=ma equation (e.g., adding resistance instead of subtracting it from the driving force).
  • Forgetting to include the component of weight ($mg \sin \theta$) if the motion is on an inclined plane.
  • Using `a=g` in a vertical motion problem when other forces (like engine thrust or air resistance) are present.
Kinematics with Calculus (Variable Acceleration)
1× 13m
The acceleration, velocity, or displacement of a particle is given as a function of time, `t` (e.g., $v = 3t^2 - 5t + 2$). SUVAT equations cannot be used.
Oct 2024 Q4Jun 2021 Q2
 velocity $v(t) = ...$acceleration $a(t) = ...$find an expression forinstantaneously at restinitial displacement
A: Differentiation and Integration: This is the only method for variable acceleration problems.
$v = \frac{ds}{dt}$ (MEMORIZE)
$a = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2s}{dt^2}$ (MEMORIZE)
$s = \int v \, dt$ (MEMORIZE)
$v = \int a \, dt$ (MEMORIZE)
  • Forgetting the constant of integration `+ C` after integrating.
  • Applying SUVAT equations to a problem where acceleration is clearly not constant.
  • Calculating displacement (`s(final) - s(initial)`) when asked for total distance travelled, and failing to account for a change in direction.

Direct Collisions, Momentum & Impulse
14× 4-10m
Two or more particles (A, B) are moving along the same straight line and collide. You are given initial velocities and masses, and asked to find final velocities, impulse, or loss in kinetic energy.
Oct 2023 Q3Jan 2019 Q1Jan 2022 Q2Jan 2020 Q1Jun 2023 Q1Jan 2019 Q3Jan 2021 Q2Jan 2023 Q2Jan 2024 Q2Jun 2021 Q1
 collisionimpulsemomentumcoalescedirect impactparticles collide
The Standard Collision Algorithm: This is the universal method for all 1D collision problems.
$p = mv$ (Definition of momentum) (MEMORIZE)
$\sum m_i u_i = \sum m_i v_i$ (Conservation of Linear Momentum) (formula booklet)
$I = mv - mu$ (Impulse-Momentum Principle) (formula booklet)
  • **Sign Errors:** Forgetting to assign negative signs to velocities in the opposite direction to your defined positive direction.
  • **Impulse Confusion:** Confusing the impulse *on particle A* with the impulse *on particle B*. They are equal and opposite.
  • **Units:** Forgetting to convert mass from grams to kilograms before calculation.
  • **Speed vs. Velocity:** Answering with a negative velocity when the question asks for final speed (which must be positive).

  • **Pulley Forces:** When asked for force on the pulley, remember it's the vector sum of the two tension forces acting on it.
N2L: Connected Particles (Horizontal & Vertical Motion)
4× 7-18m
A classic pulley system. One particle rests on a smooth or rough horizontal table, connected by a string over a pulley at the edge of the table to a second, hanging particle.

A: Separate FBDs and Simultaneous Equations: Always works. The most reliable method, especially if you need to find tension.

N2L: Single Particle on an Inclined Plane
3× 6-15m
A single particle or object is on a rough or smooth inclined plane. It might be held in equilibrium, on the point of sliding, or accelerating under various forces. Often combined with SUVAT.

Resolve Parallel and Perpendicular to the Plane: This is the standard and only method for this question type.
$\vec{F}_{net} = m\vec{a}$ (Newton's Second Law) (formula booklet)
$F_f \le \mu R$ (Friction Model) (formula booklet)
$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$, etc. (SUVAT equations) (formula booklet)

  • **Normal Reaction:** Forgetting that $R$ changes if an applied force has a component perpendicular to the plane.
N2L: Lift / Apparent Weight Problems
2× 6-7m

Oct 2022 Q4Jan 2023 Q7

Apply N2L to the Object or the System: This is the standard approach for all lift problems.
$\vec{F}_{net} = m\vec{a}$ (Newton's Second Law) (formula booklet)
$W = mg$ (Weight) (formula booklet)
Apparent Weight (Accelerating Up): $R = m(g+a)$
Apparent Weight (Accelerating Down): $R = m(g-a)$
  • **Sign Errors:** The most common mistake. Using $mg - R = ma$ when accelerating up, or mixing up signs for deceleration.
  • **System Choice:** Using the mass of just the person when calculating the tension in the main lift cable, or vice-versa.
  • **Deceleration:** Confusing deceleration with direction. 'Decelerating while moving up' means acceleration is downwards (negative). 'Decelerating while moving down' means acceleration is upwards (positive).

Equilibrium on a Rough Horizontal Surface
5× 9-12m
A particle (box, block, ring) on a flat rough horizontal surface, acted on by one or more forces at angles. Questions ask for the magnitude of friction, the coefficient of friction (mu), or the range

A: Resolve Horizontally and Vertically: Always. This is the standard and most reliable method for these problems.
Resultant Force = 0 (for equilibrium)
F <= mu*R (Friction Law for equilibrium)
F = mu*R (Limiting Friction, for when an object is on the point of moving)
W = mg
  • Assuming friction is limiting (F = mu*R) when the particle is just in equilibrium but not stated to be on the point of slipping.
  • Incorrectly resolving applied force components (e.g., mixing up sin and cos).
  • Forgetting to include the vertical component of an angled applied force when calculating the normal reaction R. R is not always equal to mg.
Equilibrium on a Rough Inclined Plane
3× 8-12m
A particle on a rough inclined plane, held in equilibrium by an external force (often acting horizontally or parallel to the plane). The question usually involves 'impending motion' either up or down
Jan 2022 Q5Oct 2024 Q6Oct 2022 Q3Jan 2021 Q3
 inclined planeangle of inclinationrough surfaceon the point of moving up the planeon the point of sliding downsmallest force to prevent slipping
A: Resolve Parallel and Perpendicular to the Plane: Almost always the most efficient method for inclined plane problems.
Component of weight parallel to plane = mg*sin(theta)
Component of weight perpendicular to plane = mg*cos(theta)
F = mu*R (Limiting Friction)
  • Incorrectly identifying the direction of friction. For the 'smallest' force to prevent slipping down, friction acts UP the plane. For motion UP the plane, friction acts DOWN the plane.
  • Swapping sin and cos when resolving weight. A good mnemonic is 'sine slides', as mg*sin(theta) is the component that causes sliding down the slope.
  • Resolving a horizontal force (P) incorrectly into components. Parallel to plane is P*cos(theta), perpendicular is P*sin(theta).
Equilibrium with Multiple Forces (Smooth/No Friction)
3× 6-10m
A particle (e.g., a knot, a ring) held in place by two or more forces, typically tensions from strings. The geometry of the setup (angles, lengths) is usually given. There is no friction.

A: Resolve Horizontally and Vertically: The standard, most reliable method for any number of forces.
B: Triangle of Forces / Lami's Theorem: Only when there are exactly THREE forces acting on the particle in equilibrium.
Sum Fx = 0, Sum Fy = 0
Lami's Theorem: P/sin(alpha) = Q/sin(beta) = R/sin(gamma) (for 3 forces only)
  • Incorrectly calculating the angles used for resolving, often mixing up angles with the horizontal vs. vertical.
  • Making algebraic errors when solving the simultaneous equations.
  • Attempting to use a triangle of forces when more than three forces are acting.

Hybrid Statics & Dynamics Problems
2× 12m

A: Sequential Analysis (Statics then Dynamics): This is the required structure for these problems.

Resultant of Two Forces (magnitude & bearing)
1× 5-7m
Two forces given in vector or magnitude-direction form. Find the magnitude and direction (often as a bearing) of their resultant. Pure force-resolution, NOT equilibrium.
Jan 2021 Q5
 resultantmagnitude and directionmagnitude and bearingtwo forcesfind the resultant
A: Component method: When forces are given in i-j vector form or as magnitude+angle. Resolve each into x,y components, sum, then take magnitude and bearing.
B: Triangle of forces (geometric): When forces are given as vectors with a known angle between them. Use cosine rule for magnitude, sine rule for direction.
$|\mathbf{R}| = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2}$
Bearing $\theta = $ angle measured clockwise from North
Cosine rule: $|\mathbf{R}|^2 = F_1^2 + F_2^2 + 2 F_1 F_2 \cos\alpha$
  • Confusing bearing (clockwise from N) with standard math angle (anti-clockwise from x-axis).
  • Sign errors when resolving forces: a force at angle alpha below horizontal has component -F sin(alpha) in y.
  • Reporting bearing in the wrong quadrant (must be 0-360°).
