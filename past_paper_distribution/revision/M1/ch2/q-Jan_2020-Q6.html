6.	 A force F is given by F = (10i + j) N.
	
(a)	 Find the exact value of the magnitude of F.

(b)	 Find, in degrees, the size of the angle between the direction of F and the direction of

The resultant of the force F and the force (–15i + aj) N, where a is a constant, is parallel

Worked solution

We recognize this as a vector mechanics problem, testing our ability to manipulate vectors in component form (i.e. $\mathbf{i}$ and $\mathbf{j}$). Part (a) is a direct calculation of magnitude. Part (b) requires finding the angle between two vectors, for which the scalar (dot) product is a robust method. Part (c) involves finding a resultant force and using the concept of parallel vectors, which are scalar multiples of each other.

Part (a)

Thought Process:
The magnitude of a vector $\mathbf{v} = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j}$ is its length, found using Pythagoras' theorem: $|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$. The question asks for the *exact* value, so we must leave the answer in surd form if it's not a perfect square.

Working:
Given the force $\mathbf{F} = (10\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j})$ N.
Its magnitude is calculated as:
$$|\mathbf{F}| = \sqrt{10^2 + 1^2}$$
$$|\mathbf{F}| = \sqrt{100 + 1}$$
$$|\mathbf{F}| = \sqrt{101}$$

Answer: The exact magnitude is $\sqrt{101}$ N.

Part (b)

Thought Process:
To find the angle $\theta$ between two vectors $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$, we use the scalar product formula: $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = |\mathbf{a}| |\mathbf{b}| \cos\theta$. We will define our two vectors, calculate each part of the formula (the dot product and the magnitudes of both vectors), and then rearrange to find $\theta$.

Working:
Let $\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{F} = 10\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j}$ and $\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j}$.
First, calculate the scalar product:
$$\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = (10)(1) + (1)(1) = 11$$
Next, we need the magnitudes. We already have $|\mathbf{a}| = |\mathbf{F}| = \sqrt{101}$ from part (a).
For $\mathbf{b}$:
$$|\mathbf{b}| = \sqrt{1^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{2}$$
Now substitute these values into the scalar product formula:
$$11 = (\sqrt{101})(\sqrt{2}) \cos\theta$$
Rearrange to solve for $\cos\theta$:
$$\cos\theta = \frac{11}{\sqrt{101}\sqrt{2}} = \frac{11}{\sqrt{202}}$$
Finally, find the angle $\theta$:
$$\theta = \arccos\left(\frac{11}{\sqrt{202}}\right)$$
$$\theta \approx 39.289...^{\circ}$$

Answer: The angle is $39.3^{\circ}$ (to 3 s.f.).

Part (c)

Thought Process:
First, find the resultant force $\mathbf{R}$ by adding the two given force vectors. The problem states $\mathbf{R}$ is parallel to, but in the opposite direction to, the vector $(2\mathbf{i} - 3\mathbf{j})$. This means $\mathbf{R}$ must be parallel to the vector $-(2\mathbf{i} - 3\mathbf{j}) = -2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j}$. For two vectors to be parallel, the ratio of their $\mathbf{j}$-component to their $\mathbf{i}$-component must be equal. We will set up and solve this ratio equation.

Working:
The resultant force $\mathbf{R}$ is the sum of $\mathbf{F} = (10\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j})$ and the second force $(-15\mathbf{i} + a\mathbf{j})$.
$$\mathbf{R} = (10\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j}) + (-15\mathbf{i} + a\mathbf{j})$$
$$\mathbf{R} = (10 - 15)\mathbf{i} + (1 + a)\mathbf{j}$$
$$\mathbf{R} = -5\mathbf{i} + (1 + a)\mathbf{j}$$
This resultant $\mathbf{R}$ is parallel to and in the opposite direction of $(2\mathbf{i} - 3\mathbf{j})$. This is equivalent to being parallel to $-(2\mathbf{i} - 3\mathbf{j}) = -2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j}$.
For these vectors to be parallel, the ratio of their components must be equal:
$$\frac{\mathbf{j} \text{ component}}{\mathbf{i} \text{ component}} \text{ must be the same for both vectors. }$$
$$\frac{1+a}{-5} = \frac{3}{-2}$$
Now, solve for $a$. Multiply both sides by -5:
$$1+a = \frac{3}{-2} \times (-5)$$
$$1+a = \frac{15}{2} = 7.5$$
$$a = 7.5 - 1 = 6.5$$

Answer: $a = 6.5$.

Key techniques

  • Use Pythagoras' theorem for vector magnitude
  • Use the scalar product to find the angle between vectors
  • Calculate a resultant vector by addition
  • Apply the condition for parallel vectors (scalar multiples or equal component ratios)

Common pitfalls

  • In part (a), providing a decimal approximation instead of the 'exact value' in surd form as requested.
  • In part (b), mixing up degrees and radians, or using an incorrect formula for the scalar product.
  • In part (c), forgetting that 'opposite direction' means the resultant is a negative scalar multiple, or setting up the ratio of components incorrectly (e.g., upside down).
