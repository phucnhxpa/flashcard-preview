6.	 Two girls, Agatha and Brionie, are roller skating inside a large empty building.  The girls 
are modelled as particles.
	
At time t  =  0, Agatha is at the point with position vector (11i + 11j) m and Brionie is at 
the point with position vector (7i + 16j) m.  The position vectors are given relative to the 
door, O, and i and j are horizontal perpendicular unit vectors.
	
Agatha skates with constant velocity (3i – j) m s–1
	
Brionie skates with constant velocity (4i – 2j) m s–1
	
(a)	 Find the position vector of Agatha at time t seconds.
(2)
	
At time t  =  6 seconds, Agatha passes through the point P.
	
(b)	 Show that Brionie also passes through P and find the value of t when this occurs.
(4)
	
At time t seconds, Agatha is at the point A and Brionie is at the point B.
	
(c)	 Show that  AB
 = [(t – 4)i + (5 – t)j] m
(2)
	
(d)	 Find the distance between the two girls when they are closest together.

Worked solution

*(a)*
Thought Process: For an object moving with constant velocity, its position vector at time $t$ is given by its initial position vector plus $t$ times its velocity vector, i.e., $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_0 + t\mathbf{v}$. We apply this directly to Agatha.
Working:
Agatha's initial position: $\mathbf{r}_{A0} = (11\mathbf{i} + 11\mathbf{j})\text{ m}$
Agatha's constant velocity: $\mathbf{v}_A = (3\mathbf{i} - \mathbf{j})\text{ m s}^{-1}$
Using $\mathbf{r}_A = \mathbf{r}_{A0} + t\mathbf{v}_A$:
$$ \mathbf{r}_A = (11\mathbf{i} + 11\mathbf{j}) + t(3\mathbf{i} - \mathbf{j}) $$
$$ \mathbf{r}_A = (11 + 3t)\mathbf{i} + (11 - t)\mathbf{j} $$

Answer: $(11 + 3t)\mathbf{i} + (11 - t)\mathbf{j}$ m

*(b)*
Thought Process: First, we need to find the specific position vector of point P by substituting $t=6$ into Agatha's position formula from part (a). Then, we will set Brionie's general position vector equal to $\mathbf{r}_P$. To show Brionie passes through P, we must solve for $t$ by equating both the $\mathbf{i}$ components and the $\mathbf{j}$ components. If both yield the same $t$ value, Brionie passes through P at that time.
Working:


  1. Agatha's position at $t=6$ (point P):


Substitute $t=6$ into $\mathbf{r}_A = (11 + 3t)\mathbf{i} + (11 - t)\mathbf{j}$:
$$ \mathbf{r}_P = (11 + 3(6))\mathbf{i} + (11 - 6)\mathbf{j} = (11 + 18)\mathbf{i} + 5\mathbf{j} = (29\mathbf{i} + 5\mathbf{j})\text{ m} $$

  1. Brionie's position vector $\mathbf{r}_B$:


Brionie's initial position: $\mathbf{r}_{B0} = (7\mathbf{i} + 16\mathbf{j})\text{ m}$
Brionie's constant velocity: $\mathbf{v}_B = (4\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j})\text{ m s}^{-1}$
Using $\mathbf{r}_B = \mathbf{r}_{B0} + t\mathbf{v}_B$:
$$ \mathbf{r}_B = (7\mathbf{i} + 16\mathbf{j}) + t(4\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j}) = (7 + 4t)\mathbf{i} + (16 - 2t)\mathbf{j} $$

  1. Set $\mathbf{r}_B = \mathbf{r}_P$ and solve for $t$:


$$ (7 + 4t)\mathbf{i} + (16 - 2t)\mathbf{j} = 29\mathbf{i} + 5\mathbf{j} $$
Equating $\mathbf{i}$ components: $7 + 4t = 29 \implies 4t = 22 \implies t = 5.5$.
Equating $\mathbf{j}$ components: $16 - 2t = 5 \implies -2t = -11 \implies t = 5.5$.
Since both components yield $t=5.5$, Brionie also passes through point P at $t=5.5$ seconds.
Answer: $t=5.5$ seconds

*(c)*
Thought Process: The relative position vector from point A to point B, denoted $\vec{AB}$, is found by subtracting the position vector of A from the position vector of B: $\vec{AB} = \mathbf{r}_B - \mathbf{r}_A$. We will use the general expressions for $\mathbf{r}_A$ and $\mathbf{r}_B$ in terms of $t$ derived in parts (a) and (b).
Working:
From parts (a) and (b):
$\mathbf{r}_A = (11 + 3t)\mathbf{i} + (11 - t)\mathbf{j}$
$\mathbf{r}_B = (7 + 4t)\mathbf{i} + (16 - 2t)\mathbf{j}$
$$ \vec{AB} = \mathbf{r}_B - \mathbf{r}_A $$
$$ \vec{AB} = ((7 + 4t) - (11 + 3t))\mathbf{i} + ((16 - 2t) - (11 - t))\mathbf{j} $$
$$ \vec{AB} = (7 + 4t - 11 - 3t)\mathbf{i} + (16 - 2t - 11 + t)\mathbf{j} $$
$$ \vec{AB} = (t - 4)\mathbf{i} + (5 - t)\mathbf{j} $$
This matches the given expression.

Answer: Shown as $\vec{AB} = [(t - 4)\mathbf{i} + (5 - t)\mathbf{j}]$ m

*(d)*
Thought Process: The distance between the girls is the magnitude of the relative position vector $\vec{AB}$, which is $|\vec{AB}|$. To find the minimum distance, it's often simpler to minimize the square of the distance, $|\vec{AB}|^2$, as this removes the square root, resulting in a quadratic expression in $t$. We can then find the minimum of this quadratic by completing the square or by differentiation. Completing the square is demonstrated here.
Working:
We have $\vec{AB} = (t - 4)\mathbf{i} + (5 - t)\mathbf{j}$.
The distance squared, $|\vec{AB}|^2$, is given by the sum of the squares of its components:
$$ |\vec{AB}|^2 = (t - 4)^2 + (5 - t)^2 $$
Expand the squared terms:
$$ |\vec{AB}|^2 = (t^2 - 8t + 16) + (25 - 10t + t^2) $$
Combine like terms to form a quadratic in $t$:
$$ |\vec{AB}|^2 = 2t^2 - 18t + 41 $$
To find the minimum value of this quadratic, we complete the square:
$$ |\vec{AB}|^2 = 2(t^2 - 9t) + 41 $$
$$ |\vec{AB}|^2 = 2\left[\left(t - \frac{9}{2}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{9}{2}\right)^2\right] + 41 $$
$$ |\vec{AB}|^2 = 2\left[(t - 4.5)^2 - 20.25\right] + 41 $$
$$ |\vec{AB}|^2 = 2(t - 4.5)^2 - 40.5 + 41 $$
$$ |\vec{AB}|^2 = 2(t - 4.5)^2 + 0.5 $$
The minimum value of this expression occurs when the squared term $2(t - 4.5)^2$ is at its minimum, which is $0$ (when $t=4.5$ seconds).
Therefore, the minimum value of $|\vec{AB}|^2$ is $0.5$.
The minimum distance between the girls is the square root of this value:
$$ \text{Minimum distance} = \sqrt{0.5} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} $$
$$ \text{Minimum distance} \approx 0.7071... \text{ m} $$
Rounding to 2 significant figures:

Answer: $0.71$ m

Key techniques

  • vector addition and subtraction
  • magnitude of a vector
  • completing the square for quadratics
  • differentiation for minima/maxima

Common pitfalls

  • Forgetting to take the square root at the end of part (d) to report the distance, not the distance squared.
  • Incorrectly calculating the relative vector $\vec{AB}$ as $\mathbf{r}_A - \mathbf{r}_B$ instead of $\mathbf{r}_B - \mathbf{r}_A$.
  • In part (b), only solving for $t$ using one component (e.g., just $\mathbf{i}$) and not verifying consistency with the other component ($\mathbf{j}$).
