Constant Velocity: Relative Position & Bearings
6×
10-13m

Two particles, A and B, move with constant velocities. You're given initial positions and velocities. The question asks for the position of one particle relative to another at a certain time, or the time when one is, for example, 'due east' or 'south-west' of the other. It might also ask for a bearing.

Triggersposition vectorconstant velocityrelative todue eastdue northbearing
Assumptions
  • Motion occurs in a 2D horizontal plane unless stated otherwise.
  • Particles are treated as point masses with no size or rotation.
  • Velocities remain constant throughout the motion.
Approaches
A: General Position Vector & Relative Vector Method
When: This is the standard and most reliable method for all such problems.
  1. 1. For each particle (A and B), clearly write down the initial position vector ($\mathbf{r}_0$) and the constant velocity vector ($\mathbf{v}$).
  2. 2. Form the general position vector at time t for each particle using $\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}t$.
  3. 3. Find the relative position vector $\vec{AB}$ by calculating $\mathbf{r}_B(t) - \mathbf{r}_A(t)$. This will be a vector with components in terms of $t$.
  4. 4. Interpret the condition given in the question. For example:
  5. - 'B is due east of A': The $\mathbf{j}$ component of $\vec{AB}$ is 0, and the $\mathbf{i}$ component is positive.
  6. - 'B is south-west of A': The $\mathbf{i}$ component of $\vec{AB}$ is negative, the $\mathbf{j}$ component is negative, and they are equal in magnitude (i.e., of the form $-k\mathbf{i} - k\mathbf{j}$ for $k > 0$).
  7. 5. Set up and solve the equation(s) from the condition to find the time $t$.
  8. 6. For bearing questions, calculate the required vector at the specific time, draw a diagram with a North line, and use trigonometry (tan) to find the angle, then convert to a 3-figure bearing.
+ Pros: A systematic method that works for all conditions and is easy to check.
– Cons: Requires careful algebraic manipulation, especially with signs in vector subtraction.
Formulas
Position vector at time t: $\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}t$ (MEMORIZE)
Relative position of B from A: $\vec{AB} = \mathbf{r}_B - \mathbf{r}_A$ (MEMORIZE)
Magnitude (for speed/distance): $|x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j}| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Incorrectly converting a vector's angle to a bearing (e.g., measuring anti-clockwise from the positive i-axis instead of clockwise from the North (j-axis)).
  • Misinterpreting relative position conditions, e.g., for 'B is south-west of A', setting $\vec{AB} = (-k, -k)$ but forgetting to check that k must be positive.
  • Confusing $\vec{AB}$ with $\vec{BA}$. Remember $\vec{AB}$ means 'the position of B relative to A'.

Minimum Distance Between Moving Particles
2×
9-12m

Two particles move with constant velocities. You are asked to find the shortest distance between them during their motion, and sometimes the time when this occurs.

Triggersminimum distanceshortest distanceclosest approachleast distance
Assumptions
  • Particles travel in straight lines at constant speed indefinitely.
  • The minimum distance is a unique point in time.
Approaches
A: Minimise Distance-Squared using Differentiation
When: This is a robust and universally applicable calculus method.
  1. 1. Find the relative position vector $\vec{AB} = \mathbf{r}_B(t) - \mathbf{r}_A(t)$, which will be a vector in terms of $t$.
  2. 2. Calculate the square of the distance between them: $D^2 = |\vec{AB}|^2$. This avoids square roots and gives a quadratic in $t$, e.g., $at^2 + bt + c$.
  3. 3. Differentiate $D^2$ with respect to $t$: $\frac{d(D^2)}{dt} = 2at + b$.
  4. 4. Set the derivative to zero and solve for $t$. This gives the time of closest approach.
  5. 5. Substitute this value of $t$ back into the expression for $D^2$ to find the square of the minimum distance.
  6. 6. Take the square root to find the minimum distance $D$.
+ Pros: A standard calculus technique that avoids complex algebraic manipulation.
– Cons: It is very easy to forget the final step of taking the square root.
B: Minimise Distance-Squared by Completing the Square
When: A good alternative, especially if the quadratic for $D^2$ has simple coefficients.
  1. 1. Find the relative position vector $\vec{AB}$ and the squared distance $D^2 = at^2 + bt + c$ as in the other method.
  2. 2. Complete the square for the quadratic expression: $D^2 = a(t + \frac{b}{2a})^2 + (c - \frac{b^2}{4a})$.
  3. 3. The minimum value of $D^2$ is the constant part of the expression, $(c - \frac{b^2}{4a})$, which occurs at time $t = -\frac{b}{2a}$.
  4. 4. Take the square root of this minimum value to find the minimum distance $D$.
+ Pros: Can be quicker than differentiation if the algebra is neat.
– Cons: Prone to algebraic errors, particularly with fractions or negative signs.
Formulas
Position vector: $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}t$ (MEMORIZE)
Relative position: $\vec{AB} = \mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a}$ (MEMORIZE)
Condition for minimum of quadratic $ax^2+bx+c$: $x = -b/2a$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to take the square root at the end to report the distance $D$, not $D^2$.
  • Making algebraic errors when expanding the brackets for the magnitude squared, e.g. $(p - qt)^2$.
  • Confusing the time of minimum distance with the minimum distance itself.

Collision / Interception Problems
2×
13-15m

Two particles move with given velocities. The question asks if they collide, or asks you to find an unknown component of a velocity or position vector for a collision to occur.

Triggerscollideinterceptmeet
Assumptions
  • A 'collision' means the particles are at the same position at the same time.
  • Particles have zero dimensions.
Approaches
A: Equate Position Vectors
When: This is the only valid method for determining a collision.
  1. 1. Write down the general position vector $\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}t$ for each particle, let's say $\mathbf{r}_A(t)$ and $\mathbf{r}_B(t)$.
  2. 2. For a collision to occur, they must be at the same place at the same time. Set $\mathbf{r}_A(t) = \mathbf{r}_B(t)$.
  3. 3. This gives a single vector equation. Equate the $\mathbf{i}$ components and the $\mathbf{j}$ components separately to form two simultaneous linear equations.
  4. 4. Solve the equation from one component (e.g., the $\mathbf{i}$ components) to find a value for the time of collision, $t$.
  5. 5. Substitute this value of $t$ into the equation for the other component (e.g., the $\mathbf{j}$ components).
  6. 6. If the equation holds true, then a collision occurs at that time. If it is not true, their paths may cross, but they do not collide.
+ Pros: Directly models the physical condition for a collision.
– Cons: A common mistake is to only solve for t from one component and not verify it in the other.
Formulas
Collision condition: $\mathbf{r}_A(t) = \mathbf{r}_B(t)$ for some $t > 0$ (MEMORIZE)
Position vector: $\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}t$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Assuming that if the paths cross, the particles collide. You must show they are at the crossing point at the same time.
  • Incorrectly forming the initial position vector, e.g., missing out $\mathbf{r}_0$.
  • Making sign errors when rearranging the vector equations to solve for $t$.

Constant Acceleration Kinematics (Vector SUVAT)
2×
6-9m

A particle moves with a constant acceleration, which is given as a vector. You'll be given initial velocity and asked to find the velocity or position at a later time, or vice-versa.

Triggersconstant accelerationinitial velocityfinal velocityspeed
Assumptions
  • The acceleration vector is constant in both magnitude and direction.
  • Air resistance is negligible.
Approaches
A: Apply SUVAT Equations in Vector Form
When: Standard method for any problem involving constant acceleration in 2D.
  1. 1. List the known SUVAT quantities in vector form: $\mathbf{s}$ (displacement), $\mathbf{u}$ (initial velocity), $\mathbf{v}$ (final velocity), $\mathbf{a}$ (acceleration), and the scalar $t$ (time).
  2. 2. Choose the appropriate vector SUVAT equation that connects the knowns with the required unknown.
  3. 3. Substitute the known vectors and scalar time into the equation.
  4. 4. Solve for the unknown vector by performing vector addition/subtraction and scalar multiplication.
  5. 5. If asked for a scalar quantity like 'speed' or 'distance', calculate the magnitude of the final vector quantity (e.g., speed is $|\mathbf{v}|$).
+ Pros: A direct extension of the familiar scalar SUVAT equations.
– Cons: Vector arithmetic, especially subtraction, can be a source of sign errors.
Formulas
$\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{a}t$ (MEMORIZE vector form)
$\mathbf{s} = \mathbf{u}t + \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{a}t^2$ (MEMORIZE vector form)
$\mathbf{s} = \frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v})t$ (MEMORIZE vector form)
Pitfalls
  • Confusing velocity (a vector) with speed (a scalar magnitude). Always calculate the magnitude at the end if speed is requested.
  • Mistaking a direction vector for a velocity vector. If a particle is 'moving in the direction of $2\mathbf{i} - \mathbf{j}$', its velocity is $\mathbf{v} = k(2\mathbf{i} - \mathbf{j})$ for some scalar $k > 0$.

Dynamics with Vectors (F=ma)
2×
8-12m

One or more forces, given in vector form, act on a particle of a given mass. You'll need to find the resultant force, the acceleration, and then often use vector kinematics to find subsequent velocity or position.

Triggersforcemassresultant forceacts on a particleacceleration
Assumptions
  • The mass of the particle is constant.
  • Newton's Second Law is applicable.
  • The particle is a point mass.
Approaches
A: Resultant Force & Newton's Second Law
When: The standard approach when forces and motion are described by vectors.
  1. 1. Identify all forces acting on the particle, given as vectors (e.g., $\mathbf{F}_1, \mathbf{F}_2, ...$).
  2. 2. Find the resultant force $\mathbf{R}$ by adding the force vectors: $\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{F}_1 + \mathbf{F}_2 + ...$.
  3. 3. Apply Newton's Second Law in vector form: $\mathbf{R} = m\mathbf{a}$.
  4. 4. Solve for the acceleration vector $\mathbf{a}$ by dividing the resultant force vector by the scalar mass: $\mathbf{a} = \frac{1}{m}\mathbf{R}$.
  5. 5. If required, use this constant acceleration vector $\mathbf{a}$ in the vector SUVAT equations to find velocity, displacement, etc.
+ Pros: Logically connects dynamics (forces) with kinematics (motion) in a single framework.
– Cons: It's easy to forget to divide by mass 'm' to get acceleration from the resultant force.
Formulas
Newton's Second Law (vector form): $\mathbf{F}_{resultant} = m\mathbf{a}$ (formula booklet)
Vector Kinematics: $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{a}t$ (MEMORIZE vector form)
Pitfalls
  • Using the resultant force $\mathbf{R}$ in a kinematics equation instead of the acceleration $\mathbf{a}$. Always remember to divide by mass.
  • Making errors in vector addition, especially with negative components.
  • If a force has unknown magnitude 'P' but known direction, write it as a magnitude multiplied by a unit vector, or resolve it into components involving P.

Must memorize

  • Position Vector (Constant Velocity): $\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}t$. This is the foundation of the chapter.
  • Relative Position Vector: $\vec{AB} = \mathbf{r}_B - \mathbf{r}_A$.
  • Magnitude of a Vector: $|x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j}| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$. Used for speed and distance.
  • Vector SUVAT Equations: $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{a}t$ and $\mathbf{s} = \mathbf{u}t + \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{a}t^2$. The scalar versions are in the booklet, but you must know how to apply them with vectors.
  • Collision Condition: $\mathbf{r}_A(t) = \mathbf{r}_B(t)$ for the same time $t$.
  • Directional Vectors: Due North = $k\mathbf{j}$, Due East = $k\mathbf{i}$, North-East = $k(\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j})$ etc, where $k$ is a scalar.

Global strategy

  • **Translate Words to Vectors First:** Before any calculation, convert all given information (positions, velocities, forces, directions like 'north-east') into $\mathbf{i}, \mathbf{j}$ vector form. This is the most crucial step.
  • **Draw a Diagram:** A quick sketch of initial positions and velocity vectors helps visualize the problem, especially for relative position and bearing questions. It can prevent major directional errors.
  • **Distinguish Vector vs. Scalar:** Be vigilant about what the question asks for. 'Velocity' or 'Force' requires a vector answer. 'Speed', 'Distance', or 'Magnitude' requires a final scalar answer, found by calculating the magnitude.
  • **Master $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}t$:** This formula is the bedrock of most M1 vector questions. Write it down for every moving particle at the start of your working.
  • **Understand Relative Vectors:** Firmly grasp that $\vec{AB} = \mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a}$ represents the position vector OF B, starting FROM A. The order of subtraction is critical.
  • **Structure Your Working:** For multi-particle problems, use clear headings or columns for Particle A and Particle B to keep your $\mathbf{r}_0$ and $\mathbf{v}$ vectors organised.
  • **Check Your Units:** Ensure all units are consistent (e.g., time in seconds or hours, distance in metres). Time conversion from minutes to hours is a common pitfall in longer questions.

Exam tips

  1. **Underline Key Info:** In the exam, use your pen to underline or circle all vector quantities ($\{\mathbf{i}, \mathbf{j}\}$ components), initial positions, velocities, and key phrases like 'due east of', 'collide', or 'minimum distance'.
  2. **Bearings Mean Clockwise from North:** For bearing questions, always draw a clear diagram with a North line (positive $\mathbf{j}$-axis). Calculate the angle inside the triangle using $\tan^{-1}(|i\text{ comp}| / |j\text{ comp}|)$ and then carefully work out the 3-figure bearing from your diagram.
  3. **Don't Forget the Final Square Root:** When finding a minimum distance or a speed, the penultimate step is often finding the value of 'distance-squared' or 'speed-squared'. Remember to take the square root for the final answer.
  4. **Show Your Vector Subtraction:** When calculating a relative vector like $\mathbf{r}_B - \mathbf{r}_A$, write out the full vectors in brackets before simplifying. This reduces the chance of sign errors, e.g., $(5\mathbf{i} - 2t\mathbf{j}) - ((3t-1)\mathbf{i} + 4\mathbf{j})$.
  5. **Use Calculators for Simultaneous Equations:** Once you have set up your simultaneous equations from equating vector components, use your calculator's equation solver to find the variables quickly and accurately, saving time and mental energy.
