Relative Position: Collision & Closest Approach
8×
9-17m

Two particles, A and B, are moving with given constant velocities. You are given their initial positions (or positions at a certain time). Questions ask for the time/position of collision, the shortest distance between them, or the time when one particle is at a specific bearing (e.g., 'due north') from the other.

Core strategy
  1. Write the general position vector for each particle at time `t`: `\mathbf{r}_A(t) = \mathbf{r}_{0A} + \mathbf{v}_A t` and `\mathbf{r}_B(t) = \mathbf{r}_{0B} + \mathbf{v}_B t`. Ensure `\mathbf{r}_0` is the position at `t=0`.
  2. Calculate the relative position vector, usually `\vec{AB} = \mathbf{r}_B - \mathbf{r}_A`. Be consistent with the order.
  3. For **collision**, set the relative position vector to zero: `\vec{AB} = \mathbf{0}`. Equate the `\mathbf{i}` and `\mathbf{j}` components to zero to form two simultaneous equations and solve for `t`. The time `t` must be the same from both equations for a collision to occur.
  4. For **closest approach**, find the squared magnitude of the relative position vector: `d^2 = |\vec{AB}|^2`. This will be a quadratic function of `t`, e.g., `at^2 + bt + c`.
  5. Minimise `d^2` by finding the time `t` at the vertex of the parabola (using differentiation `d(d^2)/dt = 0` or completing the square `t = -b/2a`). Substitute this `t` back into the expression for `d^2` to find the minimum squared distance, then square root for the actual distance.
  6. For **geometric conditions** (e.g., 'B is due north of A'), set the appropriate component of `\vec{AB}` to zero. For 'due north', the `\mathbf{i}` component is zero. For 'due east', the `\mathbf{j}` component is zero.
Must-know formulas
\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}t (constant velocity position vector) (MEMORIZE)
\vec{AB} = \mathbf{r}_B - \mathbf{r}_A (relative position vector) (MEMORIZE)
|a\mathbf{i} + b\mathbf{j}| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} (magnitude of a vector) (MEMORIZE)
Common pitfalls
  • Calculating the relative vector in the wrong order (e.g., `\mathbf{r}_A - \mathbf{r}_B` instead of `\mathbf{r}_B - \mathbf{r}_A`). This flips the signs of your result.
  • Forgetting to square root the minimum value of `d^2` to find the actual minimum distance `d`.
  • Making algebraic errors when expanding the squared magnitude, e.g., `(c - dt)^2`.
  • Misinterpreting geometric conditions, e.g., for 'B is south-west of A', both components of `\vec{AB}` must be negative.

Constant Acceleration Kinematics (vector suvat)
2×
6-9m

A particle experiences a constant, non-zero acceleration. You are given information about its velocity at two different times, or its initial velocity and acceleration. Questions ask to find the acceleration, the velocity at a later time, or the speed when moving in a certain direction.

Core strategy
  1. Identify the known and unknown `suvat` variables (s, u, v, a, t), remembering they are vectors (except for `t`).
  2. Select the appropriate vector `suvat` equation. `\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{a}t` is the most common.
  3. Substitute the known vectors into the equation. For example, to find acceleration: `\mathbf{a} = (\mathbf{v} - \mathbf{u}) / t`.
  4. Solve for the unknown vector by equating the `\mathbf{i}` and `\mathbf{j}` components separately.
  5. If asked for 'speed', calculate the magnitude of the final velocity vector: `speed = |\mathbf{v}|`.
  6. If told the particle is 'moving in the direction of `(p\mathbf{i} + q\mathbf{j})`', set its velocity vector `\mathbf{v}` proportional to this direction vector: `\mathbf{v} = k(p\mathbf{i} + q\mathbf{j})` for some scalar `k > 0`.
Must-know formulas
\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{a}t (vector suvat) (formula booklet)
\mathbf{s} = \mathbf{u}t + \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{a}t^2 (vector suvat) (formula booklet)
|a\mathbf{i} + b\mathbf{j}| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} (magnitude of a vector) (MEMORIZE)
Common pitfalls
  • Confusing velocity (vector) with speed (scalar). Always calculate the magnitude for speed.
  • Making sign errors during vector subtraction, especially in `(\mathbf{v} - \mathbf{u})`.
  • Forgetting to introduce a scalar multiplier `k` when dealing with parallel vectors (e.g., 'moving in the direction of...').

Newton's Second Law in Vector Form (F=ma)
2×
8-12m

A particle of a given mass is acted on by two or more forces, given in vector form. Some forces may be unknown. Questions ask for the resultant force, the acceleration, or the magnitude/direction of an unknown force given the particle's motion.

Core strategy
  1. Identify all forces `\mathbf{F}_1, \mathbf{F}_2, ...` acting on the particle.
  2. Calculate the resultant force `\mathbf{R}` by summing all force vectors: `\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{F}_1 + \mathbf{F}_2 + ...`
  3. Apply Newton's Second Law in vector form: `\mathbf{R} = m\mathbf{a}`. Remember to include the mass `m`.
  4. If acceleration `\mathbf{a}` is unknown, use `\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{R}/m`.
  5. If an unknown force or variable exists, equate the `\mathbf{i}` and `\mathbf{j}` components of `\mathbf{R} = m\mathbf{a}` to form simultaneous equations and solve.
  6. If the question involves kinematics after finding acceleration, use the vector `suvat` equations from the 'Constant Acceleration' type.
Must-know formulas
\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a} (Newton's Second Law) (formula booklet)
\mathbf{R} = \sum \mathbf{F}_i (Resultant Force) (MEMORIZE)
\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{a}t (vector suvat) (formula booklet)
Common pitfalls
  • Forgetting to multiply the acceleration `\mathbf{a}` by mass `m` when using `\mathbf{F}=m\mathbf{a}`.
  • Making errors when adding or resolving vector components, especially with negative signs.
  • Confusing the resultant force magnitude `|\mathbf{R}|` with its square `|\mathbf{R}|^2` when using Pythagoras.

Single Particle Kinematics (Constant Velocity)
2×
4-10m

A single particle is moving with constant velocity. You are given information about its position at one or two points in time. Questions are simpler and focus on finding its velocity, speed, or position at another time.

Core strategy
  1. Identify the given information: initial position `\mathbf{r}_0`, position at a later time `\mathbf{r}(t)`, time `t`, and velocity `\mathbf{v}`.
  2. If velocity is unknown, calculate the displacement `\mathbf{s} = \mathbf{r}(t) - \mathbf{r}_0` and use `\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{s} / t`.
  3. If the position at a future time is required, use the fundamental equation `\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}t`.
  4. If asked for speed, calculate the magnitude of the velocity vector: `speed = |\mathbf{v}|`.
  5. If a question involves finding a time `t` when the magnitude of the position vector is a certain value, set up `|\mathbf{r}(t)|^2 = d^2` and solve the resulting quadratic equation for `t`.
Must-know formulas
\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}t (constant velocity position vector) (MEMORIZE)
\mathbf{v} = \frac{\text{displacement}}{\text{time}} = \frac{\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_0}{t} (constant velocity definition) (MEMORIZE)
|a\mathbf{i} + b\mathbf{j}| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} (magnitude of a vector) (MEMORIZE)
Common pitfalls
  • Incorrectly calculating displacement (e.g., `\mathbf{r}_0 - \mathbf{r}_1` instead of `\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_0`).
  • Using constant acceleration (`suvat`) formulas when the velocity is constant.
  • Algebraic errors when setting up and solving a quadratic for `t` from a magnitude condition.

Must memorize

  • Position vector (constant velocity): `\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}t`
  • Magnitude of a vector: `|a\mathbf{i} + b\mathbf{j}| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}`
  • Relative position vector: `\vec{AB} = \mathbf{r}_B - \mathbf{r}_A`
  • Resultant force: `\mathbf{R} = \sum \mathbf{F}_i`
  • Bearing Calculation: From a vector `v_x\mathbf{i} + v_y\mathbf{j}`, calculate `\theta = \arctan(|v_x/v_y|)`. Use a diagram to determine the final 3-figure bearing from North (e.g., for a vector in the SE quadrant, bearing is `180 - \theta`).

Global strategy

  • Always distinguish between vector quantities (position, displacement, velocity, acceleration, force) and scalar quantities (distance, speed, time, mass).
  • Be methodical. For any kinematics problem, write down what you know for each particle: `\mathbf{r}_0`, `\mathbf{v}`, `\mathbf{a}`. Then write the general equation of motion (e.g., `\mathbf{r}(t) = ...`) before substituting values.
  • Master the relative vector concept: `\vec{AB} = \mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a}`. This applies to position, velocity, and displacement. Getting the order wrong is a very common source of error.
  • Draw a simple diagram. Sketching initial positions and velocity vectors helps visualize the problem, especially for questions involving bearings or geometric conditions like 'due east'.
  • Organize your calculations clearly, often by separating `\mathbf{i}` and `\mathbf{j}` components into columns. This minimizes arithmetic mistakes.
  • For 'show that' questions, ensure every step of your working is clear and logical, starting from the given information and arriving at the required result.

Exam tips

  1. Vector questions are often long and multi-part but built from simple steps. Don't be intimidated; secure early marks by writing down the initial position and velocity vectors correctly.
  2. For 'closest approach' questions, always work with the *squared* distance `d^2` to create a simple quadratic in `t`. Differentiate or complete the square. Only take the square root at the very end if asked for the distance.
  3. When asked for a bearing, always draw a quick sketch with North, East, South, West axes. This prevents quadrant errors and mistakes in converting angles (e.g., `90 - \theta` vs `180 + \theta`). Bearings are always measured clockwise from North and given as 3 figures.
  4. If a later part of a question depends on a value you couldn't find, state an assumption (e.g., 'Let t=5') and continue with your method. This can earn you method marks for the subsequent steps.
  5. Double-check your vector arithmetic, especially signs when subtracting vectors. It's one of the most common places to lose marks on an otherwise correct method.
