Question 1 continued
Worked solution

We recognize this as a direct collision problem involving two particles. Such problems almost always require the application of the Impulse-Momentum theorem and the Principle of Conservation of Linear Momentum. The key is to define a consistent positive direction for all velocities and to carefully consider all possible scenarios for particle motion after the collision.

For part (a), we need to find the impulse on particle Q. Impulse is the change in momentum, $I = m(v - u)$. We're given Q's mass and its speeds before and after the collision, and told its direction of motion is reversed. This allows us to determine its initial and final velocities with respect to our chosen positive direction.

For part (b), we need to find the constant $k$. Since it's a direct collision, we can apply the Principle of Conservation of Linear Momentum to the system of P and Q. The total momentum before the collision must equal the total momentum after. The problem states P's final *speed* is $u$, but doesn't specify its direction. This implies there are two possibilities for P's final *velocity*: it either continues in its original direction or reverses direction. Each case will lead to a different value for $k$.

Working:
First, establish a consistent positive direction. Let's define the initial direction of particle P as the positive direction.

Part (a) Find the magnitude of the impulse exerted on Q by P:


  • Particle P (mass $3m$) moves with speed $ku$ (positive direction).

  • Particle Q (mass $5m$) moves with speed $2u$ in the opposite direction to P, so its initial velocity $u_Q = -2u$.

  • After the collision, the speed of Q is $3u$ and its direction is reversed. Since it was initially moving in the negative direction, its new direction must be positive. So, its final velocity $v_Q = 3u$.

Using the impulse-momentum theorem for particle Q:
$$I_Q = m_Q(v_Q - u_Q)$$
Substitute the values:
$$I_Q = 5m(3u - (-2u))$$
$$I_Q = 5m(3u + 2u)$$
$$I_Q = 5m(5u)$$
$$I_Q = 25mu$$
The magnitude of the impulse exerted on Q by P is $25mu$.

Part (b) Find the two possible values of k:
Apply the Principle of Conservation of Linear Momentum to the system (P and Q).
Total momentum before collision = Total momentum after collision.
$$m_P u_P + m_Q u_Q = m_P v_P + m_Q v_Q$$
Substitute the masses and initial velocities (using the positive direction defined earlier):
$$3m(ku) + 5m(-2u) = 3m v_P + 5m(3u)$$
$$3mku - 10mu = 3m v_P + 15mu$$
Divide through by $m$ (since $m
eq 0$):
$$3ku - 10u = 3v_P + 15u$$
Rearrange to solve for $3v_P$:
$$3ku - 25u = 3v_P$$

Now, consider the two possible cases for the direction of particle P after the collision. We know its final *speed* is $u$.

Case 1: Particle P continues in its original direction.
In this case, its final velocity $v_P = u$.
Substitute this into the momentum equation:
$$3ku - 25u = 3(u)$$
$$3ku - 25u = 3u$$
$$3ku = 28u$$
Since $u
eq 0$, we can divide by $u$:
$$3k = 28$$
$$k = \frac{28}{3}$$

Case 2: Particle P reverses its direction.
In this case, its final velocity $v_P = -u$.
Substitute this into the momentum equation:
$$3ku - 25u = 3(-u)$$
$$3ku - 25u = -3u$$
$$3ku = 22u$$
Since $u
eq 0$, we can divide by $u$:
$$3k = 22$$
$$k = \frac{22}{3}$$

Answer: The magnitude of the impulse exerted on Q by P is $25mu$. The two possible values of $k$ are $\frac{28}{3}$ and $\frac{22}{3}$.

Key techniques

  • Apply the impulse-momentum theorem ($I = m(v-u)$)
  • Apply the principle of conservation of linear momentum ($m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2$)
  • Maintain a consistent definition of positive direction for all velocities
  • Consider all possible directions of motion for particles after a collision if not explicitly stated

Common pitfalls

  • Inconsistent definition of positive and negative directions for velocities, leading to sign errors in the impulse or momentum equations.
  • Forgetting to consider both possible directions for particle P's final velocity after the collision, leading to only one value for $k$.
  • Confusing speed (magnitude) with velocity (vector, requiring direction) when setting up equations for impulse and momentum.
