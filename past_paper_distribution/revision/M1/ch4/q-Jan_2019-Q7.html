Worked solution

This problem involves two connected particles, one on a horizontal surface and one on an inclined plane, both with friction. The string passes over a smooth pulley, implying constant tension and that the pulley itself is massless. Both particles will move with the same acceleration. We'll need to apply Newton's second law ($F=ma$) to each particle separately, forming simultaneous equations.

First, let's establish the given information for the angles and coefficients of friction:
Given $\tan \alpha = \frac{4}{3}$. From a 3-4-5 right-angled triangle, we can deduce:
$$\sin \alpha = \frac{4}{5} \quad \text{and} \quad \cos \alpha = \frac{3}{5}$$
Coefficient of friction for particle A (on table): $\mu_A = \frac{1}{5}$
Coefficient of friction for particle B (on inclined plane): $\mu_B = \frac{1}{3}$
Particle A has mass $m$, Particle B has mass $3m$ (crucial detail often missed from question excerpts).
Let $g$ be the acceleration due to gravity.

Part (a): Find the tension in the string.

Thought Process: We need to consider each particle individually. For each, we draw a free-body diagram, resolve forces perpendicular to the direction of motion to find the normal reaction, calculate the friction force, and then apply $F=ma$ in the direction of motion. Since they are connected by an inextensible string, their accelerations ($a$) will be the same. This will give us two equations with two unknowns ($T$ and $a$).

Particle A (mass $m$, on horizontal table):

  • Working (Resolving vertically): Particle A is in equilibrium vertically. The normal reaction $R_A$ balances its weight $mg$.
$$R_A - mg = 0 \implies R_A = mg$$
  • Working (Friction force): The friction force $F_{fA}$ opposes the motion (or tendency of motion). Since A is accelerating right, friction acts left.
$$F_{fA} = \mu_A R_A = \frac{1}{5} mg$$
  • Working (Newton's 2nd Law horizontally): The net force in the direction of motion (right) is $T - F_{fA}$.
$$T - F_{fA} = m a$$ $$T - \frac{1}{5} mg = ma \quad \text{(Equation 1)}$$

Particle B (mass $3m$, on inclined plane):

  • Working (Resolving perpendicular to the plane): Particle B is in equilibrium perpendicular to the plane. The normal reaction $R_B$ balances the component of its weight perpendicular to the plane, $(3m)g \cos \alpha$.
$$R_B - (3m)g \cos \alpha = 0 \implies R_B = 3mg \left(\frac{3}{5}\right) = \frac{9}{5}mg$$
  • Working (Friction force): Particle B slides *down* the plane, so friction $F_{fB}$ acts *up* the plane.
$$F_{fB} = \mu_B R_B = \frac{1}{3} \left(\frac{9}{5}mg\right) = \frac{3}{5}mg$$
  • Working (Newton's 2nd Law parallel to the plane): The net force in the direction of motion (down the plane) is the component of weight down the plane, $(3m)g \sin \alpha$, minus the tension $T$ and the friction $F_{fB}$.
$$(3m)g \sin \alpha - T - F_{fB} = (3m)a$$ $$3mg \left(\frac{4}{5}\right) - T - \frac{3}{5}mg = 3ma$$ $$\frac{12}{5}mg - \frac{3}{5}mg - T = 3ma$$ $$\frac{9}{5}mg - T = 3ma \quad \text{(Equation 2)}$$

Working (Solving simultaneous equations):
From Equation 1, we can write $ma$ in terms of $T$:
$$ma = T - \frac{1}{5}mg$$
Substitute this into Equation 2:
$$\frac{9}{5}mg - T = 3\left(T - \frac{1}{5}mg\right)$$
$$\frac{9}{5}mg - T = 3T - \frac{3}{5}mg$$
Gather terms involving $T$ on one side and $mg$ on the other:
$$\frac{9}{5}mg + \frac{3}{5}mg = 3T + T$$
$$\frac{12}{5}mg = 4T$$
$$T = \frac{12}{20}mg$$
$$T = \frac{3}{5}mg$$

Answer: The tension in the string is $T = \frac{3}{5}mg$.

Part (b): State where in your working you have used the fact that the string is modelled as being light.

Thought Process: A light string has negligible mass. This means it doesn't add to the system's inertia and, crucially, doesn't require extra force to accelerate itself. Hence, the tension is the same throughout its length.

Answer: The tension is constant throughout the entire length of the string.

Part (c): Find the magnitude of the force exerted on the pulley by the string.

Thought Process: The pulley is smooth and light. The force exerted on the pulley by the string is the vector sum of the two tension forces acting on it. One tension $T$ acts horizontally (from particle A). The other tension $T$ acts down the inclined plane (from particle B). We need to find the resultant of these two forces.

Let's assume the string from A pulls horizontally to the left on the pulley, and the string from B pulls down the incline. If we place the pulley at the origin $(0,0)$:

  • The force from string to A is $\vec{F_A} = (-T, 0)$ (pulling left).
  • The force from string to B is $\vec{F_B} = (T \cos \alpha, -T \sin \alpha)$ (pulling right and down the incline).

The resultant force $\vec{R}$ is the vector sum $\vec{F_A} + \vec{F_B}$.

Working:
$$\vec{R} = (-T + T \cos \alpha, -T \sin \alpha)$$
To find the magnitude of this resultant force, we use $R = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2}$:
$$R^2 = (-T + T \cos \alpha)^2 + (-T \sin \alpha)^2$$
$$R^2 = T^2(1 - \cos \alpha)^2 + T^2 \sin^2 \alpha$$
$$R^2 = T^2 (1 - 2\cos \alpha + \cos^2 \alpha + \sin^2 \alpha)$$
Since $\cos^2 \alpha + \sin^2 \alpha = 1$:
$$R^2 = T^2 (1 - 2\cos \alpha + 1)$$
$$R^2 = T^2 (2 - 2\cos \alpha)$$
$$R^2 = 2T^2 (1 - \cos \alpha)$$
Using the identity $1 - \cos \alpha = 2\sin^2(\alpha/2)$:
$$R^2 = 2T^2 (2\sin^2(\alpha/2)) = 4T^2 \sin^2(\alpha/2)$$
$$R = 2T \sin(\alpha/2)$$

Now, we need $\sin(\alpha/2)$. We know $\cos \alpha = \frac{3}{5}$.
From the half-angle identity $\sin^2(\alpha/2) = \frac{1 - \cos \alpha}{2}$:
$$\sin^2(\alpha/2) = \frac{1 - 3/5}{2} = \frac{2/5}{2} = \frac{1}{5}$$
$$\sin(\alpha/2) = \sqrt{\frac{1}{5}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}} = \frac{\sqrt{5}}{5}$$

Substitute the value of $T = \frac{3}{5}mg$ and $\sin(\alpha/2) = \frac{\sqrt{5}}{5}$ into the expression for $R$:
$$R = 2 \left(\frac{3}{5}mg\right) \left(\frac{\sqrt{5}}{5}\right)$$
$$R = \frac{6mg\sqrt{5}}{25}$$

Answer: The magnitude of the force exerted on the pulley by the string is $R = \frac{6\sqrt{5}}{25}mg$.
(Note: Some official mark schemes might present numerical approximations or alternative forms that appear different. This derivation is consistent with the standard physics principles and the given data.)

Key techniques

  • Drawing free-body diagrams
  • Resolving forces parallel and perpendicular to surfaces
  • Applying $F=ma$ for each particle
  • Using $F_f = \mu R$
  • Solving simultaneous equations
  • Vector addition for resultant force on pulley

Common pitfalls

  • Misinterpreting the mass of particle B (often assumed to be 'm' if not explicitly stated, but was '3m' in the original paper).
  • Incorrectly resolving forces on the inclined plane (using sin instead of cos, or vice versa, for components).
  • Making algebraic errors when solving simultaneous equations or calculating the resultant force magnitude.
  • Incorrectly determining the angle between the two tension forces acting on the pulley for part (c). A common mistake is using $\alpha$ directly, or $90+\alpha$, rather than $180-\alpha$ for the angle between the vectors acting *into* the pulley.
