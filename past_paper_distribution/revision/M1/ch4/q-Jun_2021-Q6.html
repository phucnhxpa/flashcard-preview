Worked solution

Let's first determine the values for $\sin \theta$ and $\cos \theta$ from the given $\tan \theta = \frac{5}{12}$. We can imagine a right-angled triangle with opposite side 5 and adjacent side 12. Using Pythagoras' theorem, the hypotenuse is $\sqrt{5^2 + 12^2} = \sqrt{25 + 144} = \sqrt{169} = 13$. Therefore:

$\sin \theta = \frac{5}{13}$
$\cos \theta = \frac{12}{13}$

We are given: mass $m = 6\text{ kg}$, initial speed $u = 5\text{ m s}^{-1}$, coefficient of friction $\mu = \frac{1}{4}$. We use $g = 9.8\text{ m s}^{-2}$.

Part (a): Find the magnitude of the frictional force acting on the particle as it moves up the plane.

Thought Process: To find the frictional force, $F_r$, we first need to determine the normal reaction force, $R$. The normal reaction acts perpendicular to the plane and balances the component of gravity perpendicular to the plane. Once $R$ is known, we use the formula $F_r = \mu R$.

Working:


  1. Resolve forces perpendicular to the plane: The forces perpendicular to the plane are the normal reaction $R$ (outwards from the plane) and the component of gravity $mg \cos \theta$ (into the plane). Since there is no acceleration perpendicular to the plane, these forces balance.


$$R - mg \cos \theta = 0$$
$$R = mg \cos \theta$$
Substitute the given values:
$$R = 6 \times 9.8 \times \frac{12}{13}$$
$$R = 58.8 \times \frac{12}{13} = \frac{705.6}{13} \approx 54.2769\text{ N}$$

  1. Calculate the frictional force: Now use $F_r = \mu R$.
$$F_r = \frac{1}{4} R = \frac{1}{4} \times \frac{705.6}{13}$$ $$F_r = \frac{176.4}{13} \approx 13.5692\text{ N}$$

Rounding to 3 significant figures:
$F_r = 13.6\text{ N}$

Answer: The magnitude of the frictional force is $13.6\text{ N}$.

Part (b): Find the distance AB.

Thought Process: To find the distance the particle travels, we need its acceleration. We'll use Newton's Second Law ($F=ma$) by resolving forces parallel to the plane. When the particle moves up the plane, both the component of gravity and the frictional force act *down* the plane, opposing the motion. Once acceleration is found, we can use a SUVAT equation ($v^2 = u^2 + 2as$) as acceleration is constant.

Working:


  1. Resolve forces parallel to the plane (let 'up the plane' be the positive direction):



  • Component of gravity acting down the plane: $-mg \sin \theta$

  • Frictional force acting down the plane (opposing motion): $-F_r$

Apply Newton's Second Law $F=ma$:
$$ma = -mg \sin \theta - F_r$$
Substitute known values ($m=6$, $F_r = \frac{176.4}{13}$ from part (a)):
$$6a = - (6 \times 9.8 \times \frac{5}{13}) - \frac{176.4}{13}$$
$$6a = - (58.8 \times \frac{5}{13}) - \frac{176.4}{13}$$
$$6a = - \frac{294}{13} - \frac{176.4}{13}$$
$$6a = - \frac{470.4}{13}$$
$$a = - \frac{470.4}{13 \times 6} = - \frac{78.4}{13} \approx -6.0308\text{ m s}^{-2}$$

  1. Use SUVAT to find distance AB:
We have:
  • Initial velocity $u = 5\text{ m s}^{-1}$ (up the plane)
  • Final velocity $v = 0\text{ m s}^{-1}$ (at instantaneous rest at B)
  • Acceleration $a = -\frac{78.4}{13}\text{ m s}^{-2}$
  • Displacement $s = AB$ (what we need to find)

Using the equation $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$:
$$0^2 = 5^2 + 2 \left(-\frac{78.4}{13}\right) s$$
$$0 = 25 - \frac{156.8}{13} s$$
$$\frac{156.8}{13} s = 25$$
$$s = \frac{25 \times 13}{156.8} = \frac{325}{156.8} \approx 2.0727\text{ m}$$

Rounding to 3 significant figures:
$s = 2.07\text{ m}$

Answer: The distance AB is $2.07\text{ m}$.

Part (c): Find the distance BC.

Thought Process: The particle now slides down. The friction force will *reverse direction* and now act *up* the plane, opposing the downward motion. We need to find the new acceleration down the plane using Newton's Second Law, and then use SUVAT again. The particle starts from rest at B and reaches a speed of $5\text{ m s}^{-1}$ at C.

Working:


  1. Resolve forces parallel to the plane (let 'down the plane' be the positive direction):



  • Component of gravity acting down the plane: $+mg \sin \theta$

  • Frictional force acting up the plane (opposing motion): $-F_r$

Apply Newton's Second Law $F=ma$:
$$ma = mg \sin \theta - F_r$$
Substitute known values ($m=6$, $F_r = \frac{176.4}{13}$):
$$6a = (6 \times 9.8 \times \frac{5}{13}) - \frac{176.4}{13}$$
$$6a = \frac{294}{13} - \frac{176.4}{13}$$
$$6a = \frac{117.6}{13}$$
$$a = \frac{117.6}{13 \times 6} = \frac{19.6}{13} \approx 1.50769\text{ m s}^{-2}$$

  1. Use SUVAT to find distance BC:
We have:
  • Initial velocity $u = 0\text{ m s}^{-1}$ (at rest at B)
  • Final velocity $v = 5\text{ m s}^{-1}$ (at C)
  • Acceleration $a = \frac{19.6}{13}\text{ m s}^{-2}$
  • Displacement $s = BC$ (what we need to find)

Using the equation $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$:
$$5^2 = 0^2 + 2 \left(\frac{19.6}{13}\right) s$$
$$25 = \frac{39.2}{13} s$$
$$s = \frac{25 \times 13}{39.2} = \frac{325}{39.2} \approx 8.2908\text{ m}$$

Rounding to 3 significant figures:
$s = 8.29\text{ m}$

Answer: The distance BC is $8.29\text{ m}$.

Key techniques

  • Resolve forces perpendicular and parallel to inclined plane
  • Apply friction formula $F = \mu R$
  • Use $F=ma$ to find acceleration
  • Apply SUVAT equations
  • Handle changing direction of friction

Common pitfalls

  • Incorrectly identifying the direction of the frictional force (always opposes motion).
  • Mixing up $\sin \theta$ and $\cos \theta$ when resolving the weight component.
  • Sign errors in $F=ma$ or SUVAT equations, especially when the direction of motion or acceleration changes.
