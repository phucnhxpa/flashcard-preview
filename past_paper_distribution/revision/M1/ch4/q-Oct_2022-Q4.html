Worked solution

Part (a): Find the acceleration of the lift.

Thought process: We need to find the acceleration of the lift. The problem specifies to consider the forces acting *on the lift only*. This means we apply Newton's Second Law ($F=ma$) to the lift structure itself. We need to identify all external forces acting on the lift and its mass. The lift is accelerating upwards, so we will define the upward direction as positive.

The forces acting on the lift are:


  1. The tension in the cable, acting upwards.

  2. The weight of the lift itself, acting downwards.

  3. The forces exerted *by* Alan and Bhavana *on* the lift floor, acting downwards. These are the reaction forces to the normal forces the floor exerts on them. We are given the forces the floor exerts *on* Alan and Bhavana, so by Newton's Third Law, the forces they exert *on* the floor are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction (i.e., downwards).

Working:
Let $M_L$ be the mass of the lift ($250 ext{ kg}$). Let $T$ be the tension in the cable ($3616 ext{ N}$). Let $a$ be the acceleration of the lift.

From the problem description:


  • The force exerted by the floor on Alan is $565 ext{ N}$ (upwards). Therefore, the force exerted by Alan on the floor is $565 ext{ N}$ (downwards).

  • The force exerted by the floor on Bhavana is $226 ext{ N}$ (upwards). Therefore, the force exerted by Bhavana on the floor is $226 ext{ N}$ (downwards).

Applying Newton's Second Law, $F=ma$, to the lift, resolving forces vertically upwards:
$$F_{net} = T - M_L g - ( ext{Force from Alan on floor}) - ( ext{Force from Bhavana on floor})$$
$$F_{net} = T - M_L g - 565 - 226$$
Since the lift's mass is $M_L = 250 ext{ kg}$, the equation becomes:
$$T - M_L g - 565 - 226 = M_L a$$
Substitute the known values ($T = 3616 ext{ N}$, $M_L = 250 ext{ kg}$, $g = 9.8 ext{ m/s}^2$):
$$3616 - (250 imes 9.8) - 565 - 226 = 250a$$
$$3616 - 2450 - 565 - 226 = 250a$$
$$3616 - 3241 = 250a$$
$$375 = 250a$$
$$a = rac{375}{250}$$
$$a = 1.5 ext{ m/s}^2$$

Answer: The acceleration of the lift is $1.5 ext{ m/s}^2$.

---

Part (b): Find the mass of Alan.

Thought process: We now need to find Alan's mass. We know the forces acting *on Alan* and his acceleration (which is the same as the lift's acceleration, found in part (a)). We will apply Newton's Second Law ($F=ma$) specifically to Alan, again taking the upward direction as positive.

The forces acting on Alan are:


  1. The normal reaction force from the lift floor, acting upwards. This is given as $565 ext{ N}$.

  2. Alan's weight, acting downwards.

Working:
Let $M_A$ be the mass of Alan. Let $R_{floor o Alan}$ be the normal force exerted by the floor on Alan ($565 ext{ N}$). Alan's acceleration is $a = 1.5 ext{ m/s}^2$ (from part a).

Applying Newton's Second Law, $F=ma$, to Alan, resolving forces vertically upwards:
$$F_{net} = R_{floor o Alan} - M_A g$$
Since Alan is accelerating with the lift:
$$R_{floor o Alan} - M_A g = M_A a$$
Substitute the known values ($R_{floor o Alan} = 565 ext{ N}$, $g = 9.8 ext{ m/s}^2$, $a = 1.5 ext{ m/s}^2$):
$$565 - (M_A imes 9.8) = M_A imes 1.5$$
Now, rearrange the equation to solve for $M_A$:
$$565 = 1.5 M_A + 9.8 M_A$$
$$565 = (1.5 + 9.8) M_A$$
$$565 = 11.3 M_A$$
$$M_A = rac{565}{11.3}$$
$$M_A = 50 ext{ kg}$$

Answer: The mass of Alan is $50 ext{ kg}$.

Key techniques

  • Identify the system of interest for F=ma
  • Draw/visualize free-body diagrams to identify forces
  • Apply Newton's Second Law by resolving forces in the direction of acceleration
  • Understand Newton's Third Law for action-reaction pairs

Common pitfalls

  • Incorrectly identifying the system for F=ma: For part (a), a common mistake is to include the weights of Alan and Bhavana in the *total mass* $M_L + M_A + M_B$ on the right side of $F=ma$, while simultaneously deducting their *reaction forces* from the tension on the left side. The mark scheme's approach treats the lift's mass as $250 ext{ kg}$ and the forces from Alan/Bhavana as external forces *on the lift structure*.
  • Sign errors in F=ma: Consistently taking one direction (e.g., upwards) as positive is crucial. If the object is accelerating, the net force must be in the direction of acceleration.
  • Confusing normal reaction force with weight: For part (b), students might mistakenly use Alan's normal reaction force as his weight or ignore his weight entirely.
