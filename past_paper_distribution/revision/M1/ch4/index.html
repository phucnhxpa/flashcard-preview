Direct Collisions, Momentum & Impulse
14×
4-10m

Two or more particles (A, B) are moving along the same straight line and collide. You are given initial velocities and masses, and asked to find final velocities, impulse, or loss in kinetic energy.

Triggerscollisionimpulsemomentumcoalescedirect impactparticles collide
Assumptions
  • Particles are smooth, so there are no rotational effects.
  • The collision is instantaneous, so non-impulsive forces like gravity or resistance are ignored during the moment of impact.
  • Motion occurs in one dimension (a straight line).
Approaches
The Standard Collision Algorithm
When: This is the universal method for all 1D collision problems.
  1. Draw a clear 'Before' and 'After' diagram, showing particles and their velocities.
  2. Establish a positive direction (e.g., right is positive) and write it on your diagram. This is the most critical step.
  3. List all known variables ($m_A, m_B, u_A, u_B, ...$), ensuring velocities have the correct sign according to your chosen direction.
  4. Apply the Principle of Conservation of Linear Momentum (CLM): $m_A u_A + m_B u_B = m_A v_A + m_B v_B$.
  5. If the collision is inelastic and particles coalesce, they have a common final velocity: $m_A u_A + m_B u_B = (m_A + m_B)v$.
  6. If asked for impulse, apply the Impulse-Momentum Principle to one particle, e.g., for particle A: $I = m_A v_A - m_A u_A$.
  7. Remember that the impulse on particle B is equal and opposite to the impulse on A ($I_B = -I_A$) by Newton's 3rd Law.
+ Pros: A systematic, reliable process that works for every collision question.
– Cons: Highly susceptible to sign errors if the positive direction is not defined and used consistently.
Formulas
$p = mv$ (Definition of momentum) (MEMORIZE)
$\sum m_i u_i = \sum m_i v_i$ (Conservation of Linear Momentum) (formula booklet)
$I = mv - mu$ (Impulse-Momentum Principle) (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • **Sign Errors:** Forgetting to assign negative signs to velocities in the opposite direction to your defined positive direction.
  • **Impulse Confusion:** Confusing the impulse *on particle A* with the impulse *on particle B*. They are equal and opposite.
  • **Units:** Forgetting to convert mass from grams to kilograms before calculation.
  • **Speed vs. Velocity:** Answering with a negative velocity when the question asks for final speed (which must be positive).

N2L: Connected Particles on Inclined Planes (with Friction)
7×
11-18m

The classic high-mark M1 question. A particle on a rough inclined plane is connected by a string over a pulley to another particle that is either hanging freely or on another plane. The system is released from rest.

Triggersinclined planeangle $\alpha$coefficient of frictionsmooth light pulleylight inextensible stringreleased from rest
Assumptions
  • 'Light string' → Massless, and tension is uniform throughout its length.
  • 'Inextensible string' → Cannot stretch, so the magnitude of acceleration is the same for both particles.
  • 'Smooth pulley' → No friction at the pulley, so tension is the same on both sides of the string.
  • 'Rough plane' → A frictional force acts to oppose motion, and $F_f = \mu R$ when moving.
  • 'Particle' → Dimensions are negligible, so it can be treated as a point mass with no rotation.
Approaches
A: Separate FBDs and Simultaneous Equations (Recommended)
When: This is the most robust and reliable method for these complex problems.
  1. Draw a large, separate Free-Body Diagram (FBD) for EACH particle, labelling all forces (Weight, Tension, Normal Reaction, Friction).
  2. For the particle on the incline, resolve its weight ($mg$) into components parallel ($mg \sin \theta$) and perpendicular ($mg \cos \theta$) to the plane.
  3. Apply N2L perpendicular to the plane for the inclined particle to find the Normal Reaction, $R$. (Usually $R - mg \cos \theta = 0$).
  4. Calculate the maximum/kinetic friction force using $F_f = \mu R$.
  5. Determine the direction of motion and ensure friction opposes it.
  6. Apply N2L ($F_{net}=ma$) parallel to the direction of motion for EACH particle separately. This will give you two equations with two unknowns, `a` and `T`.
  7. Solve the simultaneous equations, usually by adding them to eliminate `T` and find `a` first.
+ Pros: Methodical, forces you to account for all forces on each particle, reducing risk of error.
– Cons: Involves more algebraic steps (solving simultaneous equations) than the whole system approach.
B: Whole System Equation
When: A quick method or a check if you only need to find the acceleration `a`.
  1. Visualize the string 'straightened out' and identify all forces that drive the motion and all forces that resist it.
  2. Apply N2L to the entire system: (Sum of Driving Forces) - (Sum of Resisting Forces) = (Total Mass) $\times$ a.
  3. Solve the single equation for `a`.
  4. To find tension `T`, you must then isolate one of the particles and apply $F_{net}=ma$ to it using your calculated value of `a`.
+ Pros: The fastest way to find acceleration `a`.
– Cons: Does not find tension `T` directly and it's easy to miss a force component (like friction or a weight component).
Formulas
$\vec{F}_{net} = m\vec{a}$ (Newton's Second Law) (formula booklet)
$W = mg$ (Weight) (formula booklet)
$F_f \le \mu R$ (Friction Model), with $F_f = \mu R$ for motion or limiting equilibrium (formula booklet)
Weight component parallel to incline: $mg \sin \theta$ (MEMORIZE)
Weight component perpendicular to incline: $mg \cos \theta$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • **Resolving Errors:** Mixing up $\sin \theta$ and $\cos \theta$ for the weight components. Remember: $\sin$ is for 'sliding' down the slope.
  • **Friction Direction:** Drawing the friction force in the wrong direction. It ALWAYS opposes the direction of motion or intended motion.
  • **Missing Forces:** Forgetting a force on a diagram, most commonly the tension `T` or a component of weight.
  • **Pulley Forces:** When asked for force on the pulley, remember it's the vector sum of the two tension forces acting on it.

N2L: Connected Particles (Horizontal & Vertical Motion)
4×
7-18m

A classic pulley system. One particle rests on a smooth or rough horizontal table, connected by a string over a pulley at the edge of the table to a second, hanging particle.

Triggersparticle on a tablehangs freelysmooth pulleylight inextensible stringrough horizontal plane
Assumptions
  • 'Light string' → Massless, uniform tension.
  • 'Inextensible string' → Both particles have the same magnitude of acceleration.
  • 'Smooth pulley' → Tension is the same on both sides of the string.
  • The string is taut and remains parallel to the table for the particle on the table.
Approaches
A: Separate FBDs and Simultaneous Equations
When: Always works. The most reliable method, especially if you need to find tension.
  1. Draw separate FBDs for the particle on the table and the hanging particle.
  2. For the particle on the table: Forces are Tension (T), Weight (W), Normal Reaction (R), and possibly Friction (F_f).
  3. For the hanging particle: Forces are Tension (T) and Weight (W).
  4. Define a consistent direction of motion (e.g., clockwise around the pulley is positive).
  5. Apply $F_{net}=ma$ to each particle along the line of motion.
  6. For particle on table: e.g., $T - F_f = m_A a$.
  7. For hanging particle: e.g., $m_B g - T = m_B a$.
  8. Solve the two simultaneous equations for `a` and `T`.
+ Pros: Systematic and finds all unknowns (`a` and `T`) clearly.
– Cons: Slightly more time-consuming than the whole system approach.
B: Whole System Equation
When: Excellent for quickly finding the acceleration `a`.
  1. Identify the driving force (the weight of the hanging particle, $m_B g$).
  2. Identify any resistive forces (friction on the table, $F_f$).
  3. Apply N2L to the whole system: Driving Force - Resistive Force = (Total Mass) $\times$ a.
  4. Equation: $m_B g - F_f = (m_A + m_B)a$.
  5. Solve for `a`.
+ Pros: Very fast for finding acceleration.
– Cons: Tension is an internal force and is cancelled out, so it cannot be found with this method directly.
Formulas
$\vec{F}_{net} = m\vec{a}$ (Newton's Second Law) (formula booklet)
$W = mg$ (Weight) (formula booklet)
$F_f = \mu R$ (Friction Model, when moving) (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • **Incorrect Forces on Table Particle:** Forgetting the normal reaction R, or assuming R is always equal to mg (it is, unless a vertical force is applied).
  • **Sign Errors in Equations:** Mismatching the direction of motion with the signs in the $F=ma$ equations.
  • **Unit Conversions:** Forgetting to convert mass in tonnes to kg (e.g., Jun 2022 Q3).

N2L: Single Particle on an Inclined Plane
3×
6-15m

A single particle or object is on a rough or smooth inclined plane. It might be held in equilibrium, on the point of sliding, or accelerating under various forces. Often combined with SUVAT.

Triggersbox on a rampparticle on a planeinclined at angle $\theta$limiting equilibriumpushed up the plane
Assumptions
  • The object is treated as a particle and does not topple.
  • Air resistance is negligible.
Approaches
Resolve Parallel and Perpendicular to the Plane
When: This is the standard and only method for this question type.
  1. Draw one large, clear FBD showing all forces: Weight ($mg$) vertically down, Normal Reaction ($R$) perpendicular to the plane (outwards), Friction ($F_f$) parallel to the plane, and any other applied forces.
  2. Choose axes parallel and perpendicular to the plane.
  3. Resolve all forces into components along these axes. The only force that usually needs resolving is weight: $mg \sin \theta$ parallel and $mg \cos \theta$ perpendicular.
  4. Apply equilibrium or N2L perpendicular to the plane: $\sum F_{\perp} = 0$. This almost always gives you the value of $R$.
  5. Apply equilibrium or N2L parallel to the plane: $\sum F_{\parallel} = ma$.
  6. If in limiting equilibrium, $a=0$ and $F_f = \mu R$. This allows you to find $\mu$ or the force required.
  7. If accelerating, use the equation from the parallel step to find `a`, which can then be used in SUVAT equations if needed.
+ Pros: A consistent, logical process that breaks the problem down into two simpler 1D problems.
– Cons: Requires accurate diagram and careful trigonometry to avoid sin/cos mix-ups.
Formulas
$\vec{F}_{net} = m\vec{a}$ (Newton's Second Law) (formula booklet)
$F_f \le \mu R$ (Friction Model) (formula booklet)
$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$, etc. (SUVAT equations) (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • **Friction Direction:** Friction opposes motion or *tendency* of motion. If a box is pushed *up* a slope, friction acts *down*. If it's about to slide *down*, friction acts *up*.
  • **Resolving Errors:** Swapping $\sin \theta$ and $\cos \theta$. Double-check your right-angled triangle.
  • **Normal Reaction:** Forgetting that $R$ changes if an applied force has a component perpendicular to the plane.

N2L: Lift / Apparent Weight Problems
2×
6-7m

A person, a box, or a parcel is inside a lift (elevator). The lift accelerates up or down. The question is about the 'apparent weight', which is the Normal Reaction force, or the tension in the lift cable.

Triggersliftelevatoraccelerates upwardsdecelerates downwardsreaction forcetension in the cable
Assumptions
  • The lift cable is light and inextensible.
  • The acceleration is constant during each phase of motion.
Approaches
Apply N2L to the Object or the System
When: This is the standard approach for all lift problems.
  1. Identify the body of interest: is it the person/parcel *inside* the lift, or the lift *itself* (including contents)?
  2. Draw a FBD for that body. Define upwards as the positive direction.
  3. **To find reaction on person/parcel (R):** The forces are Weight ($mg$) down and Normal Reaction ($R$) up. Apply N2L: $R - mg = ma$.
  4. **To find tension in lift cable (T):** The body is the lift + contents (Total Mass M). Forces are Tension ($T$) up and Total Weight ($Mg$) down. Apply N2L: $T - Mg = Ma$.
  5. Carefully substitute the value of acceleration `a`. If accelerating upwards, `a` is positive. If accelerating downwards, `a` is negative. Decelerating upwards is negative `a`; decelerating downwards is positive `a`.
  6. Solve for the unknown force (R or T).
+ Pros: A direct application of Newton's Second Law.
– Cons: Very prone to sign errors with acceleration. It's crucial to get the direction of `a` correct.
Formulas
$\vec{F}_{net} = m\vec{a}$ (Newton's Second Law) (formula booklet)
$W = mg$ (Weight) (formula booklet)
Apparent Weight (Accelerating Up): $R = m(g+a)$
Apparent Weight (Accelerating Down): $R = m(g-a)$
Pitfalls
  • **Sign Errors:** The most common mistake. Using $mg - R = ma$ when accelerating up, or mixing up signs for deceleration.
  • **System Choice:** Using the mass of just the person when calculating the tension in the main lift cable, or vice-versa.
  • **Deceleration:** Confusing deceleration with direction. 'Decelerating while moving up' means acceleration is downwards (negative). 'Decelerating while moving down' means acceleration is upwards (positive).

Must memorize

  • $p = mv$ (Momentum = mass x velocity). This basic definition is assumed knowledge.
  • Newton's Three Laws conceptually. (1: Inertia, 2: F=ma, 3: Action-Reaction).
  • Weight components on a plane inclined at $\theta$ to the horizontal: $mg \sin \theta$ acting parallel (down the slope) and $mg \cos \theta$ acting perpendicular (into the slope).
  • The conditions for using the friction formula: $F_f < \mu R$ for static equilibrium, $F_f = \mu R$ for limiting equilibrium (on the point of moving) or when moving.

Global strategy

  • **1. Diagram is King:** Always start every single question by drawing a large, clear diagram. For connected particles, draw a diagram of the system AND separate free-body diagrams for each particle.
  • **2. Define Positive Direction:** Explicitly state and label your positive direction on your diagram for every question involving vectors (force, velocity, acceleration). This prevents 90% of sign errors.
  • **3. Isolate the Body:** When applying $F=ma$, be absolutely clear which object or system of objects you are applying it to. Forces external to that body are included; internal forces (like tension within a system) are not.
  • **4. Resolve, Don't Rotate:** For inclined plane problems, always resolve forces parallel and perpendicular to the plane. Do not try to resolve forces horizontally and vertically.
  • **5. Write the Principle First:** Before substituting numbers, write down the formula or principle you are using (e.g., 'CLM:', 'Impulse = mv - mu', '$F_{net}=ma$'). This secures method marks and clarifies your thinking.
  • **6. Check Units:** Be vigilant for non-SI units. Convert mass (g, tonnes) to kg, and check all lengths are in metres and times in seconds before using formulas.
  • **7. Check your Answer's Sanity:** Does the result make physical sense? Is the tension in a string less than the weight of the particle it's supporting if it's accelerating down? Is the final speed plausible?

Exam tips

  1. Use $g = 9.8 \text{ ms}^{-2}$ in all calculations unless the question specifies otherwise.
  2. Give final answers to 2 or 3 significant figures, as is standard for mechanics. Do not round intermediate calculations; use your calculator's memory function.
  3. If the direction of motion is not obvious in a connected particle problem, make an assumption. If the resulting acceleration `a` is negative, your assumed direction was wrong, but the magnitude is correct.
  4. Remember that tension `T` in a string always acts as a pull, away from the object it is attached to.
  5. Friction `F` always opposes motion or the tendency of motion. If a particle is being pulled up an incline, friction acts down the incline.
