Connected Particles (Pulleys)
11×
7-18m

Two particles, A and B, are connected by a 'light inextensible string' passing over a 'smooth light pulley'. One particle is typically hanging freely while the other is on a horizontal or inclined plane. Friction is often involved. The system is released from rest and you need to find acceleration, tension, or subsequent motion.

Core strategy
  1. 1. **Draw Big, Clear Diagrams:** Sketch a separate free-body diagram for EACH particle. Label ALL forces: Weight ($W=mg$) acting vertically down, Tension ($T$) acting along the string away from the particle, Normal Reaction ($R$) acting perpendicular to a surface, and Friction ($F_f$) opposing motion.
  2. 2. **Resolve Forces:** For any particle on an inclined plane, resolve its weight into components parallel ($mg \sin \theta$) and perpendicular ($mg \cos \theta$) to the plane.
  3. 3. **Set Up Equations Perpendicular to Motion:** For any particle on a surface, apply Newton's laws perpendicular to the direction of acceleration. The resultant force is zero. This step is almost always used to find the normal reaction, R.
  4. 4. **Set Up Equations Parallel to Motion:** Define a single direction of acceleration 'a' for the whole system. Apply $F_{net} = ma$ to each particle separately, parallel to the direction of motion. Be meticulously consistent with signs: forces in the direction of 'a' are positive, forces opposing it are negative.
  5. 5. **Incorporate Friction:** If the surface is rough, calculate the maximum friction using $F_f = \mu R$ once you have found R.
  6. 6. **Solve Simultaneously:** You will have a pair of linear equations with 'a' and 'T' as unknowns. Solve them simultaneously.
  7. 7. **Handle Post-Motion Scenarios:** If the string breaks or a particle hits the ground, the system changes. Recalculate the new acceleration (which might be just 'g' or a new value based on remaining forces) for the subsequent phase of motion and use SUVAT.
Must-know formulas
Newton's Second Law: $F_{net} = ma$ (MEMORIZE)
Weight: $W = mg$ (MEMORIZE)
Friction model: $F_f \le \mu R$ (formula booklet)
Equations of constant acceleration (SUVAT) (formula booklet)
Common pitfalls
  • Incorrectly resolving weight on an incline (mixing up $\sin \theta$ and $\cos \theta$).
  • Getting the direction of friction wrong – it ALWAYS opposes motion or the tendency of motion.
  • Sign errors in the $F=ma$ equations. Choose a positive direction for acceleration and stick to it for the entire system.
  • Forgetting to recalculate acceleration if the system changes (e.g., string snaps).

Collisions: Momentum & Impulse
14×
4-10m

Keywords: 'collides directly', 'impulse', 'momentum', 'coalesce'. Two particles P and Q are moving along the same straight line and collide. You are given initial speeds and masses, and asked to find final velocities or the impulse.

Core strategy
  1. 1. **Draw 'Before' and 'After' Diagrams:** Sketch the situation immediately before and immediately after the collision. This helps visualize directions.
  2. 2. **Define a Positive Direction:** State your sign convention explicitly on your page (e.g., arrow pointing right with a '+' sign). This is the single most important step to avoid errors.
  3. 3. **Assign Signs to Velocities:** Write down the initial velocities ($u_1, u_2$) with the correct signs according to your chosen direction. A velocity in the opposite direction is negative.
  4. 4. **Apply Conservation of Linear Momentum (CLM):** Write down the equation $m_1u_1 + m_2u_2 = m_1v_1 + m_2v_2$. Substitute your known values carefully, including the negative signs.
  5. 5. **Apply Impulse-Momentum Principle (if needed):** If asked for the impulse on a particle, use $J = \Delta p = mv - mu$ for THAT particle. Remember impulse is a vector, so its sign indicates direction.
  6. 6. **Apply Newton's Law of Restitution (if 'e' is given):** Use the formula $v_2 - v_1 = -e(u_2 - u_1)$ if the coefficient of restitution is known or asked for.
  7. 7. **Solve and Interpret:** Solve the resulting equation(s). The sign of your final answer for a velocity or impulse indicates its direction. If the question asks for 'speed', give the positive magnitude.
Must-know formulas
Momentum: $p = mv$ (MEMORIZE)
Conservation of Linear Momentum: $m_1u_1 + m_2u_2 = m_1v_1 + m_2v_2$ (MEMORIZE)
Impulse-Momentum Principle: $J = mv - mu$ (MEMORIZE)
Newton's Law of Restitution: $v_2 - v_1 = -e(u_2 - u_1)$ (formula booklet)
Common pitfalls
  • **Sign Errors:** The most common mistake. Forgetting to assign negative signs to velocities in the opposite direction to the one you defined as positive.
  • Confusing 'speed' (a scalar) with 'velocity' (a vector) in the equations.
  • Calculating impulse on the wrong particle, or getting the sign wrong. The impulse on P is equal and opposite to the impulse on Q ($J_{on P} = -J_{on Q}$).
  • Arithmetic errors when solving simultaneous equations from CLM and NLR.

Single Particle on an Inclined Plane
3×
6-15m

A single block, box, or particle is on a 'rough' or 'smooth' plane inclined at an angle. It may be held in equilibrium, be on the point of slipping (limiting equilibrium), or accelerating up/down the plane due to an applied force.

Core strategy
  1. 1. **Draw a Large, Clear Diagram:** Label all forces: Weight ($mg$) acting vertically downwards, Normal Reaction ($R$) acting perpendicular outwards from the plane, Friction ($F_f$) acting parallel to the plane opposing motion/tendency of motion, and any other applied forces.
  2. 2. **Set Up Perpendicular & Parallel Axes:** Align your coordinate system with the plane itself.
  3. 3. **Resolve Weight:** Resolve the weight vector into its components: $mg \sin \theta$ acting parallel down the plane, and $mg \cos \theta$ acting perpendicular into the plane.
  4. 4. **Find Normal Reaction:** Sum the forces perpendicular to the plane and set the resultant to zero (as there's no acceleration in this direction). This will give you the value of R.
  5. 5. **Apply $F_{net}=ma$ Parallel to the Plane:** Identify the direction of actual or potential motion. Sum the forces in this direction to find the net force, and set it equal to $ma$. If in equilibrium, $a=0$.
  6. 6. **Use the Friction Model:** If the object is moving or in limiting equilibrium, use the formula $F_f = \mu R$.
Must-know formulas
Newton's Second Law: $F_{net} = ma$ (MEMORIZE)
Friction model: $F_f \le \mu R$ (formula booklet)
Weight components on incline: $mg \sin \theta$ (parallel), $mg \cos \theta$ (perpendicular) (MEMORIZE)
Common pitfalls
  • Mixing up $\sin \theta$ and $\cos \theta$ for the weight components. Remember: $\sin$ 'slides' (parallel), $\cos$ 'compresses' (perpendicular).
  • Drawing the Normal Reaction or Weight in the wrong direction. Weight is ALWAYS vertically down.
  • Incorrectly identifying the direction of friction. If an object is pushed up the slope, friction acts down the slope.
  • Assuming friction is at its maximum ($F_f = \mu R$) when it is not (i.e., when in non-limiting equilibrium).

Newton's 2nd Law in a Lift (Apparent Weight)
2×
6-7m

A person or a parcel of mass m is inside a lift which is accelerating upwards or downwards. You are asked to find the reaction force from the floor of the lift, or the tension in the supporting cable.

Core strategy
  1. 1. **Isolate the Body:** Draw a free-body diagram for the object OF INTEREST (e.g., just the person, or just the parcel, or the entire lift system).
  2. 2. **Label Vertical Forces:** Identify the two main forces: Weight ($mg$) acting downwards, and the contact/support force (Normal Reaction $R$, or Tension $T$) acting upwards.
  3. 3. **Identify Acceleration:** Read the question carefully to determine the direction and magnitude of the acceleration, 'a'. Constant velocity means $a=0$.
  4. 4. **Apply $F_{net} = ma$ Vertically:** Choose a positive direction (it's easiest to choose the direction of acceleration as positive). Write down the equation of motion.
  5. - **Accelerating UP:** Upwards force is bigger. $R - mg = ma$.
  6. - **Accelerating DOWN:** Downwards force is bigger. $mg - R = ma$.
  7. 5. **Solve for the Unknown:** Rearrange the equation to find the required force (R or T).
Must-know formulas
Newton's Second Law: $F_{net} = ma$ (MEMORIZE)
Weight: $W = mg$ (MEMORIZE)
Common pitfalls
  • Sign errors in the $F=ma$ equation. Remember: Resultant Force = Mass x Acceleration.
  • Confusing mass with weight. The reaction force is the 'apparent weight', not the actual weight $mg$.
  • Using $a = 9.8$ m/s² when the lift is accelerating. 'g' is the acceleration due to gravity, not the acceleration of the lift.
  • Incorrectly identifying the system. If asked for tension in the cable holding the lift, the 'mass' must be the total mass of the lift and its contents.

Must memorize

  • **Newton's Second Law: $F_{net} = ma$**. The single most important equation in the chapter. It is not in the formula booklet.
  • **Weight: $W = mg$**. The force due to gravity. Use $g = 9.8$ m/s² unless told otherwise. Not in the booklet.
  • **Conservation of Linear Momentum:** The principle that 'total momentum before collision = total momentum after collision'. The formula $m_1u_1 + m_2u_2 = m_1v_1 + m_2v_2$ is the application of this and must be memorized.
  • **Impulse-Momentum Principle: $J = mv - mu$**. The definition of impulse as the change in momentum. Not in the booklet.

Global strategy

  • **Always Draw a Diagram:** For any forces question, your first step must be to draw a large, clear, well-labelled free-body diagram. This is where most marks are gained or lost.
  • **Be Consistent With Signs:** In any problem involving vectors (velocity, force, acceleration, impulse), define a positive direction at the start and apply it consistently to all quantities.
  • **Isolate the System:** When applying $F=ma$, be clear about which object or system of objects you are applying it to. Draw a conceptual 'bubble' around it to identify internal vs. external forces.
  • **Units, Units, Units:** Always convert all quantities to standard SI units before starting calculations: mass in kg, distance in m, time in s, speed in m/s. Watch out for tonnes and grams.
  • **Resolve, Don't Rotate:** For inclined plane problems, always resolve forces into components parallel and perpendicular to the slope. Do not try to resolve perpendicular to gravity.
  • **Show Your Working:** Structure your work clearly. State the principle you are using (e.g., 'CLM:', 'For particle A:') before writing the equation. This helps you think clearly and helps the examiner award method marks.

Exam tips

  1. For the long, high-mark connected particle questions, work methodically and don't rush the setup. The marks are awarded for the correct force diagrams and equations of motion, so get those right first.
  2. In collision questions, state your positive direction with an arrow on the page before you even write an equation. It takes 2 seconds and prevents the most common error.
  3. If a question has multiple parts (e.g., find acceleration, then find time), and you get stuck on part (a), read part (b). You can often use a 'show that' value or assume a reasonable value for the acceleration to attempt part (b) and get follow-through marks.
  4. When a question asks for a 'force exerted by particle A on particle B', this is an action-reaction pair. The force on B is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction to the force on A.
  5. Always give your final answers to an appropriate degree of accuracy, typically 2 or 3 significant figures, and don't forget units (N for force, Ns for impulse).
