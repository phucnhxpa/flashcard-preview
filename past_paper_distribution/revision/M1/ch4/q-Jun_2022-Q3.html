3.	 A tractor of mass 6  tonnes is dragging a large block of mass 2  tonnes along rough 
horizontal ground. The cable connecting the tractor to the block is horizontal and parallel 
to the direction of motion.
	
The cable is modelled as being light and inextensible.
	
The driving force of the tractor is 7400 N and the resistance to the motion of the tractor 
is 200 N. The resistance to the motion of the block is R newtons, where R is a constant.
	
Given that the tension in the cable is 6000 N and the tractor is accelerating,
	
(a)	 find the value of R.
(6)
	
(b)	State how you have used the fact that the cable is modelled as being inextensible.
(1)

Worked solution

Thought Process

We recognize this as a connected particles problem because there are two distinct objects (tractor and block) linked by a cable, moving together. The core principle for such problems is Newton's Second Law: $F=ma$. We will apply this law to each object separately.

First, we need to handle the masses. They are given in tonnes, so we must convert them to kilograms (kg) for use in $F=ma$. Remember that 1 tonne = 1000 kg.

We'll consider the forces acting on each object in the direction of motion. It's helpful to visualize (or sketch) a free-body diagram for each object. For horizontal motion, we typically only need to consider horizontal forces.

For the tractor:


  • Driving force: acts in the direction of motion.

  • Resistance: acts opposite to the direction of motion.

  • Tension in the cable: pulls the tractor *backwards* (it's pulling the block), so it acts opposite to the direction of motion relative to the tractor.

For the block:


  • Tension in the cable: pulls the block *forwards*, so it acts in the direction of motion.

  • Resistance ($R$): acts opposite to the direction of motion.

Crucially, because the cable is inextensible, both the tractor and the block will have the same acceleration, $a$. Since the cable is light, the tension is uniform throughout its length.

Part (a) asks for $R$. We are given the tension ($T = 6000$ N). This means we don't need to set up simultaneous equations for $a$ and $T$. We can first use Newton's Second Law on the tractor to find the acceleration ($a$), and then use that value of $a$ (along with the given tension) in Newton's Second Law for the block to find $R$.

Part (b) directly asks for the implication of the cable being inextensible, which is precisely the point about common acceleration.

Working

1. Convert masses to kg:
Mass of tractor, $M_T = 6 ext{ tonnes} = 6 imes 1000 = 6000 ext{ kg}$
Mass of block, $M_B = 2 ext{ tonnes} = 2 imes 1000 = 2000 ext{ kg}$

2. Apply Newton's Second Law ($F=ma$) to the tractor:
Let the acceleration of the system be $a ext{ m/s}^2$.
Forces on the tractor in the direction of motion:


  • Driving force ($D$) = $7400 ext{ N}$ (positive)

  • Resistance to tractor motion ($R_T$) = $200 ext{ N}$ (negative)

  • Tension in cable ($T$) = $6000 ext{ N}$ (pulls the tractor backwards, so negative)

Using $F=Ma$ for the tractor:
$$D - R_T - T = M_T a$$
$$7400 - 200 - 6000 = 6000a$$
$$1200 = 6000a$$
$$a = rac{1200}{6000}$$
$$a = 0.2 ext{ m/s}^2$$

3. Apply Newton's Second Law ($F=ma$) to the block:
Forces on the block in the direction of motion:


  • Tension in cable ($T$) = $6000 ext{ N}$ (pulls the block forwards, so positive)

  • Resistance to block motion ($R$) = $R ext{ N}$ (negative, this is what we need to find)

  • Acceleration ($a$) = $0.2 ext{ m/s}^2$ (same as tractor)

Using $F=Ma$ for the block:
$$T - R = M_B a$$
$$6000 - R = 2000(0.2)$$
$$6000 - R = 400$$
$$R = 6000 - 400$$
$$R = 5600 ext{ N}$$

Answer (a): $R = 5600 ext{ N}$

(b) State how you have used the fact that the cable is modelled as being inextensible.

Since the cable is inextensible, both the tractor and the block move together and therefore have the same acceleration.

Answer (b): The tractor and the block have the same acceleration.

Key techniques

  • Applying F=ma to individual bodies
  • Converting units (tonnes to kg)
  • Understanding properties of light, inextensible cables
  • Solving simultaneous equations (implicitly here)

Common pitfalls

  • Forgetting to convert mass from tonnes to kg, leading to incorrect calculations.
  • Incorrectly assigning the direction of forces, especially tension (it pulls the object it's attached to, so it opposes the driving force on the tractor but aids motion for the block).
  • Not explicitly stating that *both* objects have the same acceleration in part (b), or just saying 'same acceleration' without context.
