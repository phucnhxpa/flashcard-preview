Worked solution

Part (a): Find the acceleration of P

Thought Process:
This problem involves a particle on a rough inclined plane subject to an applied force. The particle is accelerating, so we need to apply Newton's Second Law, $F=ma$. To do this effectively, we must first resolve all forces into components parallel and perpendicular to the inclined plane. We'll set up two equations: one for the forces perpendicular to the plane (where acceleration is zero, so net force is zero) to find the normal reaction $R$, and one for the forces parallel to the plane (where there is acceleration $a$) to find $a$. Friction will oppose the motion, acting down the plane, and will be at its maximum value, $F_r = \mu R$, because the particle is accelerating.

Working (a):
Let the mass of the particle be $m=2$ kg. The angle of inclination of the plane is $\alpha = 30^\circ$. The coefficient of friction is $\mu = 0.3$. The applied force is $18$ N at an angle of $40^\circ$ above the line of greatest slope. We'll use $g=9.8$ m/s$^2$.

First, we resolve forces perpendicular to the plane. In this direction, there is no acceleration, so the net force is zero.
Forces perpendicular to the plane are:


  • Normal reaction $R$ (acting outwards from the plane).

  • Component of the applied force: $18 \sin 40^\circ$ (acting outwards from the plane).

  • Component of the weight $mg \cos 30^\circ$ (acting into the plane).

$$R + 18 \sin 40^\circ - mg \cos 30^\circ = 0$$
Substituting $m=2$ and $g=9.8$:
$$R = 2g \cos 30^\circ - 18 \sin 40^\circ$$
$$R = 2(9.8) \cos 30^\circ - 18 \sin 40^\circ$$
$$R = 19.6(0.8660) - 18(0.6428)$$
$$R = 16.9736 - 11.5704$$
$$R = 5.4032 \text{ N}$$

Next, we resolve forces parallel to the plane. The particle accelerates up the plane with acceleration $a$. Friction opposes motion, so it acts down the plane.
Forces parallel to the plane are:


  • Component of the applied force: $18 \cos 40^\circ$ (acting up the plane).

  • Component of the weight $mg \sin 30^\circ$ (acting down the plane).

  • Friction force $F_r$ (acting down the plane).

Applying Newton's Second Law ($F_{net} = ma$):
$$18 \cos 40^\circ - mg \sin 30^\circ - F_r = ma$$
We know that $F_r = \mu R$. Substituting $\mu = 0.3$ and the value of $R$:
$$F_r = 0.3 \times 5.4032 = 1.62096 \text{ N}$$

Substitute $m=2$, $g=9.8$, and $F_r$ into the parallel forces equation:
$$18 \cos 40^\circ - 2g \sin 30^\circ - 1.62096 = 2a$$
$$18(0.7660) - 2(9.8)(0.5) - 1.62096 = 2a$$
$$13.788 - 9.8 - 1.62096 = 2a$$
$$2.36704 = 2a$$
$$a = 1.18352 \text{ m/s}^2$$
Rounding to 3 significant figures:
$$a = 1.18 \text{ m/s}^2$$

Answer: The acceleration of P is $1.18 \text{ m/s}^2$.

Part (b): Find the speed of P after it has moved 5 m

Thought Process:
We have determined the acceleration $a$ in part (a). The particle starts from rest, so its initial velocity $u=0$. We know the distance $s=5$ m and we need to find the final speed $v$. This is a constant acceleration (SUVAT) problem. The appropriate formula is $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$.

Working (b):
Given:


  • Initial velocity $u = 0$ m/s (starts from rest)

  • Distance $s = 5$ m

  • Acceleration $a = 1.18352$ m/s$^2$ (using the more precise value from part (a) to maintain accuracy)

Using the SUVAT equation $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$:
$$v^2 = (0)^2 + 2(1.18352)(5)$$
$$v^2 = 11.8352$$
$$v = \sqrt{11.8352}$$
$$v = 3.44023... \text{ m/s}$$
Rounding to 3 significant figures:
$$v = 3.44 \text{ m/s}$$

Answer: The speed of P after it has moved 5 m is $3.44 \text{ m/s}$.

Part (c): Determine if P will remain at rest when the 18 N force is removed

Thought Process:
When the 18 N force is removed, the particle is subject only to its weight, the normal reaction, and friction. To determine if it remains at rest, we compare the component of weight acting down the plane with the *maximum possible static friction* that can act up the plane. If the weight component is less than or equal to the maximum friction, it remains at rest. If the weight component is greater, it will slide down.

Working (c):
When the 18 N force is removed, the normal reaction $R'$ changes. There is no longer the upward component from the applied force.

First, calculate the new normal reaction $R'$ perpendicular to the plane:
$$R' - mg \cos 30^\circ = 0$$
$$R' = 2g \cos 30^\circ$$
$$R' = 2(9.8) \cos 30^\circ = 19.6(0.8660) = 16.9736 \text{ N}$$

Next, calculate the maximum static friction $F_{r,max}$ that can oppose motion down the plane:
$$F_{r,max} = \mu R'$$
$$F_{r,max} = 0.3 \times 16.9736 = 5.09208 \text{ N}$$

Now, calculate the component of the particle's weight acting parallel to the plane (down the slope):
$$W_{||} = mg \sin 30^\circ$$
$$W_{||} = 2(9.8) \sin 30^\circ = 19.6(0.5) = 9.8 \text{ N}$$

Finally, compare $W_{||}$ with $F_{r,max}$:
Since $W_{||} = 9.8 \text{ N}$ and $F_{r,max} = 5.09208 \text{ N}$, we have $W_{||} > F_{r,max}$.

This means the component of the weight pulling the particle down the plane ($9.8$ N) is greater than the maximum friction force ($5.09$ N) that can prevent it from moving. Therefore, the particle will not remain at rest; it will slide down the plane.

Answer: P will not remain at rest; it will slide down the plane.

Key techniques

  • Resolve forces parallel and perpendicular to plane
  • Apply Newton's Second Law F=ma
  • Use friction model F_friction = μR
  • Apply SUVAT equations
  • Compare forces for equilibrium/motion

Common pitfalls

  • Incorrectly resolving forces (e.g., mixing sine and cosine for angles).
  • Forgetting to calculate a new normal reaction $R$ for part (c) once the applied force is removed.
  • Making sign errors in the $F=ma$ equations, especially when defining positive direction.
  • Confusing initial and final velocities in SUVAT equations, or using wrong SUVAT equation.
