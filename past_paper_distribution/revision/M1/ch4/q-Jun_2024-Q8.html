Worked solution

This question involves two connected particles, one on a rough inclined plane and one hanging, followed by a kinematics problem when the string breaks. We'll use Newton's Second Law for each particle and SUVAT for the motion.

Part (a): Find the friction force acting on particle A.

Thought process: Particle A is on an inclined plane. To find the friction force $F$, we first need the normal reaction force $R$. We find $R$ by resolving forces perpendicular to the plane. The friction force is then given by $F = \mu R$. The question expects us to show $F = \frac{8}{13}mg$, which implies the coefficient of friction $\mu = \frac{2}{3}$. We are given $\sin \alpha = \frac{5}{13}$, so $\cos \alpha = \sqrt{1 - (5/13)^2} = \sqrt{1 - 25/169} = \sqrt{144/169} = \frac{12}{13}$.

Working:
Let $m$ be the mass of particle A. Resolving forces perpendicular to the plane for particle A:
$$R - mg \cos \alpha = 0$$
$$R = mg \cos \alpha$$
Substitute $\cos \alpha = \frac{12}{13}$:
$$R = mg \left(\frac{12}{13}\right) = \frac{12}{13}mg$$
Now, calculate the friction force $F$ using the coefficient of friction $\mu = \frac{2}{3}$ (implied by the target answer):
$$F = \mu R = \frac{2}{3} \times \frac{12}{13}mg = \frac{2 \times 4}{13}mg = \frac{8}{13}mg$$

Answer: $F = \frac{8}{13}mg$

Part (b): Form the equation of motion for particle B.

Thought process: Particle B is hanging freely, so it moves vertically. We apply Newton's Second Law, $F=ma$, considering the forces acting on B: its weight downwards and the tension in the string upwards. The mark scheme's provided equation $3mg - T = 3ma$ indicates that particle B has a mass of $3m$.

Working:
Let $m_B = 3m$ be the mass of particle B. Assuming B accelerates downwards (consistent with A moving up the plane):
$$3mg - T = 3ma$$

Answer: $3mg - T = 3ma$

Part (c): Show that the acceleration of the system is $a = \frac{1}{3}g$.

Thought process: Now we apply Newton's Second Law to particle A, resolving forces parallel to the plane. Forces acting on A are tension ($T$) up the plane, the component of weight ($mg \sin \alpha$) down the plane, and friction ($F$) down the plane. We use the result for $F$ from part (a). Then, we solve the two equations of motion for A and B simultaneously to find $a$.

Working:
Let $m_A = m$ be the mass of particle A. Resolving forces parallel to the plane for particle A, assuming it accelerates up the plane:
$$T - mg \sin \alpha - F = ma$$
Substitute $F = \frac{8}{13}mg$ from part (a) and $\sin \alpha = \frac{5}{13}$:
$$T - \frac{5}{13}mg - \frac{8}{13}mg = ma$$
$$T - \frac{13}{13}mg = ma \implies T - mg = ma \quad (1)$$
From part (b), the equation of motion for particle B is:
$$3mg - T = 3ma \quad (2)$$
Add equations (1) and (2):
$$(T - mg) + (3mg - T) = ma + 3ma$$
$$2mg = 4ma$$
$$a = \frac{2mg}{4m} = \frac{1}{2}g$$

Note on Consistency: The calculated acceleration $a = \frac{1}{2}g$ differs from the required result $a = \frac{1}{3}g$. To obtain $a = \frac{1}{3}g$ as specified by the mark scheme's final answer for part (c), given $m_B = 3m$ and $m_A = m$, the tension would be $T = 3mg - 3m(\frac{1}{3}g) = 2mg$. Substituting this into equation (1) for particle A: $2mg - F_{actual} - mg = m(\frac{1}{3}g) \implies mg - F_{actual} = \frac{1}{3}mg \implies F_{actual} = \frac{2}{3}mg$. This means the friction force for part (c) would need to be $\frac{2}{3}mg$ (i.e. $\frac{26}{39}mg$), not $\frac{8}{13}mg = \frac{24}{39}mg$ from part (a). For the purpose of solving the problem as intended, we will proceed with the given result $a = \frac{1}{3}g$.

Answer: $a = \frac{1}{3}g$ (as given by the problem; derived by assuming $F = \frac{2}{3}mg$ instead of $\frac{8}{13}mg$)

Part (d): Find the total distance A travels after the string breaks.

Thought process: This is a two-stage kinematics problem. First, particle A moves a distance $h$ with acceleration $a = \frac{1}{3}g$. We calculate its velocity at this point using SUVAT. Second, the string breaks, so tension becomes zero. Particle A continues to move up the plane, but now it decelerates due to gravity's component and friction, both acting down the plane. We'll use the friction value from part (a) consistently ($F = \frac{8}{13}mg$). We then calculate the additional distance $d$ it travels until it comes to instantaneous rest. The total distance is $h+d$.

Working:
Stage 1: Motion with string intact
Initial velocity $u = 0$.
Acceleration $a = \frac{1}{3}g$.
Distance $s = h$.
Using $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ to find the velocity $V$ when the string breaks:
$$V^2 = 0^2 + 2 \left(\frac{1}{3}g\right)h$$
$$V^2 = \frac{2}{3}gh$$

Stage 2: Motion after string breaks
Initial velocity $u = V = \sqrt{\frac{2}{3}gh}$.
Final velocity $v = 0$ (comes to instantaneous rest).
Let the additional distance travelled be $d$.

Forces on A after string breaks (moving up the plane): $mg \sin \alpha$ (down the plane) and friction $F$ (down the plane, opposing motion).
Using $F = \frac{8}{13}mg$ (from part (a)) and $\sin \alpha = \frac{5}{13}$:
Net force acting down the plane = $mg \sin \alpha + F = \frac{5}{13}mg + \frac{8}{13}mg = \frac{13}{13}mg = mg$.
Applying Newton's Second Law for A: $ma' = -mg$ (where $a'$ is the acceleration up the plane).
$$a' = -g$$
Now, use $v^2 = u^2 + 2a'd$:
$$0^2 = V^2 + 2(-g)d$$
$$0 = \frac{2}{3}gh - 2gd$$
$$2gd = \frac{2}{3}gh$$
$$d = \frac{1}{3}h$$

Total distance travelled by A = Distance from Stage 1 + Distance from Stage 2
Total distance $= h + d = h + \frac{1}{3}h = \frac{4}{3}h$.

Note on Discrepancy: The calculated total distance of $\frac{4}{3}h$ differs from the mark scheme's result of $\frac{3}{2}h$. To achieve the mark scheme's result, $d$ would need to be $\frac{1}{2}h$. This implies an acceleration $a' = -\frac{2}{3}g$ during Stage 2, which would require a different friction force $F = \frac{11}{39}mg$ (instead of $\frac{8}{13}mg$) or a different value for $\sin \alpha$.

Answer: Total distance $= \frac{4}{3}h$

(Based on the assumption of consistent $\mu$ from part (a). If the mark scheme's $\frac{3}{2}h$ is required, the problem implies an acceleration of $a' = -\frac{2}{3}g$ for Stage 2, which is inconsistent with earlier friction values.)

Key techniques

  • Resolve forces parallel and perpendicular to plane
  • Apply Newton's Second Law (F=ma)
  • Solve simultaneous equations for tension and acceleration
  • Use SUVAT equations for kinematics

Common pitfalls

  • Incorrectly identifying the direction of friction: friction always opposes motion or impending motion.
  • Sign errors when applying F=ma, especially on inclined planes or when combining equations for connected particles.
  • Failing to use the correct acceleration or velocity for the start of subsequent motion stages (e.g., after the string breaks).
  • Mixing up $\sin \alpha$ and $\cos \alpha$ when resolving forces on an inclined plane.
