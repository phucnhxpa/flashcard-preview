Worked solution

We are tackling a problem involving a particle on a rough inclined plane, subjected to an applied force. The particle is stated to be at rest, which means it is in equilibrium ($F_{net}=0$). The core task is to find the maximum possible mass for this equilibrium, implying we need to consider the situation of *limiting equilibrium* where the particle is on the verge of moving.

Our strategy will be to:


  1. Draw a force diagram: This is crucial for visualizing all forces acting on the particle.

  2. Resolve forces: We'll resolve forces perpendicular and parallel to the inclined plane. This is typically the most efficient coordinate system for inclined plane problems.

  3. Apply Newton's Second Law (or equilibrium condition): Since the particle is at rest, the net force in both perpendicular and parallel directions must be zero.

  4. Use the friction model: The maximum static friction is $F_r = \mu R$. The direction of friction is vital here: it opposes the *potential* motion. Since we're finding the *maximum* mass for the particle to remain at rest with an upward force, the particle is on the verge of sliding *down* the plane. Therefore, friction will act *up* the plane, assisting the applied force.

Let's assume the following question (as the original was blank):
"A particle P of mass $m$ kg is placed on a rough plane inclined at an angle $\alpha$ to the horizontal, where $\tan \alpha = \frac{3}{4}$. The coefficient of friction between P and the plane is $\mu = 0.5$. A force of magnitude $10$ N acts on P, parallel to the line of greatest slope and up the plane. Given that P remains at rest, find the maximum possible value of $m$."

WORKING

First, we establish the trigonometric values for the angle $\alpha$. Given $\tan \alpha = \frac{3}{4}$, we can deduce from a 3-4-5 right-angled triangle that:
$$\sin \alpha = \frac{3}{5}$$
$$\cos \alpha = \frac{4}{5}$$

Next, we identify all forces acting on the particle:


  1. Weight ($mg$): Acts vertically downwards.

  2. Normal Reaction ($R$): Acts perpendicular to the plane, upwards from the surface.

  3. Applied Force ($10$ N): Acts parallel to the plane, up the plane.

  4. Friction ($F_r$): Since we are finding the *maximum* mass such that the particle *remains at rest* when the applied force pushes it *up*, the particle is on the verge of sliding *down* the plane. Thus, the friction force acts *up* the plane, opposing this potential downward motion.

Now, resolve forces perpendicular to the plane. For equilibrium, the net force is zero:
$$R - mg \cos \alpha = 0$$
Substitute the value of $\cos \alpha$:
$$R = mg \left(\frac{4}{5}\right)$$
$$R = \frac{4}{5} mg \quad \text{(Equation 1)}$$

Next, we apply the condition for limiting friction. Since the particle is on the verge of motion, the friction force is at its maximum possible value:
$$F_r = \mu R$$
Given $\mu = 0.5$ (or $\frac{1}{2}$), and substituting $R$ from Equation 1:
$$F_r = 0.5 \left(\frac{4}{5} mg\right)$$
$$F_r = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{4}{5} mg$$
$$F_r = \frac{2}{5} mg \quad \text{(Equation 2)}$$

Finally, resolve forces parallel to the plane. For equilibrium, the net force parallel to the plane must be zero. Summing forces in the direction up the plane:
$$10 + F_r - mg \sin \alpha = 0$$
Substitute $F_r$ from Equation 2 and $\sin \alpha = \frac{3}{5}$:
$$10 + \frac{2}{5} mg - mg \left(\frac{3}{5}\right) = 0$$
$$10 + \frac{2}{5} mg - \frac{3}{5} mg = 0$$
$$10 - \frac{1}{5} mg = 0$$
Rearrange to solve for $m$:
$$10 = \frac{1}{5} mg$$
$$50 = mg$$
Assuming $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$:
$$m = \frac{50}{9.8}$$
$$m \approx 5.10204$$
Rounding to 3 significant figures:
$$m = 5.10$$

Answer: The maximum possible value of $m$ is $5.10$ kg (3 s.f.).

Key techniques

  • Draw a force diagram with all forces labelled
  • Resolve forces perpendicular to the plane
  • Resolve forces parallel to the plane
  • Apply the condition for limiting friction ($F_r = \mu R$)
  • Set resultant force to zero for equilibrium ($F_{net}=0$)

Common pitfalls

  • Incorrectly identifying the direction of friction: Always assume friction opposes *potential* motion, not necessarily the applied force.
  • Errors in resolving weight components: Ensure $mg \sin \alpha$ is parallel to the slope and $mg \cos \alpha$ is perpendicular.
  • Failing to use $g$ (gravitational acceleration) when calculating weight ($mg$) or mixing up mass and weight.
