Question 1 continued
Worked solution

Part (a): Find the magnitude of the impulse exerted by P on Q.

Thought Process:
We are asked for the impulse exerted *by P on Q*. By Newton's second law, impulse is the change in momentum. Therefore, we should apply the impulse-momentum principle to particle Q. We need to establish a consistent positive direction for velocities.

Let's define the initial direction of particle P as the positive direction. This means:


  • Initial velocity of P ($u_P$) = $+u$

  • Initial velocity of Q ($u_Q$) = $-u$ (since it's moving in the opposite direction)

After the collision, the direction of motion of each particle is reversed. For Q:


  • Final velocity of Q ($v_Q$) = $+ rac{1}{3}u$ (since it was moving in the negative direction, and reverses, it now moves in the positive direction).

Working:
Particle Q has mass $m_2$.
Initial momentum of Q = $m_2 u_Q = m_2(-u)$
Final momentum of Q = $m_2 v_Q = m_2(+ rac{1}{3}u)$

The impulse exerted by P on Q ($J_{PQ}$) is the change in momentum of Q:
$$J_{PQ} = m_2 v_Q - m_2 u_Q$$
Substitute the values:
$$J_{PQ} = m_2\left( rac{1}{3}u ight) - m_2(-u)$$
$$J_{PQ} = rac{1}{3}m_2u + m_2u$$
$$J_{PQ} = \left( rac{1}{3} + 1 ight)m_2u$$
$$J_{PQ} = rac{4}{3}m_2u$$
The magnitude of the impulse is a positive value.

Answer: The magnitude of the impulse exerted by P on Q is $ rac{4}{3}m_2u$.

Part (b): Find the speed of P immediately after the collision.

Thought Process:
To find the speed of P after the collision, we can use the principle of conservation of linear momentum for the system (P and Q). The total momentum before the collision must equal the total momentum after the collision. We will continue to use the same positive direction defined in part (a).

Initial velocities:


  • $u_P = +u$

  • $u_Q = -u$

Final velocities:


  • $v_Q = + rac{1}{3}u$

  • Particle P's direction is reversed. If its initial velocity was $+u$, its final velocity ($v_P$) will be in the negative direction. Let its final speed be $v_P'$, so its final velocity $v_P = -v_P'$.

Working:
Total momentum before collision = Total momentum after collision
$$m_1 u_P + m_2 u_Q = m_1 v_P + m_2 v_Q$$
Substitute the known values:
$$m_1(+u) + m_2(-u) = m_1(-v_P') + m_2(+ rac{1}{3}u)$$
$$(m_1 - m_2)u = -m_1v_P' + rac{1}{3}m_2u$$
Rearrange to solve for $m_1v_P'$:
$$m_1v_P' = rac{1}{3}m_2u - (m_1 - m_2)u$$
$$m_1v_P' = rac{1}{3}m_2u - m_1u + m_2u$$
Combine terms involving $m_2u$:
$$m_1v_P' = \left( rac{1}{3} + 1 ight)m_2u - m_1u$$
$$m_1v_P' = rac{4}{3}m_2u - m_1u$$
Factor out $u$:
$$m_1v_P' = u\left( rac{4}{3}m_2 - m_1 ight)$$
Now, solve for $v_P'$:
$$v_P' = rac{u}{m_1}\left( rac{4}{3}m_2 - m_1 ight)$$
This can be written as:
$$v_P' = rac{u(4m_2 - 3m_1)}{3m_1}$$

Answer: The speed of P immediately after the collision is $ rac{u(4m_2 - 3m_1)}{3m_1}$.

Part (c): Hence show that $m_2 > rac{3}{4}m_1$.

Thought Process:
Part (b) gave us an expression for the speed of P after the collision, $v_P'$. A speed must be a positive quantity (since P has reversed its direction, its speed cannot be zero). We can use this physical constraint ($v_P' > 0$) to form an inequality and prove the given relationship between $m_1$ and $m_2$.

Working:
From part (b), the speed of P after the collision is $v_P' = rac{u(4m_2 - 3m_1)}{3m_1}$.
Since P reverses its direction, its speed must be positive:
$$v_P' > 0$$
$$ rac{u(4m_2 - 3m_1)}{3m_1} > 0$$
We know that $u$ (initial speed) is positive, and $m_1$ (mass) is positive. Therefore, for the entire expression to be positive, the term $(4m_2 - 3m_1)$ must be positive:
$$4m_2 - 3m_1 > 0$$
Add $3m_1$ to both sides:
$$4m_2 > 3m_1$$
Divide by 4:
$$m_2 > rac{3}{4}m_1$$
This matches the inequality we needed to show.

Answer: We have shown that $m_2 > rac{3}{4}m_1$.

Key techniques

  • Define consistent positive direction
  • Apply impulse = change in momentum ($J = mv - mu$)
  • Apply conservation of linear momentum ($m_1u_1 + m_2u_2 = m_1v_1 + m_2v_2$)
  • Solve algebraic equations for unknowns
  • Use physical constraints (e.g., speed > 0)

Common pitfalls

  • Sign errors when defining velocities, especially when particles are moving in opposite directions or reverse direction after collision. Consistent sign convention is key.
  • Confusing impulse on P with impulse on Q. By Newton's third law, the impulses are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction.
  • Forgetting that speed must be a positive quantity in part (c), or incorrectly setting up the inequality.
