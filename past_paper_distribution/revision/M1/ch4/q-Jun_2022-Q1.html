Worked solution

Thought Process

  1. Recognize the scenario: This question describes a direct collision between two particles and asks about the motion of one particle (Q) after an impulse. This immediately indicates that the *impulse-momentum principle* is the key concept to use. The impulse on a particle equals the change in its momentum ($I = \Delta p = mv - mu$).
  2. Establish a positive direction: Since velocity and impulse are vector quantities, a consistent positive direction must be chosen. A common strategy is to take the initial direction of the particle in focus (Q) as positive. So, let's say Q's initial direction of motion is positive.
  3. List knowns for particle Q:
  • Mass, $m_Q = 3m$.
  • Initial speed, $2u$. Since its initial direction is positive, its initial velocity, $u_Q = +2u$.
  • Magnitude of impulse exerted *by P on Q* is $4mu$.
  1. Determine the direction of the impulse: Particles P and Q are moving *towards each other*. If Q is moving in the positive direction (e.g., to the right), then P must be moving in the negative direction (to the left). When P collides with Q, P pushes Q. The impulse *on Q* will be in the direction that P was originally moving. Therefore, the impulse exerted by P on Q is in the negative direction. So, $I_{PQ} = -4mu$.
  2. Apply the principle and solve for $v_Q$: Substitute these values into the impulse-momentum equation for particle Q and solve for its final velocity, $v_Q$.
  3. Interpret the result: The sign of $v_Q$ will tell us the direction of motion, and its magnitude will be the speed. A positive $v_Q$ means Q continues in its original direction; a negative $v_Q$ means Q reverses direction.

Working

(a) Find the speed of Q immediately after the collision.

Let the initial direction of motion of Q be the positive direction.

Given values for particle Q:


  • Mass of Q, $m_Q = 3m$

  • Initial velocity of Q, $u_Q = +2u$ (since we defined its initial direction as positive)

  • Magnitude of impulse exerted by P on Q = $4mu$

Since P and Q are moving towards each other, P exerts an impulse on Q in the direction opposite to Q's initial motion. Therefore, the impulse $I_{PQ}$ is negative:
$I_{PQ} = -4mu$

The impulse-momentum principle states that for particle Q:
$I_{PQ} = m_Q v_Q - m_Q u_Q$

Substitute the known values into the equation:
$-4mu = (3m)v_Q - (3m)(2u)$

Expand the equation:
$-4mu = 3mv_Q - 6mu$

Rearrange to solve for $3mv_Q$:
$3mv_Q = -4mu + 6mu$
$3mv_Q = 2mu$

Divide both sides by $3m$ to find $v_Q$:
$v_Q = rac{2mu}{3m}$
$v_Q = rac{2}{3}u$

Since speed is the magnitude of velocity, and $v_Q$ is positive, the speed of Q immediately after the collision is $ rac{2}{3}u$.

(b) Find the direction of motion of Q immediately after the collision.

From part (a), we found that the final velocity of Q is $v_Q = + rac{2}{3}u$.
Since $v_Q$ is positive, its direction of motion is the same as the direction we defined as positive, which was its original direction of motion before the collision.

Answer:
(a) The speed of Q immediately after the collision is $ rac{2}{3}u$.
(b) The direction of motion of Q immediately after the collision is the same as its original direction.

Key techniques

  • Define positive direction consistently
  • Apply impulse-momentum theorem for a single particle ($I = mv - mu$)
  • Correctly assign signs to initial velocities and impulse
  • Interpret the sign of the final velocity

Common pitfalls

  • Incorrectly assigning signs to the initial velocity of Q or the impulse. Forgetting that impulse on Q by P is opposite to Q's initial direction of motion if they are moving towards each other.
  • Using 'm' instead of '3m' for the mass of particle Q in the calculations.
  • Stating the final velocity as the answer for speed in part (a) without taking the magnitude, or stating a negative speed if $v_Q$ was negative.
