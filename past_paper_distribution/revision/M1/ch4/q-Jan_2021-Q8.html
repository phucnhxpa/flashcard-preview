Worked solution

Part (a): Find the tension in the string as B descends.

Thought Process:
We are dealing with two connected particles moving under the influence of gravity, tension, and friction on an inclined plane. Since the string is light and inextensible and passes over a smooth pulley, both particles will move with the same magnitude of acceleration, $a$, and the tension, $T$, in the string will be uniform. We need to apply Newton's Second Law, $F=ma$, to each particle separately. For particle A on the inclined plane, we must resolve its weight into components perpendicular and parallel to the plane, and include the normal reaction and frictional force.

First, let's find $\sin \alpha$ and $\cos \alpha$ from $ an \alpha = rac{3}{4}$. We can form a right-angled triangle with opposite side 3, adjacent side 4, and hypotenuse 5. Thus, $\sin \alpha = rac{3}{5}$ and $\cos \alpha = rac{4}{5}$.

Working:

1. For Particle A (mass $m_A = 2$ kg) on the rough inclined plane:


  • Forces perpendicular to the plane:

  • The normal reaction, $R$, acts perpendicular to the plane, upwards.

  • The component of weight perpendicular to the plane is $m_A g \cos \alpha$, acting downwards into the plane.


Since there is no acceleration perpendicular to the plane, forces are balanced:
$$R - m_A g \cos \alpha = 0$$
$$R = 2g \left( rac{4}{5} ight) = rac{8g}{5}$$

  • Frictional Force ($F_r$):

  • The coefficient of friction is $\mu = rac{1}{4}$.

  • The frictional force $F_r = \mu R = rac{1}{4} \left( rac{8g}{5} ight) = rac{2g}{5}$.

  • Since B descends, A moves up the plane, so friction acts *down* the plane, opposing the motion.

  • Forces parallel to the plane:

  • Tension, $T$, acts up the plane.

  • Component of weight parallel to the plane is $m_A g \sin \alpha$, acting down the plane: $2g \left( rac{3}{5} ight) = rac{6g}{5}$.

  • Friction, $F_r = rac{2g}{5}$, acts down the plane.


Applying Newton's Second Law ($F=ma$), taking the direction up the plane as positive for A:
$$T - F_r - m_A g \sin \alpha = m_A a$$
$$T - rac{2g}{5} - rac{6g}{5} = 2a$$
$$T - rac{8g}{5} = 2a \quad ext{(Equation 1)}$$

2. For Particle B (mass $m_B = 4$ kg) hanging freely:


  • Forces vertically:

  • Weight, $m_B g = 4g$, acts downwards.

  • Tension, $T$, acts upwards.


Applying Newton's Second Law ($F=ma$), taking the direction downwards as positive for B (consistent with A moving up):
$$m_B g - T = m_B a$$
$$4g - T = 4a \quad ext{(Equation 2)}$$

3. Solve Equations 1 and 2 simultaneously for T and a:
From Equation 2, rearrange for $T$: $T = 4g - 4a$.
Substitute this into Equation 1:
$$(4g - 4a) - rac{8g}{5} = 2a$$
$$4g - rac{8g}{5} = 6a$$
$$ rac{20g - 8g}{5} = 6a$$
$$ rac{12g}{5} = 6a$$
$$a = rac{12g}{30} = rac{2g}{5} = 0.4g$$
Now substitute the value of $a$ back into the expression for $T$:
$$T = 4g - 4\left( rac{2g}{5} ight)$$
$$T = 4g - rac{8g}{5}$$
$$T = rac{20g - 8g}{5}$$
$$T = rac{12g}{5} = 2.4g$$
If $g \approx 9.8 ext{ m/s}^2$, then $T = 2.4 imes 9.8 = 23.52 ext{ N}$ (or $24 ext{ N}$ to 2 s.f.).

Answer: The tension in the string as B descends is $2.4g ext{ N}$.

Part (b): Find, in terms of h, the range of possible values of d.

Thought Process:
This problem has two distinct phases of motion for particle A.

Phase 1: B descends a distance $h$. During this phase, both particles are connected and accelerating with $a = 0.4g$ (calculated in part a). We need to find the velocity of the system just as B hits the ground, as this will be the initial velocity for particle A in the second phase.

Phase 2: B hits the ground and immediately comes to rest. This means the string becomes slack, and the tension $T$ becomes zero. Particle A, which was moving up the plane, will continue to move for some distance due to its inertia, but it will now decelerate because the forces acting on it (component of weight down the plane and friction down the plane) are unopposed by tension. We need to calculate this new deceleration and the distance A travels before coming to rest.

Finally, we use the condition that A comes to rest *before* reaching the pulley to establish an inequality for $d$.

Working:

Phase 1: B descends a distance $h$.


  • Initial velocity, $u = 0 ext{ m/s}$ (released from rest).

  • Acceleration, $a = 0.4g$ (from part a).

  • Displacement, $s = h$.

  • Let $v$ be the velocity when B hits the ground. Using $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$:


$$v^2 = 0^2 + 2(0.4g)h$$
$$v^2 = 0.8gh$$
This is the speed of A (up the plane) just as B hits the ground. So, $v = \sqrt{0.8gh}$.

Phase 2: B stops, A moves up the plane and decelerates to rest.


  • When B stops, the string becomes slack, so $T=0$.

  • The forces acting on A (mass 2 kg) are now:

  • Component of weight down the plane: $2g \sin \alpha = 2g(3/5) = 6g/5$.

  • Friction down the plane: $F_r = 2g/5$ (as A is still moving up, friction opposes motion and acts down the plane).

  • Applying Newton's Second Law for A (taking up the plane as positive):


$$-F_r - m_A g \sin \alpha = m_A a'$$
$$- rac{2g}{5} - rac{6g}{5} = 2a'$$
$$- rac{8g}{5} = 2a'$$
$$a' = - rac{4g}{5} = -0.8g$$
This is the deceleration of A.

  • Now calculate the distance $s'$ A travels before coming to rest:

  • Initial velocity, $u' = v = \sqrt{0.8gh}$.

  • Final velocity, $v' = 0 ext{ m/s}$ (comes to rest).

  • Acceleration, $a' = -0.8g$.


Using $v'^2 = u'^2 + 2a's'$:
$$0^2 = (0.8gh) + 2(-0.8g)s'$$
$$0 = 0.8gh - 1.6gs'$$
$$1.6gs' = 0.8gh$$
$$s' = rac{0.8gh}{1.6g} = rac{h}{2}$$

Total distance travelled by A:
Particle A moved a distance $h$ up the plane while B was descending. Then, it moved an additional distance $s' = h/2$ up the plane after B stopped.
Total distance A moves from its initial position until it comes to rest is $h + rac{h}{2} = rac{3h}{2}$.

Condition for $d$:
The problem states that A comes to rest *before* reaching the pulley. This means the total distance it travels must be strictly less than the initial distance $d$ from the pulley.
$$d > rac{3h}{2}$$
$$d > 1.5h$$

Answer: The range of possible values for d is $d > 1.5h$.

Part (c): State one physical factor, other than air resistance, that could be taken into account to make the model described above more realistic.

Thought Process:
The problem states several idealizations: light string, inextensible string, small smooth pulley. We need to choose one of these or another common simplification (like the size of the particles) that makes the model less realistic.

Working:


  • The string having mass (not being light).

  • The string being extensible (not being inextensible).

  • The pulley having friction (not being smooth).

Answer: Weight of the string (or: extensibility of the string; friction at the pulley).

---

Key techniques

  • Draw free-body diagrams
  • Resolve forces parallel and perpendicular to plane
  • Apply Newton's 2nd law to each particle
  • Use kinematic equations for stages of motion
  • Recognize changes in forces/acceleration when system configuration changes

Common pitfalls

  • Incorrectly determining the direction of the frictional force, especially when the direction of motion changes.
  • Using the same acceleration from the first phase of motion for the second phase, without recalculating it based on the new forces.
  • Sign errors when applying Newton's Second Law, especially on the inclined plane or when dealing with deceleration.
