nth roots of complex numbers using De Moivre's theorem

Given a complex number (often in Cartesian form), find all nth roots. May ask for answers in exponential form re^{iθ}, modulus-argument form, or exact Cartesian form. Often part (a) asks for conversion to polar form, then part (b) asks for roots.

Triggersfind all rootssolve z^n =exponential formcube rootsfifth rootsmodulus-argument formexpress in the form re^{iθ}
Assumptions
  • Principal argument convention: θ ∈ (−π, π] unless stated otherwise (some questions specify 0 < θ < 2π — read carefully)
  • There are exactly n distinct nth roots of any non-zero complex number
  • Roots are equally spaced around a circle of radius r^{1/n} in the Argand diagram
Approaches
A: De Moivre's theorem nth root formula
When: Standard approach for all nth root problems
  1. Convert the complex number to modulus-argument form: w = R(cos α + i sin α) or w = Re^{iα}
  2. Write z^n = Re^{iα}, so z = R^{1/n} e^{i(α + 2kπ)/n}
  3. Let k = 0, 1, 2, ..., n−1 to generate all n distinct roots
  4. Compute each argument θ_k = (α + 2kπ)/n
  5. Adjust arguments to the required range (e.g. principal argument (−π, π])
  6. Convert to requested form (exponential, mod-arg, or exact Cartesian using known trig values)
+ Pros: Systematic and works for any n; easy to check you have all roots
– Cons: Must be careful with argument range conventions
B: Factorisation / quadratic approach (for n = 2)
When: When solving z² = a + bi, or when a quadratic with complex coefficients arises
  1. Let z = x + iy, then z² = (x² − y²) + 2xyi
  2. Equate real and imaginary parts to get simultaneous equations
  3. Use |z²| = |z|² to find x² + y² = |w|^{1/2} as a third equation
  4. Solve the system for x and y
+ Pros: Gives exact Cartesian answers directly without needing to evaluate trig functions
– Cons: Only practical for square roots; tedious for higher n
Formulas
$z_k = r^{1/n} \exp\left(\frac{i(\theta + 2k\pi)}{n}\right),\; k = 0,1,\ldots,n-1$ (MEMORIZE)
$r = |z| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\theta = \arg(z) = \arctan\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)$ adjusted for quadrant (MEMORIZE)
$e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to take the nth root of the MODULUS (writing R instead of R^{1/n})
  • Missing roots by not cycling k from 0 to n−1
  • Giving arguments outside the required range (not converting to principal argument)
  • Wrong quadrant for the original argument — always check signs of Re and Im parts
  • Confusing exact trig values: e.g. cos(π/3) = 1/2 vs cos(π/6) = √3/2

Given a transformation w = (az + b)/(cz + d), find the image of the real axis, imaginary axis, a given circle, or a given line. May ask to show the image is a circle and find its centre and radius. Sometimes combined with finding invariant points.

Triggerstransformation w =image ofmaps tow-planez-planeMöbiusshow that the image is a circlefind the centre and radius
Assumptions
  • Möbius transformations map circles and lines to circles and lines (a line is a circle through infinity)
  • To find the image, either substitute parametric z into w, or rearrange for z in terms of w and substitute the locus condition
  • The imaginary axis is z = iy (y ∈ ℝ); the real axis is z = x (x ∈ ℝ)
Approaches
A: Parametric substitution (substitute z = x or z = iy into w)
When: When the source locus is a simple line (real axis, imaginary axis) and you want to trace its image
  1. Substitute z = iy (for imaginary axis) or z = x (for real axis) into w = f(z)
  2. Multiply numerator and denominator by the complex conjugate of the denominator
  3. Separate into w = u + iv to get u(y) and v(y) as parametric equations
  4. Eliminate the parameter y to find the Cartesian equation of the image in the w-plane
  5. Complete the square if needed to identify the circle
+ Pros: Direct and systematic; always works for lines
– Cons: Can be algebraically intensive; risk of sign errors when multiplying by conjugate
B: Rearrange for z in terms of w, then substitute locus condition
When: When the source locus is given as |z − a| = r or another modulus/argument condition
  1. From w = (az + b)/(cz + d), rearrange to get z = (dw − b)/(a − cw)
  2. Substitute this expression for z into the locus equation (e.g. |z| = 1 becomes |dw − b| = |a − cw|)
  3. Square both sides and expand to get a Cartesian equation in u and v (where w = u + iv)
  4. Complete the square to identify the circle centre and radius
+ Pros: Works for any source locus, not just lines; elegant for circle-to-circle mappings
– Cons: Rearranging the transformation can introduce errors
C: Three-point method
When: Quick check or when asked to verify: pick 3 points on the source locus, compute their images, then find the circle through those 3 image points
  1. Choose 3 convenient points on the source locus (e.g. z = 0, z = i, z = −i on the imaginary axis)
  2. Compute w = f(z) for each
  3. Use the 3 image points to determine the image circle (or verify it's a line if 3 points are collinear)
+ Pros: Quick sanity check; can identify the image without heavy algebra
– Cons: Doesn't constitute a full proof; may miss that the image is only part of the circle
Formulas
$w = \frac{az + b}{cz + d}$ — general Möbius transformation (MEMORIZE structure)
Rearranging: $z = \frac{dw - b}{a - cw}$ or equivalent (derive by cross-multiplying)
To separate $\frac{p + qi}{r + si}$ into real and imaginary parts, multiply by $\frac{r - si}{r - si}$ (MEMORIZE technique)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to multiply by the complex conjugate when separating real and imaginary parts
  • Sign errors when expanding products of complex numbers, especially (a − bi)(c + di)
  • Algebraic slips when cross-multiplying to rearrange w = f(z) for z
  • Forgetting to square the RHS constant when squaring both sides of a modulus equation
  • Not identifying that the image of a line can be a circle (not necessarily another line)

Triggers|z − a| = r|z − a| = k|z − b|arg(z − a) = θsketch the locusArgand diagramshade the regionhalf-linecircle centre
Assumptions
  • |z − a| = r is a circle centre a, radius r
  • arg(z − a) = θ is a HALF-LINE from point a (not through a) in direction θ
  • |z − a| = k|z − b| with k ≠ 1 gives a circle (Apollonius circle); k = 1 gives a perpendicular bisector
  • Regions defined by inequalities: |z − a| < r means inside the circle
Approaches
A: Direct Cartesian conversion (substitute z = x + iy)
When: When you need the equation of the circle or need to find intersection points algebraically
  1. Let z = x + iy and substitute into the locus equation
  2. For |z − a| = k|z − b|: square both sides to get (x−a₁)² + (y−a₂)² = k²[(x−b₁)² + (y−b₂)²]
  3. Expand, collect terms, and complete the square to identify centre and radius
  4. For arg(z − a) = θ: write (y − a₂)/(x − a₁) = tan θ, noting the half-line direction constraint
+ Pros: Rigorous and gives exact equation; needed when finding intersections
– Cons: Algebraically heavy, especially with the Apollonius circle form
B: Geometric recognition
When: When the question asks only to sketch or identify the locus, not derive the equation
  1. Recognise |z − a| = r as circle centre a, radius r — plot directly
  2. Recognise arg(z − a) = θ as half-line from a at angle θ to positive real axis
  3. For |z − a| = k|z − b|, find two points on the real line joining a and b that divide in ratio k:1 (internally and externally) — these are endpoints of a diameter
  4. Sketch and find intersections geometrically or by substitution
+ Pros: Fast for sketching; gives quick geometric insight
– Cons: May not give exact coordinates without further algebra
Formulas
$|z - a| = r$ is a circle centre $a$, radius $r$ (MEMORIZE)
$\arg(z - a) = \theta$ is a half-line from $a$ at angle $\theta$ to the positive real direction (MEMORIZE)
$|z - a| = k|z - b|,\; k \neq 1$ is a circle (Apollonius circle) (MEMORIZE)
$|z - a| = |z - b|$ is the perpendicular bisector of the line segment from $a$ to $b$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Treating arg(z − a) = θ as a FULL line instead of a HALF-line — the line only extends in one direction from a
  • Forgetting to square the constant k when squaring |z − a| = k|z − b|
  • Sign errors when completing the square, especially with negative coefficients
  • Selecting the wrong intersection point by ignoring the half-line direction constraint
  • Not recognising |z − a| < r means INSIDE the circle (strict inequality → open boundary)

Triggersz + 1/zz^n + z^{-n}cos^n θsin^n θtan nθshow thatbinomial expansionhence integratehence solve
Assumptions
  • z = cos θ + i sin θ = e^{iθ}, so z^n = cos nθ + i sin nθ (De Moivre's theorem)
  • z + z^{-1} = 2cos θ and z − z^{-1} = 2i sin θ
  • z^n + z^{-n} = 2cos nθ and z^n − z^{-n} = 2i sin nθ
  • When deriving tan nθ, separate real and imaginary parts then divide Im by Re
Approaches
A: Binomial expansion of (z + 1/z)^n for cos^n θ identity
When: When asked to express cos^n θ (or sin^n θ) as a sum of multiple angle terms
  1. Note (2cos θ)^n = (z + z^{-1})^n
  2. Expand using the binomial theorem: sum of C(n,k) z^{n-2k} terms
  3. Group terms symmetrically: z^k + z^{-k} = 2cos kθ
  4. Divide both sides by 2^n to isolate cos^n θ
  5. Simplify to get cos^n θ = (1/2^{n-1})[combination of cos kθ terms]
+ Pros: Systematic and generalises to any power; directly yields integration-ready form
– Cons: Binomial coefficients can be large for high n; easy to lose factors of 2
B: Separate real/imaginary parts of (cos θ + i sin θ)^n for tan nθ
When: When asked to derive an expression for tan nθ in terms of tan θ
  1. By De Moivre: cos nθ + i sin nθ = (cos θ + i sin θ)^n
  2. Expand RHS using binomial theorem
  3. Separate real parts (cos nθ) and imaginary parts (sin nθ)
  4. Form tan nθ = sin nθ / cos nθ = Im / Re
  5. Divide every term in numerator and denominator by cos^n θ to express in terms of tan θ
+ Pros: Gives a clean rational expression in t = tan θ; leads to polynomial root problems
– Cons: Separating real and imaginary parts requires care with i^k powers
Formulas
$z = e^{i\theta} \Rightarrow z^n + z^{-n} = 2\cos n\theta$ (MEMORIZE)
$z = e^{i\theta} \Rightarrow z^n - z^{-n} = 2i\sin n\theta$ (MEMORIZE)
$(z + z^{-1})^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} z^{n-2k}$ (formula booklet for binomial coefficients)
$\cos^6\theta = \frac{1}{32}(\cos 6\theta + 6\cos 4\theta + 15\cos 2\theta + 10)$ — typical result (derive, don't memorise)
$\tan 4\theta = \frac{4\tan\theta - 4\tan^3\theta}{1 - 6\tan^2\theta + \tan^4\theta}$ — typical result (derive)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to divide by 2^n (e.g. (2cos θ)^6 = 64 cos^6 θ, not cos^6 θ)
  • Errors in binomial coefficients for large n
  • When forming tan nθ, forgetting to divide EVERY term by cos^n θ
  • Not recognising the link between a polynomial equation and tan nθ = c (the polynomial's roots are tan(θ_k))
  • In integration parts, forgetting that ∫cos kθ dθ = (1/k)sin kθ + C

Triggersmodulus-argument formexpress in the form r(cos θ + i sin θ)exponential formfind |z| and arg zhence find z^n
Assumptions
  • Modulus r = √(x² + y²) is always non-negative
  • Argument θ must be in the correct quadrant — arctan alone may give the wrong angle
  • When multiplying: moduli multiply, arguments add. When dividing: moduli divide, arguments subtract
Approaches
A: Direct conversion using r and θ
When: Always — fundamental skill
  1. Compute r = |z| = √(x² + y²)
  2. Compute θ = arg(z): start with arctan(y/x), then adjust for quadrant
  3. Write z = r(cos θ + i sin θ) = re^{iθ}
  4. For powers: z^n = r^n e^{inθ} by De Moivre's theorem
+ Pros: Fundamental and universally applicable
– Cons: Quadrant determination requires thought for each case
Formulas
$|z_1 z_2| = |z_1||z_2|,\quad \arg(z_1 z_2) = \arg(z_1) + \arg(z_2)$ (MEMORIZE)
$\left|\frac{z_1}{z_2}\right| = \frac{|z_1|}{|z_2|},\quad \arg\left(\frac{z_1}{z_2}\right) = \arg(z_1) - \arg(z_2)$ (MEMORIZE)
$z^n = r^n(\cos n\theta + i\sin n\theta)$ — De Moivre's theorem (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Wrong quadrant for arg: e.g. z = −1 + i has arg = 3π/4, not π/4
  • Forgetting to raise the modulus to the power (only rotating the argument)
  • Using degrees instead of radians when the question specifies radians

Triggersinvariant pointfixed pointw = zmaps to itself
Assumptions
  • An invariant point satisfies w = z, i.e. z = f(z)
  • This typically gives a quadratic equation in z, so there are at most 2 invariant points
  • The given invariant point must satisfy the equation — use it to find unknown parameters
Approaches
A: Set w = z and solve the resulting equation
When: Always — this is the defining method
  1. Set w = z in the transformation equation: z = (az + b)/(cz + d)
  2. Cross-multiply: z(cz + d) = az + b
  3. Rearrange to cz² + (d − a)z − b = 0
  4. If a specific invariant point is given, substitute it to find the unknown parameter
  5. Solve the quadratic for z to find all invariant points
+ Pros: Straightforward; always works
– Cons: Must be careful with complex arithmetic when the invariant point involves surds or i
Formulas
Invariant point condition: $z = \frac{az + b}{cz + d}$ leads to $cz^2 + (d-a)z - b = 0$ (MEMORIZE derivation)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting that invariant means w = z (not w = 0 or z = 0)
  • Errors when expanding products of surds with complex numbers
  • Not substituting the given invariant point into BOTH z and w

Must memorize

  • $e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$ (Euler's formula — in formula booklet)
  • $z^n + z^{-n} = 2\cos n\theta$ where $z = e^{i\theta}$ (MEMORIZE — used constantly)
  • $z^n - z^{-n} = 2i\sin n\theta$ where $z = e^{i\theta}$ (MEMORIZE)
  • De Moivre's theorem: $(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^n = \cos n\theta + i\sin n\theta$ (formula booklet)
  • nth root formula: $z_k = r^{1/n} \exp\left(\frac{i(\theta + 2k\pi)}{n}\right)$ for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1$ (MEMORIZE)
  • $|z - a| = r$ is a circle centre $a$, radius $r$ (MEMORIZE)
  • $\arg(z - a) = \theta$ is a half-line from $a$ at angle $\theta$ (MEMORIZE)
  • $|z - a| = k|z - b|$ ($k \neq 1$) is an Apollonius circle (MEMORIZE the concept; derive the equation)
  • Invariant points satisfy $w = z$: substitute into the transformation and solve (MEMORIZE method)

Global strategy

  • Master the conversion between Cartesian, modulus-argument, and exponential forms — this underpins nearly every question in this chapter
  • Always determine the argument by checking the quadrant (signs of Re and Im parts) — never rely on arctan alone
  • For nth roots: learn the formula z_k = r^{1/n} exp(i(θ + 2kπ)/n) as a reflex — then systematically cycle k = 0 to n−1
  • For Möbius transformations: decide upfront whether to substitute z into w (parametric) or rearrange for z in terms of w (inverse substitution) — choose based on the source locus form
  • When separating real and imaginary parts of a complex fraction, ALWAYS multiply top and bottom by the conjugate of the denominator
  • For De Moivre's theorem power/identity questions, keep track of the factor of 2 (or 2i) throughout: (z + 1/z) = 2cos θ, NOT cos θ
  • In loci questions, always clearly distinguish between full lines, half-lines, and circles — and remember arg gives a HALF-line
  • Complete the square carefully and systematically when deriving circle equations from |z − a| = k|z − b|

Exam tips

  1. READ whether the question wants exponential form, modulus-argument form, or exact Cartesian — giving the wrong form loses marks even if your working is correct
  2. Check the required argument range: (−π, π] vs [0, 2π) — questions vary and you MUST match their convention
  3. When finding nth roots, ALWAYS list all n roots and verify they are distinct — a common error is getting repeated roots from wrong k values
  4. In Möbius transformation questions, label your working clearly with z-plane and w-plane — examiners want to see you distinguish between the two
  5. For 'show that' or 'hence' parts, you MUST use the result from the previous part — using an independent method may score zero
  6. When completing the square to find a circle's centre and radius, double-check by expanding your answer to verify it matches
  7. In multi-part De Moivre questions, the early parts build toward the later parts — if stuck on (c), look back at what (a) and (b) established
  8. Draw a quick Argand diagram sketch even if not asked — it helps you check arguments, loci, and root positions
