TriggersCartesian formconvert to Cartesianfind the equation of the tangentin terms of x and y
Assumptions
  • x = r cos θ and y = r sin θ are the standard conversion relations
  • r² = x² + y² and tan θ = y/x
  • A tangent at a polar point requires finding the gradient dy/dx = (dy/dθ)/(dx/dθ)
Approaches
A: Direct substitution using x = r cos θ, y = r sin θ
When: Converting a specific point or finding tangent coordinates
  1. Find r and θ at the point of interest
  2. Compute x = r cos θ and y = r sin θ
  3. If finding tangent equation: compute dy/dx = (dy/dθ)/(dx/dθ) at that θ
  4. Use y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) for the tangent line
+ Pros: Straightforward and systematic
– Cons: Need to compute both dy/dθ and dx/dθ
B: Use r² = x² + y² and r cos θ = x, r sin θ = y for curve conversion
When: Converting the entire polar equation to Cartesian form
  1. Multiply both sides of the polar equation by r if needed to create r² or r cos θ / r sin θ terms
  2. Replace r² with x² + y², r cos θ with x, r sin θ with y
  3. Simplify the resulting Cartesian equation
+ Pros: Gives the full Cartesian equation of the curve
– Cons: May introduce extraneous solutions when multiplying by r
Formulas
$x = r\cos\theta,\quad y = r\sin\theta$ (MEMORIZE)
$r^2 = x^2 + y^2$ (MEMORIZE)
$\tan\theta = \frac{y}{x}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/d\theta}{dx/d\theta} = \frac{\frac{dr}{d\theta}\sin\theta + r\cos\theta}{\frac{dr}{d\theta}\cos\theta - r\sin\theta}$ (MEMORIZE — or derive from product rule)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting the product rule when differentiating y = r sin θ or x = r cos θ
  • Sign error in dx/dθ: it has a MINUS sign (dr/dθ cos θ − r sin θ)
  • Not substituting the correct θ value when evaluating the gradient
  • Leaving the answer in polar form when Cartesian is requested

Triggersarea enclosedregion bounded byarea of the loopshaded regionpolar area
Assumptions
  • The area formula A = ½∫r² dθ applies when the curve does not cross itself within the limits
  • Limits of integration correspond to where the curve starts and ends (often where r = 0, or at specific symmetry lines)
  • If the curve is symmetric, you can integrate over half and double (or use appropriate fraction)
Approaches
A: Direct integration using A = ½∫r² dθ with trig identities
When: Standard method — use for all single-curve polar area problems
  1. Identify the correct limits of integration (where r = 0, or given bounds, or symmetry arguments)
  2. Write down A = ½∫_{α}^{β} r² dθ
  3. Expand r² — substitute the polar equation and square it
  4. Use trig identities to simplify: cos²θ = ½(1 + cos 2θ), sin²θ = ½(1 − cos 2θ), etc.
  5. Integrate term by term
  6. Evaluate at limits and simplify
  7. If using symmetry, multiply by the appropriate factor (usually 2)
+ Pros: Universally applicable; straightforward when identities are applied correctly
– Cons: Expanding r² can be algebraically heavy; easy to make sign errors
B: Exploit symmetry to reduce integration
When: When the curve has clear symmetry about the initial line or θ = π/2
  1. Identify the axis/axes of symmetry from the equation (e.g., r = a(1 + cos θ) is symmetric about θ = 0)
  2. Integrate over the half (or quarter) region
  3. Multiply by 2 (or 4) to get the total area
  4. Proceed with trig identity simplification and integration as in Approach A
+ Pros: Simpler limits and smaller integrals; less room for error
– Cons: Must correctly identify symmetry; wrong symmetry argument leads to wrong answer
Formulas
$A = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\alpha}^{\beta} r^2 \, d\theta$ (formula booklet)
$\cos^2\theta = \frac{1}{2}(1 + \cos 2\theta)$ (MEMORIZE)
$\sin^2\theta = \frac{1}{2}(1 - \cos 2\theta)$ (MEMORIZE)
$\cos^2(n\theta) = \frac{1}{2}(1 + \cos 2n\theta)$ (MEMORIZE — generalised form)
$2\sin\theta\cos\theta = \sin 2\theta$ (MEMORIZE)
$\cos 2\theta = 2\cos^2\theta - 1 = 1 - 2\sin^2\theta$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting the factor of ½ in the area formula — writing ∫r² dθ instead of ½∫r² dθ
  • Forgetting to square r — writing ½∫r dθ instead of ½∫r² dθ
  • Using wrong limits of integration: always check where r = 0 for loops/petals
  • Not applying power-reduction identities before integrating cos² or sin² terms
  • Incorrectly expanding (a + b cos θ)² — sign errors on the middle cross term 2ab cos θ
  • Evaluating sin(nπ) or cos(nπ) incorrectly at the limits

Triggerssketch the curvedraw the polar curveon the diagram below
Assumptions
  • Curves of the form r = a(1 + cos θ) are cardioids; r = a(p + q cos θ) with p > q are convex limaçons
  • Curves r² = a² cos 2θ or r = a cos nθ produce rose curves with specific petal counts
  • r must be ≥ 0 for the curve to exist (unless the question allows negative r)
Approaches
A: Table of key values
When: Always — the quickest way to get enough points for a sketch
  1. Evaluate r at θ = 0, π/6, π/4, π/3, π/2, 2π/3, π (and reflections if needed)
  2. Find where r = 0 (these are points at the pole)
  3. Find maximum r by inspection or setting dr/dθ = 0
  4. Identify symmetry: if r(θ) = r(−θ), symmetric about initial line; if r(θ) = r(π − θ), symmetric about θ = π/2
  5. Plot points and join smoothly
+ Pros: Quick and reliable; easy marks
– Cons: Need to choose enough values to capture the shape
B: Recognise standard curve types
When: When the equation matches a known form (cardioid, limaçon, rose, lemniscate)
  1. Identify the curve type from its equation
  2. Recall its standard shape and key features
  3. Adjust for parameters (a scales, sign changes reflect)
  4. Sketch directly, labelling key points
+ Pros: Very fast if you recognise the type
– Cons: Requires memorisation of standard forms
Formulas
$r = a(1 + \cos\theta)$: cardioid, symmetric about initial line, max $r = 2a$ at $\theta = 0$, $r = 0$ at $\theta = \pi$ (MEMORIZE shape)
$r = a\cos n\theta$: rose curve with $n$ petals if $n$ odd, $2n$ petals if $n$ even (MEMORIZE)
$r^2 = a^2\cos 2\theta$: lemniscate (figure-of-eight) (MEMORIZE shape)
Pitfalls
  • Drawing too few points and getting the wrong shape
  • Forgetting that r cannot be negative (unless the question/context allows it)
  • Mislabelling axes or not showing θ direction
  • Not marking the key features (max r, where r = 0, intercepts) that the mark scheme requires

Triggerstangent parallel to the initial linehorizontal tangentparallel to θ = 0
Assumptions
  • The initial line is the positive x-axis (θ = 0 direction)
  • Tangent parallel to the initial line means dy/dθ = 0 (not dr/dθ = 0)
  • Must check the point is actually on the curve and gives a valid tangent (not a cusp)
Approaches
A: Differentiate y = r sin θ with respect to θ
When: Standard method — works for all polar curves
  1. Write y = r sin θ where r = f(θ)
  2. Differentiate using the product rule: dy/dθ = (dr/dθ) sin θ + r cos θ
  3. Set dy/dθ = 0
  4. Solve the resulting trigonometric equation for θ
  5. Substitute back into r = f(θ) to find r
  6. State the polar coordinates (r, θ) and/or Cartesian coordinates of the point
+ Pros: Direct and systematic; always works
– Cons: Product rule can lead to messy algebra if r is complicated
B: Use dy/dx = (dy/dθ)/(dx/dθ) and set numerator = 0
When: When you need the gradient of the tangent explicitly as well
  1. Compute dy/dθ = (dr/dθ) sin θ + r cos θ
  2. Compute dx/dθ = (dr/dθ) cos θ − r sin θ
  3. For tangent parallel to initial line, set dy/dθ = 0 (numerator of dy/dx = 0)
  4. Verify dx/dθ ≠ 0 at the same θ (otherwise it's a singular point)
  5. Solve for θ and find r
+ Pros: Gives full gradient information; can check tangent is not vertical simultaneously
– Cons: Extra computation of dx/dθ may not be needed if question only asks for the point
Formulas
$y = r\sin\theta$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{dy}{d\theta} = \frac{dr}{d\theta}\sin\theta + r\cos\theta$ (MEMORIZE — product rule result)
Pitfalls
  • Using dr/dθ = 0 instead of dy/dθ = 0 — dr/dθ = 0 gives stationary r, NOT horizontal tangent
  • Forgetting the product rule when differentiating y = r sin θ
  • Not checking that the solution θ lies in the valid domain of the curve
  • Confusing parallel and perpendicular to the initial line

Triggerstangent perpendicular to the initial linevertical tangentperpendicular to θ = 0
Assumptions
  • Perpendicular to the initial line means the tangent is vertical, i.e. dx/dθ = 0
  • x = r cos θ is the relevant Cartesian coordinate
  • Must verify dy/dθ ≠ 0 at the same point to confirm it's a genuine tangent
Approaches
A: Differentiate x = r cos θ with respect to θ
When: Standard method for vertical tangent
  1. Write x = r cos θ where r = f(θ)
  2. Differentiate using the product rule: dx/dθ = (dr/dθ) cos θ − r sin θ
  3. Set dx/dθ = 0
  4. Solve the trigonometric equation for θ
  5. Substitute back to find r and hence the coordinates
+ Pros: Direct and reliable
– Cons: Solving the trig equation may require double-angle identities
Formulas
$x = r\cos\theta$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{dx}{d\theta} = \frac{dr}{d\theta}\cos\theta - r\sin\theta$ (MEMORIZE — product rule result)
Pitfalls
  • Setting dy/dθ = 0 instead of dx/dθ = 0 — this is the most common error
  • Sign error in the product rule: it's (dr/dθ)cosθ MINUS r sinθ
  • Errors when expanding sin 2θ and cos 2θ terms in the resulting equation
  • Failing to use double-angle identities to simplify the derivative equation

Triggersarea betweenregion enclosed by bothshaded region between curvesintersection of polar curves
Assumptions
  • The area between two curves is ½∫(r_outer² − r_inner²) dθ over the correct range
  • Intersection points are found by setting the two r expressions equal
  • Must correctly identify which curve is outer and which is inner in the integration range
Approaches
A: Subtract areas using ½∫(r₁² − r₂²) dθ
When: When both curves are expressed as r = f(θ) and one is clearly outside the other
  1. Find intersection points by setting r₁ = r₂ and solving for θ
  2. Determine which curve is outer (larger r) in the region of interest
  3. Write A = ½∫_{α}^{β} (r_outer² − r_inner²) dθ
  4. Expand, simplify using trig identities, and integrate
  5. Use symmetry if applicable to simplify
+ Pros: Single integral; efficient
– Cons: Must correctly identify outer/inner; can fail if curves cross multiple times
B: Compute each area separately and subtract
When: When the region is complex or curves swap inner/outer roles
  1. Find intersection points
  2. Compute A₁ = ½∫r₁² dθ over the relevant range
  3. Compute A₂ = ½∫r₂² dθ over the relevant range
  4. Subtract: Area between = A₁ − A₂ (choosing appropriately)
  5. Exploit symmetry if possible
+ Pros: Easier to track which area is which; less chance of sign confusion
– Cons: Two separate integrals to compute
Formulas
$A = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\alpha}^{\beta} (r_1^2 - r_2^2) \, d\theta$ (derived from formula booklet)
$A = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\alpha}^{\beta} r^2 \, d\theta$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to subtract the inner curve's area — computing only the outer area
  • Incorrectly expanding (a − b cos nθ)² — sign errors on the cross term
  • Wrong intersection angles leading to wrong limits
  • Not exploiting symmetry, making the integral unnecessarily complicated

Must memorize

  • $A = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\alpha}^{\beta} r^2 \, d\theta$ — polar area formula (in formula booklet but must apply correctly)
  • $x = r\cos\theta, \quad y = r\sin\theta$ — polar-Cartesian conversion
  • $\frac{dy}{d\theta} = \frac{dr}{d\theta}\sin\theta + r\cos\theta$ — for tangent parallel to initial line (set = 0)
  • $\frac{dx}{d\theta} = \frac{dr}{d\theta}\cos\theta - r\sin\theta$ — for tangent perpendicular to initial line (set = 0)
  • $\cos^2\theta = \frac{1}{2}(1 + \cos 2\theta)$ and $\sin^2\theta = \frac{1}{2}(1 - \cos 2\theta)$ — essential for integration
  • $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/d\theta}{dx/d\theta}$ — gradient in polar form
  • Standard curve shapes: cardioid $r = a(1 + \cos\theta)$, rose $r = a\cos n\theta$, limaçon $r = a + b\cos\theta$

Global strategy

  • Almost every FP2 polar question has two parts: (i) tangent finding and (ii) area calculation. Master both independently, then combine.
  • Always identify the type of tangent first: PARALLEL to initial line → dy/dθ = 0; PERPENDICULAR to initial line → dx/dθ = 0. This is the single most common source of errors.
  • Before integrating for area, always determine the correct limits by finding where r = 0 or where the region boundaries lie. Sketch a rough curve if no sketch is given.
  • Trig identities are essential for every area integration: memorise cos²θ = ½(1 + cos 2θ) and sin²θ = ½(1 − cos 2θ) cold.
  • Exploit symmetry whenever possible — most polar curves tested (cardioids, rose curves, limaçons) are symmetric about the initial line, so integrate over half and double.
  • Always write the area formula with both the ½ AND the r² — losing either is an instant mark loss. Check your setup before you start integrating.
  • When expanding (a + b cos θ)², write it as a² + 2ab cos θ + b² cos² θ carefully — the cross term is a frequent source of sign errors.
  • In tangent problems, always use the product rule when differentiating r sin θ or r cos θ — never skip to 'just differentiating r'.

Exam tips

  1. Read the question carefully to distinguish 'parallel to' vs 'perpendicular to' the initial line — this determines whether you set dy/dθ = 0 or dx/dθ = 0.
  2. Write out the area formula explicitly with limits before expanding r² — examiners award method marks for the correct setup.
  3. When integrating, show each trig identity substitution on a separate line — this earns intermediate method marks even if you make an arithmetic slip later.
  4. After computing an area, do a quick sanity check: is your answer positive? Is it a reasonable fraction of πa² (or similar)? Negative or huge areas indicate an error.
  5. For sketching questions, always label the maximum value of r and the angles where r = 0 — these are almost always required for full marks.
  6. If stuck on a tangent equation, convert the specific point to Cartesian (x, y) first, then find the gradient — sometimes this is quicker than working entirely in polar form.
  7. In multi-part questions, later parts often use results from earlier parts. If you can't do part (a), state a plausible answer and use it in part (b) to still earn method marks.
  8. Keep exact values throughout (fractions of π, surds) — do not use decimals unless the question asks for a numerical approximation.
