Standard first-order linear ODE solved by integrating factor
#1 most important
6×
7-11m

You are given a first-order ODE that is (or can be rearranged into) the standard linear form dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x). The question asks for the general solution and/or a particular solution given boundary conditions. No substitution is required.

Triggersdy/dxintegrating factorgeneral solutionparticular solutiongiven that y = ... when x = ...
Assumptions
  • The ODE must be in standard form dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x) with coefficient of dy/dx equal to 1 before reading off P(x)
  • The integrating factor is always positive: e^(∫P(x)dx) — no constant of integration is included when computing the IF
  • The constant of integration is introduced only when integrating the RHS after multiplying by the IF
  • The domain of the solution is assumed to be where P(x) and Q(x) are continuous unless otherwise stated
Approaches
A: Integrating Factor Method (standard)
When: Always — this is the required method for linear first-order ODEs on this specification
  1. 1. Rearrange the ODE into standard form: dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x). Ensure the coefficient of dy/dx is exactly 1 by dividing through if necessary.
  2. 2. Identify P(x) from the coefficient of y.
  3. 3. Compute the integrating factor: μ(x) = e^(∫P(x) dx). Do NOT include a constant of integration here.
  4. 4. Multiply every term of the ODE by μ(x): μ·dy/dx + μ·P(x)·y = μ·Q(x).
  5. 5. Recognise that the LHS equals d/dx[μ(x)·y].
  6. 6. Integrate both sides: μ(x)·y = ∫μ(x)·Q(x) dx + C.
  7. 7. Divide through by μ(x) to get y = ... (general solution).
  8. 8. If boundary conditions are given, substitute to find C and state the particular solution.
+ Pros: Systematic and always works for linear first-order ODEs; well-rehearsed procedure
– Cons: The integral ∫μ(x)·Q(x) dx can be difficult and may require integration by parts or partial fractions
Formulas
$\text{Standard form: } \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x)$ (MEMORIZE)
$\mu(x) = e^{\int P(x)\,dx}$ — integrating factor (formula booklet)
$\frac{d}{dx}[\mu(x)\cdot y] = \mu(x)\cdot Q(x)$ — exact derivative recognition (MEMORIZE)
$\mu(x)\cdot y = \int \mu(x)\cdot Q(x)\,dx + C$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to divide through so the coefficient of dy/dx is 1 before identifying P(x) — gives wrong IF
  • Including a constant of integration when computing the integrating factor exponent ∫P(x)dx
  • Forgetting to multiply ALL terms (including Q(x) on the RHS) by the integrating factor
  • Sign errors when P(x) involves subtraction, e.g. reading +P(x) when the ODE has −P(x)y
  • Dropping the constant of integration C when finding the general solution, making it impossible to apply boundary conditions
  • Forgetting to rearrange back to y = ... after integrating (leaving answer as μ·y = ...)

Integrating factor ODE requiring integration by parts or algebraic division for the RHS integral

A standard linear first-order ODE solved by integrating factor, but the integral ∫μ(x)Q(x)dx on the RHS requires a non-trivial technique: integration by parts, algebraic long division, or rewriting a rational function. Often involves terms like xe^{kx}, x/(x+1), or x·ln(x).

Triggersintegration by partsalgebraic divisionx/(x+1)xe^ln x
Assumptions
  • All standard IF method assumptions apply
  • When ∫μQ dx requires integration by parts, the LIATE rule helps choose u and dv
  • Rational functions like −x/(x+1) should be rewritten as −1 + 1/(x+1) before integrating
Approaches
A: IF method with integration by parts on RHS
When: When μ(x)·Q(x) is a product of polynomial and exponential (or similar)
  1. 1. Put ODE in standard form, find IF μ(x).
  2. 2. Write μy = ∫μQ dx.
  3. 3. For ∫μQ dx, set u = polynomial part, dv = exponential (or trig) part · dx.
  4. 4. Apply IBP: ∫u dv = uv − ∫v du. May need to apply twice.
  5. 5. Add constant C, divide by μ, apply boundary conditions.
+ Pros: Handles the most common 'hard integral' scenario cleanly
– Cons: Sign errors compound through multiple IBP steps
B: IF method with algebraic manipulation on RHS
When: When μQ involves rational expressions like x/(x+1)
  1. 1. Put ODE in standard form, find IF μ(x).
  2. 2. Write μy = ∫μQ dx.
  3. 3. Rewrite the integrand using algebraic division or partial fractions, e.g. x/(x+1) = 1 − 1/(x+1).
  4. 4. Integrate term by term.
  5. 5. Add constant C, divide by μ, apply boundary conditions.
+ Pros: Avoids IBP entirely; simpler once the rewrite is spotted
– Cons: Students often fail to spot the algebraic division trick
Formulas
$\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$ — integration by parts (formula booklet)
$\frac{x}{x+1} = 1 - \frac{1}{x+1}$ — algebraic division (MEMORIZE pattern)
$\int xe^{ax}\,dx = \frac{e^{ax}}{a^2}(ax - 1) + C$ (MEMORIZE or derive via IBP)
Pitfalls
  • Incorrectly integrating −x/(x+1): must rewrite as −1 + 1/(x+1) via algebraic division before integrating
  • Sign mistakes with repeated integration by parts, especially for ∫−6xe^{−4x}dx
  • Forgetting the negative sign inside the exponential when integrating e^{−kx} terms
  • Not simplifying the final answer after dividing by μ(x)

First-order ODE with given variable substitution (non-Bernoulli)

A first-order ODE that is not directly separable or linear. A substitution like v = y − 2x (or similar linear combination) is given. After substituting, the ODE becomes separable. Often includes follow-up parts on curve sketching, asymptotes, or behaviour of solutions.

Assumptions
  • The substitution is always given in the question — apply it exactly as stated
  • When differentiating v = y − f(x), remember dy/dx = dv/dx + f'(x)
  • After substitution the new ODE should be separable — if it isn't, recheck the algebra
  • Partial fractions or standard integrals may be needed for the separated form
Approaches
A: Apply given substitution, separate and integrate
When: When the question provides a substitution like v = y − ax − b or v = y/x
  1. 1. Write y in terms of v and x: y = v + f(x) (e.g. y = v + 2x).
  2. 2. Differentiate: dy/dx = dv/dx + f'(x).
  3. 3. Substitute both y and dy/dx into the original ODE.
  4. 4. Simplify — the result should be separable in v and x.
  5. 5. Separate variables: g(v) dv = h(x) dx.
  6. 6. Integrate both sides (may need partial fractions, standard results, etc.).
  7. 7. Back-substitute v = y − f(x) to express solution in terms of y and x.
  8. 8. Apply initial/boundary conditions if given to find the particular solution.
+ Pros: Converts an otherwise unsolvable ODE into a separable one; guided by the question
– Cons: Algebraic simplification after substitution can be error-prone, especially with products like (v+2x)(v−2x)
B: Verify by differentiating the answer (checking only)
When: As a check if time permits — differentiate the found solution implicitly and verify it satisfies the original ODE
  1. 1. Take your particular solution y = f(x).
  2. 2. Differentiate implicitly to find dy/dx.
  3. 3. Substitute back into the original ODE and verify LHS = RHS.
+ Pros: Confirms correctness and can rescue marks if the derivation has errors
– Cons: Time-consuming; not a solution method, only a verification
Formulas
$v = y - f(x) \Rightarrow \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dv}{dx} + f'(x)$ (MEMORIZE)
$\int \frac{1}{v^2}\,dv = -\frac{1}{v} + C$ (MEMORIZE)
$\int \frac{1}{v}\,dv = \ln|v| + C$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to differentiate the substitution: if v = y − 2x then dy/dx = dv/dx + 2, NOT dv/dx
  • Sign errors when expanding products like (v + 2x)(v − 2x) = v² − 4x²
  • Forgetting the constant of integration and then being unable to apply initial conditions
  • Not back-substituting to give the final answer in terms of the original variables y and x

Assumptions
  • The substitution is typically given in the question — do NOT need to derive it from scratch
  • y ≠ 0 is assumed so division by y^n is valid
  • After substitution the resulting ODE must be linear in the new variable z (or v)
  • The integrating factor method is then applied to the transformed linear ODE
Approaches
A: Bernoulli substitution then integrating factor
When: When the ODE has the form dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x)y^n and a substitution is given or implied
  1. 1. Divide the original ODE through by y^n to get y^{-n} dy/dx + P(x)y^{1-n} = Q(x).
  2. 2. Let z = y^{1-n}. Differentiate: dz/dx = (1-n)y^{-n} dy/dx.
  3. 3. Substitute into the ODE: (1/(1-n)) dz/dx + P(x)z = Q(x), i.e. dz/dx + (1-n)P(x)z = (1-n)Q(x).
  4. 4. This is now a standard linear first-order ODE in z. Identify the new P̃(x) = (1-n)P(x).
  5. 5. Compute integrating factor μ(x) = e^{∫P̃(x)dx}.
  6. 6. Multiply through by μ(x), recognise LHS as d/dx[μz], integrate both sides.
  7. 7. Solve for z, then back-substitute z = y^{1-n} to get y.
+ Pros: Reduces a nonlinear ODE to a linear one systematically; the substitution is usually given
– Cons: Multiple layers of substitution and chain rule make sign/coefficient errors very likely
B: Direct substitution using given z and chain rule
When: When the question says 'use the substitution z = ...' and asks you to show the transformed ODE
  1. 1. Write z = y^{1-n} (e.g. z = y^{-2}). Differentiate using chain rule: dz/dx = (1-n)y^{-n} · dy/dx.
  2. 2. From the chain rule result, express dy/dx in terms of dz/dx and y (or z).
  3. 3. Substitute dy/dx and y (expressed via z) into the original ODE.
  4. 4. Simplify to obtain a linear ODE in z and x.
  5. 5. Solve the linear ODE using the integrating factor method.
  6. 6. Back-substitute to find y.
+ Pros: Follows the question's guided structure closely; partial marks for each step
– Cons: Easy to lose a negative sign in the chain rule differentiation of z = y^{-2}
Formulas
$\text{Bernoulli form: } \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x)y^n$ (MEMORIZE)
$z = y^{1-n} \Rightarrow \frac{dz}{dx} = (1-n)y^{-n}\frac{dy}{dx}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\text{For } n=3: z = y^{-2}, \quad \frac{dz}{dx} = -2y^{-3}\frac{dy}{dx}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\mu(x) = e^{\int P(x)\,dx}$ — integrating factor (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting the negative sign when differentiating z = y^{-2}: dz/dx = −2y^{-3} dy/dx, NOT +2y^{-3} dy/dx
  • Forgetting to divide the original ODE by y^n (e.g. y³) before making the substitution
  • Sign errors in integration by parts when integrating the RHS (e.g. ∫−6xe^{−4x}dx)
  • Failing to back-substitute z = y^{-2} to give the final answer in terms of y
  • Algebraic errors when expressing y in terms of z at the end (e.g. y = ±z^{-1/2})

Must memorize

  • $\text{Standard linear form: } \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x)$ — coefficient of dy/dx must be 1
  • $\mu(x) = e^{\int P(x)\,dx}$ — integrating factor (formula booklet, but must know how to apply)
  • $\frac{d}{dx}[\mu(x) \cdot y] = \mu(x)\left[\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y\right]$ — exact derivative recognition (MEMORIZE)
  • $\text{Bernoulli: } \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x)y^n \Rightarrow z = y^{1-n}, \quad \frac{dz}{dx} = (1-n)y^{-n}\frac{dy}{dx}$ (MEMORIZE)
  • $\int xe^{ax}\,dx = \frac{e^{ax}}{a^2}(ax-1) + C$ (MEMORIZE or derive quickly via IBP)
  • $\frac{x}{x+a} = 1 - \frac{a}{x+a}$ — algebraic division trick for integration (MEMORIZE)

Global strategy

  • FIRST STEP ALWAYS: Rearrange the ODE into the standard linear form dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x) with coefficient of dy/dx = 1. This is where most errors originate.
  • READ THE QUESTION for substitution hints: if a substitution is given, USE IT. If y^n appears on the RHS, suspect Bernoulli. If a linear combination like v = y − 2x is given, the ODE will become separable after substitution.
  • When computing the integrating factor μ = e^{∫P(x)dx}, do NOT include a constant of integration — the constant is only added when integrating the full RHS.
  • After multiplying by the IF, ALWAYS check that the LHS is d/dx[μy] — this is your verification that P(x) and IF are correct.
  • For Bernoulli equations, master the chain rule step: if z = y^{1-n}, then dz/dx = (1-n)y^{-n} dy/dx. The factor (1-n) and especially its sign are critical.
  • Practice the key RHS integrals: integration by parts for products like xe^{kx}, algebraic division for rational functions like x/(x+1), and partial fractions.
  • Always state the general solution (with C) before applying boundary conditions — partial marks are awarded for each stage.
  • In multi-part questions, the 'show that' part for the transformed ODE carries marks — write every algebraic step clearly.

Exam tips

  1. If the ODE has a term like y³ or y^n on the RHS and a substitution z = y^{1-n} is given, divide the ENTIRE equation by y^n FIRST before substituting — this is the step most students skip.
  2. When computing ∫P(x)dx for the integrating factor, do NOT add a constant of integration. The arbitrary constant appears later when you integrate the RHS.
  3. After finding μy = ∫μQ dx + C, divide EVERY term (including C) by μ(x) to isolate y.
  4. Label your integrating factor clearly and box it — examiners look for this. Typical forms: e^{2x}, e^{-x²}, (x+1), x², e^{ln(x+1)} = x+1.
  5. If an integrating factor comes out as e^{ln f(x)}, simplify immediately to f(x) — don't leave it in exponential-log form.
  6. In 'show that' parts, match EXACTLY the form given in the question — if they write dz/dx − 4z = ..., make sure your signs and coefficients match precisely.
  7. For long questions (13-14m), expect multiple stages: substitution → transformed ODE → IF → general solution → particular solution → sketch/interpret. Budget time accordingly (~1.5 min per mark).
  8. Check your final answer by substituting back into the original ODE if time allows — this catches sign errors that lose multiple marks downstream.
