Worked solution

Part (a): Write down the equation of the directrix of $P$.

Thought Process: We recognize that $y^2 = 4ax$ is the standard form equation of a parabola. For this specific form, the focus is at $(a,0)$ and the directrix is a vertical line located at $x = -a$. This is a fundamental property that must be recalled directly.

Working:
The given parabola has the equation $y^2 = 4ax$.
By definition, for a parabola of the form $y^2 = 4ax$ with focus $S(a,0)$, the equation of the directrix is $x = -a$.

Answer: The equation of the directrix is $x = -a$.

---

Part (b)(i): Show that the equation of the tangent to $P$ at $Q$ is $y t = x + at^2$.

Thought Process: To find the equation of a tangent line, we need two things: a point on the line and its gradient. We are given the point $Q(at^2, 2at)$. We need to find the gradient, $ rac{dy}{dx}$, at this point. Since the parabola's equation $y^2 = 4ax$ is given implicitly, we will use implicit differentiation with respect to $x$ to find $ rac{dy}{dx}$. Once we have $ rac{dy}{dx}$, we'll substitute the coordinates of $Q$ to find the specific gradient at $Q$. Finally, we'll use the point-gradient formula $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ to form the tangent equation and rearrange it into the required form.

Working:
Given the equation of the parabola: $y^2 = 4ax$.

First, differentiate implicitly with respect to $x$ to find $ rac{dy}{dx}$:
$$ rac{d}{dx}(y^2) = rac{d}{dx}(4ax) $$
Applying the chain rule to $y^2$ and differentiating $4ax$:
$$ 2y rac{dy}{dx} = 4a $$
Isolate $ rac{dy}{dx}$:
$$ rac{dy}{dx} = rac{4a}{2y} = rac{2a}{y} $$

Next, find the gradient at the point $Q(at^2, 2at)$. Substitute $y = 2at$ into the gradient expression:
$$ m = rac{dy}{dx} \Big|_{Q} = rac{2a}{2at} = rac{1}{t} $$

Now, use the point-gradient form of a straight line, $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$, with point $Q(x_1, y_1) = (at^2, 2at)$ and gradient $m = rac{1}{t}$:
$$ y - 2at = rac{1}{t}(x - at^2) $$
Multiply both sides by $t$ to eliminate the fraction:
$$ t(y - 2at) = x - at^2 $$
Expand the left side:
$$ ty - 2at^2 = x - at^2 $$
Rearrange the terms to match the target form $ty = x + at^2$:
$$ ty = x - at^2 + 2at^2 $$
$$ ty = x + at^2 $$
This shows the required equation of the tangent at $Q$.

Answer: The equation of the tangent to $P$ at $Q$ is $ty = x + at^2$.

---

Part (b)(ii): The tangent at $Q$ meets the x-axis at point $R$. Find the coordinates of $R$.

Thought Process: A line intersects the x-axis when its y-coordinate is zero. We will substitute $y=0$ into the equation of the tangent we just derived ($ty = x + at^2$) and solve for $x$ to find the x-coordinate of $R$. The y-coordinate of $R$ will be 0.

Working:
The equation of the tangent is $ty = x + at^2$.
To find where it meets the x-axis, set $y=0$:
$$ t(0) = x + at^2 $$
$$ 0 = x + at^2 $$
Solve for $x$:
$$ x = -at^2 $$
So, the coordinates of point $R$ are $(-at^2, 0)$.

Answer: The coordinates of $R$ are $(-at^2, 0)$.

Key techniques

  • Recall standard form parabola properties
  • Differentiate implicitly to find gradient function
  • Use parametric coordinates to find specific gradient
  • Apply point-gradient form for tangent equation
  • Solve for intercepts by setting variables to zero

Common pitfalls

  • Incorrectly recalling the directrix equation (e.g., $x=a$ instead of $x=-a$).
  • Errors in implicit differentiation, such as forgetting to multiply by $\frac{dy}{dx}$ or differentiating $4ax$ incorrectly.
  • Algebraic mistakes when rearranging the tangent equation to the required form.
  • Confusing x-intercept with y-intercept (i.e., setting $x=0$ instead of $y=0$ for the x-axis intersection).
