Triggersrectangular hyperbolaxy = c²tangentnormalsecond intersectionct, c/t
Assumptions
  • Hyperbola is in standard rectangular form xy = c²
  • Parametric point: (ct, c/t) for parameter t ≠ 0
  • When finding a second intersection, the known parameter is a repeated root that can be factored out
Approaches
A: Implicit differentiation of xy = c²
When: When the question gives the Cartesian form or says 'use calculus'
  1. Differentiate xy = c² implicitly: y + x(dy/dx) = 0
  2. Solve: dy/dx = −y/x
  3. At P(ct, c/t): dy/dx = −(c/t)/(ct) = −1/t²
  4. Tangent: y − c/t = (−1/t²)(x − ct), rearrange to x + t²y = 2ct
  5. Normal gradient = t², then y − c/t = t²(x − ct)
  6. To find Q, substitute into xy = c² and solve the resulting quadratic
+ Pros: Rigorous; earns full method marks
– Cons: The algebra for finding Q can be heavy
B: Parametric differentiation
When: When working with parametric form (ct, c/t)
  1. x = ct → dx/dt = c; y = c/t = ct⁻¹ → dy/dt = −c/t²
  2. dy/dx = (−c/t²)/c = −1/t²
  3. Continue as in approach A for tangent/normal equations
  4. For second intersection, substitute x = cu, y = c/u into the line equation and solve for u
+ Pros: Natural when parametric coordinates are given
– Cons: Must be careful with the sign of dy/dt
Formulas
$xy = c^2$ — standard rectangular hyperbola (MEMORIZE)
Parametric form: $x = ct,\ y = c/t$ (MEMORIZE)
Tangent at parameter $t$: $x + t^2 y = 2ct$ (MEMORIZE)
Normal at parameter $t$: $t^3 x - ty = c(t^4 - 1)$ (MEMORIZE for speed)
Gradient at $(ct, c/t)$: $dy/dx = -1/t^2$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Algebraic errors when solving the quadratic for the second intersection point Q — factor out the known root
  • Incorrectly calculating the gradient of the normal: forgetting the negative reciprocal gives +t², not −t²
  • Differentiating y = c²/x incorrectly — remember c² is constant so dy/dx = −c²/x²
  • Confusing the rectangular hyperbola xy = c² with the standard hyperbola x²/a² − y²/b² = 1

Triggersmeets the parabola againintersectionsubstitutesecond pointdirectrix
Assumptions
  • The tangent at parameter t touches the parabola at exactly one point (repeated root at t)
  • A line meeting the parabola at two points gives a quadratic in t — use sum/product of roots
  • The directrix is x = −a for y² = 4ax
Approaches
A: Substitute line into parabola equation
When: Finding where a given line meets the parabola
  1. Write the equation of the line (tangent or normal)
  2. Substitute y from the line into y² = 4ax (or substitute x = at², y = 2at)
  3. Rearrange into a quadratic in y (or t)
  4. One root is known (the point of tangency); find the other root by factoring or sum/product of roots
  5. Use the second root to find coordinates of the second intersection
+ Pros: Systematic and reliable
– Cons: Can be algebraically heavy; watch for sign errors
B: Use parametric substitution directly
When: When working parametrically and seeking the parameter of the second intersection
  1. Let the second point have parameter s, so it lies at (as², 2as)
  2. Substitute into the tangent/normal equation
  3. Solve for s (one solution is the known parameter, factor it out)
  4. State coordinates using the new parameter
+ Pros: Keeps everything in terms of parameters; often cleaner
– Cons: Requires comfort with parametric algebra
Formulas
Tangent at $t$: $ty = x + at^2$ (MEMORIZE)
If tangent at $t_1$ meets the parabola again at $t_2$, useful to substitute $y = 2at_2$ into the tangent equation
Sum and product of roots for $At^2 + Bt + C = 0$: $t_1 + t_2 = -B/A$, $t_1 t_2 = C/A$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Incorrectly assuming the tangent meets the parabola at two points — tangent touches, so has a repeated root; normals and other lines intersect at two distinct points
  • Errors when substituting parametric coordinates into the line equation
  • Forgetting to check which root corresponds to the original point vs the new intersection
  • Dropping a factor when simplifying, leading to a lost solution

Triggersnormalparabolaintersectionperpendicularlocusparameters p and q
Assumptions
  • P and Q are distinct points on the parabola, so p ≠ q
  • Normal gradient = −1/(tangent gradient) = −t for parameter t
  • When solving simultaneous equations, (p − q) ≠ 0 so can be cancelled
Approaches
A: Derive normal equations then solve simultaneously
When: Standard approach for finding intersection of two normals
  1. Gradient of tangent at parameter t is 1/t, so gradient of normal is −t
  2. Normal at P(ap², 2ap): y − 2ap = −p(x − ap²)
  3. Simplify to y = −px + 2ap + ap³
  4. Write analogous normal at Q(aq², 2aq): y = −qx + 2aq + aq³
  5. Subtract to eliminate y: 0 = (q − p)x + 2a(p − q) + a(p³ − q³)
  6. Factor p³ − q³ = (p − q)(p² + pq + q²) and cancel (p − q)
  7. Solve for x, then back-substitute for y
  8. Simplify coordinates of intersection N
+ Pros: Systematic and works for any pair of normals; leads naturally to locus results
– Cons: Heavy algebra; factorisation of p³ − q³ is essential and easy to miss
B: Use memorised normal equation and standard results
When: When speed is needed and you have memorised the normal form
  1. Normal at parameter t: $y + tx = 2at + at^3$ (memorised)
  2. Write both normals and subtract
  3. Cancel (p − q) factor immediately
  4. State the known intersection result: $x = a(p^2 + pq + q^2 + 2)$, $y = -apq(p + q)$
+ Pros: Very fast if formulas are memorised correctly
– Cons: Risky if formula is misremembered; may not earn method marks if derivation is required
Formulas
Normal at parameter $t$: $y + tx = 2at + at^3$ (MEMORIZE)
Intersection of normals at $p$ and $q$: $x = a(p^2 + pq + q^2 + 2)$, $y = -apq(p+q)$ (MEMORIZE for speed)
Perpendicular lines condition: $m_1 \times m_2 = -1$ (MEMORIZE)
Factorisation: $p^3 - q^3 = (p-q)(p^2+pq+q^2)$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to cancel (p − q) when subtracting normal equations — it's non-zero since P ≠ Q
  • Sign errors when expanding −p(x − ap²) — watch the double negative
  • Sign errors in the y-coordinate of N, especially with −apq(p + q)
  • When showing perpendicularity, forgetting that normal gradients are −p and −q, so product must equal −1 giving pq = −1

TriggersCartesian equationeliminateparameterinterceptsshow that
Assumptions
  • The parameter t can be eliminated algebraically (often by solving one equation for t and substituting into the other)
  • The domain of t may restrict the Cartesian curve
Approaches
A: Eliminate parameter algebraically
When: Standard approach for converting parametric to Cartesian
  1. From x = f(t), solve for t in terms of x (or express t² etc.)
  2. Substitute into y = g(t) to get y in terms of x only
  3. Simplify to obtain the Cartesian equation
  4. For tangent: use dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) at the required parameter value
  5. Find intercepts by setting x = 0 or y = 0 in the tangent equation
+ Pros: Direct and universally applicable
– Cons: Can be tricky if the parametric equations are complicated
B: Use trigonometric/identity substitution
When: When parametric equations involve trig functions (e.g., x = a cos θ, y = b sin θ)
  1. Rearrange to get cos θ = x/a, sin θ = y/b
  2. Use identity cos²θ + sin²θ = 1
  3. Simplify to Cartesian form
+ Pros: Clean when trig parametrisation is used
– Cons: Only applicable for trig parametric forms
Formulas
$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}$ (formula booklet)
Point-gradient form: $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ (MEMORIZE)
$\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Failing to find the correct value of t corresponding to a given point before computing the gradient
  • Algebraic errors when squaring or cubing to eliminate the parameter
  • Forgetting to state the Cartesian equation explicitly after elimination

Triggersparabolatangentparametricy²=4axshow thatequation of the tangent
Assumptions
  • Parabola is in standard form y² = 4ax with vertex at the origin
  • Point P lies on the parabola and has parameter t (so P = (at², 2at))
  • t ≠ 0 unless stated otherwise (so gradient is well-defined)
Approaches
A: Parametric differentiation (dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt))
When: When the point is given in parametric form (at², 2at) — the default and cleanest method
  1. Write x = at², y = 2at
  2. Differentiate: dx/dt = 2at, dy/dt = 2a
  3. Compute dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) = 2a/(2at) = 1/t
  4. Use point-gradient form: y − 2at = (1/t)(x − at²)
  5. Multiply through by t: ty − 2at² = x − at²
  6. Rearrange to ty = x + at²
+ Pros: Quick, direct, and matches the standard 'show that' form examiners expect
– Cons: Must remember to use the chain rule correctly for parametric differentiation
B: Implicit differentiation of y² = 4ax
When: When the point is given in Cartesian form (x₁, y₁) or when the question says 'use calculus'
  1. Differentiate y² = 4ax implicitly: 2y(dy/dx) = 4a
  2. Solve: dy/dx = 4a/(2y) = 2a/y
  3. At point (at², 2at): dy/dx = 2a/(2at) = 1/t
  4. Use point-gradient form: y − 2at = (1/t)(x − at²)
  5. Rearrange to ty = x + at²
+ Pros: Works even when parametric form is not given; satisfies 'use calculus' instructions
– Cons: Slightly more work; must remember implicit differentiation of y²
Formulas
$y^2 = 4ax$ — standard parabola (MEMORIZE)
Parametric form: $x = at^2,\ y = 2at$ (MEMORIZE)
Tangent at parameter $t$: $ty = x + at^2$ (MEMORIZE — or derive quickly)
$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Differentiating at² as a²t instead of 2at (forgetting the chain rule on t²)
  • Algebraic errors when rearranging to the 'show that' form, especially distributing 1/t
  • When question says 'use calculus', not using differentiation (e.g., using a memorised tangent formula without derivation loses marks)
  • Forgetting to multiply both sides by t to clear the fraction

Triggersfocusdirectrixdistanceperimetery²=4axlatus rectum
Assumptions
  • Parabola is in standard form y² = 4ax
  • Focus is at (a, 0) and directrix is x = −a
  • Focus-directrix property: for any point P on the parabola, distance from P to focus = distance from P to directrix
Approaches
A: Use focus-directrix (locus) property directly
When: When asked for distance from a point on the parabola to the focus — avoids the distance formula
  1. Identify a from y² = 4ax (e.g., y² = 36x gives 4a = 36, a = 9)
  2. State focus (a, 0) and directrix x = −a
  3. For point P(x₁, y₁) on the parabola, distance to focus = x₁ + a (by focus-directrix property)
  4. Use this to compute required distances or perimeter
+ Pros: Elegant and fast — avoids messy surds from the distance formula
– Cons: Students must remember and trust the property; not always obvious when to use it
B: Direct distance formula calculation
When: When focus-directrix property is not recognised, or when the point is not on the parabola
  1. Find coordinates of the focus (a, 0)
  2. Find coordinates of the point P
  3. Apply distance formula: d = √((x₁ − a)² + y₁²)
  4. Simplify, using y₁² = 4ax₁ to reduce the expression
+ Pros: Always works; straightforward
– Cons: Often leads to complicated surds; slower than the focus-directrix shortcut
Formulas
For $y^2 = 4ax$: focus at $(a, 0)$, directrix $x = -a$ (MEMORIZE)
Focus-directrix property: distance from $P(x_1, y_1)$ on parabola to focus $= x_1 + a$ (MEMORIZE)
Distance formula: $d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Getting the directrix wrong: it is x = −a, NOT x = a
  • Forgetting the focus-directrix property and computing distances the hard way, leading to surd errors
  • Arithmetic errors simplifying surds like √405 — must recognise 405 = 81 × 5, so √405 = 9√5
  • Confusing 4a with a: if y² = 12x then a = 3, not 12

Triggerspolararear =θdy/dxtangenthalf-line
Assumptions
  • Area formula uses ½∫r² dθ with correct limits
  • For dy/dx, convert using x = r cos θ, y = r sin θ and apply chain rule
  • Limits of integration must be chosen to cover the region exactly once
Approaches
A: Polar area formula with trig identities
When: For finding enclosed area of a polar curve
  1. Write area = ½∫[α to β] r² dθ
  2. Expand r² and simplify using double angle identities (e.g., cos²θ = ½(1 + cos 2θ))
  3. Integrate term by term
  4. Evaluate at limits and simplify
+ Pros: Standard method; works for all polar area problems
– Cons: Must correctly identify limits and apply trig identities
B: Chain rule for polar tangent (dy/dx)
When: When asked for gradient of tangent to polar curve
  1. Write x = r cos θ, y = r sin θ
  2. Find dx/dθ using product rule: dx/dθ = (dr/dθ)cos θ − r sin θ
  3. Find dy/dθ using product rule: dy/dθ = (dr/dθ)sin θ + r cos θ
  4. Compute dy/dx = (dy/dθ)/(dx/dθ)
  5. Evaluate at the given θ
+ Pros: Systematic and avoids converting the entire curve to Cartesian form
– Cons: Product rule applications can be error-prone
Formulas
Polar area: $A = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\alpha}^{\beta} r^2\, d\theta$ (formula booklet)
$\cos^2\theta = \frac{1}{2}(1 + \cos 2\theta)$ (MEMORIZE)
$x = r\cos\theta,\ y = r\sin\theta$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/d\theta}{dx/d\theta}$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting the ½ factor in the polar area formula
  • Not applying double angle identities to cos²θ or sin²θ before integrating
  • Errors in the product rule when differentiating x = r cos θ and y = r sin θ
  • Choosing incorrect limits of integration

Must memorize

  • Parabola $y^2 = 4ax$: vertex $(0,0)$, focus $(a,0)$, directrix $x = -a$
  • Parabola parametric: $P = (at^2, 2at)$
  • Parabola tangent at $t$: $ty = x + at^2$
  • Parabola normal at $t$: $y + tx = 2at + at^3$
  • Gradient on parabola at parameter $t$: $dy/dx = 1/t$
  • Focus-directrix property: distance from $P$ to focus $= x_P + a$
  • Rectangular hyperbola $xy = c^2$: parametric $(ct, c/t)$
  • Rectangular hyperbola tangent at $t$: $x + t^2 y = 2ct$
  • Gradient on rectangular hyperbola at parameter $t$: $dy/dx = -1/t^2$
  • Perpendicular lines: $m_1 \times m_2 = -1$
  • Polar area: $A = \frac{1}{2}\int r^2\, d\theta$
  • $\cos^2\theta = \frac{1}{2}(1 + \cos 2\theta)$
  • $p^3 - q^3 = (p-q)(p^2 + pq + q^2)$

Global strategy

  • Master the standard parabola y² = 4ax: know its parametric form (at², 2at), focus (a, 0), directrix x = −a, tangent (ty = x + at²), and normal (y + tx = 2at + at³) — these appear in nearly every session.
  • Know the rectangular hyperbola xy = c² equally well: parametric form (ct, c/t), tangent (x + t²y = 2ct), and gradient (−1/t²).
  • Always read whether the question says 'use calculus' — if so, you must show differentiation, not just quote a memorised tangent formula.
  • When two curves/lines intersect, substitute one equation into the other and solve. For second intersections, factor out the known root to find the other.
  • Keep the focus-directrix property in mind as a shortcut for distance calculations on the parabola: distance to focus = x-coordinate + a.
  • In longer questions (10+ marks), expect multiple parts that build on each other: tangent → intersection → geometric property (perpendicularity, collinearity, locus).
  • Practice algebraic manipulation heavily — most marks are lost to sign errors and incorrect simplification, not to conceptual misunderstanding.
  • For polar questions, always write out the area formula with the ½ factor and apply double angle identities before integrating.

Exam tips

  1. Read the question stem carefully: 'use calculus' means you must differentiate; quoting a formula without derivation will lose marks.
  2. In 'show that' questions, write every algebraic step — the examiner needs to see the working, not just the final answer.
  3. When you get a quadratic for the second intersection parameter, always factor out the known root rather than using the quadratic formula — it's faster and less error-prone.
  4. Simplify surds fully: factorise the number under the root (e.g., √405 = √(81×5) = 9√5).
  5. For the directrix, double-check the sign: it is x = −a, not x = +a. This is a very common error.
  6. If a question asks for a perimeter or distance involving a point on the parabola and the focus, try the focus-directrix property first — it almost always simplifies the calculation.
  7. In multi-part questions, use your answers from earlier parts (e.g., the tangent equation) in later parts. If you couldn't complete an earlier part, state the result given in the question and proceed.
  8. Keep track of parameters: if P has parameter p and Q has parameter q, label clearly and don't mix them up during simultaneous equation solving.
