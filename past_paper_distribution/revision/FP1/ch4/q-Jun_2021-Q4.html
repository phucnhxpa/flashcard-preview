Worked solution

Part (a): Show that the equation of the tangent to H at P is $t^2y + x = 10t$

Thought Process: We are given a rectangular hyperbola $H$ and a general point $P(5t, 5/t)$ in parametric form. To find the equation of the tangent at $P$, we first need to determine the gradient, $\frac{dy}{dx}$, at this point. Since the coordinates are given in terms of a parameter $t$, parametric differentiation is the most straightforward method. We will find $\frac{dx}{dt}$ and $\frac{dy}{dt}$, then use the chain rule formula $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}$. Once we have the gradient $m$ and the coordinates of point $P(x_P, y_P)$, we can use the point-gradient form of a straight line, $Y - y_P = m(X - x_P)$, and then simplify the resulting equation to match the target form.

Working:
Given the general point $P$ on the hyperbola $H$ as $(5t, 5/t)$.
So, we have $x = 5t$ and $y = \frac{5}{t}$.

First, find the derivatives of $x$ and $y$ with respect to $t$:
$$ \frac{dx}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}(5t) = 5 $$
For $y = \frac{5}{t}$, we can write it as $y = 5t^{-1}$.
$$ \frac{dy}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}(5t^{-1}) = 5(-1)t^{-1-1} = -5t^{-2} = -\frac{5}{t^2} $$

Now, find the gradient of the tangent, $\frac{dy}{dx}$, using the chain rule:
$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt} = \frac{-5/t^2}{5} = -\frac{1}{t^2} $$

The equation of the tangent at point $P(x_P, y_P) = (5t, 5/t)$ is given by $Y - y_P = m(X - x_P)$:
$$ Y - \frac{5}{t} = -\frac{1}{t^2}(X - 5t) $$
To eliminate the denominators and simplify, multiply the entire equation by $t^2$:
$$ t^2 \left( Y - \frac{5}{t} \right) = t^2 \left( -\frac{1}{t^2}(X - 5t) \right) $$
$$ t^2 Y - 5t = -(X - 5t) $$
$$ t^2 Y - 5t = -X + 5t $$
Rearrange the terms to get $X$ and $Y$ on one side and the constant term on the other:
$$ t^2 Y + X = 5t + 5t $$
$$ t^2 Y + X = 10t $$
This matches the required equation of the tangent to $H$ at $P$.

Part (b): Find the coordinates of the points Q and R.

Thought Process: We know the general equation of the tangent at any point $P(t)$ from part (a). We are given that the tangents at two distinct points, $Q$ and $R$, intersect at the point $(15, -5)$. This means that the coordinates $(15, -5)$ must satisfy the tangent equation for point $Q$ (let its parameter be $t_Q$) and also for point $R$ (let its parameter be $t_R$). By substituting $x=15$ and $y=-5$ into the general tangent equation, we will obtain a quadratic equation in $t$. The roots of this quadratic equation will be the parameter values $t_Q$ and $t_R$. Since $Q$ and $R$ are distinct points, the quadratic will have two distinct roots. Once we find these $t$ values, we substitute them back into the general point coordinates $(5t, 5/t)$ to find the actual coordinates of $Q$ and $R$.

Working:
Let the points $Q$ and $R$ have parameters $t_Q$ and $t_R$ respectively. The general equation of the tangent at a point with parameter $t$ is $t^2y + x = 10t$.

The tangent at $Q$ passes through the point $(15, -5)$. Substitute $x=15$ and $y=-5$ into the tangent equation for $t_Q$:
$$ t_Q^2(-5) + 15 = 10t_Q $$
$$ -5t_Q^2 + 15 = 10t_Q $$
Rearrange this into a standard quadratic equation form $at^2 + bt + c = 0$:
$$ 5t_Q^2 + 10t_Q - 15 = 0 $$
Divide the entire equation by 5 to simplify:
$$ t_Q^2 + 2t_Q - 3 = 0 $$
Similarly, the tangent at $R$ also passes through $(15, -5)$, so $t_R$ must satisfy the exact same quadratic equation:
$$ t_R^2 + 2t_R - 3 = 0 $$
Thus, $t_Q$ and $t_R$ are the two distinct roots of the quadratic equation $t^2 + 2t - 3 = 0$.
Factorise the quadratic equation:
$$ (t+3)(t-1) = 0 $$
This gives the two roots for $t$: $t = 1$ or $t = -3$.

Since $Q$ and $R$ are distinct points, we assign these values to $t_Q$ and $t_R$. Let $t_Q = 1$ and $t_R = -3$ (the assignment can be swapped).

Now, find the coordinates of $Q$ and $R$ using the general point formula $(5t, 5/t)$:
For point $Q$, using $t_Q = 1$:
$$ x_Q = 5(1) = 5 $$
$$ y_Q = \frac{5}{1} = 5 $$
So, the coordinates of $Q$ are $(5, 5)$.

For point $R$, using $t_R = -3$:
$$ x_R = 5(-3) = -15 $$
$$ y_R = \frac{5}{-3} = -\frac{5}{3} $$
So, the coordinates of $R$ are $(-15, -5/3)$.

Answer: The coordinates of the points Q and R are $(5, 5)$ and $(-15, -5/3)$.

Key techniques

  • differentiate parametric equations
  • form equation of a straight line
  • substitute known point into equation
  • solve quadratic equation

Common pitfalls

  • Algebraic errors when simplifying the tangent equation, particularly when multiplying by $t^2$ or collecting terms.
  • Incorrectly assuming the problem requires simultaneous equations for $t_Q$ and $t_R$ in part (b), rather than recognizing they are roots of the same quadratic.
  • Errors in solving the quadratic equation or in substituting the $t$ values back into the parametric coordinates to find the final points.
