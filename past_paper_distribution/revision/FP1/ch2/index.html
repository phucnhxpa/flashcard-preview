Forming a new quadratic equation from symmetric functions of roots
#1 most important
5×
8-10m

You are given a quadratic equation $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ with roots $\alpha$ and $\beta$. You must find the sum and product of the original roots, then express more complex symmetric functions (e.g. $\alpha^2 + \beta^2$, $\alpha^3 + \beta^3$, $\frac{\alpha}{\beta} + \frac{\beta}{\alpha}$, $\alpha^2\beta + \alpha\beta^2$) in terms of $\alpha + \beta$ and $\alpha\beta$. Finally, you construct a new quadratic equation whose roots are given transformed expressions of $\alpha$ and $\beta$.

Assumptions
  • The original equation $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ has two roots $\alpha$ and $\beta$ (real or complex).
  • Vieta's formulas apply: $\alpha + \beta = -b/a$ and $\alpha\beta = c/a$.
  • The new quadratic equation should have integer (or rational) coefficients unless otherwise stated.
  • The new equation is typically written in the form $x^2 - (\text{sum of new roots})x + (\text{product of new roots}) = 0$ or cleared to integer coefficients.
Approaches
A: Direct Vieta's + symmetric identity substitution
When: Always the primary method; works for all symmetric function questions in this chapter.
  1. Step 1: Identify $a$, $b$, $c$ from the given quadratic $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$.
  2. Step 2: Compute $\alpha + \beta = -b/a$ and $\alpha\beta = c/a$ using Vieta's formulas.
  3. Step 3: Let the new roots be $p$ and $q$ (given in terms of $\alpha$ and $\beta$). Write expressions for $p + q$ (sum of new roots) and $pq$ (product of new roots).
  4. Step 4: Express $p + q$ and $pq$ purely in terms of $(\alpha + \beta)$ and $\alpha\beta$ using algebraic identities.
  5. Step 5: Substitute the numerical values of $\alpha + \beta$ and $\alpha\beta$ to get numerical values for $p + q$ and $pq$.
  6. Step 6: Form the new quadratic: $x^2 - (p+q)x + pq = 0$.
  7. Step 7: If coefficients are fractional, multiply through by the LCM of denominators to obtain integer coefficients.
+ Pros: Systematic, reliable, and handles every variant of the question type.
– Cons: Requires confident algebraic manipulation; easy to make sign or fraction errors.
B: Substitution method (change of variable)
When: When the new roots are simple transformations of the old roots, e.g. new roots are $2\alpha + 1$ and $2\beta + 1$, or $1/\alpha$ and $1/\beta$.
  1. Step 1: Let $y$ be the new variable representing a new root. Express the relationship, e.g. if new root $y = 2\alpha + 1$, then $\alpha = (y-1)/2$.
  2. Step 2: Since $\alpha$ is a root of the original equation, substitute $x = (y-1)/2$ (or the appropriate inverse) into the original equation $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$.
  3. Step 3: Simplify and rearrange to get a quadratic in $y$.
  4. Step 4: Multiply through to clear fractions and obtain integer coefficients.
+ Pros: Can be faster for linear transformations of roots; avoids computing symmetric sums.
– Cons: Only works cleanly when the transformation $\alpha \mapsto f(\alpha)$ is invertible and simple; not suitable for roots like $\alpha^2 + \beta$ where both roots appear in one new root.
Formulas
$\alpha + \beta = -\frac{b}{a}$ (MEMORIZE — Vieta's formula for sum of roots)
$\alpha\beta = \frac{c}{a}$ (MEMORIZE — Vieta's formula for product of roots)
$\alpha^2 + \beta^2 = (\alpha + \beta)^2 - 2\alpha\beta$ (MEMORIZE)
$\alpha^3 + \beta^3 = (\alpha + \beta)^3 - 3\alpha\beta(\alpha + \beta)$ (MEMORIZE)
$\alpha^2\beta + \alpha\beta^2 = \alpha\beta(\alpha + \beta)$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{\alpha}{\beta} + \frac{\beta}{\alpha} = \frac{\alpha^2 + \beta^2}{\alpha\beta} = \frac{(\alpha+\beta)^2 - 2\alpha\beta}{\alpha\beta}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{1}{\alpha} + \frac{1}{\beta} = \frac{\alpha + \beta}{\alpha\beta}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{1}{\alpha^2} + \frac{1}{\beta^2} = \frac{(\alpha+\beta)^2 - 2\alpha\beta}{(\alpha\beta)^2}$ (MEMORIZE)
$(\alpha - \beta)^2 = (\alpha + \beta)^2 - 4\alpha\beta$ (MEMORIZE)
New quadratic: $x^2 - (\text{sum of new roots})x + (\text{product of new roots}) = 0$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Sign error: using $\alpha + \beta = b/a$ instead of $-b/a$. Always remember the negative sign.
  • Arithmetic errors when combining algebraic fractions — find a common denominator carefully.
  • Forgetting to divide by $a$ when the leading coefficient is not 1: $\alpha + \beta = -b/a$, not $-b$.
  • Incorrectly expanding $(\alpha + \beta)^2$ as $\alpha^2 + \beta^2$ instead of $\alpha^2 + 2\alpha\beta + \beta^2$.
  • Confusing $\alpha^3 + \beta^3$ identity: it is $(\alpha+\beta)^3 - 3\alpha\beta(\alpha+\beta)$, NOT $(\alpha+\beta)^3 - 3\alpha\beta$.
  • Leaving fractional coefficients in the final answer when the question asks for integer coefficients.
  • When computing products like $\frac{\alpha}{\beta} \cdot \frac{\beta}{\alpha}$, recognising this simplifies to 1.

Global strategy

  • Every question in this chapter follows the same template: extract $\alpha + \beta$ and $\alpha\beta$ from the given quadratic, compute symmetric expressions for new roots, then form the new equation. Master this workflow.
  • Build a personal 'identity toolkit' — practise writing $\alpha^2 + \beta^2$, $\alpha^3 + \beta^3$, $1/\alpha + 1/\beta$, $\alpha/\beta + \beta/\alpha$, etc. in terms of $S = \alpha + \beta$ and $P = \alpha\beta$ until it is instant.
  • Always start by writing $S = \alpha + \beta = -b/a$ and $P = \alpha\beta = c/a$ explicitly at the top of your working — this prevents sign errors later.
  • When the new roots involve fractions (e.g. $\alpha/\beta$), compute sum and product of new roots separately, simplify each fully, then substitute $S$ and $P$.
  • Check your final quadratic by verifying that the sum and product of its roots match your computed values (quick substitution check).
  • If the algebra gets messy, consider whether the substitution method (Approach B) might be cleaner for that particular transformation.
  • Presentation matters: clearly label 'Sum of new roots = …' and 'Product of new roots = …' — examiners award method marks for these intermediate results.
  • Practise fraction arithmetic: many marks in this topic are lost to careless fraction errors, not conceptual misunderstandings.

Exam tips

  1. Write down $\alpha + \beta$ and $\alpha\beta$ as your FIRST line of working — this earns early method marks and prevents errors downstream.
  2. Double-check the sign of $\alpha + \beta$: it is $-b/a$. The most common error across all five sessions is a sign mistake here.
  3. When asked for an equation with integer coefficients, multiply through by the LCM of all denominators at the end.
  4. Show every algebraic identity you use (e.g. write '$\alpha^2 + \beta^2 = (\alpha+\beta)^2 - 2\alpha\beta$') — this earns method marks even if you make a numerical slip.
  5. If your final equation looks unusually complicated (e.g. very large coefficients), re-check your fraction arithmetic — these questions are designed to give 'nice' answers.
  6. Budget roughly 1 minute per mark. A 9-mark question should take about 9 minutes. Don't spend 15 minutes perfecting it.
  7. If you get stuck on a complex symmetric expression, try breaking it into simpler parts you already know, e.g. $\alpha^3 + \beta^3 = (\alpha+\beta)(\alpha^2 - \alpha\beta + \beta^2)$ which uses $\alpha^2 + \beta^2$ you may have already computed.
