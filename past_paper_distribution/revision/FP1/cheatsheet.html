A: Direct expansion and simplification: When multiplying two complex numbers or squaring a complex number.
B: Conjugate method for division: When dividing one complex number by another, i.e., $\frac{a+bi}{c+di}$.
$(a+bi)(c+di) = (ac-bd) + (ad+bc)i$ (MEMORIZE)
$\overline{a+bi} = a - bi$ (MEMORIZE)
$z \cdot \bar{z} = |z|^2 = a^2 + b^2$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{z_1}{z_2} = \frac{z_1 \bar{z_2}}{|z_2|^2}$ (MEMORIZE)
  • Forgetting that $i^2 = -1$, e.g., writing $i^2 = 1$ or $i^2 = i$.
  • Sign errors when expanding, especially with terms like $(-2)(6i) = -12i$ or $(-5i)(6i) = -30i^2 = +30$.
  • Using the wrong conjugate: changing the sign of the real part instead of the imaginary part.
  • Not fully simplifying fractions in the final answer — examiners require lowest terms.

A: Direct calculation: Standard approach for any complex number $z = a + bi$.
B: Using modulus properties for products/quotients: When $z$ is given as a product or quotient of complex numbers whose moduli are known.
$|z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\arg(z) = \arctan\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)$ adjusted for quadrant (MEMORIZE)
$|z_1 z_2| = |z_1||z_2|$ (MEMORIZE)
$\arg(z_1 z_2) = \arg(z_1) + \arg(z_2)$ (MEMORIZE)
$z = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)$ where $r = |z|$, $\theta = \arg(z)$ (MEMORIZE)

  • Using degrees instead of radians when the question expects radians.
  • Forgetting that $\arg(z)$ for a negative real number is $\pi$, not $0$.
  • When a parameter $\lambda$ is stated positive, forgetting to reject the negative solution.
Roots of cubic/quartic polynomials with real coefficients using Conjugate Root Theorem
10× 7-10m

A: Form quadratic factor from conjugate pair, then divide/compare: The standard approach — works for cubics and quartics alike.
B: Use Vieta's formulas (sum/product of roots): When finding unknown coefficients or when the remaining root can be deduced from sum/product relationships.
$(z - \alpha)(z - \bar{\alpha}) = z^2 - 2\text{Re}(\alpha)z + |\alpha|^2$ (MEMORIZE)
Conjugate Root Theorem: complex roots of real-coefficient polynomials occur in conjugate pairs (MEMORIZE)
For cubic $z^3 + az^2 + bz + c = 0$: $\sum \text{roots} = -a$, $\sum \text{(products in pairs)} = b$, $\text{product} = -c$ (formula booklet — Vieta's)
$(p+qi)^2 = p^2 - q^2 + 2pqi$ (MEMORIZE)
  • Forgetting the Conjugate Root Theorem entirely — writing only the given root and not its conjugate.
  • Sign error: $(qi)^2 = q^2 i^2 = -q^2$, NOT $+q^2$. E.g., $(3i)^2 = -9$, not $9$.
  • Errors in expanding $(z^2 - 2pz + (p^2+q^2))(z - r)$ — systematic expansion is essential.
  • For Vieta's formulas, confusing the sign convention (leading coefficient must be 1 or accounted for).
  • Not checking the answer by substituting the given complex root back into the polynomial.

Forming a new quadratic equation from symmetric functions of roots

A: Direct Vieta's + symmetric identity substitution: Always the primary method; works for all symmetric function questions in this chapter.
B: Substitution method (change of variable): When the new roots are simple transformations of the old roots, e.g. new roots are $2\alpha + 1$ and $2\beta + 1$, or $1/\alpha$ and $1/\beta$.
$\alpha + \beta = -\frac{b}{a}$ (MEMORIZE — Vieta's formula for sum of roots)
$\alpha\beta = \frac{c}{a}$ (MEMORIZE — Vieta's formula for product of roots)
$\alpha^2 + \beta^2 = (\alpha + \beta)^2 - 2\alpha\beta$ (MEMORIZE)
$\alpha^3 + \beta^3 = (\alpha + \beta)^3 - 3\alpha\beta(\alpha + \beta)$ (MEMORIZE)
$\alpha^2\beta + \alpha\beta^2 = \alpha\beta(\alpha + \beta)$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{\alpha}{\beta} + \frac{\beta}{\alpha} = \frac{\alpha^2 + \beta^2}{\alpha\beta} = \frac{(\alpha+\beta)^2 - 2\alpha\beta}{\alpha\beta}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{1}{\alpha} + \frac{1}{\beta} = \frac{\alpha + \beta}{\alpha\beta}$ (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{1}{\alpha^2} + \frac{1}{\beta^2} = \frac{(\alpha+\beta)^2 - 2\alpha\beta}{(\alpha\beta)^2}$ (MEMORIZE)

  • Arithmetic errors when combining algebraic fractions — find a common denominator carefully.
  • Forgetting to divide by $a$ when the leading coefficient is not 1: $\alpha + \beta = -b/a$, not $-b$.
  • Incorrectly expanding $(\alpha + \beta)^2$ as $\alpha^2 + \beta^2$ instead of $\alpha^2 + 2\alpha\beta + \beta^2$.
  • Confusing $\alpha^3 + \beta^3$ identity: it is $(\alpha+\beta)^3 - 3\alpha\beta(\alpha+\beta)$, NOT $(\alpha+\beta)^3 - 3\alpha\beta$.

A: Direct evaluation at endpoints: Always — this is the only standard approach for this sub-question
If $f(a) \cdot f(b) < 0$ and $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$, then $\exists\; \alpha \in (a,b)$ such that $f(\alpha) = 0$ (MEMORIZE)

  • Rounding intermediate values so much that the sign change is obscured

7× 4-6m

A: Standard Newton-Raphson iteration: Always — the only approach when Newton-Raphson is specified
$x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}$ — Newton-Raphson formula (formula booklet)
Power rule: $\frac{d}{dx}(x^n) = nx^{n-1}$ including negative and fractional powers (MEMORIZE)
$\frac{d}{dx}(\tan x) = \sec^2 x$ (formula booklet)
$\frac{d}{dx}(e^x) = e^x$, $\frac{d}{dx}(\ln x) = \frac{1}{x}$ (formula booklet)
  • Differentiating f(x) incorrectly — especially with negative powers (e.g., differentiating x⁻² as −2x⁻¹ instead of −2x⁻³)
  • Premature rounding of f(x₀) or f'(x₀) before dividing — use full calculator precision throughout
  • Not quoting the Newton-Raphson formula before substituting — examiners may withhold a method mark
  • Sign errors in f(x₀)/f'(x₀) leading to iteration going the wrong direction
  • Forgetting constant terms when differentiating (derivative of a constant is 0)

B: Similar triangles / geometric approach: When you prefer a visual/geometric derivation or want to cross-check the formula

A: Standard/strong induction matching recurrence: When a closed form is given and must be shown to satisfy a recurrence relation.
For second-order recurrence $u_{n+1} = au_n + bu_{n-1}$: need base cases $u_1$ and $u_2$ (MEMORIZE — structural requirement)
Power manipulation: $a^{k+1} = a \cdot a^k$ (MEMORIZE)
  • Forgetting to check TWO base cases for a second-order recurrence — this loses marks.
  • Only assuming the result for n=k when you also need n=k−1 (or vice versa) for strong induction.
  • Index errors: e.g., computing $3^{3(k+1)-2}$ incorrectly as $3^{3k-1}$ instead of $3^{3k+1}$.
  • Not clearly distinguishing between 'assuming for k' and 'proving for k+1' — structure matters.

A: Split and substitute: When asked to find a sum over a non-standard range using a known formula for sum from 1 to n.
$\sum_{r=a}^{b} f(r) = \sum_{r=1}^{b} f(r) - \sum_{r=1}^{a-1} f(r)$ (MEMORIZE)
Expanding $(2n)^2 = 4n^2$, $(2n)^3 = 8n^3$ etc. — careful with substitution (MEMORIZE)
  • Substituting 2n for n: e.g., n(n+1) becomes 2n(2n+1), NOT 2n(n+1).
  • Misidentifying the required form — if the answer must be $n(an^2+bn+c)/6$, you must factor accordingly.
  • Sign errors when subtracting two large polynomial expressions.
