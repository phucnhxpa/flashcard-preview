Proof by induction for divisibility
#1 most important
4×
5-10m

You are given an expression involving powers (e.g. $3^{3n-2} + 2^{4n-1}$, or $f(n) = 7^n + 4^n + 1$) and asked to prove it is divisible by some integer (e.g. 6, 9, 12) for all positive integers $n$. The question explicitly says 'prove by induction'.

Assumptions
  • n is a positive integer (n ∈ ℤ⁺), unless otherwise stated (sometimes n ≥ 0).
  • The inductive hypothesis must be explicitly stated as f(k) = dm for some integer m, where d is the divisor.
  • All algebraic manipulations must keep the divisor d visible as a factor.
Approaches
A: Standard substitution-and-factor method
When: Always — this is the canonical approach for divisibility induction.
  1. Step 1 (Base case): Substitute n=1 (or the starting value) into f(n). Compute the numerical value and verify it is divisible by d.
  2. Step 2 (Inductive hypothesis): Assume f(k) is divisible by d, i.e., f(k) = d·m for some integer m. Write this down explicitly.
  3. Step 3 (Inductive step): Write out f(k+1) in full.
  4. Step 4: Manipulate f(k+1) to isolate f(k) or a multiple of f(k). Common technique: express the dominant exponential term as (base) × (previous power), e.g., $3^{3(k+1)-2} = 3^3 \cdot 3^{3k-2} = 27 \cdot 3^{3k-2}$.
  5. Step 5: Substitute the inductive hypothesis f(k) = dm into your expression, replacing the identified terms.
  6. Step 6: Factor d out of the entire resulting expression: f(k+1) = d × (integer expression). The remaining factor must clearly be an integer.
  7. Step 7 (Conclusion): State: 'f(k+1) is divisible by d. Since f(1) is divisible by d and f(k) divisible by d ⟹ f(k+1) divisible by d, by mathematical induction f(n) is divisible by d for all n ∈ ℤ⁺.'
+ Pros: Systematic and always works; examiners expect this structure.
– Cons: Algebraic manipulation can be heavy with multiple exponential terms.
B: Difference method (f(k+1) − c·f(k))
When: When f(n) involves two or more exponential terms with different bases; choosing c as one of the bases simplifies one term completely.
  1. Step 1 (Base case): Verify f(1) is divisible by d.
  2. Step 2 (Inductive hypothesis): Assume f(k) = dm for some integer m.
  3. Step 3: Compute f(k+1) − c·f(k) where c is chosen to cancel one exponential term (e.g., if f(n) = 3^n + 2^n, try c = 3 to kill the 3^n term or c = 2 to kill the 2^n term).
  4. Step 4: Simplify f(k+1) − c·f(k) — this should reduce to a simpler expression involving only one exponential base.
  5. Step 5: Therefore f(k+1) = c·f(k) + (simplified remainder). Since f(k) = dm, we get f(k+1) = c·dm + remainder.
  6. Step 6: Show the remainder is also divisible by d, so the whole expression is divisible by d.
  7. Step 7: State the formal conclusion.
+ Pros: Elegant; often simplifies the algebra significantly when multiple exponential bases are present.
– Cons: Requires insight to choose the right multiplier c; can confuse students unfamiliar with the technique.
Formulas
$a^{n+1} = a \cdot a^n$ — splitting powers for inductive step (MEMORIZE)
If $f(k) = dm$ then $f(k)$ can be substituted wherever it appears as $dm$ (MEMORIZE — this IS the inductive hypothesis usage)
Pitfalls
  • Not explicitly stating the inductive hypothesis as f(k) = d·m for some integer m — examiners penalize this.
  • Algebraic errors when expanding powers, e.g., $3^{3(k+1)-2} \neq 3^{3k-2+1}$; it equals $3^{3k+1}= 3^3 \cdot 3^{3k-2} = 27 \cdot 3^{3k-2}$.
  • Forgetting to verify that the 'remaining' terms after factoring out d form an integer.
  • Omitting the formal conclusion statement — marks are specifically allocated for it.
  • Incorrectly handling exponent arithmetic: e.g., $2^{4(k+1)-1} = 2^{4k+3} = 2^4 \cdot 2^{4k-1}$, NOT $2^{4k}$.

Proof by induction for summation formulae
#2
3×
5-9m

You are given a summation $\sum_{r=1}^{n} f(r)$ and a closed-form formula (often a rational expression in n, e.g. $\frac{n(an^2+bn+c)}{6}$) and asked to prove the formula by induction. Sometimes you must first find constants a, b, c before proving.

Assumptions
  • n is a positive integer, n ≥ 1 (unless stated otherwise).
  • The closed-form formula is given or must be derived first.
  • The (k+1)th term is obtained by adding the single term f(k+1) to the sum up to k.
Approaches
A: Standard induction on summation
When: Always — this is the expected method when the question says 'prove by induction'.
  1. Step 1 (Base case): Substitute n=1 into both the LHS (compute the single term f(1)) and the RHS (substitute n=1 into the closed form). Show LHS = RHS.
  2. Step 2 (Inductive hypothesis): Assume the result holds for n=k, i.e., $\sum_{r=1}^{k} f(r) = g(k)$ where g is the closed form.
  3. Step 3 (Inductive step): Consider $\sum_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r) = \sum_{r=1}^{k} f(r) + f(k+1) = g(k) + f(k+1)$.
  4. Step 4: Substitute the inductive hypothesis to replace the sum with g(k).
  5. Step 5: Simplify g(k) + f(k+1) algebraically — combine fractions, expand, collect terms, and factor.
  6. Step 6: Show that the result equals g(k+1), i.e., the closed form with n = k+1 substituted.
  7. Step 7 (Conclusion): 'True for n=1, and if true for n=k then true for n=k+1. By mathematical induction, the result holds for all n ∈ ℤ⁺.'
+ Pros: Completely standard; examiners have a clear mark scheme for each step.
– Cons: The algebra in step 5 can be messy, especially with cubic or quartic expressions.
B: Verify constants first, then induct
When: When the closed form contains unknown constants (a, b, c) that must be found before or alongside the induction proof.
  1. Step 1: Use small values of n (n=1, 2, 3) to generate simultaneous equations in the unknown constants.
  2. Step 2: Solve the simultaneous equations to find a, b, c.
  3. Step 3: Write the complete closed form with the found constants.
  4. Step 4: Proceed with standard induction (Approach A, steps 1–7) using the now-known closed form.
+ Pros: Systematic way to handle 'find and prove' questions; small-value checks also serve as partial base cases.
– Cons: Time-consuming if the simultaneous equations are messy.
Formulas
$\sum_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r) = \sum_{r=1}^{k} f(r) + f(k+1)$ — fundamental decomposition for inductive step (MEMORIZE)
$\sum_{r=1}^{n} r = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$ (formula booklet)
$\sum_{r=1}^{n} r^2 = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}$ (formula booklet)
$\sum_{r=1}^{n} r^3 = \left[\frac{n(n+1)}{2}\right]^2$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Algebraic errors when combining g(k) + f(k+1) into a single fraction — always use a common denominator carefully.
  • Forgetting to clearly show that the simplified expression equals g(k+1) — write out g(k+1) explicitly and confirm they match.
  • When finding constants a, b, c: using n=1 gives f(1) = (a+b+c)/6, but computing f(1) incorrectly from the series definition.
  • Not stating the formal conclusion — examiners explicitly award marks for the concluding sentence.
  • Dropping a negative sign or factor when expanding cubics/quartics in the inductive step.

Proof by induction involving sum manipulation / splitting ranges
#3
1×
8-11m

A multi-part question where part (a) asks for a standard induction proof of a summation, then part (b) uses the proven result to evaluate a related sum, often by splitting the range or manipulating indices (e.g. $\sum_{r=n+1}^{2n}$ expressed using $\sum_{r=1}^{2n} - \sum_{r=1}^{n}$).

Assumptions
  • The result proved in part (a) is valid and can be applied with any positive integer argument.
  • Summation properties: $\sum_{r=a}^{b} = \sum_{r=1}^{b} - \sum_{r=1}^{a-1}$ are assumed known.
  • The final answer should be in a specific factored form as requested.
Approaches
A: Split and substitute
When: When asked to find a sum over a non-standard range using a known formula for sum from 1 to n.
  1. Step 1: Express $\sum_{r=n+1}^{2n} f(r) = \sum_{r=1}^{2n} f(r) - \sum_{r=1}^{n} f(r)$.
  2. Step 2: Apply the proven closed form with the appropriate argument: substitute '2n' and 'n' respectively.
  3. Step 3: Simplify the difference — expand, collect like terms, factor.
  4. Step 4: Present the answer in the required form (e.g., $\frac{n(an^2+bn+c)}{6}$ and identify a, b, c).
+ Pros: Clean and direct once the base formula is established.
– Cons: Substituting 2n for n in a cubic/quartic formula produces heavy algebra.
Formulas
$\sum_{r=a}^{b} f(r) = \sum_{r=1}^{b} f(r) - \sum_{r=1}^{a-1} f(r)$ (MEMORIZE)
Expanding $(2n)^2 = 4n^2$, $(2n)^3 = 8n^3$ etc. — careful with substitution (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Substituting 2n for n: e.g., n(n+1) becomes 2n(2n+1), NOT 2n(n+1).
  • Misidentifying the required form — if the answer must be $n(an^2+bn+c)/6$, you must factor accordingly.
  • Sign errors when subtracting two large polynomial expressions.

Proof by induction for a recurrence relation / sequence formula

A recurrence relation is given (e.g. $u_{n+1} = au_n + bu_{n-1}$) along with initial terms, and a closed-form formula for $u_n$. You must prove by induction that the closed form satisfies the recurrence. May require TWO base cases.

Assumptions
  • If the recurrence involves u_{n} and u_{n-1} (second-order), TWO base cases are needed: typically n=1 and n=2.
  • The inductive hypothesis may need to assume the result for TWO consecutive values: n=k and n=k-1 (strong induction).
  • Sequences are defined for n ≥ 1 (or n ≥ 0 depending on context).
Approaches
A: Standard/strong induction matching recurrence
When: When a closed form is given and must be shown to satisfy a recurrence relation.
  1. Step 1 (Base cases): Verify the closed form gives the correct value for n=1 AND n=2 (for second-order recurrences).
  2. Step 2 (Inductive hypothesis): Assume the closed form holds for n=k and (if needed) n=k-1.
  3. Step 3: Substitute the closed-form expressions for u_k and u_{k-1} into the recurrence relation to compute u_{k+1}.
  4. Step 4: Simplify the resulting expression algebraically.
  5. Step 5: Show the simplified expression matches the closed form evaluated at n=k+1.
  6. Step 6 (Conclusion): State: 'True for n=1 and n=2, and if true for n=k and n=k−1 then true for n=k+1. By mathematical induction, the result holds for all n ∈ ℤ⁺.'
+ Pros: Directly uses the recurrence, making the algebraic pathway clear.
– Cons: Requires careful handling of two base cases and two inductive assumptions.
Formulas
For second-order recurrence $u_{n+1} = au_n + bu_{n-1}$: need base cases $u_1$ and $u_2$ (MEMORIZE — structural requirement)
Power manipulation: $a^{k+1} = a \cdot a^k$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to check TWO base cases for a second-order recurrence — this loses marks.
  • Only assuming the result for n=k when you also need n=k−1 (or vice versa) for strong induction.
  • Index errors: e.g., computing $3^{3(k+1)-2}$ incorrectly as $3^{3k-1}$ instead of $3^{3k+1}$.
  • Not clearly distinguishing between 'assuming for k' and 'proving for k+1' — structure matters.

Oct 2021 Q9 part (i)QPMS

Must memorize

  • The 4-part structure of induction: Base case → Inductive hypothesis → Inductive step → Conclusion
  • $a^{m+n} = a^m \cdot a^n$ and $a^{mn} = (a^m)^n$ — essential for divisibility proofs
  • $\sum_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r) = \sum_{r=1}^{k} f(r) + f(k+1)$ — the decomposition for summation induction
  • $\sum_{r=1}^{n} r = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$, $\sum_{r=1}^{n} r^2 = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}$, $\sum_{r=1}^{n} r^3 = \left[\frac{n(n+1)}{2}\right]^2$ (formula booklet)
  • For divisibility: if $f(k) = dm$ then $f(k)$ can be replaced by $dm$ wherever it appears — this is the substitution step
  • Second-order recurrences require TWO base cases and a STRONG inductive hypothesis (assume for k and k−1)

Global strategy

  • Every induction proof has exactly 4 structural components — base case, inductive hypothesis, inductive step, conclusion. Examiners allocate marks to EACH component, so never skip any.
  • Always write the inductive hypothesis explicitly: 'Assume true for n = k, i.e., [exact statement with k]'. For divisibility: 'f(k) = dm for some integer m'.
  • In the inductive step, your primary goal is to EXPRESS f(k+1) in terms of f(k) (or g(k) for sums). Make f(k) appear so you can substitute the hypothesis.
  • For divisibility proofs, the key algebraic move is factoring: write f(k+1) = (base)·f(k) + (remainder), then show the remainder is divisible by d.
  • For summation proofs, always write $\sum_{r=1}^{k+1} = \sum_{r=1}^{k} + f(k+1)$ and replace the sum using the hypothesis. Then show the simplified expression equals the closed form at k+1.
  • Practice the algebra separately: factoring cubics, combining fractions with polynomial numerators, and power/exponent arithmetic are where most marks are lost.
  • For recurrence relation proofs, check whether you need one or two base cases — second-order recurrences need two.
  • The conclusion must reference all parts: 'True for n=1; if true for n=k then true for n=k+1; therefore true for all positive integers n by mathematical induction.'

Exam tips

  1. Label each section of your proof clearly: 'Base case:', 'Inductive hypothesis:', 'Inductive step:', 'Conclusion:' — this helps both you and the examiner.
  2. Never skip the conclusion sentence — it typically carries 1 mark and is the easiest mark in the question.
  3. In divisibility proofs, after substituting f(k) = dm, factor out d immediately and circle/underline it so the examiner can see divisibility clearly.
  4. When the algebra gets messy, work towards matching a TARGET expression. Write out what g(k+1) should look like BEFORE you start simplifying g(k) + f(k+1).
  5. If you're stuck on the inductive step for divisibility, try computing f(k+1) − f(k) or f(k+1) − a·f(k) for the base a of one of the exponential terms.
  6. Double-check exponent arithmetic by substituting a small value of k (e.g. k=1) and verifying your general expression gives the right numerical answer.
  7. For 'find constants then prove' questions: finding the constants and the induction proof are often marked independently — even if your constants are wrong, a correct induction structure on your (wrong) formula can still earn method marks.
  8. Time management: a 5-6 mark induction proof should take about 7-8 minutes; a 10-mark question about 12-14 minutes. Don't spend too long on algebra — if stuck, move on and return.
