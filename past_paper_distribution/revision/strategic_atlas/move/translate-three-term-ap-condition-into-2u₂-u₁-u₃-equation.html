Sequences & Series

Translate three-term AP condition into 2u₂ = u₁ + u₃ equation

Family: Definition Exploitation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question states that three given expressions — often trigonometric, logarithmic, or algebraic — form (in that order) an arithmetic sequence or arithmetic progression. Phrasings include 'the first three terms of an AP', 'are consecutive terms of an arithmetic sequence', or 'form an AP in this order'. The hallmark is that no common difference d is given explicitly; only the AP structure is asserted. This is the only algebraic foothold into such a problem.

Motivation — Why this works

An arithmetic progression is defined by a constant common difference, so for any three consecutive terms u₁, u₂, u₃ we must have u₂ − u₁ = u₃ − u₂, equivalently 2u₂ = u₁ + u₃ (the middle term equals the arithmetic mean of its neighbours). This single equation captures the entire AP content for three terms; no further AP machinery is required. Once written, it converts a 'sequence' problem into a pure equation in the underlying variable, opening the way to identity manipulation, factorisation, or solving.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that three quantities are declared to be in AP, so a defining property must be invoked.
  2. Rules out using a + (n−1)d or sum formulae — these need a known a or d, which aren't given.
  3. Commits to the mean condition 2u₂ = u₁ + u₃ because it is the minimal, symmetric encoding of 'in AP'.
  4. Identifies cleanly which expression is u₁, u₂, u₃ from the stated order, then substitutes them into 2u₂ = u₁ + u₃.
  5. Decides whether to keep the equation as 2u₂ = u₁ + u₃ or rearrange to u₂ − u₁ = u₃ − u₂ depending on which form simplifies the subsequent algebra/identity work.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Read off u₁, u₂, u₃ from the question in the stated order.
  2. Write the AP condition 2u₂ = u₁ + u₃.
  3. Substitute the given expressions for u₁, u₂, u₃.
  4. Simplify and rearrange into a single equation in the underlying variable(s), ready for identity work or solving.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose $\sin\alpha,\ \tan\alpha,\ 2\sin\alpha$ are consecutive terms of an AP. Then $2\tan\alpha = \sin\alpha + 2\sin\alpha = 3\sin\alpha$, so $\dfrac{2\sin\alpha}{\cos\alpha} = 3\sin\alpha$. Dividing by $\sin\alpha$ (assuming $\sin\alpha\ne 0$) gives $\cos\alpha = \tfrac{2}{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Three terms in geometric progression
Replace the AP mean condition with the geometric mean: u₂² = u₁·u₃.
Four terms in AP
Use u₂ − u₁ = u₃ − u₂ = u₄ − u₃, giving two independent equations (or equivalently 2u₂ = u₁ + u₃ and 2u₃ = u₂ + u₄).
Logarithmic terms in AP, e.g. log a, log b, log c
2 log b = log a + log c reduces to b² = ac — i.e. a, b, c are in GP.
AP with an unknown to determine
Treat 2u₂ = u₁ + u₃ as an equation in that unknown and solve.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the three terms as if they were in GP and writing u₂² = u₁u₃ instead.
  • Assuming a specific common difference d is implied and trying to use a + (n−1)d.
  • Reordering the terms (e.g. picking the 'simplest-looking' as the middle term) instead of respecting the stated order.

Pitfalls

  • Misidentifying which expression is the middle term — order in the question is binding.
  • Sign errors when expanding u₂ − u₁ = u₃ − u₂ instead of using the symmetric form 2u₂ = u₁ + u₃.
  • Dividing by a factor such as sin α or cos α without noting the case where it equals zero, losing solutions.
  • Forgetting to simplify with a Pythagorean or quotient identity once the trig equation is obtained.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 sequences and series, often combined with C2/P2 trigonometric identities or logarithm laws; no techniques beyond standard A-level scope are required.
