Algebra & Functions

Factor out shared product before combining fraction with non-fraction

Family: Strategic Factorisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in induction or algebraic identity work when you have a sum of two terms (often one fractional, one not) that visibly share a product of linear factors, and the target expression also contains that same product. Typical cue: an expression like k(k+1)(k+2)/3 + (k+1)(k+2) with target (k+1)(k+2)(k+3)/3. Recognise that (k+1)(k+2) is common to both terms and to the goal. Also fires whenever 'fraction + non-fraction' tempts you to expand — pause and look for shared factors first.

Motivation — Why this works

Factoring the shared product collapses the sum into a single product times a tiny scalar bracket, reducing the proof to a one-line fraction simplification. The underlying principle is the distributive law applied in reverse: ab/3 + ac = a(b/3 + c). Because the target is itself a product of consecutive linear factors, preserving product form (rather than expanding to a cubic) keeps the algebra aligned with the goal. Expansion would force you to re-factorise a cubic, multiplying the chances of sign or coefficient error.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that both terms in the sum contain the block (k+1)(k+2), and the target also contains (k+1)(k+2).
  2. Rule out expanding into a polynomial in k — that produces a cubic needing re-factorisation, which is slower and error-prone.
  3. Commit to factoring (k+1)(k+2) out of the entire sum because it directly bridges current expression to target form.
  4. Bridge: write the sum as (k+1)(k+2)[ k/3 + 1 ], being careful to keep the /3 attached to k inside the bracket.
  5. Inside the bracket, put k/3 + 1 over the common denominator 3 to get (k+3)/3, then reassemble as a single product.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the shared factor block, here (k+1)(k+2), present in both terms.
  2. Factor it out: k(k+1)(k+2)/3 + (k+1)(k+2) = (k+1)(k+2)[k/3 + 1].
  3. Combine the residual bracket over a common denominator: k/3 + 1 = (k+3)/3.
  4. Reassemble into a single product: (k+1)(k+2)(k+3)/3.
  5. Compare with the target expression to confirm the match.

Worked Micro-Example

Show that $\frac{k(k+1)(k+2)}{3} + (k+1)(k+2) = \frac{(k+1)(k+2)(k+3)}{3}$. Factor: $\frac{k(k+1)(k+2)}{3} + (k+1)(k+2) = (k+1)(k+2)\left[\tfrac{k}{3} + 1\right]$. Simplify the bracket: $\tfrac{k}{3} + 1 = \tfrac{k+3}{3}$. Reassemble: $(k+1)(k+2)\cdot\tfrac{k+3}{3} = \tfrac{(k+1)(k+2)(k+3)}{3}$ as required.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Induction for $\sum r\cdot r! = (n+1)! - 1$, inductive step
Factor out $(k+1)!$ from $(k+1)! - 1 + (k+1)(k+1)!$ to obtain $(k+1)![1 + (k+1)] - 1 = (k+2)! - 1$.
Induction for $\sum r(r+1) = \tfrac{n(n+1)(n+2)}{3}$
After substitution, factor $(k+1)(k+2)$ out of $\tfrac{k(k+1)(k+2)}{3} + (k+1)(k+2)$ exactly as in the canonical case.
Induction for $\sum r^2 = \tfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}$
Factor $(k+1)$ from $\tfrac{k(k+1)(2k+1)}{6} + (k+1)^2$ to get $(k+1)\left[\tfrac{k(2k+1)}{6} + (k+1)\right]$, then simplify the bracket to $\tfrac{(k+2)(2k+3)}{6}$.
Partial fractions / algebraic identities outside induction
Whenever a sum has visible common linear factors and the goal preserves product form, factor first and only combine the residual scalar bracket.

Common Misreadings

  • Seeing 'fraction + non-fraction' and immediately putting everything over a common denominator before factoring — this expands the numerator unnecessarily.
  • Failing to spot that (k+1)(k+2) divides the fractional term because the /3 visually disguises the shared product.
  • Believing the move requires expanding to verify equality, instead of trusting the factor-and-simplify approach.

Pitfalls

  • When factoring (k+1)(k+2) from $k(k+1)(k+2)/3$, forgetting to keep the /3 with the residual k, yielding k inside the bracket instead of k/3.
  • Combining $k/3 + 1$ as $(k+1)/3$ instead of $(k+3)/3$ — a careless common-denominator slip.
  • Expanding instead of factoring, then having to re-factorise a cubic such as $k^3 + 6k^2 + 11k + 6$, which is doable but slow and error-prone.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard technique inside Edexcel IAL Further Pure induction proofs (FP1) and routine algebraic manipulation in Pure Maths; uses only the distributive law and basic fraction addition.
