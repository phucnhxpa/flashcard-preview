Cross-multiply to eliminate paired algebraic fractions

Family: Fraction Elimination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The equation is already in the isolated form f(x)/g(x) = h(x)/k(x): exactly one algebraic fraction on each side, with denominators that are (typically) distinct linear or low-degree polynomials. Phrasings include 'solve x/(x−6) = 4/(x+1)' or 'find x given (2x+1)/(x−3) = (x+4)/(x+1)'. The cue is the equals sign sitting between two single quotients — no sums of fractions, no constants added, no third term.

Motivation — Why this works

Cross-multiplication is shorthand for multiplying both sides by g(x)·k(x), which is valid provided neither denominator is zero. The identity a/b = c/d ⇔ ad = bc (for b,d ≠ 0) converts the rational equation into a polynomial equation in one written line, bypassing common-denominator bookkeeping. It is the minimal-writing route to a polynomial form, after which standard quadratic/linear techniques finish the job.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation has the exact shape (one fraction) = (one fraction), e.g. x/(x−6) = 4/(x+1).
  2. Rule out combining over a common denominator on one side — that introduces a composite numerator step that is unnecessary here.
  3. Commit to cross-multiplication because the structure 'fraction = fraction' is precisely what the identity ad = bc targets.
  4. Bridge: write the polynomial equation f(x)·k(x) = h(x)·g(x) directly, with brackets preserved around each factor.
  5. Inside the move, expand carefully, collect to a standard form (usually quadratic = 0), and flag denominator-zero values to exclude at the end.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the structure f(x)/g(x) = h(x)/k(x) and note the excluded values g(x) = 0, k(x) = 0.
  2. Write f(x)·k(x) = h(x)·g(x) keeping brackets around each side.
  3. Expand both sides fully.
  4. Rearrange to standard polynomial form (e.g. quadratic = 0).
  5. Solve, then discard any root that violates the excluded values.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\dfrac{x}{x-6} = \dfrac{4}{x+1}$ (with $x \neq 6, -1$). Cross-multiply: $x(x+1) = 4(x-6)$. Expand: $x^2 + x = 4x - 24$. Rearrange: $x^2 - 3x + 24 = 0$. Discriminant $= 9 - 96 < 0$, so no real solutions.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Constant on one side: (ax+b)/(cx+d) = k
Multiply both sides by the single denominator (cx+d) to get ax+b = k(cx+d); cross-multiplication degenerates to a one-sided clear.
Three or more fractions, e.g. f/g + h/k = m
Cross-multiplication does not apply directly; multiply through by the LCD g·k (and any other denominators) instead.
Rational inequality f(x)/g(x) ≥ h(x)/k(x)
Do NOT cross-multiply blindly — the sign of g(x)·k(x) affects inequality direction; rearrange to a single fraction ≥ 0 and analyse signs.
Partial-fraction identity (A/(x−a)) + (B/(x−b)) ≡ ...
Multiply through by the full denominator (x−a)(x−b)... rather than cross-multiplying, since multiple fractions are summed.
Trig/exp equations of the form sin x / cos x = a/b
Same identity ad = bc applies, giving b sin x = a cos x; then divide to obtain tan x = a/b.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating sums of fractions as if cross-multiplication applies — e.g. attempting to cross-multiply 1/x + 1/(x+1) = 2.
  • Cancelling factors diagonally across the equals sign before multiplying out, which is not a valid operation.
  • Applying cross-multiplication to an inequality without tracking the sign of the denominators.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to record and later check the excluded values where denominators vanish (extraneous roots).
  • Sign errors during expansion, especially with negative constants, e.g. writing 4(x−6) as 4x+24.
  • Dropping brackets, leading to errors like x·x+1 instead of x(x+1).
  • Failing to fully simplify to standard form before applying the quadratic formula or factorising.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1–P4): solving rational equations, partial fractions setup, and trigonometric equations. The technique is foundational and reused throughout algebra and calculus topics.
