Coordinate Geometry

Use axis intercepts to find right-triangle area with origin

Family: Intercept Area Method Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a triangle has the origin O as one vertex and its other two vertices on the coordinate axes (typically labelled A on the x-axis and B on the y-axis). Common phrasings: 'the tangent meets the axes at A and B, find the area of triangle OAB', or 'the line cuts the axes at P and Q'. The two sides OA and OB lie along perpendicular axes, so a right angle at O is guaranteed. Recognise this before reaching for shoelace or vector methods.

Motivation — Why this works

Because the x-axis and y-axis are perpendicular, OA ⟂ OB and the triangle is right-angled at O. The area formula ½ × base × height applies directly with the two intercept lengths as base and height, bypassing shoelace, determinants, or cross products. Each intercept is found by a single substitution (y=0 for x-intercept, x=0 for y-intercept), which is trivial for any linear equation such as a tangent or normal line. This converts a geometry question into two one-line algebra steps plus a multiplication.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that two vertices lie on the axes and the third is the origin — the sides along the axes are automatically perpendicular.
  2. Rule out shoelace formula and ½|x₁y₂ − x₂y₁|: correct but unnecessarily heavy for a right triangle aligned with the axes.
  3. Commit to the right-triangle shortcut: Area = ½ × |x-intercept| × |y-intercept|.
  4. Bridge: take the line equation given (tangent, normal, or chord) and substitute y=0 then x=0 to extract the two intercept values.
  5. Take absolute values when forming the lengths, since intercepts can be negative but lengths must be positive.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the line equation defining sides AB (or the relevant tangent/normal).
  2. Set y = 0 and solve for x to find A = (x₀, 0); record |OA| = |x₀|.
  3. Set x = 0 and solve for y to find B = (0, y₀); record |OB| = |y₀|.
  4. Compute Area = ½ × |OA| × |OB| = ½|x₀ y₀|.

Worked Micro-Example

The tangent to a curve has equation $y = x + 2$, meeting the axes at $A$ and $B$. Setting $y=0$: $x=-2$, so $A=(-2,0)$ and $|OA|=2$. Setting $x=0$: $y=2$, so $B=(0,2)$ and $|OB|=2$. Hence area of $\triangle OAB = \tfrac{1}{2}\times 2 \times 2 = 2$ square units.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Tangent to parabola $y^2 = 4ax$ at parameter $t$, equation $ty = x + at^2$
x-intercept $-at^2$, y-intercept $at$, giving area $= \tfrac{1}{2}\,a t^2 \cdot at = \tfrac{1}{2}a^2 t^3$ (take modulus).
Tangent to rectangular hyperbola $xy = c^2$ at $(ct, c/t)$
Tangent $x + t^2 y = 2ct$ has intercepts $2ct$ and $2c/t$; area $= 2c^2$, independent of $t$.
Tangent/normal to ellipse or circle meeting axes at $A,B$
Same shortcut applies — substitute $y=0$ and $x=0$ into the tangent/normal equation.
Triangle whose vertices are not at the origin, but two sides still lie along the axes
Translate so the right-angle vertex sits at $O$, or use shoelace directly; the ½ × intercept × intercept shortcut no longer applies.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $A$ and $B$ as points on the original curve rather than the axis intercepts of the tangent/line.
  • Mixing up which intercept corresponds to which axis (using x-intercept as height).
  • Assuming the triangle is right-angled at a non-origin vertex when it is not.

Pitfalls

  • Omitting absolute values: a negative intercept gives a negative 'length' and a negative area.
  • Using ½ × base × height when the angle at $O$ is not $90°$ (e.g. when neither side lies along an axis).
  • Algebra slips when rearranging implicit tangent equations like $ty = x + at^2$ before reading off intercepts.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL scope: applies to P1–P4 coordinate geometry of lines, and FP1/FP3 conics (parabola, ellipse, rectangular hyperbola) where tangents/normals at parametric points meet the axes.
