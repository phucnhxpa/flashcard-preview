Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a sketch question describes one x-intercept as 'touches' and another as 'passes through' or 'crosses', then asks for the sketch of a transformed curve such as $y=f(x+a)$, $y=f(-x)$, $y=2f(x)$, or $y=f(2x)$. The deliberate contrast in wording is the cue. Also fires when a graph is given with a clear tangent-to-axis feature at one root and a transverse crossing at another. Any time root multiplicity is implicit in a diagram, the touch/cross tags must travel with the points.

Motivation — Why this works

A touch corresponds to a repeated root (even multiplicity) and a crossing to a simple root (odd multiplicity). Affine transformations of the form $y=af(b(x+c))+d$ are bijective and smooth, so they preserve the order of contact between the curve and the x-axis: a tangency stays a tangency, a transverse crossing stays a crossing. Losing this distinction in the transformed sketch loses a dedicated examiner mark, since it is the qualitative feature being tested alongside the new coordinates.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the examiner's split phrasing: 'passes through' vs 'touches' — these are not interchangeable.
  2. Rule out treating both intercepts uniformly; the question is signalling that root multiplicity matters.
  3. Commit to tagging each x-intercept with its qualitative type before applying any transformation.
  4. Bridge: apply the coordinate map only to the locations, while carrying the touch/cross tag with each image point.
  5. Tactically, when sketching, exaggerate the bounce at the touch point and draw a clean transverse crossing at the simple-root image, and annotate if ambiguous.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Tag each original x-intercept: simple crossing vs tangential touch (record on scrap).
  2. Apply the coordinate mapping induced by the transformation to each tagged point.
  3. Carry the tag to the image: a touch image is still a touch, a cross image is still a cross.
  4. Sketch with the curve bouncing off the axis at touch images and passing through at cross images.
  5. Label the new coordinates and, if helpful, annotate 'touch' on the tangential image.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $y=f(x)$ crosses the x-axis at $(-1,0)$ and touches at $(3,0)$, sketch $y=f(x-2)$. Mapping $x\mapsto x+2$ on intercepts: cross at $(1,0)$ and touch at $(5,0)$. Draw the curve transverse through $(1,0)$ and tangent to the x-axis at $(5,0)$, bouncing back without crossing.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Sketching $y=f(2x)$ or $y=f(x/2)$ (horizontal stretch)
Scale x-coordinates of intercepts by $1/2$ or $2$; touch/cross tags are unchanged.
Reflection $y=f(-x)$ or $y=-f(x)$
Reflect intercepts across the y-axis or x-axis respectively; tags are preserved (a touch from above becomes a touch from below under $y=-f(x)$).
Vertical stretch $y=af(x)$ with $a>0$
x-intercepts and their touch/cross nature are unchanged; only y-extrema scale.
Sketching from a given factored polynomial like $y=(x-a)^2(x-b)$
Read multiplicity directly from exponents: even exponent → touch, odd exponent → cross (or inflection-cross for cubic factor).

Common Misreadings

  • Treating both x-intercepts as ordinary crossings, ignoring the 'touches' wording.
  • Assuming a vertical stretch or reflection can convert a touch into a crossing.
  • Reading 'touches' as merely 'passes through close to' rather than tangent contact.

Pitfalls

  • Swapping which image point is the touch after applying the mapping, especially when intercepts cross over each other under a reflection or shift.
  • Drawing a touch as a sharp corner rather than a smooth tangential bounce.
  • Forgetting to relabel the new coordinates while correctly preserving the qualitative feature.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P1/P2 graph-transformation territory: sketches of $y=f(x+a)$, $y=f(x)+a$, $y=f(ax)$, $y=af(x)$, $y=f(-x)$, $y=-f(x)$, where touch/cross at axis intercepts is a routinely marked feature.
