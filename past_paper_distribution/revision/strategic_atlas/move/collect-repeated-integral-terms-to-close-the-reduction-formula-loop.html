Algebra & Functions

Collect repeated integral terms to close the reduction-formula loop

Family: Recursive Closure Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

After integration by parts (often combined with a Pythagorean or trig identity), the original integral $I_n$ reappears on the right-hand side, e.g. $I_n = [\text{boundary}] - (n-2)I_n + (n-2)I_{n-2}$. The cue is seeing the same labelled integral on both sides of the equation. This is not circularity — it signals that the recurrence can be solved algebraically for $I_n$. Common phrasings include 'show that $(n-1)I_n = \ldots$' or 'derive a reduction formula for $I_n$'.

Motivation — Why this works

Treating $I_n$ as an algebraic unknown converts what looks like a stalled IBP into a closed recurrence. Adding $(n-2)I_n$ to both sides combines coefficients $1 + (n-2) = n-1$, isolating $I_n$ in terms of a boundary term and a lower-order $I_{n-2}$. This 'loop closure' is the defining mechanic of reduction-formula proofs: the reappearance of $I_n$ is the engine, not an obstacle.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the integral appearing on the right after IBP is the very same $I_n$ defined at the start, not a new object.
  2. Rule out re-applying IBP again or trying a fresh substitution — both lead nowhere because the structure is already algebraically solvable.
  3. Commit to treating $I_n$ as an unknown and rearranging, since the question target (a clean recurrence) requires $I_n$ alone on the left.
  4. Bridge: add the $(n-2)I_n$ term to both sides to consolidate all $I_n$ terms on the left.
  5. Tactically, factor the coefficient carefully — $1 + (n-2) = n-1$ — and present the boundary term in simplified closed form before quoting the final recurrence.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify both occurrences of $I_n$ in the post-IBP equation.
  2. Add $(n-2)I_n$ to both sides: $I_n + (n-2)I_n = [\text{boundary}] + (n-2)I_{n-2}$.
  3. Combine coefficients on the left: $(1 + n - 2)I_n = (n-1)I_n$.
  4. State the reduction formula: $(n-1)I_n = \tan x \sec^{n-2}x + (n-2)I_{n-2}$.
  5. If a definite integral, evaluate the boundary term at the limits before quoting.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $I_n = \int \sec^n x\,dx$. IBP with $u=\sec^{n-2}x$, $dv=\sec^2 x\,dx$ and $\tan^2 x = \sec^2 x - 1$ gives $I_n = \sec^{n-2}x\tan x - (n-2)I_n + (n-2)I_{n-2}$. Add $(n-2)I_n$ to both sides: $(n-1)I_n = \sec^{n-2}x\tan x + (n-2)I_{n-2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Reduction formula for $\int \sin^n x\,dx$ or $\int \cos^n x\,dx$
After IBP and applying $\sin^2 + \cos^2 = 1$, $I_n$ reappears with coefficient $-(n-1)$; collecting yields $nI_n = -\sin^{n-1}x\cos x + (n-1)I_{n-2}$.
Reduction formula for $\int \tan^n x\,dx$
Splitting $\tan^n x = \tan^{n-2}x(\sec^2 x - 1)$ produces $I_n + I_{n-2}$ directly; the 'collect' step is replaced by isolating $I_n$ from a single equation, no IBP loop needed.
$\int e^{ax}\sin bx\,dx$ via two IBPs
The original integral reappears after two rounds of IBP with coefficient $-b^2/a^2$ (or similar); collect to solve $(1 + b^2/a^2)I = \ldots$ for $I$ algebraically.
Reduction formula for $\int x^n e^x\,dx$
Here $I_n$ does NOT reappear — the recurrence is direct, $I_n = x^n e^x - nI_{n-1}$, so no collecting step is required.

Common Misreadings

  • Thinking the reappearing $\int \sec^n x\,dx$ on the right is a 'different' integral because it sits inside a longer expression.
  • Believing the IBP has failed because the original integral came back, and abandoning the method.
  • With definite integrals, treating $I_n$ on the right (a number) as somehow distinct from $I_n$ on the left.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slip: writing $1 + (n-2)$ as $n-3$ or $n-1$ inconsistently — always verify the coefficient.
  • Sign error when moving $-(n-2)I_n$ across the equals sign.
  • Forgetting to evaluate the boundary term at limits in definite-integral versions before stating the recurrence.
  • Dividing by $n-1$ without noting the recurrence is invalid at $n=1$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope (FP2/FP3 reduction formulae). Standard surfaces are $\int \sec^n x$, $\int \sin^n x$, $\int \cos^n x$, $\int \tan^n x$, $\int x^n e^x$, and trig–exponential products.
