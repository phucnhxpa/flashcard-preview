Coordinate Geometry

Apply point-gradient form through the origin to get y = mx

Family: Line Equation Construction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when you have determined a gradient m and know the line passes through the origin (0,0). Typical cues: a problem says 'the line through O with gradient ...', or you have just computed a perpendicular/parallel gradient and the new line is anchored at the origin. Equivalent phrasings include 'line from the origin' or 'passes through O'. The two standard inputs for point-gradient form (a point and a gradient) are both present, with the point being the trivial case (0,0).

Motivation — Why this works

Point-gradient form $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ converts a (point, gradient) pair into a line equation in one substitution. When $(x_1, y_1) = (0,0)$, both shift terms vanish and the formula collapses to $y = mx$, so no separate computation of the y-intercept is needed. This is faster and less error-prone than substituting into $y = mx + c$ to solve for $c$. The underlying principle is that any line through the origin has zero y-intercept, so its equation is fully determined by its gradient.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the required line passes through the origin and a gradient $m$ has just been found.
  2. Rule out the slower route of using $y = mx + c$ and substituting $(0,0)$ to solve for $c$ — it gives $c=0$ trivially.
  3. Commit to point-gradient form because it is the standard formula for (point, gradient) data.
  4. Bridge: substitute $(x_1,y_1) = (0,0)$ into $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$.
  5. Tactical: simplify immediately to $y = mx$ rather than carrying the $-0$ terms; leave the gradient as a fraction unless asked for $ax+by+c=0$ form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the gradient $m$ and confirm the line passes through $(0,0)$.
  2. Substitute into $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$: $y - 0 = m(x - 0)$.
  3. Simplify to $y = mx$.
  4. If required, rearrange into the demanded form (e.g. $ax + by + c = 0$ with integer coefficients).

Worked Micro-Example

Line $l_1$ has gradient $\tfrac{3}{2}$. Find the equation of the line $l_2$ through the origin perpendicular to $l_1$. Perpendicular gradient: $m_2 = -\tfrac{2}{3}$. Through $(0,0)$: $y - 0 = -\tfrac{2}{3}(x - 0)$, giving $y = -\tfrac{2}{3}x$, or equivalently $2x + 3y = 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line passes through a non-origin point $(a,b)$ with known gradient $m$
Use the full form $y - b = m(x - a)$ and rearrange; the shift terms no longer vanish.
Two points given instead of (point, gradient)
First compute $m = (y_2 - y_1)/(x_2 - x_1)$, then apply point-gradient form with either point.
Answer required in form $ax + by + c = 0$ with integer coefficients
After writing $y = mx$, multiply through by the denominator of $m$ and move all terms to one side.
Line through origin parallel to a given line
Take the same gradient $m$ as the given line, then write $y = mx$ directly.

Common Misreadings

  • Using a different known point (e.g. the y-intercept of another line such as $(0,13)$) instead of the origin specified in the question.
  • Confusing 'through the origin' with 'has y-intercept equal to the original line's intercept'.
  • Reusing the original line's gradient when a perpendicular/parallel adjustment was required first.

Pitfalls

  • Swapping coordinates in the formula, e.g. writing $x - y_1 = m(y - x_1)$.
  • Sign errors when the gradient is negative, e.g. writing $y = \tfrac{2}{3}x$ instead of $y = -\tfrac{2}{3}x$.
  • Failing to clear fractions when the question demands integer-coefficient form.
  • Dropping the gradient's sign when moving from $y - 0 = m(x-0)$ to $y = mx$.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure 1 coordinate geometry: straight-line equations using point-gradient form, parallel and perpendicular gradients. No content outside the specification.
