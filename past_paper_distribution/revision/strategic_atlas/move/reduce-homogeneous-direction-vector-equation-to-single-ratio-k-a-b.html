Trigger — When to use

Fires when an unknown direction vector $(a,b,c)$ must satisfy a perpendicularity/coplanarity constraint (giving one linear relation among $a,b,c$) plus an angle condition $\cos^2\theta = (\mathbf{d}\cdot\mathbf{e})^2/(|\mathbf{d}|^2|\mathbf{e}|^2)$. After eliminating one component, the remaining equation is homogeneous of degree 0 in the two surviving parameters. Phrases like 'find a direction vector such that...' or 'the line makes angle $\theta$ with...' combined with one already-used linear constraint signal this move.

Motivation — Why this works

Direction vectors are defined only up to nonzero scalar multiple, so only the ratio $a:b$ carries geometric content. The squared-cosine angle equation has numerator and denominator both quadratic in $(a,b)$, so dividing by $b^2$ converts a two-unknown equation into a single-variable quadratic in $k = a/b$. This dimension reduction turns an apparently underdetermined system into a standard quadratic, which is the key to tractability.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice that after eliminating $c$, both $\mathbf{d}\cdot\mathbf{e}$ and $|\mathbf{e}|^2$ are homogeneous in $(a,b)$ — degrees 1 and 2 respectively, so the squared cosine equation has matching degree 2 on both sides.
  2. I rule out trying to pin down $a$ and $b$ individually; the equation is invariant under $(a,b)\mapsto(\lambda a,\lambda b)$, so no unique solution exists for the pair — only their ratio is determined.
  3. I commit to introducing $k = a/b$ because the homogeneity guarantees the equation collapses to a polynomial in $k$ alone.
  4. Concretely, I divide the squared angle equation by $b^2$ and read off a quadratic in $k$.
  5. After solving for $k$, I pick a convenient scaling (e.g. $b=1$) to write down a clean direction vector, and check whether $b=0$ separately yields a missed solution.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Use the linear constraint to eliminate one component, leaving direction vector in terms of two parameters $a,b$.
  2. Compute $\mathbf{d}\cdot\mathbf{e}$ (linear in $a,b$) and $|\mathbf{e}|^2$ (quadratic in $a,b$).
  3. Square the angle condition to get $(\mathbf{d}\cdot\mathbf{e})^2 |\mathbf{d}|^2 \cos^{-2}\theta\cdot\text{(rearrange)}$ — both sides quadratic in $(a,b)$.
  4. Divide through by $b^2$ and substitute $k = a/b$ to obtain a quadratic $Ak^2 + Bk + C = 0$.
  5. Solve for $k$; for each root set $b=1$ (or any convenient value) so that $a = k$, and back-substitute to recover the full direction vector.
  6. Check the case $b = 0$ separately, and verify each candidate direction is not a degenerate/spurious solution.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose $\mathbf{e} = (a,b,2a+4b)$, with $\cos^2\theta = 1/21$ against a fixed $\mathbf{d}$. Computation gives $\mathbf{d}\cdot\mathbf{e} = 18a+26b$ and $|\mathbf{e}|^2 = 5a^2+16ab+17b^2$. The equation $21(18a+26b)^2 = 1400(5a^2+16ab+17b^2)$ divided by $b^2$ becomes $21(18k+26)^2 = 1400(5k^2+16k+17)$, i.e. $196(k+7)^2 = 0$, so $k=-7$. Taking $b=1$ gives $\mathbf{e}=(-7,1,-10)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line through a fixed point making a specified angle with a given line, with one component free
Use the angle constraint directly; the same $k = a/b$ reduction applies once one component is fixed or eliminated.
Direction perpendicular to one vector and at a specified angle to another
Perpendicularity gives the linear constraint to eliminate $c$; angle condition then yields the quadratic in $k$.
Plane normal vector satisfying two angle/perpendicularity conditions
Same homogeneity logic — only the ratio of the two surviving components matters.
Line of intersection direction satisfying an extra angle constraint
After parametrising the family of valid directions by $(a,b)$, reduce via $k=a/b$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $a,b,c$ as three independent unknowns needing three equations, then declaring the system underdetermined.
  • Forgetting to square the cosine and instead working with $\cos\theta = (\mathbf{d}\cdot\mathbf{e})/(|\mathbf{d}||\mathbf{e}|)$, which introduces a square root and obstructs the homogeneity reduction.
  • Believing a unique $(a,b)$ should emerge, rather than a unique ratio.

Pitfalls

  • Dividing by $b^2$ silently assumes $b\neq 0$; the case $b=0$ must be checked separately.
  • If the quadratic in $k$ has two distinct roots, both directions are geometrically valid and may need to be reported or filtered against context (e.g. one may coincide with a given line).
  • Sign errors when squaring: $(\mathbf{d}\cdot\mathbf{e})^2$ loses sign information, so the recovered direction may need a sign flip for a chosen orientation.
  • Arithmetic slips when expanding $(18k+26)^2$ or similar — keep common factors visible to simplify (here, factor 196).

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only IAL Core/Further Pure vector tools: dot product, $|\mathbf{v}|^2 = \mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v}$, the angle formula $\cos\theta = (\mathbf{d}\cdot\mathbf{e})/(|\mathbf{d}||\mathbf{e}|)$, and solving a quadratic. No matrix or eigenvalue methods required.
