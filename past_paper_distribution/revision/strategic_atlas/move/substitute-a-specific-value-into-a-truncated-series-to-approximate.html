Sequences & Series

Substitute a specific value into a truncated series to approximate

Family: Series Evaluation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires on a 'hence' part following a Maclaurin/Taylor expansion, with phrasing like 'find an approximate value of f(0.1)', 'estimate ∫₀^{0.5} f(x) dx', 'show that f(a) ≈ …', or 'by choosing a suitable value of x, show that π ≈ …'. The earlier part has produced a polynomial expansion up to a stated power, and the later part asks for a numerical estimate. The word 'hence' or 'use your expansion' is the strongest cue.

Motivation — Why this works

A truncated Maclaurin/Taylor series is a polynomial, so evaluating it at a chosen x (or integrating it term-by-term and then evaluating) is just arithmetic. For x-values close to the expansion point, the remainder term is small, so the polynomial value is a faithful approximation of the true function value. This transforms an otherwise intractable evaluation (e.g. of a non-elementary integral, or a transcendental constant) into elementary computation, and the marks reward using the series rather than a calculator.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice 'hence find an approximation' or 'estimate' — this links directly to the polynomial expansion I produced in the previous part.
  2. I rule out evaluating the original function or using a calculator value, since marks are tied to the series method.
  3. I commit because the polynomial is short, x is small, so substitution will give an answer matching the requested precision.
  4. I identify what to substitute: a given x, an integration over a given interval (term-by-term integrate first), or a clever x I must select (e.g. x = 1/√3 in an arctan series for π).
  5. I decide how many decimal places to carry through intermediate terms so cumulative rounding does not corrupt the final answer.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the truncated series from the earlier part: f(x) ≈ c₀ + c₁x + c₂x² + c₃x³ + … up to the stated order.
  2. If approximating f(a): substitute x = a into the polynomial and evaluate each term separately.
  3. If approximating ∫_p^q f(x) dx: integrate term-by-term to get C₀x + (c₁/2)x² + (c₂/3)x³ + …, then evaluate between the limits.
  4. If x is not given: pick a value that makes the target constant appear (e.g. x = 1/2 in ln(1+x) for ln(3/2); x = 1/√3 in arctan x for π/6).
  5. Sum the numerical terms keeping at least one extra decimal place beyond the required accuracy.
  6. Round only at the final step and state the answer to the requested precision.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\ln(1+x) \approx x - \tfrac{x^2}{2} + \tfrac{x^3}{3} - \tfrac{x^4}{4}$, estimate $\ln(1.1)$. Substitute $x = 0.1$: $0.1 - 0.005 + 0.000333\ldots - 0.000025 = 0.09530833\ldots$, so $\ln(1.1) \approx 0.0953$ (4 d.p.), agreeing with the true value $0.09531$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Approximate a definite integral whose integrand has no elementary antiderivative (e.g. $\int_0^{0.5} e^{-x^2}dx$)
Integrate the Maclaurin polynomial term-by-term first, then substitute the limits; the constant of integration cancels.
Choose x to recover a transcendental constant (e.g. show $\pi \approx \ldots$ from $\arctan x$)
Pick x so the LHS becomes a known multiple of the constant, e.g. $x = 1/\sqrt{3}$ giving $\arctan(1/\sqrt{3}) = \pi/6$, then multiply through by 6.
Taylor series about $x = a$ rather than 0
Substitute the deviation $(x-a)$ into the polynomial, not x itself; e.g. for expansion about $a=1$, substituting $x=1.05$ means using $0.05$ in the series.
Approximation via a composed series, e.g. $f(x) = (1+x)\,e^x$ expanded by multiplying two series
Use the combined truncated polynomial obtained in the earlier part, not the individual factors evaluated separately.

Common Misreadings

  • Substituting the value into the original function on a calculator instead of into the truncated series — earns no method marks.
  • Mistaking 'hence' for an invitation to start over with a fresh method rather than reusing the series just derived.
  • When asked to approximate an integral, evaluating the polynomial at a single point instead of integrating term-by-term first.
  • Choosing the substitution x without checking it lies inside (or near) the interval of validity stated earlier.

Pitfalls

  • Rounding each term to too few decimal places, so cumulative error spoils the final figure.
  • Using x too large relative to the expansion point, giving an answer that does not match the printed target.
  • Forgetting to multiply through by the appropriate constant after solving for the target (e.g. multiplying by 6 when x = 1/√3 was used to obtain π/6).
  • Dropping a sign when the series is alternating, especially in the integrated form.
  • Quoting the answer to more figures than the series can justify given its truncation.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P4/FP2 territory: Maclaurin/Taylor expansions of $e^x, \ln(1+x), \sin x, \cos x, (1+x)^n, \arctan x$, and 'hence approximate' parts. No formal remainder-term bound is required — students just trust that small x and a stated number of terms give the requested accuracy.
