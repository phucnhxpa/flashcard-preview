Complete the square using the half-coefficient identity

Family: Completing The Square Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a monic quadratic $x^2 + kx + \ldots$ must be rewritten in vertex form $a + b(x+c)^2$ or $(x+c)^2 + d$. Typical cues: a question asking for the form $p(x+q)^2 + r$, requests for the maximum/minimum value of a quadratic, finding the line of symmetry or vertex, or solving a quadratic without the formula. Also triggers as a sub-step after factoring out a leading coefficient from a non-monic quadratic.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity $x^2 + kx \equiv (x + k/2)^2 - (k/2)^2$ converts a sum of two unlike terms in $x$ into a single squared term plus a constant. Once $x$ appears inside only one bracket, the vertex, range, and axis of symmetry can be read directly. This is also the algebraic engine behind the quadratic formula and is the standard route to range/turning-point information without calculus.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the bracket contains a monic quadratic in $x$ with no constant term (or a constant that needs absorbing) — the canonical completing-the-square setup.
  2. Rules out factorising (no obvious roots required) and the quadratic formula (the question wants a structural form, not roots).
  3. Commits to the identity $x^2 + kx = (x + k/2)^2 - (k/2)^2$ because the target form $a + b(x+c)^2$ literally names the output of completing the square.
  4. Reads off $k$, halves it to get $c = k/2$, and prepares to subtract $(k/2)^2$ to compensate for the constant introduced by squaring.
  5. Plans to substitute back into the outer expression and distribute any leading factor carefully, watching signs, before combining constants to land on $a + b(x+c)^2$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify $k$, the coefficient of $x$ inside the monic bracket; compute $k/2$.
  2. Rewrite $x^2 + kx = (x + k/2)^2 - (k/2)^2$.
  3. Substitute this back into the original expression, preserving any outer factor and outer constant.
  4. Distribute the outer factor across both the squared term and the compensating constant, tracking signs.
  5. Combine the loose constants to obtain the final form $a + b(x+c)^2$.
  6. Cross-check by reading off $a$, $b$, $c$ and matching the requested form exactly.

Worked Micro-Example

Express $f(x) = -2x^2 - 4x + 11$ in the form $a + b(x+c)^2$.\\ Factor the leading coefficient from the $x$-terms: $f(x) = -2(x^2 + 2x) + 11$.\\ Inside, $k = 2$, so $x^2 + 2x = (x+1)^2 - 1$.\\ Substitute: $f(x) = -2[(x+1)^2 - 1] + 11 = -2(x+1)^2 + 2 + 11$.\\ Therefore $f(x) = 13 - 2(x+1)^2$, giving $a=13,\ b=-2,\ c=1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Odd coefficient of $x$, e.g. $x^2 + 5x$
Half is a fraction: $(x + 5/2)^2 - 25/4$. Same identity, fractional arithmetic.
Non-monic quadratic, e.g. $3x^2 + 12x + 7$
First factor the leading coefficient from the $x$-terms: $3(x^2 + 4x) + 7$, then complete the square inside the bracket.
Negative leading coefficient (maximum problems)
Factor out the negative; the sign flips when distributing back, turning the compensating $-(k/2)^2$ into $+b(k/2)^2$ in the constant.
Solving $x^2 + kx + m = 0$
Complete the square then take square roots of both sides — this derives the quadratic formula.
Finding range of a quadratic function
After completing the square, the constant $a$ is the min (if $b>0$) or max (if $b<0$); state range as $f(x) \geq a$ or $f(x) \leq a$.
Circle equation $x^2 + y^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$
Complete the square separately in $x$ and in $y$ to recover centre–radius form $(x+D/2)^2 + (y+E/2)^2 = r^2$.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to complete the square on a non-monic quadratic without first factoring out the leading coefficient.
  • Treating $a + b(x+c)^2$ as identical to $b(x+c)^2 + a$ for purposes of identifying $c$, but then getting confused when the bracket is written as $(x-c)$ vs $(x+c)$.
  • Reading the question as asking for roots and pivoting to the formula, missing that the target is a structural rewrite.
  • Confusing completing the square with factorising — attempting to find two linear factors instead of one squared bracket.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to subtract $(k/2)^2$: writing $x^2 + 2x = (x+1)^2$ instead of $(x+1)^2 - 1$.
  • Sign error when distributing the outer factor: e.g. $-2 \times (-1) = +2$, not $-2$.
  • Mismatching the target form: producing $13 - 2(x+1)^2$ but quoting $c = -1$ because the student confuses $(x+c)$ with $(x-c)$.
  • Halving incorrectly when $k$ is a fraction or surd; leaving $(k/2)^2$ unsimplified.
  • Dropping the outer constant (here $+11$) while focusing on the bracket, then forgetting to add it back.
