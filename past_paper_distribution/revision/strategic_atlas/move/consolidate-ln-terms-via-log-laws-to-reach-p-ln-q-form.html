Algebra & Functions

Consolidate ln terms via log laws to reach p + ln q form

Family: Log Law Simplification Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the question explicitly demands an answer in a structured form such as 'p + ln q', 'a + b ln 2 + c ln 3', or 'ln(single fraction)', but the working has produced a messy linear combination of logarithms — multiple ln(...) terms with integer (or scalar) coefficients, plus and minus signs, often arising from substituting bounds into a definite integral or from partial-fraction integration. Visual cues: several separate ln(...) summands with coefficients like 9 ln 6 − 9 ln 4 + 17 ln 7 − 17 ln 5, or instructions like 'show that ... = p + ln q where p, q ∈ ℤ'. Equivalently, you have an expression that is mathematically correct but not yet in the prescribed display form.

Motivation — Why this works

Three logarithm laws — k·ln A = ln(A^k), ln A + ln B = ln(AB), and ln A − ln B = ln(A/B) — together let any linear combination of logarithms be collapsed into a single ln of one algebraic expression. This is the only route from a multi-term log expression to the single-ln form demanded by the question, and it is the standard 'final tidy' step that earns the presentation marks. Non-logarithmic constants are gathered separately into the integer/rational p, leaving a clean p + ln q output.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the answer is mathematically complete but cosmetically wrong: the question prescribes p + ln q (or similar) and I currently have an integer plus several ln terms with coefficients.
  2. Rule out alternative techniques — there is no clever substitution or identity that bypasses this; log laws are the only mechanism for fusing separate ln terms.
  3. Commit to a clean order of operations: first separate the non-log constants from the log terms (these will form p), then process the log terms.
  4. Inside the log terms, deal with scalar coefficients FIRST via the power rule k·ln A = ln(A^k), so every term becomes a plain ln(...).
  5. Tactical choice: pair terms that share a coefficient before applying the power rule (e.g. 9 ln 6 − 9 ln 4 = 9 ln(6/4) = 9 ln(3/2)) — this keeps the bases inside the logarithm small and avoids huge intermediate numbers like 6^9.
  6. Combine the resulting single logarithms using ln A + ln B = ln(AB) and ln A − ln B = ln(A/B), being careful with chained subtractions.
  7. Finally collect the constants to give p, and read off q from the consolidated logarithm.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Separate constant terms from log terms; sum the constants to form p.
  2. Where two log terms share a coefficient k, factor first: k ln a ± k ln b = k ln(a/b) or k ln(ab).
  3. Apply the power rule on any remaining scalar multiple: k ln A → ln(A^k).
  4. Combine all resulting ln(...) terms using ln A + ln B = ln(AB) and ln A − ln B = ln(A/B), tracking signs carefully.
  5. Write the final result as p + ln q (or the requested form), simplifying the fraction inside ln where possible.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose evaluation gives $-4 + 9\ln 6 - 9\ln 4 + 17\ln 7 - 17\ln 5$. Pair: $9\ln(6/4) + 17\ln(7/5) = 9\ln(3/2) + 17\ln(7/5)$. Power rule: $\ln((3/2)^9) + \ln((7/5)^{17})$. Combine: $\ln\!\left(\dfrac{3^9 \cdot 7^{17}}{2^9 \cdot 5^{17}}\right)$. Final: $-4 + \ln\!\left(\dfrac{3^9 \cdot 7^{17}}{2^9 \cdot 5^{17}}\right)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Question asks for the form a + b ln 2 + c ln 3 (selective separation)
Do NOT fully combine; instead decompose each ln into ln of primes (e.g. ln 6 = ln 2 + ln 3, ln 8 = 3 ln 2) and collect coefficients of each ln-of-prime.
Bounds give logs of the same base (e.g. ln 8, ln 4, ln 2)
Decompose first as multiples of ln 2, then sum coefficients — often gives a single integer multiple of ln 2.
Other bases such as log₁₀ or log₂ from Further Pure
Identical laws apply — power, product and quotient rules are base-independent.
Partial-fraction integration yielding A ln|x−a| + B ln|x−b| evaluated at bounds
Same procedure; the modulus signs disappear once the numerical arguments are positive.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating ln(A+B) as ln A + ln B — confusing sum-inside-log with sum-of-logs.
  • Reading 'p + ln q' as permitting multiple ln terms, and stopping before consolidation.
  • Misidentifying the trigger as a need for change of base, when only standard log laws are required.

Pitfalls

  • Power-rule misuse: writing k ln(a/b) = ln(a^k / b) — the exponent must apply to the entire argument, giving ln(a^k / b^k).
  • Sign errors with chained subtractions: ln A − ln B − ln C = ln(A/(BC)), NOT ln(A/B − C) or ln(A/B)/C.
  • Arithmetic slips when simplifying fractions inside logs (e.g. writing 6/4 = 2/3 instead of 3/2).
  • Forgetting to combine the non-log constants into a single p, leaving the answer as '−25 + 21 + ln(...)'.
  • Applying the power rule before pairing, producing unwieldy numbers like 6^9 that are hard to simplify.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 (logarithms and integration leading to ln). The three log laws are in the formula booklet under Logarithms. Avoid complex logarithms or change-of-base manipulations beyond log_a x = ln x / ln a, which are not needed for this move.
