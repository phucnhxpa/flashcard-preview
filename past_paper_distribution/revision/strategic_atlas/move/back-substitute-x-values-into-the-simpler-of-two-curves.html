Back-substitute x-values into the simpler of two curves

Family: Back Substitution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

After solving an intersection problem for x-values, y-coordinates are still required. Two source equations are available (e.g. one involving a modulus, surd, trig, or piecewise rule, and one a clean polynomial or rational expression). The cue is recognising that one equation is structurally simpler — no case split, no sign ambiguity, no domain restriction at the relevant x.

Motivation — Why this works

Both curves pass through every intersection point, so either equation yields the same y. Choosing the simpler equation minimises arithmetic and removes the need to resolve modulus signs, surd branches, or piecewise cases — each of which is a common source of error. This is a pure efficiency-and-accuracy choice, not a mathematical necessity.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that x-values are now known and y is still needed; either curve equation will work.
  2. Rule out the equation containing a modulus (or surd, or piecewise definition) because it forces a sign/case decision per x-value.
  3. Commit to the polynomial/rational equation because it is single-valued and direct.
  4. Bridge: substitute each x-value into that simpler expression and evaluate.
  5. Tactical: keep fractions exact, simplify before subtracting, and pair each y back to its x for the final coordinate.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify both curve equations and select the one without modulus/surd/piecewise structure.
  2. Substitute the first x-value into the chosen equation and simplify to obtain y.
  3. Repeat for the second x-value.
  4. Pair each y with its x and state the intersection points as ordered pairs.
  5. (Optional check) Verify one point in the other equation to catch arithmetic slips.

Worked Micro-Example

Curves $C_1: y = |x-6|-6$ and $C_2: y = \tfrac{1}{9}x^2 - 9$ intersect at $x=9$ and $x=3$. Use $C_2$: at $x=9$, $y = \tfrac{81}{9}-9 = 0$; at $x=3$, $y = \tfrac{9}{9}-9 = -8$. Intersection points: $(9,0)$ and $(3,-8)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line meets a circle $x^2+y^2=r^2$
Back-substitute x-values into the linear equation rather than the circle, avoiding a $\pm$ square root.
Curve $y=f(x)$ meets a curve given implicitly
Substitute into the explicit $y=f(x)$ form to read y directly.
Parametric intersections
Once parameter values are known, evaluate the simpler of the two parametric component formulas.
Modulus equation $|f(x)|=g(x)$
After solving, substitute into $g(x)$ to bypass the modulus case split.
Logarithm/exponential pair
Substitute into the polynomial side rather than the $\ln$ or $e^x$ side to avoid transcendental evaluation.

Common Misreadings

  • Believing both equations must be checked to confirm y, doubling the work.
  • Thinking the modulus equation is 'more authoritative' because it was the original problem statement.
  • Assuming the simpler equation might miss a y-value, when in fact intersection points satisfy both.

Pitfalls

  • Substituting into the modulus curve and choosing the wrong sign branch, producing an incorrect y.
  • Arithmetic slip when squaring or dividing, e.g. computing $81/9$ as $8$ instead of $9$.
  • Pairing y-values with the wrong x when listing the final coordinates.
  • Forgetting to state final answers as coordinate pairs after computing y.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard technique across Edexcel IAL Pure (P1–P4) intersection questions involving modulus, circles, polynomials, and parametric curves; no content beyond syllabus is required.
