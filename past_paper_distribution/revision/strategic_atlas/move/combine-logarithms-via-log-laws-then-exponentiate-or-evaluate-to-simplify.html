Integration

Combine logarithms via log laws then exponentiate or evaluate to simplify

Family: Log Law Then Exponentiate Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

After integrating (typically a partial-fraction decomposition or a rational integrand), the result contains two or more separate logarithm terms — e.g. a ln|f(x)| ± b ln|g(x)| — that need to be either (i) rearranged to make a variable explicit (separable ODE context, ending with ln|...| = expression in t) or (ii) evaluated between definite bounds to produce a clean exact form like ln k. Visual cues: a difference/sum of logs equalling a linear expression in t plus C; an antiderivative of the form A ln|...| − B ln|...| about to be evaluated at two limits; a 'show that ... = ln k' instruction. Also fires when an implicit solution must be made explicit by removing the logarithm.

Motivation — Why this works

Two separate logarithms are algebraically inert — you cannot solve for the variable inside them, and evaluating them separately at bounds doubles the substitutions and the chance of sign errors. The log laws ln a − ln b = ln(a/b), ln a + ln b = ln(ab), and k ln a = ln(a^k) consolidate them into a SINGLE logarithm of a rational expression. From there, exponentiating (using e^{ln X} = X) strips the log entirely, yielding an algebraic/exponential equation suitable for solving; alternatively, a single combined log is far cleaner to evaluate at the bounds of a definite integral. This is the standard bridge from integrated-log form to either an explicit closed-form solution or a tidy exact value.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the antiderivative or integrated equation contains two or more log terms with different arguments — these block any further algebraic progress on their own.
  2. Rule out trying to substitute or rearrange the logs separately: that either leaves an implicit equation (in the ODE case) or forces six substitutions instead of two (in the definite-integral case).
  3. Commit to log-law consolidation because the coefficients in front of each log can be absorbed via k ln a = ln(a^k), and the ± between logs becomes ×/÷ inside a single log.
  4. Bridge step: first pull every coefficient inside as a power, then use ln a ± ln b = ln(a·b) or ln(a/b) to fuse into one logarithm of a rational expression.
  5. Tactical decision: if the goal is an explicit y(t), clear any leading fraction (e.g. multiply both sides by 8) BEFORE exponentiating, so the right-hand side is a clean exponential rather than a fractional power; if the goal is a definite-integral value, evaluate the combined log at each bound and use ln(p) − ln(q) = ln(p/q) one final time.
  6. Absorb constants: when exponentiating, e^{C} (a positive constant) and the ± from removing |·| merge into one arbitrary constant A = ±e^C, whose sign is fixed later from initial conditions.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Pull coefficients inside the logs: a ln|f| = ln|f|^a.
  2. Apply ln A ± ln B = ln(AB) or ln(A/B) to obtain a single log: ln|f^a / g^b| = RHS.
  3. If solving an ODE: clear any leading fractional coefficient by multiplying both sides through, then exponentiate to get |f^a/g^b| = A e^{kt}, with A = ±e^C.
  4. If evaluating a definite integral: substitute the upper and lower bounds into the single combined log, then use ln p − ln q = ln(p/q) once more to get a single exact ln value.
  5. Rearrange algebraically (cross-multiply, isolate the dependent variable) to obtain the explicit form, or simplify the final exact answer.

Worked Micro-Example

After integrating a separable ODE: $\tfrac{1}{8}\ln|H-5| - \tfrac{1}{8}\ln|H+3| = -\tfrac{t}{40} + C$. Combine: $\tfrac{1}{8}\ln\left|\tfrac{H-5}{H+3}\right| = -\tfrac{t}{40} + C$. Multiply by 8: $\ln\left|\tfrac{H-5}{H+3}\right| = -\tfrac{t}{5} + K$. Exponentiate: $\tfrac{H-5}{H+3} = A e^{-t/5}$, then solve for $H$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Separable ODE with logistic-type partial fractions, e.g. integrating 1/(P(K−P))
Same combine-then-exponentiate path; the resulting algebraic equation (P/(K−P)) = A e^{kt} is then rearranged for P explicitly.
Definite integral with three or more log terms, e.g. ln|x−1| + ln|x+1| − 2 ln|x|
Combine all into ln[(x²−1)/x²] before substituting bounds; the consolidation principle is identical, just more terms.
'Show that the integral equals ln k' style questions
After consolidating to a single log, evaluating at the bounds will reveal k directly via ln(p/q) = ln k.
Integrated form involves coefficients like 2 ln|x−1| − ln(x²+1)
Use 2 ln|x−1| = ln(x−1)² first, then combine to ln[(x−1)²/(x²+1)]; note (x−1)² is automatically non-negative so the absolute value is unnecessary.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating ln|f| − ln|g| as ln|f − g| (a frequent log-law confusion) — this destroys the calculation.
  • Trying to exponentiate term-by-term while the logs are still separate, producing meaningless e^{ln|f|} − e^{ln|g|} expressions.
  • Believing the absolute value bars must be carried symbolically forever rather than absorbed into the constant of integration.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to clear the leading fractional coefficient (e.g. the 1/8) before exponentiating, leading to messy fractional powers like ((H−5)/(H+3))^{1/8}.
  • Losing the ± when removing absolute values — the sign of A must be determined from the initial condition, not assumed positive.
  • Arithmetic slips when evaluating the combined fraction inside the log at each bound (e.g. at x=3, computing x²+1 as 9 instead of 10).
  • Combining ln terms whose coefficients haven't been folded inside as powers first — e.g. writing 2 ln|x−1| − ln(x²+1) = ln|(x−1)/(x²+1)|² incorrectly.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 (integration of rational functions via partial fractions) and FP-level separable first-order ODEs. The log laws used are standard from C2/P2. Avoid invoking complex logarithms or branches — the absolute-value-with-±-constant treatment is sufficient at IAL.
