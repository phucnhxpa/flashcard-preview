Sequences & Series

Select Maclaurin or Taylor template based on expansion point

Family: Formula Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question asks for a power-series expansion of a function and you must decide which template to instantiate. Cues include 'expand f(x) in ascending powers of x' (Maclaurin), 'expand about x = a' or 'in ascending powers of (x − a)' (Taylor), or 'use Taylor's series to approximate f(b) near x = a'. The decision must be made before any differentiation, because it fixes the point at which every derivative is evaluated. It also applies when a question mixes wording, e.g. 'find a series for f(x) near x = π/4' — this is Taylor about π/4, not Maclaurin.

Motivation — Why this works

Maclaurin's series is just Taylor's series with a = 0, so the only structural difference is whether powers are of x or (x − a) and whether derivatives are evaluated at 0 or at a. Choosing correctly ensures the series matches the local behaviour of f near the requested point and converges in the intended neighbourhood. An expansion about the wrong point will still be a valid series for the function (where it converges) but will not match the form the marker expects and will give wrong numerical approximations near a. Locking in the template up front prevents the very common error of computing f'(a), f''(a), … and then writing them next to powers of x.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the phrase that pins down the expansion point: 'in ascending powers of x' vs 'in ascending powers of (x − a)' vs 'about x = a'.
  2. Rule out Maclaurin the moment a non-zero centre appears, even if the variable is still called x; rule out Taylor-about-a if the question only mentions powers of x with no shift.
  3. Commit to one template — Maclaurin f(x) = Σ f⁽ⁿ⁾(0) xⁿ/n! or Taylor f(x) = Σ f⁽ⁿ⁾(a)(x−a)ⁿ/n! — and write it down before differentiating.
  4. Bridge: list the derivatives you will need (up to the requested order) and label each with the evaluation point a (or 0).
  5. Tactically decide whether to compute derivatives by repeated differentiation or to leverage a known standard series (e.g. eˣ, sin x, ln(1+x)) and shift it; either way the chosen template fixes the form of the answer.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the expansion point a from the wording (a = 0 for Maclaurin, a ≠ 0 for Taylor).
  2. Write the template explicitly: f(x) ≈ f(a) + f'(a)(x−a) + f''(a)(x−a)²/2! + … up to the required order.
  3. Compute f(a), f'(a), f''(a), … by differentiating f and substituting x = a (not 0).
  4. Substitute these values into the template, keeping the powers of (x − a) intact.
  5. If asked to approximate f(b), substitute x = b at the end so (x − a) becomes (b − a).

Worked Micro-Example

Expand $f(x)=\ln x$ in ascending powers of $(x-1)$ up to the term in $(x-1)^3$. Centre $a=1$, so use Taylor. $f(1)=0$, $f'(x)=1/x\Rightarrow f'(1)=1$, $f''(x)=-1/x^2\Rightarrow f''(1)=-1$, $f'''(x)=2/x^3\Rightarrow f'''(1)=2$. Hence $\ln x \approx (x-1)-\tfrac{1}{2}(x-1)^2+\tfrac{1}{3}(x-1)^3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Question says 'in ascending powers of x' for f(x) = e^x sin x
Use Maclaurin: evaluate all derivatives at 0, write series in powers of x.
Question gives Taylor expansion about x = a and asks for an approximation to f(b)
Same Taylor template; after building the series, substitute x = b so (x − a) becomes the numerical (b − a).
Expansion of f(x) = sin x about x = π/4
Taylor with a = π/4; derivatives cycle through ±sin, ±cos evaluated at π/4, giving factors of √2/2.
Series for f(a + h) in powers of h
Equivalent to Taylor about a with the substitution h = x − a; template becomes f(a+h) = Σ f⁽ⁿ⁾(a) hⁿ/n!.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'expand about x = π/4' as Maclaurin and evaluating derivatives at 0.
  • Reading 'in ascending powers of (x − 1)' as a Maclaurin expansion in powers of x.
  • Assuming any series expansion of f(x) is automatically a Maclaurin series.
  • Confusing f(a + h) form with a Maclaurin in h without recognising the implicit centre at a.

Pitfalls

  • Writing the Taylor template with (x − a) but evaluating f⁽ⁿ⁾ at 0 instead of a.
  • Forgetting to divide by n! on each term.
  • Stopping one term short of the requested order, or including an extra term beyond it.
  • Mixing up the sign in (x − a) when a is negative, e.g. expanding about x = −2 and writing (x − 2).

A-level Scope Notes

Both Maclaurin (Core Pure / FP) and Taylor expansions about x = a are within Edexcel IAL Further Maths; convergence is treated informally and series are typically truncated at a stated order.
