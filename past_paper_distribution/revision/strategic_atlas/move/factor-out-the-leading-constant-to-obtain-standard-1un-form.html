Binomial Expansion

Factor out the leading constant to obtain standard (1+u)^n form

Family: Constant Extraction Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The expression has the form $(a + bx)^n$ or $(a - bx)^n$ where $a \neq 1$ and the index $n$ is fractional or negative (e.g. $(8-kx)^{1/2}$, $(4-2x)^{-1}$, $(9+3x)^{1/2}$). The generalised binomial series is only stated for $(1+u)^n$, so any non-unit constant inside the bracket is a visual cue that pre-processing is required. Equivalently, whenever you see a binomial expansion question with a leading number other than 1 inside the bracket and a non-positive-integer exponent, this move fires before any expansion can begin.

Motivation — Why this works

The generalised binomial expansion $(1+u)^n = 1 + nu + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}u^2 + \cdots$ is only valid when the bracket starts with 1. Factoring out the constant $a$ from $(a \pm bx)^n$ yields $a^n\left(1 \pm \frac{bx}{a}\right)^n$, which separates a clean scalar multiplier $a^n$ from a bracket in the required $(1+u)^n$ shape. This is a direct application of the index law $(ab)^n = a^n b^n$ and is the only reliable way to handle fractional/negative powers of two-term brackets. As a bonus, the factored form $u = \pm\frac{bx}{a}$ immediately determines the convergence range $|u| < 1 \Leftrightarrow |x| < \frac{a}{|b|}$.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the bracket is $(a \pm bx)^n$ with $a \neq 1$ and $n$ fractional or negative — the standard $(1+u)^n$ template does not apply directly.
  2. I rule out expanding $(a \pm bx)^n$ as a two-term binomial with general term $\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}(\pm bx)^r$: that formula is intended for positive integer $n$, and even when it can be made to work it is messier and prone to errors with fractional powers.
  3. I commit to factoring out $a$ because the index law $(ab)^n = a^n b^n$ lets me cleanly separate a scalar from a $(1+u)^n$-shaped bracket, which is exactly what the formula booklet entry requires.
  4. Bridge to operation: I write $a \pm bx = a\left(1 \pm \frac{bx}{a}\right)$, then raise both sides to the power $n$ to get $a^n\left(1 \pm \frac{bx}{a}\right)^n$.
  5. Tactical decision: I evaluate $a^n$ as a clean number now (e.g. $8^{1/2} = 2\sqrt{2}$, $4^{-1} = \tfrac{1}{4}$, $9^{1/2} = 3$) and park it as a multiplier outside, so the expansion proceeds purely on the $(1+u)^n$ part.
  6. I also read off $u = \pm\frac{bx}{a}$ to record the validity condition $|x| < \frac{a}{|b|}$ for later.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rewrite the bracket: $(a \pm bx)^n = \left[a\left(1 \pm \frac{bx}{a}\right)\right]^n$.
  2. Apply the index law: $= a^n\left(1 \pm \frac{bx}{a}\right)^n$.
  3. Evaluate the scalar $a^n$ to a tidy constant (e.g. $2\sqrt{2}$, $\tfrac{1}{4}$, $3$) and place it outside.
  4. Identify $u = \pm\frac{bx}{a}$ and the index $n$ for substitution into $(1+u)^n = 1 + nu + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}u^2 + \cdots$.
  5. Record the validity range $\left|\frac{bx}{a}\right| < 1$, i.e. $|x| < \frac{a}{|b|}$.

Worked Micro-Example

Expand $(8-kx)^{1/2}$ in ascending powers of $x$. Factor: $(8-kx)^{1/2} = 8^{1/2}\left(1-\tfrac{kx}{8}\right)^{1/2} = 2\sqrt{2}\left(1-\tfrac{kx}{8}\right)^{1/2}$. With $u = -\tfrac{kx}{8}$, $n = \tfrac{1}{2}$: $\left(1-\tfrac{kx}{8}\right)^{1/2} \approx 1 - \tfrac{kx}{16} - \tfrac{k^2x^2}{512} - \cdots$, so $(8-kx)^{1/2} \approx 2\sqrt{2}\left(1 - \tfrac{kx}{16} - \tfrac{k^2x^2}{512}\right)$, valid for $|x| < \tfrac{8}{|k|}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cube root form, e.g. $(27 + 6x)^{1/3}$
Factor out $27$: $27^{1/3}\left(1+\tfrac{2x}{9}\right)^{1/3} = 3\left(1+\tfrac{2x}{9}\right)^{1/3}$.
Negative integer index, e.g. $(2+x)^{-3}$
Factor out $2$: $2^{-3}\left(1+\tfrac{x}{2}\right)^{-3} = \tfrac{1}{8}\left(1+\tfrac{x}{2}\right)^{-3}$.
Reciprocal form, e.g. $(4-x)^{-1}$
Factor out $4$: $4^{-1}\left(1-\tfrac{x}{4}\right)^{-1} = \tfrac{1}{4}\left(1-\tfrac{x}{4}\right)^{-1}$.
Inverse square root with negative bx, e.g. $(4-2x)^{-1/2}$
Factor out $4$: $4^{-1/2}\left(1-\tfrac{x}{2}\right)^{-1/2} = \tfrac{1}{2}\left(1-\tfrac{x}{2}\right)^{-1/2}$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the leading constant as if it were 1 and expanding $(8-kx)^{1/2}$ as $1 + \tfrac{1}{2}(-kx) + \cdots$ — produces completely wrong coefficients.
  • Factoring out the $x$-dependent term (e.g. pulling out $-kx$) instead of the constant — the resulting bracket no longer starts with 1.
  • Factoring out a non-tidy divisor (e.g. pulling $2$ out of $(4-2x)$ to get $2(2-x)$) — leaves a constant $\neq 1$ inside, requiring a second factoring.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to apply the exponent $n$ to the extracted constant: writing $a\left(1\pm\tfrac{bx}{a}\right)^n$ instead of $a^n\left(1\pm\tfrac{bx}{a}\right)^n$.
  • Sign error when transferring the $\pm$: writing $\left(1+\tfrac{bx}{a}\right)^n$ from $(a-bx)^n$ instead of $\left(1-\tfrac{bx}{a}\right)^n$.
  • Mis-evaluating $a^n$ for fractional/negative $n$ (e.g. writing $8^{1/2} = 4$, or $4^{-1/2} = -2$).
  • Stating the validity range from $a$ alone (e.g. $|x|<4$) rather than from $\left|\tfrac{bx}{a}\right|<1$ (which gives $|x|<\tfrac{a}{|b|}$).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 binomial expansion scope. Invokes the formula booklet entry $(1+x)^n = 1 + nx + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}x^2 + \cdots$ for $|x|<1$, valid for any real $n$. Avoid generalising to multinomial $(a+b+c)^n$ expansions or to complex-valued bases — these are out of scope.
