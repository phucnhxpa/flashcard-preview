Trigger — When to use

The question gives an expansion equal to a power series with numerical coefficients (e.g. $1 - 4x + px^2 + \ldots$ or $1 + \tfrac{1}{3}x + mx^2 + \ldots$) where one or more coefficients contain unknown letters (such as $k$, $a$, $n$, $p$, $m$). Equivalently, the expansion of $(1+ax)^n$, $(1+kx)^n$, or a product like $(2+kx)\cdot\text{(series)}$ is presented alongside given numerical first few terms. The visual cue is: a polynomial/series identity with letters in some coefficients on one side and explicit numbers on the other side. As many independent coefficient equations are needed as there are unknowns (typically two: match $x^1$ and $x^2$).

Motivation — Why this works

Two power series (or polynomials) are identically equal iff their coefficients of every power of $x$ agree — this is the uniqueness-of-power-series principle. It converts a single series identity into a system of scalar equations, one per power of $x$, which is far more tractable than substituting specific $x$-values (which would mix all coefficients into one equation). Crucially, the equations are naturally hierarchical: the $x^1$ coefficient typically involves only one unknown, the $x^2$ coefficient introduces the next, and so on. This permits sequential solving rather than tackling a coupled system simultaneously.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structural feature: an expansion involving unknown letters is set equal to a series with explicit numerical coefficients $1 + c_1 x + c_2 x^2 + \ldots$ — this is the signature trigger for coefficient comparison.
  2. Rule out alternatives: substituting a specific value of $x$ would entangle all coefficients into one equation; dividing one series by another is messy and error-prone. Direct coefficient matching is cleanest.
  3. Count unknowns and plan: $u$ unknowns require $u$ independent equations, so identify which powers of $x$ to match — usually the lowest non-trivial powers ($x^1$, $x^2$, possibly $x^3$).
  4. Check the constant term first as a sanity check (it should match automatically) — this confirms the expansion is set up correctly but yields no information.
  5. Match $x^1$ coefficient: this typically gives a single linear equation in one unknown — solve immediately to fix that unknown.
  6. Match $x^2$ coefficient: substitute the just-found value, isolating the next unknown. Continue sequentially for higher powers if needed.
  7. When products of polynomials and series are involved (e.g. $(2+kx)\cdot\text{series}$), remember the $x^r$ coefficient receives contributions from MULTIPLE products — list them all systematically.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write out the general expansion symbolically up to the required power, e.g. $(1+ax)^n = 1 + n(ax) + \tfrac{n(n-1)}{2}(ax)^2 + \tfrac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{6}(ax)^3 + \ldots$
  2. Verify the constant terms agree on both sides (consistency check).
  3. Equate the coefficient of $x^1$: form Equation (1) involving the first unknown.
  4. Solve Equation (1) for that unknown.
  5. Equate the coefficient of $x^2$: form Equation (2). For products, sum ALL contributions (e.g. $2\cdot[x^2\text{-term}] + k\cdot[x^1\text{-term}]$).
  6. Substitute the known value into Equation (2) and solve for the next unknown.
  7. If a product expansion, double-check no cross-term contribution is missed.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $(1+kx)^{16} = 1 - 4x + px^2 + \ldots$. The general expansion is $1 + 16(kx) + \binom{16}{2}(kx)^2 + \ldots = 1 + 16k\,x + 120k^2 x^2 + \ldots$. Match $x^1$: $16k = -4 \Rightarrow k = -\tfrac{1}{4}$. Match $x^2$: $p = 120k^2 = 120 \cdot \tfrac{1}{16} = \tfrac{15}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two unknowns inside $(1+ax)^n$ (both $a$ and the index $n$ unknown)
Form simultaneous equations $na = c_1$ and $\tfrac{n(n-1)}{2}a^2 = c_2$; divide or substitute to eliminate $a$, then solve for $n$.
Series multiplied by a linear polynomial, e.g. $(2+kx)(1 + \alpha x + \beta x^2 + \ldots)$
The $x^r$ coefficient on the LHS becomes $2\cdot[\text{coeff of }x^r] + k\cdot[\text{coeff of }x^{r-1}]$ — list both contributions.
Partial fractions: $\tfrac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \tfrac{A}{\ldots} + \tfrac{B}{\ldots}$
Multiply through by the common denominator, then equate coefficients of like powers of $x$ — same logic, different context.
Maclaurin/Taylor series identification
Match coefficient of $x^r$ on each side directly; the underlying uniqueness principle is identical.

Common Misreadings

  • Forgetting that in $(1+ax)^n$ the binomial variable is the WHOLE quantity $ax$, so the $x^2$ coefficient carries an $a^2$, not just $a$.
  • Treating the constant term as an equation to solve (it gives no information — it's a consistency check).
  • Trying to divide one series by another instead of multiplying out — much harder and error-prone.
  • Sign errors: when the matched coefficient is negative (e.g. $na = -6$), losing the sign mid-calculation.

Pitfalls

  • In a product like $(2+kx)\cdot\text{series}$, the $x^2$ coefficient receives TWO contributions ($2\cdot[\text{series } x^2] + k\cdot[\text{series } x^1]$); missing one is the most frequent error.
  • Sign confusion: $k^2$ is positive even when $k$ is negative — don't carry a spurious minus into $p = 120k^2$.
  • Arithmetic slips with binomial coefficients: $\binom{16}{2} = \tfrac{16\cdot 15}{2} = 120$, not $\tfrac{16\cdot 15}{2!}$ misapplied.
  • Fraction arithmetic with awkward denominators (e.g. LCD of 128 and 192 is 384) — work carefully step-by-step.
  • Forgetting to use any range/sign constraint stated in the question (e.g. '$n$ is a negative integer') as a sanity check on the solution.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 Binomial Expansion (formula booklet entry: $(1+x)^n = 1 + nx + \tfrac{n(n-1)}{2!}x^2 + \ldots$, $|x|<1$). Do not generalise to formal power series convergence theorems or to multivariate series — those are out of scope.
