Algebra & Functions

Factor strategically to match the printed target form in 'show that' questions

Family: Reverse Engineering Target Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever the question is phrased 'show that ... = [specific form]' and your correct working has produced an algebraically equivalent but visually different expression. Look for cues like: two terms sharing a common surd/factor, a target written as a single product, a target containing a bracket like (x−2) or (4−h) where your numerator is an unfactored linear expression, or a target with a particular denominator (e.g. 320) that yours doesn't yet have. Equivalent phrasings: 'reverse-engineer from the answer', 'let the target dictate the factoring', 'finish the show-that by re-shaping'.

Motivation — Why this works

In 'show that' questions the printed form is non-negotiable — full marks require the exact stated structure, not a rearrangement of your own. Reading the target tells you precisely what to factor out, what to convert (decimals→fractions), and what bracket should remain. Mathematically nothing new happens (the expressions are identical), but algebraically you exploit the distributive law in reverse, guided by the target as a template. This is the standard closing move for proof-style integration, differentiation, and simplification problems.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that my correct expression and the printed target are equivalent but not identical in form — e.g. I have (2/3)(x+1)√(x+1) − 2√(x+1) but the target shows (2/3)(x−2)√(x+1).
  2. Rule out further integration/differentiation tricks — the calculus is done; this is purely algebraic re-shaping.
  3. Commit to reverse-engineering: read the target and ask 'what must I factor out so the leftover bracket matches?' Here √(x+1) is common to both terms, and the target has a single √(x+1) factor, so that is what to extract.
  4. Bridge to operation: pull out the largest common factor including any numerical coefficient (e.g. (2/3)√(x+1)), then compute what remains in the bracket by dividing each original term by the extracted factor.
  5. Tactical decision: if decimals are present (0.15, 0.6), convert to fractions only after factoring, because the target's denominator (e.g. 320) reveals which fraction form is needed (0.15 = 3/20 → 3/320).
  6. Verify by mentally re-expanding the factored result to confirm it matches my pre-factored expression.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write your current expression and the target side-by-side; identify the common factor F dictated by the target.
  2. Factor: rewrite expression = F · [bracket], computing each term of the bracket as (original term)/F.
  3. Simplify the bracket arithmetically (e.g. (x+1) − 3 = x − 2, or 0.6 − 0.15h = 0.15(4 − h)).
  4. Convert any remaining decimals to fractions to match the target's denominator (e.g. 0.15/16 = 3/320).
  5. State the final form exactly as printed and write 'as required' or QED.

Worked Micro-Example

Show $\int \sqrt{x+1}\,dx$ leads to $\tfrac{2}{3}(x-2)\sqrt{x+1}+C$. After substitution $u=\sqrt{x+1}$ and back-substituting one obtains $\tfrac{2}{3}(x+1)\sqrt{x+1} - 2\sqrt{x+1} + C$. Factor out $\tfrac{2}{3}\sqrt{x+1}$: $=\tfrac{2}{3}\sqrt{x+1}\big[(x+1)-3\big]+C = \tfrac{2}{3}(x-2)\sqrt{x+1}+C$. ✓

Variants — Same move, different surface

Target contains a linear bracket (a − bh) but your numerator is unfactored decimals like 0.6 − 0.15h
Factor the smaller decimal coefficient out of the numerator first to expose the bracket, then convert the leading decimal to a fraction matching the target's denominator.
Target is a completed-square form a(x−p)² + q but you have an expanded quadratic
Same principle — let the target's structure dictate the algebraic move (here completing the square rather than factoring a common factor).
Target is a single fraction but you have a sum of fractions
Combine over a common denominator chosen to match the target denominator exactly.

Common Misreadings

  • Believing the integration/differentiation alone is sufficient and stopping before re-shaping to the printed form, losing the final 'show' marks.
  • Trying to simplify each term separately (e.g. 0.6/16 and 0.15/16) instead of preserving the factored structure that matches the target.
  • Treating the target as merely 'one possible form' rather than a binding template.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slip when dividing terms by the extracted factor — e.g. converting −2√(x+1) under factor (2/3)√(x+1) requires −2 ÷ (2/3) = −3, not −4/3.
  • Decimal-to-fraction conversion errors under pressure, e.g. writing 0.15 = 3/200 instead of 3/20.
  • Factoring the wrong common term because you didn't read the target carefully — always match factor choice to the target's bracket.
  • Forgetting the +C in indefinite integrals after the algebraic rearrangement.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths — uses only the distributive law and basic fraction/decimal conversion. No formula booklet entry is required; this is a presentational/algebraic skill expected in any 'show that' proof in P2–P4.
