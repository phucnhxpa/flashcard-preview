Differentiation

Match curve's derivative to required line gradient to locate tangent point

Family: Tangent Gradient Matching Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a line is described as tangent to a curve C and its gradient is determined indirectly by another geometric relationship — most commonly 'parallel to a given line l₁', 'perpendicular to a given line', or 'is the normal at another point A on the same curve'. The visual cue is two simultaneous conditions on one line: a tangency condition (which links it to dy/dx of C) plus a direction condition (which fixes its gradient to a specific number, possibly via m₁m₂ = −1). Phrasings include 'tangent to C parallel to l₁', 'tangent at B where l is the normal at A', 'tangent perpendicular to …'. The move is appropriate as soon as you realise the tangent's gradient can be pinned down without knowing the line's intercept.

Motivation — Why this works

A line tangent to C at point P has gradient equal to dy/dx evaluated at P; if a second condition fixes that gradient to a known number m, then setting dy/dx = m yields a single equation whose solution is the x-coordinate of P. This collapses two geometric constraints into one calculus equation and avoids the heavier alternative of substituting y = mx + c into the curve and forcing a repeated root (discriminant = 0). The underlying principle is that parallel lines share gradients and perpendicular lines have gradients m and −1/m, so direction conditions translate cleanly into derivative values. It is the central bridge between coordinate geometry of straight lines and differentiation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the line in question is tangent to C — so its gradient must equal dy/dx at the (unknown) point of tangency.
  2. Identify the second condition that fixes the line's gradient: parallel to a known line (same gradient), perpendicular to a known line (negative reciprocal), or coincident with a normal at another point A on C (gradient = −1/(dy/dx at A)).
  3. Rule out the discriminant route: substituting y = mx + c into the curve equation and demanding Δ = 0 also works, but introduces the unknown intercept c and produces a quadratic in two unknowns — strictly more algebra for the same answer.
  4. Commit to the gradient-matching route because it gives one equation in one unknown (the x-coordinate of the tangent point) and never needs the intercept.
  5. Compute the target gradient m: from two given points use rise/run; from a perpendicularity or normal condition use −1/(slope at A), evaluating dy/dx at A first.
  6. Differentiate the curve to obtain dy/dx as a function of x, then solve dy/dx = m for x. Substitute back into the curve to get y, then form the line via y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) if the full equation is required.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Determine the required gradient m of the tangent line: m = (y₂ − y₁)/(x₂ − x₁) for parallel-to-a-line cases, or m = −1/m_A where m_A = dy/dx|_{x=x_A} for normal/perpendicular cases.
  2. Differentiate the curve: compute dy/dx by power rule.
  3. Set dy/dx = m and solve the resulting equation for x (the x-coordinate of the tangent point).
  4. Substitute the x value into the original curve equation y = f(x) to obtain the y-coordinate of the tangent point.
  5. If the full tangent equation is needed, apply y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) and simplify to the requested form.

Worked Micro-Example

Curve $C: y = 4 + 12x - 3x^2$; tangent line $l_2$ parallel to the line through $(0,0)$ and $(2,16)$. Gradient of given line $= 16/2 = 8$. Differentiate: $\frac{dy}{dx} = 12 - 6x$. Set $12 - 6x = 8 \Rightarrow x = \tfrac{2}{3}$. Then $y = 4 + 8 - \tfrac{4}{3} = \tfrac{32}{3}$, giving tangent $y - \tfrac{32}{3} = 8(x - \tfrac{2}{3})$, i.e. $3y = 24x + 16$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Tangent parallel to a given line l₁
Target gradient m = gradient of l₁; set dy/dx = m directly.
Tangent perpendicular to a given line (or l is the normal at point A on C)
Compute m_A = dy/dx at A, take negative reciprocal m = −1/m_A, then set dy/dx = m to find the other point.
Tangent to a circle parallel to a given line
Same logic: gradient of tangent fixed by the parallel line; combine with the radius-perpendicular-to-tangent property instead of differentiation.
Tangent at one point parallel to tangent at another
Set dy/dx at both points equal to each other, giving one equation linking the two x-values.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'tangent to C' as 'tangent at the maximum/given point on C' and reusing that point, ignoring the parallel/perpendicular condition.
  • Confusing normal with tangent and using the tangent gradient at A as the line's gradient, forgetting the negative reciprocal.
  • Trying to find the equation of the line first (and hence needing an intercept c) when only gradients are required to locate the tangent point.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when forming the negative reciprocal (writing 1/m instead of −1/m).
  • Arithmetic slip substituting fractional x back into the curve, e.g. mishandling −3(2/3)² = −4/3 or (−7/2)² = 49/4.
  • Forgetting to compute the y-coordinate at the tangent point and using a stray y-value (such as the y-intercept of the parallel reference line).

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics: power-rule differentiation (P1/P2) and coordinate-geometry gradient relationships m₁ = m₂ for parallel and m₁m₂ = −1 for perpendicular lines. No formula-booklet entry is required. Avoid invoking implicit differentiation or parametric tangents unless the question is explicitly in P3/P4 territory.
