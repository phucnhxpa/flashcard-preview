Strategic Atlas / Trigonometry / R Formula
Trigonometry

Apply or invert sector/triangle area formulas to extract the unknown

Family: R Formula Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

The figure involves a circular sector or a triangle formed by two radii, and the question supplies any two of {area, radius, included angle (in radians)} while asking for the third. Visual cues: a sector diagram with two radii and an arc, or an isosceles triangle OAB with OA = OB = r and a marked angle θ at the centre. Equivalent phrasings: 'sector of area … and angle … rad, find the radius', 'triangle with two sides and included angle, find the area', 'find the area of the minor sector / triangle OAB'. Whenever exactly one of the three quantities in A = ½r²θ (sector) or A = ½ab sin C (triangle) is missing, this move fires.

Motivation — Why this works

The formulas A = ½r²θ (sector, θ in radians) and A = ½ab sin C (triangle, SAS) each tie together exactly three quantities, so knowing any two determines the third by direct substitution and one-step algebra. This bypasses constructive geometry (dropping perpendiculars, finding heights, or using arc length) which would introduce extra unknowns. Algebraically, when r is unknown the formula is quadratic in r, so we isolate r² first and take the positive root since r is a length. The underlying principle is that radian measure makes A = ½r²θ a clean polynomial identity, and the SAS area formula follows from ½ × base × height with height = a sin C.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the configuration: either a sector (one curved side, two radii) or a triangle bounded by two radii — both fall under the 'r-formula' family.
  2. Identify which two of {A, r, θ} (or {a, b, C} for the triangle) are given numerically and which one is the target.
  3. Rule out alternatives: arc length s = rθ is unhelpful unless s is given; dropping a perpendicular to compute ½ × base × height adds an unnecessary trig step; degree-based formulas (θ/360)·πr² are wrong when θ is already in radians.
  4. Commit to the matching formula because it is the unique single-equation, single-unknown route.
  5. Decide the algebraic order: if r is unknown, multiply by 2 first to clear the ½, then divide by θ to isolate r², then take the positive square root. If A is unknown, just substitute and evaluate sin or simplify.
  6. Check angle-mode: confirm θ is in radians before substituting into ½r²θ; evaluate sin C using exact values when C is a standard angle (e.g. sin(2π/3) = √3/2).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the appropriate formula: A = ½r²θ (sector) or A = ½ab sin C (triangle).
  2. Substitute the two known values, leaving one symbolic unknown.
  3. If solving for r: multiply both sides by 2, then divide by θ to obtain r² = 2A/θ.
  4. Take the positive square root: r = √(2A/θ).
  5. If solving for A: evaluate the trig term using exact surd values where possible, then simplify the arithmetic product.

Worked Micro-Example

Sector with $A = 15$ and $\theta = 1.25$ rad: $15 = \tfrac{1}{2}r^2(1.25) \Rightarrow r^2 = \dfrac{30}{1.25} = 24 \Rightarrow r = 2\sqrt{6}$. Triangle OAB with $OA=OB=6$, $\angle AOB = \tfrac{2\pi}{3}$: $A = \tfrac{1}{2}(6)(6)\sin\tfrac{2\pi}{3} = 18 \cdot \tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = 9\sqrt{3}\,\text{m}^2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Arc length given instead of area
Use s = rθ; rearrange as r = s/θ — same one-equation-one-unknown logic.
Angle supplied in degrees
Convert to radians (multiply by π/180) before applying ½r²θ, or use the degree form (θ/360)·πr².
Segment area required (sector minus triangle)
Compute ½r²θ − ½r² sin θ = ½r²(θ − sin θ), combining both formulas.
Non-isosceles SAS triangle (two distinct sides)
Apply A = ½ab sin C with the two given sides and the included angle — same formula, different surface.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing the sector area formula ½r²θ with the triangle area formula ½r² sin θ and applying the wrong one to the wrong region.
  • Reading θ as degrees when it is in radians (or vice versa) and using a mismatched formula.
  • Treating the chord AB as a radius, or assuming the triangle is right-angled when only an isosceles configuration is given.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the ½ factor, producing r² = A/θ instead of r² = 2A/θ.
  • Calculator left in degree mode when θ is in radians (or the reverse).
  • Mis-evaluating sin at non-acute angles — e.g. assuming sin(2π/3) is negative; in fact sin(2π/3) = sin(π/3) = √3/2 (only cos changes sign in QII).
  • Dropping the positive-root requirement and keeping ±√, then choosing the wrong sign for a length.
  • Decimal-division slips when θ is non-integer (e.g. 1.25, 2.4).

A-level Scope Notes

Both formulas A = ½r²θ and A = ½ab sin C are in the Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics formula booklet (P2/P3 trigonometry section), with θ strictly in radians for the sector formula. Stay within radian measure for circular-measure questions; do not generalise to non-Euclidean or spherical geometry.
