Vectors

Rescale normal vector by its GCD to match target plane form

Family: Scalar Equivalence Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A 'show that' or 'verify' question fixes the plane equation in a specific integer form such as $3x + y - z = k$, but your computed normal (e.g. from a cross product of two direction vectors) comes out as a scalar multiple like $(6, 2, -2)$. The cue is that every component of your normal shares a common factor matching the ratio to the target coefficients. Also fires when comparing two plane equations to check they represent the same plane, or when the mark scheme demands a 'simplest integer' form.

Motivation — Why this works

The Cartesian plane equation $\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{r} = d$ is preserved under multiplication of both sides by any nonzero scalar $\lambda$, since $\lambda \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{r} = \lambda d$ describes the same locus. Hence $(6, 2, -2)$ and $(3, 1, -1)$ are normals to the same family of parallel planes, distinguished only by $d$. Dividing through by the GCD gives the unique simplest integer representative, which is what 'show that' rubrics require for an exact match.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice my computed normal $(6, 2, -2)$ has all even components, while the target equation uses coefficients $(3, 1, -1)$.
  2. I rule out the worry that I made an arithmetic error in the cross product — the ratio $6:2:-2 = 3:1:-1$ is exact, so the normals are parallel and define the same plane.
  3. I commit to rescaling because a 'show that' demands the exact given form, not merely an equivalent one.
  4. First operation: extract $\gcd(6,2,2) = 2$ and divide every component (and eventually $d$) by it.
  5. Tactically, I postpone computing $d$ until after rescaling, so I substitute a known point into $3x + y - z$ rather than $6x + 2y - 2z$ — this halves the arithmetic load.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute the normal $\mathbf{n}$ (typically via cross product of two direction vectors in the plane).
  2. Compute $\gcd$ of the absolute values of the components, e.g. $\gcd(6, 2, 2) = 2$.
  3. Divide every component of $\mathbf{n}$ by this GCD to obtain the reduced normal $\mathbf{n}'$.
  4. Confirm the reduced normal matches the target coefficients (including signs).
  5. Compute $d$ using the reduced normal: substitute a known point $\mathbf{a}$ on the plane into $\mathbf{n}' \cdot \mathbf{r} = \mathbf{n}' \cdot \mathbf{a}$.
  6. State the final equation in the required form.

Worked Micro-Example

Two direction vectors give $\mathbf{n} = (6, 2, -2)$, and the question asks to show the plane is $3x + y - z = 4$. Since $\gcd(6,2,2)=2$, divide to get $\mathbf{n}' = (3, 1, -1)$. Using the point $(1, 2, 1)$ on the plane: $d = 3(1) + 1(2) - 1(1) = 4$. Hence $3x + y - z = 4$ as required.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line direction vector in Cartesian/symmetric form
Direction $(4, -6, 2)$ can be reduced to $(2, -3, 1)$ to match a target symmetric equation $\frac{x-x_0}{2} = \frac{y-y_0}{-3} = \frac{z-z_0}{1}$.
Comparing two plane equations for identity
Check whether the coefficient vectors are scalar multiples AND the constants $d$ scale by the same factor; if only the normals scale, the planes are parallel but distinct.
Sign-flip rescaling ($\lambda = -1$)
If the target has opposite signs throughout, e.g. computed $(3,1,-1)=k$ vs target $-3x - y + z = -k$, multiply through by $-1$; the plane is unchanged.
Vector equation of a line where a 'nice' direction is preferred
Replace $\mathbf{d}$ with $\frac{1}{\gcd}\mathbf{d}$; the parameter $t$ rescales but the line is identical.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $(6,2,-2)$ and $(3,1,-1)$ as different normals giving different planes.
  • Believing the cross product must be 'wrong' because it doesn't directly produce the target coefficients.
  • Assuming any scaling is acceptable in a 'show that' — it isn't; the form must match exactly.

Pitfalls

  • Scaling the normal but forgetting to scale $d$ correspondingly when both sides of $\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{r}=d$ are already written.
  • Dividing by a number that isn't actually a common factor of all components (e.g. dividing $(6,3,-2)$ by 2).
  • Losing a sign when the GCD extraction involves negative components.
  • In an 'find an equation' question, wasting time rescaling when any equivalent form scores full marks.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Core Pure / FP3 vectors: scalar equivalence of plane and line equations, with normals obtained by cross product. No content beyond standard 3D Cartesian and vector forms is invoked.
