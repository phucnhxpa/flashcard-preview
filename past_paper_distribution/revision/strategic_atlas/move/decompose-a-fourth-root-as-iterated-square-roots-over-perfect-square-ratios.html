Decompose a fourth root as iterated square roots over perfect-square ratios

Family: Nested Root Simplification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation reduces to $x^4 = p/q$ (or $x^4 = k$) and both numerator and denominator are perfect squares, so a clean inner square root exists. Also triggers on $x^{1/4}$ expressions where the radicand factors as a square. Cues: 'find $x$ exactly', 'give answer as a surd', or any context where leaving $(p/q)^{1/4}$ would be unsimplified. Particularly common after solving quadratics in $x^2$ that yield rational squares.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity $a^{1/4} = \sqrt{\sqrt{a}}$ lets a fourth root be evaluated in two stages. When the inner radicand is a ratio of perfect squares, the inner square root collapses to a rational number, leaving only a single surd $\sqrt{p/q}$ outside. This produces a presentable exact form rather than an opaque quartic-root expression, and it makes any subsequent rationalisation (e.g. $\sqrt{2/3} = \sqrt{6}/3$) straightforward.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the equation has been reduced to $x^4 = 4/9$ with both 4 and 9 perfect squares.
  2. Rules out leaving the answer as $(4/9)^{1/4}$ since exam convention demands a simplified surd form.
  3. Commits to the iterated-root identity $a^{1/4} = \sqrt{\sqrt{a}}$ because the inner square root will be rational.
  4. Computes $\sqrt{4/9} = 2/3$, reducing the problem to a single square root $\sqrt{2/3}$.
  5. Applies the domain restriction $x > 0$ to discard the negative root, and rationalises if a tidier form is required: $\sqrt{2/3} = \sqrt{6}/3$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write $x = (p/q)^{1/4} = \sqrt{\sqrt{p/q}}$.
  2. Evaluate the inner square root using the perfect-square structure: $\sqrt{p/q} = \sqrt{p}/\sqrt{q}$.
  3. Take the outer square root of the resulting rational: $x = \sqrt{\sqrt{p}/\sqrt{q}}$.
  4. Apply the sign/domain restriction to select the correct root.
  5. Optionally rationalise the denominator to present a cleaner surd.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $x^4 = 4/9$ for $x>0$.\\ $x = \sqrt{\sqrt{4/9}} = \sqrt{2/3}$.\\ Rationalising: $x = \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{3}} = \dfrac{\sqrt{6}}{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$x^4 = 16/81$
Both layers resolve rationally: $\sqrt{16/81} = 4/9$, then $\sqrt{4/9} = 2/3$, giving a fully rational answer.
$x^6 = 64/729$
Decompose as cube-root of square-root (or vice versa): $\sqrt{64/729} = 8/27$, then $(8/27)^{1/3} = 2/3$.
$x^4 = k$ with $k$ a perfect square but not a ratio
Same identity: $x = \sqrt{\sqrt{k}}$; the inner root is an integer, leaving a single surd.
Solving $x^2 = a$ from a disguised quartic in $x$
After substitution $u = x^2$ and solving for $u$, taking $\sqrt{u}$ is the second of the two iterated roots.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $(4/9)^{1/4}$ as already simplified and writing it as the final answer.
  • Confusing fourth root with square root and computing only one stage, e.g. answering $2/3$ instead of $\sqrt{2/3}$.
  • Believing $\sqrt{a/b} = a/\sqrt{b}$ or other invalid surd manipulations during the inner step.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting that $x^4 = k$ has both $\pm k^{1/4}$ as real roots; failing to use the stated domain ($x>0$) to select.
  • Leaving the answer as $(4/9)^{1/4}$ — loses presentation marks under Edexcel mark schemes that require simplified surd form.
  • Errors when rationalising $\sqrt{2/3}$: writing $\sqrt{6}/9$ instead of $\sqrt{6}/3$.
  • Splitting $\sqrt[4]{p/q}$ as $\sqrt[4]{p}/\sqrt[4]{q}$ and stopping there, missing the further simplification available.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1/P2 surds and indices, P3 solving polynomial equations). Uses only the index laws $a^{1/4} = (a^{1/2})^{1/2}$ and standard surd manipulation; no complex roots required since the domain restriction selects a single real value.
