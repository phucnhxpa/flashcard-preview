Complex Numbers

Equate real or imaginary parts of two complex representations

Family: Equating Parts Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

You have two expressions for the same complex number — typically $\cos n\theta + i\sin n\theta$ from de Moivre on one side and a binomial expansion of $(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^n$ on the other. The question asks you to prove an identity for $\cos n\theta$, $\sin n\theta$, or $\tan n\theta$ in terms of $\cos\theta$ and $\sin\theta$. The cue word is 'hence show that' or 'express ... in terms of'. This move is the bridge between the complex setup and the real trig identity.

Motivation — Why this works

In $\mathbb{C}$, two complex numbers are equal if and only if their real parts match and their imaginary parts match. So a single complex equation $a+bi = c+di$ delivers two independent real equations $a=c$ and $b=d$. When one side is $\cos n\theta + i\sin n\theta$, the real part isolates a $\cos n\theta$ identity and the imaginary part isolates a $\sin n\theta$ identity. This is the structural payoff that makes the whole de Moivre + binomial method work.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice both sides describe the same complex number $(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^n$, just written in two different forms.
  2. I rule out manipulating the equation as a whole — that would keep $\cos n\theta$ and $\sin n\theta$ entangled.
  3. I commit to splitting into real and imaginary parts because the target identity is real-valued, so only one of these two equations is needed.
  4. I check the target: if it asks for $\cos n\theta$, I equate real parts (even powers of $i$); if $\sin n\theta$, imaginary parts (odd powers, with $i$ factored out); if $\tan n\theta$, I'll need both and divide.
  5. Inside the move, I sort binomial terms by parity of the $i$-power, and if the target is purely in $\cos\theta$ I plan to replace any $\sin^2\theta$ by $1 - \cos^2\theta$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Expand $(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^n$ by the binomial theorem and group terms by powers of $i$ using $i^2=-1$, $i^3=-i$, $i^4=1$.
  2. Write the result as $A(\theta) + iB(\theta)$, where $A$ collects even-power-of-$i$ terms and $B$ collects coefficients of $i$ from odd-power terms.
  3. Set this equal to $\cos n\theta + i\sin n\theta$ from de Moivre.
  4. Equate real parts: $\cos n\theta = A(\theta)$. Or equate imaginary parts: $\sin n\theta = B(\theta)$.
  5. If the required identity is purely in $\cos\theta$, substitute $\sin^2\theta = 1-\cos^2\theta$ (and pair off remaining $\sin\theta$ factors); if purely in $\sin\theta$, do the analogous swap.
  6. For a $\tan n\theta$ identity, divide the imaginary-part equation by the real-part equation and then divide numerator and denominator by $\cos^n\theta$.

Worked Micro-Example

From $(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)^3 = \cos 3\theta + i\sin 3\theta$, expand: $\cos^3\theta + 3i\cos^2\theta\sin\theta - 3\cos\theta\sin^2\theta - i\sin^3\theta$. Equating real parts: $\cos 3\theta = \cos^3\theta - 3\cos\theta\sin^2\theta$. Replacing $\sin^2\theta = 1-\cos^2\theta$ gives $\cos 3\theta = 4\cos^3\theta - 3\cos\theta$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Target is $\sin n\theta$ in terms of $\sin\theta$ only (odd $n$).
Equate imaginary parts and replace $\cos^2\theta = 1-\sin^2\theta$; for odd $n$ all surviving $\cos\theta$ factors come in even powers, so the substitution closes cleanly.
Target is $\tan n\theta$ in terms of $\tan\theta$.
Form the ratio $\sin n\theta / \cos n\theta$ from the imaginary-over-real equations, then divide top and bottom by $\cos^n\theta$ to convert every term into a power of $\tan\theta$.
Equating parts of $z = x+iy$ from a Cartesian equation problem (e.g. find locus from $|z-1|=|z+i|$).
Substitute $z=x+iy$, simplify, then equate real and imaginary parts (or just real, since the equation often reduces to a real one) to obtain the Cartesian locus.
Solving $z^n = w$ by writing both sides in modulus-argument form.
Equate moduli and equate arguments (mod $2\pi$); this is the polar analogue of equating real and imaginary parts.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the equation $\cos n\theta + i\sin n\theta = A+iB$ as one scalar equation and trying to manipulate it without splitting.
  • Equating real parts when the target is $\sin n\theta$ (or vice versa) because of failing to track which side carries the $i$.
  • Thinking $A$ and $B$ are themselves complex — they are real-valued functions of $\theta$ only after $i$ has been fully factored out.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to substitute $\sin^2\theta = 1-\cos^2\theta$ when the question demands an identity purely in $\cos\theta$.
  • Sign errors from $i^2=-1$, $i^3=-i$ when sorting binomial terms by parity.
  • Leaving an $i$ inside $B(\theta)$ when equating imaginary parts — the imaginary part is the coefficient of $i$, not the $iB$ term itself.
  • When deriving $\tan n\theta$, forgetting to divide numerator and denominator by $\cos^n\theta$, leaving a mixed expression.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure: de Moivre's theorem and its application to multiple-angle identities and to finding loci from $z=x+iy$. No use of complex analysis beyond equating real and imaginary parts of finite expressions.
