Strategic Atlas / Differentiation / Gradient At Point
Differentiation

Differentiate then substitute x-coordinate to get gradient at a point

Family: Gradient At Point Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks for 'the gradient of the curve/tangent/normal at point P' (or equivalent: 'rate of change at x = a', 'slope at P'), and the curve is given as y = f(x) in a form you can differentiate (polynomial, sum of power-functions, possibly after expansion). The x-coordinate of P is either given directly or readable from the point's coordinates. Visual cues: a specific point named with coordinates, the words 'gradient', 'tangent', or 'normal', and an explicit y = f(x).

Motivation — Why this works

The derivative f'(x) is a gradient FUNCTION — it returns the slope of the tangent at every x. To pin down the slope at one specific point, we evaluate f'(x) at x = x_P, producing a single number. This number is the tangent gradient at P (and, via m_normal = −1/m_tangent, also unlocks the normal). The technique works because differentiability means the tangent's slope is exactly lim_{h→0}[f(x+h)−f(x)]/h, which the power rule computes mechanically for polynomials and power-functions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the phrasing: 'gradient at P' or 'tangent/normal at P' signals I need a NUMBER, not a function — so I must differentiate AND substitute.
  2. Inspect the form of y. If it's a clean polynomial or sum of power terms, the power rule applies directly. If it's factorised (e.g. (2x+5)(x−3)²), I weigh expanding versus product/chain rule — expanding is usually safer unless the evaluation point makes a factor vanish.
  3. Rule out alternatives: first principles is unnecessarily slow here; implicit differentiation is needed only if y is not given explicitly.
  4. Commit: differentiate term-by-term using the power rule d/dx(x^n) = n x^(n−1), being careful with constants (→ 0), negative exponents, and fractional exponents.
  5. Identify the correct coordinate to substitute — it is the x-coordinate of P, never the y-coordinate.
  6. Substitute x = x_P into f'(x) and simplify carefully, especially when x_P is a fraction or produces surds via fractional/negative powers.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write y = f(x) in a differentiation-ready form (expand brackets or rewrite roots/reciprocals as powers, e.g. 1/x² = x^(−2), √x = x^(1/2)).
  2. Differentiate term-by-term using the power rule: d/dx(a x^n) = a n x^(n−1); constants → 0.
  3. Record the gradient function f'(x) clearly.
  4. Substitute the x-coordinate of P into f'(x).
  5. Simplify the resulting numerical (possibly surd) expression to obtain m = f'(x_P).

Worked Micro-Example

Curve: $y = 2x^3 - 7x^2 - 12x + 45$, point $P(0, 45)$. Differentiate: $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 6x^2 - 14x - 12$. Substitute $x = 0$: $f'(0) = 0 - 0 - 12 = -12$. Gradient at $P$ is $-12$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Curve given in factorised form with P at a root of one factor
Keep the factorised form and use the product rule — at the root, vanishing factors collapse the expression and save algebra.
Curve defined implicitly, e.g. x² + y² = 25
Use implicit differentiation to obtain dy/dx in terms of x and y, then substitute BOTH coordinates of P.
Curve given parametrically x = x(t), y = y(t)
Compute dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) and substitute the parameter value t corresponding to P.
Question asks for the normal gradient
Find m_tangent by this move, then take m_normal = −1/m_tangent.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing 'gradient of the tangent' (a number) with 'equation of the tangent' (a line equation requiring y − y₁ = m(x − x₁)).
  • Treating 'gradient at P' as if it required first principles when the question allows standard differentiation.

Pitfalls

  • Substituting the y-coordinate of P instead of the x-coordinate into f'(x).
  • Forgetting that the derivative of a constant is 0 (e.g. dropping or keeping the constant term incorrectly).
  • Arithmetic slips in the power-rule coefficients (e.g. 7×2 = 14, 2×3 = 6) which silently corrupt the answer.
  • Mishandling negative/fractional exponents of fractions: (1/2)^(−1/2) = √2, not 1/√2; the negative exponent flips the base first.
  • If the normal is wanted, forgetting to invert and negate after computing m_tangent.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1–P4). Uses the power rule d/dx(x^n) = n x^(n−1) (in the formula booklet for standard derivatives). Stay with explicit y = f(x) at P1 level; implicit and parametric variants belong to P3/P4. Avoid invoking partial derivatives or vector-valued generalisations — these are out of scope.
