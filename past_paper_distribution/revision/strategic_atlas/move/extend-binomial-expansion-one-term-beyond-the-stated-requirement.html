Binomial Expansion

Trigger — When to use

Part (a) gave a binomial expansion up to a fixed power (typically $x^2$), and part (b) multiplies that expansion by an expression containing a negative power of $x$ such as $\frac{1}{x}$, $\frac{2}{x}$, or $(p + qx^{-1})$. The negative-degree prefactor shifts which expansion terms contribute to the requested coefficient: $\frac{1}{x} \cdot x^{n+1}$ produces an $x^n$ term, so the expansion must be extended one further power. Cue phrases include 'hence find the coefficient of $x^2$ in $\frac{1}{x}(\ldots)^n$' or any product where the prefactor lowers degrees.

Motivation — Why this works

The general term $\binom{n}{r}(kx)^r$ is valid for every $r \le n$, so producing one extra term costs only a single additional binomial coefficient and power computation. The strategic point is recognising that the multiplication by $x^{-1}$ effectively re-indexes which power of $x$ each term contributes to in the final product. Missing this re-indexing causes a contribution to be silently dropped, giving a wrong coefficient even though every computed term is individually correct.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that part (b) multiplies the part (a) expansion by something containing $x^{-1}$ (or a similar negative-degree term).
  2. Rule out reusing only the part (a) terms — the $x^{-1}$ prefactor pairs with the $x^3$ term to give $x^2$, which was never computed.
  3. Commit to extending the expansion by exactly one term because only one negative-degree power is present in the prefactor.
  4. Apply the general term formula $\binom{n}{r}(kx)^r$ with $r$ set to the new required power.
  5. Track signs carefully when $k$ is negative, and simplify the resulting fraction before using it in the pairing sum.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the highest power of $x$ now needed from the expansion: (target power) + (magnitude of negative power in prefactor).
  2. Apply the general term: $\binom{n}{r}(kx)^r$ with $r$ equal to that new power.
  3. Compute $\binom{n}{r}$ using $\frac{n(n-1)\cdots(n-r+1)}{r!}$.
  4. Compute $k^r$, watching the sign when $k<0$ and $r$ is odd.
  5. Simplify the coefficient to lowest terms before pairing it with the prefactor.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $(1 - \tfrac{x}{4})^{16}$ expanded to $x^2$ in part (a), part (b) asks for the $x^2$ coefficient in $\tfrac{1}{x}(1 - \tfrac{x}{4})^{16}$. The $x^3$ term is needed: $\binom{16}{3}\left(-\tfrac{1}{4}\right)^3 x^3 = 560 \cdot \left(-\tfrac{1}{64}\right) x^3 = -\tfrac{35}{4}x^3$. Multiplying by $\tfrac{1}{x}$ contributes $-\tfrac{35}{4}x^2$ to the final answer.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Prefactor is $(a + bx^{-1})$ with a single negative power.
Extend the expansion by exactly one extra term to capture the $x^{-1}$-pairing.
Prefactor is $(a + bx^{-1} + cx^{-2})$.
Extend the expansion by two extra terms so that both $x^{-1}$ and $x^{-2}$ pairings are captured.
Target coefficient is $x^0$ (constant term) in $\frac{1}{x^2}(1+kx)^n$.
Extend to the $x^2$ term of the expansion; lower powers contribute to negative powers of $x$ and are discarded.
Expansion of $(1+x)^{-n}$ or $(1+x)^{1/2}$ in C4-style infinite series.
Same logic — compute one extra term of the infinite series when the prefactor is $\frac{1}{x}$ or similar.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming the part (a) expansion (up to $x^2$) is automatically sufficient for part (b).
  • Treating $\frac{1}{x}(\ldots)$ as a typo or factor that cancels, rather than as a degree-shifting prefactor.
  • Extending the expansion by too many terms and including contributions that produce powers of $x$ irrelevant to the target.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error on odd powers of a negative $k$: $k^3 < 0$ when $k < 0$.
  • Arithmetic slip when simplifying $\binom{n}{r}k^r$; e.g. leaving $\tfrac{560}{64}$ unsimplified risks later error.
  • Forgetting to add the new term's contribution to existing contributions from the part (a) terms when forming the final coefficient.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (binomial expansion of $(a+bx)^n$ for positive integer $n$, and the C4-style expansion for rational $n$). Uses only the standard general-term formula and basic coefficient arithmetic.
