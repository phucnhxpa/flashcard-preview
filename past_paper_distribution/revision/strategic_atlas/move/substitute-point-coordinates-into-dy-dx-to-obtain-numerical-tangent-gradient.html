Trigger — When to use

The question asks for a tangent or normal line at a specific point P, and you have already produced dy/dx (either as an explicit function of x, or, after implicit differentiation, as an expression in both x and y). Phrasings include 'find the equation of the tangent at P(a, b)', 'show that the gradient at P is...', or 'find the normal at the point where x = a'. The structural cue is: a general gradient formula exists, and a specific point is supplied — you need to convert the formula into a single number. If dy/dx contains y, both coordinates must be substituted; if dy/dx contains only x, only the x-coordinate is needed.

Motivation — Why this works

The derivative dy/dx is a gradient function — a formula valid at every point of the curve. To write the equation of a tangent or normal at a particular point, you need a single numerical slope. Substituting the point's coordinates into dy/dx evaluates this gradient function at that point, producing the specific slope m needed for y − y₁ = m(x − x₁). For implicit derivatives, dy/dx depends on both x and y, so both coordinates are required; the question guarantees the point lies on the curve, so the substitution is consistent. This is the geometric interpretation of the derivative made concrete.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question wants a tangent (or normal) at a specific point, and I already have dy/dx from the previous part — so the next concrete need is a numerical gradient.
  2. I rule out re-differentiating, re-deriving y from the original equation, or substituting into the original y expression — none of these give the slope. Substituting into dy/dx is the direct route.
  3. I check whether dy/dx is in terms of x alone (explicit case) or in terms of x and y (implicit/exponential case). This tells me whether I need one coordinate or both.
  4. I commit to substituting the given point's coordinates directly into the simplified form of dy/dx — using the simplified form minimises arithmetic risk.
  5. I look for friendly simplifications the examiner has built in: e.g. x = 0 makes e^{kx} = 1, integer coordinates collapse rational powers cleanly. This is a hint I'm on the right track.
  6. I evaluate the numerator and denominator separately, watching signs carefully, and reduce to a single fraction m. If the question asks for the normal, I then take −1/m.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write down dy/dx in its simplified form from the previous part.
  2. Identify the coordinates (x₁, y₁) of the given point P.
  3. Substitute x = x₁ (and y = y₁ if dy/dx contains y) into dy/dx.
  4. Evaluate numerator and denominator separately, simplifying any exponentials (e⁰ = 1) or rational powers (e.g. 4^{3/2} = 8) cleanly.
  5. Combine to get a single numerical gradient m_tangent.
  6. If a normal is required, compute m_normal = −1/m_tangent.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1 + 2y e^{-2x}}{e^{-2x} + 4y}$ and point $(0, 1)$: substitute $x=0, y=1$. Since $e^{0}=1$, numerator $= 1 + 2(1)(1) = 3$ and denominator $= 1 + 4(1) = 5$. So $m_{\text{tangent}} = \tfrac{3}{5}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Explicit derivative dy/dx = f(x) only
Substitute only the x-coordinate; the y-coordinate is not needed for the gradient (it is used only in the line equation y − y₁ = m(x − x₁)).
Implicit derivative dy/dx = g(x, y)
Substitute both x and y simultaneously. The point is guaranteed to lie on the curve, so this is consistent.
Parametric curve
Substitute the parameter value t = t₀ into dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt), not the (x, y) coordinates.
Normal line required
Evaluate dy/dx at P as above, then take the negative reciprocal: m_normal = −1/m_tangent.
Tangent satisfies a geometric condition (e.g. horizontal/vertical)
Instead of being given a point, set numerator = 0 (horizontal) or denominator = 0 (vertical) and solve simultaneously with the curve equation.

Common Misreadings

  • Substituting the x-value into the original y expression instead of dy/dx — this gives a y-coordinate, not a gradient.
  • Substituting the y-coordinate into dy/dx in place of x (or vice versa) when the derivative is explicit.
  • Forgetting to substitute y when dy/dx is implicit, leaving y as an unknown in the final 'gradient'.
  • Trying to re-derive the y-coordinate from the original equation when it has already been given.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips with negative signs in numerator or denominator, especially after implicit differentiation.
  • Mis-evaluating rational powers, e.g. computing 4^{3/2} as 6 instead of 8, or treating (−6)/(2³) as ((−6)/2)³.
  • Writing e⁰ = 0 instead of 1, collapsing the wrong terms.
  • Substituting into an unsimplified or pre-tidied version of dy/dx and accumulating sign errors.
  • Forgetting to take the negative reciprocal when the question asks for the normal rather than the tangent.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3/P4 (Pure) and FP1 scope. Uses the geometric interpretation of the derivative (gradient of tangent) and, where applicable, implicit differentiation from P4. No formula booklet entry is invoked — this is a direct evaluation. Avoid generalising to surfaces or partial derivatives, which are out of scope.
