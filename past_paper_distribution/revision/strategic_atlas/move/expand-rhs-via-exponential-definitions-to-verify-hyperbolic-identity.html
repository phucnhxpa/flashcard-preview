Proof

Expand RHS via exponential definitions to verify hyperbolic identity

Family: Exponential Definition Proof Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A 'prove that' question involving a hyperbolic identity (compound angle, double angle, or Osborn-type relation) where no prior hyperbolic identity has been established. Cues include products like cosh A cosh B ± sinh A sinh B, expressions in cosh 2x or sinh 2x, or the fundamental relation cosh²x − sinh²x. The only foundational tool available at this stage is the exponential definition, so the move fires whenever the identity must be justified from first principles.

Motivation — Why this works

Every hyperbolic function is defined by cosh x = (e^x + e^{-x})/2 and sinh x = (e^x − e^{-x})/2. Substituting these definitions reduces any hyperbolic identity to pure exponential algebra, where equality follows by expansion and cancellation. Starting from the more complex side (typically the side with products) is natural because expansion creates cross-terms whose cancellation is the heart of the proof. The simpler side then matches a recognisable exponential form.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the identity is hyperbolic and no compound-angle formula has been quoted as given — the only legal tool is the exponential definition.
  2. Rule out attempts to mimic trig identities directly (signs differ for hyperbolics — Osborn's rule is a memory aid, not a proof).
  3. Commit to substituting definitions on the side with products, because expansion will produce cross-terms whose cancellation is the proof's mechanism.
  4. Bridge: write each hyperbolic factor as its exponential fraction over 2, so each product sits over a common denominator of 4.
  5. Tactically track signs carefully when expanding (e^A − e^{-A})(e^B − e^{-B}); identify which middle terms cancel between the two products and combine the surviving terms into the target exponential form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Replace cosh A cosh B with ((e^A + e^{-A})/2)·((e^B + e^{-B})/2) and sinh A sinh B with ((e^A − e^{-A})/2)·((e^B − e^{-B})/2).
  2. Expand each product to obtain four exponential terms over denominator 4.
  3. Add (or subtract, per the identity) the two fractions; the e^{A−B} and e^{−A+B} cross-terms cancel.
  4. Combine surviving terms: (2e^{A+B} + 2e^{−(A+B)})/4 = (e^{A+B} + e^{−(A+B)})/2.
  5. Identify this as cosh(A+B) by the definition, completing the proof.

Worked Micro-Example

Prove $\cosh(A+B) = \cosh A\cosh B + \sinh A\sinh B$. RHS $= \tfrac{1}{4}(e^A+e^{-A})(e^B+e^{-B}) + \tfrac{1}{4}(e^A-e^{-A})(e^B-e^{-B})$. Expanding: $\tfrac{1}{4}(e^{A+B}+e^{A-B}+e^{-A+B}+e^{-A-B}) + \tfrac{1}{4}(e^{A+B}-e^{A-B}-e^{-A+B}+e^{-A-B})$. The $e^{A-B}$ and $e^{-A+B}$ terms cancel, giving $\tfrac{1}{4}(2e^{A+B}+2e^{-(A+B)}) = \tfrac{e^{A+B}+e^{-(A+B)}}{2} = \cosh(A+B)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Proving $\sinh(A+B) = \sinh A\cosh B + \cosh A\sinh B$
Same expansion structure, but the surviving terms are $e^{A+B} - e^{-(A+B)}$ over 2; the cancelling cross-terms are different pairs.
Proving $\cosh^2 x - \sinh^2 x = 1$
Square each definition; the $e^{2x}$ and $e^{-2x}$ terms cancel between the two squares, leaving constant terms summing to 1.
Proving double-angle $\cosh 2x = 2\cosh^2 x - 1$ or $\sinh 2x = 2\sinh x\cosh x$
Substitute definitions on the RHS, expand, and recognise the resulting exponential form as $\cosh 2x$ or $\sinh 2x$.
Proving $\cosh(A-B)$ or $\sinh(A-B)$
Replace $B$ with $-B$ in the +B identity, using $\cosh(-B)=\cosh B$ and $\sinh(-B)=-\sinh B$, or expand definitions directly with $-B$.

Common Misreadings

  • Importing the trig sign convention and writing $\cosh(A+B) = \cosh A\cosh B - \sinh A\sinh B$ — the hyperbolic version has a plus.
  • Assuming a compound-angle identity may be quoted without proof when the question explicitly says 'prove'.
  • Treating Osborn's rule as a proof rather than a mnemonic for converting trig to hyperbolic identities.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors expanding $(e^A - e^{-A})(e^B - e^{-B})$: the product $(-e^{-A})(-e^{-B}) = +e^{-(A+B)}$, not negative.
  • Forgetting the factor of $1/4$ when multiplying two fractions each with denominator 2.
  • Combining unlike exponential terms (e.g. mixing $e^{A-B}$ with $e^{-(A+B)}$).
  • Stopping at $(e^{A+B} + e^{-(A+B)})/2$ without explicitly identifying it as $\cosh(A+B)$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (Further Maths) hyperbolic functions: definitions of cosh and sinh in terms of e^x are given, and proofs of compound-angle and related identities by expansion are standard exam tasks.
