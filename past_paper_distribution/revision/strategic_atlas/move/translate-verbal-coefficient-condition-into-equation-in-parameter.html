Translate verbal coefficient condition into equation in parameter

Family: Coefficient Matching Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question imposes a verbal relationship between named coefficients of an expansion (e.g. 'the constant term is three times the coefficient of x', 'the coefficient of x^2 equals the coefficient of x^3', 'there is no x term'). Both quantities have already been written as expressions in an unknown parameter k. The relationship is a single scalar condition, so it produces one equation in k. Look for keywords like 'equals', 'is k times', 'is twice', 'vanishes', or ratios between coefficients.

Motivation — Why this works

Each named coefficient has already been isolated as an expression in k by a prior expansion (binomial, partial fractions, or polynomial multiplication). A verbal relation between them is therefore just a numerical equation linking those expressions, almost always linear in k. Solving it pins down k uniquely. Common scalar factors (like $\sqrt{2}$ here) cancel cleanly because they multiply both sides of the relation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question gives a relation between two specific coefficients I have already computed in terms of k.
  2. I rule out re-expanding or matching whole polynomials — only two coefficients are constrained, so I only need one equation.
  3. I commit to writing the verbal statement as an equation in the exact direction stated ('A = 3B', not 'B = 3A').
  4. I bridge to algebra by substituting my expressions for the constant term and the coefficient of x into that equation.
  5. Tactically, I cancel any common factor (here $\sqrt{2}$) before expanding, then clear fractions by multiplying through by the denominator to keep arithmetic clean.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two coefficients named in the condition and write them as expressions in k.
  2. Translate the verbal statement literally into an equation, preserving the direction (which side is the multiple of which).
  3. Cancel any common scalar factor on both sides (e.g. $\sqrt{2}$).
  4. Expand brackets and collect k-terms on one side, constants on the other.
  5. Clear fractional coefficients by multiplying through by the denominator.
  6. Solve the resulting linear equation for k and report the signed value.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose constant term $= 10\sqrt{2}$ and coefficient of $x$ is $\sqrt{2}\left(-\tfrac{5k}{8} - 6\right)$. The condition 'constant = 3 × (coefficient of x)' gives $10\sqrt{2} = 3\sqrt{2}\left(-\tfrac{5k}{8} - 6\right)$. Cancel $\sqrt{2}$: $10 = -\tfrac{15k}{8} - 18$, so $28 = -\tfrac{15k}{8}$, giving $k = -\tfrac{224}{15}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

'The coefficient of $x^2$ is twice the coefficient of $x^3$' in a binomial expansion of $(a+bx)^n$ with parameter k.
Form the equation $[x^2]\text{-coeff} = 2 \times [x^3]\text{-coeff}$ and solve linearly (or as a quadratic) in k.
'There is no term in $x$' (the $x$-coefficient vanishes).
Set the expression for the coefficient of $x$ equal to 0 and solve for k.
Partial fractions identity where matching at one power of x gives one equation in an unknown constant.
Equate the coefficient on the LHS to the corresponding coefficient on the RHS and solve.
Two expansions are stated to be equal up to some power.
Match coefficients power-by-power, generating one equation per power and solving the resulting (often linear) system in the parameters.

Common Misreadings

  • Reversing the relation: reading 'constant = 3 × coefficient of x' as 'coefficient of x = 3 × constant'.
  • Treating 'coefficient of x' as the whole term (including the x) rather than the scalar multiplying x.
  • Confusing 'constant term' with the leading term or with $f(0)$ when the expansion is not in standard form.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips when clearing the fraction $-\tfrac{15k}{8}$: forgetting to multiply both sides by 8.
  • Sign errors when moving $-18$ across the equality.
  • Reporting $|k|$ instead of the signed value, e.g. $\tfrac{224}{15}$ instead of $-\tfrac{224}{15}$.
  • Failing to cancel the common surd factor early, leading to messier (but still correct) algebra.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure scope: binomial expansion, manipulation of surds, and solving a linear equation in one unknown. No methods beyond C1–C2/P1–P2 are required.
