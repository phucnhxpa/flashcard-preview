Algebra & Functions

Chain-substitute via linking equation to reduce to one variable

Family: Systematic Elimination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a problem yields three (or more) equations in three unknowns and one of them is a simple linking relation (e.g. d = 2r, y = kx, b = a + c). Typical surfaces: combined AP/GP problems, simultaneous conditions on sequences, geometry problems mixing a ratio condition with sum/area conditions. Cue phrases include 'the common difference is twice the common ratio' or any equation already solved for one variable. Use it when at least one of the remaining equations is nonlinear and you want to confine that nonlinearity to the final step.

Motivation — Why this works

A linear linking equation already expresses one unknown explicitly, so substituting it costs nothing and immediately drops the system to two unknowns. Cascading that result through the next-simplest (usually linear) equation produces an explicit formula for a second unknown, leaving the nonlinear equation to absorb both substitutions in one move. The result is a single-variable polynomial — the cleanest object to solve. Choosing the substitution order by 'linear first, nonlinear last' minimises expansion work and the chance of sign errors.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices three unknowns spread across three equations and identifies which equation is nonlinear (the one to defer).
  2. Rules out attacking the nonlinear equation first, since substituting two unknowns into it simultaneously produces messy bivariate expressions.
  3. Commits to a chain: use the linking equation to kill one variable, then use the remaining linear equation to express a second variable in terms of the third.
  4. Bridges to operation by isolating the cleanest unknown (the one already solved for in the linking equation) and feeding it into the next-simplest equation.
  5. Tactically decides the substitution order by ranking equations from linear to nonlinear, so only the final substitution involves higher-degree algebra.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the linking equation (here d = 2r) that already isolates one unknown.
  2. Substitute it into the next-simplest (linear) equation: 2a + 3d = 14 becomes 2a + 6r = 14.
  3. Solve the resulting linear equation for a second unknown: a + 3r = 7, so a = 7 − 3r.
  4. Substitute both expressions into the remaining (nonlinear) equation: a(1 + r + r²) = 39 becomes (7 − 3r)(1 + r + r²) = 39.
  5. Expand and rearrange to obtain a single-variable polynomial in r, ready to solve.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $2a + 3d = 14$, $a(1 + r + r^2) = 39$, and $d = 2r$. Substitute $d = 2r$: $2a + 6r = 14 \Rightarrow a = 7 - 3r$. Substitute into the GP sum: $(7 - 3r)(1 + r + r^2) = 39$. Expanding gives $7 + 7r + 7r^2 - 3r - 3r^2 - 3r^3 = 39$, i.e. $-3r^3 + 4r^2 + 4r - 32 = 0$, a single cubic in $r$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Simultaneous AP conditions where one equation gives a = kd directly
Substitute a = kd into the sum/term equations first; the remaining equation becomes single-variable in d.
Coordinate geometry: line equation y = mx + c combined with a circle equation
Use the linear equation as the linking relation, substitute into the quadratic circle equation to obtain a single-variable quadratic in x.
GP/AP hybrid where the linking condition is nonlinear, e.g. d = r²
Still substitute the linking equation first, but the resulting equation in a and r may already be nonlinear; rank remaining equations by degree to choose the next substitution.
Three-variable simultaneous equations in Further Pure with one trivially solved equation
Eliminate the pre-solved variable first, then use a second equation to express another variable, leaving the hardest equation for last.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating all three equations as equally complex and attempting matrix/elimination methods instead of exploiting the explicit linking equation.
  • Substituting directly into the nonlinear equation first, producing a bivariate nonlinear expression that is much harder to manage.
  • Mistaking the linking equation as an extra constraint to check at the end rather than the primary substitution tool.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when expanding (7 − 3r)(1 + r + r²), especially on the −3r·r² term.
  • Forgetting to simplify 2a + 6r = 14 to a + 3r = 7 before isolating a, leading to fractional coefficients downstream.
  • Losing track of which variable has been eliminated and reintroducing d or a later in the working.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure scope: arithmetic and geometric series (P2), simultaneous equations and polynomial manipulation (P1/P2). The resulting cubic is solvable by inspection or factor theorem, both standard A-level techniques.
