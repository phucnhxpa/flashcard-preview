Merge same-exponent factors into a single base via (ab)^x

Family: Base Merging Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The equation contains two (or more) exponential terms multiplied together that share the same exponent but have different bases, e.g. $4^x \times 6^x$ or $2^x \times 5^x$. Equivalent surface cues: a product like $a^x b^x$ on one side, or $\frac{a^x}{b^x}$ which merges to $(a/b)^x$. Fires whenever a single log step is blocked because two distinct bases share the same unknown exponent.

Motivation — Why this works

The index law $a^x b^x = (ab)^x$ (and $a^x/b^x = (a/b)^x$) collapses two exponential factors sharing the same exponent into one exponential with a combined base. This reduces the equation to the canonical form $c = k^x$, which is solved in one log step as $x = \log_k c$. Without merging, you would need a more cumbersome simultaneous-log manipulation; merging is the cleanest route on IAL papers.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice two exponentials with identical exponent $x$ but different bases multiplied together.
  2. Rule out taking logs immediately — that would give $\log a^x + \log b^x = x(\log a + \log b)$, workable but messier; also rule out adding bases, which is not a valid law.
  3. Commit to the product-of-powers law because the matching exponent makes $(ab)^x$ exact and the equation becomes a single-base exponential.
  4. Multiply the bases to form one exponential $(ab)^x$, leaving the equation in the form $c = (ab)^x$.
  5. Apply $\log$ (any base) to isolate $x = \dfrac{\log c}{\log(ab)}$; choose base 10 or $\ln$ to match the demanded answer form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the matching exponent across the two factors.
  2. Apply $a^x \times b^x = (ab)^x$ to merge the bases.
  3. Rewrite the equation as $c = (ab)^x$.
  4. Take logs of both sides and solve $x = \dfrac{\log c}{\log(ab)}$.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $2 = 4^x \times 6^x$.\\ Merge: $4^x \times 6^x = (4 \cdot 6)^x = 24^x$.\\ So $2 = 24^x \Rightarrow x \log 24 = \log 2 \Rightarrow x = \dfrac{\log 2}{\log 24}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quotient of same-exponent powers, e.g. $\dfrac{6^x}{2^x} = 5$
Apply $a^x/b^x = (a/b)^x$ to get $3^x = 5$, then log.
Three or more factors, e.g. $2^x \times 3^x \times 5^x = 60$
Merge all bases: $30^x = 60$, then log.
Reverse split: need $12^x$ to match a $3^x$ elsewhere
Split $12^x = 3^x \times 4^x$ to expose a common factor with the other term.
Exponents are linear in $x$ but identical, e.g. $a^{2x+1} \times b^{2x+1}$
Factor common exponent first: $(ab)^{2x+1}$, then log and solve linear equation in $x$.

Common Misreadings

  • Adding bases: writing $4^x + 6^x = 10^x$ — no such identity exists; the law is for products only.
  • Treating $4^x \times 6^x$ as $24^{2x}$ by also multiplying the exponents — the exponent stays $x$, only bases multiply.
  • Believing the merge requires the bases to share a prime factor; it does not — any two real positive bases combine.

Pitfalls

  • Applying the law when exponents differ: $4^x \times 6^{2x} \neq 24^x$. First rewrite to a common exponent (e.g. $6^{2x} = 36^x$) before merging.
  • Forgetting to handle a coefficient outside the powers, e.g. $2 \cdot 4^x \times 6^x$ — only the powers merge; the constant stays.
  • Sign/quotient slip: $a^x/b^x = (a/b)^x$, not $(b/a)^x$.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard index-law manipulation in Edexcel IAL P2/P3 logarithms and exponentials; relies only on $a^x b^x = (ab)^x$ and basic log laws, both within syllabus.
