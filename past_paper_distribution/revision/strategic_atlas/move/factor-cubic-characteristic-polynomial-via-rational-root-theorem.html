Matrices

Factor cubic characteristic polynomial via rational root theorem

Family: Polynomial Factorisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The characteristic equation det(A − λI) = 0 has expanded to a cubic in λ, typically of the form −λ³ + (tr A)λ² − cλ + det A = 0 (or its negation). In Edexcel FP3 the eigenvalues of a 3×3 matrix are almost always integers or simple rationals, so trial of small integer divisors of the constant term is the fastest route. Cue phrases: 'find the eigenvalues of the 3×3 matrix', 'show that λ = k is an eigenvalue', or a cubic in λ that does not factor obviously by grouping.

Motivation — Why this works

By the rational root theorem, any rational root p/q of an integer-coefficient cubic must have p dividing the constant term and q dividing the leading coefficient. Once one root λ₁ is found by inspection, the factor theorem guarantees (λ − λ₁) divides the cubic, reducing it to a quadratic solvable by factorising or the quadratic formula. This sidesteps the general cubic formula, which is outside A-level scope.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the characteristic equation is a cubic in λ with integer coefficients, so I expect integer (or small rational) eigenvalues.
  2. I rule out attempting the cubic formula or numerical methods — neither is in scope, and Edexcel guarantees a 'nice' root.
  3. I commit to the rational root theorem: list divisors of the constant term as candidate roots.
  4. I evaluate p(λ) at λ = ±1, ±2, … (and ± factors of det A) until p(λ₁) = 0 to identify the first root.
  5. I then divide the cubic by (λ − λ₁) — by inspection, synthetic division, or comparing coefficients — and solve the resulting quadratic for the other two eigenvalues.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the cubic in standard form, e.g. λ³ + aλ² + bλ + c = 0 (multiply through by −1 if needed so the leading coefficient is +1).
  2. List integer divisors of the constant term c as candidate roots.
  3. Evaluate p(λ) at each candidate until one yields 0; call this root λ₁.
  4. Divide the cubic by (λ − λ₁) to obtain a quadratic λ² + pλ + q.
  5. Solve λ² + pλ + q = 0 by factorisation or the quadratic formula to get λ₂ and λ₃.
  6. Check using λ₁ + λ₂ + λ₃ = tr(A) and λ₁λ₂λ₃ = det(A).

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose the characteristic equation is $\lambda^3 - 6\lambda^2 + 11\lambda - 6 = 0$. Test $\lambda = 1$: $1 - 6 + 11 - 6 = 0$, so $(\lambda - 1)$ is a factor. Dividing gives $\lambda^3 - 6\lambda^2 + 11\lambda - 6 = (\lambda - 1)(\lambda^2 - 5\lambda + 6) = (\lambda - 1)(\lambda - 2)(\lambda - 3)$. Eigenvalues: $1, 2, 3$. Check: sum $= 6 = \operatorname{tr}(A)$, product $= 6 = \det(A)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cubic has λ as an obvious factor (constant term is 0, i.e. det A = 0)
Factor out λ immediately to give λ(λ² + pλ + q) = 0; one eigenvalue is 0, then solve the quadratic.
Repeated eigenvalue
After dividing by (λ − λ₁), the quadratic factorises as a perfect square (λ − λ₂)², giving a repeated root; this is common in symmetric matrices Edexcel sets.
One eigenvalue is given in the question stem
Skip the trial step — divide directly by (λ − λ₁) using the stated root, then solve the quadratic.
Coefficient of λ³ is −1 (from expanding det(A − λI) directly)
Multiply through by −1 first so leading coefficient is +1, making sign tracking and the rational root search cleaner.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the cubic as if it must factor by grouping and giving up when it doesn't, instead of trying integer roots.
  • Confusing the characteristic polynomial with the minimal polynomial (out of scope) and looking for unnecessary structure.
  • Reading a 'show that λ = k is an eigenvalue' prompt as requiring full expansion, when substituting into p(λ) suffices.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when evaluating p(λ) at negative trial values, leading to rejection of a correct root.
  • Arithmetic slips during polynomial division producing a wrong quadratic factor.
  • Forgetting to find all three eigenvalues — particularly missing a repeated root.
  • Not checking λ₁ + λ₂ + λ₃ = tr(A), which would catch most arithmetic errors immediately.

A-level Scope Notes

This is the standard FP3 technique for diagonalising 3×3 matrices in Edexcel IAL Further Maths; eigenvalues are designed to be integers or simple rationals, so trial of divisors of the constant term always succeeds within exam time.
