Algebra & Functions

Factor out lowest power of common base to combine product-rule terms

Family: Lowest Power Factorisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires after differentiation (product or chain rule) or integration by parts produces a sum of two terms sharing a common base raised to different fractional/negative powers, e.g. $2x^3(4x-1)^{-1/2} + 3x^2(4x-1)^{1/2}$. The instruction asks for a 'single fraction', 'simplified form', or 'fully factorised' answer. Look for shared variable powers ($x^3, x^2$) and shared bracket powers with one positive and one negative half-integer exponent.

Motivation — Why this works

Factoring out the algebraically lowest power converts every remaining exponent into a non-negative integer, so the bracket becomes a clean polynomial. This works because $a^m / a^n = a^{m-n}$: dividing by the lowest power adds a positive amount to every other exponent. The result is a fully factorised expression that can be written as a single fraction by moving the negative-power factor to the denominator, avoiding the algebraic mess of rationalising surds.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice two terms sharing $x$-powers and $(4x-1)$-powers, with one bracket exponent negative and one positive — a classic post-product-rule shape.
  2. I rule out putting everything over $\sqrt{4x-1}$ as a common denominator: it works but multiplies clutter and risks sign errors; factoring is cleaner.
  3. I commit to factoring out the lowest power of each common factor: $x^2$ (the smaller of $x^3, x^2$) and $(4x-1)^{-1/2}$ (the smaller of $-1/2, 1/2$).
  4. Bridge: write the expression as $x^2(4x-1)^{-1/2}[\ldots + \ldots]$, computing each bracket entry by subtracting the factored-out exponents.
  5. Inside the bracket, the second term gains exponent $1/2 - (-1/2) = 1$, so $(4x-1)^{1/2}$ becomes $(4x-1)$ — a polynomial. Expand and collect to finish.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the lowest power of each common factor: here $x^2$ and $(4x-1)^{-1/2}$.
  2. Write the expression as (lowest powers)$\times$[bracket], subtracting exponents term by term: $x^2(4x-1)^{-1/2}[2x \cdot (4x-1)^0 + 3 \cdot (4x-1)^1]$.
  3. Simplify the bracket to a polynomial: $2x + 3(4x-1) = 14x - 3$.
  4. Move the negative-power factor to the denominator: $\dfrac{x^2(14x-3)}{\sqrt{4x-1}}$.

Worked Micro-Example

Differentiate $y = x^3\sqrt{4x-1}$. Product + chain rule: $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2(4x-1)^{1/2} + x^3 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2}(4x-1)^{-1/2}\cdot 4 = 3x^2(4x-1)^{1/2} + 2x^3(4x-1)^{-1/2}$. Factor out $x^2(4x-1)^{-1/2}$: $= x^2(4x-1)^{-1/2}[3(4x-1) + 2x] = \dfrac{x^2(14x-3)}{\sqrt{4x-1}}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

After differentiating $y = x^2(2x+3)^{3/2}$ via product rule
Factor out $x(2x+3)^{1/2}$ (lowest power of each); bracket becomes $2(2x+3) + 3x \cdot 1 = 7x + 6$.
Quotient rule output like $\dfrac{(x+1)^{1/2} - x(x+1)^{-1/2}}{(x+1)}$ after simplifying the numerator
Factor $(x+1)^{-1/2}$ from the numerator first, then cancel against denominator.
Integration by parts result $x(2x-1)^{3/2} - \int (2x-1)^{3/2}\,dx$ partially simplified to two surd terms
Factor out the lowest power of $(2x-1)$ to combine before final simplification.
Implicit differentiation giving $2x(y^2+1)^{1/2} + x^2 y(y^2+1)^{-1/2}\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ on one side
Same idea: factor $(y^2+1)^{-1/2}$ to clear the negative exponent before solving for $dy/dx$.

Common Misreadings

  • Factoring out the highest power $(4x-1)^{1/2}$ instead of the lowest, leaving $(4x-1)^{-1}$ inside the bracket and undoing the simplification.
  • Rationalising by multiplying numerator and denominator by $\sqrt{4x-1}$ before factoring — valid but slower and error-prone.
  • Treating $(4x-1)^{-1/2}$ as 'too messy to factor' and leaving the answer as a sum of two surd terms, losing exam marks for 'fully simplified'.

Pitfalls

  • Subtracting exponents incorrectly: $(4x-1)^{1/2} \div (4x-1)^{-1/2} = (4x-1)^{1}$, not $(4x-1)^{0}$.
  • Forgetting to expand the bracket: $3(4x-1) = 12x - 3$, not $12x - 1$.
  • Dropping the $x^2$ factor outside while focused on the $(4x-1)$ manipulation.
  • Sign error when the lowest power is more negative than students expect (e.g. $-3/2$ vs $-1/2$).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard simplification step in Edexcel IAL P3/P4 differentiation (product/chain rule with surds) and FP1/FP2 integration by parts; uses only index laws and polynomial expansion within syllabus.
