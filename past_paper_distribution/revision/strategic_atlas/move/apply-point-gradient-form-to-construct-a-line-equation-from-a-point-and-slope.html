Coordinate Geometry

Apply point-gradient form to construct a line equation from a point and slope

Family: Line Equation Construction Frequency: 12 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever you have (i) a specific point (x₁, y₁) on the line and (ii) the line's gradient m, and you are asked to produce the equation of that line. Typical surface cues: 'find the equation of the tangent/normal/perpendicular bisector at...', 'find the equation of line l through M with gradient...', or two points have just been used to compute a midpoint/intersection plus a gradient has been derived (e.g. via differentiation, perpendicularity, or the two-point gradient formula). Equivalent phrasings: 'show that l has equation ax + by + c = 0', 'find the equation of the line in the form y = mx + c'.

Motivation — Why this works

The point-gradient form y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) is the unique line of slope m passing through (x₁, y₁), and it ingests exactly the two pieces of data you have in one substitution. The alternative — starting from y = mx + c, substituting the point to solve for c, then rewriting — adds an unnecessary solve step. The form is essentially the definition of slope ((y − y₁)/(x − x₁) = m) cleared of its denominator, so it is the most direct algebraic route whenever a point and gradient are simultaneously available.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the two ingredients on the table: a numerical gradient m and a concrete point (x₁, y₁) on the target line.
  2. Rule out y = mx + c then solve for c: workable but requires an extra rearrangement, with more chances for sign error.
  3. Rule out the two-point form (y − y₁)/(y₂ − y₁) = (x − x₁)/(x₂ − x₁): only relevant if you have two points and have not yet computed m.
  4. Commit to point-gradient form because it absorbs both inputs in a single substitution.
  5. Tactical choice: if two points were available and you computed m, pick the point with simpler (especially non-negative) coordinates to keep the arithmetic clean.
  6. Bridge to action: write y − y₁ = m(x − x₁), then expand, then rearrange to whichever final form (y = mx + c or ax + by + c = 0 with integer coefficients) the question demands.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify (x₁, y₁) and m clearly; write them down to avoid mis-substitution.
  2. Substitute into y − y₁ = m(x − x₁), being careful with negative coordinates: y − (−3) becomes y + 3, x − (−5) becomes x + 5.
  3. Expand the right-hand side: distribute m through (x − x₁).
  4. Rearrange to the required form: for y = mx + c isolate y; for ax + by + c = 0 collect all terms on one side and clear fractions to get integer coefficients (with a > 0 if the mark scheme demands).
  5. (Optional check) Substitute (x₁, y₁) back into the final equation to verify it satisfies it.

Worked Micro-Example

Given point $M(5,2)$ and gradient $m=3$, find the line in the form $ax+by+c=0$. Write $y-2 = 3(x-5)$. Expand: $y-2 = 3x-15$. Rearrange: $3x - y - 13 = 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two points known, gradient not yet computed
First compute m = (y₂ − y₁)/(x₂ − x₁), then apply point-gradient form using whichever point has cleaner coordinates.
Line must be perpendicular/parallel to a given line
Take m of the given line, replace with −1/m (perpendicular) or keep it (parallel), then proceed identically.
Final answer required as y = mx + c (e.g. for sketching or substitution into a curve)
After expanding, isolate y rather than collecting to ax + by + c = 0.
Tangent/normal at a point on a curve
Obtain m from dy/dx evaluated at x₁ (tangent) or its negative reciprocal (normal); the construction step is unchanged.

Common Misreadings

  • Substituting endpoint coordinates A or B instead of the midpoint M when the line is the perpendicular bisector.
  • Reading 'normal' as 'tangent' (or vice versa) and using the wrong gradient.
  • When two points are given, mixing up which is (x₁, y₁) versus (x₂, y₂) and computing −m instead of m.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors with negative coordinates: x − (−5) must become (x + 5), not (x − 5).
  • Distribution slip when expanding m(x − x₁), especially with fractional or negative m (e.g. −(5/4)(x+5)).
  • When converting to ax + by + c = 0, leaving non-integer coefficients or a negative leading coefficient when the mark scheme requires integers and a > 0.
  • Forgetting to move all terms to one side, leaving the equation in mixed form.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P1/P2 coordinate geometry; the formula y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) is given in the formula booklet. Stays within straight-line geometry — do not over-generalise to curves, and note that vertical lines (undefined gradient) require the separate form x = x₁.
