Sequences & Series

Apply arithmetic nth-term formula to obtain a linear expression in n

Family: Nth Term Formula Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question explicitly asks for the value of a term, or a closed-form expression for the general term, of an arithmetic sequence. Cues include phrases like 'amount saved in week n', 'find an expression for u_n', or 'show that the nth term is...'. The first term a and common difference d are either given or can be read off from the context (e.g. starts at £5, increases by £0.25 each week). It also fires when a later part of the question will need u_n substituted into a sum or inequality.

Motivation — Why this works

The arithmetic nth-term formula u_n = a + (n−1)d encodes the defining property of an AP: each term is obtained from the first by adding d a total of (n−1) times. Substituting known a and d converts the recursive description into a closed form, which is a linear function of n. The linear form pn + q is the most flexible representation: it can be evaluated, summed, or inserted into an inequality without further reformulation. Keeping it tidy avoids arithmetic slips downstream.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the sequence increases (or decreases) by a fixed amount each step, so it is arithmetic with identifiable a and d.
  2. Rule out the geometric formula ar^{n−1} and any recursive-only description, since these don't give a clean closed form for substitution later.
  3. Commit to u_n = a + (n−1)d because it is the standard AP closed form and produces a linear expression suitable for subsequent parts.
  4. Bridge to the first operation by substituting the specific values of a and d into the formula.
  5. Decide tactically to expand and simplify into the form pn + q (rather than leaving as a + (n−1)d), since this is the form needed for sums, inequalities, or numerical evaluation.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify a (first term) and d (common difference) from the context.
  2. Write u_n = a + (n−1)d.
  3. Substitute the numerical values: u_n = a + (n−1)·d.
  4. Expand the bracket: u_n = a + dn − d.
  5. Collect constants: u_n = (a − d) + dn, presented in a clean linear form.

Worked Micro-Example

A saver deposits £5 in week 1 and increases the deposit by £0.25 each week. Then a = 5, d = 0.25, so u_n = 5 + (n−1)(0.25) = 5 + 0.25n − 0.25 = 4.75 + 0.25n. Hence the amount saved in week n is £(4.75 + 0.25n).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Geometric sequence with first term a and common ratio r
Use u_n = ar^{n−1} instead; the result is exponential in n, not linear.
AP described by 'kth term is X and mth term is Y'
Form two equations a + (k−1)d = X and a + (m−1)d = Y, solve simultaneously for a and d, then write u_n.
Decreasing AP (e.g. quantity reduces each period)
Take d as negative; the linear expression has negative gradient, e.g. u_n = a − (n−1)|d|.
Sequence indexed from n = 0 rather than n = 1
Use u_n = a + nd (no shift), being careful which term corresponds to which index.
'Show that' style question giving a target form pn + q
After expansion, match coefficients of n and the constant to the required p and q.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating a constant ratio (multiplicative) situation as arithmetic, leading to a wrong linear formula instead of u_n = ar^{n−1}.
  • Mis-identifying the first term, e.g. taking the week-0 value as a when the question indexes from week 1.
  • Reading d off the wrong direction (sign error) when the sequence decreases.
  • Confusing u_n with the sum S_n and applying the AP sum formula instead.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the (n−1) and writing u_n = a + nd, which off-sets every term by one position.
  • Failing to simplify to pn + q, leaving u_n in a form that is awkward to substitute later.
  • Sign errors when expanding (n−1)d with negative d.
  • Arithmetic slips with non-integer d (e.g. 0.25), producing incorrect constants like 5.25 instead of 4.75.
  • Substituting a specific n too early before establishing the general expression the question asked for.

A-level Scope Notes

This is the standard Edexcel IAL P1/Core arithmetic sequences result u_n = a + (n−1)d; all manipulations stay within elementary algebra and remain inside the syllabus.
