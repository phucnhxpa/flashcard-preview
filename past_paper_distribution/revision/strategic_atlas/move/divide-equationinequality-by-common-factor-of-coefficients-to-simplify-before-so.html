Algebra & Functions

Divide equation/inequality by common factor of coefficients to simplify before solving

Family: Strategic Simplification Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a polynomial equation or inequality is set equal to zero (or to another expression that is also divisible) and all coefficients share a non-trivial common factor. Visual cues: large coefficients (e.g. 256, 1280, 1024), all coefficients clearly even, all divisible by a small prime (3, 5, 7), or a negative leading coefficient combined with other negatives suggesting division by a negative common factor. Also fires when a 'show that' target form has leading coefficient 1, signalling that division to monic form is intended. Equivalent phrasings: 'extract the HCF of coefficients', 'reduce to monic form', 'cancel the common factor across the whole equation'.

Motivation — Why this works

Dividing every term by the GCF (or by a signed GCF that also makes the leading coefficient positive/equal to 1) preserves the solution set of the equation and — provided the divisor is positive — preserves the direction of an inequality. The mathematical principle is simply that ax² + bx + c = 0 is equivalent to (a/k)x² + (b/k)x + (c/k) = 0 for any non-zero k. The payoff is dramatic: the product ac that drives the splitting-the-middle-term factorisation shrinks (often by a factor of k²), the discriminant b² − 4ac under the square root becomes manageable, and arithmetic error risk drops sharply. For inequalities, monic form also makes sketching the parabola and reading off the solution interval immediate.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Inspect the three coefficients and test for a common factor — start with small primes (2, 3, 5) and powers of 10.
  2. Rule out attempting to factorise the unsimplified quadratic directly: the product ac is large, so the factor-pair search is wide and error-prone; rule out the quadratic formula as overkill if rational roots are likely.
  3. Decide on the sign of the divisor: if the leading coefficient is negative, divide by the negative GCF so the result is monic with positive leading term — but check that ALL signs flip correctly.
  4. If the equation is an inequality, confirm the divisor's sign: positive ⇒ keep ≤/≥; negative ⇒ flip the inequality.
  5. Commit to dividing every single term (LHS and RHS if RHS ≠ 0) by the chosen factor, then proceed with factorisation or the formula on the simpler quadratic.
  6. Sanity check by substituting one term back, e.g. confirm 1280/256 = 5 or −36400/(−10) = 3640 before continuing.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify GCF: g = gcd(|a|, |b|, |c|); optionally take −g if the leading coefficient is negative and you want a monic positive form.
  2. Check sign: if dividing an inequality, ensure g > 0 (else flip the inequality direction).
  3. Divide each term: ax²/g + bx/g + c/g (= 0 or ≤ 0).
  4. Write down the simplified equation/inequality and verify each division arithmetically.
  5. Proceed to factorise, complete the square, or apply the quadratic formula on the reduced form.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $256k^{2} - 1280k + 1024 \le 0$. Note $\gcd(256,1280,1024)=256>0$, so divide through: $k^{2} - 5k + 4 \le 0$. Factorise: $(k-1)(k-4) \le 0$, giving $1 \le k \le 4$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Negative leading coefficient, e.g. −10n² + 1210n − 36400 = 0
Divide by −10 (not just 10) so the result is monic with positive leading term: n² − 121n + 3640 = 0. Carefully flip the sign of every term.
Quadratic inequality with negative common factor
Dividing by a negative reverses the inequality direction; if you'd rather keep the direction, divide by the positive GCF and live with a negative leading coefficient (or multiply by −1 separately and flip).
GCF not immediately obvious
Partial simplification still helps — e.g. divide by 2 first, then check whether the result has a further common factor.
Discriminant calculation b² − 4ac
Simplifying the quadratic first keeps the number under the square root small and reduces arithmetic error.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the large coefficients as a signal to jump straight to the quadratic formula, missing the cheap simplification.
  • Believing the equation must be left in its 'natural' expanded form (e.g. as it came out of a modelling step) rather than simplified.
  • Confusing factoring out a common factor (e.g. writing 2(5x² + 9x − 2) = 0) with dividing through — both are valid here because RHS = 0, but only division generalises when RHS ≠ 0.

Pitfalls

  • Dividing only some terms by the common factor and not others, breaking the equation's balance.
  • Dividing an inequality by a negative number without flipping the inequality sign.
  • Sign errors when the divisor is negative — e.g. forgetting that −36400/(−10) = +3640.
  • Arithmetic slips on a single term (e.g. miscomputing 1280/256) — always verify each quotient.
  • Assuming you may divide both sides of an equation by a common factor when one side contains a variable that could be zero (here we are dividing by a numerical constant, which is always safe).

A-level Scope Notes

Wholly within Edexcel IAL P1 quadratic methods (factorisation, completing the square, quadratic formula) and P1/P2 quadratic inequalities. No formula booklet entry is required — this is a basic algebraic equivalence. Do not generalise to dividing by an algebraic expression that may equal zero (that would risk losing solutions, which is a separate, more advanced concern).
