Integration

Collapse square root of perfect square to linearise integrand

Family: Radical Elimination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in arc length or surface area problems once 1 + (dy/dx)² (or (dx/dt)² + (dy/dt)²) has been algebraically rearranged into a perfect square (A + B)². Typical cues: the expression under the root contains a sum of three terms whose middle term is exactly 2AB, or the curve involves ln x, eˣ, or fractional powers that famously give such structure (e.g. y = x²/4 − (ln x)/2). Recognising the square form is the signal to drop the radical and reduce the integral to elementary terms.

Motivation — Why this works

If 1 + (dy/dx)² = (A + B)² with A + B > 0 on the integration interval, then √((A+B)²) = A + B exactly, with no absolute value needed. This converts an otherwise intractable radical integral into a sum of standard power/log/exp integrals which can be tackled term-by-term. The identity is just (a+b)² = a² + 2ab + b², applied in reverse — no calculus tool beyond A-level is required.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the expression under the root has three terms and the outer two are perfect squares (e.g. x²/4 and 1/(4x²)), prompting a perfect-square check.
  2. Rule out trig substitution, hyperbolic substitution, or numerical methods — these are unnecessary once the perfect square is spotted, and trig substitution is out of scope here anyway.
  3. Commit when the cross term 2AB matches the middle term exactly (e.g. 2·(x/2)·(1/(2x)) = 1/2), confirming (A+B)² structure.
  4. Verify A + B > 0 on the domain so √((A+B)²) = A + B without absolute value or case-splitting.
  5. Replace the radical with A + B, set up the definite integral, and integrate each term using elementary antiderivatives.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute dy/dx and form 1 + (dy/dx)².
  2. Recognise the result as (A + B)² where A and B are simple monomials or log/exp terms.
  3. Check A + B > 0 on [a, b]; then √(1 + (dy/dx)²) = A + B.
  4. Write L = ∫_a^b (A + B) dx.
  5. Integrate term-by-term using standard antiderivatives (powers, 1/x → ln x, eˣ, etc.).
  6. Evaluate at the limits and combine logarithms or simplify surds for exact form.

Worked Micro-Example

For y = x²/4 − (ln x)/2 on [1, 2]: dy/dx = x/2 − 1/(2x), so 1 + (dy/dx)² = 1 + x²/4 − 1/2 + 1/(4x²) = x²/4 + 1/2 + 1/(4x²) = (x/2 + 1/(2x))². Since x > 0, √(...) = x/2 + 1/(2x). Then L = ∫_1^2 (x/2 + 1/(2x)) dx = [x²/4 + (ln x)/2]_1^2 = 3/4 + (ln 2)/2.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Surface area of revolution about the x-axis
Integrand becomes 2πy·(A+B); expand the product and integrate each resulting term separately.
Parametric arc length √((dx/dt)² + (dy/dt)²)
Same idea: rearrange into (A(t) + B(t))² and collapse to A(t) + B(t), checking positivity on the t-interval.
Curves involving eˣ, e.g. y = (eˣ + e⁻ˣ)/2
1 + (dy/dx)² simplifies to ((eˣ + e⁻ˣ)/2)², giving a clean exponential integrand.
Difference-of-squares structure (A − B)²
Identical method, but verify the sign of A − B on the interval — if negative, take B − A instead.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the radical as irreducible and reaching for substitution before testing for perfect-square structure.
  • Dropping √((A+B)²) to A + B without checking positivity, missing a needed |·| or sign flip.
  • Confusing 1 + (dy/dx)² with (1 + dy/dx)² and squaring incorrectly.

Pitfalls

  • Losing the factor 1/2 on the 1/(2x) term when integrating to (ln x)/2.
  • Failing to combine ln a − ln b into ln(a/b) when 'exact form' is required.
  • Arithmetic slips when verifying the cross term equals 2AB — a sign error here invalidates the whole collapse.
  • Forgetting to apply the limits to both the polynomial and the logarithmic part.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope: arc length and surface area formulas, plus standard integrals of xⁿ, 1/x, and eˣ. No hyperbolic or trigonometric substitution is invoked.
