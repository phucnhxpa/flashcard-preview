Choose factorisation over the quadratic formula to solve

Family: Factorisation To Solve Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A quadratic equation (or quadratic-in-disguise) is set equal to zero with small integer coefficients whose discriminant is a perfect square. The question stem says 'solve by factorising', or the mark scheme tag explicitly rewards factorisation. Cues include monic quadratics x² + bx + c with c easy to factor pair, or non-monic ax² + bx + c with a, c admitting only a few factor pairs. Also fires when a substitution reduces a higher-degree or transcendental equation to a tractable quadratic in u.

Motivation — Why this works

If a quadratic factorises over the integers, the zero-product principle reduces solving to two linear equations, which is faster and surd-free compared with the quadratic formula. A perfect-square discriminant guarantees rational roots, so factor pairs of the constant term (or of a·c for non-monic) must reproduce the middle coefficient. Choosing factorisation also satisfies method-mark requirements when the question demands it, whereas the formula would forfeit those marks. It additionally avoids arithmetic slips from evaluating √(b² − 4ac).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation is quadratic (or becomes quadratic after a substitution) and is already, or can be, set to zero.
  2. Rule out the quadratic formula and completing the square because the question instructs 'factorise' or because the integer coefficients make factor pairs obvious.
  3. Commit to factorisation after a quick discriminant or factor-pair sanity check confirming rational roots.
  4. Bridge: classify as monic vs non-monic, then enumerate factor pairs of c (monic) or of a·c (non-monic) to find the pair summing to b.
  5. Tactically decide sign conventions from the signs of b and c, then write the bracketed form and read off roots via the zero-product rule.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rearrange the equation to the form ax² + bx + c = 0.
  2. Identify whether the quadratic is monic (a = 1) or non-monic (a ≠ 1).
  3. Monic case: find integers p, q with p + q = b and pq = c; write (x + p)(x + q) = 0.
  4. Non-monic case: find integers m, n, p, q with mn = a, pq = c, mq + np = b; write (mx + p)(nx + q) = 0. (Or use the a·c method: split bx into two terms whose coefficients multiply to ac and sum to b, then factor by grouping.)
  5. Apply the zero-product principle: set each bracket to zero and solve the resulting linear equations.
  6. State the solutions clearly, and if a substitution was used, back-substitute to recover the original variable.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $2x^2 - 5x - 3 = 0$. Seek $mq + np = -5$ with $mn = 2$, $pq = -3$: try $(2x + 1)(x - 3) = 2x^2 - 6x + x - 3 = 2x^2 - 5x - 3$. ✓ So $(2x + 1)(x - 3) = 0$ gives $x = -\tfrac{1}{2}$ or $x = 3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Disguised quadratic in an exponential, e.g. $e^{2x} + 5e^{x} + 6 = 0$
Substitute $u = e^{x}$, factorise $u^2 + 5u + 6 = (u+2)(u+3)$, then solve $e^{x} = -2$ (reject) or $e^{x} = -3$ (reject); choose examples where roots are positive.
Quadratic in a trig function, e.g. $2\sin^2\theta - \sin\theta - 1 = 0$
Treat $\sin\theta$ as the variable: $(2\sin\theta + 1)(\sin\theta - 1) = 0$, then solve each trig equation over the given interval.
Quadratic in a logarithm, e.g. $(\ln x)^2 - 3\ln x + 2 = 0$
Let $u = \ln x$, factorise $(u-1)(u-2) = 0$, then exponentiate: $x = e$ or $x = e^2$.
Quartic reducible to quadratic, e.g. $x^4 - 5x^2 + 4 = 0$
Let $u = x^2$, factorise $(u-1)(u-4) = 0$, recover $x = \pm 1, \pm 2$.
Polynomial division has reduced a cubic to a quadratic factor
After factor theorem extracts $(x - \alpha)$, factorise the remaining quadratic by inspection rather than reaching for the formula.

Common Misreadings

  • Defaulting to the quadratic formula when the question explicitly says 'by factorisation', losing method marks.
  • Treating a non-monic quadratic as monic and writing $(x + p)(x + q)$ with $pq = c$ but ignoring the leading coefficient.
  • Failing to recognise a disguised quadratic and trying to factorise the original transcendental expression directly.
  • Stopping at the bracket form $(mx+p)(nx+q) = 0$ without stating the actual roots.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors in factor pairs: when $c > 0$ and $b < 0$, both factors must be negative; when $c < 0$, factors have opposite signs.
  • For non-monic quadratics, testing only one arrangement of factor pairs of $a$ and missing the correct cross-product.
  • Forgetting to back-substitute after a substitution $u = e^x$, $u = \sin\theta$, or $u = x^2$.
  • Discarding valid roots or keeping invalid ones (e.g. $e^x < 0$, $|\sin\theta| > 1$) after back-substitution.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1–P4 and FP1: factorising integer-coefficient quadratics, quadratics in disguise via substitution, and quadratic factors arising after the factor theorem. Roots requiring surds or complex numbers are routed to the formula or completing the square instead.
