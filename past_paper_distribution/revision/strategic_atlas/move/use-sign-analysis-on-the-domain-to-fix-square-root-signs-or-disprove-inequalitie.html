Proof

Use sign analysis on the domain to fix square-root signs or disprove inequalities

Family: Sign Analysis Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever the working forces a decision about the SIGN of a quantity: (a) taking a square root of a squared trig expression like √(sin²(2y)) where you must pick + or −, or (b) deciding whether a sum/expression like k + 9/k can meet a bound. Visual cues include a stated domain (e.g. 0 < y < π/4, k < 0), the appearance of |·| implicitly via √(·²), or a 'show that' / 'explain why' instruction that demands explicit justification of sign. If you ever write '= ±…' and then need to choose, this move is the tool.

Motivation — Why this works

Square roots and reciprocals are sign-ambiguous: √(a²) = |a|, and 1/k inherits the sign of k. The domain restriction collapses the ambiguity by pinning the variable to a region where every relevant quantity has a known, single sign. This converts |·| into ±(·) cleanly, or shows a sum of like-signed terms cannot cross a threshold. The principle is simply: in 'show that' / 'explain' work, sign claims must be argued from the domain, not assumed.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the step requires committing to a sign — either taking √ of a square, or comparing an expression to a bound — and that the question has supplied a domain restriction (an interval for y, or k < 0).
  2. Rule out brute alternatives: squaring everything to avoid the sign issue (loses information), or giving a single counter-example when the question asks WHY (incomplete for 'explain').
  3. Commit to sign analysis because the domain pins down the sign of each component (sin, cos, k, 9/k) categorically across the whole interval.
  4. Translate the domain into derived domains: e.g. 0 < y < π/4 ⟹ 0 < 2y < π/2 (first quadrant), or k < 0 ⟹ 9/k < 0.
  5. Read off the sign of each trig function or term from the quadrant / sign rules.
  6. Make the sign choice explicit in writing: 'since 2y is in Q1, sin(2y) > 0, hence we take the positive root', or 'k < 0 and 9/k < 0, so their sum is < 0 < 6'.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the ambiguous step: √(f(x)²) = ±f(x), or comparing g(k) to a bound.
  2. Restate the given domain and derive any consequential domain (e.g. double the interval for 2y, or note k and 9/k share sign).
  3. Determine the sign of each relevant quantity on that domain, citing quadrant / arithmetic of signs.
  4. Resolve the ambiguity: write sin(2y) = +√(x/6), cos(2y) = +√((6−x)/6), or k + 9/k < 0.
  5. Conclude with the consequence (substitute into the next line of the proof, or state the inequality fails).
  6. Optional: support with a numerical check (e.g. k = −3 gives −6 < 6) to reinforce the categorical argument.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $00$. Hence $\sin(2y)=+\sqrt{x/6}$ (positive root chosen because $2y$ is in Q1). Similarly $\cos(2y)=+\sqrt{(6-x)/6}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

y lies in (π/4, π/2), so 2y ∈ (π/2, π) (second quadrant)
sin(2y) > 0 still, but cos(2y) < 0; take cos(2y) = −√((6−x)/6).
Disproving an inequality for k < 0 where the expression is k + c/k with c > 0
Both terms negative ⟹ sum negative ⟹ inequality ≥ positive bound automatically fails.
Modulus / complex expressions where 'sign' is replaced by magnitude
Replace sign argument with a |·| bound, but the structure (use the constraint to pin down the relevant quantity) is identical.

Common Misreadings

  • Writing sin(2y) = √(x/6) without justifying the positive root — examiner deducts marks in 'show that'.
  • Offering only a single counter-example (e.g. k = −3 gives −6) for 'explain why ≥ 6 fails for all k < 0' without the general sign argument.
  • Pointing to where the part (a) proof method breaks down rather than addressing the inequality itself.

Pitfalls

  • Mentioning the domain but not explicitly linking it to the sign of the trig function or term — the connection must be written out.
  • Assuming both square roots are positive without checking the quadrant — fails when the domain places 2y outside Q1.
  • Confusing 'why the proof fails' with 'why the inequality fails' — the sign argument addresses the inequality directly.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits within Edexcel IAL Pure (P2/P3 trig identities, P3/P4 proof) and Further Pure inequality work. No formula booklet entry needed; relies on the quadrant sign chart for sin/cos/tan and elementary sign arithmetic. Avoid invoking complex-analytic branch cuts — square-root sign choice here is purely real-analysis on a stated interval.
