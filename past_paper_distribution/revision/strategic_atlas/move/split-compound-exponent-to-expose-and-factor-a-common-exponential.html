Split compound exponent to expose and factor a common exponential

Family: Exponent Law Factoring Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation or expression contains two (or more) exponential terms whose exponents differ only by an additive constant, e.g. e^{a+kx} and e^{kx}, or 3^{2x+1} and 3^{2x}. Cues include: same base, exponents that share a variable part plus a constant offset, and a need to solve for the variable. Also applies when simplifying sums/integrals of the form A·e^{c+f(x)} + B·e^{f(x)}.

Motivation — Why this works

The index law a^{m+n} = a^m · a^n lets any constant offset in an exponent be peeled off as a numerical multiplier. Once peeled, every term carries the same exponential factor, which can be pulled out, collapsing a multi-term exponential equation into one of the form (constant)·e^{f(x)} = RHS. That single-exponential form is solvable in one ln-step. The move trades exponent manipulation (hard to undo) for ordinary linear factoring (easy).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the two exponential terms share the same variable exponent up to an additive constant — a structural symmetry.
  2. Rules out adding the exponentials directly (no law lets e^a + e^b combine) and rules out immediate ln (taking ln of a sum is not useful).
  3. Commits to factoring because once a common exponential is exposed, the remaining equation is linear in that exponential.
  4. Bridges by applying e^{a+b} = e^a · e^b to the term with the offset, turning the constant-shift into an explicit numerical coefficient.
  5. Tactically keeps any leading constants (like 15) attached to their exponentials during factoring, and writes the bracketed factor (e^{-1} + 1) as a single number before isolating.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the common variable exponent (here -0.2T) and the additive constant offsets in the others (here -1).
  2. Apply the index law: rewrite e^{-1-0.2T} as e^{-1} · e^{-0.2T}.
  3. Factor the common exponential: 15e^{-0.2T}(e^{-1} + 1) = 7.5.
  4. Isolate the single exponential: e^{-0.2T} = 7.5 / [15(e^{-1} + 1)] = 1/[2(1 + e^{-1})].
  5. Take ln of both sides and solve for the variable: -0.2T = -ln[2(1+e^{-1})], so T = 5 ln[2(1+e^{-1})].

Worked Micro-Example

Solve 3^{2x+1} + 3^{2x} = 12. Split: 3^{2x+1} = 3 · 3^{2x}. Factor: 3^{2x}(3 + 1) = 12, so 3^{2x} = 3. Hence 2x = 1 and x = 1/2.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two exponentials with the same base and offset exponents, e.g. 3^{2x+1} + 3^{2x} = 12
Pull out 3^{2x} after writing 3^{2x+1} = 3·3^{2x}; solve the resulting linear equation in 3^{2x}.
Geometric series term a^{n+k} inside a sum
Factor out a^k so the summand becomes a^k · a^n, exposing the standard geometric ratio a.
Integral of the form ∫(e^{c+kx} + e^{kx})dx
Rewrite as (e^c + 1)e^{kx} before integrating, giving (e^c+1)·e^{kx}/k + C.
Differential equation solutions like Ae^{kt} + Be^{kt+c}
Combine to (A + Be^c)e^{kt} to read off a single effective amplitude.
Logarithmic mirror: ln(x) + ln(x) + ln(a) terms
Use ln-laws (the additive analogue) to combine before solving — same structural idea in additive form.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating e^{a+b} as e^a + e^b, or trying to add the two exponentials by summing exponents.
  • Reading the equation as quadratic in e^{-0.2T} when it is actually linear after factoring.
  • Assuming ln can be applied directly to a sum of exponentials without first combining them.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the leading coefficient (e.g. the 15) when factoring, producing the wrong RHS.
  • Sign slips when separating e^{-1-0.2T}: writing e^{1}·e^{-0.2T} instead of e^{-1}·e^{-0.2T}.
  • Leaving (e^{-1} + 1) un-evaluated and then mishandling it as e^0 or 1 when isolating.
  • Forgetting the negative sign on the exponent when finally taking ln, giving the wrong sign for the variable.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 exponential and logarithm work; uses only the index law e^{a+b}=e^a·e^b and basic ln-solving, with no calculus required.
