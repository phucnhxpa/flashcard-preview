Algebra & Functions

Clear fractional gradient by multiplying through by the denominator

Family: Integer Coefficient Presentation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a line equation in point-slope form $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ has a fractional gradient $m = p/q$ and the question demands the answer in the form $ax + by + c = 0$ with integer coefficients (a standard Edexcel instruction). Also triggers when a perpendicular gradient $-q/p$ arises from negating-and-reciprocating an integer slope. Recognise the cue phrases 'where $a$, $b$, $c$ are integers' or 'in the form $ax + by + c = 0$'. The move is preferred whenever distributing the fraction first would create awkward fractional intermediate terms.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying both sides of $y - y_1 = (p/q)(x - x_1)$ by $q$ produces $q(y - y_1) = p(x - x_1)$, an equivalent equation (since $q \neq 0$) with integer coefficients on both sides before any expansion. This sidesteps the error-prone step of distributing a fraction across a bracket and then re-clearing denominators. The underlying principle is that scaling a linear equation by a non-zero constant preserves its solution set, so the line is unchanged. Doing this early keeps every subsequent arithmetic step in the integers, matching the form required by the mark scheme.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the gradient is a fraction like $25/4$ and that the target form requires integer $a$, $b$, $c$.
  2. Rules out expanding $(p/q)(x - x_1)$ directly, since this would produce fractional terms that must later be cleared anyway.
  3. Commits to clearing the denominator first because one multiplication handles the fraction globally and keeps later steps in $\mathbb{Z}$.
  4. Writes the point-slope equation, then multiplies both sides by $q$ to get $q(y - y_1) = p(x - x_1)$.
  5. Expands both brackets, collects all terms on one side, and orders them as $ax + by + c = 0$ with $a > 0$ if convention dictates.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the line in point-slope form $y - y_1 = (p/q)(x - x_1)$.
  2. Multiply both sides by $q$ to obtain $q(y - y_1) = p(x - x_1)$.
  3. Expand each side into integer linear expressions.
  4. Move all terms to one side to reach $ax + by + c = 0$.
  5. Optionally adjust signs so the leading coefficient is positive.

Worked Micro-Example

Tangent at $(4, 12)$ with gradient $25/4$: $y - 12 = \tfrac{25}{4}(x - 4)$. Multiply by $4$: $4(y - 12) = 25(x - 4)$. Expand: $4y - 48 = 25x - 100$. Rearrange: $25x - 4y - 52 = 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Normal line with gradient $-q/p$ (negative reciprocal of an integer slope)
Multiply through by $p$ instead; the sign of the resulting $x$- and $y$-coefficients flips accordingly.
Chord or secant joining two points where the gradient simplifies to $p/q$
Same procedure: form $y - y_1 = (p/q)(x - x_1)$ then multiply by $q$ before expanding.
Gradient is a fraction with surds cleared, e.g. $m = 3/2$ from implicit differentiation
Multiply by the denominator $2$; no further rationalisation needed.
Gradient already an integer but the constant is fractional after substitution
Multiply through by the LCD of the constants instead of the gradient's denominator.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $ax + by + c = 0$ as merely 'tidy' rather than a strict integer-coefficient requirement.
  • Believing the fraction must be distributed first before any clearing is allowed.
  • Confusing this with rationalising a denominator (an algebraic, not equation-scaling, operation).

Pitfalls

  • Multiplying only the right-hand side by $q$, leaving $y - y_1$ untouched.
  • Sign errors when moving $25x$ across, e.g. writing $-25x - 4y - 52 = 0$ then forgetting to negate.
  • Arithmetic slip in $-48 - (-100) = 52$ giving the wrong constant.
  • Failing to simplify by a common factor at the end (e.g. leaving $50x - 8y - 104 = 0$).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P1/P2 coordinate-geometry presentation requirement; appears routinely in tangent/normal questions in P2 differentiation and in straight-line work.
