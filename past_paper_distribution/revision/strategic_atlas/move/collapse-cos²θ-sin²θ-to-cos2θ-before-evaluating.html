Trigonometry

Collapse cos²θ − sin²θ to cos2θ before evaluating

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever the expression cos²θ − sin²θ (or an equivalent rearrangement like 2cos²θ − 1 or 1 − 2sin²θ) appears inside a derivative, integral, or expression to be evaluated at a specific angle. Common surface: after applying the product rule to sinθcosθ, the result cos²θ − sin²θ emerges. Also fires when an expression contains both cos²θ and sin²θ with opposite signs and equal coefficients. The cue is the squared-trig pair with a minus sign linking them.

Motivation — Why this works

The double-angle identity cos2θ = cos²θ − sin²θ converts a two-term squared expression into a single cosine of a doubled angle. This compresses subsequent arithmetic — one trig lookup instead of two squarings and a subtraction — and reveals structural simplifications (e.g. that the original sinθcosθ was really ½sin2θ all along). It also reduces the chance of sign errors when evaluating at standard angles like π/3, π/4, π/6.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the pattern cos²θ − sin²θ sitting inside my derivative, with matching coefficients and opposite signs.
  2. I rule out expanding cos²θ and sin²θ separately via cos²θ = (1+cos2θ)/2 and sin²θ = (1−cos2θ)/2 — that works but is heavier than needed.
  3. I commit to the direct identity cos²θ − sin²θ = cos2θ because it is the cleanest one-step substitution.
  4. I rewrite the expression with cos2θ in place of the squared pair, leaving any other terms untouched.
  5. When evaluating at θ = π/3, I compute cos(2π/3) = −½ as a single lookup rather than computing cos²(π/3) and sin²(π/3) separately.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the cos²θ − sin²θ subexpression within dy/dθ (or wherever it appears).
  2. Replace it with cos2θ using the double-angle identity.
  3. Rewrite the full expression, e.g. dy/dθ = 2cosθ + cos2θ.
  4. Substitute the required value of θ, computing cos2θ as a single evaluation.
  5. Combine with remaining terms to obtain the final value.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $y = 2\sin\theta + \sin\theta\cos\theta$, differentiate: $\dfrac{dy}{d\theta} = 2\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta$. Recognize $\cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta = \cos 2\theta$, so $\dfrac{dy}{d\theta} = 2\cos\theta + \cos 2\theta$. At $\theta = \pi/3$: $\dfrac{dy}{d\theta} = 2\cdot\tfrac{1}{2} + \cos\tfrac{2\pi}{3} = 1 - \tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{1}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Integrand contains cos²θ − sin²θ
Replace with cos2θ, then integrate to ½sin2θ + C.
Expression contains 2cos²θ − 1 or 1 − 2sin²θ
These are alternative forms of cos2θ; collapse them the same way.
Integrand contains 2sinθcosθ
Recognize as sin2θ (sister identity); integrate to −½cos2θ + C.
Pre-emptive rewrite before differentiating sinθcosθ
Rewrite sinθcosθ = ½sin2θ first, then differentiate to cos2θ directly, bypassing the product rule.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading cos²θ − sin²θ as cos2θ − sin2θ (dropping the squares but doubling the angle of each term).
  • Believing the identity is cos2θ = sin²θ − cos²θ (sign flipped).
  • Treating cos²θ − sin²θ as factoring to (cosθ − sinθ)(cosθ + sinθ) and stopping there, when cos2θ is more useful for evaluation.

Pitfalls

  • Confusing the three equivalent forms cos2θ = cos²θ − sin²θ = 2cos²θ − 1 = 1 − 2sin²θ and mixing them mid-calculation.
  • Forgetting that the angle inside cos becomes 2θ, then substituting θ = π/3 into cosθ rather than cos2θ.
  • Applying the identity to cos²θ + sin²θ (which is 1, not cos2θ) by misreading the sign.

A-level Scope Notes

Double-angle identities cos2θ = cos²θ − sin²θ = 2cos²θ − 1 = 1 − 2sin²θ are core Edexcel IAL Pure content (P3), and their use to simplify derivatives evaluated at standard angles is standard exam technique.
