Trigonometry

Drop perpendicular from circle point to form right triangle for central angle

Family: Right Triangle Construction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A point B lies on a circle of known radius centred at O, and you are given only one coordinate of B (or a perpendicular distance from a diameter/axis through O). The question asks for the central angle AOB (often in radians, frequently as a setup for arc length, sector area, or segment area). Cues include phrases like 'B lies on the circle', 'the perpendicular from B meets OA at M', or a diagram showing a foot of perpendicular. The radius and one leg are visible; the angle is not.

Motivation — Why this works

A perpendicular from B to the line OA creates a right angle at the foot M, so triangle OMB is right-angled with hypotenuse OB equal to the radius r. The given coordinate becomes a leg of this triangle, so the central angle is recovered by a single inverse trig ratio (cos = adj/hyp, sin = opp/hyp). This converts a circle-geometry configuration into elementary SOHCAHTOA, avoiding coordinate geometry or vector dot products. It is the natural bridge between Cartesian information and the angular measure needed for sector/segment formulas.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice B is on a circle of radius r and I only have one perpendicular distance from O, not the angle itself.
  2. I rule out using tan⁻¹(y/x) because I do not have both coordinates, and rule out the cosine rule because I only have one triangle side beyond the radius.
  3. I commit to building a right triangle by dropping a perpendicular from B to OA, since the radius will serve as the hypotenuse.
  4. I label the foot M, identify OM as the adjacent side and OB = r as the hypotenuse, and write cos(∠AOB) = OM/OB.
  5. I switch the calculator to radian mode (since the answer will feed into s = rθ or A = ½r²θ) and store the unrounded angle for downstream parts.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify M as the foot of the perpendicular from B to the line OA.
  2. Recognise triangle OMB as right-angled at M with hypotenuse OB = r.
  3. Match the given distance to the correct leg: adjacent (along OA) → cos, opposite (perpendicular to OA) → sin.
  4. Write the trig ratio, e.g. cos(∠AOB) = OM/OB.
  5. Apply the inverse function in radian mode and retain full precision: ∠AOB = arccos(OM/r).

Worked Micro-Example

Circle centre $O$, radius $4$. Point $B$ on the circle has its foot of perpendicular to $OA$ at $M$ with $OM = 1.5$. Then $\cos(\angle AOB) = \dfrac{OM}{OB} = \dfrac{1.5}{4} = 0.375$, so $\angle AOB = \arccos(0.375) \approx 1.186$ radians.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Only the perpendicular (y-coordinate) distance from B to OA is given.
Use sin(∠AOB) = opp/hyp and take arcsin instead of arccos.
Both coordinates of B relative to O are given.
Use tan(∠AOB) = y/x and take arctan (or atan2 for the correct quadrant).
The chord AB length is given instead of a perpendicular distance.
Bisect the isoceles triangle OAB with a perpendicular from O to AB; then sin(½∠AOB) = (½ chord)/r.
Angle is wanted in degrees (e.g. bearings/geometry context).
Switch calculator to degree mode; do not multiply by 180/π unless converting from a stored radian value.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the perpendicular distance as the opposite side and using sin when it is actually the adjacent side (or vice versa).
  • Reading 1.5 (or similar) as a chord length AB rather than the projected length OM.
  • Assuming the angle is at B or M instead of at O.

Pitfalls

  • Leaving the calculator in degree mode when the question demands radians (corrupts later s = rθ or area = ½r²θ calculations).
  • Rounding ∠AOB too early; later parts often reuse it and small errors compound.
  • Forgetting that arccos returns only the principal value in [0, π], which is fine here but fails if B is below OA and a directed angle is needed.

A-level Scope Notes
