Integration

Iterate two-step reduction formula down to base case

Family: Wallis Type Descent Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A specific value $I_n$ (e.g. $I_4$, $I_5$, $I_6$) of an integral family is requested, and an earlier part of the question has already established a recurrence of the form $I_n = \frac{n-1}{n}I_{n-2}$ (or similar two-step descent). Typical surfaces: $\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^n x\,dx$, $\int_0^{\pi/2}\cos^n x\,dx$, $\int_0^1 x^n e^{-x}\,dx$, $\int_0^1 x^n(1-x)^m\,dx$. The cue word is 'Hence find $I_k$' immediately after a 'Show that' part proving the recurrence.

Motivation — Why this works

The recurrence telescopes: each application reduces the index by 2 while contributing one rational factor, so after $\lfloor n/2\rfloor$ steps the index reaches a trivially computable base case ($I_0$ or $I_1$). The product of the successive fractions, multiplied by the base value, gives the exact closed form. This avoids ever computing an antiderivative for the high-power integrand directly.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the previous part has handed me a recurrence $I_n = \frac{n-1}{n}I_{n-2}$, and the current part asks for a specific numerical $I_k$.
  2. I rule out integrating $\sin^k x$ directly by parts again — the recurrence already encodes that work, so re-doing it wastes time.
  3. I commit to descent because the parity of $k$ tells me exactly where I land: even $k$ ends at $I_0$, odd $k$ ends at $I_1$, both elementary.
  4. First concrete step: write the chain $I_k = \frac{k-1}{k}\cdot\frac{k-3}{k-2}\cdots$ explicitly so I can spot the pattern and avoid arithmetic slips.
  5. Tactically I evaluate the base case once ($I_0=\pi/2$ or $I_1=1$ depending on the integrand and limits), then multiply the fraction chain in one line.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Determine parity of $n$ to fix the base case: even $\Rightarrow I_0$, odd $\Rightarrow I_1$.
  2. Compute the base case directly: e.g. $I_0=\int_0^{\pi/2}1\,dx=\frac{\pi}{2}$ or $I_1=\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin x\,dx=1$.
  3. Apply the recurrence repeatedly: $I_n=\frac{n-1}{n}I_{n-2}=\frac{n-1}{n}\cdot\frac{n-3}{n-2}I_{n-4}=\cdots$
  4. Stop at the base case and multiply all collected fractions together with the base value.
  5. Simplify the resulting product to a single closed form.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $I_n=\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^n x\,dx$ satisfies $I_n=\frac{n-1}{n}I_{n-2}$, find $I_4$.\newline $I_4=\tfrac{3}{4}I_2=\tfrac{3}{4}\cdot\tfrac{1}{2}I_0=\tfrac{3}{4}\cdot\tfrac{1}{2}\cdot\tfrac{\pi}{2}=\tfrac{3\pi}{16}.$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Odd index, e.g. $I_5$ for $\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^n x\,dx$
Descent ends at $I_1=1$: $I_5=\tfrac{4}{5}\cdot\tfrac{2}{3}\cdot 1=\tfrac{8}{15}$ (no $\pi$ in answer).
$\int_0^{\pi/2}\cos^n x\,dx$
Same recurrence and same numerical values by the symmetry $x\mapsto \tfrac{\pi}{2}-x$.
$I_n=\int_0^1 x^n e^{-x}\,dx$ with $I_n=nI_{n-1}-e^{-1}$
One-step (not two-step) descent; base case $I_0=1-e^{-1}$, and each step adds a constant rather than just multiplying.
$I_n=\int_0^1 x^n(1-x)^m\,dx$ via reduction in $n$
Descent terminates at $I_0=\frac{1}{m+1}$; fraction chain involves both $n$ and fixed $m$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the recurrence as one-step ($I_n\to I_{n-1}$) when it actually skips by two, leading to wrong base case.
  • Picking $I_1=1$ for an even-index target (or $I_0=\pi/2$ for an odd-index target).
  • Believing the recurrence must be re-derived each time rather than quoted from the earlier 'Show that' part.

Pitfalls

  • Off-by-one in the fraction chain: writing $\frac{n}{n-1}$ instead of $\frac{n-1}{n}$.
  • Forgetting the $\frac{\pi}{2}$ factor from $I_0$, producing a rational answer where one involving $\pi$ is expected.
  • Arithmetic slip when multiplying three or more fractions; safer to write the full unsimplified product before cancelling.
  • Stopping one step too early (e.g. at $I_2$) and leaving the answer in non-closed form.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Further Pure technique in Edexcel IAL FP2/FP3-style reduction-formula questions; the recurrence is always proved earlier in the question by integration by parts, and the descent uses only arithmetic with elementary base integrals.
