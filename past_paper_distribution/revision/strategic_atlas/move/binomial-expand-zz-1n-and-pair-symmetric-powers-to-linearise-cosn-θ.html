Binomial Expansion

Binomial-expand (z+z^{-1})^n and pair symmetric powers to linearise cos^n θ

Family: Linearisation Via Binomial Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks to express cos^n θ (typically n = 4, 5, 6) as a linear combination of cos(kθ) terms, often as a setup for integration or for proving an identity. Cue phrases include 'express in the form', 'show that cos^n θ =', or a part (a) that established z + z^{-1} = 2cos θ and z^k + z^{-k} = 2cos(kθ). Also fires when an integral ∫cos^n θ dθ is required and reduction formulae are not in scope.

Motivation — Why this works

Since z = cos θ + i sin θ on the unit circle, z + z^{-1} = 2cos θ, so (2cos θ)^n = (z + z^{-1})^n. The binomial theorem expands this into a Laurent polynomial whose coefficients C(n,r) are palindromic, guaranteeing that z^k and z^{-k} appear with equal coefficients and pair into 2cos(kθ). Dividing by 2^n recovers cos^n θ as a finite Fourier cosine series, converting a non-integrable power into a sum of easily handled harmonics.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the target is a power of cos θ that must become a sum of cos(kθ) — a linearisation request.
  2. Rule out product-to-sum identities (too slow for n ≥ 4) and reduction formulae (out of A-level scope).
  3. Commit to the de Moivre route because z + z^{-1} = 2cos θ converts the power into a clean binomial expansion.
  4. Bridge: write (2cos θ)^n = (z + z^{-1})^n and expand using C(n,r).
  5. Tactically pair outermost terms inwards (z^n + z^{-n}, then z^{n-2} + z^{-(n-2)}, …), leaving the middle term untouched if n is even, then divide by 2^n.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. State 2cos θ = z + z^{-1}, hence (2cos θ)^n = (z + z^{-1})^n, i.e. 2^n cos^n θ = (z + z^{-1})^n.
  2. Apply the binomial theorem: (z + z^{-1})^n = Σ_{r=0}^{n} C(n,r) z^{n-2r}.
  3. Group symmetric pairs (z^{n-2r} + z^{-(n-2r)}) using equality of C(n,r) and C(n,n−r); isolate the middle constant if n is even.
  4. Replace each pair z^k + z^{-k} by 2cos(kθ).
  5. Divide both sides by 2^n to obtain cos^n θ as a linear combination of cos(kθ) plus (if n even) a constant.

Worked Micro-Example

Linearise cos^4 θ. Write 16 cos^4 θ = (z + z^{-1})^4 = z^4 + 4z^2 + 6 + 4z^{-2} + z^{-4}. Pair: (z^4 + z^{-4}) + 4(z^2 + z^{-2}) + 6 = 2cos 4θ + 8cos 2θ + 6. Hence cos^4 θ = \tfrac{1}{8}(cos 4θ + 4cos 2θ + 3).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Linearising sin^n θ
Use (2i sin θ)^n = (z − z^{-1})^n; signs alternate via (−1)^r, and the prefactor i^n means even n give cosines while odd n give sines after pairing z^k − z^{-k} = 2i sin(kθ).
Odd powers like cos^5 θ
There is no constant middle term; all binomial terms pair into cos(odd·θ), giving cos^5 θ = \tfrac{1}{16}(cos 5θ + 5cos 3θ + 10cos θ).
Mixed product cos^p θ sin^q θ
Multiply (z + z^{-1})^p (z − z^{-1})^q, expand, then pair; track the i^q factor and use both 2cos(kθ) and 2i sin(kθ) pairings.
Setup for ∫ cos^n θ dθ
After linearising, integrate term by term using ∫ cos(kθ) dθ = (1/k) sin(kθ); avoids reduction formulae.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating (2cos θ)^n as 2 cos^n θ instead of 2^n cos^n θ, losing the 2^n factor.
  • Believing every term pairs — forgetting the unpaired middle constant C(n, n/2) when n is even.
  • Confusing the cosine setup with the sine setup and dropping the i factors.

Pitfalls

  • Miscounting binomial coefficients, especially central ones like C(6,3) = 20 or C(8,4) = 70.
  • Forgetting to divide by 2^n at the final step.
  • Writing z^k + z^{-k} = cos(kθ) instead of 2cos(kθ).
  • Dropping the constant term during the pairing step.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure technique under de Moivre's theorem; relies only on the binomial theorem for positive integer n and the identities z^k + z^{-k} = 2cos(kθ), z^k − z^{-k} = 2i sin(kθ).
