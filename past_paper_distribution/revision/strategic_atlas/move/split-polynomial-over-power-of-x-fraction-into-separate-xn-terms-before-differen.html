Differentiation

Split polynomial-over-power-of-x fraction into separate x^n terms before differentiating

Family: Algebraic Preparation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when y is given as a single algebraic fraction with a polynomial numerator over a monomial in x (e.g. x^k or √x), or as a sum of such fractions, and differentiation is required. Phrasings include 'find dy/dx', 'differentiate with respect to x', or 'find the gradient function' applied to expressions like (ax² + bx + c)/x^k. The cue is a denominator that is a single power of x (positive, negative, or fractional), making term-by-term division trivial. Also fires when constants like 5/x or 3/√x appear alongside polynomial terms.

Motivation — Why this works

Dividing a sum by a single power x^k distributes exactly: (A + B)/x^k = A x^{-k} + B x^{-k}. Once every term is in the form a x^n, the power rule d/dx(x^n) = n x^{n-1} applies directly, with no need for the quotient rule. This is faster, less error-prone, and aligns with how Edexcel IAL marks expect candidates to handle such fractions. The quotient rule, while valid, introduces unnecessary algebra and additional sign and product errors.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Student notices the expression is a fraction (or sum of fractions) whose denominators are pure powers of x — no compound denominators like (x+1).
  2. They rule out the quotient rule because the denominator is a monomial, so direct division is available and cleaner.
  3. They commit to the splitting strategy because it reduces the problem to a sum of a x^n terms — the canonical input for the power rule.
  4. Bridge: write the fraction as a sum, then convert each 1/x^k factor into x^{-k} so every term has explicit exponent form.
  5. Inside the move, they track each term carefully — including any constant produced by cancellation (e.g. 2x²/x² = 2) — before applying d/dx term by term.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify each fraction with a monomial denominator x^k (k may be a positive integer, negative, or fractional).
  2. Distribute the division: (A + B + C)/x^k = A/x^k + B/x^k + C/x^k.
  3. Simplify each quotient: cancel powers of x and rewrite remaining 1/x^m as x^{-m}.
  4. Collect all terms into the form Σ a_i x^{n_i}, including any constant terms.
  5. Differentiate term by term using d/dx(a x^n) = a n x^{n-1}.
  6. Optionally rewrite the final answer back in fractional form if the question requests it.

Worked Micro-Example

Differentiate $y = \dfrac{3 - 2x^2}{x^2} - \dfrac{5}{x}$.\\ Split: $y = 3x^{-2} - 2 - 5x^{-1}$.\\ Differentiate: $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = -6x^{-3} + 5x^{-2}$, i.e. $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = -\dfrac{6}{x^3} + \dfrac{5}{x^2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Numerator over √x, e.g. y = (ax³ + bx + c)/√x
Rewrite √x = x^{1/2} and split: terms become a x^{5/2}, b x^{1/2}, c x^{-1/2}; then apply the power rule.
Numerator over a higher power, e.g. y = (x⁴ - 2x² + 7)/x³
Split into x - 2x^{-1} + 7x^{-3}; differentiate to 1 + 2x^{-2} - 21x^{-4}.
Integration of the same fraction type
Same preparatory split is required before applying ∫x^n dx = x^{n+1}/(n+1) + C; watch the n = -1 case for ln|x|.
Mixed surd/fraction expressions like y = (2 + √x)/x
Split into 2x^{-1} + x^{-1/2}; both terms now follow the power rule directly.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the expression as needing the quotient rule because it 'looks like a fraction', ignoring that the denominator is a monomial.
  • Splitting only some terms in the numerator (e.g. dividing the leading term but not the constant).
  • Confusing √x with x and writing x^{-1} instead of x^{-1/2} when the denominator is √x.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the negative sign on the exponent when rewriting 1/x^k as x^{-k}.
  • Losing a constant term that arises from cancellation (e.g. 2x²/x² = 2) and then having no contribution to dy/dx from it — correct, but students sometimes also drop it from y itself prematurely.
  • Sign errors differentiating negative powers: d/dx(3x^{-2}) = -6x^{-3}, not +6x^{-3}.
  • Arithmetic slips with fractional exponents, e.g. d/dx(x^{1/2}) = (1/2)x^{-1/2}, not (1/2)x^{1/2}.
  • Failing to convert the final answer back to fraction or surd form when the question asks for it in that format.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (C12/P2) differentiation of x^n for rational n; no chain, product, or quotient rule required. The same preparation underpins the corresponding integration questions in C12/P2.
