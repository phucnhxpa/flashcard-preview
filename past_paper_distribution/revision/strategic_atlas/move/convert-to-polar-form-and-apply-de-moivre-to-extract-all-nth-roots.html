Strategic Atlas / Complex Numbers / De Moivre Root Extraction
Complex Numbers

Convert to polar form and apply de Moivre to extract all nth roots

Family: De Moivre Root Extraction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question asks for all solutions of $z^n = w$ where $n \geq 2$ and $w$ is a given non-zero complex number. Cues include phrases like 'find all roots', 'solve for z', 'find the nth roots of...', or 'show the roots on an Argand diagram'. The target $w$ may be presented in Cartesian form $a+bi$, polar form $r(\cos\alpha + i\sin\alpha)$, or exponential form $re^{i\alpha}$.

Motivation — Why this works

By de Moivre's theorem, if $z = \rho e^{i\theta}$ then $z^n = \rho^n e^{in\theta}$, so $z^n = re^{i\alpha}$ forces $\rho = r^{1/n}$ and $n\theta \equiv \alpha \pmod{2\pi}$. This gives exactly $n$ distinct arguments $(\alpha + 2k\pi)/n$ for $k = 0,1,\dots,n-1$, producing $n$ roots equally spaced by $2\pi/n$ on a circle of radius $r^{1/n}$. Polar form is essential because raising/rooting moduli and arguments separately is direct, while Cartesian root-taking requires solving an $n$th-degree polynomial.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the equation has the form $z^n = w$ asking for all $n$ roots, not factorisation.
  2. I rule out Cartesian expansion or polynomial factor methods — they don't generalise cleanly to arbitrary $n$.
  3. I commit to de Moivre because it gives a closed-form list of all $n$ roots with guaranteed completeness.
  4. First concrete step: rewrite $w$ as $re^{i\alpha}$, choosing $\alpha$ in the correct quadrant.
  5. Inside the move, I generate roots by adding $2\pi/n$ to the argument $n-1$ times, then convert to the form the question demands.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. If $w$ is Cartesian, compute $r = \sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ and $\alpha = \arg(w)$ using the correct quadrant.
  2. Write $w = re^{i\alpha}$ and equate $z^n = re^{i(\alpha + 2k\pi)}$.
  3. Take modulus: each root has $|z_k| = r^{1/n}$.
  4. Take argument: $\arg(z_k) = (\alpha + 2k\pi)/n$ for $k = 0, 1, \dots, n-1$.
  5. Write $z_k = r^{1/n}\bigl[\cos\tfrac{\alpha+2k\pi}{n} + i\sin\tfrac{\alpha+2k\pi}{n}\bigr]$.
  6. Convert to Cartesian or simplify surds if the question requires, and check arguments lie in the requested range (often $(-\pi,\pi]$).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $z^3 = 8i$. Write $8i = 8e^{i\pi/2}$, so $|z| = 8^{1/3} = 2$ and $\arg z = \tfrac{\pi/2 + 2k\pi}{3}$ for $k=0,1,2$. This gives arguments $\tfrac{\pi}{6},\ \tfrac{5\pi}{6},\ -\tfrac{\pi}{2}$, hence $z = \sqrt{3}+i,\ -\sqrt{3}+i,\ -2i$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Roots of unity, $z^n = 1$
Take $r=1,\ \alpha = 0$: roots are $z_k = e^{2k\pi i/n}$, equally spaced on the unit circle.
$w$ given in Cartesian form $a+bi$
First convert: $r=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$, $\alpha = \arg(a+bi)$ with quadrant care, then proceed.
Question asks for arguments in $(-\pi,\pi]$
After computing $(\alpha+2k\pi)/n$, subtract $2\pi$ from any argument exceeding $\pi$ to bring into principal range.
Argand diagram requested
Plot the $n$ roots as vertices of a regular $n$-gon centred at origin with circumradius $r^{1/n}$.
Equation $z^n + c = 0$ or $(z-a)^n = w$
Rearrange to standard form $z^n = -c$ or substitute $u = z-a$, solve $u^n = w$, then back-substitute.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $z^n = w$ as having a unique 'nth root' rather than $n$ distinct roots.
  • Reading $\arg(a+bi) = \arctan(b/a)$ blindly without checking quadrant when $a<0$.
  • Confusing 'cube root of $w$' (one principal value) with 'solutions of $z^3 = w$' (three values).

Pitfalls

  • Wrong principal argument for $w$, especially when $w$ lies in the second or third quadrant.
  • Listing $n+1$ roots or starting at $k=1$ and missing the $k=0$ root.
  • Writing $r$ instead of $r^{1/n}$ for the modulus of each root.
  • Failing to reduce arguments into the range required by the question.
  • Arithmetic slip when adding $2k\pi/n$ — best to compute incrementally.
