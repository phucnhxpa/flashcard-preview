Predict from the log-linear equation rather than the rounded exponential form

Family: Error Avoidance Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A modelling question gives an exponential law $P = ab^t$ (or $y = ax^n$) where $a$ and $b$ have just been recovered from a log-linear graph and quoted to 2-3 s.f. A later part asks for a prediction at a specific $t$ (often a value like 10 or 20 that magnifies error through high powers). The exact gradient and intercept from the graph — i.e. the coefficients of the line $\log P = c + mt$ — are still available. Cue phrases: 'Use your model to estimate...', 'Hence find P when t = ...', especially when $b^t$ would involve a large exponent.

Motivation — Why this works

The two forms $P = ab^t$ and $\log_{10} P = \log_{10} a + (\log_{10} b)\, t$ are algebraically equivalent, but numerically they are not equally stable. Once $a$ and $b$ have been rounded, raising $b$ to a high power compounds the rounding error multiplicatively. Working in log-space uses the original line parameters directly, so the only rounding occurs at the final anti-log step. This both improves accuracy and keeps the arithmetic to a single substitution and one $10^x$ key-press.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice that $a$ and $b$ in the answer to the previous part are rounded decimals, and the new part wants $P$ at a moderately large $t$.
  2. I rule out plugging into $P = ab^t$ directly: $b^{10}$ would amplify the 2-d.p. rounding in $b$ noticeably.
  3. I commit to using the linear equation $\log_{10} P = c + m t$ with the exact $c$ and $m$ read from the graph, because those weren't rounded in the same lossy way.
  4. First operation: substitute $t$ into the log equation to get $\log_{10} P$ as a clean decimal.
  5. Tactically, I do one anti-log at the end and round only there, and I sanity-check by comparing roughly with the rounded-form answer.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the exact line equation $\log_{10} P = c + m t$ from the graph (gradient $m$, intercept $c$).
  2. Substitute the required $t$-value to obtain $\log_{10} P$ as a single number.
  3. Apply $10^{(\cdot)}$ once to recover $P$.
  4. Round only at the final step to the precision the question demands.

Worked Micro-Example

Graph gives $\log_{10} P = 0.68 + 0.09 t$, so the rounded model is $P \approx 4.79 \times 1.23^{t}$. For $t = 10$: stay in log form, $\log_{10} P = 0.68 + 0.09(10) = 1.58$. Then $P = 10^{1.58} \approx 38.0$. Going via $4.79 \times 1.23^{10}$ would propagate rounding through a 10th power and give a less reliable value.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Power-law model $y = a x^n$ from a $\log y$ vs $\log x$ plot
Use $\log_{10} y = \log_{10} a + n \log_{10} x$ directly with the graph's intercept and gradient, then anti-log once.
Exponential decay / growth modelled with $\ln$
Work in $\ln y = \ln A - k t$ with the exact $k$ from the graph; finish with a single $e^{(\cdot)}$ rather than raising a rounded base.
Solving for $t$ given a target $P$
Rearrange the log-linear equation linearly for $t$ instead of taking logs of $P = a b^t$ with rounded $a,b$.

Common Misreadings

  • Believing the question requires the $P = ab^t$ form because that was the 'final model' in a previous part — the two forms are equivalent and either is acceptable.
  • Treating the rounded $a$ and $b$ as exact and not realising that $b^{t}$ for large $t$ is the main source of error.
  • Thinking 'use your model' forbids going back to the log equation.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the final anti-log and quoting $\log_{10} P = 1.58$ as the answer instead of $P \approx 38$.
  • Computing $10 \times 1.58 = 15.8$ instead of $10^{1.58}$.
  • Mixing logs: using $\ln$ on the calculator when the line was $\log_{10}$, or vice versa.
  • Rounding $\log_{10} P$ heavily before anti-logging, which reintroduces the very error this move was meant to avoid.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 logarithms and exponential modelling: linearising $y = ab^x$ or $y = ax^n$ via logs and reading gradient/intercept from a straight-line graph. No content beyond standard log laws and $10^x$/$e^x$.
