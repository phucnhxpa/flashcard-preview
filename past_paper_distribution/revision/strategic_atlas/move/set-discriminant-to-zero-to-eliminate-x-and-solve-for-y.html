Algebra & Functions

Set discriminant to zero to eliminate x and solve for y

Family: Discriminant Elimination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a curve (often a conic or implicit relation) has been rewritten as a quadratic $Ax^2 + Bx + C = 0$ whose coefficients depend on $y$, and the question asks for points where the tangent is horizontal, where a horizontal line meets the curve once, or for extreme $y$-values on the curve. Cue phrases include 'find the greatest/least value of $y$', 'horizontal tangent', 'find $y$ such that the line $y = k$ touches the curve', or 'turning points' on an implicit curve. The y-dependent coefficients signal that eliminating $x$ via the discriminant will collapse the system to a single equation in $y$.

Motivation — Why this works

A quadratic in $x$ has a repeated root precisely when $\Delta = B^2 - 4AC = 0$, which is the algebraic image of geometric tangency: the horizontal slice $y = \text{const}$ meets the curve at exactly one $x$. Imposing $\Delta = 0$ therefore enforces both existence and uniqueness of $x$ in one stroke, replacing the two-equation system (curve plus $\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x = 0$) with a single polynomial in $y$. This is purely algebraic and avoids implicit differentiation entirely.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that after grouping by powers of $x$, the equation is quadratic in $x$ with coefficients $A$, $B$, $C$ that involve $y$.
  2. Rules out implicit differentiation as unnecessarily heavy, since horizontal-tangent / extremal-$y$ conditions correspond exactly to the quadratic-in-$x$ having a repeated root.
  3. Commits to the discriminant route because $\Delta = 0$ encodes tangency as one clean equation in $y$ alone.
  4. Bridges by writing $\Delta = B(y)^2 - 4 A(y) C(y)$, expanding carefully and collecting like powers of $y$.
  5. Inside the move, decides whether the resulting quadratic in $y$ factorises (looks for integer factor pairs of $AC$ summing to $B$) or needs the quadratic formula, and back-substitutes if $x$-coordinates are also required.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rearrange the curve into the form $A x^2 + B x + C(y) = 0$, identifying $A$, $B$, $C$ explicitly.
  2. Compute $\Delta = B^2 - 4 A C(y)$ and set $\Delta = 0$.
  3. Expand and simplify to obtain a polynomial equation in $y$ — here $2y^2 + 15y + 25 = 0$.
  4. Factorise (e.g. $(2y+5)(y+5)=0$) or apply the quadratic formula.
  5. Read off the $y$-values; if needed, substitute each back into $A x^2 + B x + C = 0$ to recover the unique $x = -B/(2A)$.

Worked Micro-Example

Curve: $x^2 + 15x + (2y^2 + 15y + 25 + \text{const}) = 0$ rearranged as a quadratic in $x$ with $A=1$, $B=15$, $C=C(y)$. Setting $\Delta = 15^2 - 4C(y) = 0$ gives $2y^2 + 15y + 25 = 0$. Factorising: $(2y+5)(y+5)=0$, so $y = -\tfrac{5}{2}$ or $y = -5$. The corresponding $x$ at each tangency is $x = -B/(2A) = -\tfrac{15}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Tangent lines from an external point to a conic (e.g. circle, parabola)
Substitute $y = mx + c$ into the conic, form a quadratic in $x$, and set $\Delta = 0$ to solve for $m$ (or $c$) instead of $y$.
Proving a given line is tangent to a circle/parabola
Substitute the line into the curve and verify $\Delta = 0$; no solving required, just a check.
Finding the range of values of $y$ on an implicit curve
Treat as quadratic in $x$ and impose $\Delta \ge 0$ (real $x$ exists), giving an inequality in $y$ whose boundary values are the extremal $y$.
Finding $k$ such that $y = k$ meets a curve once (vertical-line analogue uses quadratic in $y$)
If the curve is quadratic in $y$ with $x$-dependent coefficients, swap roles and set discriminant in $y$ to zero to solve for $x$.

Common Misreadings

  • Setting $\Delta > 0$ (just wanting real intersections) rather than $\Delta = 0$ for tangency.
  • Misidentifying which variable to make the quadratic in — using $y$ when the question's symmetry favours $x$, leading to messier coefficients.
  • Confusing 'horizontal tangent' with 'vertical tangent' and eliminating the wrong variable.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic/sign errors in $B^2 - 4AC$, especially when $C(y)$ contains negative terms or constants picked up from rearrangement.
  • Failing to fully expand $4AC$ before collecting in $y$, leaving an un-simplified equation that resists factorisation.
  • Stopping at $y$-values when the question asks for full coordinates — must back-substitute $x = -B/(2A)$.
  • Applying $\Delta = 0$ when the original equation is not actually quadratic in the chosen variable (e.g. a hidden $x^3$ term).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure technique used for tangents to circles/conics (P1–P3) and for extremal values on implicit quadratic curves; stays within quadratic discriminant theory and routine factorisation.
