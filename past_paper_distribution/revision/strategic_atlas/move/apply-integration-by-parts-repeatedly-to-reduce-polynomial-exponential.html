Motivation â€” Why this works

Integration by parts âˆ«u dv = uv âˆ’ âˆ«v du transfers a derivative from u to v. If u is the polynomial, then du has degree one lower; if dv is the exponential, then v stays in the same exponential family. Each IBP round therefore strictly reduces the polynomial's degree by 1 while the exponential is reproduced (up to a constant). After exactly n rounds for a degree-n polynomial, the polynomial factor becomes a constant and the chain terminates with a pure exponential integral. This is the canonical 'product rule reversed' strategy because substitution fails â€” there is no composite to unwind.

Thought Process â€” The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand is a product of two unlike factors: a polynomial x^n and an exponential e^(ax). This pattern is the textbook signal for IBP.
  2. Rule out substitution: there is no inner function whose derivative appears as the other factor, so u = x^n or u = e^(ax) substitutions both fail to simplify.
  3. Commit to integration by parts using the LIATE heuristic: choose u = x^n (Algebraic) so du lowers the degree, and dv = e^(ax) dx so v = (1/a)e^(ax) stays in the exponential family. The reverse choice would increase the polynomial's degree and never terminate.
  4. Anticipate the iteration count: a degree-n polynomial requires exactly n rounds of IBP, after which the integrand becomes a constant times e^(ax), trivially integrable.
  5. Tactical decisions: factor out any leading constants first to keep arithmetic clean; track the âˆ’1/a (or analogous) factor from each integration of e^(ax) carefully because signs propagate through every round.
  6. Optionally use the tabular (DI) method for higher degrees: list derivatives of the polynomial down one column and integrals of the exponential down the other, alternating signs.

Common Misreadings

  • Choosing u = e^(ax) and dv = x^n dx â€” this raises the polynomial degree from n to n+1 each round, so the chain never terminates.
  • Attempting a substitution like u = x^n or u = ax â€” neither factor is the derivative of an inner function of the other, so substitution does not simplify.
  • Treating the integral as if it needs a single IBP when degree â‰¥ 2 â€” leads to an unfinished answer with an integral still present.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when integrating e^(-x) or e^(-x/3): v = -e^(-x) (or -3e^(-x/3)), and the negative propagates through every subsequent term.
  • Alternating IBP signs (âˆ’âˆ«v du) compounding across rounds â€” easy to drop a minus on round 2 or 3.
  • Losing track of accumulated constants (the n/a factor multiplied across rounds, or an outer factor like 6 pulled out at the start).
  • Forgetting +C on indefinite integrals; for definite integrals, forgetting to apply limits to every term in the combined antiderivative.
