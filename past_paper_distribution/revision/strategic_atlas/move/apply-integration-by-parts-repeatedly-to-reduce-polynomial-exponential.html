Integration

Apply integration by parts repeatedly to reduce polynomial × exponential

Family: Repeated Ibp Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the integrand is a polynomial of degree n multiplied by an exponential function, e.g. ∫x²e^x dx, ∫6x²e^(-x/3) dx, or ∫x³e^(kx) dx. Visual cue: a product of an algebraic factor (power of x) and a function from the exponential family (e^x, e^(-x), e^(kx)). There is no inner-function/composite structure that would suggest substitution, and the two factors are of unlike type. Equivalent phrasings: 'polynomial times exponential', 'x^n e^(ax) integrand', 'product of algebraic and exponential factor'.

Motivation — Why this works

Integration by parts ∫u dv = uv − ∫v du transfers a derivative from u to v. If u is the polynomial, then du has degree one lower; if dv is the exponential, then v stays in the same exponential family. Each IBP round therefore strictly reduces the polynomial's degree by 1 while the exponential is reproduced (up to a constant). After exactly n rounds for a degree-n polynomial, the polynomial factor becomes a constant and the chain terminates with a pure exponential integral. This is the canonical 'product rule reversed' strategy because substitution fails — there is no composite to unwind.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand is a product of two unlike factors: a polynomial x^n and an exponential e^(ax). This pattern is the textbook signal for IBP.
  2. Rule out substitution: there is no inner function whose derivative appears as the other factor, so u = x^n or u = e^(ax) substitutions both fail to simplify.
  3. Commit to integration by parts using the LIATE heuristic: choose u = x^n (Algebraic) so du lowers the degree, and dv = e^(ax) dx so v = (1/a)e^(ax) stays in the exponential family. The reverse choice would increase the polynomial's degree and never terminate.
  4. Anticipate the iteration count: a degree-n polynomial requires exactly n rounds of IBP, after which the integrand becomes a constant times e^(ax), trivially integrable.
  5. Tactical decisions: factor out any leading constants first to keep arithmetic clean; track the −1/a (or analogous) factor from each integration of e^(ax) carefully because signs propagate through every round.
  6. Optionally use the tabular (DI) method for higher degrees: list derivatives of the polynomial down one column and integrals of the exponential down the other, alternating signs.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify polynomial factor x^n and exponential factor e^(ax); set u = x^n, dv = e^(ax) dx.
  2. Compute du = n x^(n-1) dx and v = (1/a) e^(ax).
  3. Apply IBP: ∫x^n e^(ax) dx = (x^n/a) e^(ax) − (n/a) ∫x^(n-1) e^(ax) dx.
  4. Repeat IBP on the new integral with u = x^(n-1), dv = e^(ax) dx; continue until the polynomial reduces to a constant.
  5. Integrate the final ∫e^(ax) dx = (1/a) e^(ax) and back-substitute through every level.
  6. Combine all terms; the antiderivative has the form (polynomial of degree n in x) · e^(ax) + C.

Worked Micro-Example

Compute $\int x^2 e^x\,dx$. Round 1: $u=x^2$, $dv=e^x dx \Rightarrow du=2x\,dx$, $v=e^x$, giving $x^2 e^x - \int 2x e^x\,dx$. Round 2: $u=2x$, $dv=e^x dx \Rightarrow du=2\,dx$, $v=e^x$, giving $2x e^x - \int 2 e^x\,dx = 2x e^x - 2e^x$. Combine: $\int x^2 e^x\,dx = (x^2 - 2x + 2)e^x + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Exponential with non-unit coefficient, e.g. ∫x² e^(-x/3) dx
Same procedure but v = -3 e^(-x/3); the factor (1/a) = -3 propagates through every round, so track it carefully. Often easier to factor out leading constants first.
Higher-degree polynomial, e.g. ∫x³ e^(kx) dx
Three rounds of IBP instead of two — same logic, one extra iteration. Tabular (DI) method becomes especially efficient here.
Polynomial × trig function, e.g. ∫x² sin(x) dx
Same repeated-IBP structure with u = polynomial, but signs cycle through sin → −cos → −sin → cos rather than reproducing. Still terminates in n rounds.
Definite integral version
Apply limits to every uv term and to the final integrated piece; do not substitute limits prematurely between rounds — keep antiderivatives symbolic until the end.

Common Misreadings

  • Choosing u = e^(ax) and dv = x^n dx — this raises the polynomial degree from n to n+1 each round, so the chain never terminates.
  • Attempting a substitution like u = x^n or u = ax — neither factor is the derivative of an inner function of the other, so substitution does not simplify.
  • Treating the integral as if it needs a single IBP when degree ≥ 2 — leads to an unfinished answer with an integral still present.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when integrating e^(-x) or e^(-x/3): v = -e^(-x) (or -3e^(-x/3)), and the negative propagates through every subsequent term.
  • Alternating IBP signs (−∫v du) compounding across rounds — easy to drop a minus on round 2 or 3.
  • Losing track of accumulated constants (the n/a factor multiplied across rounds, or an outer factor like 6 pulled out at the start).
  • Forgetting +C on indefinite integrals; for definite integrals, forgetting to apply limits to every term in the combined antiderivative.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3 (integration by parts) and used routinely in P4/FP. The IBP formula ∫u dv/dx dx = uv − ∫v du/dx dx is in the formula booklet. The tabular/DI shortcut is not in the booklet but is an equivalent presentation; students should still show u, dv working in exam scripts. Do not invoke reduction-formula notation beyond what FP problems explicitly request.
