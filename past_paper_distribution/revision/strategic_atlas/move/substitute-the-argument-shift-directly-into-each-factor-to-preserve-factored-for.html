Algebra & Functions

Substitute the argument shift directly into each factor to preserve factored form

Family: Transformation In Factored Form Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a new function is defined as $g(x) = f(x+a)$ (or $f(kx)$, $f(-x)$, etc.) and $f(x)$ is already presented in factored form such as $(ax-b)(x-c)^2$. Especially likely when the question asks for $g(x)$ in 'simplest form', or follows up by asking for intercepts, roots, or a sketch of the transformed curve. Also triggered when expansion of $f(x)$ would produce a cubic or higher polynomial that would then need re-factoring.

Motivation — Why this works

Function transformation $f(x+a)$ replaces every occurrence of $x$ in $f$ by $x+a$; this rule applies equally to a factored expression because each factor is itself a function of $x$. Substituting inside each factor keeps every factor linear (or a power of a linear factor), so the output is automatically in factored form — the structure examiners regard as 'simplest' and which makes roots and intercepts immediate. Expanding first destroys this structure and forces a redundant re-factoring step.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that $f(x)$ is given in factored form and the new function is just $f$ with its argument shifted, e.g. $g(x)=f(x+1)$.
  2. Rule out expanding $f(x)$ to a cubic first — that creates a messy polynomial and a second expansion after substitution.
  3. Commit to substituting $x \mapsto x+a$ directly into each factor, since each factor is independently a function of $x$.
  4. Translate this to the concrete operation: rewrite every factor $(\ldots x \ldots)$ as $(\ldots (x+a) \ldots)$ and simplify the constants.
  5. Check that the squared (or higher-power) factor still has its exponent, and read off intercepts/roots from the new factored form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify each factor of $f(x)$, including any repeated factor with its exponent.
  2. Replace $x$ by $(x+a)$ inside every factor (do not touch the exponents).
  3. Simplify the constants inside each factor to recover a clean linear factor.
  4. Reassemble the product, keeping any squared/cubed factor squared/cubed.
  5. Use the new factored form to read off roots, $y$-intercept ($x=0$), or to sketch.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $f(x) = (4x-3)(x-5)^2$, find $g(x)=f(x+1)$. First factor: $4(x+1)-3 = 4x+1$. Second factor: $(x+1)-5 = x-4$, so squared gives $(x-4)^2$. Hence $g(x) = (4x+1)(x-4)^2$, and $g(0) = (1)(16) = 16$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Horizontal stretch $g(x)=f(kx)$ with $f$ factored
Replace $x$ by $kx$ in each factor, e.g. $(x-a) \to (kx-a)$; exponents unchanged.
Reflection $g(x)=f(-x)$
Replace $x$ by $-x$ in each factor; may want to factor out $-1$ from each linear factor afterwards.
Combined shift and stretch $g(x)=f(k(x+a))$ or $f(kx+c)$
Substitute the entire new argument into each factor in one step, then simplify constants.
Higher repeated factors, e.g. $f(x)=(x-a)^3(x-b)$
Same principle — substitute inside the base and keep the cube exponent intact.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 'simplest form' as 'fully expanded polynomial' and so expanding $f(x)$ first into a cubic before substituting.
  • Treating $f(x+1)$ as $f(x)+1$ or $f(x)+f(1)$ rather than an argument substitution.
  • Applying the substitution only to the first factor and leaving the squared factor untouched.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips in the constant of a factor, e.g. writing $(x+1)-5$ as $(x-6)$ instead of $(x-4)$.
  • Dropping the exponent: writing $(x-4)$ instead of $(x-4)^2$ after substituting into $(x-5)^2$.
  • Forgetting to multiply the leading coefficient by the substitution, e.g. writing $4(x+1)-3$ as $4x-3+1 = 4x-2$.
  • Mis-signing in $f(x-a)$ vs $f(x+a)$ — the curve shifts opposite to the sign inside the bracket.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P2/P3 function-transformation content: horizontal translations and stretches of polynomial graphs given in factored form, with follow-up intercept/sketch questions.
