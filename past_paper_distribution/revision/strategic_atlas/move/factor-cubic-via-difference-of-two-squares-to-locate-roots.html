Algebra & Functions

Factor cubic via difference of two squares to locate roots

Family: Strategic Factorisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a cubic (or higher) has been partially factorised into a monomial times a binomial of the form a² − (linear)² or constant − (variable)². Typical surface forms include x(4 − x²), 2x(9 − x²), or (x+1)(16 − x²) appearing in curve-sketching, root-finding, or definite-integral setup. The cue is a constant minus a perfect square term, with no x-linear term in that bracket.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity a² − b² = (a − b)(a + b) converts a quadratic factor into two linear factors in one line, bypassing the quadratic formula and producing exact integer or surd roots. Combined with the pre-existing monomial factor, the cubic decomposes fully into linear factors, making roots and sign behaviour immediately readable. This is the cleanest route when sketching or finding axis intercepts.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the cubic is already presented as a product: a monomial factor times a bracket of form constant − x².
  2. Rules out the quadratic formula and polynomial long division as overkill — the bracket is a textbook difference of two squares.
  3. Commits because matching a² − b² with a = √(constant) and b = x gives exact linear factors with no surds or discriminant work.
  4. Bridges to action by writing constant − x² = (a − x)(a + x), then combining with the existing monomial factor.
  5. Sets the full product to zero and reads off all roots, checking that the monomial contributes its own root (often x = 0).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Set y = 0 to expose the product structure, e.g. x(4 − x²) = 0.
  2. Identify a² − b² pattern: 4 − x² with a = 2, b = x.
  3. Apply the identity: 4 − x² = (2 − x)(2 + x).
  4. Write the full factorisation: x(2 − x)(2 + x) = 0.
  5. Read off all roots from each linear factor, including the one from the monomial.

Worked Micro-Example

Sketch y = x(4 − x²). Set y = 0: x(4 − x²) = 0. Recognise 4 − x² = (2 − x)(2 + x), so x(2 − x)(2 + x) = 0. Hence x = −2, 0, 2 — three distinct x-intercepts, so the cubic crosses the axis three times.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Coefficient on x² inside the bracket, e.g. x(9 − 4x²)
Match a = 3, b = 2x giving (3 − 2x)(3 + 2x); roots are x = 0, ±3/2.
Higher power, e.g. x(16 − x⁴)
Apply difference of two squares twice: 16 − x⁴ = (4 − x²)(4 + x²) = (2 − x)(2 + x)(4 + x²); only the real factors give roots.
Shifted square, e.g. (x − 1)(9 − (x − 1)²)
Treat b = x − 1; factor as (3 − (x−1))(3 + (x−1)) = (4 − x)(2 + x).
Negative leading sign, e.g. x(x² − 4)
Same identity with roles swapped: x² − 4 = (x − 2)(x + 2); sign of the cubic for sketching flips accordingly.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 4 − x² as irreducible and reaching for the quadratic formula on −x² + 4 = 0.
  • Mistaking 4 + x² for a difference of two squares (it is a sum of squares and does not factor over the reals).
  • Reading x(4 − x²) as a quadratic because of the visible x² term, missing the cubic degree.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the root x = 0 contributed by the pre-existing monomial factor.
  • Sign slip when expanding (a − x)(a + x) back to check — getting a² + x² instead of a² − x².
  • Dropping a coefficient when b is not just x, e.g. writing (3 − x)(3 + x) instead of (3 − 2x)(3 + 2x) for 9 − 4x².

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure Maths content (P1/P2 algebra and curve sketching); difference of two squares is an assumed identity and this strategic use is expected without prompting.
