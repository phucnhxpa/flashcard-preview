Differential Equations

Form auxiliary equation to convert constant-coefficient ODE into algebra

Family: Auxiliary Equation Method Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

The ODE is linear with constant coefficients in standard form a·y'' + b·y' + c·y = f(x), where a, b, c are real numbers (not functions of x). This is the textbook signal for the auxiliary equation method — whenever you see a homogeneous (or homogeneous part of a) linear ODE with numerical coefficients on y'', y', y, this move fires. Equivalent phrasings: 'find the complementary function', 'solve y'' + py' + qy = 0', or any FP2/FP3-style second-order linear ODE with constants.

Motivation — Why this works

Substituting the trial y = e^{mx} into the homogeneous equation a·y'' + b·y' + c·y = 0 turns each derivative into a power of m times e^{mx} (since e^{mx} is an eigenfunction of d/dx with eigenvalue m). The common factor e^{mx}, which is never zero, cancels, leaving the purely algebraic quadratic am² + bm + c = 0. The roots of this 'auxiliary' (or 'characteristic') equation directly encode the structural form of the complementary function, so a hard differential problem is reduced to school-level algebra.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the ODE is linear with CONSTANT coefficients on y'', y', y — this is the unmistakable cue for the auxiliary equation method.
  2. Rule out heavier alternatives: variation of parameters and reduction of order are unnecessary here; the integrating factor is for first-order only. Auxiliary equation + Particular Integral is the efficient standard route.
  3. Commit to the trial y = e^{mx} because differentiation of e^{mx} just multiplies by m, so the exponential will factor out cleanly and leave a polynomial in m.
  4. Read coefficients a, b, c directly off the ODE (being careful with signs when terms are subtracted) and write the auxiliary equation am² + bm + c = 0. Note that the RHS f(x) plays no role at this stage — only the homogeneous part matters.
  5. Decide how to solve the quadratic: try factorisation first, otherwise use the discriminant b² − 4ac (or the quadratic formula). The sign of the discriminant classifies the root type and dictates the CF template to write down.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Put the ODE into standard form a·y'' + b·y' + c·y = f(x) and identify constants a, b, c.
  2. Replace y'' → m², y' → m, y → 1 to obtain the auxiliary equation am² + bm + c = 0.
  3. Solve for m by factorisation, completing the square, or the quadratic formula; compute the discriminant Δ = b² − 4ac to classify the roots.
  4. Write the complementary function according to the root type: (i) Δ > 0, distinct real roots m₁, m₂ → y_CF = Ae^{m₁x} + Be^{m₂x}; (ii) Δ = 0, repeated root m → y_CF = (A + Bx)e^{mx}; (iii) Δ < 0, complex conjugate roots p ± qi → y_CF = e^{px}(A cos qx + B sin qx).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $y'' + 5y' + 6y = 0$: substitute $y = e^{mx}$ to get $m^2 + 5m + 6 = 0$. Factorise: $(m+2)(m+3)=0$, so $m=-2,\,-3$ (two distinct real roots). Therefore the complementary function is $y_{CF} = Ae^{-2x} + Be^{-3x}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

First-order linear ODE with constant coefficients, e.g. ay' + by = 0
Auxiliary equation is linear: am + b = 0, giving a single root and y = Ce^{mx}.
Higher-order constant-coefficient ODE (third- or fourth-order in FP2/FP3)
Auxiliary equation becomes a cubic/quartic; factorise and combine the CF terms in the same root-classification way.
Non-homogeneous ODE a·y'' + b·y' + c·y = f(x)
Use the auxiliary equation only on the homogeneous part to get y_CF; then add a Particular Integral y_PI separately.

Common Misreadings

  • Misreading the sign of b when the ODE has a minus sign, e.g. writing m² + 3m + 2 = 0 instead of m² − 3m + 2 = 0 for y'' − 3y' + 2y = 0.
  • Treating the RHS f(x) as if it contributes to the auxiliary equation — it does not; it only enters via the Particular Integral.
  • Confusing coefficients when the ODE is presented out of standard order (e.g. y' term written before y'').

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic errors in the discriminant, especially when b is negative, leading to the wrong root classification.
  • For repeated roots writing Ae^{mx} + Be^{mx} (which collapses to one constant) instead of the correct (A + Bx)e^{mx}.
  • For complex roots p ± qi, dropping the e^{px} factor and writing only A cos qx + B sin qx, or swapping the roles of p (exponential decay/growth) and q (oscillation frequency).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully in scope for Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2). The three CF templates for distinct real, repeated, and complex roots are the standard results expected; no formula booklet entry is invoked beyond the quadratic formula. Avoid generalising to variable-coefficient ODEs (e.g. Cauchy–Euler equations beyond their FP2 reduction substitution) or to systems of ODEs.
