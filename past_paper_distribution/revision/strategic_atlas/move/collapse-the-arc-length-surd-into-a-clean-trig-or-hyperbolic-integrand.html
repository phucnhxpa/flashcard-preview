Integration

Collapse the arc-length surd into a clean trig or hyperbolic integrand

Family: Surd Collapse Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an FP3 question asks for arc length s = ∫√(1+(dy/dx)²) dx, surface area S = ∫2πy√(1+(dy/dx)²) dx, or a parametric arc length ∫√((dx/dt)²+(dy/dt)²) dt. The integrand under the radical looks ugly until an algebraic or trig identity reveals a perfect square. Cue phrases include 'show that the arc length is…', 'hence using the reduction formula in part (a)', or a curve given in a form (e.g. y = ln(sec x), x = a cosh t, y = a(t − sin t)) that is engineered for the surd to collapse.

Motivation — Why this works

Exam curves are chosen so that 1 + (dy/dx)² factors as a perfect square via Pythagorean or hyperbolic identities (sec²x = 1+tan²x, cosh²t = 1+sinh²t, (a+b)² = a²+2ab+b²). Once the surd collapses, the integral reduces to something elementary or to a sin^k / cos^k form that matches a reduction formula derived earlier in the question. This is the mechanical bridge between the geometric definition and the analytic technique the paper is testing.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question wants arc length or surface area and that an earlier part produced a reduction formula for ∫sin^n x dx (or similar) — this signals the surd must collapse to that family.
  2. I rule out direct numerical integration or substitution before simplifying: tackling √(1+(dy/dx)²) as written is almost never intended.
  3. I commit to algebraic simplification because the curve's form (involves ln, cosh, or a parametric trig pair) is a strong hint that 1+(dy/dx)² is engineered to be a perfect square.
  4. I bridge to the first operation by computing dy/dx (or dx/dt, dy/dt), squaring, and adding 1 with a clear eye for an identity match.
  5. Tactically I keep constants outside the radical, factor before squaring where possible, and check the sign so the positive square root is taken on the given interval.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Differentiate to obtain dy/dx, or both dx/dt and dy/dt for parametric curves.
  2. Form 1 + (dy/dx)² (or (dx/dt)²+(dy/dt)²) and simplify using a Pythagorean or hyperbolic identity.
  3. Recognise the result as a perfect square A(x)² and take √ to get |A(x)|, choosing the sign valid on the integration interval.
  4. Substitute back into the arc-length or surface-area integral, including any 2πy factor for surfaces of revolution.
  5. Match the resulting integrand to the reduction formula or standard integral and proceed.

Worked Micro-Example

For y = ln(sec x), 0 ≤ x ≤ π/3: dy/dx = tan x, so 1+(dy/dx)² = 1+tan²x = sec²x. Then √(1+(dy/dx)²) = sec x, giving s = ∫₀^{π/3} sec x dx = [ln|sec x + tan x|]₀^{π/3} = ln(2+√3).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Surface of revolution about the x-axis
Multiply the collapsed surd by 2πy before integrating; the y-factor often combines with the simplified radical to produce another reduction-formula-friendly integrand.
Parametric curve, e.g. x = a(t−sin t), y = a(1−cos t)
Compute (dx/dt)²+(dy/dt)² = 2a²(1−cos t) = 4a²sin²(t/2), so the surd becomes 2a|sin(t/2)|.
Catenary y = a cosh(x/a)
1+(dy/dx)² = 1+sinh²(x/a) = cosh²(x/a), so the integrand is cosh(x/a) directly.
Polar arc length ∫√(r²+(dr/dθ)²) dθ
Same hunt for an identity collapse, typically using r = f(θ) chosen so r²+(dr/dθ)² is a perfect square in sin/cos.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating √(1+(dy/dx)²) as 1+dy/dx (illegal distribution of the square root).
  • Squaring 1+dy/dx instead of (dy/dx)² and then adding 1.
  • Forgetting the 2πy factor when the question is surface area, not arc length.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to spot the perfect square and leaving the surd unsimplified, making the integral appear intractable.
  • Dropping a constant factor (e.g. the 'a' in a cosh(x/a)) during simplification.
  • Taking the wrong sign of the square root on an interval where the inside expression is negative, producing a sign error in the final answer.
  • Mismatching the simplified integrand to the wrong reduction formula (e.g. sin^n vs cos^n).

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL FP3 arc length and surfaces of revolution, and pairs naturally with reduction formulae from FP3 integration. All identities used (Pythagorean, hyperbolic, double-angle) are within the IAL Further Maths syllabus.
