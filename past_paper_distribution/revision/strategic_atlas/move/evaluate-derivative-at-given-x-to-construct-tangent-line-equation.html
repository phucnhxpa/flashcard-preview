Differentiation

Evaluate derivative at given x to construct tangent line equation

Family: Tangent Line Construction Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question asks for 'the equation of the tangent to the curve at the point where x = a' or 'at point P'. Visual cues: a curve y = f(x) (often from an earlier part), a specified x-value or named point, and the word 'tangent' (or sometimes 'normal', which uses the same gradient as a stepping stone). Often appears as the final part of a multi-part question where part (b) has already produced either an expanded polynomial or a derivative formula, signalling that this part is meant to use that result. Alternative phrasings: 'find the tangent at P', 'show that the tangent has equation ...', 'find an equation of the tangent line'.

Motivation — Why this works

The tangent line at a point on y = f(x) is fully determined by two pieces of data: the point (x₁, y₁) on the curve and the gradient m = f'(x₁). Geometrically, f'(x₁) is the instantaneous rate of change at x₁, which is exactly the slope of the tangent. Once both are known, the point–slope form y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) writes the line directly. This works because differentiation linearises the curve at the point of contact — the tangent is the best linear approximation there.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question wants a tangent line, so I need a point (x₁, y₁) and a gradient m at that point.
  2. The x-coordinate is given (or readily found from the named point); the y-coordinate comes from substituting into the original curve, NOT into the derivative.
  3. Consider how to get dy/dx: if a previous part already gave a derivative formula or an expanded polynomial, reuse it — re-deriving from a factored form via triple product rule would be unnecessarily messy and error-prone.
  4. Commit: differentiate (or quote part (b)'s derivative), then substitute x = x₁ to get a numerical gradient m.
  5. Tactical decision: if the gradient comes out as a surd like 1/√2, rationalise to √2/2 for a presentable final form; if the constant terms are messy, expand the point–slope form into y = mx + c.
  6. Assemble using y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) and simplify.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify x₁ from the question (or from the named point P).
  2. Compute y₁ by substituting x₁ into the ORIGINAL curve equation y = f(x).
  3. Obtain dy/dx — either differentiate term-by-term using the power rule on an expanded form, or quote the derivative from a previous part.
  4. Substitute x = x₁ into dy/dx to get the numerical gradient m = f'(x₁).
  5. Write y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) and simplify to the requested form (e.g. y = mx + c).

Worked Micro-Example

Curve: $y = 6x^3 + 67x^2 + 126x - 360$, tangent at $x = 0$. Point: $y_1 = -360$, so $(0, -360)$. Derivative: $\frac{dy}{dx} = 18x^2 + 134x + 126$, so $m = 126$ at $x=0$. Tangent: $y - (-360) = 126(x - 0)$, i.e. $y = 126x - 360$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Curve given in factored form y = (x−a)(x−b)(x−c)
Expand first (or use the expansion from an earlier part), then differentiate term-by-term — much cleaner than triple product rule.
Parametric curve x = x(t), y = y(t)
Compute dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) and evaluate at the parameter value t corresponding to the given point.
Implicitly defined curve F(x,y) = 0
Use implicit differentiation to obtain dy/dx in terms of x and y, then substitute both coordinates of the point.
Normal line instead of tangent
Take the negative reciprocal −1/m as the slope, then apply the same point–slope form.
Gradient comes out as a surd, e.g. 1/√(4−x²) at x=√2
Evaluate to 1/√2, then rationalise to √2/2 before writing the line equation.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'tangent' as 'tan(x)' or confusing with a tangent to a circle (which uses radius ⊥ tangent rather than calculus).
  • Trying to differentiate a factored cubic via repeated product rule when an expanded form is already available from a previous part.

Pitfalls

  • Substituting x₁ into dy/dx to get y₁ instead of into the original curve — this gives the gradient where the y-coordinate should be.
  • Arithmetic slips in differentiation: e.g. d/dx(67x²) = 134x, not 67x.
  • Forgetting to rationalise surd gradients, or mishandling (√2/2)(√2) = 1 (not √2/2 or 2).
  • Writing y = m(x − x₁) and dropping the +y₁ term entirely.
  • Substituting the y-coordinate into a derivative expressed in x (e.g. plugging y = π/4 into dy/dx = 1/√(4−x²)).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P1/P2/P3 material. Uses the power rule, chain rule, product/quotient rule, and (in P3) parametric/implicit differentiation — all in the formula booklet's differentiation section. The point–slope form y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) is assumed knowledge from coordinate geometry. Avoid invoking Taylor expansions or formal limit definitions — these are out of A-level scope.
