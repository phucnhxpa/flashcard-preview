Integration

Carry forward part (a) simplified √(1+(dy/dx)²) into part (b) surface area integral

Family: Cross Reference Reuse Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a multi-part question has part (a) computing or simplifying √(1+(dy/dx)²) (or the analogous parametric √((dx/dt)²+(dy/dt)²)) and part (b) asks for a surface area S = 2π ∫ y √(1+(dy/dx)²) dx (or arc length, or volume involving the same radical). The cue is a shared sub-expression between consecutive parts. Phrases like 'Hence find…', 'Use part (a) to…', or simply identical radicals appearing in both integrands all signal reuse.

Motivation — Why this works

Edexcel multi-part questions are deliberately scaffolded: part (a) builds a clean closed form precisely so part (b) becomes tractable. The surface-area integrand y√(1+(dy/dx)²) differs from the arc-length integrand only by the factor y, so the simplified radical from (a) plugs in directly. Reusing it avoids re-differentiating, eliminates algebraic risk, and often produces dramatic cancellation with y that makes the integral elementary.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the radical √(1+(dy/dx)²) needed in part (b)'s surface-area formula is identical to the expression simplified in part (a).
  2. Rule out re-deriving dy/dx and re-squaring — that path duplicates work and invites sign/algebra slips.
  3. Commit to the cross-reference because the question's structure (and often the word 'Hence') signals it.
  4. Bridge: write S = 2π ∫ y · [part (a) result] dx with the correct limits.
  5. Tactically check whether y times the radical produces a cancellation; if so, the integral usually collapses to a polynomial or standard form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Quote the part (a) result, e.g. √(1+(dy/dx)²) = 3/√(9−x²).
  2. Write y in terms of x from the original curve, e.g. y = √(9−x²).
  3. Substitute into S = 2π ∫ₐᵇ y √(1+(dy/dx)²) dx.
  4. Simplify the integrand by cancelling common factors (here √(9−x²) cancels, leaving a constant).
  5. Evaluate the resulting elementary integral over the stated limits.

Worked Micro-Example

Curve y = √(9−x²), 0 ≤ x ≤ 3. Part (a) gives √(1+(dy/dx)²) = 3/√(9−x²). Part (b): S = 2π ∫₀³ √(9−x²) · 3/√(9−x²) dx = 2π ∫₀³ 3 dx = 18π.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Parametric curve with part (a) simplifying √((dx/dt)²+(dy/dt)²)
Reuse that radical inside S = 2π ∫ y √((dx/dt)²+(dy/dt)²) dt with parameter limits.
Arc length in (a), surface of revolution about x-axis in (b)
Multiply the (a) integrand by y; about y-axis, multiply by x instead.
Reduction formula derived in (a), specific Iₙ requested in (b)
Apply the recurrence iteratively from a base case rather than integrating from scratch.
Partial fractions decomposition in (a) used inside an integral in (b)
Substitute the decomposition directly and integrate term by term.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating part (a) as a standalone simplification and ignoring its role in (b).
  • Reusing the radical but forgetting to multiply by y (so computing arc length instead of surface area).
  • Revolving about the wrong axis and using y instead of x (or vice versa) as the radius factor.

Pitfalls

  • Re-deriving dy/dx from scratch, wasting time and introducing sign errors.
  • Failing to cancel y with the denominator radical when cancellation is the whole point.
  • Carrying the wrong limits from (a) into (b) when the surface integral uses a different range.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure: arc length and surface area of revolution in Cartesian and parametric form, with standard 'hence' scaffolding across question parts.
