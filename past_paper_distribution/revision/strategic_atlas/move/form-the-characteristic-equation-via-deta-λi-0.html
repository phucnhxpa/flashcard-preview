Matrices

Form the characteristic equation via det(A − λI) = 0

Family: Eigenvalue Setup Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question asks for eigenvalues of a given square matrix, or for anything that requires them first (eigenvectors, diagonalisation P⁻¹AP = D, matrix powers Aⁿ, or verifying invariant lines/planes). Phrasings include 'find the eigenvalues of', 'show that λ = ... is an eigenvalue', 'find values of λ for which (A − λI)x = 0 has non-trivial solutions', or 'diagonalise A'. Also fires implicitly when a Cayley–Hamilton-style identity is to be exploited.

Motivation — Why this works

By definition λ is an eigenvalue of A iff Ax = λx has a non-zero solution x, equivalently (A − λI)x = 0 has a non-trivial solution. A homogeneous square system has non-trivial solutions exactly when its coefficient matrix is singular, i.e. det(A − λI) = 0. This turns the abstract eigenvalue condition into a polynomial equation in λ — quadratic for 2×2, cubic for 3×3 — which can then be solved by standard algebra.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the task needs eigenvalues, either directly or as a prerequisite for diagonalisation/powers.
  2. Rules out trial-and-error guessing of λ and rules out solving Ax = λx as a vector system before λ is known.
  3. Commits to det(A − λI) = 0 because it is the only systematic route to all eigenvalues at once.
  4. Bridges to computation by writing A − λI explicitly, subtracting λ from every diagonal entry, and choosing a determinant expansion route (row/column with most zeros).
  5. Tactically decides whether to expand fully into a polynomial in λ or to spot an obvious factor (e.g. a row sum giving λ = constant) before expanding.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write A − λI by subtracting λ from each diagonal entry of A, leaving off-diagonal entries unchanged.
  2. Choose a row or column for cofactor expansion (prefer one containing zeros or simple entries).
  3. Compute det(A − λI) as a polynomial in λ, being careful with alternating cofactor signs.
  4. Set the polynomial equal to zero to obtain the characteristic equation.
  5. Simplify/collect terms so the polynomial is in standard form ready for factoring.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$. Then $A - \lambda I = \begin{pmatrix} 2-\lambda & 1 \\ 1 & 2-\lambda \end{pmatrix}$. So $\det(A-\lambda I) = (2-\lambda)^2 - 1 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = 0$, giving $\lambda = 1$ or $\lambda = 3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

2×2 matrix
det(A − λI) = λ² − (trace A)λ + det A = 0; a quick shortcut bypassing full expansion.
Triangular or diagonal matrix
Eigenvalues are simply the diagonal entries; no determinant expansion needed.
3×3 matrix with a known eigenvalue (e.g. given in the question)
Substitute the given λ to verify det = 0, then factor (λ − λ₀) out of the cubic to find the others.
Matrix with a row or column summing to a constant k
λ = k is an eigenvalue (eigenvector (1,1,…,1)ᵀ); use this to factor the characteristic polynomial faster.

Common Misreadings

  • Setting up det(A + λI) = 0 or det(λI − A) = 0 with sign errors instead of det(A − λI) = 0.
  • Subtracting λ from only one diagonal entry, or from off-diagonal entries.
  • Trying to solve Ax = λx directly as a system before finding λ.
  • Confusing eigenvalues with diagonal entries for a non-triangular matrix.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when forming A − λI, particularly with negative diagonal entries (e.g. −3 − λ, not −3 + λ).
  • Forgetting alternating signs in cofactor expansion of a 3×3 determinant.
  • Algebra slips when expanding (a−λ)(b−λ)(c−λ) and collecting like powers of λ.
  • Stopping after finding one root of the cubic and not factoring out to find the remaining eigenvalues.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope: 2×2 and 3×3 real matrices with real eigenvalues, leading to quadratic or cubic characteristic equations solvable by inspection or factoring.
