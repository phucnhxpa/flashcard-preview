Coordinate Geometry

Transfer sector angle to sub-triangle when point lies on bounding radius

Family: Geometric Configuration Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A sector OAB has central angle θ at O, and a point P is stated to lie on one of the bounding radii (e.g. OB). You need an angle inside a sub-triangle such as △AOP for an area, cosine-rule, or sine-rule step. The cue is the phrase 'P lies on OB' (or 'on OA') combined with a known sector angle. Equivalent phrasings include 'P is a point on the radius OB' or 'OP extended meets...'.

Motivation — Why this works

If P lies on segment OB, then rays OP and OB are identical, so ∠AOP = ∠AOB = θ. This collinearity transfers the sector's central angle directly into the triangle without further computation, supplying the included angle needed for $\tfrac{1}{2}ab\sin C$ or the cosine rule. Without spotting this, the triangle appears under-determined and students invent extra assumptions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the wording 'P lies on OB' and mark O, P, B as collinear with P on the segment from O to B.
  2. Rule out the misreading that P is on arc AB or interior to the sector — those would require additional angle data.
  3. Commit to ∠AOP = ∠AOB because ray OP coincides with ray OB.
  4. Bridge: import this angle (in radians) directly into whichever triangle formula the question demands — usually area or cosine rule on △AOP.
  5. Tactically, keep the angle in radians throughout to match the sector's $\tfrac{1}{2}r^{2}\theta$ and avoid mode errors.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify that P is on radius OB, so O, P, B are collinear.
  2. Conclude ∠AOP = ∠AOB = θ (the given sector angle, in radians).
  3. Substitute this angle into the relevant triangle formula (area $\tfrac{1}{2}\cdot OA\cdot OP\sin\theta$, or cosine rule on △AOP).
  4. Solve for the unknown length (e.g. OP) using the given area/length condition.

Worked Micro-Example

Sector OAB has $OA=OB=5$, $\angle AOB=1.2$ rad. P lies on OB with area of △AOP equal to $6$. Since P is on OB, $\angle AOP=1.2$. Then $\tfrac{1}{2}(5)(OP)\sin 1.2 = 6 \Rightarrow OP = \dfrac{12}{5\sin 1.2} \approx 2.574$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Point Q lies on radius OA instead of OB
By symmetry ∠BOQ = ∠AOB = θ; apply the same logic to △BOQ.
Point P lies on chord AB rather than a radius
The angle transfer fails; instead use the cosine rule in △OAB to find AB, then locate P along AB by ratio or distance.
Point P on OB extended beyond B
Ray OP still coincides with ray OB, so ∠AOP = θ remains valid; only OP > OB changes.
Two sub-sectors sharing a radius (P on OB, Q on OA)
Each smaller triangle inherits the same central angle θ; combined area uses θ twice.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming P is the midpoint of OB and fixing OP = r/2 without using the given condition.
  • Reading 'P on OB' as 'P on arc AB' and trying to use circle theorems.
  • Switching to degrees for the triangle while the sector is in radians.

Pitfalls

  • Using $\sin\theta$ with θ in degree mode on the calculator while θ was given in radians.
  • Confusing ∠AOP with ∠APO or ∠OAP when applying the sine/cosine rule.
  • Forgetting that OP is unknown and treating it as equal to the radius OB.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P2/P3 sector-and-triangle material: uses radian measure, area $\tfrac{1}{2}r^{2}\theta$, triangle area $\tfrac{1}{2}ab\sin C$, and the cosine rule — no content beyond the IAL Pure syllabus.
