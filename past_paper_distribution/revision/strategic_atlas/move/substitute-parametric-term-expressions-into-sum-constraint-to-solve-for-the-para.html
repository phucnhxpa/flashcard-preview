Sequences & Series

Substitute parametric term expressions into sum constraint to solve for the parameter

Family: Parameter Determination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when each of the first few terms of a sequence is given as an expression in a single unknown parameter (e.g. $a_1 = p-3$, $a_2 = p-7$, $a_3 = p-11$) and the question imposes a numerical or parametric constraint on their sum, such as $\sum_{n=1}^{3} a_n = p + 15$. Equivalent phrasings include 'the sum of the first three terms is...', '$S_3 = \ldots$', or 'given that $a_1 + a_2 + a_3 = \ldots$'. The cue is that everything in the constraint can be written in terms of one variable, so the constraint becomes a single equation in that variable.

Motivation — Why this works

If each term is linear (or polynomial) in $p$, the sum is also linear (or polynomial) in $p$, so the constraint reduces to a one-variable equation. This is just a direct application of forming and solving an equation from given conditions — the standard A-level technique for parameter determination. It bypasses any need for AP/GP machinery when the terms are already explicit in $p$.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that all three terms $a_1, a_2, a_3$ are explicit linear expressions in the same parameter $p$, and the constraint involves their sum.
  2. Rule out invoking the AP sum formula $S_n = \tfrac{n}{2}(2a + (n-1)d)$ — it is no shorter here since the terms are already written out, and it would require extracting $a$ and $d$ first.
  3. Commit to direct substitution: build the LHS by adding the given expressions, then equate to the given RHS.
  4. Bridge to the first operation: write $(p-3) + (p-7) + (p-11)$ and simplify to $3p - 21$ before equating.
  5. Tactically, keep $p$-terms on both sides until the simplification is done, then collect $p$ on one side and constants on the other to solve cleanly; finally re-read the question to identify what is actually required (often $a_2$, not $p$).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write down each term in terms of $p$ and form the LHS sum: $(p-3) + (p-7) + (p-11)$.
  2. Simplify: $3p - 21$.
  3. Equate to the given RHS: $3p - 21 = p + 15$.
  4. Solve the linear equation: $2p = 36 \Rightarrow p = 18$.
  5. Back-substitute into the requested expression (e.g. $a_2 = p - 7 = 11$).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $a_1 = p-3$, $a_2 = p-7$, $a_3 = p-11$ with $\sum_{n=1}^{3} a_n = p + 15$. Sum: $(p-3)+(p-7)+(p-11) = 3p - 21$. Equation: $3p - 21 = p + 15 \Rightarrow 2p = 36 \Rightarrow p = 18$. Hence $a_2 = 18 - 7 = 11$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Terms quadratic in the parameter, e.g. $a_1 = p^2$, $a_2 = p^2 - 4$, with a sum constraint.
The resulting equation is quadratic in $p$; solve by factorising or the quadratic formula and report all valid roots (the question typically says 'possible values of $p$').
Product constraint instead of sum, e.g. $a_1 a_2 a_3 = k$ with each $a_i$ linear in $p$.
Expand the product to obtain a polynomial equation in $p$; solve and check any domain restrictions (e.g. positivity for a GP).
AP/GP context where consecutive terms are given parametrically and a common difference/ratio condition is implied.
Use $a_2 - a_1 = a_3 - a_2$ (AP) or $a_2/a_1 = a_3/a_2$ (GP) to form the equation in $p$ instead of a sum constraint.
Sum to $n$ given as $S_n = f(p)$ with terms parametric.
Either sum the terms directly or apply $S_n = \tfrac{n}{2}(2a + (n-1)d)$ if $n$ is large; equate to $f(p)$ and solve.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the RHS $p + 15$ as a constant, giving $3p - 21 = 15$ and $p = 12$.
  • Reading $\sum_{n=1}^{3} a_n$ as $a_2 + a_3$ only, omitting $a_1$.
  • Assuming the sequence is automatically arithmetic and computing $d$ before checking that the terms as given are consistent.

Pitfalls

  • Sign/arithmetic slip when collecting constants: $-3 - 7 - 11 = -21$ is sometimes miscomputed.
  • Stopping at $p = 18$ when the question asks for $a_2$ (or another specific term).
  • Failing to simplify before equating, leading to algebra errors when moving $p$-terms across.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 sequences and series: forming and solving a linear (occasionally quadratic) equation from a sum-of-terms constraint, with no techniques beyond standard algebra.
