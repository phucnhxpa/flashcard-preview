Sequences & Series

Split piecewise sum into AP block plus shifted GP partial sum

Family: Sum Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a sequence is defined piecewise across an index boundary (e.g. AP for the first M terms, then GP from term M+1 onward) and a question asks for a total or an inequality on the cumulative sum past that boundary. Cues include phrases like 'after the 20th bounce/hit', 'from the kth term onwards the rule changes', or two distinct recurrence rules joined at a fixed index. No single closed-form S_n covers both regimes, so a decomposition is forced.

Motivation — Why this works

Sums are additive over disjoint index ranges, so Total = S_AP(first M) + S_GP(remaining k = N − M). Re-indexing the GP block so its first term is the term immediately after the boundary lets the standard formula a(1 − r^k)/(1 − r) apply directly, with the AP portion frozen as a known constant. This converts a two-regime problem into one inequality or equation in the single unknown k, which is the form A-level techniques can solve.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the rule for successive terms changes at a fixed index, so no single AP or GP formula spans the whole range.
  2. Rule out trying to force one closed form (e.g. treating the whole thing as a GP) — the first M terms genuinely follow a different rule.
  3. Commit to additivity of sums: split the total at the boundary index and handle each block with its own formula.
  4. Introduce a shifted counter k = N − M for the GP block so its 'first term' is the term just after the AP ends, and r is the GP ratio.
  5. Substitute the known AP block as a numerical constant, leaving a clean GP inequality/equation in k that can be solved by logarithms.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the boundary index M where the rule changes and compute S_AP for the first M terms as a number.
  2. Let k = N − M count the terms in the second regime.
  3. Identify the first term a' of the GP block (the term at index M+1) and the common ratio r.
  4. Write S_GP = a'(1 − r^k)/(1 − r) using the partial-sum formula.
  5. Form the equation/inequality S_AP + S_GP ⋛ Target.
  6. Isolate r^k, take logs (noting inequality flip if log of a number < 1) to solve for k, then recover N = M + k.

Worked Micro-Example

First 20 hits form an AP with sum 1620; from hit 21 the distances form a GP with first term 62 and ratio 30/31. Find smallest N with total > 3000. Let k = N − 20, so S_GP = 62(1 − (30/31)^k)/(1/31) = 1922(1 − (30/31)^k). Inequality: 1620 + 1922(1 − (30/31)^k) > 3000 ⇒ (30/31)^k < 271/961. Take logs: k > \ln(271/961)/\ln(30/31) ≈ 38.6, so k = 39 and N = 59.

Variants — Same move, different surface

AP followed by a constant tail (terms become fixed after index M)
Replace S_GP with k × c, where c is the constant value; inequality becomes linear in k.
GP followed by a different GP at index M
Use two GP partial-sum formulas; re-index the second GP so its first term sits at position M+1.
Sum to infinity past the boundary (|r| < 1, asking whether total ever exceeds T)
Replace S_GP with a'/(1 − r) and test S_AP + a'/(1 − r) against T to decide if T is reachable at all.
Two AP regimes joined at index M
Use n/2(2a + (n−1)d) on each block separately with the second block's own first term and common difference.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 'from hit 21 the distances form a GP with first term 62' as meaning the GP starts at index 1 with a = 62 of the whole sum, rather than as a shifted block.
  • Assuming the question wants a sum to infinity because a GP with |r| < 1 appears, when it actually wants a partial sum up to a finite N.
  • Treating the boundary index inclusively on both sides and double-counting the term at index M+1.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to add the AP block (e.g. 1620) back in and solving the GP inequality against the full target.
  • Using S_∞ = a/(1 − r) instead of the partial sum a(1 − r^k)/(1 − r).
  • Failing to flip the inequality when dividing by ln(r) with 0 < r < 1 (a negative number).
  • Mis-shifting the index: writing k = N − 19 or k = N − 21 instead of k = N − 20.
  • Rounding k down instead of up when seeking the smallest N satisfying a strict inequality.

A-level Scope Notes
