Polar Coordinates

Set up polar area integral with correct limits and symmetry

Family: Polar Area Setup Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question asks for the area enclosed by a polar curve, the area of one petal/loop of a curve like r = a sin(nθ) or a cardioid/limaçon, the area between two polar curves, or the area of a sector-type region bounded by the curve and one or two radial lines θ = α, θ = β. Visual cues include phrases like 'find the area of the finite region bounded by...', 'shaded region', or a diagram showing a closed loop or sector. Symmetry about the initial line, about θ = π/2, or n-fold rotational symmetry of a rose curve is a strong further cue.

Motivation — Why this works

The area swept by the radius vector from θ = α to θ = β is A = ½∫_α^β r² dθ, derived from summing infinitesimal sector triangles of area ½r²δθ. For two curves with r₁ ≥ r₂ throughout, A = ½∫(r₁² − r₂²) dθ by direct subtraction of swept areas. Exploiting symmetry (integrate over half/quarter/one petal and multiply) avoids sign errors and shortens algebra; alternatively, integrating over a full period [0, 2π] can be advantageous when the integrand contains cos(nθ) terms that vanish over a full period.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question asks for an enclosed area in polar form — the formula A = ½∫r² dθ (or ½∫(r₁² − r₂²) dθ for two curves) is the standard tool, listed in the formula booklet.
  2. Rule out converting to Cartesian (messy) and rule out treating the inner circle's area as a separate πr² subtraction — keeping everything inside the integrand is cleaner.
  3. Examine the symmetry/structure: is the curve symmetric about θ = 0 or θ = π/2? Does it have n-fold rotational symmetry? Where does r = 0 (giving the natural petal/loop limits)? Are radial lines θ = α, β given as boundaries?
  4. Decide the integration domain. Three common choices: (i) one petal/loop using r = 0 endpoints; (ii) half the curve and double via symmetry; (iii) a full period [0, 2π] when cos(nθ) terms will integrate to zero and simplify evaluation.
  5. Commit to writing A = ½∫_α^β r² dθ with the chosen limits. For two-curve problems, verify which curve is outer on the chosen interval (so r₁² − r₂² ≥ 0).
  6. Substitute r(θ) and expand r² immediately to a trig polynomial ready for standard reduction (double-angle / power-reduction).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify boundary type: closed loop (use r = 0 to find limits), petal of rose r = a sin(nθ) (limits where sin(nθ) = 0), sector bounded by radial lines (limits given), or two-curve region (find intersections by solving r₁(θ) = r₂(θ)).
  2. State symmetry, if any, and the resulting multiplier (×2 for symmetry about initial line, ×n for n petals).
  3. Write A = (multiplier) × ½∫_α^β r² dθ, or A = ½∫_α^β (r₁² − r₂²) dθ for area between curves with r₁ outer.
  4. Substitute r(θ) and expand: e.g. (1 + cos θ)² = 1 + 2cos θ + cos²θ, ready for power-reduction cos²θ = ½(1 + cos 2θ).
  5. Check the ½ factor and the squared r are both present before evaluating.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the area of one petal of $r = 2\sin 3\theta$. Petal limits: $r = 0$ when $3\theta = 0, \pi$, so $\theta \in [0, \pi/3]$. Then $A = \tfrac{1}{2}\int_0^{\pi/3} 4\sin^2 3\theta\, d\theta = \int_0^{\pi/3} (1 - \cos 6\theta)\, d\theta = \left[\theta - \tfrac{1}{6}\sin 6\theta\right]_0^{\pi/3} = \tfrac{\pi}{3}.$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Rose curve r = a sin(nθ) — area of one petal vs all petals
One petal: integrate over [0, π/n]. All petals: multiply by n (for odd n) or 2n (for even n, since negative-r repetitions create extra petals).
Two curves with one always outside the other (e.g. r₁ = 4 − 3cos 6θ, r₂ = 1)
Use A = ½∫₀^{2π}(r₁² − r₂²) dθ over the full period — cos(nθ) integrals vanish, leaving only constant terms.
Limaçon with inner loop (e.g. r = 1 + 2cos θ)
Find the two θ-values with r = 0 (cos θ = −½, so θ = 2π/3, 4π/3); integrate ½r² between these for inner-loop area.
Region bounded by curve and two radial lines (sector type)
The radial lines give the limits directly; no symmetry doubling and no subtraction needed — A = ½∫_α^β r² dθ.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the inner circle's area as a separate πr² to subtract instead of including −r₂² inside the integrand.
  • Computing the whole curve's area when only one petal is asked, or vice versa.
  • Setting limits 0 to 2π for r = a cos θ, which traces the circle twice and doubles the area.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the ½ factor in front of the integral.
  • Writing r in the integrand instead of r².
  • For two-curve regions, assuming the same curve is outer throughout when in fact they swap at intersection points — must split the integral.
  • Choosing limits that don't correspond to r = 0 for a closed petal/loop, leaving the region not properly closed.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) polar coordinates. The formula A = ½∫r² dθ is in the formula booklet. Avoid out-of-scope generalisations to arc-length-weighted or signed-area conventions; treat r ≥ 0 on the integration interval.
