Strategic Atlas / Trigonometry / Ratio Elimination
Trigonometry

Divide the two coefficient equations to isolate tan α and recover the auxiliary angle

Family: Ratio Elimination Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

This move fires immediately after the R cos α / R sin α coefficient-matching step in an Rsin(x ± α) or Rcos(x ± α) problem: you have a pair of simultaneous equations of the form R cos α = a and R sin α = b (or the swapped version), R has typically just been found via R = √(a² + b²), but α is still unknown. Visual cue: two equations sharing the common factor R with constants on the right-hand sides. Equivalent phrasings: 'eliminate R by taking the ratio', 'form tan α from the coefficient pair', 'kill the common R-factor'. The move is also recognisable when, in a right-angled triangle context, you have two known sides and want the angle between them via a single ratio.

Motivation — Why this works

Dividing the sine-equation by the cosine-equation cancels R on both sides because (R sin α)/(R cos α) = sin α / cos α = tan α, leaving a single-variable equation tan α = b/a that inverts cleanly with arctan. This is preferable to substituting the computed R back into one equation (cos α = a/R or sin α = b/R) because it (i) avoids dependence on a possibly rounded R, (ii) avoids the ±-ambiguity that arccos and arcsin can introduce, and (iii) is the standard examiner-expected method. The underlying principle is the quotient identity tan = sin/cos.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structural cue: two equations R cos α = a and R sin α = b share the common factor R, and α is the only remaining unknown.
  2. Rule out re-substituting the numerical R into one equation — that would force a choice between arccos and arcsin, each carrying its own quadrant ambiguity, and would also propagate any rounding error in R.
  3. Commit to taking the ratio (sine equation) ÷ (cosine equation) because the R's cancel exactly and the quotient identity sin α / cos α = tan α delivers α through a single arctan call.
  4. Check the signs of R cos α and R sin α from the original equations to fix the quadrant of α before pressing arctan: if both are positive, α is in Q1 and the principal arctan value is correct; if signs are mixed, adjust by ±180° using a CAST diagram.
  5. Tactical choice: put the sine equation on top so the ratio is b/a (not a/b) — this is the easy place to slip up. Then evaluate α = arctan(b/a), and finally verify α lies in the range required by the question.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Form the ratio: (R sin α)/(R cos α) = b/a.
  2. Simplify using sin α / cos α = tan α: tan α = b/a.
  3. Apply inverse tangent: α = arctan(b/a).
  4. Use the signs of the original a and b to confirm the correct quadrant; adjust by 180° if necessary.
  5. State α to the required precision and in the required units (degrees or radians).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $R\cos\alpha = 8$ and $R\sin\alpha = 15$ with $R = 17$: divide to get $\tan\alpha = \tfrac{15}{8}$. Both $a=8>0$ and $b=15>0$, so $\alpha$ is in Q1. Hence $\alpha = \arctan(15/8) \approx 61.93°$, giving $8\sin x + 15\cos x = 17\sin(x+61.93°)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Form R cos(x − α) used instead of R sin(x + α)
The coefficient equations swap roles (R cos α now equals the cosine-coefficient, R sin α equals the sine-coefficient); the divide-to-get-tan technique is unchanged, but be careful which equation supplies the numerator.
One of a, b is negative
arctan returns a value in (−90°, 90°); use the signs of R cos α and R sin α with a CAST diagram to push α into the correct quadrant (add 180° for Q2/Q3, etc.).
Right-triangle angle from two known sides
Pick the trig ratio (sin/cos/tan) whose definition uses exactly the two known sides, then invert — same ratio-selection logic, no R to cancel.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the problem as still needing R, and substituting the rounded R back in to find α via arccos — works but loses marks and accuracy.
  • Reading the cue as a Pythagorean-identity problem (sin² + cos² = 1) rather than a ratio problem.

Pitfalls

  • Inverting the ratio: writing tan α = a/b instead of b/a — the sine-coefficient must be in the numerator because tan = sin/cos.
  • Failing to check the quadrant when a or b is negative; arctan alone returns values in (−90°, 90°) and may need a 180° adjustment.
  • Calculator in the wrong angle mode — giving α in degrees when radians are required (or vice versa).
  • Stating α outside the range specified by the question (e.g. 0 < α < 90°), indicating an earlier sign-selection error in the R-form.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P3 harmonic-form / R sin(x ± α) topic) and used routinely in M1/M2 force-resolution and Further Pure auxiliary-angle work. The identity tan α = sin α / cos α is in the IAL formula booklet. Stay with principal-value arctan plus quadrant correction; do not invoke complex-exponential or general-solution machinery, which is out of scope.
