Trigger — When to use

Fires after taking logs (or otherwise reducing) an exponential equation, yielding an equation where x appears multiplied by and added to fixed irrational constants such as $\ln 3$, $\ln 4$, $\sin 1$, or $e$. Concretely, the post-log form looks like $(2x-1)\ln 3 = (x+2)\ln 4$ — structurally identical to $ax+b=cx+d$. The cue is: x appears only to the first power, with no $x^2$ or product of x-terms, but the coefficients are unfamiliar symbols rather than integers.

Motivation — Why this works

Logarithms and other transcendentals like $\ln 3$, $\ln 4$, $\pi$, or $\sin 1$ are just fixed real numbers — they obey the same field axioms as $2$ or $\tfrac{1}{2}$. So an equation linear in x with such coefficients is solved by the same routine: expand, collect x-terms, factor, divide. Mentally relabelling $\ln 3 \to a$, $\ln 4 \to b$ exposes the linear structure and removes the psychological barrier created by unfamiliar-looking coefficients.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice x appears to the first power on both sides, multiplied by $\ln 3$ and $\ln 4$ — no $x^2$, no $\ln x$, no products of x with itself.
  2. I rule out trying to combine $\ln 3$ and $\ln 4$ further (e.g., into $\ln 12$ or $\ln(3/4)$) — that would only matter if they multiplied the same x-coefficient, which they don't yet.
  3. I commit to the linear-equation template because mentally substituting $a=\ln 3$, $b=\ln 4$ reveals the form $(2x-1)a = (x+2)b$, a routine GCSE-level rearrangement.
  4. First operation: expand the brackets to separate x-terms from constant terms.
  5. Tactical decisions: keep $\ln 3$ and $\ln 4$ as exact symbols throughout (don't decimalise), factor x cleanly, and present the answer as a single fraction $x=\dfrac{2\ln 4+\ln 3}{2\ln 3-\ln 4}$, optionally simplifying via $2\ln 4=\ln 16$ etc.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Expand both sides: $2x\ln 3 - \ln 3 = x\ln 4 + 2\ln 4$.
  2. Move all x-terms to one side, constants to the other: $2x\ln 3 - x\ln 4 = 2\ln 4 + \ln 3$.
  3. Factor x: $x(2\ln 3 - \ln 4) = 2\ln 4 + \ln 3$.
  4. Divide: $x = \dfrac{2\ln 4 + \ln 3}{2\ln 3 - \ln 4}$.
  5. Optionally tidy using log laws: numerator $=\ln 48$, denominator $=\ln(9/4)$, giving $x=\dfrac{\ln 48}{\ln(9/4)}$.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $3^{2x-1}=4^{x+2}$. Take $\ln$: $(2x-1)\ln 3=(x+2)\ln 4$. Expand: $2x\ln 3-\ln 3=x\ln 4+2\ln 4$. Collect: $x(2\ln 3-\ln 4)=2\ln 4+\ln 3$. Hence $x=\dfrac{2\ln 4+\ln 3}{2\ln 3-\ln 4}=\dfrac{\ln 48}{\ln(9/4)}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation of the form $e^{ax}=k\,e^{bx}$ (P3/C3 style)
Take $\ln$ to get $ax=\ln k+bx$, then $x=\dfrac{\ln k}{a-b}$ — same factor-and-divide routine.
Mixed-base exponential like $5^{x+1}=2\cdot 7^{x}$
Take $\ln$: $(x+1)\ln 5=\ln 2+x\ln 7$; expand and isolate x exactly as before.
Trig-coefficient linear equation, e.g. $x\sin 1+3=x\cos 1+1$
Treat $\sin 1,\cos 1$ as constants; collect, factor: $x(\sin 1-\cos 1)=-2$.
Equations with $\pi$ or $e$ as coefficients, e.g. $\pi x+1=ex+2$
Identical structure: $x(\pi-e)=1$, so $x=1/(\pi-e)$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $\ln 3-\ln 4$ as $\ln(3-4)=\ln(-1)$ instead of $\ln(3/4)$ — confusing subtraction with the quotient rule.
  • Believing the equation is 'non-linear' because the coefficients are irrational, and reaching for numerical methods unnecessarily.
  • Trying to 'cancel' $\ln 3$ against $\ln 4$ as if they were like terms.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors moving the $-\ln 3$ term across the equals sign.
  • Incomplete factoring: writing $x(2\ln 3)-\ln 4$ instead of $x(2\ln 3-\ln 4)$, forgetting that both x-terms share the factor.
  • Decimalising $\ln 3, \ln 4$ early and losing the exact form expected by the markscheme.
  • Forgetting to check $2\ln 3-\ln 4\ne 0$ (i.e. $\ln 9\ne\ln 4$) before dividing — trivially true here but a habit worth keeping.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 (exponentials and logarithms) and reappears in P4 with mixed-base equations. All manipulations use only the linear-equation template and standard log laws.
