Match given polynomial to tan(nθ)=k identity to fix k by coefficient comparison

Family: Coefficient Matching Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a polynomial equation in x appears immediately after a part (a) that derived an expansion of tan(nθ) as a rational function in t = tan θ. Cues: the polynomial's degree equals n, leading and constant coefficients are equal (often both 1), and the middle coefficients echo the binomial pattern from (1+it)^n. The phrasing 'hence solve' or 'show that the roots are tan(...)' confirms the link.

Motivation — Why this works

Setting tan(nθ) = k and cross-multiplying the identity tan(nθ) = N(t)/D(t) yields k·D(t) − N(t) = 0, a degree-n polynomial in t whose n roots are tan θ for the n solutions of tan(nθ) = k in one period. Matching this to the given polynomial determines k uniquely, converting algebraic root-finding into evaluating arctan((arctan k + rπ)/n). The structural fingerprint (binomial coefficients with alternating signs) makes the match unambiguous.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the given polynomial has degree equal to n from part (a), and its leading and constant coefficients match — a hallmark of the tan(nθ) form after dividing by k.
  2. Rule out direct factorisation or rational root theorem because the coefficient pattern (binomial-like, with mixed signs) signals irrational tan-valued roots.
  3. Commit to the identification once the t^(n−2) coefficient (which is k-independent after dividing by k) matches: this confirms the structural fit rather than coincidence.
  4. Bridge: write tan(nθ) = k as k·D(t) = N(t), expand, divide by k, then read off k from the t^(n−1) or t coefficient.
  5. Tactically, use one coefficient to solve for k and a second to cross-check; then state the n roots as tan((arctan k + rπ)/n) for r = 0,1,…,n−1.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Recall the part (a) identity, e.g. tan 4θ = (4t − 4t^3)/(1 − 6t^2 + t^4).
  2. Set tan(nθ) = k and cross-multiply: k·D(t) − N(t) = 0.
  3. For n = 4: k·t^4 − 4t^3 − 6k·t^2 + 4t + k = 0.
  4. Divide through by k (assuming k ≠ 0): t^4 − (4/k)t^3 − 6t^2 + (4/k)t + 1 = 0.
  5. Compare with the given polynomial coefficient by coefficient to solve for k.
  6. Verify with a second coefficient, then write roots as t = tan((arctan k + rπ)/n).

Worked Micro-Example

Given x^4 + 2x^3 − 6x^2 − 2x + 1 = 0 and tan 4θ = (4t − 4t^3)/(1 − 6t^2 + t^4). Setting tan 4θ = k and rearranging gives t^4 − (4/k)t^3 − 6t^2 + (4/k)t + 1 = 0. Matching the t^3 coefficient: −(4/k) = 2, so k = −2 (check: 4/k = −2 ✓). Hence the four roots are x = tan((arctan(−2) + rπ)/4) for r = 0,1,2,3.

Variants — Same move, different surface

tan 3θ problem yielding a cubic in t
Use tan 3θ = (3t − t^3)/(1 − 3t^2); set equal to k, rearrange to k·t^3 − 3t^2 − 3k·t + 1 = 0, divide by k and match to the given cubic to find k.
tan 5θ problem yielding a quintic
Use tan 5θ = (5t − 10t^3 + t^5)/(1 − 10t^2 + 5t^4); same comparison procedure delivers k and five tan-valued roots.
Polynomial scaled or shifted (e.g. ax^4 + …)
Divide the given polynomial by its leading coefficient first to normalise, then match.
Sum-of-roots or product-of-roots required instead of solving
After identifying k, use Vieta's formulae on the polynomial to express Σtan θ_r or Πtan θ_r in closed form.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to factor the polynomial by inspection or via the rational root theorem when the roots are irrational tan-values.
  • Failing to spot the palindrome-like coefficient structure (e.g. 1, 2, −6, −2, 1) that signals the tan(nθ) origin.
  • Treating part (a) as unrelated scaffolding rather than the key to part (b).

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when cross-multiplying tan(nθ) = k, especially confusing numerator and denominator terms.
  • Solving −(4/k) = 2 as k = +2 instead of k = −2 by mishandling the negative sign.
  • Forgetting to divide by k before matching, leading to inconsistent comparisons.
  • Listing only one root (r = 0) instead of all n roots from r = 0,…,n−1.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure technique: combines de Moivre-derived multiple-angle tan identities with polynomial coefficient comparison, both within the FP syllabus.
