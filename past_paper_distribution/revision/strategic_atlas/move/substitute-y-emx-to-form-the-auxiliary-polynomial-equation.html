Strategic Atlas / Differential Equations / Auxiliary Equation Method
Differential Equations

Substitute y = e^{mx} to form the auxiliary polynomial equation

Family: Auxiliary Equation Method Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The ODE is linear with constant (numerical) coefficients on y, y', y'' — i.e. of the form a y'' + b y' + c y = f(x), where a, b, c are constants, not functions of x. The cue is purely structural: every coefficient in front of a derivative is a number. This applies whether the RHS is zero (pure homogeneous) or non-zero (where you still need this move to find the complementary function). Phrasings include 'find the general solution', 'find the complementary function', or 'solve d²y/dx² + ... = 0'.

Motivation — Why this works

For a constant-coefficient linear ODE, the homogeneous solutions must be exponentials, because e^{mx} is the unique function (up to scalar) whose derivatives are scalar multiples of itself. Substituting y = e^{mx} turns d^k y/dx^k into m^k e^{mx}; the common factor e^{mx} ≠ 0 cancels, collapsing the differential equation into a polynomial equation in m. This converts a calculus problem into an algebra problem — solving a quadratic (or higher) instead of a differential equation. The roots of this polynomial then encode the entire complementary function via the standard CF rules.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the ODE is second-order linear with constant coefficients (every coefficient in front of y, y', y'' is a number).
  2. Rule out integrating factor (which is for first-order linear ODEs) and separation of variables (which needs the equation to factor as g(y)dy = h(x)dx); neither applies here.
  3. Recall that for constant-coefficient linear ODEs the homogeneous solutions are exponentials, so commit to the trial y = e^{mx}.
  4. Bridge to the operation: differentiate y = e^{mx} to get y' = m e^{mx} and y'' = m² e^{mx}, substitute into the homogeneous equation, and divide through by e^{mx}.
  5. Read off the auxiliary equation directly from the coefficients: a y'' + b y' + c y = 0 ⟶ a m² + b m + c = 0, being careful with signs.
  6. Solve the quadratic and classify the roots into one of three cases (two distinct real, one repeated real, or complex conjugate p ± qi) so the correct CF template can be applied next.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Set the RHS to zero to obtain the associated homogeneous equation a y'' + b y' + c y = 0.
  2. Substitute y = e^{mx}, y' = m e^{mx}, y'' = m² e^{mx}.
  3. Cancel the common factor e^{mx} to obtain the auxiliary equation a m² + b m + c = 0.
  4. Solve for m by factorisation, the quadratic formula m = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac))/(2a), or completing the square.
  5. Classify the roots: distinct real m₁, m₂; repeated real m; or complex p ± qi — to feed into the CF formula at the next step.

Worked Micro-Example

For $2y'' - 5y' + 3y = 0$: substitute $y = e^{mx}$ to get $(2m^2 - 5m + 3)e^{mx} = 0$. Cancelling $e^{mx}$ gives the auxiliary equation $2m^2 - 5m + 3 = 0$, which factors as $(2m-3)(m-1)=0$, so $m = \tfrac{3}{2}$ or $m = 1$. Hence the CF is $y = A e^{x} + B e^{3x/2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

First-order constant-coefficient ODE a y' + b y = 0
Auxiliary equation is linear: a m + b = 0, giving a single root m = −b/a and CF y = A e^{mx}.
Third-order constant-coefficient ODE (Further Pure extension)
Auxiliary equation is cubic; use the factor theorem to find one rational root, then factor out the linear factor and solve the remaining quadratic.
Non-homogeneous ODE a y'' + b y' + c y = f(x)
Use this move to find the CF only; then add a particular integral found by trial form matched to f(x).

Common Misreadings

  • Treating an ODE with variable coefficients (e.g. x y'' + y' = 0) as constant-coefficient — the auxiliary-equation method does NOT apply there.
  • Forgetting that the RHS must be set to zero first; substituting y = e^{mx} into the full non-homogeneous equation gives nonsense.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when the ODE has negative coefficients — e.g. writing 2m² + 5m + 3 instead of 2m² − 5m + 3 from 2y'' − 5y' + 3y = 0.
  • Dropping the leading coefficient (writing m² − 5m + 3 instead of 2m² − 5m + 3), which gives wrong roots.
  • Arithmetic slips in the discriminant b² − 4ac, especially with fractional or large coefficients.
  • Forcing real roots when the discriminant is negative — failing to handle the complex-conjugate case p ± qi.

A-level Scope Notes

This move is the standard FP2 (Edexcel IAL Further Pure 2) technique for second-order linear ODEs with constant coefficients; the auxiliary equation and CF root-classification are in the FP2 specification. Avoid generalising to variable-coefficient equations (e.g. Euler/Cauchy equations) or systems of ODEs, which are outside the IAL scope.
