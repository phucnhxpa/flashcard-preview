Algebra & Functions

Apply square-of-sum identity to express α²+β² via Vieta's

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the target is a symmetric expression in two roots α, β of a quadratic — specifically α² + β² — but only the sum α+β and product αβ are accessible (typically via Vieta's formulas from the coefficients). Visual cue: a quadratic equation is given (or implied) and you are asked for a degree-2 symmetric quantity that is NOT directly readable from coefficients. Equivalent phrasings: 'find the sum of squares of the roots', 'roots α, β of ax²+bx+c=0, find α²+β²', or any setup where individual roots would be irrational/messy but their elementary symmetric functions are clean.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity (α+β)² = α² + 2αβ + β² is the bridge between the power sum p₂ = α²+β² and the elementary symmetric polynomials e₁ = α+β and e₂ = αβ. Rearranging gives α² + β² = (α+β)² − 2αβ, which expresses the target purely in terms of quantities already obtainable from Vieta's (−b/a and c/a). This avoids solving the quadratic explicitly, dodging surds and arithmetic risk, and is the standard Newton's-identity entry point for symmetric functions of roots.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question asks for α² + β², but solving the quadratic would give irrational or awkward roots — operating on those individually is error-prone.
  2. I recall that Vieta's gives me α+β and αβ cleanly from the coefficients, so I should aim to express the target in those two quantities only.
  3. I rule out: (a) explicit root extraction via the quadratic formula (messy), and (b) full Newton's identities machinery (overkill for degree 2).
  4. I commit to the square-of-sum identity because (α+β)² expanded contains both α²+β² (what I want) and 2αβ (what I know), so a single rearrangement isolates the target.
  5. Tactical step: write α² + β² = (α+β)² − 2αβ, then substitute the Vieta values and simplify carefully (watch the −2αβ sign and any common-denominator arithmetic).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. From the quadratic ax² + bx + c = 0, read off α + β = −b/a and αβ = c/a (Vieta's).
  2. Write the bridge identity: α² + β² = (α + β)² − 2αβ.
  3. Substitute the Vieta values: α² + β² = (−b/a)² − 2(c/a).
  4. Simplify to a single fraction (find common denominator if needed): α² + β² = (b² − 2ac)/a².

Worked Micro-Example

Given 2x² − 5x + 4 = 0 with roots α, β. Vieta's: α+β = 5/2, αβ = 2. Then α² + β² = (5/2)² − 2(2) = 25/4 − 16/4 = 9/4.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Target is α³ + β³
Use α³ + β³ = (α+β)³ − 3αβ(α+β); same family of power-sum-to-elementary-symmetric reduction, one degree higher.
Target is α⁴ + β⁴
Iterate: α⁴ + β⁴ = (α²+β²)² − 2(αβ)², reusing the degree-2 result.
Target is (α − β)² or |α − β|
Use (α − β)² = (α+β)² − 4αβ — same square-expansion logic with a sign flip on the cross-term.
Forming a new quadratic with roots α², β²
Need both sum α²+β² (this move) and product α²β² = (αβ)², then assemble x² − (α²+β²)x + (αβ)² = 0.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing α² + β² with (α + β)² and forgetting to subtract 2αβ.
  • Reading αβ off Vieta's with the wrong sign (writing αβ = −c/a instead of c/a, or α+β = b/a instead of −b/a).

Pitfalls

  • Sign error on the −2αβ term, especially when αβ is itself negative (double-negative gives an addition).
  • Arithmetic slip when combining (α+β)² and 2αβ over a common denominator — e.g. converting an integer 4 to 16/4 incorrectly.
  • Forgetting to square the denominator: (−b/a)² = b²/a², not b²/a.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL scope: Vieta's relations for quadratics appear in P1/P2 and Further Pure (FP1) work on roots of polynomial equations. The identity (α+β)² = α²+2αβ+β² is assumed known (not in the formula booklet). Avoid invoking general Newton's identities or symmetric-function theory by name — stay with the direct algebraic rearrangement.
