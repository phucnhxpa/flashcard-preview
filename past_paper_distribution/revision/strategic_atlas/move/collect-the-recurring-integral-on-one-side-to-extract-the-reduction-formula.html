Algebra & Functions

Collect the recurring integral on one side to extract the reduction formula

Family: Self Reference Resolution Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires during a reduction-formula derivation when, after integration by parts (often combined with a Pythagorean identity such as sin²x = 1 − cos²x or sec²x = 1 + tan²x), the original integral I_n reappears on the right-hand side. Visually, the equation has the form I_n = [boundary term] + (expression)·I_{n−2} ± (expression)·I_n. Equivalently: 'the integral I started with has come back', or 'I_n is on both sides'. This self-reference is not an error — it is precisely the structural cue that a reduction relation can now be solved for algebraically.

Motivation — Why this works

Once I_n appears on both sides, the equation is linear in the unknown I_n, so it can be solved by ordinary algebra rather than by further calculus. Collecting like-terms in I_n converts a tangled identity into a clean recurrence I_n = f(n)·I_{n−2} (+ boundary terms), which is the entire point of the reduction-formula technique. The underlying principle is simply 'treat the integral as an unknown variable in a linear equation' — the same move one uses to solve x = a + bx for x.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that after applying integration by parts (and possibly a Pythagorean identity to rewrite a factor like sin²x or tan²x), the original integral I_n has reappeared on the right-hand side.
  2. Rule out the temptation to restart or to try a different IBP split — the reappearance of I_n is the intended structure of a reduction derivation, not a dead end.
  3. Rule out attempting another integration: the equation is now algebraic in I_n, so calculus is no longer required.
  4. Commit to treating I_n as an unknown in a linear equation: identify all terms containing I_n and prepare to collect them on the left-hand side.
  5. Tactically decide the sign: if the right has −(n−1)I_n, add (n−1)I_n to both sides; if it has +cI_n, subtract cI_n. Then factor the coefficient of I_n and divide, noting the divisor (e.g. n) is non-zero in the relevant range.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Start from the post-IBP equation, e.g. I_n = (n−1)I_{n−2} − (n−1)I_n (or with a boundary term included).
  2. Add (n−1)I_n to both sides: I_n + (n−1)I_n = (n−1)I_{n−2}.
  3. Factor the left-hand side: nI_n = (n−1)I_{n−2}.
  4. Divide both sides by n (valid for n ≥ 1) to isolate I_n: I_n = ((n−1)/n)·I_{n−2}.
  5. If a boundary term [g(x)]_a^b was present, carry it through the same collect-and-divide step so it is divided by the same coefficient.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose IBP on $I_n = \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^n x\,dx$ yields $I_n = (n-1)I_{n-2} - (n-1)I_n$. Add $(n-1)I_n$ to both sides: $nI_n = (n-1)I_{n-2}$. Divide by $n$: $I_n = \dfrac{n-1}{n}\,I_{n-2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Reduction for ∫ sec^n x dx, where IBP gives I_n = sec^{n−2}x tan x − (n−2)(I_n − I_{n−2}) (with a boundary term in definite case).
Collect I_n terms: (n−1)I_n = sec^{n−2}x tan x + (n−2)I_{n−2}; divide by (n−1) instead of n. Same logic, different coefficient.
Reduction for ∫ tan^n x dx using tan²x = sec²x − 1.
The identity directly produces I_n + I_{n−2} = (tan^{n−1}x)/(n−1); rearrange to I_n = (tan^{n−1}x)/(n−1) − I_{n−2}. No division needed, but the same 'isolate I_n' principle applies.
Definite-integral version with non-vanishing boundary term.
Keep the boundary term [uv]_a^b on the right throughout the collect-and-divide; it is divided by the same coefficient as I_{n−2}.

Common Misreadings

  • Believing the reappearance of I_n indicates a wrong choice of u and dv, and abandoning the derivation to start over.
  • Trying to integrate the I_n term again instead of recognising it as a known unknown to be solved for algebraically.
  • Confusing I_n with I_{n−2} in the final line and writing I_n = ((n−1)/n)·I_n.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when moving the I_n term across the equals sign — subtracting when one should add (or vice versa) gives the wrong coefficient.
  • Forgetting to divide a boundary term by the same factor (n or n−1) as the I_{n−2} term.
  • Dividing by n without noting the restriction n ≠ 0 (or n ≠ 1 in the sec^n case).

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (reduction formulae for ∫ sin^n x, ∫ cos^n x, ∫ sec^n x, ∫ tan^n x, ∫ x^n e^x). No formula-booklet entry is needed beyond standard integration by parts. Avoid generalising to non-linear self-referential equations or to complex recurrences with three or more I_k terms — those lie outside the syllabus.
