Complex Numbers

Convert polar roots to exact Cartesian form using standard-angle trig values

Family: Polar To Cartesian Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Roots of a complex equation (often from de Moivre or nth-root extraction) have been obtained in modulus-argument form $r\,\text{cis}\,\theta$, and the question asks for exact answers in the form $a+bi$. The arguments are standard angles — integer multiples of $\pi/6$, $\pi/4$, or $\pi/3$ — so exact surd values of $\cos\theta$ and $\sin\theta$ are available. Phrasings include 'give your answers in the form $a+bi$', 'find the roots in Cartesian form', or 'express exactly'.

Motivation — Why this works

By definition $r\,\text{cis}\,\theta = r\cos\theta + i\,r\sin\theta$, so converting is just evaluating the real and imaginary parts. For standard angles the trig values are exact surds, so the conversion is exact and often simplifies dramatically (e.g. $\sqrt{2}\cdot\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}=1$). Cartesian form is also what most downstream tasks (sums, products, plotting, equating real/imaginary parts) require.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the roots are written as $r\,\text{cis}\,\theta$ but the question demands the form $a+bi$.
  2. I rule out leaving them in polar/exponential form because the marks are for exact Cartesian values.
  3. I commit to direct conversion because each $\theta$ is a recognisable standard angle with known exact $\cos$ and $\sin$.
  4. I write down the exact-value table I need ($\cos\pi/6=\sqrt{3}/2$, $\cos\pi/4=1/\sqrt{2}$, etc.) and identify the quadrant of each $\theta$ to fix signs.
  5. For each root I multiply the modulus into both components, simplify surds (rationalising where helpful), and present $a+bi$ with surds in standard form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. For each root $z_k = r^{1/n}\,\text{cis}(\alpha_k)$, identify the quadrant of $\alpha_k$ and the reference angle.
  2. Look up exact values: $\cos(\alpha_k)$ and $\sin(\alpha_k)$ as surds with correct signs.
  3. Compute real part $= r^{1/n}\cos(\alpha_k)$ and imaginary part $= r^{1/n}\sin(\alpha_k)$.
  4. Simplify: combine surds with the modulus, rationalise denominators, cancel where possible.
  5. Write $z_k = a_k + b_k\,i$ and repeat for all roots; optionally check $|z_k|=r^{1/n}$.

Worked Micro-Example

Roots of $z^4 = -4$ are $z_k = \sqrt{2}\,\text{cis}\!\left(\tfrac{\pi}{4}+\tfrac{k\pi}{2}\right)$, $k=0,1,2,3$.\\ For $k=0$: $\sqrt{2}\!\left(\cos\tfrac{\pi}{4}+i\sin\tfrac{\pi}{4}\right)=\sqrt{2}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}+\tfrac{i}{\sqrt{2}}\right)=1+i$.\\ For $k=1$: $\sqrt{2}\,\text{cis}\tfrac{3\pi}{4}=\sqrt{2}\!\left(-\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}+\tfrac{i}{\sqrt{2}}\right)=-1+i$.\\ Similarly $k=2,3$ give $-1-i$ and $1-i$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cube roots of a complex number with arguments that are multiples of $\pi/3$ or $\pi/6$
Use $\cos\pi/6=\sqrt{3}/2$, $\sin\pi/6=1/2$ etc.; expect surds involving $\sqrt{3}$ in the answer.
Roots of unity $z^n = 1$
Modulus is $1$, so $z_k=\cos(2k\pi/n)+i\sin(2k\pi/n)$ — no modulus multiplication needed.
Question gives answers in exponential form $re^{i\theta}$
Same conversion via Euler: $re^{i\theta}=r\cos\theta+ir\sin\theta$.
Arguments are non-standard (e.g. $\arg = \tfrac{1}{4}\arctan(3/4)$)
Leave in polar/exponential form; exact Cartesian conversion is not expected.
Modulus is itself a surd like $r^{1/n}=2^{1/4}$
Multiply through carefully and simplify (e.g. $2^{1/4}\cdot 2^{-1/2}=2^{-1/4}$); keep surds in simplest exact form.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $\text{cis}\,\theta$ as the final answer when the question explicitly asks for $a+bi$.
  • Believing the conversion requires a calculator/decimal approximation when standard-angle exact values suffice.
  • Assuming Cartesian form is always required — for non-standard angles polar form is the expected exact form.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors in Q2/Q3/Q4: e.g. writing $\cos(3\pi/4)=+1/\sqrt{2}$ instead of $-1/\sqrt{2}$.
  • Forgetting to multiply both real and imaginary parts by the modulus $r^{1/n}$.
  • Failing to rationalise denominators or simplify $\sqrt{2}\cdot\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ to $1$.
  • Mixing up $\sin$ and $\cos$ values for $\pi/3$ vs $\pi/6$.
  • Using a principal-argument convention inconsistently across the $n$ roots, leading to misplaced signs.
