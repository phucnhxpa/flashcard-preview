Differential Equations

Take t→∞ limit of explicit solution to read off equilibrium

Family: Asymptotic Analysis Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks for a long-run value (e.g. 'find k such that H → k as t → ∞', 'state the limiting population', 'find the steady-state depth') and an explicit closed-form solution H(t) is already available. Look for decaying exponentials e^(−αt) with α > 0 inside the solution. Common phrasings: 'as t becomes large', 'in the long term', 'eventually', 'limiting value'. Often appears as the final 1–2 mark part after solving the ODE.

Motivation — Why this works

Any term of the form Ce^(−αt) with α > 0 tends to 0 as t → ∞, so the limit of H(t) is obtained by literally replacing every such exponential by 0 in the closed form. This bypasses any equilibrium analysis of the ODE itself and avoids stability arguments. It works because the explicit solution already encodes which equilibrium is approached from the given initial condition. It is also self-consistent: substituting the limit back into dH/dt = f(H) gives 0, matching the equilibrium condition f(H*) = 0.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the part says H → k (or similar) as t → ∞, signalling a single finite limit is expected.
  2. Rule out re-solving the ODE or using L'Hôpital — the explicit solution from an earlier part already contains the answer.
  3. Commit to the substitution approach because every t-dependent term is a decaying exponential e^(−αt) with α > 0.
  4. Identify each occurrence of e^(−αt) in numerator and denominator and mark them as → 0.
  5. Evaluate the resulting constant expression carefully, watching signs in any rational form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write down the explicit solution H(t) from the earlier part.
  2. Identify all decaying exponentials e^(−αt) with α > 0.
  3. Replace each such term by 0 in the expression for H(t).
  4. Simplify the resulting constant to obtain the limit k.
  5. (Optional check) Verify by setting dH/dt = 0 in the original ODE and selecting the physically meaningful root.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $H(t) = \dfrac{10 + 3e^{-0.2t}}{2 - e^{-0.2t}}$, find $k = \lim_{t\to\infty} H$. As $t \to \infty$, $e^{-0.2t} \to 0$, so $H \to \dfrac{10 + 0}{2 - 0} = 5$. Hence $k = 5$. (Check: $dH/dt = 0$ gives $(H-5)(H+3)=0$, and the depth context selects $H=5$.)

Variants — Same move, different surface

Logistic growth $\frac{dP}{dt} = rP(1 - P/K)$ solved explicitly
The decaying exponential sits in the denominator; letting $e^{-rt} \to 0$ gives $P \to K$.
Newton's law of cooling $T(t) = T_a + (T_0 - T_a)e^{-kt}$
The exponential is an additive term; as $t \to \infty$, $T \to T_a$ (ambient temperature).
Linear ODE solution of form $y = A + Be^{-kt}$ from integrating factor
Limit is simply the particular-integral constant $A$.
Mixing problem with concentration $C(t) = C_\infty + (C_0 - C_\infty)e^{-t/\tau}$
Limit is $C_\infty$, the inflow concentration.
Solution containing $e^{+\alpha t}$ with $\alpha > 0$
No finite limit exists; state that $H$ grows without bound rather than substituting.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $e^{-\alpha t}$ as tending to 1 (confusing with $e^0$ at $t=0$) instead of 0 as $t \to \infty$.
  • Believing one must re-solve the ODE or set $dH/dt = 0$ when the closed form is already in hand.
  • Reading 'long-run' as requiring a stability discussion rather than a direct substitution.

Pitfalls

  • Applying L'Hôpital's rule unnecessarily — the expression is not indeterminate after substitution.
  • Sign slips in rational forms, e.g. writing $\frac{10}{2-0}$ as $\frac{10}{2+0}$ or vice versa.
  • Selecting the wrong equilibrium (e.g. $H = -3$) when cross-checking via $dH/dt = 0$, ignoring physical context.
  • Forgetting to state the limit explicitly as the value of $k$ requested.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure/Further Pure: relies only on the standard limit $e^{-\alpha t} \to 0$ as $t \to \infty$ for $\alpha > 0$ and basic substitution into a closed-form ODE solution.
