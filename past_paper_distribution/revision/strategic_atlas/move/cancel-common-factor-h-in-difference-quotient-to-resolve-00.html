Cancel common factor h in difference quotient to resolve 0/0

Family: Factor And Cancel Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a chord-gradient or difference-quotient expression of the form [f(a+h) − f(a)]/h reduces to a fraction whose numerator visibly shares a factor of h with the denominator (e.g. (h² + 9h)/h). Cue phrases include 'simplify', 'in simplest form', 'gradient of the chord PQ', or set-up for 'hence find the gradient at P' via h → 0. The condition h ≠ 0 is implicit because P and Q are distinct points on the curve.

Motivation — Why this works

The quotient [f(a+h) − f(a)]/h takes the indeterminate form 0/0 at h = 0 only because the chord is undefined there; for a polynomial f, the numerator always contains h as a factor since f(a+h) − f(a) vanishes at h = 0. Cancelling that factor removes the removable singularity and leaves a polynomial in h whose value at h = 0 is the derivative. This is the algebraic engine behind first-principles differentiation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that every term in the expanded numerator carries an h, because the constant terms of f(a+h) and f(a) cancelled.
  2. Rule out leaving the answer as a fraction — 'simplest form' and the upcoming limit step both demand a denominator-free polynomial in h.
  3. Commit to factoring h from the numerator because h ≠ 0 is guaranteed by P ≠ Q, making cancellation algebraically legal.
  4. Bridge: write the numerator as h × (polynomial in h), then cancel against the h in the denominator.
  5. Tactically, check that the resulting polynomial has no leftover h in the denominator; if it does, an earlier expansion error is likely.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Expand f(a+h) − f(a) and collect like terms.
  2. Factor h out of every term in the numerator: e.g. h² + 9h = h(h + 9).
  3. Cancel the common factor h with the denominator, citing h ≠ 0.
  4. State the simplified chord gradient as a polynomial in h.

Worked Micro-Example

Given f(x) = x² + 9x at P(0, 0) and Q(h, h² + 9h), the chord gradient is [f(0+h) − f(0)]/h = (h² + 9h)/h. Factor: h² + 9h = h(h + 9). Cancel h (valid since h ≠ 0): gradient = h + 9. Letting h → 0 gives the gradient at P as 9.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Higher-degree polynomial, e.g. f(x) = x³ at x = a
Numerator expands to 3a²h + 3ah² + h³; factor h to give h(3a² + 3ah + h²), then cancel.
Surd curve, e.g. f(x) = √x
Direct factoring fails; rationalise the numerator by multiplying by the conjugate, after which an h emerges and cancels with the denominator.
Rational function, e.g. f(x) = 1/x
Combine the two fractions in the numerator over a common denominator; an h appears in the new numerator and cancels with the outer h.
General first-principles derivative
Same template: expand, factor h, cancel, then take h → 0.

Common Misreadings

  • Leaving the answer as (h² + 9h)/h and not recognising 'simplest form' demands cancellation.
  • Treating the 0/0 form as 'undefined' and giving up rather than simplifying first.
  • Cancelling only one term's h (e.g. writing h + 9h) by distributing the cancellation incorrectly.

Pitfalls

  • Cancelling h without the implicit caveat h ≠ 0 — accepted at A-level but conceptually sloppy.
  • Arithmetic slips when expanding f(a+h), leaving a stray constant that prevents h from factoring cleanly.
  • Forgetting to factor h from every numerator term before cancelling, leading to expressions like (h + 9)/1 + something.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL P1 differentiation from first principles; the same factor-and-cancel pattern recurs in P2/P3 limit work and in Further Pure when deriving standard derivatives.
