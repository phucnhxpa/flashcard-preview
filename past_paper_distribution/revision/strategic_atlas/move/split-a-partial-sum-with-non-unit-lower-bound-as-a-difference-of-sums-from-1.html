Trigger — When to use

A summation is requested from r=a to r=b where a>1 (e.g. ∑_{r=10}^{25}), but the only available closed form (from a 'show that' part, or the standard results ∑r, ∑r², ∑r³) evaluates ∑_{r=1}^{n}. Cues include phrases like 'Hence find ∑_{r=10}^{25}…' immediately after a part (b) proving a formula for ∑_{r=1}^{n}. Also fires when the lower index is shifted (r=k onwards) or when the question asks for a 'middle slice' of a series.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity ∑_{r=a}^{b} f(r) = ∑_{r=1}^{b} f(r) − ∑_{r=1}^{a−1} f(r) follows directly from telescoping the index range, and converts a non-standard partial sum into two standard ones. Each of these can be evaluated by substituting into the closed form already established. This avoids term-by-term summation and re-uses the proven result, which is exactly what 'Hence' instructs the student to do.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the closed form just proved (or quoted) only covers sums starting at r=1, while the requested range starts at r=a>1.
  2. Rule out summing the b−a+1 terms individually — too slow and error-prone — and rule out re-deriving a new closed form from scratch.
  3. Commit to the difference-of-sums identity because it preserves the 'Hence' instruction and reuses the established formula twice.
  4. Bridge: write ∑_{r=a}^{b} = ∑_{r=1}^{b} − ∑_{r=1}^{a−1}, being careful that the upper limit of the subtracted sum is a−1, not a.
  5. Tactically, evaluate the larger sum first (n=b), then the smaller (n=a−1), and subtract; keep fractions exact until the final subtraction.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write ∑_{r=a}^{b} f(r) = ∑_{r=1}^{b} f(r) − ∑_{r=1}^{a−1} f(r).
  2. Substitute n=b into the closed form to get the first value.
  3. Substitute n=a−1 into the closed form to get the second value.
  4. Subtract the second from the first; simplify to an integer or single fraction.

Worked Micro-Example

Given ∑_{r=1}^{n} r(r+2) = \tfrac{n(n+1)(2n+7)}{6}, find ∑_{r=10}^{25} r(r+2).\\ ∑_{r=10}^{25} r(r+2) = ∑_{r=1}^{25} r(r+2) − ∑_{r=1}^{9} r(r+2).\\ n=25: \tfrac{25\cdot 26\cdot 57}{6} = 6175. \quad n=9: \tfrac{9\cdot 10\cdot 25}{6} = 375.\\ Answer: 6175 − 375 = 5800.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Arithmetic or geometric series with a non-unit starting index, e.g. ∑_{r=5}^{20} ar^{k}
Use S_{20} − S_{4} where S_{n} is the standard sum-to-n formula; the subtracted upper limit is (lower bound − 1).
Sum involving ∑r, ∑r², ∑r³ from r=k to r=n in Further Pure
Apply the identity to each standard sum separately, e.g. ∑_{r=k}^{n} r² = \tfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} − \tfrac{(k-1)k(2k-1)}{6}.
Definite integral with a known antiderivative-from-0 formula
Write ∫_a^b f = ∫_0^b f − ∫_0^a f; the analogue is exact rather than off-by-one because integrals are continuous.
Telescoping sums proved for ∑_{r=1}^{n}
Same split: ∑_{r=a}^{b} = ∑_{r=1}^{b} − ∑_{r=1}^{a-1}, then apply the telescoped closed form twice.

Common Misreadings

  • Using ∑_{r=1}^{a} (i.e. upper limit a) instead of ∑_{r=1}^{a−1}, which incorrectly drops the r=a term.
  • Reading 'Hence' as licence to derive a fresh formula rather than reuse the proven one.
  • Confusing this with splitting at a midpoint to telescope, when only a shifted lower bound is at issue.

Pitfalls

  • Off-by-one on the subtracted upper limit (writing a instead of a−1).
  • Arithmetic errors evaluating the formula for large n (e.g. 25·26·57).
  • Substituting the wrong value into a linear factor like 2n+7 (should give 57 at n=25, 25 at n=9).
  • Premature decimal rounding inside the two sums leading to a wrong final difference.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure technique used with the proven results for ∑r, ∑r², ∑r³ and with 'show that … hence find' style questions; no material beyond the IAL Maths/Further Maths specification is required.
