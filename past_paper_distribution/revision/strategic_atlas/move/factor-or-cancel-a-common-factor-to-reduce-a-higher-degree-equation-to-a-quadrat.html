Algebra & Functions

Factor or cancel a common factor to reduce a higher-degree equation to a quadratic

Family: Degree Reduction Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation of degree 3 or higher has a common factor visible across every term (one side) or shared by both sides. Concrete cues: (i) no constant term in a polynomial like 2x³ + 3x² − 35x = 0, so every term contains x; (ii) both sides of an equation contain an identical product like n(n+1) or (x−1) as a factor; (iii) a trigonometric or exponential expression like sin³θ + sin²θ − 2sinθ where every term shares sinθ. In short: 'I see the same factor everywhere — pulling it out (or dividing it out) will drop the degree by at least one.'

Motivation — Why this works

If a polynomial expression P(x) = x·Q(x) is set to zero, the zero-product property splits it into x = 0 or Q(x) = 0, immediately giving one root for free and leaving a lower-degree equation. If both sides share a non-zero factor F, dividing by F preserves the solution set and reduces the degree of the equation. The underlying principle is the zero-product property combined with the fact that a·b = a·c ⟹ b = c whenever a ≠ 0. This avoids brute-force expansion into a quartic/cubic and bypasses the need for the rational root theorem or synthetic division.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Scan every term: is there a literal factor (a variable, a bracket, a trig ratio) present in all of them? Equivalently, is there no 'lone constant' term that would block factoring?
  2. If the equation has the shape P(x) = Q(x) with both sides sharing a factor F, decide whether F can be guaranteed non-zero from the problem context (e.g. n a positive integer, or an angle restriction) so that division is legal.
  3. Rule out heavier alternatives: rational root theorem, expanding everything to a quartic, or synthetic division — these are slower and more error-prone when a common factor is staring at you.
  4. Commit to the move: factor (don't divide) when one side is zero, so that the trivial root from F = 0 is preserved; divide (with justification) when both sides share F and F ≠ 0 is guaranteed.
  5. Write the residual quadratic explicitly and check it is in standard form ax² + bx + c = 0 ready for factorisation, completing the square, or the quadratic formula.
  6. At the end, reunite the roots from F = 0 (if applicable) with those from the quadratic, and reject any that violate domain restrictions stated in the problem.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the highest common factor F across all terms (or across both sides).
  2. If one side is 0: write the expression as F · (quadratic) = 0 and apply the zero-product property: F = 0 or quadratic = 0.
  3. If both sides share F and F ≠ 0 is justified: divide both sides by F and state the justification (e.g. 'since n ≥ 1, n(n+1) > 0').
  4. Clear fractions if needed (multiply through by the LCM of the denominators) to obtain integer coefficients.
  5. Expand and rearrange the residual equation into standard quadratic form ax² + bx + c = 0.
  6. Solve the quadratic by factorisation, completing the square, or the formula.
  7. Combine all roots, then reject any inadmissible ones (non-integer, negative, outside a stated interval, etc.).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $2x^3 + 3x^2 - 35x = 0$. Every term has factor $x$, so factor: $x(2x^2 + 3x - 35) = 0$. Then $x = 0$ or $2x^2 + 3x - 35 = 0$. The quadratic factors as $(2x - 7)(x + 5) = 0$, giving $x = 7/2$ or $x = -5$. Final roots: $x = 0,\ 7/2,\ -5$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Both sides of an equation share a product like n(n+1), with n a positive integer
Divide both sides by n(n+1), explicitly justifying n(n+1) > 0; then clear fractions by the LCM of the constants and solve the resulting quadratic.
Quartic with only even powers, e.g. x⁴ − 5x² + 4 = 0
Substitute u = x² to reduce to a quadratic in u; solve, then back-substitute to recover x.
Trigonometric equation like sin³θ + sin²θ − 2sinθ = 0
Factor out sinθ to get sinθ(sin²θ + sinθ − 2) = 0; keep the sinθ = 0 branch and solve the quadratic in sinθ.
Equation with binomial coefficients or factorials sharing a common factorial
Cancel the shared factorial (which is positive, so safe) to leave a polynomial equation in n.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the equation as 'a generic cubic' and reaching for the rational root theorem when a free common factor is in plain sight.
  • Thinking that because both sides share a factor, the equation must be an identity rather than just degree-reducible.
  • Confusing 'no constant term' with 'no solution' rather than recognising that 0 is automatically a root.

Pitfalls

  • Dividing both sides by x (or by sinθ, etc.) when one side is zero — this silently discards the root corresponding to that factor. Always factor in this case, never divide.
  • Cancelling a shared factor without justifying it is non-zero; if the domain allows the factor to vanish, those solutions are lost.
  • Arithmetic slip when clearing fractions: take the true LCM of the denominators (e.g. 12 for ¼ and 17/6, not 24).
  • After factorising the quadratic, forgetting to reject roots that violate domain restrictions (e.g. n must be a positive integer).
  • Sign errors when expanding (n−1)(n+2) or similar brackets after cancellation.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely inside Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1/P2) — factor theorem, zero-product property, and quadratic solution methods from the formula booklet. No out-of-scope tools (e.g. Cardano's formula, complex factorisation over ℂ) are needed; if a residual quadratic has negative discriminant in a real-roots context, simply state 'no real solutions' for that branch.
