Algebra & Functions

Divide by dominant exponential to expose horizontal asymptote

Family: Asymptotic Normalisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks for the 'upper limit', 'long-run value', 'limiting population', or horizontal asymptote of a function with exponentials in both numerator and denominator (e.g. logistic-type N = A·e^{kt}/(B + Ce^{kt})). Phrases like 'as t becomes large', 'eventually', or 'long-term behaviour' are strong cues. Direct substitution of t → ∞ produces an ∞/∞ indeterminate form. The growing exponential appears with the same rate constant in top and bottom.

Motivation — Why this works

Dividing numerator and denominator by the dominant growing exponential e^{kt} converts each occurrence either into a constant (when it cancels) or into a decaying e^{-kt} that tends to 0. This collapses the indeterminate ∞/∞ into a finite ratio of constants, which is precisely the horizontal asymptote. The technique mirrors the standard rational-function trick of dividing by the highest power of x, applied with e^{kt} playing the role of the dominant term.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the phrase 'upper limit' / 'long-term value' and read this as a limit as t → ∞, not a maximum found by calculus.
  2. Rule out setting dN/dt = 0: a logistic-style increasing function has no turning point, so a derivative approach won't find the asymptote.
  3. Commit to limit-taking because both numerator and denominator contain e^{kt} with the same exponent, signalling a clean ∞/∞ that will simplify.
  4. Bridge: identify e^{kt} as the dominant term in the denominator and prepare to divide every term in the fraction by it.
  5. Tactical: after dividing, replace each e^{-kt} by 0 and read off the surviving constants as the limit.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the dominant growing exponential common to numerator and denominator (here e^{0.3t}).
  2. Divide every term in numerator and denominator by that exponential, turning e^{kt}/e^{kt} into 1 and constants into Ce^{-kt}.
  3. Apply e^{-kt} → 0 as t → ∞.
  4. Simplify the resulting ratio of constants to obtain the horizontal asymptote.
  5. State that the value is approached but not attained.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the upper limit of $N = \dfrac{600 e^{0.3t}}{2 + e^{0.3t}}$ as $t \to \infty$.\\ Divide top and bottom by $e^{0.3t}$: $N = \dfrac{600}{2e^{-0.3t} + 1}$.\\ As $t \to \infty$, $e^{-0.3t} \to 0$, so $N \to \dfrac{600}{1} = 600$.\\ The population approaches but never reaches 600.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Pre-rearranged form N = A/(B + Ce^{-kt})
No division step needed; just let e^{-kt} → 0 to read off A/B directly.
Rational function in x as x → ∞
Divide numerator and denominator by the highest power of x; same logic with x^n replacing e^{kt}.
Lower limit / initial value at t = 0
Substitute t = 0 directly (e^{0} = 1) rather than taking a limit.
Different exponential rates, e.g. e^{0.5t} on top and e^{0.3t} on bottom
Divide by the larger-rate exponential; the limit may be 0 or ∞ rather than a finite ratio.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'upper limit' as a maximum and setting dN/dt = 0, even though the function is monotonic.
  • Substituting t = ∞ into the original form and giving up at ∞/∞ without normalising.
  • Confusing the upper limit with the initial value N(0).

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting that the asymptote is approached but not attained, so writing N = 600 as if achieved.
  • Dividing only some terms by e^{kt} instead of every term, producing an algebraically wrong expression.
  • Mishandling signs in the exponent: e^{kt}/e^{kt} = 1, not e^{2kt}, and constants become Ce^{-kt}, not Ce^{kt}.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Maths (P2/P3 exponential models) and reinforced in Further Maths; uses only the standard fact that e^{-kt} → 0 as t → ∞ for k > 0.
