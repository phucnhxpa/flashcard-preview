Vectors

Split skew-lines proof into non-parallel and non-intersecting checks

Family: Proof Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question explicitly says 'show/prove the lines are skew' or asks to verify two 3D lines are skew before computing a distance or angle. The lines are given in vector form $\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}+\lambda\mathbf{d}$ with 3D direction vectors. Phrasings like 'show $L_1$ and $L_2$ do not meet and are not parallel' are the same trigger spelled out. Typically appears as a 5–7 mark structured proof, often as a lead-in to a shortest-distance calculation.

Motivation — Why this works

By definition, two lines in 3D are skew iff they are (i) not parallel and (ii) do not intersect — both conditions are independent and necessary. Parallel non-intersecting lines are coplanar, not skew, so non-intersection alone is insufficient; conversely, two non-parallel lines in 3D may still meet at a point, so non-parallelism alone is also insufficient. Splitting the proof guarantees both logical gaps are closed and matches the examiner mark scheme, which awards marks separately for each condition and for the concluding statement.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that 'skew' is a compound definition requiring two distinct geometric facts, not a single computation.
  2. Rules out using a one-shot test (e.g. cross product non-zero), recalling that this only certifies non-parallelism, not non-intersection.
  3. Commits to a two-stage plan because in 3D the system from equating the two lines has 3 equations in 2 parameters — overdetermined, so inconsistency is the natural way to rule out intersection.
  4. Bridges to the first concrete operation by inspecting the direction vectors $\mathbf{d}_1, \mathbf{d}_2$ for proportionality, since this is the cheapest check.
  5. Plans Step 2 by setting $\mathbf{r}_1=\mathbf{r}_2$, solving any two component equations for $\lambda,\mu$, then substituting into the third to expose a contradiction.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. State the two conditions: (1) $\mathbf{d}_1 \neq k\mathbf{d}_2$ for any scalar $k$; (2) the system $\mathbf{a}_1+\lambda\mathbf{d}_1 = \mathbf{a}_2+\mu\mathbf{d}_2$ has no solution.
  2. Step 1: compare components of $\mathbf{d}_1$ and $\mathbf{d}_2$; show the ratios disagree, so the lines are not parallel.
  3. Step 2: equate the two position vectors component-wise to get three equations in $\lambda,\mu$.
  4. Solve any two equations simultaneously for $\lambda$ and $\mu$.
  5. Substitute into the remaining equation; obtain a numerical contradiction (e.g. $5=7$), proving non-intersection.
  6. Conclude with an explicit statement: 'Since the lines are neither parallel nor intersecting, they are skew.'

Worked Micro-Example

Let $L_1:\mathbf{r}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\\2\end{pmatrix}+\lambda\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\\1\end{pmatrix}$, $L_2:\mathbf{r}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\\0\end{pmatrix}+\mu\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\\0\end{pmatrix}$. Step 1: $(1,1,1)$ is not a scalar multiple of $(2,1,0)$ (third components give $k=0$, first give $k=\tfrac12$), so not parallel. Step 2: equating gives $1+\lambda=2\mu$, $\lambda=1+\mu$, $2+\lambda=0$. From the third $\lambda=-2$, then $\mu=-3$, but $1+(-2)=-1\neq 2(-3)=-6$, contradiction, so they do not intersect. Hence $L_1$ and $L_2$ are skew.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Proving two lines are parallel but distinct (not coincident)
Show $\mathbf{d}_1 = k\mathbf{d}_2$ for some scalar $k$, then take a point on $L_1$ and show it does not satisfy $L_2$'s equation.
Proving two lines intersect and finding the point
Show directions are not parallel, then solve two of the three component equations for $\lambda,\mu$ and verify the third is consistent; substitute back to give the intersection point.
Showing a line is skew to an edge of a given solid (in a 3D geometry context)
Parametrise the edge as a line segment with its own direction vector and apply the same two-stage check; the segment restriction does not affect the skew argument.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating non-intersection alone as proof of skew, ignoring the parallel case.
  • Using $\mathbf{d}_1\times\mathbf{d}_2\neq\mathbf{0}$ as a single test for skew — this only proves non-parallelism.
  • Assuming that because the lines are in 3D and 'look different', they must be skew, without any algebraic check.
  • Confusing 'skew' with 'perpendicular and non-intersecting'; skew lines need not be perpendicular.

Pitfalls

  • Solving all three component equations simultaneously and finding 'no solution' from the solver without explicitly displaying the contradiction line — examiners want the inconsistent equation shown.
  • Forgetting the concluding sentence linking both parts; the final 'therefore skew' mark is often dropped.
  • Reusing the same parameter letter for both lines (writing $\lambda$ in both), which spuriously forces the lines to share a parameter and can lead to a false intersection.
  • Checking proportionality of direction vectors using only one pair of components; one matching ratio is not enough to establish parallelism either way.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (3D vector lines, skew lines, intersection of lines). Uses only vector equations of lines, scalar multiples, and simultaneous equations — no projection or distance formulae are required for the skew proof itself.
