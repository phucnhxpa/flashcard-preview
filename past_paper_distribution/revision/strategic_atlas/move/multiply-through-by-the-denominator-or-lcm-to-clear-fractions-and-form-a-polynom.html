Algebra & Functions

Multiply through by the denominator (or LCM) to clear fractions and form a polynomial

Family: Clearing Fractions Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an equation contains rational terms (a/x, k/x², A/B = C/D, or fractional coefficients like 26/3) mixed with polynomial terms, AND the next step you want — applying the discriminant, factorising, finding roots, or stating 'integer coefficients' — requires a clean polynomial. Visual cues: a 1/x or 1/x² term sitting next to linear/quadratic terms; two single fractions joined by '='; a coefficient written as a fraction p/q when integers are demanded. Equivalent phrasings of the trigger: 'there's a denominator in the way', 'the equation isn't yet polynomial', 'cross-multiplication will tidy this'. The move is the prerequisite step, not the goal — it sets up the real work that follows.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying every term of an equation by the same non-zero quantity preserves its solution set, so this is a free structural rewrite. Clearing denominators converts a rational equation into a polynomial equation, unlocking the standard toolkit: discriminant b² − 4ac, factorisation, the quadratic formula, or polynomial root analysis. For two single fractions A/B = C/D, the operation reduces to cross-multiplication AD = BC — a one-step shortcut to the same polynomial. The principle is simply 'equality is preserved under multiplication by a non-zero quantity', applied with care to track the excluded values where that quantity vanishes.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the equation mixes denominators (1/x, 1/x², or A/B) with polynomial terms — so it is not yet in a form any standard quadratic/polynomial tool can act on.
  2. Rule out alternatives: substitution (e.g., u = 1/x) sometimes works but obscures structure; numerical solving abandons the algebraic question (e.g., 'no real roots' or 'integer coefficients' demands).
  3. Commit to clearing fractions because the downstream goal — discriminant, factorisation, or stating coefficients — REQUIRES a polynomial form, and multiplying by the denominator (or LCM of denominators) is the minimal route there.
  4. Identify the multiplier: the single denominator if there is one (x, x², or x+k), the LCM of denominators if there are several, or recognise A/B = C/D and cross-multiply directly.
  5. Note the excluded values implied by the multiplier (x ≠ 0, x ≠ −k) — these would normally be checked for extraneous roots, though they are often already excluded by the original equation's domain.
  6. Multiply EVERY term — including constants and the term on the right — then rearrange to standard form ax² + bx + c = 0 (or higher-degree analogue), preferably with a positive leading coefficient to minimise sign errors when reading off coefficients.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify all denominators present in the equation.
  2. Choose the multiplier: the single denominator, the LCM of multiple denominators, or — for A/B = C/D — apply cross-multiplication AD = BC.
  3. Multiply every term on both sides by the chosen multiplier; do not omit constant terms.
  4. Expand any products carefully, watching signs (e.g., (12−x)(x+1) gives −x² from −x·x).
  5. Rearrange to standard polynomial form, e.g. ax² + bx + c = 0, with a > 0 if convenient.
  6. Note any excluded values (x ≠ 0, etc.) and, after solving, check the roots are not excluded.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\frac{3}{x} + 5 = -2x + c$ for the condition of no real roots. Multiply every term by $x$: $3 + 5x = -2x^2 + cx$. Rearrange: $2x^2 + (5-c)x + 3 = 0$. Now apply discriminant: $(5-c)^2 - 24 < 0$, giving the range of $c$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation has 1/x² alongside polynomial terms (e.g., 1/x² − 9 = kx²)
Multiply through by x² to obtain a quartic; often this quartic is a 'quadratic in x²' and admits a substitution u = x².
Two single fractions equated, A/B = C/D
Cross-multiply directly to AD = BC — a one-step version of multiplying both sides by BD.
Denominator is (x + k) rather than x
Multiply by (x + k); record x ≠ −k as an excluded value to check after solving.
Equation has fractional coefficients (e.g., x² − (26/3)x + 1 = 0) and integer coefficients are required
Multiply every term by the LCM of the denominators (here 3) to obtain 3x² − 26x + 3 = 0; roots unchanged.
Multiple fractions on one side (e.g., 1/x + 1/(x+1) = 3)
Combine into a single fraction over the common denominator first, then cross-multiply.

Common Misreadings

  • Multiplying only the fractional terms by the denominator and forgetting the constants and the RHS, producing an incorrect equation.
  • Leaving an equation with fractional coefficients when the question explicitly requires integer coefficients.
  • Treating cross-multiplication as a separate magic rule rather than recognising it as 'multiply both sides by both denominators'.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to multiply EVERY term — especially constants and the term on the far right.
  • Sign errors during expansion or rearrangement (moving −2x² across the '=' must give +2x²; moving cx must give −cx).
  • Not recording excluded values (x ≠ 0, x ≠ −k) introduced by the multiplier; clearing fractions can in principle introduce extraneous roots.
  • Choosing too small a multiplier (e.g., only one denominator) when several are present — use the LCM.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1–P4) and Further Pure. No formula booklet entry is required — this is a pre-formula manipulation. Avoid generalising to multiplying by expressions that may equal zero without flagging the excluded values; rigorous extraneous-root checking is expected.
