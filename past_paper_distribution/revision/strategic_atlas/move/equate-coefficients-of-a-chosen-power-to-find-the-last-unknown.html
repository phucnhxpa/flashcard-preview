Algebra & Functions

Equate coefficients of a chosen power to find the last unknown

Family: Coefficient Comparison Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an identity of the form P(x) ≡ (known factor)·(unknown factor) or a partial-fraction decomposition has been partly resolved — e.g. some constants have been found by root-substitution or one factor is known — but at least one unknown remains that no further substitution can isolate cleanly. Visual cues: a polynomial identity holding for all x with one or two constants still missing; a cubic/quartic with a known quadratic factor and an unknown linear/quadratic cofactor; a partial fraction with a repeated linear factor whose 'middle' constant cannot be reached by cover-up. Equivalently: 'I've used every substitution that kills a bracket — what now?' or 'I know some coefficients of the original polynomial but not all — can I avoid long division?'

Motivation — Why this works

An algebraic identity P(x) ≡ Q(x) holds for ALL x, so the coefficients of each power x^k must be equal on both sides. Picking the simplest power (often x² or the leading power) yields a single linear equation in the remaining unknown(s), bypassing the need to expand everything or solve a full simultaneous system. This is the polynomial analogue of 'matching components' — the monomials {1, x, x², …} are linearly independent, so coefficient-by-coefficient equality is forced. It is faster than substituting an arbitrary x (which mixes all unknowns) and cleaner than polynomial long division when some original coefficients are themselves unknown.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the situation: an identity holds for all x, but the standard root-substitution trick has been exhausted (or is unavailable for one unknown), leaving one or two constants still to determine.
  2. Rule out alternatives: substituting another arbitrary x-value would produce an equation mixing all the unknowns and usually messy fractions; full polynomial long division is possible but laborious, especially when some coefficients in the target polynomial are themselves letters (like a, b).
  3. Commit to coefficient comparison because the monomials are linearly independent — equating the coefficient of one well-chosen power gives a single clean equation in the remaining unknown.
  4. Choose WHICH power to compare strategically: pick the power that involves the FEWEST unknowns. Often x² or the leading power is best because some terms (e.g. a constant times a linear factor) contribute nothing to it.
  5. Expand only the parts of the RHS that produce that chosen power — avoid full expansion. Track cross-terms carefully (e.g. (z² − 4z + 13)(Pz + Q) has z² coefficient Q − 4P, not just Q).
  6. Set the LHS coefficient equal to the RHS coefficient, substitute the already-known constants, and solve the resulting one-variable linear equation.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the identity in factored/decomposed form with the unknown(s) symbolic, e.g. 2z³ − 11z² + az + b ≡ (z² − 4z + 13)(Pz + Q), or f(x) ≡ A(x−2)² + B(x+1)(x−2) + C(x+1).
  2. Identify which power x^k is cleanest — typically the one with the fewest contributing terms (often the leading power or x²).
  3. Compute the coefficient of x^k on the LHS by inspection.
  4. Compute the coefficient of x^k on the RHS by partial expansion of only the relevant brackets, including all cross-term contributions.
  5. Equate the two coefficients to obtain a linear equation in the remaining unknown(s).
  6. Substitute the previously-determined constants and solve.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\tfrac{x^2+1}{(x+1)(x-2)^2} \equiv \tfrac{A}{x+1}+\tfrac{B}{x-2}+\tfrac{C}{(x-2)^2}$, cover-up gives $A=-\tfrac{8}{9}$, $C=\tfrac{5}{3}$. Multiplying through: $x^2+1 \equiv A(x-2)^2 + B(x+1)(x-2) + C(x+1)$. Comparing $x^2$ coefficients: $1 = A + B$, so $B = 1 - (-\tfrac{8}{9}) = \tfrac{17}{9}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cubic with a known quadratic factor and unknown coefficients elsewhere in the polynomial
Write the linear cofactor as (Pz + Q) — do NOT assume P = 1. Compare the leading and second-highest coefficients to fix P and Q without ever touching the unknown lower coefficients.
Quartic with a known quadratic factor
The cofactor is itself quadratic (Pz² + Qz + R + …), so up to four coefficient comparisons may be needed; pick those involving fewest unknowns first.
Partial fractions where the missing constant appears in a term whose linear factor is shared with another term
Compare x² (or another simple) coefficient instead of substituting x = 0; the constant-term route is also valid but usually involves more arithmetic.
All coefficients of the original polynomial are known numerically
Polynomial long division becomes a viable alternative — coefficient comparison is still typically faster for one or two unknowns.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming the unknown linear factor has leading coefficient 1 when the original polynomial does not (e.g. writing (z + Q) instead of (Pz + Q) for a cubic with leading coefficient 2).
  • Expanding the entire RHS and trying to solve a full simultaneous system, not realising that one well-chosen coefficient comparison suffices once other constants are known.
  • Treating 'compare coefficients' as the same operation as 'substitute a convenient x' — they are equivalent in principle but the former is usually arithmetically lighter.

Pitfalls

  • Missing cross-term contributions when expanding — e.g. the z² coefficient of (z² − 4z + 13)(Pz + Q) is Q − 4P, not just Q; the x² coefficient of B(2x+1)(x−1) is 2B, not B.
  • Sign errors when rearranging the resulting equation, especially with negative fractions (e.g. 1 = −8/9 + B → B = 17/9).
  • Forgetting that a term like C(x+1) contributes nothing to the x² coefficient, then mistakenly including a C-term in the comparison equation.
  • Converting integers to common denominators incorrectly (e.g. 10 = 90/9, not 9/9).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL technique appearing in P3 partial fractions and P4 polynomial factorisation/complex roots; no formula booklet entry — relies on the identity principle that polynomials equal for all x have equal coefficients. Avoid invoking abstract concepts like 'basis of polynomial space' — stay with the concrete 'coefficients must match'.
