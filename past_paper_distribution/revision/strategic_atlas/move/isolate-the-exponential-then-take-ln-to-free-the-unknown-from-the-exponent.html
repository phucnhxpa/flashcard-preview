Algebra & Functions

Isolate the exponential then take ln to free the unknown from the exponent

Family: Log Inversion Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever the unknown is trapped inside an exponent, typically in the form e^{kx} or e^{-kx}, and no algebraic manipulation can extract it. Visual cues: an equation like A − Be^{-kx} = C, or Ce^{kt} = L, or e^{-λt} = constant, where you are asked to solve for the variable in the exponent. Equivalent phrasings: 'find t when…', 'find the value of k such that…', or rearrangement of an exponential growth/decay model. The signal is always: one exponential term, one unknown in its exponent, everything else numeric or symbolic constants.

Motivation — Why this works

The natural logarithm is the inverse of the exponential function, so applying ln to both sides of e^{f(x)} = c collapses the equation to the linear form f(x) = ln c, which can then be solved by ordinary algebra. The move only works once the exponential is fully isolated — ln does not distribute over sums, so any additive constants must be cleared first. When the exponent carries a negative coefficient, the log-law −ln(a/b) = ln(b/a) is used afterwards to flip the argument and produce a clean, positive form of the answer.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the unknown sits inside an exponent (e.g. e^{-5k} or e^{-0.2T}); standard algebraic moves (factoring, expanding, dividing) cannot reach it.
  2. Rule out trying to ln the equation immediately while there are still additive terms — ln(A − Be^{-kx}) does not simplify because ln does not split over sums or differences.
  3. Commit to log-inversion because ln is the unique inverse of e^x, and once the exponential is isolated it converts the equation to linear form in one stroke.
  4. Bridge to action: rearrange to the form e^{(coeff)·unknown} = (positive constant), checking that the right-hand side is positive (else ln is undefined).
  5. Apply ln to both sides; the left side collapses to (coeff)·unknown by ln(e^x) = x, the right side becomes ln(constant).
  6. If the coefficient is negative, divide through and use −ln(a/b) = ln(b/a) to absorb the sign into a reciprocal, presenting the answer with a positive logarithm.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rearrange the equation to isolate the exponential: write it as e^{αx} = c with c > 0.
  2. Take natural logarithms of both sides: αx = ln c.
  3. Divide by α to obtain x = (ln c)/α.
  4. If α < 0, rewrite (ln c)/α as −(ln c)/|α| = (ln(1/c))/|α| using −ln c = ln(1/c) to keep the answer in tidy positive form.
  5. Simplify the logarithmic argument using ln(ab) = ln a + ln b, ln(a/b) = ln a − ln b, and ln e = 1 as appropriate.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $90 = 198 - 180e^{-5k}$ for $k$. Isolate: $180e^{-5k} = 108 \Rightarrow e^{-5k} = \tfrac{3}{5}$. Take ln: $-5k = \ln\tfrac{3}{5}$. Divide and flip: $k = -\tfrac{1}{5}\ln\tfrac{3}{5} = \tfrac{1}{5}\ln\tfrac{5}{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Negative exponent with messy constant, e.g. e^{-0.2T} = e/[2(e+1)]
After taking ln and dividing by the negative coefficient, apply −ln(a/b) = ln(b/a) to present T = 5 ln(2(e+1)/e) instead of the equivalent T = −5 ln(e/[2(e+1)]).
Base other than e, e.g. 10^{kx} = c or 2^x = c
Use the matching logarithm (log₁₀ for base 10, log₂ for base 2) — same isolation-then-invert structure.
Logistic / exponential growth Ce^{kt} = L
Divide by C first, then ln; identical mechanic with positive coefficient.
Half-life: e^{-λt} = 1/2
Take ln, divide by −λ to get t = (ln 2)/λ — the sign flip is automatic via ln(1/2) = −ln 2.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating ln(A − Be^{-kx}) = ln C as a simplification — it isn't, because ln doesn't distribute over subtraction.
  • Forgetting the sign flip when the exponent is negative, ending with a negative answer for a quantity (time, rate constant) that must be positive.
  • Misreading e^{-kx} = c with c < 0 as solvable — the exponential is always positive, so a negative RHS signals an earlier algebra error.

Pitfalls

  • Taking ln before fully isolating the exponential, e.g. writing ln(198 − 180e^{-5k}) = ln 90, which does not simplify.
  • Splitting ln(2(e+1)) incorrectly as ln 2 · ln(e+1) instead of ln 2 + ln(e+1).
  • Losing the constant term that comes from ln e = 1, e.g. dropping the '−1' when simplifying ln(e/[2(e+1)]).
  • Arithmetic slips when reducing fractions like 108/180 — failing to spot the common factor of 36 (= 3/5).

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 exponentials and logarithms (formula booklet: laws of logarithms, ln e = 1, ln(ab) = ln a + ln b). Avoid invoking complex logarithms or principal-branch issues — within IAL all arguments of ln are real and positive by construction.
