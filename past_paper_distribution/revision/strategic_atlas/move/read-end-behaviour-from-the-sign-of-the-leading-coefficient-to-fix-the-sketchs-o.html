Algebra & Functions

Read end-behaviour from the sign of the leading coefficient to fix the sketch's orientation

Family: End Behaviour Analysis Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a polynomial is presented in factored form and a sketch is requested. Knowing the x-intercepts alone is insufficient — you must decide which way the curve enters and exits before threading it through the roots. Visual cues: factors of the form (a − x) or (k − x) that hide a negative sign, or a question asking to 'sketch the curve' / 'show the shape' of y = f(x). Also fires when a later part of the question depends on the geometric orientation (e.g. counting intersections, identifying which branch is in a region).

Motivation — Why this works

The end behaviour of any polynomial is governed entirely by its leading term: degree fixes the symmetry of the ends, and the sign of the leading coefficient fixes their direction. For an odd-degree polynomial, a positive leading coefficient sends the curve from bottom-left to top-right; negative reverses it. For even degree, positive opens upward (U), negative opens downward (∩). Establishing this skeleton first prevents drawing a mirror-image of the correct curve — a very common source of lost marks.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the polynomial is given in factored form, so the roots are read off immediately, but the global shape is not yet visible.
  2. Rule out fully expanding the product — that wastes time; only the leading term is needed for end behaviour.
  3. Commit to extracting the leading coefficient by multiplying the highest-degree term of each factor, paying close attention to factors of the form (k − x) which contribute a hidden −1.
  4. Use the sign and degree to classify: positive even → U-shape; negative even → ∩-shape; positive odd → bottom-left to top-right; negative odd → top-left to bottom-right.
  5. Combine this orientation with the known x-intercepts to thread the curve through the roots in the correct direction, alternating above/below the axis as it passes each simple root.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the degree n by counting linear factors (with multiplicity).
  2. From each factor, pick out only the term in x (e.g. from (5 − x) take −x, from (x − 2) take x).
  3. Multiply these leading terms together to obtain the leading term cx^n; record the sign of c.
  4. Apply the rule: even n with c>0 → both ends ↑; even n with c<0 → both ends ↓; odd n with c>0 → left ↓, right ↑; odd n with c<0 → left ↑, right ↓.
  5. Sketch the curve passing through the x-intercepts consistent with this end behaviour.

Worked Micro-Example

Sketch y = x(x-2)(5-x). Leading terms: x · x · (-x) = -x^3, so the leading coefficient is negative and the degree is 3. Hence the curve enters from the top-left and exits to the bottom-right, crossing the x-axis at x=0, 2, 5 in that order.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic given as y = x(3 − x)
Leading terms x·(−x) = −x², so a negative quadratic — sketch as a ∩-shaped parabola through the two roots.
Quartic such as y = x(x−1)²(x−3)
Leading terms x · x² · x = x⁴ (positive), so both ends go up; the repeated root at x=1 produces a touch-point rather than a crossing.
Factor written as (k − x) rather than (x − k)
Treat (k − x) as contributing −x to the leading-term product; equivalently, factor out −1 to rewrite as −(x − k) and track the sign flip.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading only the first factor's sign (e.g. seeing leading 'x' in x(2−x)(2+x)) and concluding a positive cubic.
  • Assuming all cubics go bottom-left to top-right by default, ignoring the leading-coefficient sign.
  • Confusing 'leading coefficient' with 'constant term' when the polynomial is in factored form.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the −1 hidden inside factors like (5 − x) or (2 − x), which flips the overall sign.
  • Miscounting the degree when a factor is squared or cubed, e.g. (x−1)² contributes x² not x.
  • Drawing a curve consistent with the intercepts but reflected vertically — the classic 'right shape, wrong way up' error.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 curve sketching; no formula-booklet entry is required. Avoid invoking calculus arguments about turning points to determine orientation — end behaviour is purely an algebraic reading of the leading term.
