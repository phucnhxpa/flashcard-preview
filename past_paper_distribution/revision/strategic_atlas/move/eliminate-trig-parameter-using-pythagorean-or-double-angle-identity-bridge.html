Trigonometry

Eliminate trig parameter using Pythagorean or double-angle identity bridge

Family: Parametric Elimination Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an expression (a derivative dy/dx, dx/dy, or a coordinate pair) is written in terms of a parameter θ/t/y, but the required answer must be purely in terms of x (or a Cartesian equation in x and y). Visual cues: one equation gives sin²θ (or cos²θ) directly in terms of x, while the other expression involves sin 2θ, cos 2θ, or a different trig function of the same parameter. Equivalent phrasings: 'show that dy/dx = f(x)', 'find the Cartesian equation of the locus', 'eliminate the parameter t'. The presence of squared trig functions on one side and double-angle or mixed trig on the other is the strongest tell.

Motivation — Why this works

Parametric elimination requires bridging two expressions through a shared intermediate quantity. The Pythagorean identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 converts one squared trig into the other; the double-angle identities (sin 2θ = 2sinθ cosθ, cos 2θ = 1 − 2sin²θ = 2cos²θ − 1) decompose mixed-argument trig into pieces matching what the original equation supplies. Together these identities let us replace every trig term with an algebraic expression in x (or x and y), eliminating the parameter without solving for it explicitly — which would require messy inverse trig functions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the mismatch: the working expression contains θ (or y, or t) but the target must be in x only — parameter elimination is required.
  2. Rule out solving for θ explicitly (e.g. θ = arcsin(...)) — this introduces inverse trig and signs become awkward; the identity-bridge route is cleaner.
  3. Read off what the original equation supplies directly: typically sin²θ = (linear in x)/(constant), so cos²θ = 1 − sin²θ follows immediately from the Pythagorean identity.
  4. Identify what trig form appears in the expression to be converted: if it's sin 2θ, square it to get sin²2θ = 4sin²θ cos²θ (matches squared quantities); if it's cos 2θ, choose the version of the double-angle identity (1 − 2sin²θ vs 2cos²θ − 1) that uses exactly the squared trig the original equation provides.
  5. Substitute the squared trig expressions in x, simplify the constants, then take the (positive) square root if needed — justifying the sign from the range of the parameter.
  6. Back-substitute into the working expression and tidy: combine surds, cancel constants, invert if dy/dx was obtained from dx/dy.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. From the original equation, isolate sin²θ (or cos²θ) as a linear expression in x. E.g. x = 6sin²(2y) ⇒ sin²(2y) = x/6.
  2. Apply sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 to get the partner: cos²(2y) = 1 − x/6 = (6−x)/6.
  3. If the target trig is a double-angle product, square it: sin²2θ = 4sin²θ cos²θ; or pick cos 2θ = 1 − 2sin²θ when only sin²θ is available.
  4. Substitute the algebraic expressions for sin²θ and cos²θ; simplify the constant factors.
  5. Take the positive square root to recover sin 2θ (or sinθ·cosθ): e.g. sin(2y)cos(2y) = √[x(6−x)]/6.
  6. Substitute back into dy/dx (or dx/dy) and invert if necessary to obtain the answer in x only.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $x = 6\sin^2(2y)$, so $\frac{dx}{dy} = 24\sin(2y)\cos(2y)$. From the equation $\sin^2(2y) = x/6$, and Pythagoras gives $\cos^2(2y) = (6-x)/6$. Hence $\sin(2y)\cos(2y) = \sqrt{x(6-x)}/6$, so $\frac{dx}{dy} = 4\sqrt{x(6-x)}$ and $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{4\sqrt{x(6-x)}}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Both parametric equations give a single trig each (e.g. X = a cosθ, Y = b sinθ)
Isolate cosθ = X/a and sinθ = Y/b, then square and add using sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 — yields ellipse/circle Cartesian form directly.
One equation involves cos 2t and the other involves sin t (or cos t)
Use cos 2t = 1 − 2sin²t (if sin t is the partner) or cos 2t = 2cos²t − 1 (if cos t is the partner) — choose the form matching what's available to avoid an extra Pythagorean step.
Hyperbolic parametrics, e.g. x = a cosh²t
Use cosh²t − sinh²t = 1 in place of the Pythagorean identity; double-angle hyperbolic identities play the analogous bridging role.
Target involves sin 2θ as a single factor (not squared)
Compute sin²2θ = 4sin²θ cos²θ first using what the original equation provides, then take the positive square root, justifying the sign from the range of θ.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to solve for the parameter explicitly via inverse trig (θ = arcsin(...)) and substituting — valid but introduces sign/branch issues and clutter.
  • Computing sin θ and cos θ separately as two square roots and multiplying, instead of squaring the product first — doubles the sign-tracking work.
  • Picking the 'wrong' double-angle form (e.g. cos 2t = 2cos²t − 1 when only sin²t is available), forcing an extra Pythagorean substitution.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to square-root after using the Pythagorean identity: writing sin(2y) = x/6 instead of √(x/6).
  • Forgetting the factor of 4 in sin²2θ = 4sin²θ cos²θ.
  • Sign error in 1 − x/c: writing (x − c)/c instead of (c − x)/c.
  • Dropping a 1/2 or constant when taking the square root of a fraction like (x+1)(3−x)/4.
  • Failing to justify the positive square root from the parameter's range; sign errors when rearranging to standard form (e.g. y² = 4x − 8 vs y² = 8 − 4x).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P3/P4 parametric differentiation and parametric curves) and Further Pure (loci). Identities used — sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 and the three forms of cos 2θ, plus sin 2θ = 2sinθ cosθ — are all in the IAL formula booklet. No need for inverse-trig manipulation or branch analysis beyond justifying a positive square root from the stated parameter range.
