Binomial Expansion

Match x-powers across a linear-times-expansion product to isolate one term

Family: Term Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A question asks for a specific power of x (often the constant term or coefficient of x^k) in a product of a short polynomial factor and a binomial expansion, e.g. (3x - 1)(2 - x/4)^{10}. Cues include 'find the constant term', 'find the coefficient of x', or 'hence find...' immediately after a part (a) that produced selected terms of the expansion. The presence of two factors with known/asked structure signals that you should NOT fully multiply out.

Motivation — Why this works

In a product (A_1 x^{a_1} + A_2 x^{a_2} + ...)(B_0 + B_1 x + B_2 x^2 + ...), the coefficient of x^k is the sum over all pairs (i, j) with a_i + j = k of A_i B_j. So only a small finite set of pairings contribute, and many are ruled out instantly because the expansion is a polynomial (no negative powers). This converts a potentially long expansion into a couple of targeted lookups, often reusing terms already computed in part (a).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question wants a specific power of x (here x^0) in a product, not a full expansion.
  2. I rule out brute-force expansion: the expansion E has 11 terms and I only need certain pairings.
  3. I commit to power-matching: list each term of the short factor with its x-power, then identify which power is needed from E to combine to the target power.
  4. First concrete operation: tabulate the powers from the short factor (3x gives x^1, -1 gives x^0) and demand from E the powers x^{-1} and x^0 respectively.
  5. Tactical: discard impossible requests (negative powers from a polynomial expansion), then look up or compute only the surviving terms from E and sum the contributions with correct signs.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the target power of x in the final product (here x^0).
  2. List the terms of the short factor with their individual x-powers: 3x (power 1), -1 (power 0).
  3. For each, determine the matching power required from E so that powers add to the target.
  4. Eliminate any required power that E cannot produce (negative powers from a finite polynomial).
  5. Look up or compute the surviving coefficients in E (often from part (a)).
  6. Multiply each pair and sum with correct signs to give the requested coefficient.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the constant term in $(3x-1)(2-\tfrac{x}{4})^{10}$, given $(2-\tfrac{x}{4})^{10} = 1024 - 1280x + 720x^2 - \dots$ from part (a). The $3x$ would need an $x^{-1}$ term from $E$ — impossible. The $-1$ pairs with the constant $1024$ in $E$. So constant term $= (-1)(1024) = -1024$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Coefficient of x^1 in (3x-1)(2-x/4)^{10}
Now 3x pairs with the x^0 term of E, and -1 pairs with the x^1 term of E; sum: 3(1024) + (-1)(-1280) = 3072 + 1280 = 4352.
Pre-factor is quadratic, e.g. (ax^2 + bx + c) E
Three pairings contribute to each target power; for the constant term only c·[x^0 of E] survives because x^{-1}, x^{-2} are unavailable.
E is a series expansion of (1+x)^{-n} or (1+x)^{1/2} (P4 binomial series)
All non-negative powers of x are present up to the requested power; sum every viable pairing — no terms are eliminated by polynomial truncation, but you stop at the required degree.
Pre-factor like (1 + kx)^m with small m, multiplied by another expansion
Treat (1+kx)^m as its own short list of terms (binomial-expanded), then power-match against the second expansion as before.

Common Misreadings

  • Multiplying the full expansion by (3x - 1) and only afterwards picking out the term, losing time and risking arithmetic errors.
  • Confusing 'constant term of the product' with 'coefficient of x in E', or vice versa, because of the 3x factor.
  • Treating the binomial expansion as if it had negative powers and including a spurious x^{-1} contribution.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the minus sign on the -1 in (3x - 1) and reporting +1024 instead of -1024.
  • Using the wrong sign on terms of (2 - x/4)^{10} (the alternating signs from (-x/4)^r are easy to mishandle).
  • Forgetting to sum contributions when more than one pairing is valid (especially for non-constant target powers).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL C2 (finite binomial expansion of (a+bx)^n) and P4 (series expansions of (1+x)^n for non-integer or negative n); only requires power-matching and basic coefficient arithmetic.
