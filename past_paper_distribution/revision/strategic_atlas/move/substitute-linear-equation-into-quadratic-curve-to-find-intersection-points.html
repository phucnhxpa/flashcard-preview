Coordinate Geometry

Substitute linear equation into quadratic curve to find intersection points

Family: Simultaneous Substitution Frequency: 5 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever you need the intersection(s) of a straight line with a quadratic curve (parabola, ellipse, hyperbola, circle, or general conic), or the intersection of two lines where one is already in y = mx + c form. Visual cues: one equation is degree 1 (e.g. ax + by = c, or y = mx + c) and the other is degree 2 (e.g. y = f(x), xy = k, x²/a² ± y²/b² = 1). Phrasings include 'find where l meets C', 'find the second intersection point', 'show the line is a tangent to', or 'find the coordinates of P and Q'. Also fires for parametrised tangent/normal problems where the line carries a parameter θ or t.

Motivation — Why this works

The linear equation rearranges in one step to give y (or x) explicitly, providing a clean expression to plug into the curve equation. Substituting collapses the two-variable system into a single quadratic in one variable, which is solvable by factorisation, completing the square, or the quadratic formula. The number of real roots reveals the geometry: two distinct roots = secant (two intersection points), one repeated root = tangent (touches once), no real roots = line misses the curve. Elimination by subtraction is generally messier here because of the squared term in the curve.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice two equations in x and y: one linear (degree 1), one quadratic (degree 2). Their simultaneous solution gives intersection points.
  2. I rule out elimination by subtraction — the x² (or y², or xy) term in the curve equation prevents clean cancellation, and elimination would still leave a quadratic to solve.
  3. I commit to substitution because the linear equation rearranges trivially for one variable, while the curve equation is already arranged around y or has a clear product/quadratic structure.
  4. I decide which variable to isolate: typically y from the line if the curve is y = f(x); but if the curve is xy = k or x²/a² + y²/b² = 1, I pick whichever rearrangement avoids fractions or squaring later.
  5. After substitution I expect a quadratic Ax² + Bx + C = 0; I bring it to standard form, factorise or apply the formula, and back-substitute each root into the LINE (cheaper than the curve) to recover the matching y-coordinate.
  6. Sanity check: if the line is claimed to be a tangent, the discriminant must be zero — if it isn't, I've made an algebra slip somewhere.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rearrange the linear equation to isolate one variable, e.g. y = (c − ax)/b.
  2. Substitute this expression into the curve equation, replacing y everywhere.
  3. Expand carefully (squaring binomials, clearing fractions by multiplying through by any denominator).
  4. Collect like terms into standard quadratic form Ax² + Bx + C = 0.
  5. Solve by factorisation, quadratic formula, or completing the square.
  6. Back-substitute each x-root into the LINEAR equation to obtain the corresponding y-coordinate.
  7. State the intersection points as ordered pairs (x, y); if a tangent was expected, verify the discriminant B² − 4AC = 0.

Worked Micro-Example

Find where $x + y = 6$ meets $y = 6x - 2x^2 + 1$. From the line: $y = 6 - x$. Substitute: $6 - x = 6x - 2x^2 + 1 \Rightarrow 2x^2 - 7x + 5 = 0 \Rightarrow (2x-5)(x-1) = 0$. So $x = 1$ or $x = 5/2$, giving points $(1, 5)$ and $(5/2, 7/2)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line meets a rectangular hyperbola xy = k
Isolate y from the line, substitute into xy = k, multiply through by any denominator to clear fractions, giving a quadratic in x.
Line meets an ellipse or hyperbola x²/a² ± y²/b² = 1
Isolate y, substitute, expand the square (y)² carefully — this is the most error-prone step — then collect into quadratic form.
Parametrised tangent/normal at point (a t², 2at) on parabola y² = 4ax
Substitution produces a quadratic with parameter-dependent coefficients; one root is the known parameter t, allowing factorisation and identification of the second intersection.
Two linear equations (line meets line) where one is already y = mx + c
Substitute directly into the other line — the same reduction logic, but the resulting equation is linear, not quadratic.
Axis intercepts only required
Shortcut: set x = 0 or y = 0 in the line equation directly; no need to involve the curve.
Two conics intersecting
Subtract one equation from the other first to eliminate the squared terms and produce a linear relation, then substitute back into either conic.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to equate the line ax + by = c with the curve y = f(x) without first rearranging the line into y = ... form, leading to sign errors.
  • Substituting the found x-values back into the curve equation rather than the line — wasteful and error-prone, especially for quadratic curves.
  • Mistaking a tangency problem for a general intersection — failing to use the discriminant = 0 condition when the line is stated to be a tangent.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when transposing terms across the equals sign after substitution.
  • Expansion errors when squaring a binomial like (mx + c)² — missing the cross term 2mcx.
  • Forgetting to multiply through by the denominator when the substituted expression involves a fraction.
  • Finding x-values but failing to compute the matching y-values to give complete coordinates.
  • For tangent problems: not verifying that the quadratic has a repeated root, missing a chance to catch earlier algebra slips.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (P1–P4) and Further Pure scope. Relevant for circle geometry (P2), conics (FP1/FP3), and tangent/normal problems on parametric curves. No formula booklet entry needed — this is foundational algebraic technique. Avoid generalising to systems of three or more equations or to non-conic curves of degree ≥ 3, which lie outside A-level scope.
