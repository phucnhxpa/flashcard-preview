Algebra & Functions

Translate 4 distinct real roots into two distinct positive roots of the quadratic-in-u

Family: Root Multiplicity Analysis Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a problem demands a specific number of distinct real x-values (typically 4) and the equation reduces to a quadratic in u = x² (or another even substitution like u = x⁴, u = cos²x). Cues: phrases like '4 distinct intersection points', '4 distinct real solutions', 'curves cross 4 times', combined with a biquadratic structure. Also fires when symmetry x ↔ −x is visible in the original equation.

Motivation — Why this works

Under u = x², each positive u-root produces exactly two real x-values (±√u), a zero u-root produces only one (x = 0), and a negative u-root produces none. So '4 distinct real x' is equivalent to 'two distinct strictly positive u-roots'. The three Vieta-style conditions — discriminant > 0, sum of roots > 0, product of roots > 0 — are necessary and sufficient for that, avoiding the need to solve the quadratic explicitly in terms of the parameter.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation is even in x (only x² appears), so the substitution u = x² collapses it to a quadratic, and roots come in ± pairs.
  2. Rule out counting strategies that work directly in x (discriminant of the quartic, sketching) — they're harder than translating the count to u.
  3. Commit to the principle: # of real x-roots = 2·(positive u-roots) + 1·(zero u-roots), so 4 distinct x ⇔ 2 distinct positive u.
  4. Bridge: write the quadratic in u in standard form au² + bu + c = 0 and read off Vieta's: sum = −b/a, product = c/a.
  5. Tactically impose three strict inequalities — Δ > 0, sum > 0, product > 0 — then intersect the resulting parameter ranges.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Substitute u = x² to reduce the equation to au² + bu + c = 0.
  2. Impose Δ = b² − 4ac > 0 for two distinct u-roots.
  3. Impose sum of roots −b/a > 0 (both roots positive ⇒ positive sum).
  4. Impose product of roots c/a > 0 (both roots positive ⇒ positive product).
  5. Solve each inequality for the parameter and intersect to get the final range.
  6. Check edge cases: a = 0 (degenerate), and confirm strict inequalities (not ≥) so that no repeated or zero u-root sneaks in.

Worked Micro-Example

For $kx^4 + 9x^2 - 1 = 0$, set $u = x^2$ to get $ku^2 + 9u - 1 = 0$. Need two distinct positive $u$: $\Delta = 81 + 4k > 0 \Rightarrow k > -\tfrac{81}{4}$; sum $= -9/k > 0 \Rightarrow k < 0$; product $= -1/k > 0 \Rightarrow k < 0$. Intersection: $-\tfrac{81}{4} < k < 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Exactly 2 distinct real x-values from a biquadratic
Need exactly one positive u-root: either one positive and one negative (product < 0), or a repeated positive u-root (Δ = 0 with sum > 0).
Exactly 3 distinct real x-values
Need one positive u-root and u = 0 as the other root: impose c = 0 and the remaining root −b/a > 0.
Trig equation reducing to quadratic in cos x or cos²x with a count of solutions in [0, 2π)
Translate root count via the multiplicity of x-preimages of each u-value in the chosen interval; positivity is replaced by 'lies in [−1, 1]' or '[0, 1]'.
Line y = mx + c meeting curve y = a/x giving quadratic in x directly
Use Δ > 0 alone for two distinct intersections; sign-of-roots logic only needed when an extra positivity constraint (e.g. both intersections in x > 0) is imposed.

Common Misreadings

  • Applying only Δ > 0 and forgetting that the u-roots must also be positive.
  • Counting a repeated positive u-root (Δ = 0) as giving 4 distinct x-values; it gives only 2.
  • Treating u = 0 as a 'positive' root and including it in the count of 4.
  • Forgetting that the leading coefficient may itself depend on the parameter, so the case a = 0 must be handled separately.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors in Vieta's: for $ku^2 + 9u - 1 = 0$ the sum is $-9/k$ and product is $-1/k$, not $9/k$ and $1/k$.
  • Dividing inequalities by the parameter without tracking its sign, flipping inequalities incorrectly.
  • Reporting one of the three sub-ranges instead of intersecting all three.
  • Using ≥ instead of > in the discriminant or positivity conditions, which admits repeated or zero roots.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure scope: quadratic discriminant, Vieta's relations (sum/product of roots), and substitution u = x² for biquadratics — all standard P2/P3/FP1 material.
