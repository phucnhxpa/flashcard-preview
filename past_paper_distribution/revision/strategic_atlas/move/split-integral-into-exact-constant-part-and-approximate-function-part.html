Integration

Split integral into exact constant part and approximate function part

Family: Linearity Of Integration Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an integrand is a sum where one term is elementary (constant or polynomial) and another term is a scalar multiple of a function whose integral has been given or computed earlier (e.g. 'use your answer from part (a)'). Typical cues: integrand of the form a + b·f(x), or after a log/exp/identity decomposition leaves a constant plus a scaled f(x). Also fires when the question links parts via 'hence' and provides a numerical value for ∫f(x)dx on the same interval.

Motivation — Why this works

By linearity, ∫(a + b·f(x))dx = a(β−α) + b∫f(x)dx, so the integral cleanly separates into an exactly computable piece and a piece reusing the given value. This is the only sensible route when no closed-form antiderivative for f is available in scope (e.g. log₁₀x in IAL Pure). Splitting also maximises accuracy: only the f-term carries approximation error, while the constant contribution is exact.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand is a sum a + b·f(x), where f(x) matches a function whose integral was supplied in an earlier part.
  2. Rule out trying to antidifferentiate f(x) directly — it is outside A-level technique (or just wasteful given the hint).
  3. Commit to linearity: ∫(a + b·f) = ∫a + b∫f, because both pieces are now accessible.
  4. Bridge: evaluate ∫a dx exactly using [ax] between the limits, then plug the given numerical value of ∫f(x)dx into the second piece.
  5. Tactically, keep the exact part exact and only introduce the approximation at the end so rounding does not propagate.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write ∫ₐᵇ (a + b·f(x)) dx = ∫ₐᵇ a dx + b∫ₐᵇ f(x) dx by linearity.
  2. Evaluate ∫ₐᵇ a dx = a(b − a) exactly.
  3. Substitute the given/earlier value V for ∫ₐᵇ f(x) dx, giving b·V.
  4. Add: a(b − a) + b·V, presenting to the precision implied by V.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\int_{2}^{14} \log_{10} x\,dx \approx 10.10$, evaluate $\int_{2}^{14}(2 + 3\log_{10} x)\,dx$.\\ By linearity: $\int_{2}^{14} 2\,dx + 3\int_{2}^{14} \log_{10} x\,dx$.\\ Exact part: $[2x]_{2}^{14} = 28 - 4 = 24$.\\ Approximate part: $3 \times 10.10 = 30.30$.\\ Total: $24 + 30.30 = 54.30$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 3log₁₀x as if it were log₁₀(3x) and trying to apply the given value directly.
  • Reading 'use your answer from (a)' as requiring substitution into an antiderivative, instead of reusing the numerical integral.
  • Assuming the constant term integrates to the constant itself (forgetting the (β−α) factor).

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to add the constant integral and reporting only b·V (e.g. 30.30 instead of 54.30).
  • Evaluating ∫ₐᵇ a dx as a·b instead of a(b−a) (dropping the lower limit).
  • Pulling the scalar b out incorrectly or losing it (e.g. using V instead of b·V).
  • Rounding the approximate part early and losing accuracy in the final sum.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure: relies only on linearity of the definite integral and basic evaluation of ∫a dx, with reuse of a given numerical integral from an earlier part.
