Trigonometry

Subtract the minor angle from 2π to obtain the reflex angle for major arcs and sectors

Family: Angle Identification Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question names a sector or arc whose label traces the 'long way round' the circle (e.g. sector ABCOA, arc ABC passing through a far point B), but the angle you can compute or are given is the small angle between two radii at the centre. Visual cues: a diagram where the named arc clearly bulges around the far side of the circle, or labelling that lists three or more boundary points instead of just the two endpoints. Equivalent phrasings: 'find the area of the major sector', 'find the length of the major arc', or 'the shaded region containing the centre on the far side'. Whenever the given/derived angle is acute or obtuse (less than π) but the region wanted wraps past π, this move fires.

Motivation — Why this works

A full revolution at the centre is 2π radians, so the minor angle θ and the reflex angle 2π − θ together make a complete turn. The sector-area formula A = ½r²θ and the arc-length formula s = rθ both require the angle actually subtended by the region in question, not just any angle in the diagram. Substituting θ directly when the major region is wanted gives the minor area/arc — a sign-free but conceptually wrong answer. Replacing θ with 2π − θ converts the easily-computed small angle into the genuine subtended angle of the major region.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the labelling: the sector/arc passes through an intermediate point on the far side of the circle, signalling the major region rather than the direct one between the two radii.
  2. Rule out simply plugging the given angle into ½r²θ or rθ — that would compute the minor sector/arc, which is not what is asked.
  3. Rule out the supplementary angle π − θ; that is a reflection across a diameter, not a reflex angle, and applies only when triangles or straight lines force it.
  4. Commit to the reflex relationship because the two regions (minor + major) must together fill the full disc/circumference, so their central angles sum to 2π.
  5. Bridge to computation: write θ_major = 2π − θ_minor, then substitute into the appropriate formula (½r²θ for area, rθ for arc length).
  6. Tactical check: confirm θ_minor < π (otherwise it was already the reflex angle), and confirm that 2π − θ_minor > π as expected for a reflex angle.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the minor central angle θ from the given data (e.g. θ = ∠AOC).
  2. Compute the reflex angle: θ_reflex = 2π − θ.
  3. Substitute into the sector-area formula: A_major = ½ r² (2π − θ).
  4. Or substitute into the arc-length formula: s_major = r(2π − θ).
  5. Evaluate numerically to the required precision and attach correct units (cm², m, etc.).

Worked Micro-Example

Given r = 6 cm and minor ∠AOC = 0.7 rad, find the major sector area. Reflex angle = $2\pi - 0.7$. Then $A = \tfrac{1}{2}(6)^2(2\pi - 0.7) = 18(2\pi - 0.7) \approx 100.5\ \text{cm}^2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Degree-measured problems
Replace 2π with 360°: reflex angle = 360° − θ°, then use A = (θ/360)πr² or s = (θ/360)·2πr.
Major arc length instead of major sector area
Same reflex angle 2π − θ, but substitute into s = rθ rather than ½r²θ.
Angle θ obtained indirectly (e.g. via cosine rule or right-triangle trig before this step)
Compute θ_minor first from the triangle, then apply 2π − θ; the move is the final stage rather than the only stage.

Common Misreadings

  • Using the given minor angle directly in ½r²θ, producing the minor sector area instead of the major sector area.
  • Substituting π − θ (supplementary angle) instead of 2π − θ (reflex angle), confusing reflection with full-turn complement.
  • Misreading the diagram and assuming the named sector is the minor one when the labelling actually traces the long way round.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to check which arc/sector is named (e.g. ABCOA vs AOCA) — always trace the labelling on the diagram.
  • Mixing radians and degrees: using 360 − θ when θ is in radians, or 2π − θ when θ is in degrees.
  • Misplacing an auxiliary point (such as where a line meets the circle) so that the 'minor' angle you compute is wrong, propagating the error into the reflex angle.

A-level Scope Notes

This is core Edexcel IAL P2/P3 circular-measure material; the formulae s = rθ and A = ½r²θ (with θ in radians) appear in the formula booklet. No out-of-scope generalisation is needed — stay with radians for the standard formulae and convert at the end if the question demands degrees.
