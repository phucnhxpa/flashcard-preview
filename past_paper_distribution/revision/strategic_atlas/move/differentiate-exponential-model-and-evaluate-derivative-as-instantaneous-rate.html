Differentiation

Differentiate exponential model and evaluate derivative as instantaneous rate

Family: Physical Interpretation Of Derivative Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a modelling question asks for the 'rate of increase/decrease' of a quantity governed by an exponential law such as θ = A − Be^{−kt} or P = P_0 e^{kt}, and gives a specific instant t = t_0. Phrases like 'rate at which …', 'how fast … is changing', 'instantaneous rate', or units of (quantity)·(time)^{−1} all signal this. The cue is a value of t supplied together with a request for a rate, not for the quantity itself.

Motivation — Why this works

By definition, the rate of change of Y with respect to X is dY/dX, so 'rate of increase of θ at t = 9' is dθ/dt|_{t=9}. For exponential models the chain rule gives d/dt[Ae^{f(t)}] = A f'(t) e^{f(t)}, so the structure of the derivative mirrors the original function up to a multiplicative constant. This makes evaluation cheap once e^{f(t_0)} is known from an earlier part. The sign of the derivative also confirms whether the quantity is increasing or decreasing, providing a built-in sanity check.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question says 'rate' with a specific t-value supplied — that flags an instantaneous derivative, not a function value.
  2. Rule out re-substituting into θ itself (that would just repeat the value question) and rule out average rate Δθ/Δt since no interval is given.
  3. Commit to computing dθ/dt because 'rate of change of θ with respect to t' is, by definition, this derivative.
  4. Bridge: differentiate term by term, applying the chain rule to e^{−kt} to pick up the factor −k.
  5. Tactically reuse any previously computed value of e^{−kt_0} or k from earlier parts rather than recomputing, and check the sign matches the stated direction (increase ⇒ positive).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the quantity to differentiate (the model for θ, P, s, etc.) and the variable (usually t).
  2. Differentiate using the chain rule: d/dt[Ae^{−kt}] = −kAe^{−kt}; constants differentiate to 0.
  3. Write dθ/dt in closed form, e.g. dθ/dt = 180k e^{−kt}.
  4. Substitute the given instant t = t_0 and any known constants (k, e^{−kt_0}).
  5. Evaluate numerically and state with correct units (e.g. °C min^{−1}); check the sign matches the physical context.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\theta = 198 - 180e^{-kt}$ with $k = \tfrac{1}{5}\ln(5/3)$, find the rate of increase at $t=9$. Differentiate: $\dfrac{d\theta}{dt} = 180k\,e^{-kt}$. At $t=9$: $e^{-9k} = (3/5)^{9/5} \approx 0.3988$, so $\dfrac{d\theta}{dt} = 180 \times \tfrac{1}{5}\ln(5/3) \times 0.3988 \approx 7.33$ °C min$^{-1}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Population growth P = P_0 e^{kt}
dP/dt = kP_0 e^{kt} = kP; often quickest to write rate as kP(t_0).
Newton's law of cooling θ = θ_∞ + (θ_0 − θ_∞)e^{−kt}
dθ/dt = −k(θ − θ_∞); derivative proportional to current excess temperature.
Kinematics with s(t) given
'Velocity' = ds/dt; 'acceleration' = d²s/dt²; differentiate then evaluate.
Rate of change of a difference, e.g. d/dt(198 − θ)
Differentiate the difference directly, or use −dθ/dt; sign flips accordingly.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'rate of increase at t = 9' as the value θ(9) and substituting into the original model.
  • Computing an average rate (θ(9) − θ(0))/9 instead of the instantaneous derivative.
  • Reading k as the rate itself rather than a parameter inside the exponent.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the chain-rule factor −k when differentiating e^{−kt}.
  • Sign error: leaving the answer negative when context says the quantity is increasing.
  • Forgetting units, or mixing minutes/seconds when k was defined per minute.
  • Recomputing e^{−kt_0} from scratch and introducing rounding error rather than reusing an exact form from an earlier part.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure/Core content: chain-rule differentiation of e^{f(t)} and interpretation of derivatives as rates of change in modelling contexts (P3/P4 exponential models, kinematics).
