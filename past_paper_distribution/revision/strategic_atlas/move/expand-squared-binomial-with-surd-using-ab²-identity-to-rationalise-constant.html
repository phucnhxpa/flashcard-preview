Algebra & Functions

Expand squared binomial with surd using (A−B)² identity to rationalise constant

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A binomial of the form (px ± q√n)² appears and must be expanded into ax² + bx + c form. One term contains a surd, and the target form requires a rational constant c. Cues include 'expand and simplify', 'write in the form ax² + bx + c', or a follow-up that needs the constant term separated from x-coefficients. Also fires when completing the square produces (x − k√n)² that must be expanded back out.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity (A − B)² = A² − 2AB + B² partitions the expansion into three slots whose surd-status is predictable: A² is purely polynomial in x, B² rationalises because (k√n)² = k²n, and the cross-term −2AB carries the surd but only as a coefficient of x. This guarantees the constant term is rational and isolates the surd into exactly one place, which is exactly what the target form demands. FOIL gives the same answer but obscures this structural separation, making sign and grouping errors more likely.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the expression is a single binomial squared with one surd term, and the target form ax² + bx + c demands a rational constant.
  2. I rule out FOIL-by-brute-force: it would work but mixes the four products together, making it easy to lose track of which pieces are rational vs surd.
  3. I commit to (A − B)² = A² − 2AB + B² because it splits the work into three clearly-typed terms: x² term, x term (with surd coefficient), constant (rational after squaring the surd).
  4. First operation: assign A = px (the polynomial part) and B = q√n (the surd part), keeping the sign of B with B itself.
  5. Inside the move, I square the surd carefully as (q√n)² = q²·n (not q·n, not q² + n), and I track the minus sign on the cross-term since it determines the sign of b.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Match the expression to (A − B)² with A = polynomial part, B = surd part
  2. Compute A² (a clean polynomial term)
  3. Compute −2AB (the cross-term — keeps the surd as a coefficient of x)
  4. Compute B² = (coefficient)² × (radicand) to rationalise the constant
  5. Sum the three terms in standard ax² + bx + c order

Worked Micro-Example

Expand $(3x - 10\sqrt{2})^2$.\\ With $A = 3x$, $B = 10\sqrt{2}$:\\ $A^2 = 9x^2$,\quad $-2AB = -2(3x)(10\sqrt{2}) = -60\sqrt{2}\,x$,\quad $B^2 = 100 \cdot 2 = 200$.\\ Result: $9x^2 - 60\sqrt{2}\,x + 200$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Completing the square with surd vertex, e.g. expanding $(x - \sqrt{5})^2 + c$
Same identity; the constant 5 from $(\sqrt{5})^2$ combines with c to give a rational final constant.
Sum form $(a + b\sqrt{n})^2$ in surd manipulation questions
Use (A + B)² = A² + 2AB + B²; cross-term sign flips, B² still rationalises.
Modulus-squared of a complex number $|a + bi|^2 = a^2 + b^2$
Structural analogue: squaring eliminates the 'i' just as it eliminates √n; only A² and |B|² survive because cross-terms cancel via conjugation.
Expanding $(f(x) - k\sqrt{n})^2$ where f(x) is itself a polynomial
A² becomes (f(x))² requiring its own expansion; cross-term is −2k√n·f(x); B² still rationalises to k²n.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading $(10\sqrt{2})^2$ as $10^2 + (\sqrt{2})^2 = 102$, confusing $(AB)^2 = A^2 B^2$ with $A^2 + B^2$.
  • Treating the surd as if it would persist in the constant, and so leaving the answer as $9x^2 - 60\sqrt{2}x + 100\sqrt{2}$.
  • Misidentifying the move as requiring rationalising the denominator (no denominator is present here).

Pitfalls

  • Computing $(10\sqrt{2})^2 = 10 \cdot 2 = 20$ instead of $100 \cdot 2 = 200$ — failing to square the coefficient.
  • Sign error in the cross-term: writing $+60\sqrt{2}\,x$ instead of $-60\sqrt{2}\,x$ when the binomial has a minus.
  • Dropping the factor of 2 in $-2AB$, giving $-30\sqrt{2}\,x$.
  • Leaving $\sqrt{2}\cdot\sqrt{2}$ unsimplified rather than writing it as 2.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1 surds and quadratic expansion; reappears in completing-the-square and in Further Pure for complex modulus-squared computations).
