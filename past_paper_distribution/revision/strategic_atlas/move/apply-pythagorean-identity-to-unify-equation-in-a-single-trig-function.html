Trigonometry

Apply Pythagorean identity to unify equation in a single trig function

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

The equation contains a squared trig term of one function (e.g. cos²θ or sin²θ) alongside linear terms in the other function — for instance 3sin²x + 7cosx − 5, or 3cos²θ − sinθ = 2, or 3sinθ·cos²θ on one side with sinθ-only on the other. The mixed presence of two trig functions blocks direct factoring or solving, but only one of them appears squared (or only as an even power). The target is a polynomial (quadratic or cubic) purely in the linear function. Equivalent phrasing: 'one variable appears only via its square, and I want to rewrite that square so everything is in the other variable.'

Motivation — Why this works

The Pythagorean identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 — equivalently cos²θ = 1 − sin²θ or sin²θ = 1 − cos²θ — is the canonical bridge between sin and cos when one appears squared. Substituting it converts the lone squared term into an expression of the desired variable, collapsing the equation into a single-variable polynomial that can be factored or solved by the quadratic formula. This is the standard pathway whenever an equation involves only even powers of one trig function and the other linearly. It avoids the unnecessary complexity that double-angle forms (like cos2θ = 1 − 2sin²θ) would introduce.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation mixes two trig functions, but only one of them appears as a square (e.g. cos²θ) while the other appears linearly (e.g. sinθ).
  2. Rule out double-angle identities (cos2θ = 1 − 2sin²θ, cos2θ = 2cos²θ − 1, half-angle forms): these would introduce a new angle 2θ rather than collapse to one variable. Rule out tan-substitutions: there's no free linear term of the squared variable to motivate them.
  3. Commit to the basic Pythagorean swap because it's the only identity that turns the squared term directly into the other variable while preserving the single-angle θ.
  4. Choose the direction by inspection: replace the variable that is ONLY squared, leaving the variable that already appears linearly untouched. So if the equation has cos²θ and sinθ, use cos²θ = 1 − sin²θ (not the reverse).
  5. Substitute, expand carefully (watch the sign on the −1·(squared term)), and rearrange to standard polynomial form: ax² + bx + c = 0 or ax³ + bx² + cx = 0.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify which trig function appears only as an even power; call it f(θ), and the other g(θ).
  2. Apply the Pythagorean identity: f²(θ) = 1 − g²(θ).
  3. Substitute into the equation, replacing every occurrence of f²(θ).
  4. Expand brackets carefully, tracking signs (e.g. 3(1 − sin²θ) = 3 − 3sin²θ, not 3 + 3sin²θ).
  5. Collect like terms and rearrange all terms to one side to obtain a polynomial in g(θ) equal to zero.
  6. Proceed to factor, apply the quadratic formula, or factor out a common g(θ) (for cubics).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $3\cos^2\theta - \sin\theta = 2$. Substitute $\cos^2\theta = 1 - \sin^2\theta$: $3(1-\sin^2\theta) - \sin\theta = 2$. Expand: $3 - 3\sin^2\theta - \sin\theta = 2$. Rearrange: $3\sin^2\theta + \sin\theta - 1 = 0$ — a standard quadratic in $\sin\theta$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation has sin²θ and cosθ (mirror case)
Use sin²θ = 1 − cos²θ instead — same Pythagorean logic, opposite direction, yielding a polynomial in cosθ.
Equation has tanθ and sec²θ (or cotθ and csc²θ)
Use the related Pythagorean identity sec²θ = 1 + tan²θ (or csc²θ = 1 + cot²θ) instead of the sin/cos form.
Cubic target after cross-multiplying (e.g. 3sinθ·cos²θ = …)
Same substitution; the result is a cubic 3sinθ − 3sin³θ = … which factors via a common sinθ.
Equation contains a genuine cos2θ (double angle, not cos²θ)
Switch to cos2θ = 1 − 2sin²θ (or 2cos²θ − 1) depending on which single variable is desired.

Common Misreadings

  • Mistaking cos²θ for cos2θ and reaching for a double-angle identity when the simple Pythagorean swap is what's needed.
  • Believing 'double-angle is more advanced so more likely correct' and over-engineering the substitution.
  • Substituting in the wrong direction (e.g. replacing cosθ via cos²θ = 1 − sin²θ when sin appears squared instead) and creating extra terms.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when expanding a(1 − f²θ): writing a − a·f²θ as a + a·f²θ flips the leading coefficient of the resulting polynomial.
  • Choosing cos2θ = 2cos²θ − 1 by mistake when the expression contains cos²θ of a single angle — this identity is for cos of a doubled angle, not cos squared.
  • Forgetting to bring all terms to one side before factoring/applying the quadratic formula.
  • Losing solutions by dividing through by a trig factor (e.g. sinθ) instead of factoring it out — always factor, never divide.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P2/P3) trigonometric identities and equations. The identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 is in the formula booklet; its rearrangements are standard. Avoid invoking complex-exponential or sum-to-product derivations, which are out of scope.
