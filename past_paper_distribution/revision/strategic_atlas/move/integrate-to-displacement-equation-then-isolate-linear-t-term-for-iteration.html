Integration

Integrate to displacement equation then isolate linear-T term for iteration

Family: Rearrange To Iteration Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question says 'show that T satisfies T = g(T)' (or x = g(x)) following a kinematics or rates setup where v(t) or dx/dt mixes a constant/polynomial term with transcendental terms (exponentials, trig). The integral $\int_0^T v\,dt = $ (given displacement) produces an equation containing one isolated linear-T term plus transcendental terms in T. The phrasing 'show that' with a target rearrangement is the strongest cue. Also fires when the target form has a single $T$ on the left and $g(T)$ on the right with $T$ trapped inside exponentials/logs.

Motivation — Why this works

Integrating a constant gives a term linear in the upper limit T, while integrating exponentials/trig in t gives transcendental terms in T. Only the linear-T term can be algebraically isolated, so dividing through by its coefficient produces the canonical $T = g(T)$ form. Transcendental terms cannot be solved for T in closed form, which is exactly why the question subsequently asks for fixed-point iteration. The lower-bound evaluation contributes constants that combine with the given displacement to form the constant in g(T).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question gives a target form $T = g(T)$ and a velocity (or rate) function with a constant term plus transcendental terms — this is a reverse-engineering task from $\int_0^T v\,dt = D$.
  2. Rule out solving algebraically: the transcendental terms in T cannot be unwound, so no closed-form solution exists; iteration is the only path.
  3. Commit to the route: integrate v(t) term by term, evaluate at both bounds, set equal to D, then isolate the linear-T term and divide by its coefficient.
  4. Bridge: write the antiderivative carefully, watching chain-rule factors $1/k$ for $e^{kt}$ terms and sign flips when $k$ is negative.
  5. Tactical: collect the lower-bound constants together with D on one side; the linear-T coefficient (often the constant term in v) becomes the divisor that produces the $1/c$ prefactor in g(T).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write $\int_0^T v(t)\,dt = D$ where D is the given displacement.
  2. Antidifferentiate term by term: constant $c \to cT$; $e^{kt} \to (1/k)e^{kt}$; mind sign of k.
  3. Evaluate at upper limit T and subtract value at lower limit 0; keep the lower-bound constants explicit.
  4. Set the result equal to D and gather the linear-T term on one side, all other terms (constants and transcendental-in-T) on the other.
  5. Divide through by the coefficient of T to obtain $T = g(T)$ matching the printed target.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $v = 12 - e^{t-10} - 12e^{-0.75t}$ and $\int_0^T v\,dt = 100$: antiderivative is $12t - e^{t-10} + 16e^{-0.75t}$. Evaluating: $12T - e^{T-10} + 16e^{-0.75T} - (-e^{-10} + 16) = 100$, so $12T = 116 + e^{T-10} - 16e^{-0.75T} - e^{-10}$, giving $T = \tfrac{1}{12}\bigl(116 - 16e^{-0.75T} + e^{T-10} - e^{-10}\bigr)$. $\square$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Newton's law of cooling or growth: $\int_0^T (a - be^{-kt})\,dt = D$
Antiderivative is $aT + (b/k)e^{-kt}$; isolate aT and divide by a.
Kinematics with trig velocity: $v = c - \sin(kt)$ over $[0,T]$
Integral gives $cT + (1/k)\cos(kT) - 1/k$; isolate cT, divide by c to get $T = g(T)$ involving cosine.
Area equation: 'show that x satisfies x = g(x)' from $\int_0^x f(t)\,dt = A$
Same mechanic but the linear term comes from a constant term in f; lower bound may be nonzero, giving extra constants.
Equation of the form $f(x)=0$ rearranged for iteration (no integral)
Skip integration; just isolate the dominant linear term and divide by its coefficient to produce $x = g(x)$.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading the target $T = g(T)$ as something to solve algebraically rather than reverse-engineer from the integral.
  • Treating the integration as indefinite and forgetting the lower-bound contribution, losing the constant in g(T).
  • Confusing the antiderivative of $-12e^{-0.75t}$: forgetting the sign flip from dividing by $-0.75$, writing $-16e^{-0.75t}$ instead of $+16e^{-0.75t}$.

Pitfalls

  • Omitting evaluation at $t=0$, which typically contributes the constants that combine with D to form the constant in g(T).
  • Chain-rule scaling errors on $e^{kt}$: dividing by $k$ incorrectly when k is negative or fractional.
  • Sign errors when transposing transcendental terms across the equality before dividing.
  • Dividing only some terms by the coefficient of T instead of the whole right-hand side.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure (P3/P4) material: definite integration of exponentials and constants, plus the iteration $x_{n+1}=g(x_n)$ topic. No techniques beyond syllabus are required.
