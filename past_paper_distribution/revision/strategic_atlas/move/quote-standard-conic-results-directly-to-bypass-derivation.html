Coordinate Geometry

Quote standard conic results directly to bypass derivation

Family: Standard Result Recall Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question concerns a conic in standard form (parabola y² = 4ax, ellipse x²/a² + y²/b² = 1, hyperbola x²/a² − y²/b² = 1, or rectangular hyperbola xy = c²) and asks for a defining feature (focus, directrix) or the tangent/normal at a parametric point. Phrasings such as 'write down the directrix', 'state the focus', or 'find the equation of the tangent at the point P(a cos θ, b sin θ)' are strong cues. The verbs 'write down' or 'state' especially signal that a one-line bookwork answer is expected. Whenever you see a clean standard form together with a request for a textbook feature, this move fires.

Motivation — Why this works

These results are bookwork on the Edexcel FP3 (Further Pure 3) syllabus and are derivable from the focus-directrix definition or from implicit/parametric differentiation, but deriving them under timed conditions burns minutes and invites sign and reciprocal errors. Quoting, for example, directrix x = −a for y² = 4ax, or tangent (x cos θ)/a + (y sin θ)/b = 1 for the ellipse, gives the answer in one line and in a form (e.g. equated to 1) that is convenient for follow-up work like finding intercepts. The underlying principle is that the standard parametric tangent is precisely what implicit differentiation produces after simplification — recall short-circuits the algebra.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the conic is presented in pristine standard form and the question uses 'write down' / 'state' / asks for a textbook object (focus, directrix, tangent at parametric P) — this is bookwork.
  2. I rule out from-scratch derivation: focus-directrix definition for the parabola, or implicit differentiation followed by point-gradient substitution for the tangent. These are valid but slow and error-prone, and the question's wording does not demand them.
  3. I commit to recall: the formula booklet / FP3 bookwork supplies these results, so quoting is both legitimate and rewarded.
  4. I bridge to action by reading off the parameters: identify a (and b where relevant) from the equation, and read off the parameter (θ for ellipse, t for hyperbola/parabola) for any parametric point given.
  5. I make a tactical check on signs and form: ellipse tangent uses '+', hyperbola tangent uses '−'; parabola directrix is x = −a, not x = a (which is the focus). I match the formula's parameter letter to the one in the question to avoid notational mismatch.
  6. I write the result in one line; if the next part asks for intercepts or a normal, the form-equated-to-1 (or the gradient I can read off) is already set up for me.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the conic and read off a (and b if applicable) from the standard form.
  2. Identify the parametric point if given, e.g. (a cos θ, b sin θ) for the ellipse, (a sec t, b tan t) for the hyperbola, (at², 2at) for the parabola, (ct, c/t) for xy = c².
  3. Quote the appropriate standard result: directrix x = −a for y² = 4ax; tangent (x cos θ)/a + (y sin θ)/b = 1 for the ellipse; (x sec t)/a − (y tan t)/b = 1 for the hyperbola; ty = x + at² for the parabola; x + t²y = 2ct for xy = c².
  4. Substitute the specific numerical a, b from the question.
  5. If a normal is required, take the negative reciprocal of the tangent's gradient and apply point-gradient form through the parametric point.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the tangent to $\tfrac{x^2}{9}+\tfrac{y^2}{4}=1$ at $P(3\cos\theta, 2\sin\theta)$. Identify $a=3, b=2$. Quote $\tfrac{x\cos\theta}{a}+\tfrac{y\sin\theta}{b}=1$. Substitute: $\tfrac{x\cos\theta}{3}+\tfrac{y\sin\theta}{2}=1$. Done in one line; setting $y=0$ gives x-intercept $3\sec\theta$ immediately.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Parabola y² = 4ax, asked for directrix or focus
Directrix is x = −a; focus is (a, 0); vertex at origin. Extract a from 4a = coefficient of x first if needed (e.g. y² = 12x ⇒ a = 3).
Hyperbola x²/a² − y²/b² = 1 at (a sec t, b tan t)
Tangent is (x sec t)/a − (y tan t)/b = 1 — note the MINUS sign between terms (not plus as in the ellipse).
Rectangular hyperbola xy = c² at (ct, c/t)
Tangent is x + t²y = 2ct; normal is t³x − ty = c(t⁴ − 1) — same recall strategy, different surface.
Parabola y² = 4ax at (at², 2at)
Tangent is ty = x + at²; normal is y + tx = 2at + at³.
Conic given in non-standard form, e.g. y² = 8x
First match to y² = 4ax to extract a (here 4a = 8 so a = 2), then quote the result.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing the directrix x = −a with the focus x-coordinate x = a.
  • Mistaking the coefficient of x in y² = 4ax for a itself (e.g. reading a = 12 from y² = 12x instead of a = 3).
  • Swapping the ellipse tangent (+) with the hyperbola tangent (−), or vice versa.
  • Confusing the tangent formula with the normal formula at the parametric point.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error: hyperbola tangent has a minus between the two terms, ellipse has a plus.
  • Notational mismatch: the question may use t where the standard formula uses θ — rewrite consistently.
  • If the question says 'show that the tangent has equation …', merely quoting may not score full marks; a derivation may be required.
  • Forgetting to extract a (or b) when the conic is not pre-normalised — always rewrite into standard form first.

A-level Scope Notes

All results here are Edexcel IAL Further Pure 3 (FP3) bookwork on conics; the parabola, ellipse, hyperbola and rectangular hyperbola tangent/normal forms appear in the FP3 formula booklet. Do not extend this recall to rotated or translated conics (e.g. (x − h)²/a² + (y − k)²/b² = 1) without first translating to standard form, and avoid invoking general conic theory (eccentricity-based directrix for ellipse/hyperbola) beyond what FP3 examines.
