Algebra & Functions

Exponentiate both sides with base 10 to recover the variable from its logarithm

Family: Anti Logarithm Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

After substitution or rearrangement, the equation has been reduced to the form log₁₀M = k where k is a numerical value (or a closed expression), but the question asks for M itself, not log₁₀M. Look for cues like 'find M', 'calculate the mass/population/intensity' when the working model is logarithmic. Also fires when a linear regression model in log-space (e.g. log₁₀y = m log₁₀x + c) has been evaluated and the original-scale variable is required.

Motivation — Why this works

By definition of the common logarithm, log₁₀M = k is equivalent to M = 10^k; the function 10^x is the inverse of log₁₀ on (0, ∞). Applying 10 to the power of both sides therefore unwraps the logarithm exactly, with no algebraic manipulation of log laws required. This is the only legitimate way to extract M from a log equation once the log side has been fully simplified to a single log₁₀M term.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the working has produced a numerical value for log₁₀M, but the question demands M on the original scale.
  2. Rule out further log-law manipulation (no products, quotients, or powers remain to combine) — the LHS is already an isolated log₁₀M.
  3. Commit to the inverse-function step: log₁₀M = k ⟺ M = 10^k, a direct definition-of-logarithm application.
  4. Bridge to operation: keep the RHS unrounded as long as possible, then raise 10 to that power on the calculator.
  5. Decide on final rounding (s.f. or d.p.) only after the exponentiation, and attach the correct units.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Confirm the equation is in the form log₁₀M = k with M isolated inside a single log term.
  2. Carry the unrounded value of k from the previous arithmetic (avoid premature rounding).
  3. Apply the inverse: M = 10^k.
  4. Evaluate 10^k on the calculator.
  5. Round to the required precision and state units.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\log_{10}M = 1.93\log_{10}(45) + 0.684$, evaluate the RHS: $\log_{10}(45) \approx 1.6532$, so $\log_{10}M \approx 1.93(1.6532) + 0.684 \approx 3.8806$. Then $M = 10^{3.8806} \approx 7600$ (2 s.f.).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Natural-log model ln M = k
Exponentiate with base e instead: M = e^k.
General base-a model log_a M = k
Use M = a^k (e.g. base 2 in information/decay contexts).
Power-law fit log₁₀y = m log₁₀x + c after substituting an x-value
Same anti-log step recovers y on the original scale.
Exponential decay/growth log₁₀N = at + b
After evaluating the RHS at given t, apply N = 10^(RHS) to obtain the population/quantity.
Equation log₁₀M = expression involving another unknown
Still write M = 10^(expression); leave in exact form if a numerical value is not yet available.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating log₁₀M ≈ 3.88 as the final answer because it is a 'number'.
  • Confusing log₁₀ with ln and exponentiating with e instead of 10 (or vice versa).
  • Trying to apply log laws to 'simplify' when the LHS is already a single log term and only the inverse operation is needed.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to anti-log and reporting log₁₀M ≈ 3.88 instead of M ≈ 7600.
  • Premature rounding of log₁₀(45) or other intermediate logs, causing the final 10^k to be off by a significant amount.
  • Applying 10^k only to one side or only to part of the RHS (e.g. 10 × 3.88 instead of 10^{3.88}).
  • Omitting units or stating the answer to the wrong number of significant figures specified in the question.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 logarithms and exponentials, including their use in modelling (power laws and exponential growth/decay); requires only the inverse relationship log_a x = y ⟺ x = a^y.
