Polar Coordinates

Convert polar tangent-direction condition into dy/dθ = 0 or dx/dθ = 0 via Cartesian parametric form

Family: Parametric Tangent Condition Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question asks for points on a polar curve r = f(θ) where the tangent is parallel to the initial line (horizontal) or perpendicular to the initial line (vertical). Equivalent phrasings include 'tangent parallel to the x-axis', 'tangent perpendicular to the initial line', or 'find where the curve has a horizontal/vertical tangent'. The trigger may also fire when a problem requires verifying that a stated tangent at a known point is horizontal or vertical (often as a setup for an area/triangle decomposition). The cue is geometric (a direction relative to the x-axis), but no Cartesian equation is given — only r(θ).

Motivation — Why this works

Polar form r(θ) does not give tangent direction directly; only Cartesian projections do. Writing x = r cos θ and y = r sin θ turns the curve into a parametric pair in θ, so dy/dx = (dy/dθ)/(dx/dθ). A horizontal tangent (parallel to initial line) needs dy/dθ = 0 with dx/dθ ≠ 0; a vertical tangent (perpendicular to initial line) needs dx/dθ = 0 with dy/dθ ≠ 0. The product rule applied to r(θ)sin θ and r(θ)cos θ then converts the geometric condition into a single trig equation in θ, which is the underlying mechanism.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the direction word: 'parallel to the initial line' means horizontal (dy/dx = 0); 'perpendicular to the initial line' means vertical (dy/dx → ∞).
  2. Rule out using dr/dθ = 0 — that locates stationary values of r, not stationary tangent directions; also rule out the ψ-angle approach (tan ψ = r/(dr/dθ)) since that measures the angle between the tangent and the radius vector, not the initial line.
  3. Commit to the parametric route: parametrise by θ via x = r cos θ, y = r sin θ, because dy/dx = (dy/dθ)/(dx/dθ) cleanly converts direction conditions into derivative-equals-zero equations.
  4. Bridge to operation: pick the right component — y = r sin θ for parallel-to-initial-line, x = r cos θ for perpendicular-to-initial-line — and differentiate using the product rule (r is a function of θ, not a constant).
  5. Tactical decision: substitute the given r(θ) only after writing the symbolic derivative, so the structure of the trig equation is visible; then solve over the appropriate θ-range, and finally check that the OTHER derivative is non-zero at each solution to rule out cusps.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. For tangent parallel to initial line: write y = r(θ) sin θ and apply the product rule: dy/dθ = (dr/dθ) sin θ + r cos θ.
  2. Set dy/dθ = 0, substitute r and dr/dθ, and solve the resulting trig equation for θ in the required range (usually [0, 2π) or as restricted by the curve's domain).
  3. For tangent perpendicular to initial line: write x = r(θ) cos θ and apply the product rule: dx/dθ = (dr/dθ) cos θ − r sin θ (note the minus sign).
  4. Set dx/dθ = 0 and solve similarly for θ.
  5. For each candidate θ, check the other parametric derivative is non-zero (to exclude cusps/simultaneous zeros).
  6. Substitute valid θ into r = f(θ) to obtain polar coordinates (r, θ); convert to Cartesian (r cos θ, r sin θ) if required.

Worked Micro-Example

Curve: $r = 1 + \sin\theta$. Find tangents perpendicular to the initial line. Set $x = (1+\sin\theta)\cos\theta$, so $\frac{dx}{d\theta} = \cos^2\theta - \sin\theta - \sin^2\theta = 1 - 2\sin^2\theta - \sin\theta$. Setting this to 0 and using $\cos 2\theta$ identities (or treating as a quadratic in $\sin\theta$): $2\sin^2\theta + \sin\theta - 1 = 0 \Rightarrow (2\sin\theta - 1)(\sin\theta + 1) = 0$, giving $\sin\theta = \tfrac12$ or $\sin\theta = -1$, hence $\theta = \pi/6, 5\pi/6$ (the value $3\pi/2$ gives $r=0$, a cusp at the pole, which must be checked separately).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Tangent at a general angle α to the initial line
Use dy/dx = (dy/dθ)/(dx/dθ) = tan α and solve; same parametric framework, just non-zero target gradient.
Tangent at the pole (r = 0)
Solve r(θ) = 0 directly; the tangent direction at the pole is the value of θ satisfying r(θ) = 0 (no differentiation of x or y needed).
Verifying a stated tangent at a known point Q (e.g. as setup for an area question)
Substitute the given θ into dy/dθ (or dx/dθ) and confirm it equals zero, then read off the horizontal/vertical line as y = r sin θ or x = r cos θ.

Common Misreadings

  • Setting dr/dθ = 0 in the belief it gives horizontal tangents — it only finds stationary values of r.
  • Confusing 'parallel to initial line' (horizontal, use dy/dθ = 0) with 'perpendicular to initial line' (vertical, use dx/dθ = 0).
  • Confusing 'perpendicular to the initial line' with 'perpendicular to the radius vector' — the latter uses the angle ψ with tan ψ = r/(dr/dθ).
  • Confusing the tangent at the pole condition with the general parametric condition.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the product rule: r is a function of θ, so d/dθ (r sin θ) = (dr/dθ) sin θ + r cos θ, not just r cos θ.
  • Sign error in dx/dθ = (dr/dθ) cos θ − r sin θ (the minus sign is easy to drop).
  • Failing to check that the other derivative is non-zero at the candidate θ — simultaneous zeros indicate a cusp, not a genuine horizontal/vertical tangent.
  • Restricting θ to too narrow a range and missing solutions, or including θ values outside the curve's stated domain.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) polar-curves content. Uses the parametric chain rule dy/dx = (dy/dθ)/(dx/dθ) (in the formula booklet under parametric differentiation) together with x = r cos θ, y = r sin θ. Avoid invoking the ψ-angle formula tan ψ = r/(dr/dθ) for direction relative to the initial line — that formula measures angle to the radius vector and is a different (and easily confused) tool.
