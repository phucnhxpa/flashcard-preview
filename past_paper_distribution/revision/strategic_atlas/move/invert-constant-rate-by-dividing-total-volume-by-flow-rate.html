Trigger — When to use

Fires when a related-rates or filling/emptying problem states a CONSTANT rate (e.g. 'water flows in at a constant rate of k cm³/s') and asks for the total time to reach a given state. A volume (or quantity) formula V is given, often as a function of a depth h, with an explicit maximum value of h from a domain constraint or geometric capacity. Key linguistic cues: 'constant rate', 'fill the container', 'how long does it take'. Distinguish from variable-rate problems where dV/dt depends on h or t and integration is required.

Motivation — Why this works

If dV/dt = k is constant, then V = kt + C, so the time to accumulate a total volume V_total starting from empty is simply t = V_total / k. No calculus is needed — the constancy of the rate collapses the ODE into a linear relation. Recognising this shortcut saves time and avoids spurious integration, and it cleanly separates 'find the capacity' (a substitution) from 'divide by rate' (arithmetic).

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the phrase 'constant rate' attached to dV/dt — this signals the rate is independent of h and t.
  2. Rule out separation of variables or integration of dV/dh · dh/dt: those are needed only when the rate varies.
  3. Commit to the linear identity V = (dV/dt) · t, because constant rate makes accumulation purely proportional to time.
  4. Bridge to operations: identify h_max from the stated domain or geometry, then evaluate V at h_max to get the total volume to be supplied.
  5. Inside the move, decide whether the bowl/container starts empty (so V_total = V(h_max)) or partially filled (so V_total = V(h_max) − V(h_0)); then divide by the constant rate.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Read off the constant rate k = dV/dt from the question.
  2. Identify the final depth/state h_max from the domain constraint or capacity statement.
  3. Substitute h_max into the given V(h) to compute V_total.
  4. If the container is not initially empty, subtract the initial volume V(h_0).
  5. Compute t = V_total / k and attach correct units (seconds, minutes, etc.).

Worked Micro-Example

Water flows into a bowl at a constant rate of $160 \text{ cm}^3/\text{s}$. The volume at depth $h$ cm is $V = \tfrac{1}{3}h^2(h+4)$ for $0 \le h \le 20$. At $h=20$: $V = \tfrac{1}{3}(400)(24) = 3200 \text{ cm}^3$. Time to fill: $t = \dfrac{3200}{160} = 20 \text{ s}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Container partially filled at start (e.g. fill from h=5 to h=20)
Use V_total = V(h_max) − V(h_0), then divide by k.
Constant rate of mass or charge accumulation rather than volume
Same identity: t = (total quantity)/(constant rate); units differ (g/s, C/s).
Outflow at constant rate (draining)
t = (initial volume − final volume)/k, with k taken as the magnitude of the outflow rate.
Variable rate dV/dt = f(h) instead of constant
Switch families: use chain rule dV/dt = (dV/dh)(dh/dt) and separate variables, integrating to find t.
Linear motion with constant velocity giving distance
Same structure: time = distance/speed; identical inversion of a constant rate.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating a stated constant rate as variable and launching into integration of dV/dh · dh/dt unnecessarily.
  • Misreading the domain and using the wrong h_max (e.g. taking h = 4 from the formula instead of 20 from the constraint).
  • Confusing dV/dt with dh/dt and dividing by a height-rate instead of a volume-rate.
  • Assuming the bowl starts non-empty when the question states it begins empty (or vice versa).

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slip in evaluating V(h_max), e.g. computing $\tfrac{1}{3}(400)(24)$ as 9600 instead of 3200.
  • Forgetting to convert units if the rate and volume are given in inconsistent units (cm³ vs m³).
  • Dropping the factor 1/3 (or other constant) from the volume formula before substitution.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics: this is the trivial-rate case of related rates of change (P3/P4), requiring only substitution and arithmetic, no integration.
