Trigonometry

Verify solutions against original denominators after cross-multiplying

Family: Validity Checking Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a trig equation containing fractions (e.g. tan θ, sec θ, cosec θ, or expressions like 1/(2sin θ − 1)) has been cleared by cross-multiplication. Typical cues: tan/cot/sec/cosec rewritten in terms of sin/cos, or two fractions set equal and then cross-multiplied. Also fires when a substitution θ = 2x or y = θ/2 leaves an angle whose forbidden values must be re-expressed in the original variable. Any time multiplying by a variable expression could introduce roots that make a denominator zero, this check is mandatory.

Motivation — Why this works

Cross-multiplication is only an equivalence when the multipliers are non-zero; multiplying by zero turns any equation into 0 = 0 and admits spurious roots. So every candidate solution must be tested against the original denominators (here cos θ and 2sin θ − 1). If a candidate makes either denominator zero, the original equation is undefined there and the root is extraneous. Confirming non-vanishing denominators certifies the solutions and secures the final accuracy mark.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the cleared equation came from cross-multiplying two fractions, so denominators cos θ and 2sin θ − 1 carry hidden domain restrictions.
  2. Rule out the possibility that any candidate root coincides with cos θ = 0 or sin θ = 1/2 — these are the forbidden values.
  3. Commit to a checking pass because cross-multiplication can manufacture roots, and full marks require explicit validation.
  4. Translate the forbidden conditions back into the working variable (e.g. sin y = 0 or sin y = 2/3) and test each.
  5. For each candidate, compute cos θ and 2sin θ − 1 (or just check the corresponding sin/cos value) and confirm both are non-zero before accepting.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. List every denominator from the original (pre-cross-multiplied) equation.
  2. Convert each 'denominator = 0' condition into a constraint on the solving variable.
  3. For each candidate solution, evaluate the constraints and check none are met.
  4. Reject any candidate that makes a denominator vanish; retain the rest as the final solution set.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\tan\theta = \dfrac{1}{2\sin\theta - 1}$ on $[0,2\pi)$. Cross-multiplying gives $\sin\theta(2\sin\theta - 1) = \cos\theta$. After solving, suppose candidates include $\theta = \pi/6$. Check: $\sin(\pi/6) = 1/2 \Rightarrow 2\sin\theta - 1 = 0$, so the original RHS is undefined — reject $\pi/6$. Remaining candidates with $\cos\theta \neq 0$ and $\sin\theta \neq 1/2$ are accepted.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Algebraic rational equation cleared by cross-multiplication
Substitute each root into the original denominators; reject any that produce zero.
Logarithmic equation $\ln f(x) = \ln g(x)$
Check $f(x) > 0$ and $g(x) > 0$ at every candidate root, not denominator zeros.
Equation involving $\sec\theta$ or $\csc\theta$ rewritten via $1/\cos\theta$ or $1/\sin\theta$
Forbidden values become $\cos\theta = 0$ or $\sin\theta = 0$ respectively; verify each retained root.
Squaring both sides of a trig equation
Substitute candidates back into the unsquared equation to discard sign-flipped extraneous roots.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating cross-multiplication as an unconditional equivalence and skipping the check.
  • Checking only the LHS denominator and forgetting the RHS one (or vice versa).
  • Forgetting to translate the forbidden values back through the substitution (e.g. θ = 2x) before testing.

Pitfalls

  • Mis-identifying the forbidden sin/cos value (e.g. writing $\sin\theta = -1/2$ instead of $1/2$ from $2\sin\theta - 1 = 0$).
  • Applying the check only at the end and missing a candidate produced earlier in the working.
  • Including roots where $\tan\theta$ is undefined ($\cos\theta = 0$) because the rewritten equation no longer shows that singularity.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P3/P4 trig-equation hygiene: any equation involving tan, sec, cosec or fractional trig requires explicit domain checks after cross-multiplication, and examiners reserve a final accuracy mark for it.
