Trigger — When to use

Fires when the problem describes a physical object (e.g. dumbbell, double-ended capsule, symmetric vase, joined cones) formed by two identical solids of revolution placed back-to-back or end-to-end. The given curve and limits typically describe only one half, often from 0 to some endpoint where the radius is maximum or zero. Look for phrases like 'two identical solids joined', 'symmetric about the y-axis', or a diagram showing mirrored halves. Also fires in mass/density follow-ups where mishandling the factor of 2 propagates.

Motivation — Why this works

If both halves are generated by rotating the same curve about the same axis, congruence guarantees their volumes are equal, so V_total = 2V_one without recomputing. This avoids re-parametrising the mirrored half (which would require shifting the curve and adjusting limits). The doubling step is the bridge from a pure calculus result (one definite integral) to the modelling answer (total volume, then density = mass/volume).

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the object is described as two identical solids joined — the integral set up only captures one half.
  2. Rule out re-integrating over a shifted interval for the mirrored half: by symmetry it gives the same value.
  3. Commit to V_total = 2 × V_one as the modelling step before any density or mass calculation.
  4. Bridge: finish the single integral cleanly in exact form, then multiply by 2.
  5. Tactically keep exact form (e.g. 200π(2 − 122e^{-10})) until the final numeric step, and check units (cm³ vs m³, g vs kg) before dividing mass by volume.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Set up and evaluate the single solid volume V₁ = π∫_a^b y² dx (or π∫ x² dy as appropriate).
  2. Express V₁ in exact form, e.g. V₁ = 100π(2 − 122e^{-10}).
  3. Double: V_total = 2V₁ = 200π(2 − 122e^{-10}).
  4. Convert to a decimal carefully, retaining enough significant figures (V_total ≈ 1253.1 cm³).
  5. Convert mass units to match volume units (e.g. 5 kg → 5000 g for g cm⁻³).
  6. Compute density = mass / V_total ≈ 5000 / 1253.1 ≈ 3.99 g cm⁻³.

Worked Micro-Example

A weight is two identical solids formed by rotating $y = \sqrt{x}e^{-x/2}$, $0 \le x \le 10$, about the x-axis, joined at $x=10$. Then $V_1 = \pi\int_0^{10} xe^{-x}\,dx = \pi[1 - 11e^{-10}]$. Total volume $V = 2V_1 = 2\pi(1 - 11e^{-10}) \approx 6.28\text{ cm}^3$. If mass is $25$ g, density $= 25/6.28 \approx 3.98$ g cm⁻³.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Symmetric vase or barrel formed by rotating one half about the axis of symmetry
Double the half-volume; check whether the 'half' is the top or one side.
Object made of two different solids joined (e.g. cone + hemisphere)
Do NOT double — sum the two distinct integrals V_total = V_A + V_B.
Curve is already given over the full symmetric interval [−a, a]
Do NOT double — the integral already covers the whole solid; doubling would double-count.
Rotation about the y-axis with the curve giving only one lobe
Use π∫ x² dy for one half, then multiply by 2.

Common Misreadings

  • Forgetting to double, computing density from one half only — giving exactly twice the correct density.
  • Doubling when the integral already spans the whole symmetric region, halving the correct density.
  • Forgetting the unit conversion from kg to g (using 5 instead of 5000) when working in cm³.

Pitfalls

  • Mixing units: curve in cm gives volume in cm³, but mass given in kg must be converted to g for g cm⁻³.
  • Rounding e^{-10} too aggressively and losing significant figures in the final density.
  • Dropping the factor of π when transcribing V₁ into the doubling step.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure/Mechanics-style modelling: volume of revolution via π∫y²dx combined with density = mass/volume. No surface-area-of-revolution or non-circular cross-sections required.
