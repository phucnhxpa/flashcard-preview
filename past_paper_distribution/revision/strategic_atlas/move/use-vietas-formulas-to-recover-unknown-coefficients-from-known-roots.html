Complex Numbers

Use Vieta's formulas to recover unknown coefficients from known roots

Family: Root Coefficient Relations Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A polynomial (typically cubic or quartic) has unknown real coefficients and we know — or can deduce via the conjugate root theorem — all of its roots. We need to determine those unknown coefficients efficiently. Cue phrases: 'given that 1−2i is a root of z³+az²+bz+c=0, find a and b', or 'the equation has roots α, β, γ, find...'.

Motivation — Why this works

Vieta's formulas express each coefficient as a symmetric function of the roots, so once roots are known the coefficients fall out by direct evaluation. This bypasses polynomial long division (forming the real quadratic factor (z−α)(z−ᾱ) then dividing) and bypasses substituting complex roots into the polynomial (which forces expansion of high powers of a+bi). For a cubic with three unknowns we get exactly three relations — sum, pairwise sum, product — each isolating one unknown.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice all three roots are known or one more can be deduced, and the unknowns are coefficients — that's a roots↔coefficients problem.
  2. I rule out polynomial division: forming (z−(1−2i))(z−(1+2i))=z²−2z+5 and dividing z³+az²+bz+10 by it leaves a, b inside the long-division steps — messy.
  3. I rule out direct substitution: plugging 1±2i into the cubic forces me to expand (1±2i)² and (1±2i)³, which is heavy complex arithmetic for two simultaneous equations.
  4. I commit to Vieta's: three symmetric relations match my three unknowns (a, b, third root), each a clean scalar computation.
  5. Tactically I order them: use the product first to pin down the missing real root, then sum to get a, then pairwise sum to get b — each step uses the previously found value.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the polynomial in standard form and identify the Vieta relations: for z³+az²+bz+c=0 with roots α,β,γ — α+β+γ=−a, αβ+βγ+γα=b, αβγ=−c.
  2. Use the product relation to determine any missing root from the known constant term.
  3. Use the sum relation to find the coefficient of z².
  4. Use the pairwise-product relation to find the coefficient of z.
  5. Verify by substituting one root back into the now fully-determined polynomial.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $z^3+az^2+bz+10=0$ has root $1-2i$ (so also $1+2i$), let third root be $z_3$. Product: $(1-2i)(1+2i)z_3 = 5z_3 = -10 \Rightarrow z_3=-2$. Sum: $(1-2i)+(1+2i)+(-2)=0=-a \Rightarrow a=0$. Pairwise: $5 + (1-2i)(-2)+(1+2i)(-2) = 5-4 = 1 = b$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quartic with two conjugate pairs and unknown coefficients
Use all four Vieta relations (sum, sum of pairs, sum of triples, product); pair conjugates to keep arithmetic real.
Cubic where only one complex root is given
First invoke the conjugate root theorem to get a second root, then deduce the third from the product relation before applying sum/pairwise.
Non-monic polynomial $kz^3+az^2+bz+c=0$
Divide through by $k$ first, or use the adjusted Vieta forms: sum $=-a/k$, pairwise $=b/k$, product $=-c/k$.
Quadratic with one known complex root
Vieta's collapses to sum and product only — two relations for two coefficients.

Common Misreadings

  • Defaulting to substitution because it feels 'direct', missing that Vieta's is dramatically shorter when roots are known.
  • Attempting polynomial long division with literal unknowns a, b still in the dividend — leads to algebra that is harder than the original problem.
  • Forgetting to use the conjugate root theorem to identify the second root before counting unknowns.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors: for $z^3+az^2+bz+c=0$ the sum of roots is $-a$ and the product is $-c$; the alternating signs are easy to misremember.
  • Applying monic Vieta formulas to a non-monic polynomial without dividing through by the leading coefficient.
  • Arithmetic slips when summing pairwise products of complex numbers — imaginary parts must cancel for real coefficients; failure to cancel signals an error.

A-level Scope Notes

Vieta's formulas for quadratics, cubics, and quartics are core Further Pure content (Edexcel IAL FP1/FP2), as is the conjugate root theorem for polynomials with real coefficients.
