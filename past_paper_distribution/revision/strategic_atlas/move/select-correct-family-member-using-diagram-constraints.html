Trigger — When to use

Fires when a trig graph question labels specific points (P, Q, R, etc.) and the diagram imposes implicit constraints — sign of x, position relative to ticks, 'first' maximum/minimum, or zero past a given x-value. Equivalent phrasings include 'the smallest positive x for which …', 'the first turning point after x = a', or simply a labelled point whose location is fixed by the sketch. The cue is that the trig feature (zero, max, min) recurs periodically but the diagram singles out exactly one instance.

Motivation — Why this works

Sine and cosine have infinitely many zeros and critical points spaced by 180° or 360°, so any defining property (e.g. 'cos x = 0') yields a whole family of solutions. The diagram acts as a filter that converts the family into a single representative. Recognising this turns a vague 'read off the graph' instruction into a precise two-step procedure: list the family, then apply the visible constraint. This is the bridge between general graph knowledge and specific coordinate answers.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the labelled point is a recognisable feature (zero / max / min) of a standard trig curve, so it belongs to a known infinite family.
  2. Rule out family members that violate the visible constraint — wrong sign of x, wrong side of a tick, or beyond the sketched window.
  3. Commit to the unique remaining candidate by cross-checking with neighbouring labelled features (e.g. P sits left of Q, which sits left of R).
  4. Bridge to operation: write the family explicitly as {…, a − T, a, a + T, …}, then substitute the constraint to pick one term.
  5. Tactically, watch boundary cases: 'positive' excludes 0, 'past 360°' excludes 360° itself, 'first' means smallest in absolute terms within the constraint.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the feature type at each labelled point (zero, maximum, minimum) from the curve's shape.
  2. Write the full family of x-coordinates for that feature, e.g. minima of cos x at x = 180° + 360°n.
  3. Read the diagram constraint precisely (sign, inequality, ordering relative to other labels).
  4. Filter the family by the constraint to obtain a unique x-value.
  5. Pair with the known y-value of that feature (amplitude, 0, or −amplitude, after any vertical scaling) to form the coordinate.

Worked Micro-Example

For $y = 7\cos x$ on a sketch showing P (a minimum with $x<0$), Q (a zero with $x>360°$), R (the first maximum with $x>0$): \\ Minima family: $x = 180° + 360°n$; constraint $x<0$ gives $x = -180°$, so $P = (-180°, -7)$. \\ Zeros family: $x = 90° + 180°n$; smallest with $x>360°$ is $x = 450°$, so $Q = (450°, 0)$. \\ Maxima family: $x = 360°n$; smallest with $x>0$ is $x = 360°$, so $R = (360°, 7)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

First positive root of $\sin x = 0$
List zeros $\{0°, 180°, 360°, \dots\}$ and apply 'positive' to exclude 0, giving $x = 180°$.
Transformed curve $y = a\cos(bx) + c$ with labelled max/min
Period becomes $360°/b$ and y-values shift by $c$; family spacing changes but filtering logic is identical.
Radian-domain sketch with point labelled past $2\pi$
Replace $360°$ with $2\pi$ in the family; constraint 'past $2\pi$' selects the next term.
Tangent graph with labelled asymptote or zero
Family for $\tan x = 0$ is $x = 180°n$; asymptotes at $x = 90° + 180°n$. Same filter-by-constraint approach.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $x = 0$ as 'positive' when the question says 'smallest positive x-coordinate'.
  • Placing Q at $270°$ instead of $450°$ by forgetting the sketch domain extends past $360°$.
  • Confusing 'first maximum after P' (next family member) with 'absolute maximum' (just the y-value).

Pitfalls

  • Picking the wrong family member when the sketch domain is wider than the standard $0°$–$360°$ window.
  • Forgetting that for $y = a\cos(bx)$ the family spacing is $360°/b$, not $360°$.
  • Reading the y-coordinate as $\pm 1$ instead of $\pm a$ after an amplitude scaling.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P2/P3 graph-sketching content: periodic features of $\sin$, $\cos$, $\tan$ and their simple transformations, working in degrees or radians.
