Factorise quadratic denominator to expose shared linear factor with LCD

Family: Factorise For Lcd Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Two algebraic fractions must be combined (added or subtracted) where one denominator is quadratic and the other is linear. A 'show that' target with a simpler denominator signals that cancellation will occur, so the quadratic almost certainly factors to include the linear denominator as one of its factors. Cues include matching constant terms, matching coefficients, or a target form noticeably simpler than a brute-force LCD would produce.

Motivation — Why this works

Any quadratic that splits into two linear factors over the rationals can be rewritten as a product (x+a)(x+b). If one of those factors matches the other fraction's denominator, the LCD is just the quadratic itself, so only one fraction needs adjusting. After combining, the shared factor cancels with a matching factor in the numerator, collapsing the result to the target form. This avoids expanding to a quartic-looking expression and then re-factorising.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that one denominator is quadratic and the other linear, and that the target denominator is simpler than the product of the two given denominators.
  2. Rule out cross-multiplying or using the brute-force LCD (quadratic × linear), since the simpler target implies a shared factor must cancel.
  3. Commit to factorising the quadratic first: if it splits and one factor matches the linear denominator, the LCD is just the quadratic.
  4. Bridge to operation: factorise the quadratic, identify the shared factor, then scale only the fraction whose denominator is missing the other factor.
  5. Tactically, factor the numerator of the resulting single fraction to cancel the shared factor cleanly, watching the minus sign across the full numerator on subtraction.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Factorise the quadratic denominator into two linear factors (x+a)(x+b).
  2. Identify which factor matches the other fraction's linear denominator.
  3. Take the LCD as the factorised quadratic; multiply the linear-denominator fraction's numerator and denominator by the missing factor.
  4. Combine numerators over the common denominator, distributing any minus sign across every term.
  5. Simplify the combined numerator and factor it to expose the shared linear factor.
  6. Cancel the shared factor (noting any domain restriction) to obtain the target form.

Worked Micro-Example

Simplify $\dfrac{5x^2+20x}{x^2+7x+12}-\dfrac{5x}{x+4}$. Factorise: $x^2+7x+12=(x+3)(x+4)$. LCD $=(x+3)(x+4)$, so $\dfrac{5x^2+20x}{(x+3)(x+4)}-\dfrac{5x(x+3)}{(x+3)(x+4)}=\dfrac{5x^2+20x-5x^2-15x}{(x+3)(x+4)}=\dfrac{5x}{(x+3)(x+4)}$. Factoring the first numerator as $5x(x+4)$ before subtraction gives the same cancellation, leaving $\dfrac{5x}{x+3}$ after the $(x+4)$ cancels.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Both denominators quadratic, sharing one linear factor
Factorise both, take LCD as product of the three distinct linear factors, and scale each fraction by the factor it lacks.
Cubic denominator vs quadratic or linear denominator
Factor the cubic fully (e.g. via factor theorem) to expose all shared factors before choosing the LCD.
Difference of two squares denominator, e.g. x^2−9 with denominator x−3
Recognise $x^2-9=(x-3)(x+3)$ so the LCD is the quadratic; multiply the linear fraction by $(x+3)/(x+3)$.
Partial fractions setup in C4/IAL P4
Same factorisation step is the gateway: factor the quadratic denominator first to determine the partial fraction form.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the quadratic and linear denominators as coprime and using their product as LCD, missing the cancellation entirely.
  • Cross-multiplying both fractions blindly, producing a quartic numerator that is then hard to factor back to the target.
  • Assuming the quadratic does not factor and abandoning the algebraic-fraction route.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the minus sign across the second numerator: $-5x(x+3)$ must expand to $-5x^2-15x$, not $-5x^2+15x$.
  • Cancelling a factor before checking that it actually divides the entire numerator.
  • Forgetting to state the domain restriction (e.g. $x\ne -4$) when cancelling, where the question expects it.
  • Missing an early factorisation of the numerator (e.g. $5x^2+20x=5x(x+4)$) that would simplify the work substantially.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (P2/P3 algebraic fractions, P4 partial fractions); uses only quadratic factorisation and standard fraction manipulation.
