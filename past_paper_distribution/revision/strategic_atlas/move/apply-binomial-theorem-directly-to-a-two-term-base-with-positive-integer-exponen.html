Binomial Expansion

Apply binomial theorem directly to a two-term base with positive integer exponent

Family: Direct Binomial Expansion Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The expression has the form $(a+b)^n$ where $n$ is a positive integer and both $a$ and $b$ are nonzero constants or simple terms (e.g. $(2+\tfrac{1}{8}x)^{13}$). The expansion terminates after $n+1$ terms, so no convergence condition $|x|<1$ is needed. Cues include phrases like 'find the first four terms in ascending powers of $x$' on a base that is not already in $(1+u)^n$ form.

Motivation — Why this works

For positive integer $n$, the finite binomial theorem $(a+b)^n=\sum_{r=0}^{n}\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r$ applies to any two-term base directly. Factoring out $a^n$ to rewrite as $a^n(1+\tfrac{b}{a})^n$ is a valid alternative but introduces an extra multiplicative constant that must be threaded through every coefficient, raising the risk of arithmetic slips. Keeping $a$ and $b$ inside the expansion lets each term be evaluated and simplified independently, often with clean cancellation between powers of related bases (e.g. $2^{13-r}$ against $8^r=2^{3r}$).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the exponent $n=13$ is a positive integer, so the expansion is finite and the standard binomial theorem applies with no convergence worry.
  2. Rule out the 'factor out $a^n$' route: it would force $2^{13}$ to be multiplied back into every term, doubling the arithmetic.
  3. Commit to direct expansion with $a=2$, $b=\tfrac{1}{8}x$, because powers of 2 and powers of 8 will cancel cleanly inside each coefficient.
  4. Bridge to action: write the general term $\binom{13}{r}(2)^{13-r}(\tfrac{x}{8})^r$ and plan to evaluate it for $r=0,1,2,3$.
  5. Tactically, simplify $2^{13-r}/8^r = 2^{13-4r}$ first, then multiply by $\binom{13}{r}$ to get a single integer coefficient per term.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify $a$, $b$, and $n$ in $(a+b)^n$; here $a=2$, $b=\tfrac{1}{8}x$, $n=13$.
  2. Write the general term $T_{r+1}=\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r=\binom{13}{r}2^{13-r}\left(\tfrac{x}{8}\right)^r$.
  3. Evaluate the term for each required $r$ (e.g. $r=0,1,2,3$ for the first four terms in ascending powers of $x$).
  4. Simplify each coefficient by combining powers of the same prime: $\tfrac{2^{13-r}}{8^r}=2^{13-4r}$.
  5. Multiply by the binomial coefficient $\binom{13}{r}$ and attach the appropriate power of $x$.
  6. Assemble the terms in ascending order of $x$.

Worked Micro-Example

Expand $(2+\tfrac{1}{8}x)^{13}$ up to $x^3$. General term: $\binom{13}{r}2^{13-r}(\tfrac{x}{8})^r=\binom{13}{r}2^{13-4r}x^r$. For $r=0$: $2^{13}=8192$. For $r=1$: $13\cdot 2^{9}x=13\cdot 512\,x=6656x$. For $r=2$: $78\cdot 2^{5}x^2=78\cdot 32\,x^2=2496x^2$. For $r=3$: $286\cdot 2^{1}x^3=572x^3$. Hence $(2+\tfrac{1}{8}x)^{13}\approx 8192+6656x+2496x^2+572x^3+\cdots$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$(3-2x)^{10}$ or similar with negative second term
Take $b=-2x$; the sign $(-1)^r$ alternates the signs of the coefficients, and powers $(-2)^r$ must be carried carefully.
$(a+bx^2)^n$ where the variable carries a power
Each $r$ contributes $x^{2r}$, so to get terms up to $x^k$ only $r\leq k/2$ are needed.
Fractional or negative exponent, e.g. $(2+x)^{-3}$ or $(4+x)^{1/2}$
Direct two-term expansion no longer applies; factor out to the form $a^n(1+\tfrac{x}{a})^n$ and use the generalised binomial series with convergence $|\tfrac{x}{a}|<1$.
Coefficient extraction only (e.g. find the coefficient of $x^5$)
Use the general term once with the specific $r$ that produces $x^5$, rather than expanding term by term.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $b$ as $\tfrac{1}{8}$ rather than $\tfrac{1}{8}x$, producing a numerical sum instead of a polynomial in $x$.
  • Reading 'first four terms' as $r=1,2,3,4$ instead of $r=0,1,2,3$, dropping the constant term.
  • Assuming a convergence condition is needed even though $n$ is a positive integer.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to raise the whole of $\tfrac{1}{8}x$ to the power $r$, e.g. writing $\tfrac{1}{8}x^2$ instead of $\tfrac{1}{64}x^2$ for $r=2$.
  • Arithmetic slips computing $2^{13-r}$; safer to anchor on $2^{13}=8192$ and divide by $2^{4}=16$ at each successive $r$ when using $2^{13-4r}$.
  • Computing $\binom{13}{r}$ incorrectly, especially for $r=3$ where $\binom{13}{3}=286$.
  • Sign errors when $b$ is negative — forgetting that $(-b)^r$ alternates signs.

A-level Scope Notes

This is the standard finite binomial expansion in Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P2/P3), used whenever $n$ is a positive integer; the generalised series for fractional/negative $n$ is treated as a separate move requiring the $(1+u)^n$ form with $|u|<1$.
