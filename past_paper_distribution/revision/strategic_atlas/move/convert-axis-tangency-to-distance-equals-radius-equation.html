Coordinate Geometry

Convert axis tangency to distance-equals-radius equation

Family: Tangency Distance Condition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The problem states a coordinate axis (typically the x-axis or y-axis) is tangent to a circle whose centre and radius are known or expressible in terms of an unknown. Look for phrases like 'the x-axis is a tangent to C' or 'C touches the y-axis'. The centre (a, b) and radius r are either given or read off after completing the square. The unknown to solve for usually sits inside r² (e.g. r² = 25 − k).

Motivation — Why this works

A line is tangent to a circle iff the perpendicular distance from the centre to the line equals the radius. For the x-axis (y = 0) this distance is just |b|, and for the y-axis (x = 0) it is |a|, so no formula is needed — the centre's coordinate is the distance. This converts a geometric condition directly into a one-variable algebraic equation, bypassing any discriminant work on a substituted quadratic.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that one of the axes is named as a tangent — that is a tangency-distance-condition cue, not an intersection cue.
  2. Rule out substituting y = 0 and forcing discriminant = 0; it works but is slower and more error-prone than the distance approach.
  3. Commit to 'distance from centre to line = radius', because for an axis the distance is just the absolute value of one coordinate of the centre.
  4. Read off the centre (a, b) and radius r (completing the square if the circle is given expanded), then write |b| = r for the x-axis or |a| = r for the y-axis.
  5. Square both sides to remove the absolute value/root, and check that the resulting r² is positive so the circle is genuine.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the centre (a, b) and radius r from the circle equation (complete the square if needed).
  2. Identify the tangent axis: x-axis ⇒ distance = |b|; y-axis ⇒ distance = |a|.
  3. Set this distance equal to r: e.g. |b| = r.
  4. Square both sides to obtain b² = r² and substitute the expression for r² in terms of the unknown.
  5. Solve for the unknown and verify r² > 0.

Worked Micro-Example

Circle $x^2 + y^2 - 6x + 8y + k = 0$ has the x-axis as a tangent. Completing the square: $(x-3)^2 + (y+4)^2 = 25 - k$, so centre $(3,-4)$, radius $\sqrt{25-k}$. Tangency to $y=0$ gives $|-4| = \sqrt{25-k}$, so $16 = 25 - k$, hence $k = 9$ (and $r^2 = 16 > 0$, valid).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Circle tangent to the y-axis
Use |a| = r instead of |b| = r, where a is the x-coordinate of the centre.
Circle tangent to a general line ax + by + c = 0
Use the full point-to-line distance |a·x₀ + b·y₀ + c|/√(a² + b²) = r.
Circle tangent to a horizontal line y = k₀
Distance is |b − k₀|; set equal to r.
Discriminant alternative
Substitute y = 0 into the circle equation and impose Δ = 0 on the resulting quadratic in x; equivalent answer but more algebra.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing tangency with general intersection — tangency means exactly one point of contact (distance = radius), not two.
  • Using the x-coordinate of the centre when computing distance to the x-axis (it should be the y-coordinate).
  • Treating 'touches the axis' as if the centre lies on the axis instead of the circle just meeting it.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the absolute value: the y-coordinate may be negative but the distance is positive.
  • Forgetting to complete the square first, so reading off the wrong centre or radius.
  • Not verifying that the resulting r² is positive — a negative r² means no real circle exists.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths circle geometry: completing the square for circle equations and the tangent ⇔ distance-from-centre-equals-radius criterion.
