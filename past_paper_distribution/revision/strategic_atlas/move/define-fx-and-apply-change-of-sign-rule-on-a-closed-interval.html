Strategic Atlas / Algebra & Functions / Root Existence Proof
Algebra & Functions

Define f(x) and apply change-of-sign rule on a closed interval

Family: Root Existence Proof Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question says 'show that the equation has a root in [a, b]' or 'show that a root α lies between a and b', and the equation cannot be solved in closed form (typically a mix of polynomial, trig, exponential, or logarithmic terms). The verb 'show' combined with an explicit interval is the canonical cue. Also fires when the question gives two boundary values and asks to verify a solution exists between them. Distinguish from 'find the root to a given accuracy', which uses iteration after this step.

Motivation — Why this works

If f is continuous on [a, b] and f(a), f(b) have opposite signs, the Intermediate Value Theorem guarantees at least one c ∈ (a, b) with f(c) = 0. Packaging the equation as f(x) = 0 reduces the existence proof to two substitutions and a sign comparison — no algebraic solving needed. Continuity is automatic for sums, products, and compositions of polynomials, sin, cos, exp, and ln on their domains, so it is usually a one-line remark. The argument is constructive only in the sense that it locates a root in a known interval.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the verb 'show' paired with a specific interval [a, b] — this is the standard sign-change cue, not a solve-the-equation instruction.
  2. Rule out algebraic solving: the equation mixes incompatible families (e.g. polynomial + trig), so no closed-form root is available.
  3. Commit to defining f(x) equal to the LHS minus RHS, so that roots of the equation become zeros of f.
  4. Bridge: check that f is continuous on [a, b] (sums/compositions of standard functions), and confirm whether radians or degrees are required.
  5. Tactically, evaluate f(a) and f(b) to enough decimal places that the sign is unambiguous, then write a one-line conclusion citing continuity and the sign change.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rearrange the equation to the form (LHS) = 0 and define f(x) = LHS.
  2. State (briefly) that f is continuous on [a, b].
  3. Compute f(a) to 2–3 s.f. and note its sign.
  4. Compute f(b) to 2–3 s.f. and note its sign.
  5. Conclude: 'Since f is continuous and f(a), f(b) have opposite signs, by the change-of-sign rule there exists a root α ∈ (a, b).'

Worked Micro-Example

Show $4x - 2\sin x - 1 = 0$ has a root in $[0.2, 0.6]$. Let $f(x) = 4x - 2\sin x - 1$, continuous on $\mathbb{R}$. Then $f(0.2) = 0.8 - 2\sin(0.2) - 1 \approx -0.597 < 0$ and $f(0.6) = 2.4 - 2\sin(0.6) - 1 \approx 0.271 > 0$ (radians). By the change-of-sign rule, a root $\alpha$ exists in $(0.2, 0.6)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation involves $e^x$ or $\ln x$, e.g. show $e^x - 3x = 0$ has a root in $[1.5, 2]$.
Define $f(x) = e^x - 3x$; continuity holds on $\mathbb{R}$ (or on $x>0$ for $\ln$); evaluate at endpoints as before.
Pure polynomial root location, e.g. show $x^3 - 5x + 1 = 0$ has a root in $[0, 1]$.
Continuity is automatic; no radian/degree concern; otherwise identical.
Question pre-defines f(x) and asks to show a root exists between two values.
Skip the definition step; go straight to evaluating f(a), f(b) and stating the conclusion.
Showing a root lies in a smaller interval after iteration (e.g. $[1.85, 1.86]$).
Same mechanic, but evaluate to more decimal places so the sign is unambiguous.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to solve the equation algebraically rather than applying the sign-change method.
  • Treating 'show a root exists' as 'find the root', leading to unnecessary iteration.
  • Forgetting to explicitly state continuity, losing the concluding mark.
  • Confusing the change-of-sign rule with a uniqueness claim — it only guarantees existence.

Pitfalls

  • Using degrees instead of radians when evaluating $\sin x$ or $\cos x$, giving wrong signs.
  • Failing to state that $f$ is continuous — the sign change alone is not a complete argument.
  • Not showing the actual numerical values of $f(a)$ and $f(b)$; examiners require this evidence.
  • Rounding so aggressively that the sign of $f(a)$ or $f(b)$ becomes ambiguous (e.g. writing $0.0$).
  • Applying the rule on an interval where $f$ has a discontinuity (e.g. $\tan x$ across $\pi/2$, or $1/x$ across $0$).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL C3/P3 numerical methods content; the change-of-sign rule (informal IVT) is examinable, and continuity may be cited without formal proof for sums/compositions of standard functions.
