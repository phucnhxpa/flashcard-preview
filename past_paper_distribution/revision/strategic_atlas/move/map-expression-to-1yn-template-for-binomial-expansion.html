Binomial Expansion

Map expression to (1+y)^n template for binomial expansion

Family: Template Matching Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The expression has the shape $(1 + \text{something})^n$ where the leading term inside the bracket is exactly 1. Cues include $(1\pm kx)^n$, $(1-\tfrac{x}{6})^9$, or any bracket whose first term is 1 raised to an integer (or in P4, fractional/negative) power. When you see this form, the standard binomial series template applies directly without any pre-factoring.

Motivation — Why this works

The binomial theorem in the form $(1+y)^n = \sum_{r=0}^{n}\binom{n}{r}y^r$ is calibrated so that the leading 1 produces $1^{n-r}=1$ in every term, leaving only powers of $y$. Matching directly to this template means each term is generated mechanically with no extra factors. Using the more general $(a+b)^n$ form adds redundant $a^{n-r}$ factors, while clearing constants by factoring out (e.g. $\tfrac{1}{6^9}(6-x)^9$) introduces large numerators that must later be cancelled. The $(1+y)^n$ form is the cleanest computational route.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the bracket already starts with 1: the form $(1+\text{stuff})^n$ matches the standard template exactly.
  2. Rule out the $(a+b)^n$ general form (adds trivial $1^{n-r}$ factors) and rule out rewriting as $\tfrac{1}{c^n}(c+\dots)^n$ (creates huge coefficients).
  3. Commit to the $(1+y)^n$ template because each term will fall out directly with no extra bookkeeping.
  4. Identify $y$ explicitly — including any sign or coefficient — so $y = -\tfrac{x}{6}$ (sign attached) rather than $y=\tfrac{x}{6}$ with manual sign-flipping.
  5. Decide how many terms are needed (e.g. up to $x^3$) and write the template $1 + ny + \tfrac{n(n-1)}{2!}y^2 + \tfrac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}y^3 + \dots$ before substituting.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify $n$ and $y$ from the bracket, attaching any minus sign or coefficient to $y$.
  2. Write the binomial template $1 + ny + \tfrac{n(n-1)}{2!}y^2 + \tfrac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}y^3 + \dots$ to the required number of terms.
  3. Substitute $n$ and $y$ into each term and simplify the powers of $y$ carefully (signs come from $y^r$).
  4. Combine numerical coefficients term-by-term and present in ascending powers of $x$.

Worked Micro-Example

Expand $(1-\tfrac{x}{6})^9$ up to $x^3$. Set $n=9$, $y=-\tfrac{x}{6}$. Then $1 + 9y + \tfrac{9\cdot 8}{2}y^2 + \tfrac{9\cdot 8\cdot 7}{6}y^3 = 1 + 9(-\tfrac{x}{6}) + 36(\tfrac{x^2}{36}) + 84(-\tfrac{x^3}{216})$ $= 1 - \tfrac{3x}{2} + x^2 - \tfrac{7x^3}{18}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Bracket of form $(a+bx)^n$ with $a\neq 1$ (e.g. $(2+3x)^{10}$)
Factor out $a^n$ first: $(2+3x)^{10}=2^{10}(1+\tfrac{3x}{2})^{10}$, then apply the $(1+y)^n$ template with $y=\tfrac{3x}{2}$.
P4 negative or fractional index, e.g. $(1+x)^{-2}$ or $(1-2x)^{1/3}$
Same template but the series is infinite; you must state the validity condition $|y|<1$.
Bracket with leading constant and fractional/negative $n$, e.g. $(4-x)^{-1/2}$
Factor out the constant raised to that power: $4^{-1/2}(1-\tfrac{x}{4})^{-1/2}$, then apply the template; validity becomes $|\tfrac{x}{4}|<1$.
Only a specific term (e.g. coefficient of $x^4$) is requested
Write only the general term $\binom{n}{r}y^r$, set the power of $x$ in $y^r$ equal to the target, solve for $r$, and evaluate.

Common Misreadings

  • Setting $y=\tfrac{x}{6}$ instead of $y=-\tfrac{x}{6}$, then trying to insert alternating signs by hand.
  • Treating $(a+bx)^n$ with $a\neq 1$ as if it were already in $(1+y)^n$ form and forgetting to factor out $a^n$.
  • Confusing $(1+y)^n$ template (which uses $\binom{n}{r}y^r$) with the general $(a+b)^n$ form and unnecessarily writing $1^{n-r}$ factors.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting that the negative sign is part of $y$, so $y^2=+\tfrac{x^2}{36}$ and $y^3=-\tfrac{x^3}{216}$ — sign alternation must come from $y^r$, not be added separately.
  • Mis-simplifying $\tfrac{n(n-1)\cdots}{r!}\cdot y^r$ when $y$ contains a fraction; arithmetic slips on cancelling $36$, $216$, etc.
  • Stopping at the wrong term — counting $1$ as the first term but expecting four terms 'up to $x^3$' (you need terms in $1, x, x^2, x^3$, i.e. four terms).

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL: positive integer $n$ is C2 content; the same template extends to negative/fractional $n$ in P4 with a validity condition $|y|<1$.
