Coordinate Geometry

Substitute x into the curve equation to recover the missing y-coordinate

Family: Point Recovery Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question asks for a tangent or normal 'at the point where x = a' but supplies only the x-coordinate, not a full coordinate pair. Common phrasings include 'find the equation of the tangent at x = 4', 'the normal to the curve at the point where x = 2', or 'P is the point on C with x-coordinate a'. The visual cue is a curve equation y = f(x) plus a single x-value, with no y-value given. You need a complete point (x₁, y₁) before you can write any straight-line equation, so the missing y must be recovered.

Motivation — Why this works

Any straight line is determined by a point and a gradient; the tangent/normal touches the curve at the stated x, so the point of tangency must lie on the curve and therefore satisfy y = f(x). Substituting x = a into the curve equation (not the derivative) directly produces the missing y-coordinate. The derivative f'(x) gives gradients, not positions — these are different objects and must not be confused. This step supplies the 'point' ingredient of the point-gradient form y − y₁ = m(x − x₁).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question wants a tangent/normal but only gives me an x-value — I do not yet have a full point.
  2. I rule out substituting into dy/dx for the y-coordinate: the derivative outputs gradients, not points on the curve.
  3. I rule out guessing y = x or y = 0; the point of tangency must genuinely lie on C, so it must satisfy y = f(x).
  4. I commit to substituting x = a into the original curve equation y = f(x), since this is the defining relation of points on the curve.
  5. I plan to keep the gradient calculation (dy/dx at x = a) as a separate, parallel computation so I don't mix the two roles.
  6. I anticipate fraction/sign arithmetic and choose a sensible common denominator before combining terms.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the curve equation y = f(x) and the given x-value, x = a.
  2. Substitute x = a into y = f(x), not into dy/dx.
  3. Evaluate each term carefully, paying attention to signs and negative exponents.
  4. Combine terms over a common denominator if fractions are involved to obtain a single value y₁.
  5. Record the point of tangency as (a, y₁) for use in y − y₁ = m(x − a).

Worked Micro-Example

Curve: $y = 2x^3 - 5x^2 + x - 1$, find the tangent point at $x = 2$. Substitute: $y = 2(8) - 5(4) + 2 - 1 = 16 - 20 + 2 - 1 = -3$. So the point of tangency is $(2, -3)$, ready to be paired with the gradient $f'(2)$ in the line equation.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Parametric curve x = f(t), y = g(t) with a given parameter value t = t₀
Substitute t₀ into BOTH x(t) and y(t) to recover the full point (x(t₀), y(t₀)).
Implicit curve F(x, y) = 0
Substitute x = a into F to obtain an equation in y, then solve for y (selecting the branch indicated by the question).
Curve given in the form y = f(x) with negative or fractional powers (e.g. y = (1/3)x³ − 8x⁻² + 24x⁻¹)
Same substitution; take care with x⁻ⁿ terms by computing 1/aⁿ first, then combining over a common denominator.

Common Misreadings

  • Substituting x = a into dy/dx and treating the output as the y-coordinate (it is the gradient).
  • Assuming y = a (i.e. that the y-coordinate equals the x-coordinate) without justification.
  • Skipping the substitution entirely and trying to write a tangent line with only an x-value.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when evaluating polynomial terms such as −5(4) = −20 or −8(1/16) = −1/2.
  • Fraction-arithmetic slips when combining terms like 64/3, −1/2, and 6 — choose the LCD (here 6) carefully.
  • Mishandling negative exponents: x⁻² at x = 4 is 1/16, not −16 or 1/8.
  • Carrying the gradient value forward as if it were y₁ when writing y − y₁ = m(x − x₁).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure content (P1–P3): tangents and normals to curves, including parametric and implicit forms in P3/P4. No formula booklet entry is required — this is a structural step preceding the point-gradient line equation y − y₁ = m(x − x₁). Avoid invoking implicit/parametric machinery unless the question presents the curve in those forms.
