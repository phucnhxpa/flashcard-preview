Trigonometry

Rewrite every trig function in terms of tan x using t = tan x

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question explicitly instructs 'use the substitution t = tan x' (or asks to 'show that' a polynomial in tan x results) and the equation contains a mixture of trig functions — typically tan 2x, cot x, sec²x, sin 2x or cos 2x — each of which has a clean identity in terms of tan x. Also fires when the target form is a rational or polynomial equation in a single variable, signalling that all trig must collapse to one function. Common phrasings: 'hence show that t satisfies …', 'by writing t = tan x, show …'.

Motivation — Why this works

The double-angle identity tan 2x = 2t/(1−t²), the reciprocal identity cot x = 1/t, and the Pythagorean identity sec²x = 1 + t² together let every trig term be expressed as a rational function of t alone. This converts a multi-function trig equation into a single-variable algebraic equation, which is the only route to a polynomial 'show that' target. The substitution is the bridge from trigonometry to algebra; once made, the rest is GCSE-level rearrangement.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Student notices three different trig functions (tan 2x, cot x, sec²x) and the instruction t = tan x — the target must be a polynomial in t.
  2. They rule out converting to sin/cos first: that path works but is longer and reintroduces two variables before collapsing back to tan.
  3. They commit because each function has a direct, memorised identity in tan x — no auxiliary algebra needed before substituting.
  4. Bridge: list the three identities side by side (tan 2x = 2t/(1−t²), cot x = 1/t, sec²x = 1+t²) and substitute simultaneously into the original equation.
  5. Tactical: keep denominators (1−t²) and t intact for now; clear them only after all substitutions are visible, to avoid sign errors mid-substitution.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify each trig function present and its identity in t = tan x.
  2. Replace tan 2x with 2t/(1−t²).
  3. Replace cot x with 1/t.
  4. Replace sec²x with 1+t².
  5. Write the resulting rational equation in t with denominators left visible.
  6. Multiply through by the LCM of denominators (here t(1−t²)) to clear fractions and obtain a polynomial in t.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $24\tan 2x + 5\cot x \sec^2 x = 0$ via $t=\tan x$. Substitute: $\dfrac{24\cdot 2t}{1-t^2} + \dfrac{5(1+t^2)}{t} = 0.$ Multiply by $t(1-t^2)$: $48t^2 + 5(1+t^2)(1-t^2) = 0$, i.e. $48t^2 + 5(1-t^4) = 0$, giving $5t^4 - 48t^2 - 5 = 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation in sin 2x and cos 2x with t = tan x
Use sin 2x = 2t/(1+t²) and cos 2x = (1−t²)/(1+t²); denominators are 1+t² rather than 1−t².
Equation mixing sec x and tan x
Use sec²x = 1 + tan²x as the sole bridge; if sec x appears to an odd power, isolate it and square to remove it.
Equation mixing cosec x and cot x
Use cosec²x = 1 + cot²x and substitute u = cot x instead, mirroring the structure.
Half-angle substitution t = tan(x/2)
Use sin x = 2t/(1+t²), cos x = (1−t²)/(1+t²), tan x = 2t/(1−t²) — the Weierstrass family.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading the problem as 'convert to sin and cos' rather than directly to tan x, producing a longer route.
  • Treating tan 2x as a separate variable rather than expanding via the double-angle identity.
  • Missing that cot x and sec²x must also be rewritten — substituting only tan 2x and leaving the others untouched.

Pitfalls

  • Writing tan 2x = 2 tan x (forgetting the denominator 1 − tan²x).
  • Sign error: writing 1 + tan²x instead of 1 − tan²x in the denominator of tan 2x.
  • Clearing fractions before all substitutions are made, which scrambles which terms still need rewriting.
  • Losing solutions where t = 0 or 1 − t² = 0 when multiplying through; these must be checked separately against the original equation.

A-level Scope Notes

All three identities (double-angle for tan, reciprocal cot = 1/tan, Pythagorean sec² = 1 + tan²) are in Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P3/P4). The substitution technique is standard for 'show that' polynomial-in-t questions.
