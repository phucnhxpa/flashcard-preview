Switch to quadratic formula when discriminant is non-square

Family: Root Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires on a quadratic $ax^2+bx+c=0$ where a quick scan of integer factor pairs of $ac$ fails to produce a pair summing to $b$, or where a mental discriminant check $b^2-4ac$ yields a positive non-square integer. Typical cues: leading coefficient $a\neq 1$ with awkward $ac$, the question demands 'exact roots' or 'in surd form', or marks are allocated for simplification rather than inspection. Equivalent phrasings: 'roots are irrational', 'no rational factorisation', 'use an appropriate method to solve'.

Motivation — Why this works

If $b^2-4ac$ is not a perfect square, the roots $\frac{-b\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}$ are irrational, so factorisation over $\mathbb{Q}$ is impossible by the rational root theorem. The quadratic formula is a closed-form algorithm that returns exact surd roots in a single pass, while completing the square produces the same answer with extra fractional manipulation when $a\neq 1$. Choosing the formula minimises arithmetic risk and matches the mark scheme's expectation of an exact simplified surd.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the quadratic has $a\neq 1$ and $c$ negative, so the relevant product $ac$ is awkward (here $-36$).
  2. Rule out integer factorisation after a brief search: no factor pair of $36$ with appropriate signs sums to $8$; equivalently, compute $b^2-4ac=208$ and observe it is not a perfect square.
  3. Commit to the quadratic formula because the roots must be irrational and the formula is the most direct route to exact surd form.
  4. Bridge to operation: write down $a,b,c$ explicitly, being careful with the sign of $c$ when forming $-4ac$.
  5. Inside the move, plan two simplifications in advance: extract the largest square factor from the surd, then cancel any common factor between the numerator and $2a$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify $a$, $b$, $c$ with correct signs.
  2. Compute the discriminant $\Delta=b^2-4ac$ and confirm it is positive but not a perfect square.
  3. Substitute into $x=\dfrac{-b\pm\sqrt{\Delta}}{2a}$.
  4. Simplify $\sqrt{\Delta}$ by extracting the largest square factor.
  5. Cancel any common numerical factor between the numerator and denominator to give the exact simplified form.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $3x^2+8x-12=0$ exactly. Here $a=3,\ b=8,\ c=-12$, so $\Delta=64-4(3)(-12)=64+144=208$, not a perfect square. Then $x=\dfrac{-8\pm\sqrt{208}}{6}=\dfrac{-8\pm 4\sqrt{13}}{6}=\dfrac{-4\pm 2\sqrt{13}}{3}.$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic in disguise, e.g. $2u^4-5u^2-4=0$ with substitution $y=u^2$
Apply the formula in $y$, then back-substitute and discard $y<0$ before taking square roots.
Trigonometric quadratic such as $3\sin^2\theta+8\sin\theta-12=0$ on a given interval
Use the formula in $s=\sin\theta$, reject any root with $|s|>1$, then solve $\sin\theta=s$ over the interval.
Discriminant is a perfect square
Abandon the formula plan and factorise by inspection or the $ac$-method for speed.
Question asks for the form $p+q\sqrt{r}$ after completing the square
Use completing the square instead, since the required form mirrors that method's output.
Complex roots required (Further Pure)
Apply the formula with $\Delta<0$ and write $\sqrt{\Delta}=i\sqrt{|\Delta|}$, then simplify the surd inside.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating a non-square positive discriminant as evidence of 'no real roots' rather than irrational real roots.
  • Persisting with factor-pair searches after the discriminant has already shown rational factorisation is impossible.
  • Confusing 'exact form' with 'decimal answer' and reaching for the calculator instead of the formula.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error on $-4ac$ when $c<0$: $-4(3)(-12)=+144$, not $-144$.
  • Leaving $\sqrt{208}$ unsimplified; mark schemes require the largest square factor extracted ($4\sqrt{13}$).
  • Forgetting to cancel a common factor between numerator and denominator (here $2$).
  • Dividing only one of the numerator terms by the common factor, breaking the equality.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL P1 (quadratic formula, surd simplification); the complex-root variant belongs to FP1.
