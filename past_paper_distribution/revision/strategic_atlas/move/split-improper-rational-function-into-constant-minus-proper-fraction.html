Split improper rational function into constant minus proper fraction

Family: Polynomial Division Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A rational function $(ax+b)/(cx+d)$ (or higher-degree numerator with degree equal to denominator) appears and you must determine its range, sketch it, find its horizontal asymptote, or integrate it. Equal degrees in numerator and denominator are the key cue. Phrases like 'find the range', 'state the equations of the asymptotes', or 'show that $f(x)$ can be written in the form $A + B/(x+k)$' all fire this move.

Motivation — Why this works

Polynomial division rewrites the function as a constant (the horizontal asymptote) plus or minus a proper fraction whose sign and monotonicity are easy to read. The constant is the limit at infinity; the proper fraction measures the deviation from it. This decomposition turns range, monotonicity, and asymptote questions into trivial sign analysis of $1/(x+k)$, and it is also the standard preparation for integrating such fractions to a logarithm.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the numerator and denominator have the same degree, so the function is improper and has a horizontal (not slant) asymptote.
  2. I rule out direct range analysis by inverting $y = f(x)$ for $x$ — possible but slower — and rule out calculus, since division will expose monotonicity for free.
  3. I commit to polynomial division because it produces the form $A + B/(x+k)$, where $A$ is the asymptote and the sign of $B$ tells me whether $f$ approaches $A$ from above or below.
  4. First concrete operation: write $ax+b = a/c\cdot(cx+d) + r$ to extract the quotient $a/c$ and remainder $r$.
  5. Inside the move I track the sign of the remainder on the given domain, evaluate $f$ at the endpoint, and use monotonicity of $1/(x+k)$ to conclude both bounds of the range.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify quotient $A = a/c$ from leading coefficients.
  2. Compute remainder: $ax+b - A(cx+d) = b - Ad$.
  3. Write $f(x) = A + (b - Ad)/(cx+d)$.
  4. Determine the sign of the proper fraction on the given domain.
  5. Evaluate $f$ at any finite endpoint and take the limit as $x \to \pm\infty$ to obtain the range.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the range of $f(x) = (22x-3)/(x+13)$ for $x > 4$. Divide: $22x - 3 = 22(x+13) - 289$, so $f(x) = 22 - 289/(x+13)$. For $x > 4$, $x+13 > 17 > 0$, hence $289/(x+13) > 0$ and $f(x) < 22$. As $x \to 4^+$, $f(x) \to 22 - 289/17 = 22 - 17 = 5$; as $x \to \infty$, $f(x) \to 22$. Since $f$ is increasing, the range is $(5, 22)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Integrating $(ax+b)/(cx+d)$
After splitting, integrate the constant directly and the proper fraction as $(B/c)\ln|cx+d|$.
Curve sketching of $y = (ax+b)/(cx+d)$
The split immediately gives the horizontal asymptote $y = a/c$ and vertical asymptote $x = -d/c$, plus the side from which the curve approaches each.
Numerator degree exceeds denominator (e.g. $(x^2+1)/(x-2)$)
Long division yields a polynomial quotient (slant or higher) plus a proper fraction; same logic, but the asymptote is now linear/oblique.
Showing $f(x) = A + B/(x+k)$ in 'express in the form' questions
The split itself is the answer — read off $A$ and $B$ directly from the division.
Finding the inverse of $f(x) = (ax+b)/(cx+d)$
After splitting, isolating $x$ from $y = A + B/(cx+d)$ is one rearrangement step.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the function as already proper and concluding the asymptote is $y=0$.
  • Reading the leading-coefficient ratio as the answer for the range, forgetting it is only the asymptote and may not be attained.
  • Mistaking the form $A + B/(x+k)$ for partial fractions — here there is only one linear factor, so no decomposition is needed beyond the division.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slip computing the remainder, e.g. $22 \times 13 = 286$ giving remainder $-289$; sign and magnitude must both be checked.
  • Forgetting that the horizontal asymptote is never attained, so the corresponding endpoint of the range is open.
  • Failing to check whether the boundary value of $x$ is included in the domain when deciding open vs closed endpoints.
  • Confusing 'approaches from below' (negative correction) with 'approaches from above' (positive correction) — the sign of $B$ decides this.
