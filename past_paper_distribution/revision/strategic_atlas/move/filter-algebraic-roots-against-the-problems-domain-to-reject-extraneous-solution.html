Algebra & Functions

Filter algebraic roots against the problem's domain to reject extraneous solutions

Family: Domain Filtering Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

This move fires whenever an algebraic manipulation (squaring, substituting, factoring, exponentiating) produces multiple candidate roots but the original problem carries a domain restriction. Concrete cues include: a substitution like u = √x, u = e^{kT}, or u = |f(x)| that is intrinsically positive/non-negative; a stated domain such as x ≥ 0, x > √5, or t ≥ 0; a composite function fg(x) inheriting g's domain; or a logarithm/square-root in the original expression forcing its argument to be positive. Equivalent phrasings: 'check validity of roots', 'discard inadmissible solutions', 'apply domain to filter answers'.

Motivation — Why this works

Algebraic operations frequently enlarge the solution set: squaring introduces sign-flipped roots, substituting hides the original variable's range, and factoring can produce roots that lie outside the function's natural domain. The filtering step restores correctness by intersecting the algebraic solution set with the admissible domain. This relies on the basic principle that a value is a solution only if it satisfies BOTH the derived equation AND every domain constraint of the original problem (the substitution's range, the function's domain, and any restriction stated in the question). Edexcel mark schemes routinely award a dedicated mark for stating the rejection with reason.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the algebra has produced two (or more) candidate values — typically from a quadratic, a ± from squaring, or a factorisation.
  2. Pause before reporting: ask 'what was the original variable, and what constraints does it carry?' Rule out simply taking both roots, since at least one may not correspond to any real input.
  3. Identify the source of the constraint: (a) the substitution itself (√x ≥ 0, e^{kT} > 0), (b) the question's stated domain (x > √5, T ≥ 0), or (c) the inner function of a composition (fg inherits domain of g).
  4. Commit to filtering: test each candidate against the constraint and prepare to discard those that fail, with explicit written justification.
  5. Tactical choice: if the constraint is on the substitution variable u, check u directly; if it's on the original variable x, back-substitute first to find the corresponding x and then test.
  6. Report only the surviving root(s), and write a one-line reason such as 'reject u = −2 since u = √x ≥ 0' so the examiner sees the domain argument.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. List all algebraic candidates from the equation (e.g. u = 7/2 or u = −2; x = ±√13).
  2. State the domain constraint explicitly: e.g. 'u = √x ⇒ u ≥ 0', or 'g has domain x ≥ 0', or 'question requires T ≥ 0 ⇒ e^{0.3T} ≥ 1'.
  3. Test each candidate against the constraint; if working with a substitution, back-substitute to the original variable when needed.
  4. Discard each failing candidate with a written reason (e.g. 'reject since T < 0').
  5. Carry only the surviving root(s) into the next step of the solution.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $x - 5\sqrt{x} + 6 = 0$ for $x > 0$. Let $u = \sqrt{x}$, so $u \geq 0$ and $u^2 - 5u + 6 = 0$, giving $u = 2$ or $u = 3$. Both are non-negative, so both are valid here, yielding $x = 4$ or $x = 9$. (If instead the quadratic had given $u = -2$, we would write 'reject $u = -2$ since $u = \sqrt{x} \geq 0$'.)

Variants — Same move, different surface

Substitution u = √x or u = |f(x)|
Reject any negative root of u immediately, citing u ≥ 0.
Substitution u = e^{kT} with T ≥ 0 and k > 0
Reject roots with u < 1 (since they correspond to T < 0); note u > 0 alone is not enough.
Composite function fg(x) = k
Filter solutions through the domain of g (the inner function), not f's domain.
Equation containing ln(h(x)) or √h(x)
Require h(x) > 0 (or ≥ 0) and reject roots violating this — even if they satisfy the algebraically simplified equation.
Trigonometric substitution u = sin θ or u = cos θ
Reject |u| > 1 since sin/cos are bounded in [−1,1].

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the range of the inner function as a domain restriction on x in a composite.
  • Assuming 'exponential ≥ 1' is universal, when it actually depends on the sign of the exponent's domain.
  • Believing the negative root should always be rejected — sometimes both roots are valid and a check is required, not reflex.

Pitfalls

  • Silently dropping the extraneous root without writing the justification — Edexcel reserves a mark for the stated reason.
  • Rejecting a root for the wrong reason (e.g. 'it's small' instead of 'it gives T < 0').
  • Forgetting to back-substitute: a positive u may still produce an x outside the stated domain.
  • Reporting both ± roots after squaring without verifying which actually satisfies the original (un-squared) equation.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (P2–P4) scope: covers domain/range of functions and compositions (P2), exponential/logarithmic models (P3), and substitution methods in equation solving. No formula booklet entry is invoked; the move is a reasoning step. Avoid generalising to complex roots or multi-valued functions, which are out of scope.
