Differential Equations

Translate verbal rate description into signed ODE

Family: Mathematical Modelling Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question describes a rate of change in words rather than symbols: phrases like 'the rate of decrease of r is inversely proportional to r²', 'the rate at which V increases is proportional to …', or 'the temperature falls at a rate proportional to …'. Any modelling problem that asks you to 'form a differential equation' from a physical/contextual description triggers this move. Also fires when the variable is implicitly time-dependent (melting, cooling, leaking, population, dilution) and you must produce dy/dt = … before solving.

Motivation — Why this works

A verbal rate statement encodes three pieces of information: which derivative (dy/dt vs dx/dy etc.), the functional form of the right-hand side (proportional, inversely proportional, linear in a difference), and the sign (increase vs decrease). Capturing all three correctly is the entire modelling step — every subsequent separation, integration, and boundary-condition fit depends on it. Writing k > 0 and carrying the sign explicitly keeps later algebra clean and physically interpretable.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the rate phrase and identify which variable is changing with respect to which — here 'rate of … of r' with implicit time means dr/dt.
  2. Rule out writing kr² for 'inversely proportional to r²'; inverse proportionality puts r² in the denominator, giving 1/r².
  3. Commit to a positive constant k and attach the sign separately, because 'rate of decrease' forces dr/dt < 0.
  4. Bridge to the equation: dr/dt = −k/r² with k > 0.
  5. Sanity-check the physics: as r shrinks, |dr/dt| grows, matching a ball melting faster as it gets smaller — model is consistent.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the rate quantity (e.g. dr/dt, dV/dt, dT/dt).
  2. Translate 'proportional to f' as = k·f and 'inversely proportional to f' as = k/f.
  3. Decide the sign from 'increase' (+) vs 'decrease' (−), keeping k > 0.
  4. Write the final ODE explicitly, e.g. dr/dt = −k/r², k > 0.
  5. State any domain restrictions (e.g. r > 0) needed for the model to make sense.

Worked Micro-Example

A spherical ball melts so that its radius decreases at a rate inversely proportional to $r^2$. Form an ODE for $r$. Step 1: rate is $\frac{dr}{dt}$. Step 2: inversely proportional to $r^2$ gives $\frac{k}{r^2}$. Step 3: 'decreases' attaches a minus sign. Hence $\frac{dr}{dt} = -\frac{k}{r^2},\ k>0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Rate of increase proportional to (A − x), e.g. limited growth/learning models
Write dx/dt = k(A − x) with k > 0; positive sign because x increases toward A.
Newton's law of cooling: temperature falls toward ambient T₀
Write dT/dt = −k(T − T₀), k > 0; negative sign because T decreases when T > T₀.
Volume of liquid leaking from a tank at rate proportional to √h
Write dV/dt = −k√h, k > 0; convert to dh/dt using V = V(h) via chain rule.
Population growth proportional to current population
Write dP/dt = kP, k > 0; positive sign for growth, negative for decay.
Surface-area-driven evaporation of a sphere (rate of volume change ∝ surface area)
Write dV/dt = −k·4πr²; then convert to dr/dt using V = 4πr³/3, giving dr/dt = −k.

Common Misreadings

  • Writing dr/dt = k/r² and forgetting the negative sign demanded by 'decrease'.
  • Writing dr/dt = −kr², confusing 'inversely proportional to r²' with 'proportional to r²'.
  • Treating 'rate' as d(something else)/dt — e.g. modelling dV/dt when the question specifies the rate of the radius.
  • Inserting an equals sign without a constant of proportionality, e.g. dr/dt = −1/r².

Pitfalls

  • Embedding the sign into k (writing dr/dt = k/r² with k < 0) — works algebraically but causes confusion when fitting boundary conditions and reporting k.
  • Forgetting to state k > 0 explicitly, which examiners often expect.
  • Mixing up which variable is independent when a chain rule is needed (e.g. dV/dt vs dr/dt for a sphere).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Maths (P3/P4 differential equations) and Further Maths FP-level modelling questions; only first-order separable or linear ODEs with one proportionality constant are expected.
