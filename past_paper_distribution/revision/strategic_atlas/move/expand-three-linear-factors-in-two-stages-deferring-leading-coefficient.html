Expand three linear factors in two stages, deferring leading coefficient

Family: Staged Expansion Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

A product of three (or more) linear factors must be rewritten in standard polynomial form $Ax^3+Bx^2+Cx+D$, typically after finding a curve from its roots or after factor-theorem work. Visual cues: the expression appears as $k(x-\alpha)(x-\beta)(x-\gamma)$ or similar, and the question demands explicit coefficients. Equivalent phrasings: 'write in the form $ax^3+bx^2+cx+d$', 'find the values of $a, b, c, d$', 'expand and simplify'. The presence of a fractional or negative leading constant $k$ is an extra signal to delay its distribution.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying three binomials in one go produces eight terms with high risk of sign and combination errors. Pairing two factors first reduces the task to a single quadratic-times-linear expansion, which is systematic and checkable. Saving the leading coefficient $k$ (especially if fractional like $-\tfrac12$) for the very last step keeps all intermediate arithmetic in integers. The underlying principle is simply associativity and distributivity of multiplication: $(fg)h = f(gh)$, which lets us choose the easiest grouping.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the answer demands a standard-form cubic, but the input is a product of three linear factors (possibly with a constant $k$ outside).
  2. Rule out expanding all three at once — too many cross-terms — and rule out polynomial division or synthetic methods, which don't apply here since there's nothing to divide.
  3. Commit to staged expansion because associativity lets us pick the most arithmetically friendly pair.
  4. Choose the pair whose coefficients are simplest: e.g. two factors with unit leading coefficient, or a pair forming a difference-of-squares pattern. This minimises the size of intermediate coefficients.
  5. Decide to keep any external constant $k$ (e.g. $-\tfrac12$) outside the brackets until the integer cubic is fully assembled, then distribute $k$ in one clean pass.
  6. After multiplying the chosen pair to a quadratic, distribute each term of the third linear factor across all three quadratic terms systematically (top term times all three, then bottom term times all three) and combine like powers.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the leading constant $k$ (if any) and set it aside; work with $(x-\alpha)(x-\beta)(x-\gamma)$.
  2. Pick the two simplest factors and expand: $(x-\alpha)(x-\beta) = x^2 - (\alpha+\beta)x + \alpha\beta$.
  3. Multiply this quadratic by the remaining linear factor, distributing each term: $(x^2+px+q)(x+r) = x^3 + rx^2 + px^2 + prx + qx + qr$.
  4. Collect like terms to get an integer cubic $x^3 + Bx^2 + Cx + D$.
  5. Multiply through by $k$: $kx^3 + kBx^2 + kCx + kD$.
  6. Read off the coefficients $A=k$, $B$, $C$, $D$.

Worked Micro-Example

Expand $-\tfrac12(x+4)(x+2)(x-3)$. Step 1: $(x+4)(x+2)=x^2+6x+8$. Step 2: $(x^2+6x+8)(x-3)=x^3-3x^2+6x^2-18x+8x-24=x^3+3x^2-10x-24$. Step 3: multiply by $-\tfrac12$: $-\tfrac12 x^3 - \tfrac32 x^2 + 5x + 12$. So $A=-\tfrac12,\ B=-\tfrac32,\ C=5,\ D=12$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

One pair of factors fits a special identity, e.g. $(x+a)(x-a)$
Pair those two first to exploit $(x+a)(x-a)=x^2-a^2$, regardless of which factor has the simplest coefficients.
All three factors have non-unit leading coefficients, e.g. $(x+6)(2x-3)(3x+20)$
Pair the two with the smallest combined coefficients first to keep the intermediate quadratic compact, then multiply by the heaviest factor last.
Leading constant is a clean integer like $2$ or $-1$
It's safe to distribute early, but deferring is still a good habit for consistency.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the problem as a factor-theorem question and trying polynomial long division — there is nothing to divide here, only to expand.
  • Assuming the leading coefficient is $1$ when an external constant $k$ is present, and forgetting to distribute it at the end.
  • Trying to expand all three brackets simultaneously by writing out all eight terms — error-prone and unstructured.

Pitfalls

  • Distributing a fractional or negative leading constant inside the brackets early, producing fractions in intermediate steps and increasing arithmetic errors.
  • Sign errors when multiplying a positive constant by a negative one, e.g. $(+8)(-3)=-24$.
  • Losing a term during the six-product distribution in the second stage; mitigate by listing each top-row product, then each bottom-row product, before combining.
  • Forgetting to multiply every term — including the constant — by the final leading coefficient $k$.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays squarely within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 algebraic manipulation. No formula booklet entry is required — this is a direct application of distributivity. Avoid generalising to symbolic expansions like the binomial theorem unless explicitly cued; that's a separate technique with its own scope.
