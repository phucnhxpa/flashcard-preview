Sequences & Series

Split a hybrid summand into independent component sums

Family: Linearity Of Summation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the general term u_n is an additive combination of structurally different pieces — e.g. a polynomial plus an alternating term, an arithmetic part plus a geometric part, or a constant plus a power. Cues include expressions like 5n + 3(-1)^n, n + 2^n, or 3 + 4n + 5^n inside a Σ. No single standard formula (AP, GP, Σn, Σn²) covers the whole term at once. Whenever you see Σ acting on a sum of terms with different growth/oscillation behaviour, suspect this move.

Motivation — Why this works

Summation is linear: Σ(f(n)+g(n)) = Σf(n) + Σg(n), and Σ(c·f(n)) = c·Σf(n). This identity lets us decompose an intractable hybrid sum into separate sums, each of which is a textbook case (arithmetic series, geometric series, Σn, Σn², Σn³ standard results). It converts one unfamiliar problem into two familiar ones, which can then be added back together. The principle is the discrete analogue of the linearity of integration.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that u_n is built from two pieces with different character — one grows linearly/polynomially, the other alternates or grows geometrically.
  2. Rule out applying a single AP or GP formula directly, since neither model fits the combined term.
  3. Commit to linearity of Σ as the unlocking principle: it is always valid for finite sums and lets each component be handled by its own standard tool.
  4. Bridge: rewrite Σu_n as a sum of two Σ's, then pull constant multipliers outside each Σ.
  5. Tactically decide which formula attaches to each piece — Σn = n(n+1)/2 for the linear part, finite GP sum a(1−r^n)/(1−r) for the alternating/geometric part — and recombine at the end.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify u_n as a sum f(n) + g(n) of structurally different components.
  2. Apply linearity: Σ_{n=1}^{N}(f(n)+g(n)) = Σ_{n=1}^{N} f(n) + Σ_{n=1}^{N} g(n).
  3. Factor out any constant multipliers: Σ c·f(n) = c·Σ f(n).
  4. Evaluate each sum independently using the matching standard formula (AP, GP, Σn, Σn², Σn³).
  5. Add the two evaluated sums and simplify.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate S = Σ_{n=1}^{10}(5n + 3(-1)^n). By linearity, S = 5·Σ_{n=1}^{10} n + 3·Σ_{n=1}^{10}(-1)^n. The first part: 5·(10·11/2) = 275. The second part: 3·(-1)·(1-(-1)^{10})/(1-(-1)) = 3·(-1)(0)/2 = 0. Hence S = 275 + 0 = 275.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Polynomial + geometric, e.g. Σ(n² + 2^n)
Split into Σn² (use n(n+1)(2n+1)/6) and Σ2^n (finite GP with a=2, r=2).
Constant + linear, e.g. Σ(3 + 4n)
Split into Σ3 = 3N and 4·Σn; equivalent to AP formula but obtained via linearity.
Multiple components, e.g. Σ(n³ − 2n + 5)
Split into three sums Σn³, −2·Σn, and Σ5, each evaluated by its standard result.
Method of differences problem with extra additive term
Split off the telescoping piece from the additive constant/polynomial piece and handle each separately.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating u_n = 5n + 3(-1)^n as an AP because of the 5n and applying a + (n−1)d directly.
  • Treating the whole expression as a GP because of the (-1)^n factor.
  • Believing linearity also applies to products, i.e. wrongly writing Σf(n)g(n) = Σf(n)·Σg(n).

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to factor out constants — writing Σ5n as Σn instead of 5·Σn.
  • Mismatching the upper/lower limits when splitting; both component sums must inherit the same range.
  • Using r = -1 in the GP formula carelessly, leading to division-by-zero confusion if not simplified to the alternating pattern (−1, +1, −1, …).
  • Dropping a sign when extracting a negative constant, e.g. Σ(−2n) = −2·Σn, not 2·Σn.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully inside Edexcel IAL Pure/Further Pure scope: relies only on linearity of finite sums, AP/GP formulae, and the standard results Σn, Σn², Σn³ from the FP1 formula booklet.
