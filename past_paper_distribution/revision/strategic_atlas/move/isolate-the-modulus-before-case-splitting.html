Isolate the modulus before case-splitting

Family: Modulus Isolation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an equation or inequality contains a single modulus expression buried among other terms, e.g. $a\,x^2 + b = c - |f(x)|$ or $|f(x)| + h(x) = k$. The signal is that you intend to remove the modulus by case-splitting (rather than by squaring), and the modulus is not yet alone on one side. Typical surfaces include intersections of a curve with a modulus graph, or solving $|f(x)| = g(x)$ where $g$ depends on $x$.

Motivation — Why this works

Rearranging to the canonical form $|f(x)| = g(x)$ makes the two cases $f(x) = g(x)$ and $f(x) = -g(x)$ immediate and symmetric, eliminating sign confusion. It also exposes the validity condition $g(x) \ge 0$, which acts as a built-in filter on candidate solutions. Keeping the modulus isolated avoids accidentally distributing a sign through $|\cdot|$, which is not a legal algebraic operation. Compared with squaring, this route keeps the resulting equations at the original degree, so quadratics stay quadratic.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation contains a single $|\cdot|$ term mixed in with polynomial terms on the same side.
  2. Rule out squaring both sides immediately — that would inflate the degree (e.g. quadratic becomes quartic) and introduce extraneous roots.
  3. Commit to case-splitting, which requires the modulus to be alone so the $\pm$ branches drop out cleanly.
  4. Move every non-modulus term to the opposite side and, if the modulus carries a leading minus, multiply through by $-1$.
  5. Once isolated as $|f(x)| = g(x)$, note the constraint $g(x) \ge 0$ for later filtering, then write the two cases $f(x) = \pm g(x)$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the single modulus term $|f(x)|$ in the equation.
  2. Transpose all other terms to the opposite side so that $\pm|f(x)|$ stands alone.
  3. If the coefficient of the modulus is negative, multiply the whole equation by $-1$ to obtain $|f(x)| = g(x)$.
  4. Record the validity condition $g(x) \ge 0$.
  5. Split into two cases $f(x) = g(x)$ and $f(x) = -g(x)$, solve each, then filter solutions against the validity condition.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\tfrac{1}{9}x^2 - 9 = 5 - |3x - 22|$. Move the polynomial terms across: $|3x - 22| = 14 - \tfrac{1}{9}x^2$. Require $14 - \tfrac{1}{9}x^2 \ge 0$. Case 1: $3x - 22 = 14 - \tfrac{1}{9}x^2$. Case 2: $3x - 22 = -(14 - \tfrac{1}{9}x^2)$. Solve each quadratic, then discard any root failing the validity condition.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Modulus equation with two moduli, $|f(x)| = |g(x)|$
Squaring both sides is cleaner: $f(x)^2 = g(x)^2$, then factor as $(f-g)(f+g)=0$.
Modulus inequality $|f(x)| < g(x)$ or $|f(x)| > g(x)$
After isolating, replace case-splitting with the standard expansions $-g < f < g$ or $f < -g$ or $f > g$, again requiring $g \ge 0$.
Modulus equals a constant, $|f(x)| = k$
Already isolated; just check $k \ge 0$ and write $f(x) = \pm k$.
Modulus on both sides plus extra terms, e.g. $|f| + |g| = h$
Cannot isolate one modulus cleanly; instead split the real line into intervals defined by the zeros of $f$ and $g$ and remove moduli per interval.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $|\cdot|$ as ordinary brackets and 'distributing' the minus sign inside, e.g. writing $-|3x-22| = |-3x+22|$ as if it changed the value.
  • Believing the equation must be squared because a modulus is present, even when only one modulus appears.
  • Reading $|f(x)| = g(x)$ as automatically equivalent to $f(x) = \pm g(x)$ without imposing $g(x) \ge 0$.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to flip the sign when moving $-|f(x)|$ across, e.g. writing $|3x-22| = \tfrac{1}{9}x^2 - 14$ instead of $14 - \tfrac{1}{9}x^2$.
  • Solving both cases but failing to test candidates against $g(x) \ge 0$, retaining extraneous roots.
  • Combining isolation with squaring midway, doubling the algebra and losing track of the validity constraint.
  • Dropping the modulus entirely after isolation instead of producing both $\pm$ branches.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard technique within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 for modulus equations and graph intersections of $y=f(x)$ with $y=|g(x)|$; no methods beyond rearrangement, quadratic solving, and a non-negativity check are required.
