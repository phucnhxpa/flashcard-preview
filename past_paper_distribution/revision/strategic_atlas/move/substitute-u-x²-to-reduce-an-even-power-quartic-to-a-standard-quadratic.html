Algebra & Functions

Substitute u = x² to reduce an even-power quartic to a standard quadratic

Family: Disguised Quadratic Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

The equation contains only even powers of the variable (x⁴, x²) plus a constant, with NO odd-power terms (x³ or x). Equivalently, the highest exponent is exactly twice the middle exponent and the lowest is zero — a 4:2:0 (or more generally 2n:n:0) pattern. Phrasings to listen for: 'solve the quartic', 'hence find all real values of x', or a quartic that resists obvious linear factoring. The same cue applies in trigonometric or exponential disguise: a·f(x)² + b·f(x) + c = 0 with f(x) = sinθ, cosθ, eˣ, etc.

Motivation — Why this works

An equation of the form ax⁴ + bx² + c = 0 is structurally identical to au² + bu + c = 0 under u = x². The substitution exchanges a degree-4 problem (no general elementary factoring) for a degree-2 problem solvable by factorisation, completing the square, or the quadratic formula. After finding u, reversing the substitution via x = ±√u recovers the original roots, and the constraint u ≥ 0 (since u = x² for real x) automatically filters out non-real solutions. This is the cleanest, most systematic route — far safer than attempting to factor a quartic by inspection.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Inspect the exponents present: I see x⁴, x², and a constant — no x³, no x. The exponent pattern 4, 2, 0 is the hallmark of a disguised quadratic.
  2. Rule out alternatives: factoring out x fails (no common x factor); rational root theorem on the quartic is laborious; treating it as a generic quartic is overkill. The substitution route is faster and more systematic.
  3. Commit: let u = x², so x⁴ = u². The equation becomes au² + bu + c = 0, which I can factor or apply the quadratic formula to.
  4. Bridge to operation: rewrite the equation in u, then either factor by splitting the middle term (search for two numbers multiplying to ac and summing to b) or use the formula.
  5. Tactical check after solving: u = x² requires u ≥ 0 for real x. Any negative u-root must be rejected (or noted as giving complex roots if the question permits).
  6. Back-substitute: for each acceptable u, write x² = u and take x = ±√u — keep BOTH signs unless the context forbids one.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Let u = x², so x⁴ = u². Rewrite ax⁴ + bx² + c = 0 as au² + bu + c = 0.
  2. Factorise the quadratic in u: search for two numbers with product a·c and sum b; split the middle term and group.
  3. Solve to obtain u = u₁ or u = u₂.
  4. Apply the constraint u ≥ 0: reject any negative u (no real x); keep non-negative u.
  5. For each surviving u-value, back-substitute x² = u and take x = ±√u — preserve the ± sign.
  6. State all real solutions; check by substituting one back into the original equation.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $2x^4 - 15x^2 - 50 = 0$. Let $u = x^2$: $2u^2 - 15u - 50 = 0 \Rightarrow (2u+5)(u-10) = 0$, so $u = -\tfrac{5}{2}$ (reject, $u \geq 0$) or $u = 10$. Hence $x^2 = 10 \Rightarrow x = \pm\sqrt{10}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Trigonometric disguised quadratic, e.g. 2sin²θ + 3sinθ − 2 = 0
Let u = sinθ; solve the quadratic in u, then enforce −1 ≤ u ≤ 1 before solving sinθ = u for θ in the given range.
Exponential disguised quadratic, e.g. e^{2x} − 5eˣ + 6 = 0
Let u = eˣ; solve for u, then reject any u ≤ 0 (since eˣ > 0) before taking x = ln u.
Sextic with only x⁶, x³, constant terms
Let u = x³; solve the quadratic in u, then take x = u^{1/3} (cube root, single real value per u).
Quartic in another letter, e.g. 2c⁴ − 3c² − 2 = 0
Identical method with u = c²; the choice of letter is cosmetic.

Common Misreadings

  • Failing to notice the even-powers-only structure and trying to factor x out (no common x factor exists).
  • Applying the quadratic formula directly to the quartic, confusing the degree.
  • Assuming the substitution works for any quartic — it requires the 4:2:0 exponent pattern with no x³ or x term.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to back-substitute: leaving the answer as u-values rather than x-values.
  • Losing the ± when taking the square root of x² = u.
  • Accepting a negative u and writing x = √(negative) as a real solution.
  • Reporting only one sign, e.g. giving x = √2 but omitting x = −√2.
  • Arithmetic slips when using the quadratic formula on awkward coefficients — factorisation by splitting the middle term is usually cleaner.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1/P2) — quadratic equations and the quadratic formula are in the formula booklet. The substitution itself is standard A-level technique. Avoid extending to complex roots (x² = negative) unless the question is in Further Pure where complex numbers are in scope.
