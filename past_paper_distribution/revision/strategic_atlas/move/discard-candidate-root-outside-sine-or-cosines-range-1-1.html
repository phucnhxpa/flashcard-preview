Trigonometry

Discard candidate root outside sine or cosine's range [−1, 1]

Family: Domain Filtering Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires after substituting u = sin θ (or cos θ) reduces a trig equation to a polynomial — typically a quadratic — and solving yields two values of u. If at least one value has |u| > 1, that branch cannot correspond to any real angle. Common phrasings: 'solve for sin 2x', 'hence find x given …', where factorisation produces roots like sin θ = −3, cos θ = 2, or sin θ = 5/4.

Motivation — Why this works

The substitution u = sin θ is a one-way algebraic rewrite: every solution of the trig equation gives a polynomial root, but not every polynomial root corresponds to an angle. Sine and cosine are bounded by [−1, 1], so any candidate u outside this interval is extraneous. Filtering before angle-finding prevents wasted work and calculator-domain errors when applying arcsin or arccos.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the factorisation has produced two candidate values for sin 2x (or cos θ).
  2. Rule out any value with absolute value greater than 1, since sine and cosine never exceed this bound.
  3. Commit to the surviving root(s) only — the rejected branch contributes no solutions in x.
  4. Bridge: take the surviving equation (e.g. sin 2x = 1/3) into the standard angle-finding routine.
  5. Tactically, record the rejection explicitly in working so the marker sees the validity check, not a silent omission.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Factorise the polynomial in u = sin θ (or cos θ), e.g. (3u − 1)(u + 3) = 0
  2. Read off candidate values: u = 1/3 or u = −3
  3. Test each against −1 ≤ u ≤ 1; reject u = −3 as out of range
  4. State the rejection in working ('no solution from sin 2x = −3')
  5. Continue solving sin 2x = 1/3 on the given interval for x

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $3\sin^2 2x + 8\sin 2x - 3 = 0$ for $0 \le x < \pi$. Let $u = \sin 2x$: $(3u-1)(u+3) = 0$, so $u = \tfrac{1}{3}$ or $u = -3$. Since $|\sin 2x| \le 1$, reject $u = -3$. Solve $\sin 2x = \tfrac{1}{3}$: $2x = \arcsin(1/3),\ \pi - \arcsin(1/3),\ \ldots$, then divide by 2 and keep values in $[0, \pi)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic in cos θ producing cos θ = 2
Reject cos θ = 2 by the same |cos θ| ≤ 1 bound; solve the surviving cos equation.
Quadratic in sec θ or cosec θ producing |sec θ| < 1
Reverse logic: reject roots with |sec θ| < 1 or |cosec θ| < 1, since these reciprocal functions satisfy |·| ≥ 1.
Both roots lie inside [−1, 1]
No rejection — solve both branches and combine solution sets, deduplicating any coincidences.
Substitution u = tan θ
No range filter applies — tan is unbounded, so every real root of the polynomial yields angles.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating sin θ = −3 as solvable and entering arcsin(−3) on the calculator, producing a math error or NaN.
  • Assuming one root is 'always' rejected and discarding a valid in-range root by mistake.
  • Forgetting that the bound is |u| ≤ 1, not u ≥ 0, and rejecting legitimate negative roots like sin θ = −1/2.

Pitfalls

  • Skipping the explicit rejection statement — markers may penalise missing justification.
  • Applying the [−1, 1] filter to tan θ or cot θ, which are unbounded.
  • Rejecting u = ±1 as 'edge cases' — these are valid and give θ at the boundary.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard in Edexcel IAL P2/P3 trig equation chapters; the [−1, 1] range of sine and cosine is foundational and used routinely after quadratic-in-trig substitutions.
