Differentiation

Deduce monotonicity of f∘f from sign of f' via chain rule

Family: Sign Analysis Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires on a 'deduce' or 'hence' part asking whether a self-composition $ff(x)=f(f(x))$ (or any composition) is increasing or decreasing, after an earlier part has already established the sign of $f'$ on the relevant interval. Typical cues: 'Hence deduce that $ff$ is increasing on …', 'State, with reasons, whether $f\circ f$ is increasing or decreasing'. Also fires whenever you must determine the range of $ff$ and need monotonicity to read off endpoints.

Motivation — Why this works

By the chain rule $(f\circ f)'(x) = f'(f(x))\cdot f'(x)$, so the sign of the derivative of the composition is the product of the signs of $f'$ at two points. If $f'<0$ throughout and the inner output stays in the same interval, both factors are negative and their product is positive. This bypasses re-differentiating an explicit (often messy) formula for $ff(x)$ and matches the 'deduce' instruction, which rewards arguing from prior results rather than computing afresh.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the verb 'deduce' or 'hence' — this signals reuse of earlier sign information about $f'$ rather than fresh differentiation of $ff(x)$.
  2. Rule out computing $ff(x)$ explicitly and differentiating: it is algebraically heavier and not what the examiner is rewarding.
  3. Commit to the chain rule identity $(f\circ f)'(x) = f'(f(x))\cdot f'(x)$ as the structural backbone.
  4. Bridge by checking the domain compatibility: confirm that for $x$ in the given interval, $f(x)$ also lies in that interval, so the sign statement about $f'$ applies to $f'(f(x))$ too.
  5. Multiply signs: $(-)(-)=+$ (or whichever combination applies), conclude monotonicity, and if a range is needed read off the limiting values at the endpoints.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Recall from previous part: state $f'(x)<0$ (or $>0$) on the interval $I$.
  2. Verify range containment: show $f(I)\subseteq I$, so $f(x)\in I$ whenever $x\in I$.
  3. Hence $f'(f(x))$ has the same sign as $f'$ on $I$.
  4. Apply chain rule: $(ff)'(x)=f'(f(x))\cdot f'(x)$, and multiply signs to get the sign of $(ff)'$.
  5. Conclude $ff$ is strictly increasing/decreasing on $I$.
  6. If asked for the range, evaluate or take limits of $ff$ at the endpoints of $I$ to read off the interval.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose $f'(x)<0$ for all $x>4$ and $f$ maps $(4,\infty)$ into $(5,\infty)\subset(4,\infty)$. For $x>4$, $f(x)>4$, so $f'(f(x))<0$. Then $(ff)'(x)=f'(f(x))\cdot f'(x)=(-)(-)>0$. Hence $ff$ is strictly increasing on $(4,\infty)$, and its range is $\bigl(\lim_{x\to 4^{+}}ff(x),\;\lim_{x\to\infty}ff(x)\bigr)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Both $f$ and $g$ increasing on $I$ with $g(I)\subseteq$ domain of $f$
$(f\circ g)'=f'(g(x))g'(x)=(+)(+)>0$, so $f\circ g$ is increasing.
One of $f,g$ increasing and the other decreasing
Product of signs is negative, so $f\circ g$ is decreasing on $I$.
Showing an inverse function is increasing
If $f$ is increasing then $f^{-1}$ is increasing; argue via $(f^{-1})'(y)=1/f'(f^{-1}(y))$ inheriting the sign of $f'$.
Iterated map $f^{(n)}=f\circ f\circ\cdots\circ f$ on an invariant interval
Sign of $(f^{(n)})'$ is the product of $n$ copies of $f'$ evaluated along the orbit; even number of negatives gives increasing, odd gives decreasing.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming 'decreasing ∘ decreasing = decreasing' by analogy with single functions, missing the double-negative.
  • Treating $f'(f(x))$ as $f'(x)$ and forgetting the inner evaluation step.
  • Reading 'deduce' as permission to start a fresh differentiation of an explicit $ff(x)$ formula.

Pitfalls

  • Not checking that $f(x)$ lies in the interval where $f'$'s sign was established, so the sign of $f'(f(x))$ is unjustified.
  • Confusing 'increasing' with 'positive' — a function can be increasing while taking negative values.
  • Forgetting strict vs. non-strict: if $f'(x)<0$ strictly, conclude $ff$ is strictly increasing, not merely non-decreasing.
  • When stating the range, mixing up which endpoint corresponds to which limit because monotonicity direction was misidentified.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL Pure (Core) chain rule and monotonicity from the sign of the derivative; the iterated-map variant is touched on lightly in Further Pure when discussing inverse-function differentiation.
