Strategic Atlas / Differential Equations / Integrating Factor Method
Differential Equations

Construct integrating factor μ = e^{∫P dx} and multiply through to collapse LHS into d/dx[μy]

Family: Integrating Factor Method Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

A first-order linear ODE has been arranged into standard form dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x), where P and Q are functions of x only. The equation is NOT separable (the y term is glued to dy/dx by addition, not multiplication) and the LHS is not already recognisable as the derivative of a product. Equivalently: you see a 'dy/dx plus something times y equals something else in x' shape, and direct integration would fail because of the lone Py term.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying every term by μ(x) = e^{∫P(x) dx} forces the LHS to satisfy μ·dy/dx + μP·y = μ·dy/dx + μ'·y, which is exactly d/dx[μy] by the product rule in reverse — because μ was constructed so that μ' = Pμ. This collapses two terms into a single exact derivative, so the equation becomes d/dx[μy] = μQ(x), which is directly integrable. The exponential form is the unique (up to scalar) solution of μ' = Pμ and is never zero, so multiplying by it preserves the equation's solution set.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the ODE is first-order and linear in y, in the form dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x), but the y term blocks direct integration.
  2. I rule out separation (variables don't separate because of the +P(x)y term) and rule out treating it as exact in (x,y) — those don't apply to this shape.
  3. I commit to the integrating factor method: I want a multiplier μ(x) so that μ(dy/dx) + μPy becomes d/dx[μy]. Comparing with the product rule, I need μ' = Pμ, i.e. dμ/μ = P dx, giving μ = e^{∫P dx}.
  4. Bridge to action: I compute ∫P(x) dx (no constant of integration needed — any nonzero scalar multiple of μ works), exponentiate, and simplify using e^{k ln|x|} = |x|^k whenever possible.
  5. Tactical choice: I keep μ in its simplest algebraic form (e.g. x^k rather than e^{k ln x}) to make the upcoming RHS integral tractable.
  6. I multiply EVERY term of the standard-form ODE by μ, then immediately rewrite the LHS as d/dx[μy] without re-expanding it — the whole point is to keep it collapsed.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Ensure the ODE is in standard form: dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x) (divide through by any coefficient of dy/dx first).
  2. Compute ∫P(x) dx, omitting the arbitrary constant.
  3. Form μ(x) = e^{∫P(x) dx} and simplify (e.g. e^{k ln|x|} = |x|^k, e^{ax} stays as is).
  4. Multiply every term of the ODE by μ(x): μ·dy/dx + μP(x)·y = μ·Q(x).
  5. Rewrite the LHS as d/dx[μ(x)·y], giving d/dx[μ(x)·y] = μ(x)·Q(x).
  6. Integrate both sides w.r.t. x: μ(x)·y = ∫μ(x)·Q(x) dx + C.
  7. Divide through by μ(x) to isolate y.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve dy/dx + (2/x)y = x. Here P = 2/x, so ∫P dx = 2 ln|x| and μ = e^{2 ln|x|} = x². Multiply through: x²·dy/dx + 2x·y = x³, i.e. d/dx[x²y] = x³. Integrate: x²y = x⁴/4 + C, so y = x²/4 + C/x².

Variants — Same move, different surface

ODE expressed as x in terms of y: dx/dy + P(y)x = Q(y).
Use μ(y) = e^{∫P(y) dy}; the method is identical with the roles of x and y swapped.
P(x) = k/x, giving μ = x^k (a power), or P(x) = constant a, giving μ = e^{ax}.
Simplify e^{k ln|x|} = |x|^k before multiplying — leaving it in exponential form makes the RHS integral much harder.
μ·Q(x) is a product like x·e^x or x²·sin x.
Use integration by parts on the RHS — a sub-strategy invoked after the IF step.
Coefficient of dy/dx is not 1 (e.g. x·dy/dx + 2y = x³).
Divide through by that coefficient FIRST to reach standard form before reading off P(x).

Common Misreadings

  • Reading P(x) off without first putting the equation in standard form (dy/dx coefficient = 1), giving the wrong P.
  • Reading P(x) with the wrong sign because the Py term was on the RHS and not moved over first.
  • Exponentiating P(x) itself rather than its integral, writing μ = e^{P(x)} instead of μ = e^{∫P(x) dx}.
  • Treating the equation as separable and skipping the IF entirely, which fails because of the additive Py term.

Pitfalls

  • Errors in computing ∫P dx (sign slips, missing factors) — these corrupt every subsequent step.
  • Including a constant of integration inside the exponent of μ — unnecessary and only adds a redundant scalar factor.
  • Failing to simplify e^{k ln|x|} to |x|^k, leaving μ in an unwieldy form that obstructs the RHS integration.
  • Forgetting to multiply Q(x) by μ on the RHS after multiplying the LHS.
  • Re-expanding the collapsed LHS d/dx[μy] back into μy' + μ'y instead of integrating it directly.
  • Forgetting the constant of integration C after integrating the RHS, or forgetting to divide through by μ at the end to isolate y.

A-level Scope Notes

This is the standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) integrating factor method for first-order linear ODEs; the formula μ = e^{∫P dx} is expected to be known and is not given in the formula booklet. Stay within ODEs where P and Q are functions of one variable only — do not generalise to multi-variable exact equations or to non-linear ODEs (e.g. Bernoulli equations require a substitution first).
