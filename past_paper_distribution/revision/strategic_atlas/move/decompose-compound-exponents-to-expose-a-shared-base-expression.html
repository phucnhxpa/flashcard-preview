Algebra & Functions

Decompose compound exponents to expose a shared base expression

Family: Index Law Decomposition Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

This move fires when an equation contains exponential terms whose exponents are linearly related — typically one term involves $a^{2x+c}$ (or $a^{kx+c}$) and another involves $a^x$, plus possibly a constant. The visual cue is a 'doubling' pattern in the exponents combined with an additive constant inside the exponent. Equivalently: you see two exponential terms with the same base where one exponent is (a multiple of) the other plus a constant, hinting at a hidden polynomial structure. Phrased differently — whenever an exponential equation 'looks quadratic in disguise' because of paired exponents like $2x+1$ and $x$, this restructuring is the right opening.

Motivation — Why this works

The move works because of two index laws: $a^{m+n} = a^m \cdot a^n$ (additive exponent law) and $a^{mn} = (a^m)^n$ (power-of-a-power law). Together they let you split $a^{2x+c}$ into $a^c \cdot (a^x)^2$, which expresses every term in the equation as a polynomial in the single quantity $a^x$. This is the essential precondition for the substitution $u = a^x$ that converts an apparently exotic exponential equation into a standard quadratic, unlocking the quadratic formula or factorisation. Without this restructuring, the terms appear unrelated and no standard technique applies.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the equation contains $2^{2x+1}$ and $2^x$ with the same base — the exponents $2x+1$ and $x$ are linearly related, suggesting a hidden quadratic structure in $2^x$.
  2. I rule out logarithms as a first move: taking logs of a sum of exponentials doesn't simplify (no $\log(A+B)$ identity), so direct log-attack fails here.
  3. I commit to index-law decomposition because if I can rewrite the highest-exponent term purely in terms of $2^x$, every term becomes algebraic in one quantity and I can substitute $u = 2^x$.
  4. Bridge to action: I split the compound exponent. First peel off the additive constant via $2^{2x+1} = 2^{2x} \cdot 2^1$, then recognise $2^{2x} = (2^x)^2$.
  5. Tactical check: I keep the coefficient $2^1 = 2$ explicit so the leading term is $2(2^x)^2$, not $(2^x)^2$ — losing this factor is the most common slip.
  6. After restructuring, the equation has the form $A(2^x)^2 + B(2^x) + C = 0$, ready for the substitution $u = 2^x$ (with the constraint $u > 0$ to be applied when back-substituting).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the compound exponent: e.g. $2^{2x+1}$ with paired term $2^x$.
  2. Apply the additive exponent law: $2^{2x+1} = 2^{2x} \cdot 2^1 = 2 \cdot 2^{2x}$.
  3. Apply the power-of-a-power law in reverse: $2^{2x} = (2^x)^2$.
  4. Combine: $2^{2x+1} = 2(2^x)^2$.
  5. Rewrite the full equation in terms of $2^x$, e.g. $2(2^x)^2 - 7(2^x) + 6 = 0$, ready for the substitution $u = 2^x$.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $2^{2x+1} - 7 \cdot 2^x + 6 = 0$. Decompose: $2^{2x+1} = 2^1 \cdot 2^{2x} = 2(2^x)^2$. Equation becomes $2(2^x)^2 - 7(2^x) + 6 = 0$. Let $u = 2^x$: $2u^2 - 7u + 6 = 0 \Rightarrow (2u-3)(u-2) = 0 \Rightarrow u = \tfrac{3}{2}$ or $u = 2$. Hence $2^x = \tfrac{3}{2}$ or $2^x = 2$, giving $x = \log_2(3/2)$ or $x = 1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Different base with offset exponent, e.g. $3^{2x} - 4 \cdot 3^{x+1} + 27 = 0$
Rewrite $3^{x+1} = 3 \cdot 3^x$ and $3^{2x} = (3^x)^2$ — same index-law splitting, just applied to the middle term instead of the leading term.
Natural exponential with no offset, e.g. $e^{2x} + 5e^x - 6 = 0$
Restructuring is immediate: $e^{2x} = (e^x)^2$, no additive constant in the exponent to peel off.
Both terms carry offsets, e.g. $2^{2x+3} - 5 \cdot 2^{x+1} + 2 = 0$
Peel off both constants: $2^{2x+3} = 8(2^x)^2$ and $2^{x+1} = 2 \cdot 2^x$, then substitute.

Common Misreadings

  • Misreading $2^{2x+1}$ as $(2^{2x}) + 1$ or as $2^{2(x+1)} = 4^{x+1}$, leading to incorrect algebraic form.
  • Treating $2^{2x+1}$ as simply $(2^x)^2$ and losing the coefficient of $2$ from the $2^1$ factor.
  • Rewriting $2^{2x+1}$ as $4^x \cdot 2$ but then failing to connect $4^x$ back to $(2^x)^2$, stalling the substitution.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the coefficient $2$ from $2^1$ when splitting $2^{2x+1}$, writing the leading term as $(2^x)^2$ instead of $2(2^x)^2$.
  • After substitution $u = 2^x$, forgetting that $u > 0$ and accepting a negative root for $u$ as a valid solution.
  • Confusing $2^{2x}$ with $2 \cdot 2^x$ — the former is $(2^x)^2$, the latter is $2^{x+1}$.

A-level Scope Notes

This is core Edexcel IAL P2/P3 material on exponentials and indices, using only the laws of indices ($a^{m+n}=a^m a^n$ and $a^{mn}=(a^m)^n$) which are assumed knowledge (not in the formula booklet). Stay within real-valued exponentials with positive bases; do not generalise to complex exponents.
