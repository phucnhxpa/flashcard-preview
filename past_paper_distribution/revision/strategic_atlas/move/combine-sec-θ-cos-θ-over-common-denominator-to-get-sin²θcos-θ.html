Trigonometry

Combine sec θ − cos θ over common denominator to get sin²θ/cos θ

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an expression of the form sec θ − cos θ (or cosec θ − sin θ) appears and must be squared, integrated, or placed under a square root. Typical context: parametric arc-length or surface-area problems where dx/dθ comes out as a difference of reciprocal-and-direct trig functions. Cue: you see two unlike trig terms that resist direct squaring, but share an obvious common denominator. Also fires when the next step requires a single rational trig expression to factorise with another term.

Motivation — Why this works

Writing sec θ − cos θ = (1 − cos²θ)/cos θ and applying the Pythagorean identity 1 − cos²θ = sin²θ collapses the difference into the single quotient sin²θ/cos θ. Squaring then gives sin⁴θ/cos²θ, a form that shares the denominator cos²θ with companion terms (e.g. sin²θ rewritten as sin²θ·cos²θ/cos²θ), enabling clean factorisation under a square root. The principle is: convert differences of reciprocal trig functions into a single fraction so Pythagorean identities can act on the numerator.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the expression is a difference of sec θ and cos θ — unlike functions but with cos θ as a natural common denominator.
  2. Rule out squaring directly: sec²θ − 2 + cos²θ produces three terms with no obvious simplification path.
  3. Commit to the common-denominator route because the numerator 1 − cos²θ is a textbook Pythagorean target.
  4. First concrete operation: rewrite sec θ as 1/cos θ and combine into (1 − cos²θ)/cos θ.
  5. Replace 1 − cos²θ with sin²θ to land at sin²θ/cos θ; anticipate that squaring will give sin⁴θ/cos²θ which will pair cleanly with the dy/dθ term.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rewrite sec θ as 1/cos θ, giving 1/cos θ − cos θ.
  2. Combine over the common denominator cos θ: (1 − cos²θ)/cos θ.
  3. Apply the Pythagorean identity 1 − cos²θ = sin²θ to obtain sin²θ/cos θ.
  4. If squaring is the next step, write (sin²θ/cos θ)² = sin⁴θ/cos²θ ready for factorisation.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $x = \ln(\sec\theta) + \sin\theta\cos\theta$ so $\dfrac{dx}{d\theta} = \tan\theta + \cos 2\theta\cdot\ldots$ — for the cleaner case $\dfrac{dx}{d\theta} = \sec\theta - \cos\theta$: \[\sec\theta - \cos\theta = \frac{1}{\cos\theta} - \cos\theta = \frac{1-\cos^2\theta}{\cos\theta} = \frac{\sin^2\theta}{\cos\theta}.\] Then $\left(\dfrac{dx}{d\theta}\right)^2 = \dfrac{\sin^4\theta}{\cos^2\theta}$, which combines with $\left(\dfrac{dy}{d\theta}\right)^2 = \sin^2\theta = \dfrac{\sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta}{\cos^2\theta}$ to give $\dfrac{\sin^2\theta(\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta)}{\cos^2\theta} = \dfrac{\sin^2\theta}{\cos^2\theta} = \tan^2\theta$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

cosec θ − sin θ appearing in an arc-length integrand
Combine over sin θ to get (1 − sin²θ)/sin θ = cos²θ/sin θ; square to cos⁴θ/sin²θ.
sec θ + cos θ (sum rather than difference)
Common denominator gives (1 + cos²θ)/cos θ; no Pythagorean collapse — leave as is or use 1 + cos²θ = 2 − sin²θ if useful.
tan θ − sin θ in surface-area problems
Factor sin θ first: sin θ(1/cos θ − 1) = sin θ(1 − cos θ)/cos θ; uses factor-out before identity rather than common-denominator alone.
cot θ − tan θ
Common denominator sin θ cos θ gives (cos²θ − sin²θ)/(sin θ cos θ) = 2cos 2θ/sin 2θ = 2cot 2θ via double-angle, not Pythagorean.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating sec θ − cos θ as already 'simple' and squaring term-by-term.
  • Recognising the Pythagorean identity but writing 1 − cos²θ = sin²θ in the wrong place (e.g. equating sec θ − cos θ to sin²θ directly without the /cos θ denominator).
  • Confusing this move with the factor-out variant (tan θ − sin θ = sin θ(sec θ − 1)).

Pitfalls

  • Squaring sec θ − cos θ directly to sec²θ − 2 + cos²θ and losing the simplification path.
  • Writing 1 − cos²θ as tan²θ instead of sin²θ.
  • Dropping the /cos θ when applying the identity, ending up with sin²θ alone.
  • Forgetting to square the denominator: (sin²θ/cos θ)² = sin⁴θ/cos²θ, not sin⁴θ/cos θ.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the Pythagorean identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 and the definition sec θ = 1/cos θ, both core to Edexcel IAL P3/P4 and FP topics; appears naturally in parametric arc-length and surface-area integrals within the syllabus.
