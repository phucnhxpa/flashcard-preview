Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a trig equation involves a compound (linear) argument such as sin(2x), cos(2x+10°), tan(2x+π/4), or sin(3x−20°) — i.e. the variable appears as ax+b inside the trig function rather than as plain x. The cue is doubly strong when (i) the question gives a domain in x but the natural solving machinery (general solution / CAST / unit circle) operates on the raw argument, and (ii) the coefficient of x is greater than 1, so the function completes more periods than a naive [0,2π] sweep would suggest. Equivalent phrasings: 'multiple-angle equation', 'shifted-argument equation', 'argument is a linear function of x'. Also fires when an earlier part of the question gives a factorised result in sin θ / cos θ and a later part replaces θ by an expression in x.

Motivation — Why this works

Letting u = ax+b reduces the equation to the standard form sin u = k (or cos u = k, tan u = k), for which the general solution is known and the CAST/quadrant analysis is routine. The substitution decouples two distinct tasks: (1) solving the trigonometric equation in u, and (2) the algebraic back-substitution x = (u−b)/a. The crucial mathematical content is the linear change of variable: because u is a linear function of x, the x-domain must be stretched by |a| and shifted by b to give the correct u-domain — and this expanded u-range is precisely what guarantees all solutions are captured. Skipping this domain transformation is the single most common source of lost solutions in multiple-angle problems.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the trig function's argument is not just x but a linear expression ax+b (e.g. 2x, 2x+10°, 2x+π/4, 3x−20°), so the equation is not in the standard sin/cos/tan-of-x form.
  2. Rule out trying to solve directly in x: tracking the shift and the multiplied period at every CAST step is error-prone, and rule out applying the general solution formula naively to x — it must be applied to the raw argument.
  3. Commit to the substitution u = ax+b because it converts the problem to the canonical form sin u = k, whose solution set is fully tabulated.
  4. Bridge to first concrete step: compute the new u-domain by substituting the x-endpoints into u = ax+b. Because |a|>1 the u-interval is wider than the x-interval, signalling that more solutions will appear than in a single-period problem.
  5. Tactical check: count expected solutions by dividing the u-range width by the period (2π for sin/cos, π for tan) and multiplying by the number of solutions per period — this acts as a sanity check on the final answer count.
  6. Finally, plan the back-substitution x = (u−b)/a in advance, so each valid u is converted to x at the end without re-deriving the inverse map.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the compound argument ax+b inside the trig function and let u = ax+b.
  2. Transform the domain: if α < x < β then α' < u < β' where α' = aα+b and β' = aβ+b (reverse the inequality if a<0).
  3. Rewrite the equation in u, e.g. sin u = k, cos u = k, tan u = k, or a polynomial in sin u / cos u.
  4. Solve for all u in the new (wider) u-domain using the general solution and CAST/quadrant analysis.
  5. Back-substitute x = (u − b)/a for each valid u to obtain the x-solutions.
  6. Count and cross-check: roughly (u-range width)/(period) × (solutions per period) should match the answer count.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\cos(2x+10°) = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$ for $0 \le x < 90°$. Let $u = 2x+10°$; the domain becomes $10° \le u < 190°$. Solve $\cos u = 1/\sqrt{3}$: principal $u \approx 54.7°$, and the second-quadrant partner $360°-54.7° = 305.3°$ lies outside $[10°,190°)$, so only $u \approx 54.7°$ is valid. Back-substitute: $x = (u-10°)/2 \approx 22.4°$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Argument is 2x with a domain in radians (e.g. solving sin(2x) = p on 0 < x < π)
Let θ = 2x; double the domain to 0 < θ < 2π. After solving, halve each θ-solution to recover x.
Argument has a coefficient > 2, e.g. sin(3x − 20°) = k on 0° ≤ x ≤ 360°
Let u = 3x − 20°; the u-domain becomes −20° ≤ u ≤ 1060°, spanning nearly three full periods, so expect ~6 solutions.
Earlier part gives a factorised cubic in sin θ; later part sets θ = 2x
Substitute y = 2x to recover sin y, double the x-range to get the y-range, then solve each factor in y before halving back.
Argument involves tan with a π-shift, e.g. tan(2x + π/4) = k on −2π < x < 2π
Let u = 2x + π/4; u-domain becomes (−15π/4, 17π/4), spanning 8π — expect ≈8 solutions for tan (period π).

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the compound argument as if it were plain x and applying the general solution directly to x — produces wrong principal values.
  • Solving sin u = p in the original x-domain (e.g. 0 < u < 180° instead of 0 < u < 360°), thereby losing roughly half the solutions.
  • Assuming the number of solutions is the same as for a non-compound version of the equation.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to transform the domain at all — the most frequent error and almost guaranteed to lose solutions.
  • Arithmetic slip on the endpoints, especially with negative shifts or mixed-sign bounds (e.g. mishandling −2π·2 + π/4).
  • Reversing the inequality direction when the coefficient of x is negative.
  • Using ≤ instead of < (or vice versa) — important when an endpoint solution is at risk of being included/excluded incorrectly.
  • Forgetting to back-substitute, leaving answers in u rather than x.
  • Halving when one should double, or vice versa, when transforming the domain.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P2/P3) scope; uses only the general solutions of sin/cos/tan equations from the formula booklet and a linear change of variable. Do not generalise to non-linear arguments (e.g. sin(x²)) — those are out of scope and the simple domain-stretch logic fails.
