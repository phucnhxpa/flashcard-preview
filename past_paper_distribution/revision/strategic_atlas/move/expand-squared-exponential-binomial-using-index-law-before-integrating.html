Integration

Expand squared exponential binomial using index law before integrating

Family: Integrand Preparation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an integrand contains a squared binomial of the form $(e^{kx} \pm c)^2$ or similar, typically arising from volume of revolution $V = \pi \int y^2\,dx$ where $y$ is a two-term expression involving an exponential. Cue phrases include 'volume generated', 'rotated about the x-axis', or any integral where $y^2$ must be computed before integrating. The move also fires whenever a product like $(e^{ax}+b)(e^{cx}+d)$ appears under an integral sign.

Motivation — Why this works

Expanding via $(A-B)^2 = A^2 - 2AB + B^2$ and then applying the index law $(e^{kx})^2 = e^{2kx}$ converts the squared exponential into a sum of standard integrable terms: pure exponentials and constants. Each resulting term has a known antiderivative ($\int e^{mx}\,dx = \tfrac{1}{m}e^{mx}+C$), avoiding the need for substitution or integration by parts. The expansion is reversible algebra and the index law is exact, so no approximation is introduced.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the integrand requires $y^2$ where $y$ is a two-term exponential-minus-constant expression.
  2. I rule out substitution $u = e^{0.5x}$ because it would still leave a squared binomial in $u$ to expand — same work, extra notation.
  3. I commit to direct expansion because each expanded term will be a standard exponential or constant, both trivially integrable.
  4. I write $(A-B)^2 = A^2 - 2AB + B^2$ with $A = e^{0.5x}$, $B = 2$, generating three terms.
  5. I apply the index law carefully: $(e^{0.5x})^2 = e^{2 \cdot 0.5x} = e^x$ — multiplying the exponent by 2, not squaring the exponent expression.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify $y^2 = (e^{0.5x} - 2)^2$ as a binomial square.
  2. Apply $(A-B)^2 = A^2 - 2AB + B^2$ to get $(e^{0.5x})^2 - 2(e^{0.5x})(2) + 4$.
  3. Apply index law: $(e^{0.5x})^2 = e^{2 \times 0.5x} = e^x$.
  4. Simplify the cross-term: $-2 \cdot e^{0.5x} \cdot 2 = -4e^{0.5x}$.
  5. Write the integrand in standard form: $y^2 = e^x - 4e^{0.5x} + 4$, ready for term-by-term integration.

Worked Micro-Example

Find $V = \pi \int_0^{\ln 4} (e^{0.5x} - 2)^2\,dx$.\\ Expand: $(e^{0.5x}-2)^2 = e^x - 4e^{0.5x} + 4$.\\ Integrate: $\int (e^x - 4e^{0.5x} + 4)\,dx = e^x - 8e^{0.5x} + 4x + C$.\\ Evaluate from $0$ to $\ln 4$: $(4 - 16 + 4\ln 4) - (1 - 8 + 0) = -5 + 4\ln 4$.\\ So $V = \pi(4\ln 4 - 5)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Sum of two exponentials, e.g. $y = e^{2x} + e^x$
Square gives $e^{4x} + 2e^{3x} + e^{2x}$; same expand-then-index-law pattern, all terms standard exponentials.
Product of two distinct exponential binomials, e.g. $(e^x + 1)(e^x - 3)$
Use FOIL instead of binomial square; index law gives $e^{2x} - 2e^x - 3$.
Squared logarithmic binomial, e.g. $y = \ln x - 1$
Expansion gives $(\ln x)^2 - 2\ln x + 1$; the $(\ln x)^2$ and $\ln x$ terms then require integration by parts — expansion alone is not sufficient.
Squared trig binomial, e.g. $y = \sin x + \cos x$
Expand to $\sin^2 x + 2\sin x\cos x + \cos^2 x = 1 + \sin 2x$ via Pythagorean and double-angle identities, not index law.

Common Misreadings

  • Writing $(e^{0.5x})^2 = e^{0.25x^2}$, confusing the index law (multiply exponent by 2) with squaring the exponent expression itself.
  • Treating $(e^{0.5x} - 2)^2$ as $e^x - 4$, omitting the cross-term entirely (analogous to the classic $(a-b)^2 \ne a^2 - b^2$ error).
  • Pulling the $\pi$ inside the squaring, e.g. squaring $\pi y$ instead of $y$.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the cross-term coefficient: $-2 \times e^{0.5x} \times 2 = -4e^{0.5x}$, not $-2e^{0.5x}$.
  • Sign error on the constant: $(-2)^2 = +4$, not $-4$.
  • Integrating $e^{0.5x}$ as $e^{0.5x}$ instead of $2e^{0.5x}$ (forgetting to divide by $0.5$, i.e. multiply by $2$).
  • Dropping the leading $\pi$ from the volume formula after expansion.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P3/P4 volumes of revolution and integration of exponentials). Uses only the binomial square identity, the index law $(e^{kx})^2 = e^{2kx}$, and the standard result $\int e^{kx}\,dx = \tfrac{1}{k}e^{kx} + C$.
