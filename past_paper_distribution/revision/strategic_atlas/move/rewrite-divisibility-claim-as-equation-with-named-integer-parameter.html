Proof

Rewrite divisibility claim as equation with named integer parameter

Family: Divisibility Parameterisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in induction proofs of statements like 'prove $f(n)$ is divisible by $d$ for all positive integers $n$'. Cues include phrases such as 'divisible by 9', 'is a multiple of 7', or 'show $d \mid f(n)$'. Also fires whenever the inductive hypothesis must be carried forward into algebraic manipulation rather than left as a verbal claim. Applies equally if the expression mixes exponentials, polynomials, or factorials with integer coefficients.

Motivation — Why this works

A divisibility statement is qualitative and cannot be substituted or rearranged. Writing $f(k) = dm$ for some integer $m$ converts it into an equation, giving a named handle that can be moved around algebraically. This parameter $m$ is exactly what transmits the hypothesis through to the inductive step, where one must exhibit $f(k+1)$ as $d \times (\text{integer})$. Without the equation form, the inductive step has nothing concrete to substitute into.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the goal is divisibility by a fixed integer $d$, so an induction on $n$ is the natural framework.
  2. I rule out working purely with the statement '$d$ divides $f(k)$' — it cannot be substituted into $f(k+1)$.
  3. I commit to introducing a named integer $m$ via $f(k) = dm$, because the inductive step will require isolating part of $f(k+1)$ as $d \times (\text{integer})$.
  4. I write the hypothesis line explicitly with the qualifier 'for some integer $m$', so the parameter is well-defined.
  5. I decide whether to keep $f(k) = dm$ grouped (for the subtraction trick $f(k+1) - c f(k)$) or to rearrange it to isolate a troublesome term like $4^k = 9m - 6k + 1$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. State the inductive hypothesis as an equation: assume $f(k) = dm$ for some integer $m$.
  2. Decide on form: keep grouped, or rearrange to isolate the term that will appear in $f(k+1)$.
  3. Substitute this expression into $f(k+1)$ during the inductive step.
  4. After simplification, factor out $d$ and identify the bracketed quantity as an integer (using a fresh letter, e.g. $J$).

Worked Micro-Example

Prove $4^n + 6n - 1$ is divisible by $9$. Assume true for $n=k$: $4^k + 6k - 1 = 9m$ for some integer $m$. Rearrange: $4^k = 9m - 6k + 1$. Then $4^{k+1} + 6(k+1) - 1 = 4 \cdot 4^k + 6k + 5 = 4(9m - 6k + 1) + 6k + 5 = 36m - 18k + 9 = 9(4m - 2k + 1)$. Since $4m - 2k + 1 \in \mathbb{Z}$, the result holds for $n=k+1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Divisibility by a different constant $d$ (e.g. $3$, $5$, $7$, $8$)
Replace $9m$ by $dm$; the structure of the proof is identical.
Subtraction approach $f(k+1) - c\,f(k)$ to eliminate awkward terms
Keep the hypothesis grouped as $f(k) = dm$ rather than rearranging; substitute $f(k) = dm$ once at the end.
Matrix-power divisibility in Further Pure
Write $\mathbf{A}^k = d\mathbf{M} + \mathbf{B}$ where $\mathbf{M}$ has integer entries; same parameter idea, vector-valued.
Showing $f(n)$ is even/odd
Use $f(k) = 2m$ (or $2m+1$) with $m \in \mathbb{Z}$.

Common Misreadings

  • Writing $f(k) \equiv 0 \pmod{9}$ — mathematically valid but not the Edexcel-expected format and harder to manipulate algebraically at A-level.
  • Treating $m$ as a specific number (e.g. computing it for $n=1$) rather than an arbitrary integer carrying the hypothesis.
  • Reading the move as 'set $f(k) = 9$' instead of $f(k) = 9m$, losing the parameter entirely.

Pitfalls

  • Omitting 'for some integer $m$' — examiners penalise this because $9m$ is not otherwise guaranteed to be a multiple of $9$.
  • Reusing the letter $m$ for the integer appearing at $n=k+1$; a fresh letter (e.g. $J$) must be introduced and stated to be an integer.
  • Failing to verify that the final bracketed expression is genuinely an integer (e.g. forgetting that $k$ is a positive integer).
  • Rearranging incorrectly when isolating an exponential, e.g. sign errors in $4^k = 9m - 6k + 1$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure proof-by-induction syllabus; the named-integer parameterisation is the standard expected format and is preferred over modular-arithmetic notation at this level.
