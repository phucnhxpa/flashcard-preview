Strategic Atlas / Proof / Exponential Bridge
Proof

Split exponent via index laws to expose induction hypothesis term

Family: Exponential Bridge Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in the inductive step when P(k+1) contains an exponential like a^{f(k+1)} and the hypothesis P(k) contains a^{f(k)}, with f(k+1) − f(k) a small constant d. Concrete cue: you see 3^{2k+4} in P(k+1) and 3^{2k+2} in your hypothesis, differing by exactly 2 in the exponent. Equivalent phrasings: 'pull out a^d', 'rewrite the new exponent in terms of the old', 'extract the multiplier so the hypothesis substitutes in'. Whenever divisibility or closed-form induction features a fixed-base exponential, this is the default first move.

Motivation — Why this works

Induction succeeds only if P(k+1) can be rewritten so that the hypothesis expression appears verbatim and can be replaced by its assumed form (e.g. 64M + 8k + 9). The exponential is the only term that grows non-linearly in k, so it is the natural bridge between the two statements. Index law a^{m+d} = a^d · a^m isolates the hypothesis term cleanly with a constant multiplier a^d. That multiplier then distributes across the assumed form, and residues modulo the target divisor combine with the remaining linear/constant terms to deliver the required factor.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the exponent in P(k+1) (here 2k+4) is just 2 more than the exponent in my hypothesis (2k+2), so the exponential pieces are linked by a constant factor.
  2. I rule out brute expansion via binomial theorem on (1+8)^{k+2} or subtracting a multiple of P(k) from P(k+1) — both work but are arithmetically heavier and obscure the structure.
  3. I commit to the index-law split because it produces the hypothesis term exactly and lets me substitute in one clean step.
  4. First concrete operation: write 3^{2k+4} = 3^2 · 3^{2k+2} = 9 · 3^{2k+2}, then replace 3^{2k+2} by 64M + 8k + 9.
  5. Inside the move I track the multiplier (9) carefully, distribute it across every term of the hypothesis form, and keep the remaining −8k − 17 from P(k+1) intact before collecting like terms and checking divisibility by 64.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write out P(k+1) explicitly, e.g. P(k+1) = 3^{2k+4} − 8k − 17.
  2. Identify the exponent gap d between P(k+1) and P(k); here d = 2.
  3. Apply the index law: a^{m+d} = a^d · a^m, giving 3^{2k+4} = 9 · 3^{2k+2}.
  4. Substitute the inductive hypothesis: 3^{2k+2} = 64M + 8k + 9.
  5. Expand: 9(64M + 8k + 9) − 8k − 17 = 576M + 72k + 81 − 8k − 17.
  6. Collect like terms: 576M + 64k + 64 = 64(9M + k + 1), explicitly a multiple of 64.

Worked Micro-Example

Assume $3^{2k+2} - 8k - 9 = 64M$ for some integer $M$. Then $3^{2(k+1)+2} - 8(k+1) - 9 = 3^{2k+4} - 8k - 17 = 9 \cdot 3^{2k+2} - 8k - 17$. Substituting gives $9(64M + 8k + 9) - 8k - 17 = 576M + 72k + 81 - 8k - 17 = 64(9M + k + 1)$, which is divisible by 64.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Divisibility proof with base 5 and exponent step 1, e.g. show 5^n + 3 is divisible by 4
Split 5^{k+1} = 5 · 5^k, then substitute 5^k from the hypothesis; multiplier becomes 5 instead of 9.
Statement 7^{2n} − 1 divisible by 48
Decompose 7^{2(k+1)} = 49 · 7^{2k} and substitute 7^{2k} = 48M + 1; multiplier 49 distributes.
Mixed exponential like 8^n − 2^n
Split each base separately: 8^{k+1} = 8 · 8^k and 2^{k+1} = 2 · 2^k, then substitute the hypothesis form.
Closed-form induction such as ∑ r·2^r = (n−1)2^{n+1} + 2
Use 2^{n+2} = 2 · 2^{n+1} to align with the hypothesis's exponential, then add the new term (k+1)·2^{k+1}.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the problem as requiring binomial expansion of (1+8)^{k+2} instead of recognising the hypothesis is already available for direct substitution.
  • Trying to subtract a multiple of P(k) from P(k+1) (a 'difference' approach) when forward substitution is simpler.
  • Misreading the exponent gap d, e.g. assuming d = 1 when the statement uses 2(k+1)+2 versus 2k+2 (gap is 2).

Pitfalls

  • Splitting the exponent incorrectly, e.g. 3^{2k+4} = 3^{2k} · 3^4 rather than 3^{2k+2} · 3^2, which fails to produce the hypothesis term.
  • Arithmetic slips when distributing the multiplier, especially 9 × 9 = 81 or sign errors on −8k − 17.
  • Forgetting to factor out the target divisor at the end (e.g. leaving 576M + 64k + 64 without writing it as 64(9M + k + 1)), so the conclusion is not explicit.
  • Substituting the hypothesis before extracting the multiplier, leading to confused expressions.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure proof-by-induction syllabus: divisibility statements with fixed-base exponentials, using index laws and integer arithmetic only. No modular arithmetic notation or non-syllabus tools required.
