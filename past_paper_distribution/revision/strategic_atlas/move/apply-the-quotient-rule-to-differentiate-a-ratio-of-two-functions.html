Strategic Atlas / Differentiation / Quotient Rule
Differentiation

Apply the quotient rule to differentiate a ratio of two functions

Family: Quotient Rule Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The function is presented explicitly as a fraction y = u/v (or N = u/v) where both numerator and denominator are differentiable expressions — typically trigonometric (e.g. (2+sin x)/(1+cos x)), exponential (e.g. 600e^{0.3t}/(2+e^{0.3t})), or polynomial. Strong visual cues: a printed target answer with v² in the denominator, or a 'show that' instruction asking for dy/dx in a specific fractional form. Equivalent phrasings: 'differentiate the quotient', 'find dN/dt for N = ...', 'show stationary points satisfy ...'. The presence of a squared denominator in the target answer is a near-certain signature of the quotient rule.

Motivation — Why this works

The quotient rule (vu' − uv')/v² is purpose-built for ratios and lands directly on the canonical fraction form with v² in the denominator, matching the printed target exactly. The alternative — rewriting u/v as u·v^{-1} and using the product rule with the chain rule — is algebraically equivalent but produces a sum of two terms with negative powers of v that must then be recombined over a common denominator, doubling the work. Keeping the expression as a single fraction also preserves the structure needed to spot cancellations (e.g. e^{0.6t} terms cancelling in logistic-type derivatives, or Pythagorean identities applying after expansion).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that y is written explicitly as one expression divided by another, and both pieces are individually easy to differentiate.
  2. Check the target form: a v² (or (denominator)²) appearing in the printed answer is the visual fingerprint that confirms the quotient rule is the intended route.
  3. Rule out alternatives: the product rule on u·v^{-1} works but creates an extra chain-rule layer and a sum to recombine; implicit/logarithmic differentiation is overkill for a clean rational expression.
  4. Commit to the quotient rule. Carefully label u and v (numerator and denominator — getting these the wrong way round is a classic sign error).
  5. Differentiate u and v separately as a clean side-calculation, applying the chain rule for compound exponents (e.g. d/dt(e^{0.3t}) = 0.3e^{0.3t}).
  6. Assemble (v u' − u v')/v² in that order — the minus sign is non-commutative, so vu' must come first.
  7. Expand the numerator fully (don't leave it factored) to expose cancellations: trig identities like sin² + cos² = 1, or matching exponential terms that subtract to zero.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify u = numerator, v = denominator.
  2. Compute u' = du/dx and v' = dv/dx as a side calculation, applying the chain rule where needed.
  3. Write dy/dx = (v·u' − u·v') / v² in exactly that order.
  4. Expand the numerator term by term — do not simplify the v² in the denominator.
  5. Collect like terms in the numerator; look actively for cancellations (matching exponentials, Pythagorean identities, common factors).
  6. Factor any remaining common factor out of the numerator to reach the printed target form.

Worked Micro-Example

Differentiate $N = \dfrac{600e^{0.3t}}{2+e^{0.3t}}$. Set $u=600e^{0.3t}$, $v=2+e^{0.3t}$, so $u'=180e^{0.3t}$, $v'=0.3e^{0.3t}$. Then $\dfrac{dN}{dt}=\dfrac{180e^{0.3t}(2+e^{0.3t})-600e^{0.3t}\cdot 0.3e^{0.3t}}{(2+e^{0.3t})^2}=\dfrac{360e^{0.3t}+180e^{0.6t}-180e^{0.6t}}{(2+e^{0.3t})^2}=\dfrac{360e^{0.3t}}{(2+e^{0.3t})^2}.$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Rational trigonometric function, e.g. y = (2+sin x)/(1+cos x)
Same template: u'=cos x, v'=−sin x. Watch the double negative when v' is negative; expand fully so identities like sin²+cos²=1 can collapse the numerator.
Exponential-over-exponential of logistic type, e.g. y = ae^{kx}/(b+ce^{kx})
The cross-term e^{2kx} cancels generically in the numerator — expect and exploit this; the answer simplifies to a single exponential over the squared denominator.
Mixed polynomial/exponential, e.g. y = e^x/(x²+1)
Same quotient rule; numerator becomes e^x(x²+1) − e^x(2x) = e^x(x²−2x+1) = e^x(x−1)², so factor after expanding.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating a quotient as a product (u·v^{-1}) and applying product rule — works but creates extra algebra to reach the printed form.
  • Attempting implicit or logarithmic differentiation on a simple explicit quotient — unnecessarily heavy.
  • Misreading the target's squared denominator as a typo and trying to 'simplify' v² away before differentiating.

Pitfalls

  • Swapping the order in the numerator: writing (uv' − vu') instead of (vu' − uv') gives a sign-flipped wrong answer.
  • Forgetting the chain-rule multiplier when differentiating e^{kx} (writing e^{0.3t} instead of 0.3e^{0.3t}).
  • Sign slip on dv/dx when v contains cos x (forgetting the minus), then mishandling the double negative inside the quotient rule.
  • Leaving the numerator partially factored, which hides the cancellation (e.g. matching e^{0.6t} terms or sin²+cos²=1).
  • Expanding or simplifying the v² denominator unnecessarily — the printed answer typically wants it left as (…)².

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P3/P4) — the quotient rule is given in the formula booklet as d/dx(u/v) = (vu' − uv')/v². Standard derivatives of e^{kx}, sin x, cos x are also in the booklet. No need to invoke higher-level results such as logarithmic differentiation of products of many factors.
