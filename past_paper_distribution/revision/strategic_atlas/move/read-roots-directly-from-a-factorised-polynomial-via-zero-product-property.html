Algebra & Functions

Read roots directly from a factorised polynomial via zero product property

Family: Root Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a polynomial equation $f(x)=0$ is presented (or has just been written) as a product of linear or low-degree factors, e.g. $(2x+5)(x-3)^2=0$ or $(x-a)(x-b)(x-c)=0$. Typical cues: the question says 'hence find the roots/zeros', 'state the values of $x$ for which $f(x)=0$', or asks where a curve meets the $x$-axis after factorising. Also fires after a successful factor-theorem extraction has reduced the polynomial to factored form.

Motivation — Why this works

Over the real (or complex) numbers, a product equals zero iff at least one factor is zero — this is the zero product property, valid because $\mathbb{R}$ has no zero divisors. So solving $\prod_i g_i(x)=0$ reduces to solving each $g_i(x)=0$ separately. For linear factors this is instantaneous; for repeated factors it just records the root with its multiplicity. No expansion, formula, or numerical work is required.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation is already a product of factors set equal to zero — no algebraic manipulation is needed before extracting roots.
  2. Rule out expanding to a polynomial in standard form and applying the quadratic/cubic formula or trial roots — that destroys the structure already given.
  3. Commit to the zero product property: solve each factor $=0$ in turn, since the product vanishes iff some factor vanishes.
  4. Translate each factor into a simple linear equation; for a repeated factor $(x-a)^k$ just record $x=a$ (with multiplicity $k$ if asked).
  5. Decide on presentation: list distinct roots if the question asks for 'values of $x$', or list with multiplicity if it asks about the factorised structure / sketch behaviour.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify each distinct factor in the product equal to zero.
  2. Set each factor equal to 0 in turn, e.g. $2x+5=0$ and $(x-3)^2=0$.
  3. Solve each linear equation: $x=-\tfrac{5}{2}$ and $x=3$.
  4. Note the multiplicity of any repeated factor (here $x=3$ has multiplicity 2).
  5. State the solution set, matching the form requested (distinct roots vs roots with multiplicity).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $(2x+5)(x-3)^2 = 0$.\\ By the zero product property: $2x+5=0$ or $(x-3)^2=0$.\\ From $2x+5=0$: $x=-\tfrac{5}{2}$.\\ From $(x-3)^2=0$: $x=3$ (repeated root).\\ Hence $x=-\tfrac{5}{2}$ or $x=3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quartic in fully factorised form, e.g. $(x-1)^2(x+2)(3x-4)=0$.
Set each distinct factor to zero: $x=1$ (double), $x=-2$, $x=\tfrac{4}{3}$.
Factor is an irreducible quadratic over $\mathbb{R}$, e.g. $(x^2+1)(x-2)=0$.
The factor $x^2+1=0$ contributes no real roots (only $x=\pm i$ in FP scope); only $x=2$ is real.
Equation arises after factor theorem, e.g. $(x-2)(x^2-x-6)=0$ then refactor to $(x-2)(x-3)(x+2)=0$.
First fully factorise the quadratic factor, then apply the zero product property to all three linear factors.
Curve sketching: finding $x$-axis intercepts from $y=(x+1)(x-2)^2$.
Same move; multiplicities now inform shape — odd multiplicity crosses the axis, even multiplicity touches it.
Trigonometric/exponential product, e.g. $\sin x \,(2\cos x-1)=0$.
Same principle: solve $\sin x=0$ and $2\cos x-1=0$ separately within the given range.

Common Misreadings

  • Thinking the product must be expanded before roots can be found.
  • Believing a squared factor $(x-a)^2$ does not give a root because the curve only touches the $x$-axis.
  • Confusing the constant inside a factor: reading $(2x+5)=0$ as $x=5/2$ or $x=-5$ instead of $x=-5/2$.
  • Treating $(x+a)(x+b)=c$ (with $c\ne 0$) as if zero product property applies — it only applies when the product equals zero.

Pitfalls

  • Listing the repeated root twice when the question asks for 'the values of $x$' (distinct roots only).
  • Omitting the repeated root entirely, thinking it is not a 'real' solution.
  • Sign slips when solving $ax+b=0$, especially with negative $b$.
  • Forgetting to check that all factors have been considered when more than two are present.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1–P4): the zero product property underpins all root-finding from factorised cubics/quartics, and links to multiplicity for curve sketching. Complex roots from irreducible quadratic factors are treated in FP1.
