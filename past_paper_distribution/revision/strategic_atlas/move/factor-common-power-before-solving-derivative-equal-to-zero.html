Differentiation

Factor common power before solving derivative equal to zero

Family: Factor Before Solving Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires after applying the product rule (or sum rule) to expressions of the form $f(x)\cdot g(x)^n$, where the resulting derivative has two or more terms sharing a common power $g(x)^{n-1}$. Strong cue: the question says 'show that' a specific stationary point exists, signalling that the answer should drop out cleanly from a factored form. Also fires whenever expanding would produce a polynomial of degree $\geq 3$ that is not obviously factorable. Recognise the cue: every term contains $(x-a)^k$ for some $k\geq 1$.

Motivation — Why this works

Setting a product equal to zero gives independent linear/simple equations via the zero-product principle, but only if the expression is in factored form. Expanding first destroys this structure and forces synthetic division or rational root searching on a high-degree polynomial. Pulling out the lowest common power preserves one factor as $g(x)^{n-1}=0$ (giving $x=a$) and reduces the remaining bracket to a linear or quadratic that solves directly. This is the form examiners expect in 'show that' stationary point questions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that after differentiating, both terms contain a power of the same bracket $(x-a)$ — there is shared structure to exploit.
  2. Rule out expanding the bracket fully: that would yield a quartic/cubic and lose the clean factor giving $x=a$.
  3. Commit to factoring because the question is 'show that' a specific value works — factored form delivers that value as one of two factors immediately.
  4. Bridge: identify the lowest power of $(x-a)$ across all terms and the numerical GCF of the coefficients; this is the common factor to extract.
  5. Inside the move, divide each term carefully by the common factor, then simplify the remaining bracket to a linear/quadratic and apply the zero-product principle.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Apply product rule (or chain/sum rule) and write $\frac{dy}{dx}$ as a sum of terms.
  2. Identify the GCF: lowest power of the repeated bracket and numerical GCF of coefficients.
  3. Factor it out, dividing each term by the GCF and writing the leftover inside square brackets.
  4. Simplify the bracket by collecting like terms (watch signs).
  5. Set $\frac{dy}{dx}=0$ and apply zero-product: each factor equals zero gives a candidate $x$.
  6. Solve each linear/quadratic factor for the stationary $x$-values.

Worked Micro-Example

For $y=2x(x-3)^4$: $\frac{dy}{dx}=2(x-3)^4+8x(x-3)^3$. Factor: $2(x-3)^3[(x-3)+4x]=2(x-3)^3(5x-3)$. Setting $=0$: $x=3$ or $x=\tfrac{3}{5}$ — the 'show that' value drops out instantly without expansion.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Product rule on $y=x^m(ax+b)^n$
Common factor is $x^{m-1}(ax+b)^{n-1}$; the leftover bracket is linear in $x$.
Quotient rule numerator equal to zero
Only the numerator matters for stationary points; factor the numerator the same way and ignore the denominator (after checking it is non-zero).
Implicit differentiation yielding $\frac{dy}{dx}$ as a ratio
Set numerator $=0$, factor common terms in $x$ and $y$ before solving simultaneously with the original curve.
Chain rule on $y=[f(x)]^n$ alone
Derivative is $n[f(x)]^{n-1}f'(x)$; already factored — just set $f(x)=0$ or $f'(x)=0$.
Trigonometric derivative like $y=\sin^n x \cos x$
Factor out $\sin^{n-1}x$ (or $\cos x$); remaining bracket reduces via Pythagorean identity to a solvable trig equation.

Common Misreadings

  • Believing expansion is required because the expression 'looks like a polynomial', then attempting to factor a quartic from scratch.
  • Treating the two product-rule terms as unrelated and solving each separately, missing the common factor entirely.
  • Assuming the lower power of $(x-a)$ is not a valid factor because one term has a higher power.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the numerical GCF (e.g. extracting $(x-3)^3$ but not the $2$), giving an incorrect leftover bracket.
  • Sign error when simplifying the inner bracket, e.g. writing $4x+(x-3)=5x+3$ instead of $5x-3$.
  • Dropping a factor: writing $\frac{8x(x-3)^3}{2(x-3)^3}=4$ instead of $4x$.
  • Forgetting to also report $x=a$ from the $(x-a)^{n-1}=0$ factor when listing all stationary points.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P2/P3 differentiation technique; combines product/chain rule with factoring and the zero-product principle, all within core A-level scope.
