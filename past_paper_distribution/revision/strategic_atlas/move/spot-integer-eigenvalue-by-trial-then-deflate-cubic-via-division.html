Matrices

Spot integer eigenvalue by trial then deflate cubic via division

Family: Root Spotting Then Deflation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the characteristic equation \(\det(A-\lambda I)=0\) expands to a cubic in \(\lambda\) with no obvious factorisation. Edexcel FP3/FM eigenvalue problems are engineered so that at least one eigenvalue is a small integer (typically in \(\{0,\pm 1,\pm 2,\pm 3\}\)) dividing the constant term. Cues include a 3×3 matrix with small integer entries and a cubic whose coefficients are modest. Also fires when the question asks to 'show that \(\lambda=k\) is an eigenvalue' — a hint that \(k\) is a root to deflate by.

Motivation — Why this works

By the Rational Root Theorem, any rational root of a monic (or leading-coefficient \(\pm 1\)) cubic with integer coefficients must divide the constant term. Once one root \(\lambda_1\) is verified, the factor theorem guarantees \((\lambda-\lambda_1)\) divides the cubic exactly, leaving a quadratic quotient that is solvable by the formula or inspection. This converts a hard cubic into a trivial linear search plus a routine quadratic — exactly the structure A-level expects.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Student notices the characteristic polynomial is a genuine cubic with no common \(\lambda\) factor and no immediate grouping.
  2. They rule out the cubic formula (out of A-level scope) and random algebraic manipulation as inefficient.
  3. They commit to root-spotting because the constant term is small and integer roots are likely by exam design.
  4. Bridge: list the divisors of the constant term as candidate roots and prepare to substitute.
  5. Tactical decision: after finding \(\lambda_1\), choose between synthetic division, long division, or comparing coefficients to extract the quadratic factor — pick whichever minimises sign errors.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the characteristic cubic in standard form, e.g. \(\lambda^3 + p\lambda^2 + q\lambda + r = 0\) (multiply by \(-1\) if needed).
  2. List divisors of the constant term \(r\) as candidate integer roots.
  3. Substitute candidates \(\lambda = \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots\) until one yields zero — call it \(\lambda_1\).
  4. Divide the cubic by \((\lambda - \lambda_1)\) using long division, synthetic division, or coefficient comparison to obtain a quadratic \(\lambda^2 + s\lambda + t\).
  5. Solve the quadratic by factorisation or the quadratic formula to obtain \(\lambda_2, \lambda_3\).
  6. State all three eigenvalues.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose \(\det(A-\lambda I) = -\lambda^3 + 2\lambda^2 + 5\lambda - 6 = 0\), i.e. \(\lambda^3 - 2\lambda^2 - 5\lambda + 6 = 0\). Test \(\lambda=1\): \(1-2-5+6 = 0\) ✓. Divide: \(\lambda^3 - 2\lambda^2 - 5\lambda + 6 = (\lambda-1)(\lambda^2 - \lambda - 6)\). Factor the quadratic: \((\lambda-3)(\lambda+2)\). Eigenvalues: \(\lambda = 1, 3, -2\).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cubic has zero constant term
Factor \(\lambda\) out immediately; one eigenvalue is \(0\) and the remaining quadratic is solved directly.
Question states 'show \(\lambda=k\) is an eigenvalue'
Verify by substitution, then deflate by \((\lambda-k)\) without searching.
Symmetric matrix with a known repeated eigenvalue
After spotting one root, expect the quadratic factor to be a perfect square; check the discriminant.
Cubic factors by grouping
Try grouping pairs of terms before trial substitution — sometimes \(\lambda^2(\lambda-a) - b(\lambda-a)\) appears.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the cubic as needing the cubic formula or numerical methods rather than recognising the exam-design hint of an integer root.
  • Testing values not dividing the constant term (e.g. \(\lambda=4\) when the constant is 6) and wasting time.
  • Confusing the characteristic polynomial sign convention and listing divisors of the wrong constant.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors during synthetic or long division, especially when the cubic has a leading \(-\lambda^3\) term — multiply through by \(-1\) first.
  • Forgetting to check whether the discriminant of the quadratic factor is zero, which would indicate a repeated eigenvalue.
  • Stopping after finding one eigenvalue and not stating all three.
  • Arithmetic slips when expanding \(\det(A-\lambda I)\) before the trial — verify the cubic carefully first.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP3) eigenvalue work for 3×3 matrices; uses only the factor theorem, polynomial division, and the quadratic formula — all core A-level techniques.
