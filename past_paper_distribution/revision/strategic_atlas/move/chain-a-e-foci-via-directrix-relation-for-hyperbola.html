Coordinate Geometry

Chain a → e → foci via directrix relation for hyperbola

Family: Chain Of Parameters Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in hyperbola problems where earlier parts have established the semi-major axis a and a relation linking e to a (typically from a directrix x = a/e or focus-directrix condition). The current part asks for the foci, eccentricity, or directrix as the final link in a cascade. Cue phrases include 'hence find the foci', 'deduce the eccentricity', or 'using your value of a'. The structure is clearly sequential rather than requiring fresh derivation.

Motivation — Why this works

Hyperbola parameters (a, b, e, foci ±ae, directrices ±a/e) are tightly linked by a²(e²−1) = b² and the directrix/focus formulas. Once any two are pinned down, the rest follow by single substitutions. Recognising the dependency chain avoids redundant algebra and reduces error risk. The exam rewards using the cheapest formula at each stage rather than reopening the conic equation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice that a is already determined and a relation between e and a has been derived earlier in the question.
  2. I rule out re-deriving e from b² = a²(e²−1) since the directrix relation gives e more directly.
  3. I commit to substituting a into the e-relation, then feeding e into the foci formula (±ae, 0).
  4. First concrete operation: plug a = 2 into e = 3a/4 to get a numerical e.
  5. Tactically, I check e > 1 (hyperbola condition) and optionally verify b² = a²(e²−1) against the given equation as a self-check.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Substitute the known value of a into the directrix-derived relation to obtain e.
  2. Check e > 1 to confirm the hyperbola condition.
  3. Compute ae.
  4. State foci as (±ae, 0) (or (0, ±ae) if the transverse axis is vertical).
  5. Optional: verify via b² = a²(e²−1) against the original equation.

Worked Micro-Example

Given a = 2 and the earlier relation e = \tfrac{3a}{4}, substitute: e = \tfrac{3 \times 2}{4} = \tfrac{3}{2} > 1. Then ae = 2 \times \tfrac{3}{2} = 3, so the foci are (\pm 3, 0). Check: b^{2} = a^{2}(e^{2}-1) = 4\left(\tfrac{9}{4}-1\right) = 5, matching the given equation \tfrac{x^{2}}{4} - \tfrac{y^{2}}{5} = 1.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Question gives foci and asks for the directrix
Run the chain in reverse: read ae from focus coordinates, find e from a²(e²−1) = b² or another relation, then compute directrix x = ±a/e.
Vertical transverse axis (\tfrac{y^{2}}{a^{2}} - \tfrac{x^{2}}{b^{2}} = 1)
Foci become (0, ±ae) and directrices y = ±a/e; the chain logic is unchanged.
Ellipse analogue with e < 1
Same chain a → e → foci (±ae, 0), but use b² = a²(1−e²) and check 0 < e < 1.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the directrix relation as if it gave a directly, skipping the substitution and pulling e from a wrong source.
  • Assuming the transverse axis is horizontal without checking which denominator is larger or which variable is positive in the standard form.
  • Reading 'foci' as 'directrices' (or vice versa) under time pressure.

Pitfalls

  • Confusing foci (±ae, 0) with directrices x = ±a/e — using the wrong formula at the final step.
  • Failing to verify e > 1 for a hyperbola (or 0 < e < 1 for an ellipse), missing a sign or arithmetic slip.
  • Arithmetic error in ae when e is fractional, e.g. writing ae = \tfrac{3}{2} instead of 3.
  • Forgetting to give both foci (±) or omitting the y-coordinate 0.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP1/FP3) treatment of conics: standard hyperbola \tfrac{x^{2}}{a^{2}} - \tfrac{y^{2}}{b^{2}} = 1 with foci (±ae, 0), directrices x = ±a/e, and b² = a²(e²−1).
