Algebra & Functions

Collect, factor, and divide to isolate dy/dx after implicit differentiation

Family: Linear Isolation Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires immediately after implicit differentiation of a polynomial (or polynomial-like) equation in x and y, when dy/dx appears linearly in two or more separate terms — for example terms like 4x·dy/dx and −4y·dy/dx — alongside other terms in x and y that do not contain dy/dx. Recognise the cue: the equation is first-degree in dy/dx and you need dy/dx as the subject. Equivalent phrasings: 'make dy/dx the subject', 'solve for dy/dx', 'rearrange the differentiated equation'. This is the standard algebraic endgame for every implicit differentiation question at IAL.

Motivation — Why this works

Because the original relation is polynomial in x and y, differentiating with the product/chain rule produces an equation that is linear (degree 1) in dy/dx. Treating dy/dx as a single unknown, you can collect like terms, factor it out, and divide — exactly as you would solve ax + b = 0 for x. This converts an implicit relation into an explicit rational expression dy/dx = f(x, y), which is what every follow-up part (gradient at a point, stationary points, tangent line) requires.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that after differentiation, dy/dx appears in two or more terms scattered across the equation, while other terms involve only x and y.
  2. Rule out further use of the chain/product rule (the differentiation step is finished) and rule out implicit-function shortcuts (not in IAL scope) — the remaining task is purely algebraic.
  3. Commit to linear isolation because dy/dx appears only to the first power: this is a one-unknown linear equation in dy/dx.
  4. Bridge to action: move every term containing dy/dx to one side of the equation and every term without it to the other, watching signs carefully.
  5. Factor dy/dx out of the LHS so the equation reads (coefficient in x, y)·dy/dx = (expression in x, y).
  6. Divide both sides by the coefficient, then simplify the resulting fraction (cancel common numerical factors, tidy signs) to present the cleanest form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Starting point (example): 2x + 4y + (4x − 4y)·dy/dx = 0.
  2. Rearrange so dy/dx terms are alone on one side: (4x − 4y)·dy/dx = −2x − 4y.
  3. Factor dy/dx if it appears in multiple terms: dy/dx·(4x − 4y) = −(2x + 4y).
  4. Divide both sides by the coefficient of dy/dx: dy/dx = (−2x − 4y)/(4x − 4y).
  5. Simplify: divide numerator and denominator by a common factor (here −2) to get dy/dx = (x + 2y)/(2y − 2x).

Worked Micro-Example

Differentiating $x^2 + 4xy - 2y^2 = c$ implicitly gives $2x + 4y + 4x\,\tfrac{dy}{dx} - 4y\,\tfrac{dy}{dx} = 0$. Group: $(4x - 4y)\tfrac{dy}{dx} = -2x - 4y$. Divide and simplify: $\tfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{-2x - 4y}{4x - 4y} = \dfrac{x + 2y}{2y - 2x}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Implicit equation involving exponentials or logarithms (e.g. e^{xy} terms)
After applying the chain/product rule the structure is still linear in dy/dx — collect, factor, and divide identically; only the coefficients become non-polynomial.
Parametric differentiation
No grouping needed: dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) directly — linear isolation is trivialised.
Second implicit derivative d²y/dx²
Differentiate the dy/dx expression again, then repeat the same collect-factor-divide procedure on d²y/dx², substituting the known dy/dx expression at the end.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the differentiated equation as still requiring more chain-rule work, instead of recognising it is now a linear algebra problem in dy/dx.
  • Trying to solve for y explicitly first — unnecessary and usually impossible.
  • Mistaking the equation as nonlinear in dy/dx because dy/dx appears more than once (it is still linear if each term has dy/dx to power 1).

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when moving terms across the equals sign — especially losing the negative on terms like −2x − 4y.
  • Failing to factor dy/dx cleanly when its coefficient itself contains a minus sign (e.g. writing (4x − 4y) as (4y − 4x) without flipping the RHS sign).
  • Leaving the answer unsimplified, which causes arithmetic errors in any follow-up substitution (e.g. evaluating the gradient at a given point).
  • Dividing by the coefficient without noting it could be zero (not usually penalised at IAL unless explicitly asked).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 (implicit differentiation) and FP scope. No formula booklet entry is invoked — this is pure algebraic manipulation. Avoid mentioning the implicit function theorem or partial derivatives, which are out of scope.
