Trigger — When to use

Two curves/lines are stated to meet at a specified x-value, and one of the equations contains an unknown constant (e.g. find $k$ such that $y = kx + 7$ meets $y = 6/(x-2)$ at $x = -4$). One equation is fully determined, the other carries the parameter. The task is to pin down the parameter using the shared intersection point. Phrasings include 'the line meets the curve at...', 'the graphs intersect when $x = a$', or 'given that $P(a, \cdot)$ lies on both'.

Motivation — Why this works

An intersection point lies on both graphs, so its coordinates satisfy both equations simultaneously. The equation without the unknown can be evaluated directly at the given x to produce a concrete y-coordinate. Substituting that complete (x, y) pair into the parameterised equation reduces it to a single linear (or low-degree) equation in the parameter. This is faster and less error-prone than setting the two expressions equal and carrying the unknown through the algebra.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Student notices: the x-coordinate of intersection is handed to them, and only one equation contains an unknown constant.
  2. Rules out: equating the two right-hand sides and solving as a simultaneous system in $x$ and $k$ — legal but introduces $k$ unnecessarily early.
  3. Commits to: evaluating the unknown-free equation at the given x first, because it instantly delivers a numerical y.
  4. Bridge: with both coordinates now known, the parameterised equation becomes a one-variable equation.
  5. Tactical decision: choose the equation with the fewest unknowns at each stage; never substitute into an equation that still contains two unknowns.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify which equation is fully determined (no unknown constants).
  2. Substitute the given x-value into that equation to compute y.
  3. Record the intersection point as a concrete coordinate pair $(x_0, y_0)$.
  4. Substitute $(x_0, y_0)$ into the equation containing the parameter.
  5. Solve the resulting linear (or simple) equation for the parameter.

Worked Micro-Example

Find $k$ given that $y = kx + 7$ meets $y = \dfrac{6}{x-2}$ at $x = -4$.\\ Curve: $y = \dfrac{6}{-4-2} = \dfrac{6}{-6} = -1$, so the point is $(-4, -1)$.\\ Line: $-1 = k(-4) + 7 \Rightarrow -4k = -8 \Rightarrow k = 2$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the problem as a full simultaneous-equations exercise and solving for $x$ as if it were unknown.
  • Assuming the given x-value also tells you y without evaluating either equation.
  • Confusing 'meets at $x = -4$' with 'meets the x-axis at $-4$' (i.e. $y = 0$).

Pitfalls

  • Substituting $x = x_0$ into the parameterised equation first — still leaves two unknowns ($k$ and $y$).
  • Sign errors when evaluating $6/(-4-2)$; mishandling the denominator.
  • Forgetting to subtract the constant when isolating $k$, e.g. writing $-1 = -4k + 7 \Rightarrow k = -1/(-4) + 7$.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure (P1/P2) coordinate-geometry technique; uses only substitution and linear-equation solving and applies routinely to line-curve intersection problems.
