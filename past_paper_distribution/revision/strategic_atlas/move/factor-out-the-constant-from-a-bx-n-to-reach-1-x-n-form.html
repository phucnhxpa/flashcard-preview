Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever you face a binomial (a+bx)^n with non-integer or negative n where the constant term inside the bracket is not 1 (e.g. (2−5x)^{-2}, (4+x)^{-1/2}, (9−2x)^{-1}, (8+3x)^{1/3}). The visual cue is a bracket of the form (a±bx)^n with a≠1 and n a fraction or negative integer. Equivalent phrasings: 'reduce (a+bx)^n to standard form', 'normalise the constant inside the bracket', 'prepare for the generalised binomial expansion'.

Motivation — Why this works

The generalised binomial theorem in the Edexcel formula booklet is stated only for (1+X)^n, valid for |X|<1. To use it on (a+bx)^n we must rewrite the bracket so its constant term is 1. Factoring a out gives a(1+(b/a)x), and the index law (ab)^n = a^n·b^n then peels the constant a^n outside as a fixed scalar multiplier, leaving the standard-form kernel (1+(b/a)x)^n ready for term-by-term expansion. This separates the problem cleanly into 'evaluate a^n' and 'expand (1+X)^n with X=(b/a)x'.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the bracket is (a+bx)^n with a≠1 and n non-integer or negative, so the formula booklet's (1+X)^n template does not apply directly.
  2. Rule out expanding as-is (no closed formula for (a+bx)^n with general n in the booklet) and rule out factoring out x (this would make the expansion variable large, violating the convergence condition |X|<1).
  3. Commit to factoring the constant a from inside the bracket because that is the only manipulation which produces a leading 1 inside while keeping the variable term small.
  4. Bridge to operations: write (a+bx) = a(1 + (b/a)x), then distribute the power using (PQ)^n = P^n Q^n to extract a^n as an external scalar.
  5. Tactical decision: evaluate a^n immediately as a clean number (e.g. 4^{-1/2}=1/2, 2^{-2}=1/4, 8^{1/3}=2) and combine it with any pre-existing scalar multiplier in the question before expanding, so the series multiplication at the end is as simple as possible.
  6. Identify n and X=(b/a)x explicitly, and note the convergence condition |bx/a|<1, i.e. |x|<|a/b|, for later use.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Factor the constant: (a+bx) = a\left(1 + \tfrac{b}{a}x\right), preserving the sign of b.
  2. Distribute the power via index law: (a+bx)^n = a^n\left(1 + \tfrac{b}{a}x\right)^n.
  3. Evaluate the scalar a^n as a simple number (e.g. 4^{-1/2}=\tfrac{1}{2}, 2^{-2}=\tfrac{1}{4}).
  4. Absorb a^n into any external scalar multiplier already present in the question.
  5. Identify n and X = \tfrac{b}{a}x for substitution into (1+X)^n = 1 + nX + \tfrac{n(n-1)}{2!}X^2 + \cdots.
  6. State the validity range |X|<1, i.e. |x|<\left|\tfrac{a}{b}\right|.

Worked Micro-Example

Expand (4+x)^{-1/2}. Factor: (4+x)^{-1/2} = \bigl(4(1+\tfrac{x}{4})\bigr)^{-1/2} = 4^{-1/2}(1+\tfrac{x}{4})^{-1/2} = \tfrac{1}{2}(1+\tfrac{x}{4})^{-1/2}. Now apply (1+X)^n with n=-\tfrac{1}{2}, X=\tfrac{x}{4}: \tfrac{1}{2}\left(1 - \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot\tfrac{x}{4} + \tfrac{(-1/2)(-3/2)}{2}\cdot\tfrac{x^2}{16} + \cdots\right) = \tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{x}{16} + \tfrac{3x^2}{256} - \cdots, valid for |x|<4.

Variants — Same move, different surface

For (3+2x)^{1/2}
Factor out 3: 3^{1/2}(1+\tfrac{2x}{3})^{1/2} = \sqrt{3}\,(1+\tfrac{2x}{3})^{1/2}; here the scalar is irrational but the technique is identical.
For (8+3x)^{1/3}
Factor out 8: 8^{1/3}(1+\tfrac{3x}{8})^{1/3} = 2(1+\tfrac{3x}{8})^{1/3}; the cube root resolves to a clean integer.
For (9-2x)^{-1} or (2-5x)^{-2} (negative coefficient on x)
Factor out the positive constant; the minus sign stays attached to the variable term, giving (1-\tfrac{2x}{9})^{-1} or (1-\tfrac{5x}{2})^{-2}. Then X is negative — track the sign carefully when substituting into the binomial series.
When the question already has an external scalar k(a+bx)^n
Combine k with a^n immediately: e.g. 8(2-5x)^{-2} = 8\cdot 2^{-2}(1-\tfrac{5x}{2})^{-2} = 2(1-\tfrac{5x}{2})^{-2} before expanding.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to factor out x (or the bx term) instead of the constant a, producing something like (x(a/x+b))^n — this puts a large quantity inside the bracket and breaks convergence.
  • Treating (a+bx)^n as if the booklet formula applies directly with X=a+bx-1 or similar ad hoc substitution, instead of normalising to a leading 1.
  • Factoring to make the x-coefficient equal to 1 (e.g. extracting b) rather than making the constant equal to 1.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to raise the factored constant to the power n — writing a(1+\tfrac{b}{a}x)^n instead of a^n(1+\tfrac{b}{a}x)^n. This loses the scalar on every term.
  • Mis-evaluating the fractional/negative power of the constant, e.g. writing 4^{-1/2}=2 instead of \tfrac{1}{2}, or 2^{-2}=\tfrac{1}{2} instead of \tfrac{1}{4}.
  • Sign error inside the bracket: writing (1+\tfrac{b}{a}x) when the original was (a-bx); the minus sign must be carried through so X = -\tfrac{b}{a}x.
  • Forgetting to multiply the final expanded series by the external scalar a^n at the end of the calculation.
  • Stating the validity range as |x|<1 (copying from the booklet) instead of the correct |x|<|a/b|.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P3/P4) using the generalised binomial expansion from the formula booklet, which is stated only for (1+X)^n with |X|<1. Do not attempt to expand around x=∞ or use complex-valued constants — these are out of scope.
