Decompose ln of a perfect power into integer multiple of ln of base

Family: Log Law Simplification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a final 'exact' answer contains ln N where N is a perfect power (e.g. ln 9, ln 8, ln 16, ln 27, ln 32) and a numerical coefficient sits in front of, or inside a fraction with, that logarithm. Cue phrases: 'give your answer in exact form', 'in the form a + b ln c where c is prime', or simply when the coefficient and the exponent share a common factor that allows cancellation. Also fires when a mark scheme box demands a simplified surd-style logarithmic answer.

Motivation — Why this works

By the power law, ln(a^k) = k ln a, so any composite argument that is a perfect power can be re-expressed in terms of its prime base. This often produces a coefficient that cancels with the existing fraction, yielding the simplest exact form. Edexcel IAL mark schemes consistently award the final accuracy mark only after such simplification, treating ln 9, ln 8 etc. as not fully simplified when a prime-base form is cleaner.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the answer contains ln 9 (or ln 8, ln 27, …), and 9 is not prime — it's 3².
  2. I rule out leaving it as ln 9: although technically exact, it usually masks a cancellation with the leading fraction and won't earn the final A mark.
  3. I commit to rewriting ln 9 = 2 ln 3 because the factor 2 will combine with the existing numerator/denominator.
  4. Bridge: substitute 2 ln 3 in place of ln 9 and re-collect the rational coefficient.
  5. Tactically, I check whether the resulting fraction reduces (e.g. 6/4 → 3/2) and whether a sign or bracket needs care before declaring the final form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the composite argument N and write N = p^k for a prime (or simplest) base p.
  2. Apply the power law: ln N = k ln p.
  3. Substitute back into the expression, multiplying k into the existing coefficient.
  4. Reduce the resulting rational coefficient to lowest terms.
  5. State the final answer in the requested form (e.g. a + b ln p).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $x = 1 - \dfrac{3 \ln 9}{4}$. Since $9 = 3^2$, $\ln 9 = 2\ln 3$. Hence $x = 1 - \dfrac{3 \cdot 2 \ln 3}{4} = 1 - \dfrac{6 \ln 3}{4} = 1 - \dfrac{3 \ln 3}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Answer contains ln 8 with coefficient /3 or /6
Use ln 8 = 3 ln 2; the 3 typically cancels the denominator, e.g. (ln 8)/3 = ln 2.
Answer contains ln 27 with coefficient /3
Use ln 27 = 3 ln 3 so (ln 27)/3 = ln 3.
Answer contains ln 16 or ln 32
Write as 4 ln 2 or 5 ln 2 respectively, then combine with the rational coefficient.
Argument is a reciprocal, e.g. ln(1/9)
Write ln(1/9) = -ln 9 = -2 ln 3, keeping careful track of the sign.
Argument is a product like ln 12
Decompose via ln(4·3) = 2 ln 2 + ln 3 only if the question asks for a form in ln 2 and ln 3; otherwise leave as ln 12.
log base 10 / log base a versions
Same power law: log_a(p^k) = k log_a p; e.g. log 100 = 2 in base 10 simplifies fully to a number.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating ln 9 as already 'exact enough' and stopping before the simplification mark.
  • Believing the rule only applies to natural logs; in fact the power law works for any base.
  • Trying to combine ln 9 with a non-logarithmic constant (e.g. writing 1 - ln 9 as ln(e/9) when not asked, obscuring rather than simplifying).

Pitfalls

  • Using the wrong exponent: writing ln 9 = 3 ln 3 (confusing 9 = 3² with 27 = 3³).
  • Arithmetic slip when reducing the coefficient (6/4 to 3/2).
  • Dropping a negative sign when the original term was subtracted.
  • Forgetting the bracket: writing 1 - 3·2 ln 3 / 4 as 1 - 6 ln 3 ÷ 4 incorrectly without the implicit grouping.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 logarithm content; uses only the power law ln(a^k) = k ln a and basic fraction simplification expected for exact-form answers.
