Vectors

Retain both ± roots when solving λ² = k for points on a line

Family: Geometric Sign Interpretation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a distance or magnitude condition on a parametrised line r = a + λd produces an equation of the form Aλ² = B (no linear term in λ). Typical cues: 'find the point(s) on line ℓ at distance d from P', or 'find points on ℓ such that |r − P| = k'. The squared parameter signals symmetry about a base point, and the geometric setup (an infinite line) places no sign restriction on λ.

Motivation — Why this works

A line r = a + λd extends infinitely in both directions, so points equidistant from a fixed reference along the line generally come in pairs — one on each side of the foot of perpendicular (or whichever base point a represents). Algebraically, λ² = k has roots λ = ±√k, and both roots correspond to genuinely distinct points in 3D. Dropping the negative root throws away half the solution set and forfeits marks.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the parameter equation reduces to λ² = constant with no linear term, signalling symmetry about the base point a.
  2. Rules out the assumption that λ must be non-negative — that only applies to ray/segment problems, not full lines.
  3. Commits to keeping both ± roots because the line is bidirectional and the question asks for 'points' (plural cue) or makes no sign restriction.
  4. Bridges to substitution: plug each λ value back into r = a + λd carefully, watching signs in every component.
  5. Presents both position vectors explicitly as the final answer, not just the λ values.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Reduce the distance/magnitude condition to the form Aλ² = B.
  2. Solve λ² = B/A and write λ = ±√(B/A) explicitly.
  3. Substitute λ = +√(B/A) into r = a + λd to get the first position vector.
  4. Substitute λ = −√(B/A) into r = a + λd to get the second position vector.
  5. State both position vectors as the required points.

Worked Micro-Example

Line $\mathbf{r} = (2,1,0) + \lambda(-5,4,3)$, find points at distance $\tfrac{2\sqrt{2}}{5}\sqrt{50}$ from $(2,1,0)$. Condition gives $50\lambda^2 = 8$, so $\lambda^2 = \tfrac{4}{25}$ and $\lambda = \pm\tfrac{2}{5}$. Substituting: $\lambda = \tfrac{2}{5}$ gives $(0, \tfrac{13}{5}, \tfrac{6}{5})$; $\lambda = -\tfrac{2}{5}$ gives $(4, -\tfrac{3}{5}, -\tfrac{6}{5})$. Both are valid answers.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Sphere–line intersection giving a quadratic in λ
Solve the full quadratic (linear term present); both roots correspond to the two intersection points and both must be reported.
2D circle–line intersection parametrised by λ
Same logic — quadratic in λ yields two intersection points; keep both unless the line is restricted to a segment.
Foot of perpendicular from P to ℓ extended to 'points on ℓ at given distance from foot'
After locating the foot, the symmetric condition reduces to λ² = k about the foot — keep both ± roots.
Ray or segment instead of full line
Apply the parameter range (e.g. 0 ≤ λ ≤ 1) and discard roots outside it — only here is sign restriction legitimate.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating λ as an unsigned 'distance' and discarding the negative root.
  • Assuming the two ± points coincide or are merely reflections of one another and reporting only one.
  • Reading 'find the point' (singular) literally when the geometry forces two answers — the question wording often still expects both.

Pitfalls

  • Sign slips when substituting negative λ into components, e.g. $-5 \times (-\tfrac{2}{5}) = +2$ mishandled.
  • Reporting λ values instead of full position vectors.
  • Forgetting to rationalise or simplify √(B/A) cleanly, leading to ugly coordinates and arithmetic errors.
  • Accidentally using a + |λ|d for both points instead of a ± λd.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Core Pure / FP vector geometry: parametrised lines in 3D, distance conditions, and quadratic-in-parameter problems. No content beyond syllabus.
