Differentiation

Read tangent form y = k as a zero-gradient condition

Family: Tangent Form Interpretation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks to show that the tangent to a curve at a specified point has equation of the form y = k, or equivalently that the tangent is horizontal/parallel to the x-axis. Also triggers on phrasings like 'show that the tangent at x = a is parallel to the x-axis' or 'find the value of k such that the tangent is y = k'. The word 'hence' following a part that produced f'(x) is a strong signal to reuse that derivative rather than re-derive it.

Motivation — Why this works

The line y = k has no x-term, so its gradient is identically zero. Since the tangent's gradient at x = a equals f'(a), the form y = k forces f'(a) = 0. Once that is verified, the constant k is just f(a), because the tangent passes through the point of contact. This reframes a 'show the form' task as two clean substitutions: one into f'(x) to confirm zero gradient, one into f(x) to pin down k.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that y = k is a horizontal line, so the tangent's slope must be 0.
  2. Rule out building a general tangent y - y₁ = m(x - x₁) with unknown m — the form already fixes m = 0.
  3. Commit to a two-step plan: verify f'(a) = 0, then compute k = f(a).
  4. Bridge to first operation: substitute the given x-value into the f'(x) found earlier (often from a previous part flagged by 'hence').
  5. Tactically, present the gradient check before stating k, so the 'show that' logic reads as: zero gradient ⇒ horizontal ⇒ y = f(a).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the x-value a at which the tangent is claimed to be y = k.
  2. Substitute x = a into f'(x) and show f'(a) = 0, justifying that the tangent is horizontal.
  3. Substitute x = a into f(x) to obtain k = f(a).
  4. State the tangent equation as y = k with the explicit numerical value.

Worked Micro-Example

Let f(x) = x^3 - 3x^2 + 4. Show the tangent at x = 2 has the form y = k. Compute f'(x) = 3x^2 - 6x, so f'(2) = 12 - 12 = 0, hence the tangent is horizontal. Then f(2) = 8 - 12 + 4 = 0, so k = 0 and the tangent is y = 0.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Tangent claimed to be of the form x = c (vertical tangent) on a parametric or implicit curve
Require dy/dx to be undefined at the point, typically by showing dx/dt = 0 (parametric) or the denominator of the implicit dy/dx vanishes while the numerator does not.
Tangent parallel to a given line y = mx + c
Set f'(a) = m instead of 0, then find the y-coordinate via f(a) and write the tangent in point-slope form.
Stationary point identification dressed as a tangent question
Same zero-gradient logic identifies a stationary point at x = a; classification (max/min/inflection) may then be required via f''(a) or a sign test.
Parametric curve with tangent of form y = k
Use dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) and require dy/dt = 0 (with dx/dt ≠ 0) at the parameter value, then evaluate y(t) for k.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating y = k as a generic tangent and trying to compute a non-zero gradient.
  • Stopping after showing f'(a) = 0 without computing k = f(a).
  • Confusing 'tangent is y = k' with 'curve has y-intercept k', and substituting x = 0 instead of x = a.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips when substituting fractional or surd x-values into f'(x) or f(x).
  • Failing to use the 'hence' instruction and re-deriving f'(x) wastes time and risks inconsistency.
  • Quoting k without showing the gradient-zero step, losing the 'show that' marks.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics: tangents from differentiation (P1/P2), with parametric and implicit variants in P3/P4. No tools beyond standard differentiation rules are needed.
