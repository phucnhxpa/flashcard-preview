Sequences & Series

Simplify recurrence algebraically before iterating to expose AP/GP structure

Family: Recurrence Simplification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a recurrence $a_{n+1}=f(a_n)$ is presented in an unsimplified form containing obvious algebraic redundancies — common factors that cancel (e.g. $2(a_n+3)/2$), like terms that combine, or brackets that expand to something cleaner. Cue phrases: 'a recurrence is defined by...' followed by a fraction, nested brackets, or mixed constants. Also fires whenever you are about to compute several terms by repeated substitution and the given form looks heavier than necessary.

Motivation — Why this works

Most IAL recurrences hide a standard structure ($a_{n+1}=a_n+d$, $a_{n+1}=ra_n$, or $a_{n+1}=ra_n+c$) behind cosmetic algebra. Simplifying first converts the problem into a recognised AP/GP/linear recurrence, so closed-form formulae and standard sums become available. It also prevents compounded arithmetic slips: each iteration on a clean form costs one addition rather than a fraction evaluation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the RHS contains a redundant structure — here a factor of $2$ in the numerator and denominator of $2(a_n+3)/2$.
  2. Rule out diving straight into substitution of $a_1$: that would carry the redundancy through every term and inflate error risk.
  3. Commit to algebraic simplification first because the cleaner form will (i) reveal whether the sequence is arithmetic/geometric and (ii) make every subsequent term trivial.
  4. Bridge: cancel the common factor, then collect constants to read off the standard form $a_{n+1}=a_n+d$ or $a_{n+1}=ra_n+c$.
  5. Tactical decision: once simplified, identify $d$ (or $r,c$) and only then begin iterating or applying $a_n=a_1+(n-1)d$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Inspect the RHS for cancellable factors, like terms, or expandable brackets.
  2. Perform the cancellation/expansion: e.g. $\dfrac{2(a_n+3)}{2}=a_n+3$.
  3. Combine constants: $a_{n+1}=a_n+3-7=a_n-4$.
  4. Classify: compare against $a_{n+1}=a_n+d$ (AP), $a_{n+1}=ra_n$ (GP), or $a_{n+1}=ra_n+c$ (linear).
  5. Record the parameter(s) ($d=-4$ here) before computing any terms or sums.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $a_{n+1}=\dfrac{2(a_n+3)}{2}-7$ with $a_1=10$.\\ Simplify: $a_{n+1}=a_n+3-7=a_n-4$, so the sequence is arithmetic with $d=-4$.\\ Then $a_n=10+(n-1)(-4)=14-4n$, giving $a_2=6,\ a_3=2,\ a_4=-2$ instantly.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Geometric recurrence disguised by a common factor, e.g. $a_{n+1}=\dfrac{6a_n}{2}$
Cancel to $a_{n+1}=3a_n$, identify GP with $r=3$, then use $a_n=a_1 r^{n-1}$.
Bracketed expansion hiding a linear recurrence, e.g. $a_{n+1}=3(a_n-2)+5$
Expand and combine: $a_{n+1}=3a_n-1$; recognise linear (non-AP/GP) and iterate or solve via fixed-point shift.
Fraction with same denominator on both terms, e.g. $a_{n+1}=\dfrac{a_n+4}{2}+\dfrac{a_n}{2}$
Combine over the common denominator to $a_{n+1}=a_n+2$, an AP with $d=2$.
Constant collapsing inside trig/log-free recurrence, e.g. $a_{n+1}=a_n+5-3$
Collect to $a_{n+1}=a_n+2$ before computing terms or applying $S_n$ formulae.

Common Misreadings

  • Substituting $a_1$ into the original unsimplified form and chasing fractions through every iteration.
  • Misreading $\dfrac{2(a_n+3)}{2}$ as $2a_n+\dfrac{3}{2}$ or $2a_n+3/2$ due to dropped brackets.
  • Treating the recurrence as geometric because of the visible factor of $2$, instead of cancelling it.

Pitfalls

  • Cancelling the $2$ correctly but forgetting to subtract the trailing $7$, leaving $a_{n+1}=a_n+3$.
  • Sign error when combining constants: writing $3-7=-3$ or $+4$ instead of $-4$.
  • Identifying the AP correctly but then using $a_n=a_1+nd$ instead of $a_n=a_1+(n-1)d$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 sequences and series: recognising AP/GP from a recurrence and applying $a_n=a_1+(n-1)d$ or $a_n=a_1 r^{n-1}$. No solution of general linear recurrences beyond term-by-term iteration is required.
