Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question lists the first few terms of a sequence (typically three) and asks you to identify, justify, or assume which type of series model applies. Common phrasings: 'show that the savings form an arithmetic series', 'state, with a reason, what type of series this is', or modelling contexts like weekly savings, salaries, or populations where the model is not pre-stated. Also fires when you must decide which formula (a + (n-1)d versus ar^{n-1}) to deploy.

Motivation — Why this works

At A-level only two series models exist: arithmetic (constant common difference d = u_{n+1} - u_n) and geometric (constant common ratio r = u_{n+1}/u_n). These are mutually exclusive except for the trivial constant case. Testing both quantities on consecutive pairs decisively classifies the series and unlocks the correct closed-form for u_n and S_n. Without this classification step, any subsequent formula application is unjustified and typically loses the explanation/method mark.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question gives at least three numerical terms and asks for the series type or assumes a model implicitly through later parts (e.g. 'find the total over 52 weeks').
  2. Rule out non-A-level models: only arithmetic and geometric are in scope, so the choice is binary.
  3. Commit to computing both u_2 - u_1 and u_3 - u_2 first (cheaper arithmetic), and only check ratios if differences are not constant.
  4. If two consecutive differences match, declare arithmetic and record a = u_1 and d; if two consecutive ratios match, declare geometric and record a = u_1 and r.
  5. Show both pairs explicitly so the examiner sees the constant pattern is verified, not assumed from a single comparison.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute d_1 = u_2 - u_1.
  2. Compute d_2 = u_3 - u_2.
  3. If d_1 = d_2, conclude arithmetic with a = u_1 and d = d_1 (state both values).
  4. Otherwise compute r_1 = u_2/u_1 and r_2 = u_3/u_2; if r_1 = r_2, conclude geometric with a = u_1 and r = r_1.
  5. Write a one-line justification referencing the equal differences (or equal ratios) before applying any sum or nth-term formula.

Worked Micro-Example

Terms: \pounds 5, \pounds 5.25, \pounds 5.50. Differences: 5.25 - 5 = 0.25 and 5.50 - 5.25 = 0.25, both equal. Ratios: 5.25/5 = 1.05 but 5.50/5.25 \approx 1.0476, not equal. Therefore the series is arithmetic with a = 5 and d = 0.25.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Terms 5, 10, 20 (or any constant-multiplier context like compound interest, depreciation, population growth)
Differences 5 and 10 are unequal, so test ratios: 10/5 = 2 and 20/10 = 2 — geometric with a = 5, r = 2; use u_n = ar^{n-1} and S_n = a(1 - r^n)/(1 - r).
Salary problem: starting salary with fixed annual rise of \pounds X
Fixed rise signals arithmetic without computation; identify a and d directly from the wording.
Question states 'percentage increase each year of p%'
Percentage multiplier signals geometric with r = 1 + p/100; no need to test differences.
Four or more terms given
Check at least two consecutive differences (or ratios) — three pairs gives stronger justification but two suffice for the mark.
Sum to infinity asked later
Classification must yield geometric with |r| < 1 before applying S_\infty = a/(1-r).

Common Misreadings

  • Checking only one pair of consecutive terms and assuming the pattern continues, instead of verifying with a second pair.
  • Confusing 'constant percentage increase' (geometric) with 'constant amount increase' (arithmetic) when the wording is in pounds and percentages mixed.
  • Treating a sequence with constant second differences as arithmetic — that is a quadratic sequence and out of A-level series scope.

Pitfalls

  • Stating 'it is arithmetic/geometric' without showing the equal differences or ratios — loses the justification mark.
  • Computing the ratio as u_1/u_2 instead of u_{n+1}/u_n, which inverts r.
  • Rounding ratios prematurely (e.g. writing 5.50/5.25 = 1.05) and falsely concluding the series is geometric.
  • Forgetting to state both a and d (or a and r) explicitly before using a formula.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL P2 (arithmetic and geometric series); classification by constant difference or ratio is the gateway to every series formula in the specification, including S_\infty for |r| < 1.
