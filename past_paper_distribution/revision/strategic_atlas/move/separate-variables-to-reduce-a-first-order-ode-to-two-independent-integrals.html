Strategic Atlas / Differential Equations / Separation Of Variables
Differential Equations

Separate variables to reduce a first-order ODE to two independent integrals

Family: Separation Of Variables Frequency: 5 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a first-order ODE dy/dx (or dr/dt, dh/dt, dH/dt, etc.) has a right-hand side that factors multiplicatively as f(x)·g(y) — that is, all dependence on the independent variable can be cleanly separated from all dependence on the dependent variable. Visual cues include: RHS is a quotient like (t² cos t)/y, a product like t·H², a single-variable expression like (3/320)(4−h) or −(H−5)(H+3)/40 (where g(t)=1), or a power like −k/r². Equivalently: the equation is NOT of the linear form dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x) with mixed terms, and the variables can be physically moved to opposite sides of the equation.

Motivation — Why this works

Separation of variables converts a differential equation into two ordinary, independent integrals — one in each variable — each evaluable by standard techniques (power rule, log rule, partial fractions, integration by parts, etc.). It works because dy/dx = f(x)g(y) can be rewritten as (1/g(y)) dy = f(x) dx, and integrating both sides with respect to their own variables is justified by the chain rule in reverse. This is the canonical and simplest first-order ODE method at A-level, and should always be the first technique checked before reaching for integrating factors or substitutions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Look at the ODE and ask: does the RHS factor as a function of the independent variable times a function of the dependent variable? If g(t)=1 (RHS depends only on y), it still counts — that's a degenerate but valid separable form.
  2. Rule out integrating factor (which suits LINEAR ODEs of the form dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x)) — many separable ODEs are non-linear (e.g. y appears in a denominator, or as y², or inside a product like (H−5)(H+3)).
  3. Rule out substitution methods (homogeneous, Bernoulli) — unnecessary heavy machinery when the equation already separates cleanly.
  4. Commit to separation: physically rearrange so dy and all y-terms sit on one side, dx and all x-terms on the other. Write the equation in the form (1/g(y)) dy = f(x) dx.
  5. Anticipate the integrals on each side: is the y-side a power (∫y dy), a logarithm (∫1/(a−y) dy), or partial fractions (∫1/((y−a)(y−b)) dy)? Is the x-side a constant, a polynomial, or requiring integration by parts? This previews any earlier sub-question (e.g. 'find partial fractions in part (a)') that the examiner has set up to make the integration tractable.
  6. Integrate both sides, attaching a SINGLE constant of integration C on one side (typically the RHS), ready to be pinned down by an initial condition.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the ODE in the form dy/dx = f(x)·g(y).
  2. Rearrange to (1/g(y)) dy = f(x) dx, multiplying or dividing both sides as needed.
  3. Integrate both sides: ∫(1/g(y)) dy = ∫f(x) dx.
  4. Evaluate each integral using the appropriate technique (power rule, log, partial fractions, by parts).
  5. Add a single constant of integration C to obtain the implicit general solution.
  6. If an initial condition is given, substitute (x₀, y₀) to determine C, then rearrange (if asked) to make y the subject.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve dy/dx = (t² cos t)/y. Separate: y\,dy = t² cos t\,dt. Integrate: ∫y\,dy = y²/2 and ∫t² cos t\,dt = t² sin t + 2t cos t − 2 sin t (by parts twice). Hence y²/2 = t² sin t + 2t cos t − 2 sin t + C, i.e. y² = 2t² sin t + 4t cos t − 4 sin t + A.

Variants — Same move, different surface

RHS depends only on y (constant t-coefficient), e.g. dh/dt = (3/320)(4−h)
Treat g(t)=1: separate as dh/(4−h) = (3/320) dt. LHS gives −ln|4−h| (mind the inner-derivative sign); RHS is linear in t.
y-side is a product like 1/((H−5)(H+3))
Apply partial fractions BEFORE integrating; examiners often set this up as part (a) of the question.
RHS is f(x)/g(y) with g(y)=y or y², e.g. dy/dx = 4/(y²(4x+5))
Multiply both sides by g(y) so y² dy = (4/(4x+5)) dx; integrate y³/3 on the LHS and a logarithm on the RHS.
Logistic-type dP/dt = kP(1−P/M)
Same separation, but the y-integral requires partial fractions of 1/(P(1−P/M)).

Common Misreadings

  • Mistaking a separable non-linear ODE (e.g. dy/dt = (t² cos t)/y) for a linear one and attempting an integrating factor — this fails because of the y in the denominator.
  • Failing to recognise an equation as separable when one side is just a constant (e.g. 3/320), and unnecessarily reaching for integrating factors.
  • Trying to expand a factored quadratic like (H−5)(H+3) and integrate 1/(H²−2H−15) directly, missing that partial fractions is required.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the constant of integration C — fatal when an initial condition is supplied.
  • Sign error on logarithmic integrals: ∫1/(a−y) dy = −ln|a−y|, not +ln|a−y| (chain-rule inner derivative is −1).
  • Dropping modulus signs on logarithms; usually fine in physical contexts but penalised in pure questions.
  • Power-rule slip: integrating r² as r³ rather than r³/3, or losing the factor of ½ from ∫y dy.
  • Mishandling a global negative sign on the RHS (e.g. −1/40) and carrying it inconsistently through to the final answer.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 (and FP equivalents). The formula booklet does not provide a separation rule explicitly, but standard integrals (∫1/(ax+b) dx, ∫ xⁿ dx) used afterwards are assumed known. Avoid generalising to systems of ODEs or to second-order equations — those are out of scope.
