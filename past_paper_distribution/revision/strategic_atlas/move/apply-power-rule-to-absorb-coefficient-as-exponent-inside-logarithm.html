Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a logarithmic equation or expression contains a term of the form k·log_b(A) where k is a numerical coefficient (integer, negative, or fractional) sitting in front of a log. Visual cue: a number multiplying a log term that you eventually need to combine with other logs (via addition/subtraction laws) or anti-log to reach a form like y = px^q or to equate arguments. Equivalent phrasings: 'there's a coefficient blocking me from collapsing the RHS into a single log', or 'I cannot exponentiate both sides cleanly while a multiplier sits outside the log'. Also fires in the reverse direction during linearisation (turning y = ax^n into log y = n log x + log a).

Motivation — Why this works

The power law log_b(A^k) = k·log_b(A) lets you transfer a multiplicative coefficient outside a log into an exponent inside its argument, producing a single logarithmic term. This matters because the addition law log(A) + log(B) = log(AB) and the quotient law log(A) − log(B) = log(A/B) only apply when each term is a single log with coefficient 1 — any external multiplier must be absorbed first. Once every term is a single log, the equation can be collapsed to log_b(F(x)) = log_b(G(x)) and the arguments equated, or both sides can be anti-logged cleanly.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice a term like k·log_b(A) with k ≠ 1 (e.g. 3·log₃(2x−1), 2·log₃(1−x), or −(2/3)·log₁₀x) and recognise the coefficient is blocking further log manipulation.
  2. Rule out exponentiating both sides immediately: if the other side has additive constants or other log terms, raising the base to that side gets messy (e.g. 3^(2 + log₃(...)) forces a separate simplification step).
  3. Rule out trying to use the addition/quotient laws directly — they require single-log terms, so the coefficient must come inside first.
  4. Commit to the power rule: rewrite k·log_b(A) as log_b(A^k). The coefficient becomes the exponent of the argument, including its sign if negative.
  5. Tactical check: bracket the entire argument before raising to the power — write (A)^k, never multiply k into A's interior. Then proceed to combine with neighbouring log terms via addition/subtraction laws.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the offending term k·log_b(A).
  2. Rewrite it as log_b(A^k), preserving the sign of k as part of the exponent.
  3. Bracket the argument: log_b((A)^k), to avoid mis-distribution.
  4. Substitute back into the original equation/expression so every log term now has coefficient 1.
  5. Proceed to the next step (addition/quotient law to merge logs, or anti-log).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\log_3 y = 2\log_3(1-x) + \log_3 27$, apply the power rule: $2\log_3(1-x) = \log_3((1-x)^2)$. Substitute: $\log_3 y = \log_3((1-x)^2) + \log_3 27 = \log_3(27(1-x)^2)$, so $y = 27(1-x)^2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Negative fractional coefficient, e.g. $-\tfrac{2}{3}\log_{10} x$ when linearising $y = px^q$
Absorb the full signed fraction as the exponent: $-\tfrac{2}{3}\log_{10} x = \log_{10}(x^{-2/3})$. The negative sign stays with the exponent.
Half-coefficient producing a surd, e.g. $\tfrac{1}{2}\log A$
Rewrite as $\log(A^{1/2}) = \log(\sqrt{A})$ — same power law, surd surface.
Reverse direction (linearisation): starting from $y = ax^n$
Take logs and use the rule forward: $\log y = \log a + n\log x$, where $n$ becomes the gradient of the log–log line.
Inductive proofs with sums of logs, e.g. $\log_2(k!) + \log_2(k+1)$
Here the addition law (not the power law) is the collapsing tool: $\log_2(k!) + \log_2(k+1) = \log_2(k!(k+1)) = \log_2((k+1)!)$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the coefficient as if it multiplies the argument: writing k·log_b(A) = log_b(kA) instead of log_b(A^k).
  • Trying to exponentiate both sides before clearing the coefficient, leading to expressions like b^(c + k·log_b A) that are harder than necessary.
  • Applying the addition law first while a coefficient still sits outside one of the terms.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error: dropping the negative when k < 0, e.g. writing $\log_{10}(x^{2/3})$ instead of $\log_{10}(x^{-2/3})$.
  • Failing to bracket the argument: writing $\log_b(1-x)^2$ ambiguously and then expanding as $1 - x^2$ instead of $(1-x)^2 = 1 - 2x + x^2$.
  • Applying the power rule to a sum inside the log: $\log(A+B)^n$ is NOT $n\log(A+B)$ unless the entire $(A+B)$ is the single argument being raised — the rule is about the log's coefficient, not internal structure.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 logarithm content; the power law $\log_b(A^k) = k\log_b A$ is in the formula booklet. Stay within real, positive arguments — do not extend to complex logs or change-of-base manipulations beyond the syllabus. Always check domain validity (argument > 0) after solving.
