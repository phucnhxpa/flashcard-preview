Algebra & Functions

Distribute outer exponent across every factor in a bracketed product

Family: Index Law Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a product sits inside brackets with an outer exponent, such as $(2x^{3/2})^2$, $(3a^2b)^3$, or $(4x^2)^{-1/2}$. The bracket contains two or more factors — typically a numerical coefficient and one or more variable powers — and the whole bracket is raised to a power. Cue phrases include 'simplify', 'write in the form $kx^n$', or any context where the bracketed product blocks a subsequent index-law step (e.g. dividing or combining with another power of $x$).

Motivation — Why this works

The power-of-a-product identity $(ab)^n = a^n b^n$ follows from associativity and commutativity of multiplication and lets a compound power be split into independent simpler powers. Each resulting factor can then be evaluated numerically or simplified via power-of-a-power, $(x^m)^n = x^{mn}$. Without this decomposition the bracket remains an opaque block, and rules like $x^a/x^b = x^{a-b}$ cannot engage because the numerator is not yet a single coefficient times a single power.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the structure '(product)^outer power' — at least two distinguishable factors inside one pair of brackets, with an exponent attached outside.
  2. Rules out expanding by repeated multiplication (slow, error-prone for fractional/negative outer exponents) and rules out applying the exponent only to the variable factor.
  3. Commits to $(ab)^n = a^n b^n$ because every factor inside the brackets — including the numerical coefficient — is part of the product being raised.
  4. Bridges to first operation: rewrite the bracket as a product of separately-exponentiated factors, e.g. $(2x^{3/2})^2 = 2^2 \cdot (x^{3/2})^2$.
  5. Inside the move, decides which sub-rule each factor needs: arithmetic for the numerical coefficient, power-of-a-power $(x^m)^n = x^{mn}$ for variable factors, and careful sign/fraction handling when the outer exponent is negative or fractional.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify every factor inside the brackets (coefficient and each variable power).
  2. Apply $(ab\cdots)^n = a^n b^n \cdots$ to write each factor with its own copy of the outer exponent.
  3. Evaluate the numerical factor (e.g. $2^2 = 4$).
  4. Apply power-of-a-power $(x^m)^n = x^{mn}$ to each variable factor, multiplying exponents.
  5. Reassemble as a single monomial $kx^n$ ready for the next step (e.g. division by another power of $x$).

Worked Micro-Example

Simplify $\dfrac{(2x^{3/2})^2}{2x^4}$.\\ Numerator: $(2x^{3/2})^2 = 2^2 \cdot (x^{3/2})^2 = 4x^{3}$.\\ So $\dfrac{4x^3}{2x^4} = 2x^{3-4} = 2x^{-1}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Three-factor product such as $(3a^2 b^{-1})^3$
Distribute to all three factors: $3^3 \cdot a^{2\cdot 3} \cdot b^{(-1)\cdot 3} = 27 a^6 b^{-3}$.
Negative fractional outer exponent, e.g. $(4x^2)^{-1/2}$
Same rule but track signs: $4^{-1/2} \cdot x^{2\cdot(-1/2)} = \tfrac{1}{2} x^{-1}$.
Surd form $(2\sqrt{x})^2 = (2x^{1/2})^2$
Rewrite the surd as a fractional power first, then distribute: $4x$.
Quotient inside brackets, e.g. $\left(\dfrac{2}{x^3}\right)^2$
Use $(a/b)^n = a^n/b^n$, the quotient analogue, distributing the exponent to numerator and denominator.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading $(2x^{3/2})^2$ as $2x^{3}$ — applying the exponent only to the variable and forgetting the coefficient is inside the brackets.
  • Reading $(x^{3/2})^2$ as $x^{3/2+2} = x^{7/2}$ — confusing power-of-a-power (multiply) with product of powers (add).
  • Treating $(a+b)^n$ as $a^n + b^n$ by analogy — the rule only applies to products, not sums.

Pitfalls

  • Fraction arithmetic: computing $(3/2)\times 2$ as $3/4$ instead of $3$.
  • Sign errors when the outer exponent is negative, e.g. writing $(x^2)^{-1/2} = x^{1}$ instead of $x^{-1}$.
  • Forgetting to square (or otherwise raise) the numerical coefficient, leaving an un-simplified $2$ where $4$ is required.
  • Mis-bracketing on rewrite: writing $2x^{3/2 \cdot 2}$ which ambiguously suggests the coefficient was also multiplied.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard index-law manipulation in Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1 algebra and indices); reappears whenever expressions must be written in the form $kx^n$ before differentiation or integration.
