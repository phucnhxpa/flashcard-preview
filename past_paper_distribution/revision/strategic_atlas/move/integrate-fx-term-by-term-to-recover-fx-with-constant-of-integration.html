Integration

Integrate f'(x) term-by-term to recover f(x) with constant of integration

Family: Antidifferentiation Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question states f'(x) (or dy/dx) explicitly — typically as a sum of power terms, possibly including negative or fractional indices after rewriting surds/reciprocals — and asks you to find f(x) (or y). Equivalent phrasings: 'given the gradient function, find the curve', 'find an expression for f(x)', or kinematics versions like 'given v = ds/dt, find s'. A second piece of information (e.g. f(4) = 12, or 'the curve passes through (a, b)') is usually supplied in a later part, signalling that a constant of integration is required and will be determined.

Motivation — Why this works

Indefinite integration is the formal inverse of differentiation, so f(x) = ∫f'(x) dx reconstructs the original function. For polynomial-style integrands the power rule ∫ax^n dx = a·x^(n+1)/(n+1) (n ≠ −1) reverses the power rule for differentiation term by term. The '+ c' is essential because differentiation annihilates constants, leaving an infinite family of antiderivatives; a boundary condition pins down which member of the family is meant.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structural cue: f'(x) is given and f(x) is wanted — this is the textbook signature of indefinite integration, the inverse of differentiation.
  2. Rule out alternatives: no need for substitution, integration by parts, or the reverse chain rule because the integrand is just a sum of power terms; differentiating again would be the wrong direction.
  3. Commit to integrating term-by-term using the power rule, after first rewriting any surds, reciprocals, or bracketed expressions as x^n form so the rule applies cleanly.
  4. Bridge to execution: write f(x) = ∫f'(x) dx, then split the integral over the sum and handle each ax^n term independently.
  5. Tactical decision: keep the constant of integration c explicit and unevaluated for now — a later part of the question (typically a given value f(x₀) = y₀) will determine it.
  6. Sanity check: differentiate the answer mentally to confirm it returns f'(x); verify each new exponent is (old + 1) and the divisor matches that new exponent.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rewrite f'(x) so every term is in the form ax^n (e.g. 1/√x = x^(−1/2), 3 = 3x^0).
  2. Apply ∫ax^n dx = a·x^(n+1)/(n+1) to each term, raising the index by 1 and dividing by the NEW index.
  3. Sum the antiderivatives and append + c: f(x) = Σ a_i·x^(n_i+1)/(n_i+1) + c.
  4. Simplify coefficients (e.g. −16·x^(1/2)/(1/2) = −32x^(1/2) = −32√x) and, if convenient, restore surd form.
  5. If a boundary condition is given in a follow-up part, substitute it to solve for c.

Worked Micro-Example

Given f'(x) = 4x^2 − 5x + 3, integrate term-by-term: ∫4x^2 dx = \tfrac{4}{3}x^3, ∫−5x dx = −\tfrac{5}{2}x^2, ∫3 dx = 3x. Therefore f(x) = \tfrac{4}{3}x^3 − \tfrac{5}{2}x^2 + 3x + c.

Variants — Same move, different surface

f'(x) contains surds or reciprocals (e.g. −16/√x)
Rewrite as a power x^n first (−16x^(−1/2)), then apply the power rule; dividing by 1/2 means multiplying by 2.
Kinematics: v(t) = ds/dt given, find s(t)
Same procedure — integrate v with respect to t and use an initial displacement s(t₀) = s₀ to fix c.
Second derivative f''(x) given, find f(x)
Integrate twice, introducing two constants c₁ and c₂; two boundary conditions are needed to determine both.
Integrand is a product or bracket like x(2x − 1)
Expand first into a polynomial in x^n form, then integrate term-by-term.

Common Misreadings

  • Differentiating instead of integrating because the previous part of the question involved differentiation.
  • Treating ∫f'(x) dx as if it must equal a definite value, and so omitting + c.
  • Trying to apply the power rule directly to a product or quotient without first expanding/rewriting as x^n.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the constant of integration '+ c' — costs a mark in every Edexcel mark scheme and prevents solving any follow-up boundary-condition part.
  • Dividing by the old exponent n instead of the new exponent n+1 (e.g. writing 4x^3/2 instead of 4x^3/3).
  • Arithmetic slips with fractional indices: miscomputing (−1/2 + 1) = 1/2, or forgetting that dividing by 1/2 means multiplying by 2.
  • Failing to rewrite surds/reciprocals in x^n form before integrating, then applying the rule incorrectly to √x or 1/x^k.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P2/P3 indefinite integration of x^n (n ≠ −1) — no formula booklet entry needed beyond the power rule. The case n = −1 (giving ln|x|) and integration of e^(kx), sin(kx), cos(kx) are separate standard results; do not over-generalise the power rule to n = −1.
