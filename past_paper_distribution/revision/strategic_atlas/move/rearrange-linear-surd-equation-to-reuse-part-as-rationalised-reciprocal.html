Algebra & Functions

Rearrange linear surd equation to reuse part (a)'s rationalised reciprocal

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a multi-part question uses the cue 'Hence' (or 'Hence, or otherwise') and part (b) presents a linear equation in x whose coefficients on the x-terms exactly match the numerator and denominator (or the denominator's components) that were rationalised in part (a). Concrete cue: the equation contains the same surd constants (e.g. 4 and 2√2) that formed the denominator in part (a). Alternative phrasings include 'Use your answer to part (a)' or 'Deduce'. The move applies whenever a known reciprocal a + b√n can replace a fresh rationalisation.

Motivation — Why this works

Collecting x-terms on one side and factoring produces x·(denominator from part (a)) = constant·√n, so dividing converts the problem into multiplication by an already-evaluated reciprocal. Because (p − q√n)(p + q√n) = p² − nq² is a rational number, part (a)'s rationalised form is the unique simplified reciprocal, and reusing it is mathematically equivalent to re-rationalising — but cheaper. The 'Hence' instruction signals the examiner expects this reuse rather than ab initio work.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the 'Hence' cue and scan part (a)'s answer: 1/(4 − 2√2) = 1/2 + (1/4)√2 is now a known reciprocal.
  2. Rule out re-rationalising from scratch — it duplicates work and risks fresh sign/arithmetic errors.
  3. Commit because the coefficients of x in the equation (4 and 2√2) match the components of part (a)'s denominator, so the equation can be folded into x = (constant)·[reciprocal from (a)].
  4. Bridge: collect x-terms on one side, factor x, then divide both sides by the resulting bracket and substitute part (a)'s value for its reciprocal.
  5. Tactically: keep the surd constant (here 20√2) outside the bracket and distribute it last, using √2·√2 = 2 to clean up.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Subtract 2√2·x from both sides: 4x − 2√2·x = 20√2.
  2. Factor x: x(4 − 2√2) = 20√2.
  3. Divide: x = 20√2 / (4 − 2√2) = 20√2 · [1/(4 − 2√2)].
  4. Substitute part (a): x = 20√2 · (1/2 + (1/4)√2).
  5. Distribute: 20√2 · 1/2 = 10√2; 20√2 · (1/4)√2 = 5·(√2)² = 10.
  6. Combine: x = 10 + 10√2.

Worked Micro-Example

Given (from part (a)) $\dfrac{1}{4-2\sqrt{2}}=\dfrac{1}{2}+\dfrac{1}{4}\sqrt{2}$. Solve $4x = 2\sqrt{2}\,x + 20\sqrt{2}$. Rearrange: $x(4-2\sqrt{2}) = 20\sqrt{2}$, so $x = 20\sqrt{2}\left(\tfrac{1}{2}+\tfrac{1}{4}\sqrt{2}\right) = 10\sqrt{2}+10 = 10+10\sqrt{2}.$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Coordinate geometry: part (a) simplifies a gradient like 1/(3−√5); part (b) asks for the line equation through a given point with that gradient.
Use part (a)'s rationalised gradient directly in y − y₀ = m(x − x₀) instead of re-rationalising.
Complex numbers (FP1): part (a) computes z₁/z₂ in a+bi form; part (b) solves a linear equation in w involving z₁ and z₂.
Rearrange to w·(z₂) = (something)·z₁ and substitute the known quotient z₁/z₂.
Logarithms: part (a) evaluates 1/(log_a b); part (b) solves an equation whose coefficients reproduce that denominator.
Factor and divide, then substitute the reciprocal log value from (a).
Binomial/series: part (a) gives a simplified expansion coefficient; part (b) solves for an unknown using an expression containing that coefficient.
Factor the unknown, divide, and insert part (a)'s value.

Common Misreadings

  • Ignoring the 'Hence' cue and re-rationalising 20√2/(4 − 2√2) from scratch — correct but wastes marks-time.
  • Failing to recognise that 4 and 2√2 in the equation correspond to the denominator from part (a), so the link is missed.
  • Treating 'Hence, or otherwise' as permission to ignore part (a) entirely, when 'Hence' methods are usually shorter and expected.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when moving 2√2·x to the left: writing (4 + 2√2) instead of (4 − 2√2).
  • Forgetting that √2·√2 = 2 when distributing 20√2 across the bracket, leaving an unsimplified (√2)² term.
  • Arithmetic slip in 20√2 · (1/4)√2: failing to combine the 20/4 = 5 with √2·√2 = 2 to get 10.
  • Dividing only one side of the equation by (4 − 2√2).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard P1/C1 surd manipulation with a 'Hence' bridge across question parts; uses only rationalising denominators of the form a + b√n, fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics.
