Binomial Expansion

Apply generalised binomial expansion for fractional or negative powers

Family: Template Matching Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The expression has the form $(1+y)^n$ where $n$ is a non-integer (fractional like $1/2$, $1/3$, $-1$, $-2$, etc.) and the question asks for expansion in ascending powers of $x$ up to a stated term. Surface cues include cube roots, square roots, or reciprocals written as powers, e.g. $(1+kx)^{1/3}$, $\sqrt{1-2x}$, or $1/(1+x)^2$. The question typically specifies a finite number of terms because the series is infinite. This signals the generalised binomial series, not Pascal's triangle.

Motivation — Why this works

Pascal's triangle and the $\binom{n}{r}$ formula terminate only when $n$ is a non-negative integer. The generalised binomial theorem $(1+y)^n = 1 + ny + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}y^2 + \dots$ extends to any real $n$, valid for $|y|<1$. It converts irrational or rational-power expressions into polynomial approximations suitable for coefficient comparison, numerical estimation, and integration.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the exponent is fractional or negative — a clear cue that finite binomial expansion via $\binom{n}{r}$ fails.
  2. Rule out Pascal's triangle since the series will not terminate; rule out direct factorial $\binom{n}{r}$ since $n!$ is undefined for non-integer $n$.
  3. Commit to the generalised series template $(1+y)^n = \sum \frac{n(n-1)\cdots(n-r+1)}{r!} y^r$, valid for $|y|<1$.
  4. Identify $y$ and $n$ from the bracket; if the bracket is $(a+bx)^n$ with $a\neq 1$, first factor out $a^n$ so the template fits.
  5. Decide how many terms to compute (matching the highest power requested) and track signs carefully through the descending factors $n, n-1, n-2, \dots$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the template: $(1+y)^n = 1 + ny + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}y^2 + \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}y^3 + \dots$
  2. Identify $y$ (the inner expression) and $n$ (the exponent), substituting carefully.
  3. Compute each coefficient using the descending product $n(n-1)(n-2)\dots$ divided by $r!$.
  4. Raise the inner expression $y$ to the appropriate power, including any constants like $k^r$.
  5. Simplify each term and combine to write the expansion to the required order.
  6. State the validity condition $|y|<1$ in terms of $x$.

Worked Micro-Example

Expand $(1+kx)^{1/3}$ up to $x^3$. With $n=\tfrac{1}{3}$, $y=kx$: coefficients are $\tfrac{1}{3}$, $\tfrac{\tfrac{1}{3}(-\tfrac{2}{3})}{2}=-\tfrac{1}{9}$, $\tfrac{\tfrac{1}{3}(-\tfrac{2}{3})(-\tfrac{5}{3})}{6}=\tfrac{5}{81}$. Hence $(1+kx)^{1/3} = 1 + \tfrac{1}{3}kx - \tfrac{1}{9}k^2x^2 + \tfrac{5}{81}k^3x^3 + \dots$, valid for $|kx|<1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$(a+bx)^n$ with $a\neq 1$, e.g. $(4+x)^{1/2}$
Factor out $a^n$ first: $(a+bx)^n = a^n\left(1+\tfrac{b}{a}x\right)^n$, then apply the template with $y=\tfrac{b}{a}x$. Validity becomes $\left|\tfrac{b}{a}x\right|<1$.
Negative integer power, e.g. $(1-2x)^{-1}$ or $(1+x)^{-2}$
Use $n=-1$ or $n=-2$ in the same template; signs alternate predictably and the series is still infinite.
Square root, e.g. $\sqrt{1-2x}$
Rewrite as $(1-2x)^{1/2}$ with $y=-2x$; carry the negative sign into every $y^r$.
Compound expression, e.g. $\frac{1+x}{\sqrt{1-x}}$
Expand each factor separately to the required order, then multiply, retaining only terms up to the required power.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to use Pascal's triangle or $\binom{n}{r}$ with factorials when $n$ is not a positive integer.
  • Treating the expansion as finite and stopping at $y^n$ as if $n$ were an integer.
  • Omitting the validity range $|y|<1$ when the question asks for it.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors in $(n-1)$, $(n-2)$ when $n$ is a small fraction — e.g. $\tfrac{1}{3}-1=-\tfrac{2}{3}$, two negatives multiplying to a positive.
  • Forgetting to raise the coefficient inside $y$ (e.g. $k$ or $b/a$) to the same power as $x$.
  • Failing to factor out $a^n$ when $a\neq 1$, leading to an invalid direct application.
  • Mis-stating validity, e.g. writing $|x|<1$ when the actual condition is $|kx|<1$ or $\left|\tfrac{b}{a}x\right|<1$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P3/P4): generalised binomial expansion for rational $n$ with explicit validity condition $|y|<1$, including the factor-out technique for $(a+bx)^n$.
