Trigonometry

Select the double-angle/Pythagorean form that cancels constants or eliminates unwanted variables

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 5 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an expression contains a double-angle term (sin 2x, cos 2x) combined with a constant ±1, OR when a target form is written in only one trig ratio (sin only, or cos only) but the working contains both. Visual cues: denominators or numerators of the form '1 ± cos 2x', expressions like 'cos 2x ± 1', or a 'show that' target written purely in sin x (or purely in cos x). Equivalent phrasings: 'simplify (sin 2x)/(1 − cos 2x)', 'show cos 2A = 2cos²A − 1', 'express sin 3x in terms of sin x'. The cue is always a structural mismatch between what the standard expansion gives you and what the target/simplification needs.

Motivation — Why this works

The cosine double-angle identity has three equivalent forms — cos 2x = cos²x − sin²x = 2cos²x − 1 = 1 − 2sin²x — and the Pythagorean identity sin²x + cos²x = 1 can be rearranged either way. These are not interchangeable in practice: each form is engineered to cancel something specific. Picking the form whose constant cancels the constant in the expression (or whose variable matches the target ratio) collapses a binomial into a monomial, enabling immediate factor cancellation. The wrong choice leaves a binomial that requires extra Pythagorean steps and invites sign errors. This is essentially a 'matching constants/variables' principle, the trig analogue of choosing the right conjugate.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structural feature of the expression: is there a stray '+1' or '−1' next to cos 2x, or is the target written in a single ratio (sin only, cos only)?
  2. List the three forms of cos 2x mentally (cos²x − sin²x, 2cos²x − 1, 1 − 2sin²x) and the two rearrangements of the Pythagorean identity (sin²x = 1 − cos²x, cos²x = 1 − sin²x).
  3. Rule out forms that would leave a binomial: e.g. for 1 − cos 2x, the form cos²x − sin²x gives 1 − cos²x + sin²x (two terms), while 1 − 2sin²x gives a clean 2sin²x.
  4. Commit to the form whose constant cancels the constant present, OR whose surviving variable matches the target ratio. The chosen form should produce a monomial in one trig ratio.
  5. Pair with the numerator: sin 2x = 2 sin x cos x almost always shares a factor with the simplified denominator, enabling cancellation to tan x, cot x, or a clean polynomial in sin x / cos x.
  6. Execute the substitution carefully, watching the minus sign when distributing across the parenthesised identity.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the constant or target ratio that dictates the choice: '+1' next to cos 2x → use 2cos²x − 1; '−1' or '1 −' next to cos 2x → use 1 − 2sin²x; target in sin only → use 1 − 2sin²x and cos²x = 1 − sin²x; target in cos only → use 2cos²x − 1 and sin²x = 1 − cos²x.
  2. Substitute sin 2x = 2 sin x cos x in the numerator (if present).
  3. Substitute the chosen cos 2x form so that the constants cancel, leaving a monomial denominator (e.g. 2sin²x or 2cos²x).
  4. If cos²x or sin²x still appears and the target is single-ratio, apply the matching Pythagorean substitution to eliminate it.
  5. Cancel common factors (typically 2 sin x or 2 cos x) between numerator and denominator.
  6. Read off the simplified form (often tan x, cot x, or a cubic in sin x / cos x).

Worked Micro-Example

Simplify $\dfrac{\sin 2x}{1-\cos 2x}$. The denominator has '1 −', so choose $\cos 2x = 1 - 2\sin^2 x$, giving $1-(1-2\sin^2 x)=2\sin^2 x$. With numerator $\sin 2x = 2\sin x\cos x$, the fraction is $\dfrac{2\sin x\cos x}{2\sin^2 x} = \dfrac{\cos x}{\sin x} = \cot x.$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Denominator is 1 + cos 2x
Select cos 2x = 2cos²x − 1 so that 1 + (2cos²x − 1) = 2cos²x; the fraction simplifies to tan x.
Target form is purely in sin x (e.g. show sin 3x = 3 sin x − 4 sin³x)
Use cos 2x = 1 − 2sin²x AND cos²x = 1 − sin²x to drive every term toward sin x.
Target form is purely in cos x (e.g. show cos 3x = 4cos³x − 3cos x)
Mirror strategy: use cos 2x = 2cos²x − 1 AND sin²x = 1 − cos²x.
Half-angle expressions like 1 + cos θ or 1 − cos θ
Apply the same logic at half-angle: cos θ = 2cos²(θ/2) − 1 or 1 − 2sin²(θ/2).
Expression involves sec/tan rather than sin/cos
Divide sin²x + cos²x = 1 by cos²x to obtain 1 + tan²x = sec²x; same selection principle.

Common Misreadings

  • Defaulting to cos 2x = cos²x − sin²x because it is the 'first' form learnt, even when it leaves an ugly binomial that needs extra work.
  • Substituting in the wrong direction of the Pythagorean identity (replacing cos²x when the target requires eliminating sin²x), then failing to spot the mismatch with the target.
  • Treating all three cos 2x forms as equally good and picking arbitrarily, missing the strategic cancellation.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when distributing the minus sign: writing 1 − (1 − 2sin²x) = 1 − 1 − 2sin²x = −2sin²x instead of +2sin²x.
  • Cancelling 2 cos x or 2 sin x without noting domain restrictions (cos x ≠ 0 or sin x ≠ 0) — important when the question asks for the domain of validity.
  • Stopping too early: producing 2sin²x / (2 sin x cos x) and writing the answer as sin x / cos x without simplifying to tan x (or vice versa).

A-level Scope Notes

All identities used (double-angle for sin and cos, Pythagorean identity, and their rearrangements 1 + tan²x = sec²x, 1 + cot²x = cosec²x) are listed in the Edexcel IAL formula booklet under 'Trigonometric identities' (P3). Stay within these; do not invoke product-to-sum or sum-to-product formulae unless explicitly needed.
