Integration

Assemble full antiderivative before applying definite limits

Family: Deferred Evaluation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A definite integral $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ requires two or more rounds of integration by parts (or a chain of reduction steps) to resolve. Cues include integrands like $x^n e^{kx}$, $x^n \sin kx$, $x^n \cos kx$ with $n\ge 2$, or $e^{ax}\sin bx$ where IBP cycles back. The temptation is to evaluate $[uv]_a^b$ at every stage, but this multiplies bookkeeping. Defer all limit substitution until the indefinite antiderivative is fully assembled.

Motivation — Why this works

Each IBP round produces its own boundary term $[uv]_a^b$ plus a residual integral; applying limits at every stage means tracking and summing several partial boundary contributions, a frequent source of sign and arithmetic errors. Building the full indefinite antiderivative $F(x)$ first reduces the problem to a single substitution $F(b)-F(a)$. Common factors (like $e^{2x}$) become visible across all terms simultaneously, aiding clean factorisation. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus guarantees the two approaches are equivalent, so deferring costs nothing.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the integrand needs repeated IBP — e.g. $x^2 e^{2x}$ drops degree by one each round, so I'll need two passes.
  2. I rule out applying $[uv]_0^1$ after each round: that would force me to track two boundary contributions and one residual integral, then re-substitute later anyway.
  3. I commit to treating the problem as 'find $F(x)$ first, evaluate $F(1)-F(0)$ second' — a clean two-stage workflow.
  4. Concretely, I drop the $+C$ throughout the indefinite work and only write the bracket $[F(x)]_0^1$ once at the end.
  5. Inside the move, I keep all terms in a common form (here, multiples of $e^{2x}$) so that the final substitution exposes shared factors for tidy simplification.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Strip the limits temporarily and compute the indefinite integral via successive IBP rounds.
  2. Combine all partial results into one antiderivative $F(x)$, omitting $+C$.
  3. Write $I = [F(x)]_a^b$ in a single line.
  4. Substitute $x=b$, simplifying using $e^0=1$, $\sin 0=0$, $\cos 0=1$ as relevant.
  5. Substitute $x=a$, being careful with the subtraction sign across all terms.
  6. Factor common constants/exponentials in the final answer.

Worked Micro-Example

Compute $\int_0^1 x^2 e^{2x}\,dx$. Two IBP rounds give the indefinite antiderivative $F(x)=\tfrac12 x^2 e^{2x}-\tfrac12 x e^{2x}+\tfrac14 e^{2x}$. Then $F(1)=\tfrac12 e^2-\tfrac12 e^2+\tfrac14 e^2=\tfrac14 e^2$ and $F(0)=\tfrac14$. Hence $I=\tfrac14 e^2-\tfrac14=\tfrac14(e^2-1)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cycling integrals such as $\int_0^{\pi} e^x \sin x\,dx$ where IBP reproduces the original integral
Defer limits until after solving algebraically for $\int e^x\sin x\,dx$ as an indefinite expression; only then evaluate $[F(x)]_0^{\pi}$.
Reduction formula problems, e.g. $I_n=\int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^n x\,dx$
Apply limits at each reduction step is actually preferred here because boundary terms often vanish; the deferred-evaluation move is mainly for non-reducing IBP chains.
Definite integrals requiring substitution then IBP, e.g. $\int_0^1 \arcsin x\,dx$
Find the full antiderivative in $x$ (not in the substituted variable), then substitute the original $x$-limits at the end.
Polynomial $\times$ trig, e.g. $\int_0^{\pi} x^2 \cos x\,dx$
Same workflow: assemble $F(x)=x^2\sin x+2x\cos x-2\sin x$ first, then evaluate once.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the residual integral after one IBP round as 'already evaluated' and applying limits before completing the second IBP.
  • Confusing this move with reduction-formula problems, where applying limits early is often advantageous.
  • Believing that $+C$ matters for definite integrals and carrying it through, then becoming confused at the final subtraction.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when distributing the $-F(a)$ across multiple terms of the antiderivative.
  • Forgetting that $e^0=1$, not $0$, so the lower-limit contribution is non-zero.
  • Dropping a coefficient (e.g. $\tfrac12$ from the first IBP) when transcribing the assembled antiderivative.
  • Failing to factor the final answer, leaving an unsimplified form like $\tfrac14 e^2-\tfrac14$ when $\tfrac14(e^2-1)$ is expected.
