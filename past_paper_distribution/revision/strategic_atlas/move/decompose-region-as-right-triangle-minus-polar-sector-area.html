Polar Coordinates

Decompose region as right triangle minus polar sector area

Family: Geometric Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a region R is bounded by a polar curve together with two perpendicular tangent lines (typically one horizontal tangent at the highest/lowest point Q and one vertical tangent at the rightmost/leftmost point P). The straight boundaries meet at a right angle at a corner S, and the curve cuts across the triangle PSQ. Cue phrases: 'tangent parallel to initial line', 'tangent perpendicular to initial line', 'region enclosed by the curve and the tangents'. A single polar integral cannot capture the straight edges, so geometric decomposition is needed.

Motivation — Why this works

The right triangle PSQ has area trivially given by ½ × base × height once P, Q, S are converted to Cartesian form. The polar integral ½∫r² dθ between the angles at P and Q sweeps out the sector from the pole bounded by OP, OQ and the arc. Adding or subtracting these pieces (with the triangle OPQ from the pole if needed) reconstructs R exactly. This trick replaces an awkward Cartesian double-region by two elementary area formulae.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice R has mixed boundary: two perpendicular straight tangents and an arc — no single ½∫r² dθ can give it directly.
  2. Rule out Cartesian integration: r = f(θ) is not easily inverted to y = g(x).
  3. Commit to decomposition: the tangents meet at right angles at S, so PSQ is a right triangle whose area is immediate.
  4. Bridge: convert P and Q from polar (r,θ) to Cartesian to read off the legs of the triangle, and identify the angles θ_P, θ_Q for the polar integral.
  5. Decide the sign: sketch to see whether the arc bulges into or out of the triangle, so R = triangle − sector or sector − triangle.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Locate P (vertical tangent: dx/dθ = 0) and Q (horizontal tangent: dy/dθ = 0); record their θ-values.
  2. Convert P and Q to Cartesian: x = r cosθ, y = r sinθ; deduce S as the intersection of the vertical line through P and horizontal line through Q.
  3. Compute triangle area: ½ × |x_P − x_S| × |y_Q − y_S|.
  4. Compute the polar sector area between OP and OQ: A_sector = ½∫_{θ_P}^{θ_Q} [r(θ)]² dθ.
  5. If the sector includes the triangle OPQ from the pole, subtract that triangle area ½|x_P y_Q − x_Q y_P| to get the area between chord PQ and the arc.
  6. Combine: Area(R) = Triangle PSQ area − (region between arc and chord), choosing signs from the sketch.

Worked Micro-Example

For $r = 1 + \sin\theta$, the vertical tangent is at $\theta = \pi/6$ giving $P = (3\sqrt3/4,\,3/4)$, and the horizontal tangent at $\theta = \pi/2$ giving $Q = (0,2)$, so $S = (3\sqrt3/4, 2)$. Triangle area $= \tfrac12 \cdot \tfrac{3\sqrt3}{4} \cdot \tfrac{5}{4} = \tfrac{15\sqrt3}{32}$. The polar sector $\tfrac12\int_{\pi/6}^{\pi/2}(1+\sin\theta)^2\,d\theta$ is computed and the triangle $OPQ$ subtracted to isolate the arc-chord region. Then $R = \tfrac{15\sqrt3}{32} - (\text{arc-chord region})$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Region between a polar curve and a single tangent line through the pole (initial line)
Use polar sector area only, possibly minus a triangle with one vertex at the pole.
Region between two polar curves over a common θ-range
Use ½∫(r_outer² − r_inner²) dθ; no triangle needed.
Region bounded by curve and a chord PQ (not tangents)
Area = sector area − triangle OPQ from the pole, where O is the origin.
Loop or petal bounded only by the curve
Single polar integral ½∫r² dθ between consecutive zeros of r suffices.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the entire region as a polar sector and writing one ½∫r² dθ — this ignores the straight tangent boundaries.
  • Mis-identifying R as the triangle PSQ itself rather than the sliver between triangle and arc.
  • Subtracting in the wrong order, giving a negative area.
  • Using ½∫r² dθ to mean 'area between curve and chord' rather than 'area swept from pole'.

Pitfalls

  • Errors converting (r,θ) to (x,y) for P and Q, especially with surds.
  • Wrong integration limits — must be the θ-values of the tangent points, not arbitrary endpoints.
  • Forgetting to subtract triangle OPQ when isolating the arc-chord piece from the polar sector.
  • Sign/absolute-value slips when the arc lies on the opposite side of the chord from the pole.

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL Further Pure: polar area formula ½∫r² dθ, tangent conditions dx/dθ = 0 and dy/dθ = 0, and basic Cartesian-polar conversion are all in scope.
