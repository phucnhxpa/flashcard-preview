Sequences & Series

Iterate reduction formula down to known base case

Family: Recurrence Descent Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A reduction formula has been established (typically in part (a)) linking I_n to I_{n-2} or I_{n-1}, and a later part demands a specific numerical value such as I_5, I_6 or a closed expression for I_n. The cue is a definite integral indexed by n where direct integration would be laborious but the recurrence collapses the index. Phrases like 'hence evaluate', 'use your result to find', or 'show that I_n = ...' signal this descent. Parity of n decides which base case is reached.

Motivation — Why this works

Each application of the recurrence (e.g. I_n = ((n-1)/n) I_{n-2}) reduces the index by a fixed step, producing a telescoping product of rational coefficients. After ⌊n/2⌋ steps the chain terminates at a directly computable elementary integral — typically I_0 = π/2 or I_1 = 1 for the standard sin^n / cos^n cases. Multiplying the accumulated coefficients with the base value yields the exact answer. This is the entire computational payoff of having derived the reduction formula in the previous part.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question asks for a specific I_n and I already have a recurrence linking I_n to I_{n-2} (or I_{n-1}), so descent is the intended route.
  2. I rule out direct integration of sin^n x or x^n e^x etc. — that ignores part (a) and is far longer.
  3. I commit to descent because the index decreases by a fixed amount each step, and I can predict the terminal index from the parity of n.
  4. I check parity: even n descends to I_0, odd n descends to I_1; I evaluate that base case as a simple elementary integral.
  5. I write the chain of equalities explicitly before multiplying, to keep the rational coefficients organised and avoid arithmetic slips.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify base case from parity: even n → I_0, odd n → I_1 (for step-2 recurrences).
  2. Compute the base case directly as an elementary integral.
  3. Write the descent chain: I_n = c_n I_{n-2}, I_{n-2} = c_{n-2} I_{n-4}, ..., down to the base.
  4. Multiply all coefficients c_n · c_{n-2} · ... together with the base value.
  5. Simplify the resulting product of fractions to lowest terms.

Worked Micro-Example

Given I_n = \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^n x\,dx satisfies I_n = \tfrac{n-1}{n} I_{n-2}, find I_6. Descend: I_6 = \tfrac{5}{6} I_4 = \tfrac{5}{6}\cdot\tfrac{3}{4} I_2 = \tfrac{5}{6}\cdot\tfrac{3}{4}\cdot\tfrac{1}{2} I_0. With I_0 = \int_0^{\pi/2} 1\,dx = \tfrac{\pi}{2}, we get I_6 = \tfrac{15}{48}\cdot\tfrac{\pi}{2} = \tfrac{5\pi}{32}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Odd index, e.g. I_5 with the same sin^n recurrence
Descend to I_1 = \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin x\,dx = 1; the answer is rational (no π factor), which itself acts as a parity sanity check.
Step-1 recurrence such as I_n = n I_{n-1} - 1 from \int x^n e^{-x} or similar
Descend one index at a time down to I_0; coefficients are no longer purely multiplicative, so additive remainder terms must be carried through carefully.
Recurrence for J_n = \int_0^1 x^n (1-x)^k dx or \int x^n \ln x\,dx style problems
Same descent logic; base case is an easy polynomial or log integral. Track sign changes if the recurrence has a minus sign.
General closed form requested ('show that I_{2n} = ...')
Iterate symbolically and recognise the product as a double factorial or factorial ratio, e.g. I_{2n} = \tfrac{(2n)!}{4^n (n!)^2}\cdot\tfrac{\pi}{2}.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the recurrence as a one-off and re-deriving each I_k by integration, defeating the purpose of part (a).
  • Believing every reduction must terminate at I_0; missing that odd n terminates at I_1.
  • Confusing the recurrence's coefficient ((n-1)/n vs (n-2)/(n-1) vs n/(n+1)) when copying from part (a).

Pitfalls

  • Using the wrong base case: computing I_0 when n is odd (should be I_1) or vice versa.
  • Arithmetic errors in the product of fractions — writing the chain out explicitly before multiplying helps.
  • Forgetting to carry additive terms when the recurrence is non-homogeneous (e.g. I_n = n I_{n-1} - 1).
  • Off-by-one in the number of descent steps, leading to terminating at I_2 instead of I_0.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure 2/3 territory: reduction formulae for \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^n x\,dx, \int x^n e^x\,dx and similar, with numerical evaluation typically requested in the part following the derivation.
