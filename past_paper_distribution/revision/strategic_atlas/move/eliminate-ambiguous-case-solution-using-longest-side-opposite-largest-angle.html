Trigonometry

Eliminate ambiguous-case solution using longest-side-opposite-largest-angle

Family: Geometric Constraint Elimination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires after applying the Sine Rule yields two candidate angles (acute and obtuse supplement) and the question imposes a side-ordering constraint such as 'AB is the longest side' or 'BC is the shortest side.' Equivalent phrasings include 'the side opposite angle C is the longest' or a diagram in which one side is visibly labelled longest. The constraint must be combined with the angle-sum check (180° total) to discriminate between the two candidates.

Motivation — Why this works

In any triangle, the Sine Rule a/sin A = b/sin B = c/sin C implies that the longest side corresponds to the largest sine and hence the largest angle (since each angle lies in (0°, 180°) and only one can exceed 90°). So 'AB longest' forces the angle opposite AB (here angle ACB) to exceed both other angles. Computing all three angles for each candidate immediately reveals which configuration respects this ordering.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the Sine Rule has produced two candidate values for x (an acute value and its 180°-supplement), so I need a discriminator beyond the equation itself.
  2. I rule out picking the acute value by default: the problem says AB is longest, so I must check which candidate makes the angle opposite AB the largest angle.
  3. I commit to the strategy of computing the third angle in each case and comparing all three angles against the side-ordering constraint.
  4. Bridge: for each candidate x, compute third angle = 180° − (given angle) − x, then identify the largest angle and the side opposite it.
  5. Tactical decision: reject any case where the largest angle is not opposite the side stated to be longest, and also reject any case where the third angle comes out non-positive.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. List the two candidate values of x from the ambiguous case: x and 180° − x.
  2. For each candidate, compute the third angle as 180° minus the sum of the other two known angles.
  3. Identify the largest of the three angles in each case.
  4. Check which side that largest angle is opposite to, using vertex labelling.
  5. Reject the case where the largest angle is not opposite the stated longest side; accept the remaining case.

Worked Micro-Example

In triangle $ABC$, $\angle BAC = 25°$, and the Sine Rule gives $\sin x = 0.682$ for $x = \angle ACB$, so $x = 43.05°$ or $x = 136.95°$. Given $AB$ is the longest side, $\angle ACB$ must be largest. Case 1: third angle $= 180° - 25° - 43.05° = 111.95°$, which exceeds $43.05°$ — reject. Case 2: third angle $= 180° - 25° - 136.95° = 18.05°$, and $136.95°$ is largest — accept. Hence $x = 136.95°$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Constraint states the triangle is acute
Reject any candidate that produces an angle ≥ 90°; typically the obtuse candidate is eliminated.
Constraint states a specific angle is obtuse
Keep only the candidate making that named angle exceed 90°.
Shortest-side constraint instead of longest
Require the angle opposite the named shortest side to be the smallest of the three angles.
Angle-sum constraint alone
One candidate forces the third angle to be ≤ 0°, eliminating it without needing side-ordering reasoning.
Diagram-only constraint (no worded inequality)
Read the relative side lengths from labelled values in the figure and apply the same opposite-angle ordering.

Common Misreadings

  • Misidentifying which angle is opposite the named longest side, especially when vertex labels and side labels are swapped.
  • Assuming the acute solution from $\sin^{-1}$ is automatically correct without testing the geometric constraint.
  • Treating 'longest side' as adjacent to (rather than opposite) the largest angle.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to verify that the third angle is positive in both cases before applying the side-ordering test.
  • Comparing only x against the given angle and ignoring the third angle, which may itself be the largest.
  • Rounding intermediate angles too early and producing a third angle that appears non-positive when it should be a small positive value.

A-level Scope Notes

This move sits squarely in Edexcel IAL Pure/Core Maths Sine Rule ambiguous-case work; it uses only the Sine Rule, the angle-sum of a triangle, and the standard 'larger side opposite larger angle' fact.
