Strategic Atlas / Trigonometry / Half Angle Substitution
Trigonometry

Apply half-angle identities to collapse (1 ± cos θ) denominators

Family: Half Angle Substitution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

An expression contains sin θ over (1 + cos θ) or sin θ over (1 − cos θ), or their reciprocals. These shapes appear when computing gradients between points like (cos θ, sin θ) and (±1, 0), or when integrating rational trig forms. The cue is a denominator built from 1 ± cos θ alongside a sin θ in the numerator. Equivalently, any ratio that matches 2 sin(θ/2) cos(θ/2) over 2 cos²(θ/2) or 2 sin²(θ/2) is a direct invitation.

Motivation — Why this works

Using sin θ = 2 sin(θ/2) cos(θ/2), 1 + cos θ = 2 cos²(θ/2) and 1 − cos θ = 2 sin²(θ/2), the awkward two-function expressions collapse to a single tan(θ/2) or cot(θ/2). This unifies multiple trig functions into one parameter t = tan(θ/2), which makes elimination, simplification, or integration tractable. The reciprocal pairing of tan(θ/2) and cot(θ/2) often produces clean products (e.g. m₁ m₂ = −4) when two such gradients meet.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the denominator is 1 + cos θ (or 1 − cos θ) sitting under a sin θ — a structural fingerprint of the half-angle identities.
  2. Rule out brute-force expansion in sin θ, cos θ, sec θ, tan θ: it will create multi-function expressions that resist clean elimination.
  3. Commit to the substitution because the target shape 2 sin(θ/2) cos(θ/2) / 2 cos²(θ/2) = tan(θ/2) collapses two functions into one.
  4. Bridge: rewrite numerator and denominator in θ/2 form, cancel the factor of 2 and one cos(θ/2) or sin(θ/2), leaving tan(θ/2) or cot(θ/2).
  5. Tactically, name t = tan(θ/2) so subsequent algebra (line equations, intersection, locus) becomes rational in t.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the (1 ± cos θ) denominator and the sin θ numerator.
  2. Substitute sin θ = 2 sin(θ/2) cos(θ/2).
  3. Substitute 1 + cos θ = 2 cos²(θ/2) or 1 − cos θ = 2 sin²(θ/2) as appropriate.
  4. Cancel common factors to obtain tan(θ/2) or cot(θ/2) (with any leading constant).
  5. Set t = tan(θ/2) so the expression becomes rational in t for downstream work.

Worked Micro-Example

Simplify $\dfrac{2\sin\theta}{1+\cos\theta}$. Using $\sin\theta = 2\sin(\theta/2)\cos(\theta/2)$ and $1+\cos\theta = 2\cos^2(\theta/2)$: $\dfrac{2\cdot 2\sin(\theta/2)\cos(\theta/2)}{2\cos^2(\theta/2)} = 2\tan(\theta/2)$. Similarly $\dfrac{-2\sin\theta}{1-\cos\theta} = -2\cot(\theta/2)$, so with $t=\tan(\theta/2)$ the two gradients are $2t$ and $-2/t$, whose product is $-4$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Gradient between a parametric point (cos θ, sin θ) on a unit circle and a fixed point (±1, 0)
The gradient automatically takes the form ±sin θ/(1 ∓ cos θ), which collapses to ±tan(θ/2) or ∓cot(θ/2).
Integrals of rational functions of sin θ and cos θ (Further Pure)
Use the Weierstrass substitution t = tan(θ/2), giving sin θ = 2t/(1+t²), cos θ = (1−t²)/(1+t²), dθ = 2 dt/(1+t²).
Proving identities involving cosec θ − cot θ or cosec θ + cot θ
Rewrite as (1 − cos θ)/sin θ = tan(θ/2) or (1 + cos θ)/sin θ = cot(θ/2).
Locus/elimination problems where two parametric lines through θ-points must be intersected
Convert each gradient to a single tan(θ/2) form, then eliminate t to get a Cartesian relation.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 1 + cos θ as needing a double-angle expansion in θ rather than a half-angle expansion in θ/2.
  • Reading sin θ/(1 + cos θ) as already 'simplified' and pushing into messy algebra instead of recognising tan(θ/2).
  • Assuming both (1 + cos θ) and (1 − cos θ) collapse to the same half-angle function.

Pitfalls

  • Confusing 1 + cos θ = 2 cos²(θ/2) with 1 − cos θ = 2 sin²(θ/2); swapping these flips tan↔cot.
  • Forgetting the leading 2 from sin θ = 2 sin(θ/2) cos(θ/2) and losing a constant factor.
  • Writing −2 sin θ/(1 − cos θ) as −2 tan(θ/2) instead of the correct −2 cot(θ/2).
  • Cancelling cos(θ/2) or sin(θ/2) without noting the implicit assumption that it is non-zero (matters for domain/range).

A-level Scope Notes

All identities used (sin θ = 2 sin(θ/2) cos(θ/2), 1 ± cos θ = 2 cos²(θ/2) or 2 sin²(θ/2)) are standard Edexcel IAL P3/P4 double-angle results applied at half-angle. The substitution t = tan(θ/2) is within Further Pure scope for integration and parametric/locus work.
