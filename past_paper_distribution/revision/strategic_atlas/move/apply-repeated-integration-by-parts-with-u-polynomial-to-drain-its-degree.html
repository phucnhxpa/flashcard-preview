Integration

Apply repeated integration by parts with u = polynomial to drain its degree

Family: Repeated Ibp Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integrand is a product of a polynomial x^n and a function that cycles or reproduces under integration (sin x, cos x, e^{kx}, e^{-x}, etc.). One application of IBP is not enough because the polynomial still has degree ≥ 1 after a single round. Look out for forms like ∫ x² cos x dx, ∫ x³ e^x dx, ∫ x² sin 2x dx — each phrased as 'find the exact value of …' or 'show that ∫… = …'.

Motivation — Why this works

Setting u = x^n means du = n x^{n−1} dx, so each IBP strips one power off the polynomial. The trig/exp partner integrates to something of the same complexity (sin↔cos, e^x↔e^x), so the residual integral is no harder than the original except in polynomial degree. After n rounds the polynomial factor becomes a constant and the remaining integral is elementary. This is the standard LIATE/'differentiate the algebraic factor' heuristic.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand is a polynomial × {trig or exponential} — neither substitution nor a standard formula handles it directly, so IBP is the tool.
  2. Rule out u = cos x (or u = e^x): differentiating cycles the trig and integrating x^n raises its degree, making the integral harder.
  3. Commit to u = x^n because differentiating it lowers the degree, while the partner integrates cleanly; predict that n applications of IBP will be needed.
  4. Bridge: write down u, dv, du, v explicitly for the first round and apply ∫ u dv = uv − ∫ v du.
  5. Track signs carefully through each round (sin x → −cos x → −sin x …) and remember to keep the outer brackets when substituting the inner result back.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify n (the degree of the polynomial) and choose u = x^n, dv = (trig or exp) dx.
  2. Compute du = n x^{n−1} dx and v by integrating dv.
  3. Apply ∫ u dv = uv − ∫ v du to obtain a new integral whose polynomial factor has degree n−1.
  4. Repeat with u = (current polynomial), dv = (current trig/exp) dx until the polynomial factor is a constant.
  5. Integrate the final pure trig/exp integral and back-substitute carefully, distributing minus signs through brackets.
  6. Add the constant of integration c (for indefinite) or evaluate at limits (for definite).

Worked Micro-Example

Find $\int x^{2}\cos x\,dx$. First IBP: $u=x^{2},\ dv=\cos x\,dx \Rightarrow du=2x\,dx,\ v=\sin x$, giving $x^{2}\sin x-\int 2x\sin x\,dx$. Second IBP on $\int 2x\sin x\,dx$: $u=2x,\ dv=\sin x\,dx \Rightarrow du=2\,dx,\ v=-\cos x$, giving $-2x\cos x+\int 2\cos x\,dx=-2x\cos x+2\sin x$. Therefore $\int x^{2}\cos x\,dx = x^{2}\sin x+2x\cos x-2\sin x+c$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

∫ x^n e^{x} dx (or e^{kx})
Same strategy: u = x^n, dv = e^{kx} dx; v = e^{kx}/k. n rounds of IBP, no sign cycling but watch the 1/k factor accumulating.
∫ x^n sin(ax) dx or ∫ x^n cos(ax) dx
u = x^n, dv = sin(ax) dx (or cos); v carries a 1/a factor. Sign pattern alternates every round.
Definite integral, e.g. ∫₀^{π} x² sin x dx
Same IBP procedure, but evaluate the uv boundary terms at each stage rather than carrying a +c.
∫ x ln x dx (one-shot, polynomial × log)
Reverse the choice: u = ln x (so du simplifies), dv = x dx. Polynomial-degree-reduction logic does NOT apply when a logarithm is present.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating it as a substitution problem (e.g. u = x²) — fails because the trig/exp factor cannot be rewritten in u alone.
  • Picking u = cos x or u = e^x for the first IBP, which makes the polynomial factor grow and the integral harder.
  • Stopping after one IBP and trying to evaluate ∫ 2x sin x dx by inspection.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when integrating sin x to −cos x, especially the double negative in the second round.
  • Failing to bracket the result of the inner IBP before subtracting it, so the outer minus sign is not distributed.
  • Dropping the factor 1/a or 1/k when dv = sin(ax) dx or e^{kx} dx.
  • Forgetting the constant of integration in indefinite integrals or mis-evaluating boundary terms in definite integrals.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL C34/Pure 4: integration by parts ∫ u dv = uv − ∫ v du, applied at most two or three times to polynomial × trig or polynomial × exponential integrands. The tabular (DI) shortcut is not required by the syllabus but mirrors the standard method.
