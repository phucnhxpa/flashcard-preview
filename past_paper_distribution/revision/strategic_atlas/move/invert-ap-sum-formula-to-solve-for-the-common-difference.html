Trigger — When to use

The problem supplies an aggregate quantity such as $S_n$ for an arithmetic progression rather than an individual term, while the first term $a$ (or another parameter) is already known. Phrases like 'the sum of the first 5 terms is 65' or 'the total of the first n terms equals ...' are the surface cues. The unknown $d$ appears only inside the sum, so a single substitution into $S_n=\tfrac{n}{2}(2a+(n-1)d)$ yields a linear equation in $d$.

Motivation — Why this works

The sum formula compactly encodes all $n$ terms of an AP, so plugging in the known $S_n$, $n$, and $a$ collapses the entire progression into one linear equation. This is faster and less error-prone than expanding $a+(a+d)+\dots+(a+(n-1)d)$ by hand. Because $d$ enters the formula linearly, isolating it requires only basic algebra, after which any term $u_k=a+(k-1)d$ or further sums become accessible.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that the only AP datum supplied is a sum $S_n$, not a specific term, so a term-by-term identity won't bite immediately.
  2. Rules out using $u_n=a+(n-1)d$ directly because $S_n\ne u_n$; equating 65 to $u_5$ would be a category error.
  3. Commits to the sum formula because $a$ and $n$ are known, leaving $d$ as the sole unknown in a linear equation.
  4. Bridges by substituting the known values into $S_n=\tfrac{n}{2}(2a+(n-1)d)$ and simplifying the bracket before clearing the fraction.
  5. Decides tactically to multiply both sides by $2/n$ first (cleaner than expanding) and to keep $d$ exact (e.g. $9/2$) rather than decimalising prematurely.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify $a$, $n$, and $S_n$ from the problem statement.
  2. Substitute into $S_n=\tfrac{n}{2}\bigl(2a+(n-1)d\bigr)$.
  3. Simplify the constant part inside the bracket.
  4. Multiply both sides by $2/n$ to clear the leading fraction.
  5. Isolate the $d$-term and divide by its coefficient $(n-1)$.
  6. Use the resulting $d$ in any subsequent term or sum the question asks for.

Worked Micro-Example

Given an AP with $a=4$ and $S_5=65$, find $u_{10}$. Substitute: $\tfrac{5}{2}(2\cdot 4+4d)=65\Rightarrow 8+4d=26\Rightarrow d=\tfrac{9}{2}$. Then $u_{10}=a+9d=4+9\cdot\tfrac{9}{2}=\tfrac{89}{2}=44.5$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$S_n$ and $d$ given, $a$ unknown
Same formula, but solve linearly for $a$: $a=\tfrac{S_n}{n}-\tfrac{(n-1)d}{2}$.
Last term $l$ supplied instead of $d$
Use the alternative form $S_n=\tfrac{n}{2}(a+l)$, which is linear in whichever of $a$ or $l$ is unknown.
Two sums given (e.g. $S_5$ and $S_{10}$)
Form a 2×2 linear system in $a$ and $d$ from two instances of the sum formula.
Sum from term $p$ to term $q$
Write it as $S_q-S_{p-1}$, each evaluated via the AP sum formula.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $S_n=65$ as $u_n=65$ and solving $a+(n-1)d=65$ instead.
  • Reading 'sum of the first 5 terms' as 'fifth partial term' and indexing from 0.
  • Assuming the sequence is geometric and reaching for $S_n=\tfrac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r}$.

Pitfalls

  • Writing $(n-1)$ as $n$, e.g. $(5/2)(2a+5d)$ instead of $(5/2)(2a+4d)$.
  • Distributing $2/n$ to only one term on the right-hand side.
  • Decimalising $d=9/2$ early and accumulating rounding error in $u_{10}$.
  • Mixing up which $n$ to use when the question later asks about a different number of terms.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the standard arithmetic series results $S_n=\tfrac{n}{2}(2a+(n-1)d)=\tfrac{n}{2}(a+l)$ from Edexcel IAL P1/P2 sequences and series, with no techniques beyond elementary linear algebra.
