Select solution method for a quadratic by reading the prescribed answer form

Family: Method Selection Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires the moment a quadratic equation has been formed and a solution method must be chosen. Look for explicit format cues in the question stem: 'give your answer in the form a ± b√c', '(P ± √Q)/R', 'p + q√r', or conversely 'factorise' / 'solve by factorisation'. A surd template signals an irrational discriminant and demands the quadratic formula (or completing the square); an instruction to 'factorise' with neat integer coefficients signals integer factor-pair hunting. Phrasings like 'exact answer in surd form' or 'leave in simplest surd form' are equivalent triggers for the formula route.

Motivation — Why this works

The quadratic formula x = (−b ± √(b²−4ac))/(2a) outputs answers natively in the surd template (P ± √Q)/R, so when that template is prescribed, the formula is the most direct path and guarantees the required shape. Conversely, when the question says 'factorise', the mark scheme rewards the bracketed product (x − p)(x − q), not just the roots, so factor-pair search is the only method that scores full marks. Reading the answer format is therefore a free signal from the examiner about which technique they expect — ignoring it costs either time (failed factorisation attempts on irrational roots) or method marks (using the formula when factorisation was demanded).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the prescribed answer format in the question stem before touching the algebra — this is the dominant signal.
  2. If the format contains a √ symbol with integer placeholders (a ± b√c, (P ± √Q)/R), infer that the discriminant is not a perfect square, so factorisation over integers will fail.
  3. If the instruction is 'factorise', infer that the discriminant IS a perfect square and that the mark scheme requires the bracketed form, ruling out the formula.
  4. Rule out completing the square as a default: it works in both surd cases but introduces extra algebraic steps and fractional intermediates when a ≠ 1.
  5. Commit to the chosen method and immediately read off (a, b, c) from the standard form ax² + bx + c = 0, being careful with signs.
  6. If using the formula, plan the surd simplification step that maps √Q onto the requested b√c shape (extract square factors).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rearrange the quadratic into standard form ax² + bx + c = 0 and read off a, b, c with signs.
  2. If surd format is prescribed: substitute into x = (−b ± √(b²−4ac))/(2a), being careful that −b flips the sign of a negative b.
  3. Compute the discriminant Δ = b² − 4ac as a single number before simplifying.
  4. Simplify √Δ by extracting the largest square factor so the answer matches the requested b√c form.
  5. If 'factorise' is requested instead: search integer factor pairs of ac that sum to b, write as (x − p)(x − q), and verify by expansion.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $x^2 - 3x + 24 = 0$, giving answers in the form $a \pm b\sqrt{c}$. The surd template forbids factorisation, so apply the formula: $x = \frac{3 \pm \sqrt{9 - 96}}{2} = \frac{3 \pm \sqrt{-87}}{2}$ — and since the discriminant is negative, the real-roots case would instead read $x = \frac{3 \pm \sqrt{87}}{2}$, already matching $a \pm b\sqrt{c}$ with $a = 3/2$, etc.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Question says 'express in the form p + q√r' for a single root after completing a square
Completing the square is equally valid and may be more natural; both routes produce the same surd structure.
Question asks for solutions 'to 3 significant figures'
Still use the quadratic formula, but the final step is decimal evaluation rather than surd simplification.
Quadratic has leading coefficient ≠ 1 with surd answer requested, e.g. 16x² − 16x + 1 = 0
Formula is preferred over completing the square because dividing through by 16 introduces fractions; let the formula's 2a denominator absorb the leading coefficient.
Question says 'factorise' with integer coefficients and perfect-square discriminant
Abandon the formula; perform factor-pair search on ac for pairs summing to b.

Common Misreadings

  • Attempting trial-and-error factorisation despite a surd answer template, wasting time before reverting to the formula.
  • Jumping to the quadratic formula out of habit even when the question explicitly says 'factorise', losing the method marks tied to the bracketed form.
  • Treating 'solve' and 'factorise' as interchangeable instructions — they are not.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error on −b when b is already negative: −(−3) = +3, not −3; similarly −(−16) = +16.
  • Arithmetic slip in 4ac, e.g. writing 4 × 16 = 48 instead of 64, or mishandling 4ac when a = 1 and c is moderately large.
  • Failing to extract square factors from √Δ, leaving the answer in a form that does not match the requested b√c template.
  • Forgetting the ± when transcribing the formula, which loses one of the two roots.

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1 onwards). The quadratic formula is given in the formula booklet; completing the square and factorisation are assumed prior knowledge. Do not extend to complex roots unless the question is in Further Pure (FP1) where √(negative) is admissible.
