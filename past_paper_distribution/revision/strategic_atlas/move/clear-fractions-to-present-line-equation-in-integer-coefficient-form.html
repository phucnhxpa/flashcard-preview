Clear fractions to present line equation in integer-coefficient form

Family: Coefficient Matching Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a final line equation has been derived in a form like y = mx + c with fractional m or c, but the question or surrounding context (e.g. the original line given as ax + by = c with integer a, b, c) signals that an integer-coefficient form is expected. Common cues: the stem presents lines as 'ax + by + c = 0' or '2y − 3x = 26'; the mark scheme phrasing 'in the form ax + by + c = 0 where a, b, c are integers'; or simply that the gradient is a clean rational like −2/3. Also fires whenever the answer would otherwise carry an avoidable fraction.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying through by the lowest common denominator preserves the equation (it's an equivalence on both sides) but removes fractions, producing the conventional Edexcel presentation ax + by + c = 0 with integer coefficients. This both matches the style of the given data and protects against mark loss for an 'unsimplified' final form. It also tends to expose structural relationships (e.g. perpendicular lines have coefficients that swap and one sign flips), which is hard to see when one side carries a fraction.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the derived equation is correct but contains a fractional coefficient (e.g. y = (−2/3)x).
  2. Rule out leaving it in y = mx + c form, since the question's source equation was given as integers and the instruction (or convention) suggests 'ax + by + c = 0, integers'.
  3. Commit to clearing fractions because it is a one-line manipulation that loses no information and aligns with the expected presentation.
  4. Identify the LCD of all coefficients (here 3) and multiply every term — including any constant — by it.
  5. Rearrange to the canonical form ax + by + c = 0 (or ax + by = c), and, if a mark scheme demands, ensure the leading coefficient is positive and any common factor is divided out.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the LCD of all fractional coefficients in the equation.
  2. Multiply every term on both sides by the LCD.
  3. Collect variable terms on one side and constants on the other (or move all to one side for ax + by + c = 0 form).
  4. If required, divide through by any common factor and/or flip signs so the leading coefficient is positive.
  5. Verify equivalence by substituting a known point or comparing gradients.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $y = -\tfrac{2}{3}x$, multiply both sides by $3$ to get $3y = -2x$. Rearranging yields $2x + 3y = 0$, the integer-coefficient form. Check: solving for $y$ gives $y = -\tfrac{2}{3}x$, confirming equivalence.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line with fractional gradient and fractional intercept, e.g. $y = \tfrac{5}{4}x - \tfrac{1}{2}$
Use LCD of 4 and 2, which is 4: multiply through to get $4y = 5x - 2$, i.e. $5x - 4y - 2 = 0$.
Equation of a tangent/normal in coordinate geometry asked 'in the form ax + by + c = 0'
After computing gradient and using point-slope, expand and clear fractions, then move all terms to one side.
Perpendicular line through a point with fractional perpendicular gradient
Same clearing step; the swap-and-sign-flip relationship between coefficients of l₁ and l₂ becomes visible only once both are in integer form.
Partial fractions or rearranged identities producing fractional constants
Multiply through by the LCD of denominators to obtain integer-coefficient polynomial identities before comparing coefficients.

Common Misreadings

  • Believing y = mx + c is always the 'final' form, when the question or convention requests integer coefficients.
  • Treating clearing fractions as optional cosmetic work, missing that Edexcel may reserve a presentation mark for it.
  • Confusing 'integer coefficients' with 'positive coefficients' and unnecessarily negating an already-correct form.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to multiply every term by the LCD, especially a constant term or a term that looks fraction-free.
  • Sign errors when moving terms across the equals sign to reach ax + by + c = 0.
  • Failing to divide out a common factor (e.g. leaving 4x + 6y = 0 instead of 2x + 3y = 0) when the mark scheme expects fully simplified integers.
  • Mishandling the LCD when denominators differ (e.g. using 6 when 12 is required).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure presentation convention, used throughout coordinate geometry (P1) and reappearing in tangent/normal questions in P2/P3 and in coefficient-matching contexts (partial fractions, identities).
