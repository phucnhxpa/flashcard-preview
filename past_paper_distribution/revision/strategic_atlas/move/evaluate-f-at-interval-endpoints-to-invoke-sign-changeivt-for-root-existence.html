Algebra & Functions

Evaluate f at interval endpoints to invoke sign-change/IVT for root existence

Family: Sign Change Root Location Frequency: 5 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question contains a directive of the form 'show that f(x) = 0 has a root in [a, b]' or 'show that α lies between a and b' or 'show that the equation has a solution in the interval ...'. Equivalent phrasings include 'verify that a root lies in ...', 'prove there is a solution between ...', or after an iteration 'confirm α ∈ (a, b)'. The function is typically continuous on the interval (polynomial, exponential, logarithmic, trigonometric, or a sum of these) and not solvable in closed form. The interval is short and given numerically (often two consecutive integers, or two decimals differing by 0.1).

Motivation — Why this works

The Intermediate Value Theorem guarantees that a continuous function which takes opposite signs at two points must equal zero somewhere between them. By computing f(a) and f(b) and observing a sign change, together with stating continuity, we obtain a complete and rigorous proof of root existence — without ever solving the equation. This is the cheapest, examiner-expected method for 'show a root exists' prompts and is the only technique that earns full marks at A-level for this directive.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the imperative verb 'show' (or 'prove'/'verify') paired with 'a root lies in [a, b]' — this is the canonical IVT/sign-change cue, not a request to solve.
  2. Rule out algebraic solving (the equation is typically transcendental or a cubic+ with no rational roots) and rule out graphical sketching (not rigorous enough for 'show that' marks).
  3. Commit to the sign-change argument: define f(x) explicitly by moving everything to one side so that the equation becomes f(x) = 0.
  4. Bridge to computation: substitute x = a and x = b into f and evaluate carefully — using radians for trig functions, and being alert to negative/fractional powers.
  5. Tactical check: confirm continuity on [a, b]. Polynomials are continuous on ℝ automatically; for functions with ln, 1/x, tan, x^(1/n) etc., verify no asymptote or undefined point lies inside [a, b].
  6. Conclude with the explicit sentence linking the two ingredients: 'sign change + continuity ⟹ a root α exists in (a, b) by the IVT' — both ingredients must be stated for full marks.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rearrange the equation to the form f(x) = 0 and define f(x) clearly.
  2. Compute f(a) numerically and record its sign.
  3. Compute f(b) numerically and record its sign.
  4. Verify f(a) and f(b) have opposite signs (one positive, one negative).
  5. State that f is continuous on [a, b] (with brief justification if non-polynomial).
  6. Conclude: 'Since f changes sign on [a, b] and f is continuous, by the IVT there exists α ∈ (a, b) with f(α) = 0.'

Worked Micro-Example

Show that $f(x) = x^3 + 4x - 6 = 0$ has a root in $[1, 1.5]$.\\ $f(1) = 1 + 4 - 6 = -1 < 0$.\\ $f(1.5) = 3.375 + 6 - 6 = 3.375 > 0$.\\ $f$ is a polynomial, hence continuous on $[1, 1.5]$. Sign change $+$ continuity $\Rightarrow$ by IVT, $\exists\,\alpha \in (1, 1.5)$ with $f(\alpha) = 0$. $\blacksquare$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Trigonometric equation, e.g. $2\cos(3x) - 3x + 4 = 0$ on $[0.8, 0.9]$
Set calculator to radians; cosine is continuous on ℝ so continuity is automatic, but state it explicitly.
Function involves $\ln x$, $1/x$, or $x^{-1/3}$
Check the interval avoids $x = 0$ (or any other discontinuity); justify continuity by noting all terms are defined on $[a, b]$.
No interval supplied — instructed to 'find an interval of width 1 containing a root'
Trial integer values $f(0), f(1), f(2), \ldots$ until consecutive values give opposite signs; same logic applies.
Trapping a root after iteration to verify accuracy to $n$ d.p.
Evaluate $f$ at $x_n \pm 0.5 \times 10^{-n}$ and check for a sign change to confirm $\alpha = x_n$ correct to $n$ d.p.

Common Misreadings

  • Mistaking 'show a root exists' for 'find the root' and attempting algebraic solution.
  • Believing $f(a) \approx 0$ at a single endpoint suffices — opposite signs at BOTH endpoints are required.
  • Forgetting to evaluate at the second endpoint, or evaluating at the wrong endpoints.
  • Using degrees instead of radians for trig functions when the equation has no degree symbol.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips with negative numbers, decimals, or fractional/negative powers — verify with calculator.
  • Omitting the explicit continuity statement — examiners deduct a mark for this even if signs are correct.
  • Omitting the concluding sentence that links sign-change AND continuity to root existence.
  • Failing to check that the interval contains no discontinuity (e.g. asymptote of $\tan x$, or $x = 0$ for $\ln x$).
  • If both endpoint values share a sign, do not conclude 'no root' — there may be an even number of roots; the wrong interval was chosen.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL scope (Core Pure / FP1 numerical methods chapter). The Intermediate Value Theorem is invoked by name or as 'sign-change rule' — no formula booklet entry is required. Do not generalise to Bolzano's theorem or topological continuity arguments; stay with 'f is continuous and changes sign, therefore a root exists'.
