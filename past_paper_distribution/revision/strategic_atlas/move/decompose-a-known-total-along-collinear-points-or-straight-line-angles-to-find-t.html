Coordinate Geometry

Decompose a known total along collinear points or straight-line angles to find the missing piece

Family: Geometric Deduction Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a diagram contains a known whole (a segment of fixed total length, or a straight-edge angle of π) that is split into adjacent parts meeting at a shared point. Cues include: a point stated to lie ON a line/segment, a centre on a diameter, a tangent point on a chord, or several adjacent angles/regions meeting at one vertex along a straight boundary. Phrased differently: 'three collinear points so DC = DO + OC' or 'three adjacent angles at B summing to π'. If two of the parts (or one part plus the total) are already known, the remaining part is forced by simple subtraction.

Motivation — Why this works

The move exploits the basic additivity of length along a line and of angle along a straight edge: collinear lengths add to the total, and adjacent angles on a straight line sum to π. This converts a geometric constraint directly into a numerical value with one subtraction — no simultaneous equations, coordinates, or trig identities are needed. It is typically the cheapest bridge from 'given data' to 'side/angle of the right triangle I actually need next'.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice a constraint phrase like 'O lies on DC' or 'AB is a straight line through the vertex' — this signals collinearity or a straight-line angle.
  2. Identify the known total: the full segment length (often a side of a rectangle / diameter) or the straight angle π.
  3. Rule out heavier machinery — coordinates, Pythagoras, cosine rule — because additivity already pins the unknown down.
  4. Commit to the decomposition: write Total = Part₁ + Part₂ (+ Part₃ …), with the unknown isolated.
  5. Check the order of the points/angles on the figure carefully so the signs are right (D-O-C, not O-D-C); then subtract to extract the missing length or angle.
  6. Feed the result straight into the next step (e.g. as the adjacent side in a right triangle, or as the apex angle for the cosine rule).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the collinear points (or adjacent angles at a common vertex) and the known total T.
  2. Write the additivity equation: T = a + x (or T = a + b + x for three parts).
  3. Substitute the known values of T, a (and b).
  4. Solve x = T − a (or x = T − a − b).
  5. State units / radian-vs-degree consistency and carry x into the next part of the question.

Worked Micro-Example

Rectangle $ABCD$ with $AB=16$. Point $O$ lies on $DC$ with $OC=12$ (a circle radius). Since $D$-$O$-$C$ are collinear, $DC = DO + OC$, so $DO = 16 - 12 = 4$. This $DO=4$ is then the side adjacent to $\angle ADO$ in right triangle $AOD$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Adjacent angles at a vertex along a straight edge (radian working)
Use total $\pi$ instead of a length: e.g. $\angle CBD = \pi - 1.3 - 1.541 \approx 0.300$ rad.
Centre of a circle lying on a diameter / chord
Same logic: diameter = (radius) + (remaining segment); subtract to get the remaining segment.
Tangent point dividing a chord of known length
Use chord = sum of the two sub-segments; one sub-segment from tangent-length theorem, subtract for the other.
Full-turn angles around an interior point
Replace $\pi$ with $2\pi$ when the angles wrap fully around the point rather than lying along a straight edge.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'O on DC' as merely 'O near DC' and reaching for coordinates instead of additivity.
  • Failing to see that several regions meeting at a vertex share a straight boundary, so their angles sum to π.
  • Confusing a straight-line configuration (sum π) with a full-turn configuration (sum 2π).

Pitfalls

  • Wrong collinear order: writing $DO = DC + OC = 28$ instead of $DC - OC = 4$ when the order is actually D-O-C.
  • Mixing degrees and radians — using 180 in a working that is otherwise in radians, or vice versa.
  • Premature rounding of one of the known parts before subtracting, which inflates the error in the missing piece.
  • Forgetting that 'opposite sides of a rectangle are equal' before substituting (e.g. using DC without first equating it to AB).

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure (P1–P3) geometry of circles, rectangles, and sector/segment problems. No formula-booklet entry is needed — it relies on additivity of length and the straight-angle fact (sum = π rad = 180°). Avoid generalising to directed angles or signed lengths beyond IAL scope.
