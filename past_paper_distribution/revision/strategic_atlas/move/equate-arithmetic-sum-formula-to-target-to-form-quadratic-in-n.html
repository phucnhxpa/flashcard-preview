Sequences & Series

Equate arithmetic sum formula to target to form quadratic in n

Family: Equation Formation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an arithmetic series problem gives a fixed total (e.g. 'after how many terms does the sum reach £18,200?') or a 'show that n satisfies the quadratic …' instruction. Cue phrases include 'total', 'altogether', 'cumulative', 'reaches', or an explicit target equation in n. The presence of both a and d (or enough info to find them) plus a numerical sum confirms the move. If the target form is given, treat it as a roadmap for the final rearrangement.

Motivation — Why this works

The arithmetic sum S_n = n/2·(2a + (n−1)d) contains the product n(n−1)d, which expands to a term in n². Setting S_n equal to a known total therefore yields a quadratic in n that can be solved by factorising or the formula. This converts a 'how many terms' question into standard GCSE/AS algebra. In 'show that' form, the printed quadratic also acts as a check on the expansion.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the question fixes a numerical total for the sum, not the nth term — so S_n is the right formula.
  2. Rule out using u_n = a + (n−1)d alone; that gives a value, not a cumulative total.
  3. Commit to S_n = target because expanding will inevitably produce an n² term, matching the requested quadratic.
  4. Bridge: substitute the given a and d into S_n, then set the expression equal to the target number.
  5. Tactically, clear the fraction early (×2), expand fully, and divide through by any common factor (often ±10) to reach the printed form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify a, d, and the target total T from the context.
  2. Write S_n = n/2·(2a + (n−1)d) and substitute the values of a and d.
  3. Simplify the bracket: combine constant terms with the −d coming from (n−1)d.
  4. Set the simplified S_n equal to T.
  5. Multiply both sides by 2 to remove the fraction.
  6. Expand the product on the left to get a term in n² and a term in n.
  7. Move everything to one side so the equation equals 0.
  8. Divide through by any common factor (watching signs) to match the target form n² + bn + c = 0.

Worked Micro-Example

Lina saves £600 in year 1, decreasing by £10 each year. Then $S_n = \tfrac{n}{2}(2(600)+(n-1)(-10)) = \tfrac{n}{2}(1210-10n)$. Setting $S_n = 18200$ gives $n(1210-10n)=36400$, i.e. $-10n^2+1210n-36400=0$. Dividing by $-10$: $n^2 - 121n + 3640 = 0$, as required.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Geometric series with a fixed total
Use S_n = a(1−r^n)/(1−r) = T; this produces an equation in r^n (exponential), solved by logs, not a quadratic.
Sum exceeds a threshold (inequality)
Form S_n > T, rearrange to a quadratic inequality in n, then solve and take the smallest integer n.
Sum between two indices Σ from k=m to n
Write as S_n − S_{m−1} = T; still expands to a quadratic in n once m is fixed.
Target given as a multiple of the first term (e.g. 'sum is k times a')
Replace T with k·a; algebra is identical, but check for the trivial root n=0 or n=1.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing u_n (nth term) with S_n (sum of first n terms) and equating the wrong quantity to the target.
  • Dropping the (n−1) and writing (2a + nd) inside the bracket.
  • Treating the series as geometric because values decrease, when the decrease is by a constant amount (arithmetic).
  • Reading the target form as already proved and skipping the algebraic working in a 'show that'.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error in expanding (n−1)(−10): the −10 × −1 = +10 is often lost, giving 1200 − 10n instead of 1210 − 10n.
  • Forgetting to multiply the right-hand side by 2 when clearing the n/2 factor.
  • Dividing through by a negative number without flipping every sign, producing wrong coefficients.
  • Stopping at −10n² + 1210n − 36400 = 0 instead of reducing to the printed monic form.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 arithmetic series content; uses only the standard sum formula and quadratic manipulation, with no techniques beyond AS algebra.
