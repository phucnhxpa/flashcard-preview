Strategic Atlas / Differential Equations / Auxiliary Equation Method
Differential Equations

Substitute y = e^(mx) to form the auxiliary equation

Family: Auxiliary Equation Method Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever you see a linear second-order ODE with constant coefficients in the form ay'' + by' + cy = f(x). The homogeneous part ay'' + by' + cy = 0 is the immediate target. Cues include all coefficients being numbers (no x, sin x, etc. multiplying y, y', y''), and the question asking for a 'general solution' or 'complementary function'. Phrases like 'find the complementary function' or 'solve the homogeneous equation' are direct prompts.

Motivation — Why this works

Exponentials are eigenfunctions of d/dx: differentiating e^(mx) returns m·e^(mx), so every term in the ODE becomes a scalar multiple of the same exponential. Substituting y = e^(mx) therefore collapses the differential equation into a polynomial equation in m. Since e^(mx) ≠ 0, dividing through is legal and the resulting auxiliary equation am² + bm + c = 0 is purely algebraic. The roots m₁, m₂ then encode the basis of the solution space, which is two-dimensional by linearity.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the ODE is linear, second-order, and every coefficient of y, y', y'' is a constant — the signature of the auxiliary equation method.
  2. Rule out separation, integrating factor, or series methods: these are either inapplicable (not first-order separable) or beyond IAL FP2 scope.
  3. Commit because the linearity-with-constant-coefficients structure guarantees exponential solutions form a basis of the homogeneous space.
  4. Bridge: write y = e^(mx) as a trial and compute y' = me^(mx), y'' = m²e^(mx) ready to substitute.
  5. Tactically decide whether to factorise the resulting quadratic by inspection or use the quadratic formula, and prepare to interpret the discriminant (two real, repeated, or complex roots) for the next step.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the homogeneous equation ay'' + by' + cy = 0 (drop f(x) for now).
  2. Write the trial solution y = e^(mx); then y' = me^(mx) and y'' = m²e^(mx).
  3. Substitute to get (am² + bm + c)e^(mx) = 0.
  4. Divide by e^(mx) ≠ 0 to obtain the auxiliary equation am² + bm + c = 0.
  5. Solve for m by factorisation or the quadratic formula, ready to build the complementary function from the roots.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve y'' - 5y' + 6y = 0. Trial y = e^(mx) gives (m² - 5m + 6)e^(mx) = 0, so m² - 5m + 6 = 0. Factorising: (m-2)(m-3) = 0, hence m = 2 or m = 3. The general solution is y = Ae^(2x) + Be^(3x).

Variants — Same move, different surface

First-order linear constant-coefficient ODE ay' + by = 0
The same trial gives a linear auxiliary equation am + b = 0, yielding a single exponential y = Ae^(-b/a · x).
Inhomogeneous equation ay'' + by' + cy = f(x)
Use the auxiliary equation only for the complementary function; add a particular integral found by trial form matched to f(x).
Repeated root of the auxiliary equation
When m² + bm + c has a double root m = α, the basis becomes {e^(αx), x·e^(αx)} rather than two distinct exponentials.
Complex roots m = p ± qi
Rewrite the complex exponential basis in real form: y = e^(px)(A cos qx + B sin qx).
Euler/Cauchy equation ax²y'' + bxy' + cy = 0
Not constant-coefficient; instead use the trial y = x^m, which produces an analogous indicial equation.

Common Misreadings

  • Applying the method to variable-coefficient ODEs such as x²y'' + xy' + y = 0, where e^(mx) is not an eigenfunction.
  • Confusing the auxiliary equation (algebraic, in m) with the original ODE and trying to 'solve' am² + bm + c = 0 as if it were a differential equation.
  • Forgetting to handle the right-hand side f(x): treating the auxiliary equation roots as the full solution rather than just the complementary function.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when reading off a, b, c, especially if the ODE is presented as y'' = 5y' - 6y rather than y'' - 5y' + 6y = 0.
  • Dropping the e^(mx) factor incorrectly (e.g. setting it to 1) instead of dividing through.
  • Using only one constant in the general solution; two arbitrary constants A, B are required for a second-order ODE.
  • Misinterpreting complex roots and writing y = Ae^((p+qi)x) + Be^((p-qi)x) as a final answer instead of converting to real trigonometric form.

A-level Scope Notes

This is the standard FP2 method for linear second-order ODEs with constant coefficients in Edexcel IAL Further Maths; cubic or higher auxiliary equations and variable-coefficient methods (other than the standard substitutions explicitly given in questions) lie outside the syllabus.
