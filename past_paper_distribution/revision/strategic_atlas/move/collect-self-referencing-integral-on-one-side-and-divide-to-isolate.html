Collect self-referencing integral on one side and divide to isolate

Family: Collect And Isolate Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a derivation (typically integration by parts on I_n = ∫ ... dx) produces an equation where I_n appears on both sides, e.g. I_n = A − k·I_n or I_n = B·I_{n-2} − c·I_n. The equation is linear in the unknown integral, with no other I_n-like terms hidden inside. Alternative phrasings: 'I_n appears recursively', 'the integral reproduces itself', 'cyclic integral'. This is the standard closing step of reduction-formula proofs.

Motivation — Why this works

The IBP step plus a Pythagorean or algebraic identity (e.g. sin²x = 1 − cos²x, sec²x = 1 + tan²x) is engineered precisely so that the original integral reappears with a numerical coefficient. Treating I_n as a single algebraic symbol turns the integral equation into a linear equation aI_n = (stuff), which can be solved by the same collect-and-divide move used for x in 5x = 3 + 2x. The principle is that ∫ … dx is just a number/function-of-n, so all linear-equation manipulations are valid.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the same symbol I_n sitting on both sides of the equation after IBP and identity substitution.
  2. I rule out further integration or substitution — there is nothing left to integrate; the remaining work is purely algebraic.
  3. I commit to treating I_n as an unknown constant in n, because the equation is linear in it.
  4. First concrete operation: add (or subtract) the I_n term from the RHS to the LHS to gather all I_n on one side.
  5. Tactical: simplify the coefficient using 1 + (n−1) = n (or similar), then divide both sides by that coefficient to express I_n in terms of I_{n−2} (or I_{n−1}).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Start from I_n = (n−1)I_{n−2} − (n−1)I_n.
  2. Add (n−1)I_n to both sides: I_n + (n−1)I_n = (n−1)I_{n−2}.
  3. Combine like terms on LHS: nI_n = (n−1)I_{n−2}.
  4. Divide both sides by n: I_n = ((n−1)/n) I_{n−2}.
  5. State the reduction formula together with its valid range of n (usually n ≥ 2).

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose IBP on I_n = ∫₀^{π/2} sinⁿx dx yields I_n = (n−1)I_{n−2} − (n−1)I_n. Add (n−1)I_n: nI_n = (n−1)I_{n−2}. Divide by n: I_n = \frac{n-1}{n} I_{n-2}, valid for n ≥ 2.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Reduction for ∫ cosⁿx dx with limits 0 to π/2
Same algebra; identity used is cos²x = 1 − sin²x, but the collect-and-isolate step is identical, giving I_n = ((n−1)/n) I_{n−2}.
Reduction for ∫ tanⁿx dx
Identity tan²x = sec²x − 1 produces I_n + I_{n−2} = (tan^{n−1}x)/(n−1); only one I_n on each side, so collect-and-isolate is a single subtraction.
Reduction for ∫ secⁿx dx
IBP gives (n−1)I_n = sec^{n−2}x tan x + (n−2)I_{n−2}; divide by (n−1) to isolate I_n.
Cyclic indefinite integrals like ∫ eˣ sin x dx
Two IBPs return the original integral with coefficient −1; collect onto one side: 2I = eˣ(sin x − cos x), then divide by 2.
Linear equations in x with x on both sides
Same collect-and-divide logic at GCSE/AS level; reduction formula is the Further-Maths analogue with I_n in place of x.

Common Misreadings

  • Thinking I_n on the RHS is a different object from I_n on the LHS because of the (n−1) factor.
  • Believing further integration is needed, when in fact the remaining step is purely algebraic.
  • Confusing I_n with I_{n−2} and trying to cancel them as if they were equal.

Pitfalls

  • Subtracting (n−1)I_n from both sides instead of adding, producing a sign error and the wrong coefficient.
  • Forgetting that 1 + (n−1) = n, and writing the coefficient as (n−1) instead.
  • Dividing by n without noting the restriction n ≠ 0 (and stating the valid n for the recurrence).
  • Losing the boundary term from IBP if the integral is indefinite or has non-vanishing limits.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard closing step in Edexcel IAL Further Pure reduction-formula questions (FP2/FP3) and in cyclic IBP integrals in Pure Maths; uses only linear-equation manipulation.
