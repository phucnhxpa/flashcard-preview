Trigonometry

Factor out sin x to split zero and reduced-equation cases

Family: Case Splitting Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a trig equation set to zero has every term sharing a common factor of sin x (or cos x, tan x). Typical surfaces: sums of sin³x, sin x, sin x·cos x, or sin 2x mixed with sin x. A clue is a question that explicitly writes the target form 'sin x(...) = 0', or asks for all roots in a range that includes points where sin x = 0. Also fires whenever a student is tempted to divide by sin x to simplify.

Motivation — Why this works

Factoring preserves all roots, whereas dividing by sin x silently discards the solutions where sin x = 0. The zero-product principle then converts one higher-degree trig equation into two independent, lower-degree problems: a trivial sin x = 0 case and a residual equation that is usually quadratic in sin x or cos x. This mirrors the standard polynomial tactic of extracting common factors before applying any identity.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that every term in the rearranged equation contains a sin x (or cos x) factor.
  2. Rule out dividing by sin x — that would lose the sin x = 0 roots, and the target form sin x(...) = 0 confirms they are wanted.
  3. Commit to factoring sin x out so the zero-product law produces two independent cases.
  4. Bridge: move all terms to one side first, then write sin x · [bracket] = 0.
  5. Inside the bracket, decide whether to use sin²x = 1 − cos²x to convert to a single-variable quadratic before solving Case 2.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rearrange the equation so the RHS is 0.
  2. Identify the common trig factor (here sin x) shared by every term.
  3. Factor it out to obtain sin x · (residual expression) = 0.
  4. Apply the zero-product principle: Case 1 sin x = 0; Case 2 residual = 0.
  5. Solve Case 1 directly (x = 0, π, 2π, … in range).
  6. Simplify Case 2 (often via sin²x = 1 − cos²x) into a quadratic in one trig ratio and solve.
  7. Combine all roots and check each lies in the required interval.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $3\sin^3 x - 5\sin x + 7\sin x\cos x = 0$ for $0 \le x \le 2\pi$.\\ Factor: $\sin x\,(3\sin^2 x - 5 + 7\cos x) = 0$.\\ Case 1: $\sin x = 0 \Rightarrow x = 0,\ \pi,\ 2\pi$.\\ Case 2: use $\sin^2 x = 1-\cos^2 x$ to get $-3\cos^2 x + 7\cos x - 2 = 0$, i.e. $3\cos^2 x - 7\cos x + 2 = 0$, giving $\cos x = \tfrac{1}{3}$ (the root $\cos x = 2$ is rejected). Solve in range and combine with Case 1.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation $\sin 2x = 2\sin x$
Expand $\sin 2x = 2\sin x\cos x$, rearrange to $2\sin x(\cos x - 1) = 0$, then split into $\sin x = 0$ and $\cos x = 1$ rather than dividing by $\sin x$.
Equation with common cos x factor, e.g. $\cos x + \sin 2x = 0$
Rewrite $\sin 2x = 2\sin x\cos x$, factor $\cos x(1 + 2\sin x) = 0$, split into $\cos x = 0$ and $\sin x = -\tfrac{1}{2}$.
Equation $\tan x \cdot f(x) = \tan x$
Rearrange to $\tan x(f(x) - 1) = 0$ rather than cancelling $\tan x$, retaining the $\tan x = 0$ solutions.
Higher-power surface like $\sin^3 x = \sin x$
Factor to $\sin x(\sin^2 x - 1) = 0$, then $\sin x(\sin x-1)(\sin x+1) = 0$, giving three sub-cases.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the equation as a cubic in sin x and trying to apply the cubic formula or factor theorem instead of extracting the common sin x.
  • Believing that because the original equation had a non-zero RHS, dividing by sin x is safe — missing that after moving to = 0 the sin x = 0 case is valid.
  • Overlooking the sin x · cos x term and only factoring sin x from the first two terms.

Pitfalls

  • Dividing by sin x and permanently losing roots such as x = 0, π, 2π.
  • Forgetting to reject impossible values from the residual quadratic (e.g. cos x = 2).
  • Failing to list every Case 1 solution in the given interval (often x = 0 or x = 2π is omitted at endpoints).
  • Mis-substituting sin²x = 1 − cos²x with a sign error when reducing the residual.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the zero-product principle and the Pythagorean identity sin²x + cos²x = 1, both standard in Edexcel IAL P3/P4 trigonometry. No techniques outside the syllabus are required.
