Differential Equations

Rewrite csc/sec/cot as reciprocals to expose separable form

Family: Variable Separation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a first-order ODE has dy/dx multiplied (or divided) by a reciprocal trig function of x such as csc(ax), sec(ax) or cot(ax), while the other side is a pure function of y. Typical surface: f(x) dy/dx = g(y) where f(x) is a reciprocal trig expression. The reciprocal form disguises an otherwise standard separable equation, so the move is to convert csc→1/sin, sec→1/cos, cot→cos/sin before separating.

Motivation — Why this works

A separable ODE requires the x-side to be directly integrable after rearrangement. Reciprocal trig functions like csc(2x) are not in the standard integral list at A-level, but their reciprocals sin(2x), cos(2x) are. Rewriting csc(ax)=1/sin(ax) ensures that when we divide through, the x-side becomes a standard integrable trig function, making the separation both legal and practically useful.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that dy/dx is multiplied by a pure function of x (a reciprocal trig term) and the other side depends only on y — the hallmark of separability.
  2. Rule out integrating factors or substitution: there is no linear-in-y structure that would benefit, and no composite to substitute away.
  3. Commit to separation because the variables are already cleanly partitioned between the two sides.
  4. Bridge: to make the x-side integrable, replace the reciprocal trig function by its reciprocal identity (csc=1/sin, sec=1/cos) so it ends up in the numerator after division.
  5. Tactical decision: divide both sides by the full y-coefficient and by the reciprocal trig factor in one go, keeping any numeric constant (like the 3) tracked carefully.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the ODE as f(x) dy/dx = g(y) with f(x) involving a reciprocal trig function.
  2. Replace csc(ax) by 1/sin(ax) (or sec(ax) by 1/cos(ax), cot(ax) by cos(ax)/sin(ax)).
  3. Divide both sides by g(y) and by the constant/trig coefficient of dy/dx so that 1/csc → sin appears on the x-side.
  4. Write the equation in differential form: dy/g(y) = [sin(ax)/c] dx.
  5. Integrate both sides separately, remembering the chain factor 1/a from integrating sin(ax) or cos(ax).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $3\csc(2x)\,\dfrac{dy}{dx}=y(1+2\ln y)$. Rewrite as $\dfrac{3}{\sin(2x)}\dfrac{dy}{dx}=y(1+2\ln y)$, then separate: $\dfrac{dy}{y(1+2\ln y)}=\dfrac{\sin(2x)}{3}\,dx$. Integrating: $\tfrac{1}{2}\ln|1+2\ln y|=-\dfrac{\cos(2x)}{6}+C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$\sec(x)\,dy/dx = y^2$
Rewrite $\sec x = 1/\cos x$ to get $dy/y^2 = \cos x\,dx$.
$\cot(x)\,dy/dx = e^y$
Rewrite $\cot x = \cos x/\sin x$, separate as $e^{-y}\,dy = (\sin x/\cos x)\,dx = \tan x\,dx$.
$dy/dx = y\,\csc(x)$
Reciprocal trig sits on the y-side direction; separate as $dy/y = \csc x\,dx$, here keeping csc since $\int\csc x\,dx$ is not in IAL scope — so prefer rearrangement that puts sin on the y-side instead when possible.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading $3\csc(2x)\,dy/dx$ as $3\csc(2x\cdot dy/dx)$, treating dy/dx as the argument of csc.
  • Believing csc(2x) is itself directly integrable and skipping the reciprocal rewrite.
  • Confusing whether to multiply or divide by csc(2x), producing $\csc^2(2x)$ on one side.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to divide the constant coefficient (e.g. the 3) when separating, leaving a stray factor.
  • Sign error when integrating sin(2x): $\int \sin(2x)\,dx = -\tfrac{1}{2}\cos(2x)+C$.
  • Missing the $1/a$ chain factor when integrating $\sin(ax)$ or $\cos(ax)$.
  • Dropping the $+C$ before applying boundary conditions.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 separable ODE techniques; uses only standard reciprocal trig identities and integrals of $\sin(ax)$, $\cos(ax)$ that are on the formula list.
