Differentiation

Rewrite roots and reciprocals as index powers before differentiating

Family: Index Form Preparation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a function to be differentiated (or integrated) contains surds like $\sqrt{x}$, $\sqrt[3]{x}$, $x\sqrt{x}$, or reciprocal terms like $1/x$, $k/x^n$, or quotients with a single-power denominator. Cue phrases include 'Find $\frac{dy}{dx}$' applied to expressions mixing polynomial, root, and fraction terms. Also fires when a function is presented as a single fraction with a monomial denominator that can be split term-by-term. The marker is: at least one term is not yet in the form $ax^n$ with an explicit numerical $n$.

Motivation — Why this works

The power rule $\frac{d}{dx}(ax^n) = anx^{n-1}$ requires each term to be a constant multiple of $x$ raised to an explicit numerical exponent. By the index laws, $\sqrt[m]{x^k} = x^{k/m}$ and $1/x^k = x^{-k}$, so every algebraic root or reciprocal can be rewritten in this canonical form. Once all terms are in $ax^n$ form, differentiation collapses to a single mechanical pass with no need for quotient or chain rules. This unification dramatically reduces both effort and error risk on routine A-level differentiation questions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the expression contains terms that are not in $ax^n$ form — a surd and/or a reciprocal sit alongside ordinary polynomial terms.
  2. I rule out the quotient rule for $k/x$ and any 'special rule' for $\sqrt{x}$: these are unnecessary heavy machinery for what is really a negative or fractional index.
  3. I commit to a uniform strategy: convert every term to $ax^n$ first, then apply the power rule in one sweep.
  4. Concretely, I read each non-standard term and pick the index-law identity it needs: $\sqrt{x}=x^{1/2}$, $1/x=x^{-1}$, $1/x^2=x^{-2}$, $\sqrt[3]{x}=x^{1/3}$, $x\sqrt{x}=x^{3/2}$.
  5. I keep the original coefficient attached to the new power (sign and constant unchanged), then differentiate term by term using $\frac{d}{dx}(ax^n)=anx^{n-1}$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify every term not already in $ax^n$ form (surds, reciprocals, single-fraction quotients).
  2. Apply index laws: $\sqrt[m]{x^k} \to x^{k/m}$, $1/x^k \to x^{-k}$, and split any quotient with monomial denominator term-by-term.
  3. Preserve the original coefficient and sign of each term during conversion.
  4. Rewrite the full function as a sum $\sum a_i x^{n_i}$ with explicit numerical exponents.
  5. Differentiate each term using the power rule: $a_i x^{n_i} \mapsto a_i n_i x^{n_i - 1}$.
  6. Optionally convert back to surd/fraction form if the question's answer style demands it.

Worked Micro-Example

Differentiate $y = \tfrac{1}{4}x^3 - 3\sqrt{x} + \tfrac{17}{x}$.\\ Rewrite: $y = \tfrac{1}{4}x^3 - 3x^{1/2} + 17x^{-1}$.\\ Differentiate term-by-term: $\frac{dy}{dx} = \tfrac{3}{4}x^2 - \tfrac{3}{2}x^{-1/2} - 17x^{-2}$.\\ If desired: $\frac{dy}{dx} = \tfrac{3}{4}x^2 - \dfrac{3}{2\sqrt{x}} - \dfrac{17}{x^2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Higher-order roots, e.g. $\sqrt[3]{x}$ or $\sqrt[3]{x^2}$
Rewrite as $x^{1/3}$ or $x^{2/3}$ before applying the power rule.
Mixed product like $x\sqrt{x}$ or $x^2\sqrt{x}$
Combine indices: $x \cdot x^{1/2} = x^{3/2}$, $x^2 \cdot x^{1/2} = x^{5/2}$.
Single fraction with monomial denominator, e.g. $\dfrac{x^2+3}{\sqrt{x}}$
Split term-by-term: $\dfrac{x^2}{x^{1/2}} + \dfrac{3}{x^{1/2}} = x^{3/2} + 3x^{-1/2}$.
Integration problems with the same surface (e.g. $\int(\sqrt{x}+1/x^2)\,dx$)
Same index-form preparation, then apply $\int x^n\,dx = \tfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}+C$ (with $n\neq -1$).
Higher reciprocal powers like $5/x^3$
Rewrite as $5x^{-3}$; differentiate to $-15x^{-4}$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $1/x$ as a candidate for the quotient rule rather than as $x^{-1}$.
  • Mistaking $\sqrt{x}$ for $x^2$ (confusing root with square).
  • Reading $\dfrac{1}{x^2}$ as $x^{1/2}$ by conflating root and reciprocal notations.
  • Trying to differentiate $\dfrac{x^2+3}{\sqrt{x}}$ as a quotient instead of splitting the fraction first.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping or altering the coefficient when rewriting, e.g. $-3\sqrt{x} \to -3x^2$ instead of $-3x^{1/2}$.
  • Sign errors on negative exponents: $17/x \to 17x^{1}$ instead of $17x^{-1}$.
  • After differentiating, miscomputing $n-1$ for fractional exponents (e.g. $1/2 - 1 = -1/2$, not $1/2$).
  • Forgetting to multiply by the original coefficient when applying the power rule (e.g. $-3x^{1/2} \to -\tfrac{1}{2}x^{-1/2}$ instead of $-\tfrac{3}{2}x^{-1/2}$).
  • Failing to split a quotient term-by-term and instead leaving it as a single fraction.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1/P2) differentiation and integration of $ax^n$; no chain, product, or quotient rule is required for this preparatory move.
