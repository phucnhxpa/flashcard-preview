Trigonometry

Apply double-angle identity to rewrite quadratic trig terms or sin·cos products as 2θ form

Family: Double Angle Rewrite Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

This move fires when the expression contains either a product sinθ·cosθ or a squared term cos²θ / sin²θ, AND either (a) the question explicitly demands a target form involving cos2θ or sin2θ, or (b) you need to simplify/optimise/solve and a single function of 2θ would collapse two trig functions into one. Visual cues: '8 sinθ cosθ = 3', '3cos²x − 7cosx + 2', 'in the form a cos2x + b cosx + c', 'minimum value of …/(sinθ cosθ)', or an integrand like ∫sin x cos x dx. If you see two trig functions of θ multiplied (or one squared) where a single function of 2θ would suffice, this is the move.

Motivation — Why this works

The double-angle identities sin2θ = 2 sinθ cosθ and cos2θ = 2cos²θ − 1 = 1 − 2sin²θ are the unique standard tools that link quadratic/product expressions in θ to linear expressions in 2θ. Rewriting collapses two trig functions into one, which (i) matches a required exam-form with integer coefficients, (ii) reduces an equation to 'sin 2θ = k' or 'cos 2θ = k' which is solved by a single arc-function, and (iii) exposes a maximum value of 1 (or −1) for optimisation. It is essentially the 'product-to-single-function' or 'square-to-linear' compressor.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structural cue: the expression contains sinθ cosθ as a product, or cos²θ / sin²θ as a square — a hallmark of a hidden double-angle.
  2. Rule out alternatives: Pythagorean identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 doesn't help here because it doesn't introduce 2θ; factoring won't reduce the function count; t = tan(θ/2) substitution is overkill.
  3. Commit because the question's target form (or the optimisation/solving goal) explicitly mentions 2θ, or because reducing to one trig function in 2θ is clearly the cleanest route.
  4. Choose the correct variant: for sinθ cosθ use sin2θ = 2 sinθ cosθ; for cos²θ when the target has cos2θ use cos²θ = (1+cos2θ)/2; for sin²θ use sin²θ = (1−cos2θ)/2.
  5. Tactically plan denominator-clearing: substitution will introduce a factor of ½, so multiply through by 2 at the end to recover integer coefficients (or absorb the ½ into the constant on the RHS when solving).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the offending term: sinθ cosθ, cos²θ, or sin²θ.
  2. Substitute the matching identity: sinθ cosθ → ½ sin2θ; cos²θ → (1+cos2θ)/2; sin²θ → (1−cos2θ)/2.
  3. Distribute and collect like terms (a constant shift typically appears from the '+1' or '−1' inside the identity).
  4. If integer coefficients are required, multiply the whole expression by 2 to clear the ½.
  5. Read off the requested constants, or solve sin2θ = k / cos2θ = k by arc-function and the standard general solution.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $8\sin\theta\cos\theta = 3$.\n Recognise $\sin\theta\cos\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}\sin 2\theta$, so $8\cdot\tfrac12\sin 2\theta = 3$, giving $4\sin 2\theta = 3$ and hence $\sin 2\theta = \tfrac{3}{4}$. Now solve $2\theta = \arcsin(3/4)$ etc.\n Similarly, $3\cos^2 x - 7\cos x + 2 = 3\cdot\tfrac{1+\cos 2x}{2} - 7\cos x + 2$; multiplying by 2 gives $3\cos 2x - 14\cos x + 7$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Expression contains sin²θ and target form needs cos2θ
Use sin²θ = (1 − cos2θ)/2 instead of the cos² version; otherwise the procedure is identical.
Optimisation: minimising an expression of the form k/(sinθ cosθ)
Rewrite sinθ cosθ = ½ sin2θ, then use |sin2θ| ≤ 1 to bound the denominator and find the extremum at 2θ = ±π/2.
Integration: ∫ sin x cos x dx or ∫ cos²x dx
Same identity rewrites the integrand into a directly integrable single function of 2x; e.g. ∫sin x cos x dx = ∫½ sin2x dx = −¼ cos2x + C.
Expression contains a tanθ ratio rather than sin·cos product
Use tan2θ = 2tanθ/(1 − tan²θ) — the analogous double-angle compressor for tan.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing sin2θ = 2 sinθ cosθ with the Pythagorean identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1; both involve sin and cos but serve different purposes.
  • Reading cos²θ as cos(θ²) or as cos2θ — the squaring is on the function value, not the angle, so the (1+cos2θ)/2 identity is needed to bridge to cos2θ.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the factor of 2: writing sin2θ = sinθ cosθ instead of 2 sinθ cosθ, or forgetting the ½ when substituting.
  • Choosing the wrong cos2θ variant (cos2θ = 1 − 2sin²θ when you needed the cos² version, or vice versa).
  • Forgetting to multiply through by 2 to clear fractions when the question demands integer coefficients a, b, c.
  • In optimisation, omitting the absolute value on |sin2θ|, missing the negative-product extremum cases.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3 (and reinforced in P4 integration). The identities sin2A = 2 sinA cosA, cos2A = 2cos²A − 1 = 1 − 2sin²A appear in the IAL formula booklet under 'Trigonometric identities'. Avoid invoking the Weierstrass t = tan(A/2) substitution here — it's a different tool and not required for these compressions.
