Algebra & Functions

Intersect all constraints including implicit positivity to fix the feasible range

Family: Constraint Intersection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires on 'hence state the range of values of x' (or 'find the set of values') after earlier parts have produced two or more separate inequalities. Also fires when x represents a physical quantity (length, time, probability, count) so an unstated positivity or boundedness condition is in force. Cue words: 'hence', 'combine', 'all values for which', or a context phrase like 'where x cm is the length of...'.

Motivation — Why this works

Each derived inequality is a necessary condition but not sufficient on its own; the feasible set is the intersection of every condition, including any implicit physical constraint. Geometrically, sketching the solution sets on a single number line and shading the overlap gives the answer immediately. The implicit constraint (e.g. x>0) can override a weaker explicit lower bound coming from a quadratic. Strict vs non-strict status is inherited from the originating inequalities.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that the question word 'hence' instructs combining results from earlier parts rather than re-deriving.
  2. Rules out reporting any single part's range as the final answer, and rules out a union ('or') interpretation.
  3. Commits to listing every active constraint, including the unstated physical one (x>0 because x is a length).
  4. Bridges to drawing a number line and marking each constraint as a shaded interval.
  5. Inside the move, identifies the binding (tightest) lower and upper bounds and inherits the strictness of each.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. List every constraint: explicit inequalities from previous parts plus implicit positivity/domain conditions.
  2. Sketch each as an interval on a single number line (open/closed circles per strictness).
  3. Identify the tightest (largest) lower bound and the tightest (smallest) upper bound.
  4. Discard dominated constraints (those superseded by a stricter one).
  5. Write the intersection as a single compound inequality, preserving strictness.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose part (a) gives $x>1.5$, part (b) gives $-20$. On a number line: $x>0$ dominates $x>-2$; then $x>1.5$ dominates $x>0$. The upper bound $x<2$ stands. Intersection: $1.5

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic inequality combined with a domain restriction from a square root or logarithm
Add the implicit domain (e.g. argument of $\ln$ positive, radicand non-negative) before intersecting.
Trigonometric equation/inequality restricted to a stated interval such as $0\le\theta<2\pi$
Intersect the periodic solution set with the stated interval rather than with a positivity condition.
Mixed strict and non-strict inequalities (e.g. $\le$ from a discriminant condition with $<$ from a context)
Track endpoint inclusion carefully; the intersection's endpoint is included only if every contributing constraint includes it.
Probability or count contexts where $0\le p\le 1$ or $n\in\mathbb{Z}^+$
Append the natural bounds and, for integer variables, list the integer values inside the intersection.

Common Misreadings

  • Forgetting the implicit $x>0$ from a length/area context and reporting a range that includes negative values.
  • Combining with 'or' (union) instead of 'and' (intersection).
  • Quoting only the most recent part's inequality as the final answer because of the word 'hence'.
  • Treating a quadratic inequality's two-sided solution as the final answer without checking the linear constraint.

Pitfalls

  • Switching strict to non-strict (or vice versa) when transcribing the bounds.
  • Mis-identifying the binding bound when the numbers are close (e.g. $1.318\ldots$ vs $1.5$).
  • Dropping a constraint that looks 'redundant' without checking it actually is dominated.
  • Writing the final answer as two separate inequalities rather than a single compound statement.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure content: combining linear and quadratic inequalities with contextual domain constraints, presented on a number line. No linear programming or multivariable feasible regions required.
