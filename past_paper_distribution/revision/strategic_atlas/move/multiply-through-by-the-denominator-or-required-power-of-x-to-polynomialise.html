Algebra & Functions

Multiply through by the denominator (or required power of x) to polynomialise

Family: Clear Denominators And Simplify Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the equation contains a rational expression with a polynomial denominator (e.g. (θ+2)²) OR negative powers of a variable (e.g. x⁻³) sitting alongside positive powers or constants. Visual cues: a fraction equated to a polynomial, a 'show that ... = 0' target written as a clean polynomial with small integer coefficients, or a stationary-point equation like −8x⁻³ + 18x = 0 mixing index signs. Equivalently phrased: 'there is a single denominator I can kill' or 'I have a negative exponent I want gone'. The presence of a printed target polynomial is a strong hint that clearing then dividing by a common factor is required.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying every term by the denominator (or by x to the magnitude of the most negative exponent) clears fractions/negative powers in one move, converting a rational equation into a polynomial equation that is far easier to expand, factorise, or solve. Mathematically this uses the principle that multiplying both sides of an equation by a non-zero quantity preserves the solution set — provided the multiplier is non-zero on the relevant domain. After clearing, comparing coefficients with the printed target often reveals a common integer factor that should be divided out so the final form matches exactly.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the obstruction: either a fraction with denominator D(x) (e.g. (θ+2)²) or a negative-power term like x⁻³ that prevents clean factoring/rearrangement.
  2. Rule out alternatives: factoring out the negative power (e.g. x⁻³(−8 + 18x⁴) = 0) is valid but awkward; cross-multiplying piecewise risks missing a term; expanding before clearing creates messy fractional coefficients.
  3. Commit to multiplying every term by D(x) (or by xᵏ where k is the magnitude of the most negative exponent) — this is the cleanest single operation that removes all denominators/negative indices simultaneously.
  4. Check validity: confirm the multiplier is non-zero on the stated domain (e.g. x > 0 given, or (θ+2)² ≠ 0 in context) so no solutions are introduced or lost.
  5. Execute, then expand carefully (e.g. (θ+2)² = θ² + 4θ + 4, NOT θ² + 4) and collect terms.
  6. Compare with the printed target: if all coefficients share a common factor (e.g. all multiples of 4), divide through before the final rearrangement to keep numbers small and match the target's leading coefficient.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the denominator D(x) or the most negative power x⁻ᵏ in the equation.
  2. Multiply EVERY term on both sides by D(x) (or by xᵏ).
  3. Simplify using index laws (e.g. x · x³ = x⁴) and expand brackets fully — middle terms in (a+b)² must not be dropped.
  4. Collect like terms on one side to obtain a polynomial equation = 0 (or isolate to solve).
  5. Inspect coefficients for a common integer factor; divide through if it gives the simplest form / matches a printed target.
  6. Rearrange to the required target form.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $12 = \dfrac{100\theta}{(\theta+2)^2}$. Multiply by $(\theta+2)^2$: $12(\theta+2)^2 = 100\theta$. Expand: $12\theta^2 + 48\theta + 48 = 100\theta$. Divide by 4: $3\theta^2 + 12\theta + 12 = 25\theta$. Rearrange: $3\theta^2 - 13\theta + 12 = 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation with a single negative-power term, e.g. −8x⁻³ + 18x = 0 with x > 0
Multiply through by x³ to get 18x⁴ − 8 = 0, then x⁴ = 4/9. Same logic — kill the negative exponent by raising the multiplier to its magnitude.
Mixed lower negative powers, e.g. 2x⁻¹ + 3x = 5
Multiply through by x (the smallest power needed): 2 + 3x² = 5x, then standard quadratic.
Surd denominator like 1/(√a − √b)
Multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate (√a + √b) — same 'kill the awkward denominator' logic, executed via difference of squares.
Equation with two separate denominators
Multiply through by the product (or LCM) of the denominators so every term becomes polynomial.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating x⁻³ as something to be 'moved across' rather than cleared, e.g. flipping signs and writing 8x⁻³ + 18x = 0.
  • Seeing a fraction and trying partial fractions or substitution when straight clearing is faster.
  • Mistaking the denominator (θ+2)² for (θ²+2²) and only multiplying by θ²+4.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to multiply EVERY term — especially the constant or the term already free of the denominator.
  • Expanding (θ+2)² incorrectly as θ² + 4 (losing the 4θ middle term).
  • Index slip: writing 18x · x³ as 18x³ instead of 18x⁴.
  • Sign error when rearranging large numbers (e.g. 12θ − 100θ): often avoided by dividing by the common factor first, before moving terms across.
  • Multiplying by an expression that could be zero on the domain, thereby introducing spurious solutions — always note the domain restriction (e.g. x ≠ 0, θ ≠ −2).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard manipulation within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1–P4); no formula booklet entry required. Stay within polynomial/rational equations as encountered in 'show that' proofs, stationary-point work, and integration follow-ups; do not invoke partial fractions or polynomial long division here unless the question explicitly demands them.
