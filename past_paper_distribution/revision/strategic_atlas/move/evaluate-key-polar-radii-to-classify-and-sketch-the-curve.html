Polar Coordinates

Evaluate key polar radii to classify and sketch the curve

Family: Feature Identification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks you to sketch a polar curve r = f(θ) such as a cardioid, limaçon, rose, or circle, especially when the mark scheme rewards labelling specific features. Cues include phrases like 'sketch the curve', 'show the curve passes through the pole', or 'state the maximum value of r'. It also fires when you must identify the type of curve before integrating for area or arc length.

Motivation — Why this works

A small set of strategic θ-values (0, π/2, π, 3π/2) usually pins down the maximum r, any zero of r (curve passes through the pole), and the symmetry behaviour. These features are exactly what determine the curve's family — cardioid, dimpled/convex limaçon, limaçon with inner loop, or rose — and they correspond to the labelled points the mark scheme expects on the sketch. This is far faster than dense point-plotting and matches Edexcel's standard sketch rubric.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the equation has the form r = a + b cos θ (or sin θ), or r = a cos(nθ) — a recognisable family.
  2. Rules out brute point-plotting and rules out treating it as Cartesian; commits to evaluating r at the cardinal angles.
  3. Commits because zeros of r and the maximum of r determine the qualitative shape (cusp, dimple, loop, petals).
  4. Bridges by tabulating r(0), r(π/2), r(π), r(3π/2) and checking r(−θ) = r(θ) (initial-line symmetry) or r(π − θ) = r(θ) (symmetry about θ = π/2).
  5. Decides which extra angles (π/6, π/3, 2π/3) are needed to resolve a dimple or loop, and how to handle any θ giving r < 0.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the family of r = f(θ) (cardioid, limaçon, rose, circle).
  2. Compute r at θ = 0, π/2, π, 3π/2; add π/6, π/3, 2π/3 if a dimple or loop is suspected.
  3. Locate the maximum value of r and any θ for which r = 0 (pole crossings).
  4. Test symmetry: r(−θ) = r(θ) gives symmetry in the initial line; r(π − θ) = r(θ) gives symmetry about θ = π/2.
  5. Plot the key (r, θ) points in the polar plane, join smoothly using the symmetry, and label the curve type plus the maximum r and any pole crossings.

Worked Micro-Example

Sketch r = 1 + cos θ. Then r(0) = 2 (max), r(π/2) = 1, r(π) = 0 (pole), r(3π/2) = 1. Since r(−θ) = r(θ), the curve is symmetric in the initial line. The zero at θ = π gives a cusp at the pole, so the curve is a cardioid with maximum r = 2 on the initial line.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Limaçon r = a + b cos θ with a > b > 0 (no inner loop)
Min r = a − b > 0 at θ = π gives a dimple, not a pole crossing; label the minimum radius rather than a zero.
Limaçon with inner loop, e.g. r = 1 + 2 cos θ
Solve 1 + 2 cos θ = 0 to locate the loop boundary at θ = 2π/3, 4π/3; show the small inner loop traced where r < 0.
Rose curve r = a cos(nθ) or r = a sin(nθ)
Find petal tips from r = ±a and zeros from cos(nθ) = 0; n petals if n odd, 2n petals if n even.
Circle r = a cos θ or r = a sin θ
Recognise as a circle of diameter a through the pole; only need centre and one extreme point to sketch.
Sine-based curves r = a(1 + sin θ)
Symmetry switches to about θ = π/2; evaluate at θ = π/2 (max) and θ = 3π/2 (pole) instead.

Common Misreadings

  • Plotting r at too few angles and missing a dimple, loop, or extra petal.
  • Treating negative r as zero rather than as a point reflected through the pole.
  • Confusing a circle r = a cos θ with a cardioid because both pass through the pole.
  • Assuming initial-line symmetry for a sine-based curve, where the symmetry is actually about θ = π/2.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to label the maximum r and the θ-values where r = 0 on the sketch.
  • Drawing a smooth rounded curve at the pole when a cusp is required (cardioid).
  • Restricting θ to [0, π] and missing the lower half of a non-symmetric curve.
  • Mis-counting petals on a rose by ignoring the odd/even rule for n.

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL Further Pure, polar sketching is restricted to standard families (circles, cardioids, limaçons, simple roses, spirals like r = aθ); identifying features via key angles and symmetry is the expected method, and tangents at the pole or area calculations build directly on this classification.
