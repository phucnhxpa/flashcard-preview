Coordinate Geometry

Differentiate parametrically then negate-reciprocate for the normal gradient

Family: Parametric Normal Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a curve is given by parametric equations x = f(t), y = g(t) (commonly trigonometric, as on an ellipse with (a cos θ, b sin θ) or hyperbola with (a sec θ, b tan θ)) and the question asks for the tangent or normal at a parameter value. Phrasings include 'find the equation of the normal at the point with parameter θ', 'show that the normal at P has equation…', or 'the normal at P meets the axis at…'. The decisive cue is that the point is already parameterised, so converting to Cartesian first is wasteful.

Motivation — Why this works

Parametric differentiation dy/dx = (dy/dθ)/(dx/dθ) yields the gradient as a clean trig ratio, ready to substitute. The normal is perpendicular to the tangent, so its gradient is the negative reciprocal −(dx/dθ)/(dy/dθ). Implicit differentiation of the Cartesian form gives the same answer but forces a re-substitution back into θ, doubling the work. Keeping everything in θ also makes any later elimination (e.g. locus problems) much easier.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the point is given as (a cos θ, b sin θ) or similar — gradient must end up a function of θ.
  2. Rules out implicit differentiation on x²/a² + y²/b² = 1 because it would require resubstituting x, y in terms of θ at the end.
  3. Commits to parametric differentiation since dx/dθ and dy/dθ are each a single trig term, so dy/dx is immediate.
  4. Bridges to operation: compute dx/dθ and dy/dθ separately, divide, then flip sign-and-invert for the normal.
  5. Tactical: leave the gradient as a single fraction (don't multiply through prematurely) so the point-slope line clears denominators cleanly when forming the normal's equation.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Differentiate: dx/dθ and dy/dθ.
  2. Form tangent gradient m_T = (dy/dθ)/(dx/dθ).
  3. Take negative reciprocal: m_N = −(dx/dθ)/(dy/dθ).
  4. Apply point-slope with the parametric point: y − y(θ) = m_N (x − x(θ)).
  5. Clear fractions (multiply by the denominator of m_N) to obtain a tidy linear form, often the requested 'show that' equation.

Worked Micro-Example

Ellipse $\tfrac{x^2}{25}+\tfrac{y^2}{16}=1$ with $P=(5\cos\theta,4\sin\theta)$. Then $\tfrac{dx}{d\theta}=-5\sin\theta$, $\tfrac{dy}{d\theta}=4\cos\theta$, so $\tfrac{dy}{dx}=-\tfrac{4\cos\theta}{5\sin\theta}$. Normal gradient $=\tfrac{5\sin\theta}{4\cos\theta}$, giving $y-4\sin\theta=\tfrac{5\sin\theta}{4\cos\theta}(x-5\cos\theta)$, i.e. $4x\sin\theta-5y\cos\theta=9\sin\theta\cos\theta$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Hyperbola with point (a sec θ, b tan θ)
dx/dθ = a sec θ tan θ, dy/dθ = b sec² θ, so m_T = b/(a sin θ); normal gradient is −a sin θ / b.
Parabola with point (at², 2at)
dx/dt = 2at, dy/dt = 2a, so m_T = 1/t; normal gradient is −t, giving the standard normal y + tx = 2at + at³.
Rectangular hyperbola xy = c² with point (ct, c/t)
dx/dt = c, dy/dt = −c/t², so m_T = −1/t²; normal gradient is t².
General non-trig parametric curve, e.g. (t², t³)
Same procedure: dy/dx = 3t/2 at parameter t; normal gradient −2/(3t). Method is curve-agnostic.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the parameter θ as if it were x, then differentiating y with respect to θ and calling that dy/dx.
  • Assuming 'normal' means the line through the origin and P (i.e. confusing normal with radius/diameter).
  • Switching to Cartesian and using implicit differentiation, then forgetting to convert x, y back to θ when the question expects a θ-form answer.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the negative sign in dx/dθ = −a sin θ, which corrupts both tangent and normal gradients.
  • Taking the reciprocal but forgetting the sign change (giving the tangent's reciprocal, not the perpendicular gradient).
  • Cancelling cos θ or sin θ without noting the excluded cases (θ = 0, π/2) where the normal is vertical or horizontal.
  • Algebra slip when clearing the fraction in the point-slope form — particularly sign errors on the constant term.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL: parametric differentiation is in C34/P3, and tangents/normals to conics with parametric coordinates appear in Further Pure (FP1/FP3). No tools beyond chain rule and perpendicular-gradient condition m₁m₂ = −1 are required.
