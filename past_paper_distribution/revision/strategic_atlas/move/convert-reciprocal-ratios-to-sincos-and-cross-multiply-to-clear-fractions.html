Trigonometry

Convert reciprocal ratios to sin/cos and cross-multiply to clear fractions

Family: Homogenisation Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The equation mixes a reciprocal trig ratio (tan, cot, sec, cosec) with a basic ratio (sin or cos), or contains a fraction whose denominator involves a linear combination of sin/cos. Visual cues: a tan θ or cot θ sitting opposite a sin/cos expression; a compound fraction with (a sin θ + b) or (a cos θ + b) in the denominator; the target form being a polynomial in sin θ alone (or cos θ alone). Equivalent phrasings: 'rationalise the trig equation', 'put everything over sin θ cos θ', 'clear denominators before applying Pythagoras'.

Motivation — Why this works

Rewriting tan/cot/sec/cosec as ratios of sin and cos puts every term on a common footing — both sides become rational expressions in sin θ and cos θ. Cross-multiplying then eliminates all denominators in one stroke, producing a polynomial equation in sin θ and cos θ. From there a single application of the Pythagorean identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 collapses it to a polynomial in one variable, ready to factorise. This is far cleaner than manipulating each side independently and is the standard homogenisation route on Edexcel IAL papers.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the mismatch: one side has a reciprocal ratio (tan, cot, sec, cosec) or a compound fraction; the other side is a clean sin/cos expression. The two sides do not yet 'speak the same language'.
  2. Recognise that the target/desired form is a polynomial in a single trig function (usually sin θ), so I must eliminate (i) the foreign ratio and (ii) all denominators.
  3. Rule out alternatives: dividing through by sin θ or cos θ risks losing roots; trying to apply identities side-by-side without clearing fractions is messy and error-prone.
  4. Commit to homogenisation: rewrite every reciprocal ratio in terms of sin θ / cos θ, exposing the denominators explicitly.
  5. Cross-multiply (or multiply through by the LCD of sin θ, cos θ, and any linear factor like (2 sin θ − 1)) to obtain an equation free of fractions.
  6. Before multiplying, audit which values make a denominator zero; check whether the original equation's domain already excludes them so no spurious or lost solutions occur.
  7. Apply sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 to convert any cos²θ (or stray cos terms paired with another cos) into 1 − sin²θ, reaching a single-variable polynomial.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Replace each reciprocal ratio: tan θ → sin θ/cos θ, cot θ → cos θ/sin θ, sec θ → 1/cos θ, cosec θ → 1/sin θ.
  2. Identify the LCD of all denominators (typically sin θ, cos θ, or a factor like (2 sin θ − 1)).
  3. Multiply both sides by the LCD — equivalently, cross-multiply the rational equation — to clear all fractions.
  4. Record any θ-values that make a denominator zero, and check them against the original equation's domain.
  5. Use sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 to express the resulting polynomial in a single trig variable.
  6. Factorise and solve in the required range.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\cot x = 3\sin x$ for $x \in (0°,180°)$.\\ Rewrite: $\dfrac{\cos x}{\sin x} = 3\sin x$.\\ Multiply by $\sin x$ (valid since $\sin x \neq 0$ on the open interval): $\cos x = 3\sin^2 x = 3(1-\cos^2 x)$.\\ Rearrange: $3\cos^2 x + \cos x - 3 = 0$, a quadratic in $\cos x$ ready to solve.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation of the form tan x = k cos x
Write tan x = sin x/cos x and multiply by cos x to get sin x = k cos²x = k(1 − sin²x).
Compound fraction with (2 sin θ − 1) in the denominator opposite a tan θ term
Rewrite tan θ = sin θ/cos θ, then cross-multiply by cos θ·(2 sin θ − 1) to clear both denominators simultaneously.
Equations involving sec θ or cosec θ
Replace sec θ → 1/cos θ, cosec θ → 1/sin θ; multiply through by cos θ or sin θ as appropriate. Same homogenisation principle.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the reciprocal ratio as if it could be 'cancelled' against a sin or cos directly, instead of converting to a quotient first.
  • Trying to divide both sides by sin θ (or cos θ) at the outset — this loses roots and complicates the algebra when other denominators are present.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting that cross-multiplication assumes the denominators are non-zero; values that zero a denominator must be checked against the original domain.
  • Multiplying by sin θ or cos θ and then including a θ that makes that factor zero as a 'solution' — these are spurious if not present in the original equation.
  • Stopping after clearing fractions and forgetting to invoke sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 to reduce to a single-variable polynomial.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P2/P3) trig-equation work. The only identity invoked beyond the definitions of tan/cot/sec/cosec is sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 (formula booklet, trig identities). Avoid invoking complex-number or Weierstrass t = tan(θ/2) substitutions — out of scope here.
