Complex Numbers

Match complex locus equation to standard geometric template

Family: Locus Identification Frequency: 5 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question presents a condition on z in the form |z − a| = r, |z − a| = |z − b|, or arg(z − a) = α, and asks for a sketch, geometric description, or Cartesian equation of the locus in the Argand diagram. Equivalent phrasings include 'find the set of points such that...', 'describe the locus of P representing z', or 'shade the region where...'. The visual cues are a single modulus equal to a constant (circle), two moduli equated (perpendicular bisector), or a fixed argument (half-line). This move fires before any algebraic expansion into x and y is attempted.

Motivation — Why this works

Each standard form maps directly to a named geometric object: |z − a| = r is a circle centre a radius r (set of points at fixed distance from a); |z − a| = |z − b| is the perpendicular bisector of segment ab (set of points equidistant from a and b); arg(z − a) = α is a half-line starting at a, directed at angle α to the positive real axis (set of points whose direction from a is fixed). Recognising the template instantly converts the algebraic condition to geometry, which earns the method marks and avoids error-prone expansion into Cartesian form. The underlying principle is the geometric meaning of |z − a| as Euclidean distance and arg(z − a) as direction angle in the Argand plane.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the condition involves |z − (something)| or arg(z − (something)) — this is a standard FP2 locus, so I should think geometrically rather than algebraically.
  2. I rule out squaring and expanding into x + iy form: that's slower, error-prone, and only needed if the question explicitly demands a Cartesian equation.
  3. I match the structure to one of three templates: one modulus = constant → circle; two moduli equal → perpendicular bisector; argument = constant → half-line.
  4. I commit to the geometric reading because it directly produces the centre/radius, midpoint/gradient, or start point/angle the examiner wants labelled on the sketch.
  5. I extract the fixed point(s) carefully — for |z − (3 + 2i)| the centre is (3, 2), not (3, −2); for arg(z − a) the half-line starts AT a (open circle) and goes in direction α, not the reverse direction or a full line.
  6. If two loci are combined (e.g. circle ∩ half-line), I sketch both and identify intersection points algebraically as a follow-up.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Parse the condition and identify which template applies: |z − a| = r, |z − a| = |z − b|, or arg(z − a) = α.
  2. For |z − a| = r: read off centre a = (Re(a), Im(a)) and radius r; draw a circle.
  3. For |z − a| = |z − b|: compute midpoint M = (a + b)/2, find the gradient of segment ab, then the perpendicular bisector passes through M with negative-reciprocal gradient.
  4. For arg(z − a) = α: plot a, mark it with an open circle (excluded), then draw a half-line (ray) from a at angle α measured anticlockwise from the positive real axis.
  5. Label all key features on the Argand diagram: centre and radius, or midpoint and perpendicular direction, or start point and angle.
  6. If a Cartesian equation is required, substitute z = x + iy and simplify (e.g. |z − a| = r gives (x − Re a)² + (y − Im a)² = r²).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $|z - (1 + 2i)| = |z - (5 + 4i)|$: identify as perpendicular bisector of segment from $A(1,2)$ to $B(5,4)$. Midpoint $M = (3, 3)$; gradient of $AB$ is $(4-2)/(5-1) = 1/2$, so bisector has gradient $-2$. Equation: $y - 3 = -2(x - 3)$, i.e. $y = -2x + 9$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Ratio of moduli: |z − a| = k|z − b| with k ≠ 1
This gives an Apollonius circle, not a perpendicular bisector. Square both sides, substitute z = x + iy, expand and complete the square to find centre and radius.
arg((z − a)/(z − b)) = α
This is an arc of a circle through a and b (angle in the same segment), not a half-line. The arc subtends angle α at any point on it.
Inequality form, e.g. |z − a| ≤ r or arg(z − a) < α
Same identification, but shade the interior/exterior region or angular sector rather than the boundary curve.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing |z − a| = |z − b| (perpendicular bisector — a line) with |z − a| + |z − b| = c (an ellipse, out of A-level scope).
  • Confusing arg(z − a) = α (half-line FROM a) with arg(z) = α (half-line from the origin).
  • Drawing a full line instead of a half-line for an argument condition.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error in extracting the centre: |z − (3 + 2i)| has centre (3, 2), not (3, −2); |z + 2 − i| = |z − (−2 + i)| has centre (−2, 1).
  • Including the point a on a half-line — it must be excluded (open circle) since arg(0) is undefined.
  • Getting the half-line direction wrong when α is negative or greater than π — always measure anticlockwise from the positive real axis from the start point a.
  • For the perpendicular bisector, using the gradient of ab itself instead of its negative reciprocal.

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL Further Pure 2 (FP2) loci in the Argand diagram. Standard loci |z − a| = r, |z − a| = |z − b|, and arg(z − a) = α are core syllabus; Apollonius circles and arc loci arg((z−a)/(z−b)) = α are also FP2. Avoid generalising to ellipse/hyperbola loci (|z − a| ± |z − b| = c) which are not in scope.
