Strategic Atlas / Differential Equations / Particular Solution Determination
Differential Equations

Substitute the boundary condition to determine the arbitrary constant C

Family: Particular Solution Determination Frequency: 6 questions

Trigger — When to use

After integrating a first-order ODE you have a general solution containing exactly one arbitrary constant C (e.g. y³/3 = ln(4x+5) + C, or μy = ∫μQ dx + C, or 2√A = (5/2)t^(3/5) + C). The question supplies a specific data point — phrased as 'y = y₀ when x = x₀', 'y(x₀) = y₀', or an initial/boundary condition — and asks for THE solution (the particular solution), not the general solution. Equivalent triggers include 'find y in terms of x given that...', 'show that y = ...', or an implicit condition such as 'y is finite as x → 0' which forces a divergent term's coefficient to zero.

Motivation — Why this works

A first-order ODE has a one-parameter family of solutions; the constant C indexes that family. A single boundary condition gives one equation in the one unknown C, which is exactly enough to pick out the unique curve through the given point. This is the standard final step for converting a general solution into a particular solution, and rests on the existence/uniqueness principle for first-order IVPs (one IC ↔ one solution).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structure: integration is complete, the solution contains a single +C, and the question has supplied a specific (x₀, y₀) pair — this is the unmistakable cue for an IC substitution, not for further integration or for separating variables again.
  2. Rule out alternatives: I am not being asked to verify the ODE (so don't differentiate), and I should not substitute the IC into the original ODE — only into the integrated general solution.
  3. Commit to the move: substitute x = x₀ and y = y₀ into the general solution to obtain a numerical equation in C alone.
  4. Tactical decision — choose the form: if the solution is currently in an implicit form like y³/3 = … or μy = …, substitute BEFORE rearranging to y = f(x), since cubing/squaring/multiplying y₀ is usually cleaner than cube-rooting or dividing the RHS.
  5. If a substitution z = f(y) was used earlier, first convert the IC: z₀ = f(y₀), then substitute (x₀, z₀).
  6. Solve the resulting linear equation for C, substitute C back into the general solution, and simplify to present the particular solution. Optionally verify by plugging (x₀, y₀) back in.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify (x₀, y₀) from the boundary condition; convert via any earlier substitution if needed (z₀ = f(y₀)).
  2. Substitute x = x₀ and y = y₀ (or z = z₀) into the general solution.
  3. Evaluate every known function at x₀ (logs, exponentials, integrating factor μ(x₀), etc.).
  4. Solve the resulting algebraic equation for C.
  5. Replace C in the general solution by its numerical value to obtain the particular solution.
  6. Rearrange to the requested form (often y = f(x)) and simplify.

Worked Micro-Example

General solution: $\tfrac{y^3}{3} = \ln(4x+5) + C$, with condition $y = \tfrac{1}{3}$ at $x = -\tfrac{1}{4}$. Substitute: $\tfrac{(1/3)^3}{3} = \ln(4\cdot(-\tfrac{1}{4})+5) + C \Rightarrow \tfrac{1}{81} = \ln 4 + C$. Hence $C = \tfrac{1}{81} - \ln 4$, giving the particular solution $\tfrac{y^3}{3} = \ln(4x+5) + \tfrac{1}{81} - \ln 4$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Linear ODE solved with integrating factor: general solution is μy = ∫μQ dx + C.
Substitute (x₀, y₀) directly into μy = ∫μQ dx + C; evaluate μ(x₀) and the antiderivative at x₀, solve for C, then divide by μ(x) to express y.
Earlier substitution z = f(y) (e.g. z = y² or z = 1/y for Bernoulli).
First convert: z₀ = f(y₀). Substitute (x₀, z₀) into the z-form solution, find C, then back-substitute z = f(y) to recover y.
Implicit condition such as 'y is finite/bounded as x → 0' or 'y → 0 as x → ∞'.
No direct point substitution: identify the divergent term in the general solution and set its coefficient (which contains C) to zero.
Boundary condition given at x₀ = 0.
Algebra simplifies dramatically since e^0 = 1, ln(1) = 0, and many polynomial terms vanish — exploit this rather than expanding generally.

Common Misreadings

  • Mistaking the IC as a check on the ODE itself, leading the student to substitute into dy/dx = … instead of into the integrated solution.
  • Treating a 'show that' question as if C is already given, and skipping the substitution step.
  • Reading 'y is finite as x → 0' as not being a usable condition, when in fact it determines C by killing a singular term.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips with fractions/cubes — e.g. computing (1/3)³/3 as 1/27 instead of 1/81.
  • Substituting into the wrong form: into the original ODE, into the derivative, or into a half-rearranged version where a constant has been absorbed.
  • Rearranging to y = f(x) BEFORE substituting, forcing messy cube roots or logs of unknowns.
  • Forgetting to convert the IC through a prior substitution (e.g. using y₀ where the solution is written in z = y²).
  • Failing to put the determined C back into the general solution to state the final particular solution.
  • Decimal approximation of clean values (e.g. writing √2.25 ≈ 1.5 rather than recognising 2.25 = 9/4 so √2.25 = 3/2).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/FP2 scope for first-order ODEs (separable, linear with integrating factor, and reducible-by-substitution types). No formula booklet entry is invoked — this is a procedural step. Do NOT generalise to second-order IVPs (which require two conditions) or to PDE boundary conditions; those are out of scope.
