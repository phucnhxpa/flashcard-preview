Algebra & Functions

Convert total-quantity constraint to linear inequality and floor for greatest integer

Family: Inequality To Integer Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a worded problem fixes a cumulative total (distance, cost, time, mass) that 'must not exceed' or 'is at most' some bound, while one repeated quantity x is the unknown and is required to be a positive integer. Look for phrases like 'greatest value of x', 'largest whole number', 'maximum number of...' attached to a sum that is linear in x. Often appears after a series sum (AP/GP) has been computed, with x as a separate repeated term added on.

Motivation — Why this works

Summing all contributions yields a linear expression a + bx, and the cap 'total ≤ N' becomes bx ≤ N − a, hence x ≤ (N − a)/b. Because x is constrained to be an integer, the greatest admissible value is ⌊(N − a)/b⌋. This reduces a wordy optimisation into a one-line inequality plus a floor, which is reliable and quick.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the problem caps a total and asks for the greatest integer x making the cap hold.
  2. Rule out trial-and-error or calculus — the total is linear in x, so a single inequality suffices.
  3. Commit to writing Total(x) ≤ N and isolating x, since the structure is a + bx ≤ N.
  4. Compute the constant part a (often a series sum) carefully, keeping enough decimal places to avoid floor errors.
  5. Solve for x, then take the floor — never round up — and verify the integer satisfies the original inequality.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the total as (fixed sum) + (coefficient)·x.
  2. Impose the cap: fixed sum + coefficient·x ≤ N.
  3. Rearrange to x ≤ (N − fixed sum)/coefficient.
  4. Take the floor of the right-hand side to get the greatest integer x.
  5. Substitute back to confirm the inequality holds (and that x+1 would fail).

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose the fixed sum of 12 Sunday runs is $295.1$ km and each of 12 Wednesdays adds $x$ km, with total $\le 360$. Then $295.1 + 12x \le 360 \Rightarrow 12x \le 64.9 \Rightarrow x \le 5.408\ldots$. Since $x \in \mathbb{Z}^+$, the greatest value is $x = 5$. Check: $295.1 + 60 = 355.1 \le 360$ ✓, while $x=6$ gives $367.1 > 360$ ✗.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Budget cap on repeated purchases: fixed cost + unit price · n ≤ budget
Solve for n and floor; n is a non-negative integer (count).
AP/GP sum problems asking for greatest n with S_n ≤ M
The inequality is non-linear in n; solve via the S_n formula then floor, but check both n and n+1 explicitly.
Logarithmic inequalities (e.g. find greatest n with a·r^n ≤ K)
Take logs to linearise; floor the resulting bound on n, mindful of inequality direction when log base < 1.
'At least' versions: find smallest integer n with total ≥ N
Use ceiling instead of floor after rearranging.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 'not exceed' as strict <; usually equivalent here for non-integer bounds, but matters when the bound is itself an integer.
  • Omitting part of the total (e.g. summing only one of the two repeated contributions).
  • Treating x as continuous and giving the decimal answer instead of the integer.

Pitfalls

  • Rounding x up or to nearest instead of taking the floor (e.g. writing 6 when x ≤ 5.41).
  • Premature rounding of the fixed sum (especially from a GP) shifting the bound across an integer boundary — keep at least 3–4 d.p.
  • Forgetting to verify the candidate integer actually satisfies the original cap.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 scope: linear inequalities, AP/GP sums, and integer/contextual reasoning. No calculus or advanced optimisation required.
