Trigger — When to use

Fires when a polynomial (typically cubic or quartic) is given in fully factored form with at least one repeated factor like $(x-a)^2$ alongside one or more linear factors, and the question asks for a sketch of the curve. Visual cues: factored polynomial with mixed exponents (e.g. $(x-5)^2(4x-3)$ or $(2x-k)(x+4)^2$), instruction to 'sketch' or 'show the shape', and possibly a parameter constraint like $0<k<4$ to fix the position of a root. Also fires whenever you see a squared, cubed, or higher-power factor and need local behavior at that root without calculus.

Motivation — Why this works

A factor $(x-a)^n$ controls the sign of $f(x)$ near $x=a$: if $n$ is even, $(x-a)^n \geq 0$ on both sides so $f$ does not change sign — the curve touches the x-axis; if $n$ is odd, the sign flips and the curve crosses. This bypasses differentiation entirely, since multiplicity directly encodes tangency vs transversality. The principle is just the sign behavior of even/odd powers, applied locally at each root, and it lets you sketch correctly using only factor inspection, end behavior, and the y-intercept.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the polynomial is already factored — no expansion needed, and each factor's exponent is visible.
  2. Rule out calculus-based sketching (finding $f'=0$ for turning points): it's slower and unnecessary when multiplicity already tells you the local shape at every root.
  3. Commit to multiplicity analysis because each factor's exponent uniquely determines whether the graph touches or crosses at that root.
  4. Read off the roots by setting each factor to zero, and tag each one as 'touch' (even exponent) or 'cross' (odd exponent).
  5. Determine end behavior by multiplying the leading terms of each factor — this fixes whether the curve goes to $+\infty$ or $-\infty$ on the right.
  6. Compute $f(0)$ for the y-intercept to anchor the sketch vertically, and apply any parameter constraints (e.g. $0<k<4$) to place roots correctly on the axis.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Set each factor to zero to list all roots: e.g. $(4x-3)(x-5)^2=0 \Rightarrow x=3/4,\ x=5$.
  2. Tag each root by its factor's exponent: even exponent → graph touches the x-axis (tangent); odd exponent → graph crosses.
  3. Compute the leading coefficient as the product of leading terms from each factor (e.g. $4x \cdot x^2 = 4x^3$, positive) to fix end behavior.
  4. Substitute $x=0$ to get the y-intercept, e.g. $f(0)=(-3)(25)=-75$, and mark $(0,-75)$.
  5. If parameters appear, use given constraints (e.g. $0<k<4 \Rightarrow 0<k/2<2$) to locate roots on the correct interval.
  6. Draw the curve respecting end behavior, passing through the y-intercept, crossing at odd-multiplicity roots and bouncing tangentially at even-multiplicity roots.

Worked Micro-Example

Sketch $y=(2x-k)(x+4)^2$ with $0<k<4$. Roots: $x=k/2$ (multiplicity 1, cross) and $x=-4$ (multiplicity 2, touch). Leading term: $2x \cdot x^2 = 2x^3$, so $y \to +\infty$ as $x \to +\infty$. y-intercept: $f(0)=(-k)(16)=-16k<0$. Constraint gives $0<k/2<2$, so the crossing lies between $0$ and $2$. Sketch: cubic rising from bottom-left, touching the axis at $x=-4$, dipping below to pass through $(0,-16k)$, then crossing upward at $x=k/2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quartic with two squared factors, e.g. $(x-a)^2(x-b)^2$
Both roots are touch points; the curve never crosses the x-axis and stays $\geq 0$ everywhere (positive leading coefficient).
Triple root, e.g. $(x-a)^3$
Odd multiplicity so the curve still crosses, but flattens at the axis producing a point of inflection on the x-axis.
Negative leading coefficient, e.g. $-(x-a)^2(x-b)$
Touch/cross classification is unchanged, but end behavior flips: curve descends to $-\infty$ on the right.

Common Misreadings

  • Drawing the curve crossing the x-axis at a squared-factor root instead of touching — forgetting that even powers prevent a sign change.
  • Treating $(x-a)^2$ as if it were just $(x-a)$, producing two crossings instead of a tangent touch.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to compute the y-intercept, leaving the sketch unanchored vertically.
  • Mis-multiplying leading terms to get the wrong sign of the leading coefficient (e.g. confusing $4x \cdot x^2$ with $-4x^3$).
  • Ignoring parameter constraints like $0<k<4$ and placing the parametric root in the wrong region of the x-axis.

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 curve sketching scope. No formula booklet entry needed — this is a structural property of polynomials. Avoid invoking general theorems about multiplicity in complex analysis or calculus-based concavity arguments; stick to sign-of-factors reasoning for sketches.
