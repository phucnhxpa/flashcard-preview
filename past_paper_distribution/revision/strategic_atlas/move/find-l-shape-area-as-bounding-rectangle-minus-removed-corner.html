Coordinate Geometry

Find L-shape area as bounding rectangle minus removed corner

Family: Area By Subtraction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a region is described as a composite shape formed by removing a rectangular (or other simple) piece from a larger rectangle — typically an L-shape, a U-shape, or a rectangle with a notch. Verbal cues include 'a rectangular garden with a corner removed', 'a path of width w around a rectangle', or a diagram showing right-angled indentations. The task asks for the area as a single expression, often in terms of a parameter $x$. Use this move whenever both the outer bounding rectangle and the removed piece have dimensions that are already known or easily deduced.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity is simply $A_{\text{region}} = A_{\text{outer}} - A_{\text{removed}}$, which is exact whenever the removed piece lies entirely inside the outer rectangle and the region is what remains. Subtraction is preferred over partitioning because the outer and removed dimensions are usually given directly, whereas a partition forces you to invent a cut line and compute sub-rectangle dimensions by differences — introducing more places for sign or length errors. It also produces a single clean algebraic expression rather than a sum that must then be simplified.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the region is a rectangle with a rectangular bite taken out of one corner — i.e. an L-shape.
  2. Rule out partitioning into two sub-rectangles: this requires choosing a cut line and recomputing one dimension as a difference, which is more error-prone.
  3. Commit to subtraction because the outer bounding rectangle dimensions and the removed rectangle dimensions are both already available (often from the perimeter calculation).
  4. Bridge: write $A = (\text{outer length})(\text{outer width}) - (\text{removed length})(\text{removed width})$.
  5. Tactically, expand each product first, then subtract and collect like terms in $x$ to leave a single simplified monomial or polynomial.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the bounding rectangle dimensions (e.g. $6x$ by $5x$) and compute its area: $30x^2$.
  2. Identify the removed corner dimensions (e.g. $4x$ by $3x$) and compute its area: $12x^2$.
  3. Subtract: $A = 30x^2 - 12x^2 = 18x^2$.
  4. State the simplified expression as the final area.

Worked Micro-Example

An L-shaped garden fits inside a $6x \times 5x$ rectangle, with a $4x \times 3x$ rectangular corner removed. Outer area $= 6x \cdot 5x = 30x^2$. Removed area $= 4x \cdot 3x = 12x^2$. Therefore $A = 30x^2 - 12x^2 = 18x^2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Annulus (washer) between two concentric circles of radii $R$ and $r$
Use $A = \pi R^2 - \pi r^2$; same subtraction principle, circular areas instead of rectangular.
Rectangle with a semicircular notch cut from one side
Compute $A = (\text{rectangle area}) - \tfrac{1}{2}\pi r^2$ with $r$ read from the notch.
Path of uniform width $w$ surrounding an inner rectangle
Outer rectangle minus inner rectangle gives the path area; inner dimensions are outer dimensions reduced by $2w$.
Region under one curve minus region under another (area between curves)
Replace rectangle areas with definite integrals: $\int_a^b (f(x) - g(x))\,dx$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the L-shape as a single rectangle and using only the outer dimensions.
  • Reading the removed-piece dimensions off the wrong sides of the diagram.
  • Confusing perimeter information with area information when both are asked in the same question.

Pitfalls

  • Using the outer width (e.g. $6x$) instead of the actual removed width (e.g. $4x$) for the cut-out rectangle.
  • Adding the two areas instead of subtracting.
  • Forgetting to square the variable when multiplying $kx \cdot mx$, writing $30x$ instead of $30x^2$.
  • Not simplifying the final expression (leaving $30x^2 - 12x^2$ unsimplified).

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics: composite-area problems with linear expressions in $x$ appear in P1/P2 algebra and mensuration contexts, and the same subtraction logic underlies area-between-curves in C2/P2 integration.
