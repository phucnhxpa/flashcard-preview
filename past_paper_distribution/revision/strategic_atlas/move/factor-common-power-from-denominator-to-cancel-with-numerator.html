Trigger — When to use

Fires when a rational expression — typically produced by a substitution inside an integral — has numerator and every term of the denominator sharing a common factor (often a power of the substitution variable u). Cues: numerator is a single monomial like 8u, denominator is a polynomial like u³ + 2u² where every term has u² as a factor. Equivalent phrasings: 'pull out the highest common power before partial fractions' or 'reduce the rational function before decomposing'. Especially common right before a partial-fractions step where you want the lowest-degree denominator possible.

Motivation — Why this works

Cancelling a common factor before partial fractions reduces the number of unknowns in the decomposition and avoids spurious repeated-factor cases. The underlying identity is simply (k·f)/(k·g) = f/g on the domain where k ≠ 0. By extracting u² from u³ + 2u² = u²(u+2) and cancelling one u from 8u, the integrand collapses from a degree-3 denominator with a repeated factor into two distinct linear factors — the cleanest possible setup for the Edexcel partial-fractions toolkit.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the numerator 8u and every term of the denominator u³ + 2u² contain u as a factor.
  2. Rule out jumping straight into partial fractions on 8u/(u³ + 2u²) — that would force a decomposition over a repeated factor u² and a linear factor (u+2), giving three unknowns instead of two.
  3. Commit to factoring because cancelling will lower the denominator's degree and remove the repeated factor entirely.
  4. Bridge: rewrite the denominator as u²(u+2), then cancel one power of u against the numerator's u.
  5. Tactical decision: cancel only one factor of u (not two), since the numerator carries only u¹; check that u ≠ 0 on the integration interval before cancelling.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the common factor: numerator 8u and denominator u³ + 2u² both contain u.
  2. Factor the denominator: u³ + 2u² = u²(u + 2).
  3. Write the quotient: 8u / [u²(u + 2)].
  4. Cancel one factor of u (valid since u ≠ 0 on the domain): result is 8 / [u(u + 2)].
  5. Proceed with partial fractions on the simplified form.

Worked Micro-Example

After substituting u = 1 + x² (so du = 2x dx) into ∫ 4x/((1+x²)³ + 2(1+x²)²) dx, the integrand becomes 8u/(u³+2u²). Factor: u³ + 2u² = u²(u+2), so the integrand is 8u/[u²(u+2)] = 8/[u(u+2)]. This is now ready for partial fractions: 8/[u(u+2)] = 4/u − 4/(u+2).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Substitution leaves numerator ku² and denominator u⁴ + au³
Factor u³ from the denominator, cancel u² from numerator against u² of the u³, leaving k/[u(u+a)].
Common factor is (u+1) rather than a power of u, e.g. (u+1)/[(u+1)²(u−3)]
Cancel one (u+1) to obtain 1/[(u+1)(u−3)] before partial fractions, noting u ≠ −1.
Rational function in x (no substitution), e.g. (3x² )/(x⁴ + 5x³)
Factor x³ from denominator, cancel x² from numerator, giving 3/[x(x+5)].
Algebraic simplification before differentiation rather than integration
Same cancellation principle reduces the quotient before applying the quotient/chain rule.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading u³ + 2u² as already factorised and going straight to partial fractions over a repeated factor.
  • Believing the cancellation 'changes' the function — it only changes the form on the valid domain.
  • Treating 8u/u² as 8 (forgetting one u remains in the denominator).

Pitfalls

  • Cancelling u when u = 0 is in the integration interval — must check bounds first.
  • Cancelling too many powers (e.g. writing 8u/u² as 8/u·... incorrectly) — count carefully.
  • Forgetting to factor fully before cancelling, leading to missed simplifications.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths: rational-function simplification, factorisation, and partial fractions over distinct linear factors are core C34/P3–P4 techniques. Domain checks for cancellation align with standard A-level rigour.
