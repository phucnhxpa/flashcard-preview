Trigonometry

Reverse-read a power-linearisation identity to collapse a multi-angle equation into a polynomial in cosθ

Family: Identity Reverse Substitution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation's LHS matches (up to a constant) the RHS of a linearisation identity just established — typically a sum like $\cos 6\theta + 6\cos 4\theta + 15\cos 2\theta$ whose coefficients $1,6,15$ echo a binomial pattern from $(z+z^{-1})^n$. Cue phrases: 'Hence solve…', or a part (c) immediately after a part (b) that proved $2^{n-1}\cos^n\theta = \cos n\theta + \dots$. Also fires for sine analogues with alternating signs. The structural giveaway is that every multi-angle term in the equation appears in a previously derived identity.

Motivation — Why this works

A linearisation identity such as $32\cos^6\theta = \cos 6\theta + 6\cos 4\theta + 15\cos 2\theta + 10$ is a two-way bridge: read forward it expresses powers as multi-angles, read backward it expresses a multi-angle sum as a single power. Reverse-reading replaces a sixth-degree trigonometric equation by a single algebraic equation in $\cos\theta$, which is then solved by taking roots. This is far cheaper than expanding each $\cos(n\theta)$ via Chebyshev/compound-angle formulas, which would generate the same polynomial after huge cancellation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the LHS of the equation is term-for-term the non-constant part of the identity proved in the previous part — the coefficients 1, 6, 15 are unmistakable.
  2. Rule out brute-force expansion of each $\cos(n\theta)$ in powers of $\cos\theta$: it would reproduce the same identity after pages of work.
  3. Commit to the reverse substitution because the 'Hence' instruction signals reuse of part (b), and the identity converts the whole LHS into a single power of $\cos\theta$ minus a constant.
  4. Bridge: rewrite LHS as $32\cos^6\theta - 10$ and set equal to the RHS of the equation, producing a clean algebraic equation in $\cos\theta$.
  5. Tactical decisions: take the sixth root keeping the $\pm$, then map each value of $\cos\theta$ to all $\theta$ in the stated interval using cosine symmetry $\theta \mapsto \pi-\theta$ etc.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Quote the identity from the previous part: $\cos 6\theta + 6\cos 4\theta + 15\cos 2\theta + 10 = 32\cos^6\theta$.
  2. Subtract the constant to match the equation's LHS: $\cos 6\theta + 6\cos 4\theta + 15\cos 2\theta = 32\cos^6\theta - 10$.
  3. Substitute into the given equation and solve algebraically: $32\cos^6\theta - 10 = 0 \Rightarrow \cos^6\theta = \tfrac{5}{16}$.
  4. Take the sixth root with both signs: $\cos\theta = \pm\left(\tfrac{5}{16}\right)^{1/6}$.
  5. Solve for $\theta$ in the prescribed interval, using $\arccos$ for the positive root and $\pi - \arccos(\cdot)$ for the negative root.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $32\cos^6\theta = \cos 6\theta + 6\cos 4\theta + 15\cos 2\theta + 10$, solve $\cos 6\theta + 6\cos 4\theta + 15\cos 2\theta = 0$ for $\theta\in[0,\pi]$.\\ Reverse-read: LHS $= 32\cos^6\theta - 10$, so $32\cos^6\theta = 10$, giving $\cos^6\theta = \tfrac{5}{16}$.\\ Hence $\cos\theta = \pm(5/16)^{1/6}$.\\ In $[0,\pi]$: $\theta_1 = \arccos\!\big((5/16)^{1/6}\big) \approx 0.442$ and $\theta_2 = \pi - \theta_1 \approx 2.700$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Sine power linearisation, e.g. $2^{n-1}\sin^n\theta = \pm\sum a_k \cos k\theta$ (n even) or $\sum a_k \sin k\theta$ (n odd)
Match the equation against the alternating-sign sum and isolate $\sin^n\theta$; then take the $n$-th root with $\pm$ as appropriate and use $\sin(\pi-\theta)=\sin\theta$.
Equation equals a non-zero constant, e.g. RHS $=k$
Carry the constant through: $2^{n-1}\cos^n\theta - c = k$ gives $\cos^n\theta = (k+c)/2^{n-1}$.
Mixed sum like $\cos 5\theta + 5\cos 3\theta + 10\cos\theta$ (odd $n$)
Identity has no constant term: directly $16\cos^5\theta = \cos 5\theta + 5\cos 3\theta + 10\cos\theta$, so set the sum equal to zero gives $\cos\theta = 0$ as the only real root.
Coefficients off by a common factor, e.g. $2\cos 6\theta + 12\cos 4\theta + 30\cos 2\theta = 0$
Divide through by the common factor first to reveal the binomial-coefficient pattern, then apply the same identity.

Common Misreadings

  • Failing to spot that the equation's coefficients are exactly those of the previous part's identity, and instead expanding $\cos 6\theta,\cos 4\theta,\cos 2\theta$ via repeated double-angle formulas.
  • Treating the equation as a polynomial in $\cos 2\theta$ via Chebyshev relations ($\cos 4\theta = 2\cos^2 2\theta - 1$ etc.) — feasible but far longer than the reverse-read.
  • Reading the identity only forwards and not realising it can be rearranged to express the multi-angle sum as a single power.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the $\pm$ when taking an even root of $\cos^n\theta$, losing the second-quadrant solution.
  • Going via $\cos^2\theta = (5/16)^{1/3}$ and then applying $\cos 2\theta = 2\cos^2\theta - 1$ unnecessarily, reintroducing the multi-angle structure just removed.
  • Reporting solutions outside the stated interval, or omitting $\theta = \pi - \theta_1$ by symmetry.
  • Dropping or mis-signing the constant when rearranging the linearisation identity (e.g. writing $32\cos^6\theta + 10$ instead of $32\cos^6\theta - 10$).

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL Further Pure: de Moivre's theorem is used to derive power-linearisation identities like $32\cos^6\theta = \cos 6\theta + 6\cos 4\theta + 15\cos 2\theta + 10$, and 'Hence solve' parts routinely exploit the reverse reading. No tools beyond FP1/FP2 trigonometry and elementary algebra are required.
