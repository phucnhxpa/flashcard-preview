Sequences & Series

Convert a summation constraint into a polynomial equation in the parameter

Family: Constraint To Equation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an earlier part has produced symbolic expressions for the first few terms of a recurrence (or sequence) in terms of an unknown parameter k, and a later part states a numerical value for a finite sum such as $u_1+u_2+u_3 = 117$ or $\sum_{n=1}^{N} u_n = S$. Look for phrases like 'given that the sum of the first three terms is...', 'the sum to N is equal to...', or a sigma expression set equal to a constant. The unknown is still the parameter, not n, so the constraint must be turned into an equation in that parameter.

Motivation — Why this works

A numerical sum is a single scalar equation; once each term is symbolic in k, substitution collapses the sequence problem into a single algebraic equation in one unknown. Because terms generated by a linear recurrence with polynomial seed are themselves polynomials in k, the resulting equation is polynomial — typically linear or quadratic at A-level — and so is solvable by routine methods. This is the standard bridge between the 'find expressions for $u_n$' part and the 'find the value of k' part of structured recurrence questions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the new part gives a numerical target (e.g. 117) but k has not yet been determined, so an equation in k is needed.
  2. Rule out trying to find k from any single term alone — only the aggregate sum is fixed, so all listed terms must contribute.
  3. Commit to the substitution route: the symbolic forms from part (a) are already in hand, so adding them and equating to the given number is the only available constraint.
  4. Translate the sigma/sum statement literally: $\sum_{n=1}^{3} u_n = u_1+u_2+u_3$, then substitute the symbolic expressions.
  5. Collect like powers of k carefully and rearrange to standard polynomial form $ak^2+bk+c=0$ ready for factorising or the quadratic formula.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the constraint explicitly: $u_1+u_2+\dots+u_N = S$.
  2. Substitute the symbolic expressions for each $u_n$ obtained earlier.
  3. Expand and collect like powers of the parameter on the left-hand side.
  4. Subtract the given constant to bring the equation to standard polynomial form $=0$.
  5. Hand off to the appropriate solver (factorise, quadratic formula, or simple linear rearrangement).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $u_1=6$, $u_2=6k+3$, $u_3=6k^2+3k+3$ and $\sum_{n=1}^{3} u_n = 117$: $6+(6k+3)+(6k^2+3k+3)=117$ $6k^2+9k+12=117$ $6k^2+9k-105=0 \;\Rightarrow\; 2k^2+3k-35=0 \;\Rightarrow\; (2k-7)(k+5)=0$, so $k=\tfrac{7}{2}$ or $k=-5$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Arithmetic series with unknown common difference d and a given $S_n$
Substitute $S_n=\tfrac{n}{2}(2a+(n-1)d)$ and solve the resulting linear/quadratic in d.
Geometric series with unknown ratio r and a given $S_n$ or $S_\infty$
Substitute $S_n=a(1-r^n)/(1-r)$ or $S_\infty=a/(1-r)$ and solve a polynomial (or rational) equation in r, checking $|r|<1$ where needed.
Product of terms equals a given value
Multiply the symbolic expressions and equate to the constant; the equation in k is still polynomial but typically of higher degree.
Ratio of terms (e.g. $u_3/u_1 = c$) is given
Cross-multiply to clear the fraction, then collect into standard polynomial form in k.
A specific term equals a number (e.g. $u_4 = 50$)
Skip the summation step; substitute directly into the symbolic form of that single term.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading $\sum_{n=1}^{3} u_n$ as just $u_3$ rather than $u_1+u_2+u_3$.
  • Mistaking the given total as the value of the last term, or as $S_\infty$ when only a finite sum is meant.
  • Treating k as the summation index and trying to sum over k instead of over n.

Pitfalls

  • Omitting $u_1$ from the sum because it is a plain constant and easy to overlook.
  • Sign errors when moving the numerical constant across the equality.
  • Failing to simplify by a common factor before solving (e.g. not dividing $6k^2+9k-105=0$ by 3), making the quadratic harder to factorise.
  • Forgetting to reject extraneous roots if the context restricts k (e.g. positive terms, convergence conditions).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 sequences and series: finite sums of recurrence-defined sequences, AP/GP sum formulae, and solving linear or quadratic equations. No infinite-series convergence theory beyond $|r|<1$ for GPs is required.
