Coordinate Geometry

Apply the shoelace formula to compute triangle area from vertex coordinates

Family: Shoelace Area Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a triangle (or polygon) has all vertices specified as coordinates and you must find its area. Especially useful when the triangle is not axis-aligned in a convenient way — i.e. no two vertices share an x- or y-coordinate, so there is no obvious horizontal or vertical base. Visual cues: three coordinate pairs (often involving fractions or negatives), a request for 'exact area', and no perpendicular pair of sides immediately visible. Equivalent phrasings: 'find the area of triangle ABC given A, B, C', 'compute the area of the region enclosed by these three points'.

Motivation — Why this works

The shoelace (or determinant) formula Area = ½|x₁(y₂ − y₃) + x₂(y₃ − y₁) + x₃(y₁ − y₂)| converts three coordinate pairs directly into an area in a single substitution. It bypasses computing side lengths via the distance formula, identifying a base, or finding a perpendicular height — operations that often introduce surds and additional algebra. The formula is equivalent to ½|(B−A) × (C−A)| (the 2D cross-product of two edge vectors), so it encodes the signed area of the parallelogram spanned by two sides, halved. It is fast, systematic, and error-resistant for arbitrary vertex positions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice: the question gives three coordinate pairs and asks for an area — this is a 'coordinates → area' problem.
  2. List options: (i) base × height after spotting an axis-aligned side, (ii) distance formula for one side then perpendicular distance from the opposite vertex to that line, (iii) shoelace/determinant formula, (iv) box method (bounding rectangle minus corner triangles).
  3. Rule out base × height unless two vertices share an x- or y-coordinate; rule out the distance-formula route because it typically introduces surds and a perpendicular-distance calculation.
  4. Commit to shoelace: it needs only arithmetic on the given coordinates, no square roots, no extra geometry.
  5. Tactical choice: if one vertex is the origin, place it as (x₁, y₁) so two of the three terms vanish — minimising arithmetic.
  6. Substitute carefully, keep signs straight inside each (yⱼ − yₖ) difference, and remember the absolute value to ensure a positive area.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Label the vertices in a fixed order: (x₁, y₁), (x₂, y₂), (x₃, y₃).
  2. Write Area = ½|x₁(y₂ − y₃) + x₂(y₃ − y₁) + x₃(y₁ − y₂)|.
  3. Substitute each coordinate, evaluating the differences (y₂ − y₃), (y₃ − y₁), (y₁ − y₂) first to control sign errors.
  4. Multiply out each product, sum the three terms, and take the absolute value.
  5. Multiply by ½ to obtain the final area; leave as an exact fraction if requested.

Worked Micro-Example

Vertices $P(2,-3)$, $A(\tfrac12,0)$, $B(0,-4)$. Area $=\tfrac12\bigl|2(0-(-4))+\tfrac12(-4-(-3))+0(-3-0)\bigr| = \tfrac12\bigl|2(4)+\tfrac12(-1)+0\bigr| = \tfrac12\bigl|8-\tfrac12\bigr| = \tfrac12\cdot\tfrac{15}{2} = \tfrac{15}{4}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two vertices share a y-coordinate (or both lie on the x-axis)
Use ½ × base × height directly, where base is the horizontal distance and height is |y| of the third vertex — much faster than full shoelace.
One vertex sits at the origin
Place it as (x₁, y₁); two of the three terms vanish and the formula reduces to ½|x₂y₃ − x₃y₂|.
Polygon with more than three vertices
Extend to the generalised shoelace: Area = ½|Σ(xᵢyᵢ₊₁ − xᵢ₊₁yᵢ)| over consecutive vertices, closing the loop.
Vector framing preferred
Use Area = ½|det([x₂−x₁, x₃−x₁; y₂−y₁, y₃−y₁])| — identical arithmetic, expressed as a 2×2 determinant of edge vectors from one vertex.

Common Misreadings

  • Choosing one side as a base and laboriously computing the perpendicular distance from the opposite vertex to that line — correct but far more work.
  • Decomposing the triangle into bounding-box minus corner triangles — viable but introduces extra steps and more chances for sign error with fractional coordinates.
  • Believing shoelace requires the vertices to be listed anticlockwise — it does for signed area, but the absolute value handles either orientation.

Pitfalls

  • Omitting the absolute value and reporting a negative area.
  • Inconsistent or jumbled vertex labelling causing sign errors inside the (yⱼ − yₖ) differences.
  • Arithmetic slips with negative coordinates, e.g. mishandling 0 − (−3) = +3 rather than −3.
  • Fraction errors when one coordinate is a fraction, especially when combining a fraction with an integer at the final step.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure scope (P2/P3 coordinate geometry). The shoelace formula itself is not in the formula booklet, so it must be reproduced from memory or derived via the equivalent ½|base × height| or determinant form (the 2×2 determinant is in scope via FP1 matrices). Avoid invoking signed area / orientation conventions beyond taking the absolute value.
