Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a product of the form (A + B)(A − B) appears — two binomials identical except for a single sign flip. Common surfaces: after a substitution that splits a quadratic-in-disguise, when factorising expressions like a² − b², when simplifying (√p + √q)(√p − √q) to rationalise, or when a 'show that' requires terms to cancel cleanly. The cue is structural symmetry: same first term, opposite-signed second term.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity (A + B)(A − B) = A² − B² replaces a two-term product with a two-term difference, killing the cross terms AB and −AB. This is the fastest route to cancellation in 'show that' problems, because it produces exactly the squared terms needed to match the target. It also avoids the sign-error risk of full FOIL expansion. In substitution-based ODE or algebra problems, the appearance of this conjugate pair is usually deliberate — the cancellation is engineered.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the bracket pair has identical first terms and opposite-signed second terms — a conjugate pair.
  2. Rule out general FOIL expansion (slower, error-prone) and rule out trying to factor further before multiplying.
  3. Commit to applying (A + B)(A − B) = A² − B² because the cross terms are guaranteed to vanish.
  4. Identify A and B explicitly (here A = v, B = 2x) so that A² − B² = v² − 4x² is written down in one step.
  5. After expanding, scan the surrounding equation for terms that match A² or B² (with coefficients) — these are the terms engineered to cancel.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the conjugate pair: write the product as (A + B)(A − B).
  2. Apply the identity directly: (A + B)(A − B) = A² − B².
  3. Substitute A and B back in and simplify (e.g. v² − (2x)² = v² − 4x²).
  4. Multiply through by any external factor (e.g. 2x · (v² − 4x²) = 2xv² − 8x³).
  5. Match the resulting terms against the other side of the equation and cancel.

Worked Micro-Example

Simplify $2x(v+2x)(v-2x)$. Recognise the conjugate pair: $(v+2x)(v-2x) = v^2 - (2x)^2 = v^2 - 4x^2$. Multiply by $2x$: $2x(v^2 - 4x^2) = 2xv^2 - 8x^3$. The $-8x^3$ now visibly cancels with any $-8x^3$ elsewhere.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Surd rationalisation, e.g. $\frac{1}{\sqrt{p}+\sqrt{q}}$
Multiply top and bottom by the conjugate $\sqrt{p}-\sqrt{q}$; denominator collapses to $p - q$.
Factorising $a^2 - b^2$ in algebraic fractions
Run the identity in reverse: split into $(a-b)(a+b)$ to cancel a common factor with numerator or denominator.
Complex numbers: $(a+bi)(a-bi)$
Yields $a^2 + b^2$ (since $i^2 = -1$); used to rationalise complex denominators.
Trig identities, e.g. $(1-\sin\theta)(1+\sin\theta)$
Collapses to $1 - \sin^2\theta = \cos^2\theta$.
Hidden difference of squares, e.g. $x^4 - 16$
Apply twice: $(x^2-4)(x^2+4) = (x-2)(x+2)(x^2+4)$.

Common Misreadings

  • Mistaking $(A+B)^2$ for $(A+B)(A-B)$ and writing $A^2 - B^2$ when cross terms should remain.
  • Treating $(A+B)(A-B)$ as an unfactorisable product and expanding term by term.
  • Failing to spot the pattern when $A$ or $B$ is itself a compound expression (e.g. $A = 2x$, not just $x$).

Pitfalls

  • Expanding $(v+2x)(v-2x)$ via FOIL and dropping a sign on the $-4x^2$ term.
  • Forgetting to square the coefficient: writing $v^2 - 2x^2$ instead of $v^2 - 4x^2$.
  • Multiplying the external factor (e.g. $2x$) into only one bracket before applying the identity, losing the conjugate structure.

A-level Scope Notes

Difference of squares is core C1/P1 algebra and reappears throughout IAL Maths and Further Maths — in surd manipulation, partial fractions, complex numbers (FP1), and as a recognition step in 'show that' differential equation questions.
