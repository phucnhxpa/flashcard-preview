Switch to quadratic formula once integer factorisation is ruled out

Family: Quadratic Solution Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a quadratic $ax^2+bx+c=0$ has been reached (often after clearing fractions or rearranging) and a quick search for integer factor pairs of $ac$ summing to $b$ fails. Equivalent cues: the discriminant $b^2-4ac$ is not a perfect square, or the leading coefficient is awkward (e.g. $a=2,3$) so trial factorisation is slow. Phrasings include 'solve exactly', 'give answers in surd form', or simply 'solve' when factorisation is not obvious.

Motivation — Why this works

The quadratic formula $x=\frac{-b\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}$ always yields exact roots, so once factorisation is shown to be unavailable over the integers it is the most efficient exact method. Checking the discriminant first both confirms irrational roots (saving wasted factorisation attempts) and gives the surd that will appear in the answer. Completing the square is mathematically equivalent but more error-prone when $a\neq 1$.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the equation has reduced to a standard quadratic $ax^2+bx+c=0$ with integer coefficients, so I scan for an obvious factorisation.
  2. I rule out integer factorisation: no pair of integers multiplying to $ac$ sums to $b$, and a quick discriminant check shows $b^2-4ac$ is not a perfect square.
  3. I commit to the quadratic formula because it is guaranteed to deliver exact (surd) roots in one pass, whereas completing the square with $a\neq 1$ multiplies the chance of arithmetic slips.
  4. Bridge: I write down $a$, $b$, $c$ explicitly with their signs before substituting, to avoid the classic $-b$ sign error.
  5. Inside the move I evaluate the discriminant carefully, simplify the surd if possible, and finally check each root against any domain restrictions inherited from the original problem.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the quadratic in the form $ax^2+bx+c=0$ and identify $a$, $b$, $c$ with correct signs.
  2. Test for integer factorisation: list factor pairs of $ac$ and check whether any sum to $b$.
  3. If no pair works, compute the discriminant $\Delta=b^2-4ac$.
  4. If $\Delta$ is not a perfect square (and not negative, in real-roots context), apply $x=\frac{-b\pm\sqrt{\Delta}}{2a}$.
  5. Simplify the surd (e.g. $\sqrt{12}=2\sqrt{3}$) and reduce the fraction if a common factor exists.
  6. Check each root against any domain restrictions from the original equation (e.g. excluded values from a denominator).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $2x^2-5x-2=0$. Factor pairs of $ac=-4$ are $(1,-4),(-1,4),(2,-2)$; none sum to $-5$, so factorisation fails. Compute $\Delta=(-5)^2-4(2)(-2)=25+16=41$, not a perfect square. Hence $x=\frac{5\pm\sqrt{41}}{4}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic arising after clearing a rational equation with denominator $(x-1)$
After applying the formula, verify each root satisfies $x\neq 1$ before stating the final solution set.
Quadratic in a substituted variable, e.g. $2u^2-5u-2=0$ with $u=e^x$ or $u=\ln x$
Apply the formula in $u$, then back-substitute and discard any $u$-values incompatible with the original variable's range (e.g. $u=e^x>0$).
Trigonometric equation reducing to a quadratic in $\sin\theta$ or $\cos\theta$
Apply the formula, then reject any root outside $[-1,1]$ before solving the trig equation.
Discriminant turns out negative
Conclude no real solutions (IAL Maths) or write complex roots $x=\frac{-b\pm i\sqrt{|\Delta|}}{2a}$ (Further Pure).
Discriminant is a perfect square after all
The formula still works but factorisation would have been quicker; record this as a check on the earlier ruling-out step.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the failure of one or two factor-pair guesses as definitive without computing the discriminant.
  • Persisting with completing the square when $a\neq 1$, leading to fraction-heavy algebra.
  • Reading 'solve' as 'give decimal answers' and skipping the exact surd form expected by the mark scheme.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error on $-b$ when $b$ is already negative: $-(-5)=+5$, not $-5$.
  • Arithmetic slip in $b^2-4ac$, especially with double negatives such as $-4(2)(-2)=+16$.
  • Failing to simplify the surd or reduce the fraction, which can lose a final mark.
  • Forgetting to discard roots that violate domain restrictions from the original equation.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics content (P1): the quadratic formula, discriminant, and surd manipulation. Complex-root extensions belong to Further Pure.
