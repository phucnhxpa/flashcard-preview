Matrices

Use trace as sum-of-eigenvalues to constrain or sanity-check

Family: Trace Constraint Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A square matrix problem asks for eigenvalues and at least one eigenvalue is given, suspected, or already found. The diagonal entries are visible and easy to sum. Phrases like 'show that the other eigenvalues satisfy…', 'find the remaining eigenvalues', or a stated eigenvalue that looks inconsistent with the matrix's appearance all cue this move. It also fires whenever you want a quick check on a computed characteristic polynomial.

Motivation — Why this works

For any square matrix, tr(M) = Σλᵢ (counted with algebraic multiplicity), because the trace equals the coefficient of −λ^(n−1) in det(M − λI) up to sign. This single scalar identity converts a hard eigen-problem into one linear constraint on the eigenvalues. Combined with one known eigenvalue, the remaining eigenvalues' sum is fixed, halving the work and catching arithmetic slips before they propagate.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the diagonal entries are easy to add and at least one eigenvalue is given or claimed.
  2. Rule out expanding det(M − λI) immediately — that is heavier than necessary if a quick constraint exists.
  3. Commit to the identity tr(M) = Σλᵢ as the cheapest piece of information about the spectrum.
  4. Sum the diagonal, set it equal to λ₁ + λ₂ + λ₃, and substitute the known eigenvalue to isolate λ₂ + λ₃.
  5. Decide whether this constraint alone suffices, or whether to pair it with det(M) = ∏λᵢ to pin down both remaining eigenvalues without a full characteristic polynomial.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute tr(M) = m₁₁ + m₂₂ + m₃₃ (or sum of all diagonal entries for n×n).
  2. Write λ₁ + λ₂ + … + λₙ = tr(M).
  3. Substitute any known eigenvalue(s) to obtain a linear relation among the remaining ones.
  4. If needed, combine with det(M) = ∏λᵢ to form a system that determines the rest.
  5. Use the relation as a check against any eigenvalues found by other means.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $M$ be $3\times 3$ with diagonal $(-6,-6,-5)$, so $\operatorname{tr}(M) = -17$. Given $\lambda_1 = -3$, the trace identity gives $\lambda_2 + \lambda_3 = -17 - (-3) = -14$. If additionally $\det(M) = -42$, then $\lambda_2\lambda_3 = -42/(-3) = 14$, so $\lambda_2,\lambda_3$ are roots of $t^2 + 14t + 14 = 0$, giving $t = -7 \pm \sqrt{35}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

2×2 matrix with no eigenvalue given
Use tr(M) = λ₁ + λ₂ and det(M) = λ₁λ₂ together; eigenvalues are roots of t² − tr(M)t + det(M) = 0, bypassing det(M − λI).
3×3 with characteristic polynomial expansion required
Trace equals the coefficient of −λ² in det(M − λI); use it to verify the expansion before solving.
Matrix powers or Cayley–Hamilton style problems
Trace of M gives Σλᵢ; trace of M² gives Σλᵢ², providing extra symmetric-function constraints.
Verifying a claimed eigenvalue
Check whether the claimed value is consistent with tr(M) and det(M); a contradiction flags an error.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the trace as the sum of distinct eigenvalues rather than the sum with algebraic multiplicities, so a repeated eigenvalue is undercounted.
  • Confusing trace with determinant and writing tr(M) = ∏λᵢ.
  • Summing only two of three diagonal entries when reading a 3×3 matrix off the page.

Pitfalls

  • Assuming the two unknown eigenvalues are equal and just halving λ₂ + λ₃ without justification.
  • Sign errors when diagonal entries are negative — losing a minus during summation.
  • Forgetting to include any further given eigenvalues before isolating the unknowns.
  • Using trace alone when two unknowns remain; one equation cannot determine two values without the determinant or another relation.

A-level Scope Notes

Trace as sum of eigenvalues and determinant as product of eigenvalues are standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure matrix tools for 2×2 and 3×3 cases, used alongside det(M − λI) for characteristic polynomials.
