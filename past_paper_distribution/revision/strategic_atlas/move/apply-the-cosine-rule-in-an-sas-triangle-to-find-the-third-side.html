Trigonometry

Apply the cosine rule in an SAS triangle to find the third side

Family: Cosine Rule Sas Frequency: 5 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fire this move whenever a triangle is presented (or can be isolated from a larger figure) with two known side lengths and the angle between them, and the side opposite that angle is required. Visual cues: a labelled angle wedged between two labelled sides; a diagonal of a parallelogram, kite, or sector-and-triangle composite that closes a triangle whose two adjacent sides and included angle are known; a perimeter problem in which one edge is the third side of such a triangle. Equivalent phrasings of the trigger include 'SAS data given, opposite side wanted', 'two sides and the angle between them are known', and 'a chord/diagonal closes a triangle whose two flanking sides and included angle are known'. The sine rule is NOT yet available because no angle-opposite-side pair is known.

Motivation — Why this works

The cosine rule c² = a² + b² − 2ab cos C is the canonical tool for the SAS configuration: it is the unique standard formula that produces the third side directly from two sides and their included angle. The sine rule needs an angle-opposite-side pair we don't yet have, and Pythagoras only applies to right triangles. Geometrically, the −2ab cos C term encodes how the third side stretches (when C is obtuse, cos C < 0 so the term is additive) or shrinks (when C is acute) compared with the right-angled baseline a² + b². It is the law-of-cosines generalisation of Pythagoras and appears in the Edexcel formula booklet.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the data: I have been given (or can read off) exactly two sides of a triangle and the angle sandwiched between them, and I need the side opposite that angle (often a diagonal, chord, or perimeter edge).
  2. Rule out the sine rule: it requires a known side together with the angle opposite to it, and I have no such pair yet — using it would force me to find another angle first, an unnecessary detour.
  3. Rule out Pythagoras: there is no right angle marked or implied, so a² + b² = c² does not apply.
  4. Commit to the cosine rule because SAS → third side is exactly what c² = a² + b² − 2ab cos C delivers in one step.
  5. Plan the substitution: identify which side is c (the unknown, opposite the known angle C) and which two sides are a and b (the flanking sides); keep the calculator in the correct angle mode (radians vs degrees) to match the given angle.
  6. Sense-check using the angle: if C is obtuse, expect c > max(a,b); if C is acute, expect c smaller than that bound. This catches sign errors immediately.
  7. Finish by taking the positive square root — the side length is c, not c².

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Label the triangle so that the unknown side c is opposite the known included angle C, with the two known sides as a and b.
  2. Write the cosine rule: c² = a² + b² − 2ab cos C.
  3. Substitute the numerical values, keeping cos C symbolic until the final evaluation to avoid early rounding.
  4. Evaluate cos C in the correct angle mode (degrees or radians as the question dictates); if C = π − θ, use cos(π − θ) = −cos θ.
  5. Compute c² as a single numeric value, carrying extra decimal places.
  6. Take the positive square root: c = √(c²).
  7. Sense-check: compare c against a, b and the size of C (obtuse ⇒ c is the largest side).

Worked Micro-Example

In $\triangle ABD$ with $AB=10$, $AD=6$, $\angle DAB = 138.19°$, find $BD$. \[BD^2 = 10^2 + 6^2 - 2(10)(6)\cos(138.19°) = 136 - 120(-0.7455) = 136 + 89.46 = 225.46.\] Hence $BD = \sqrt{225.46} \approx 15.0$ cm. The angle is obtuse, so $BD$ exceeds both flanking sides — sense-check passes.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Included angle expressed as π − θ (or 180° − θ)
Use cos(π − θ) = −cos θ; the −2ab cos C term flips sign and becomes additive, producing a longer third side.
Angle given in radians (e.g. 0.37 rad, 2 rad)
Switch the calculator to radian mode before evaluating cos C; the algebra is identical.
Three sides known, an angle wanted (SSS)
Rearrange to cos C = (a² + b² − c²)/(2ab) and take arccos.
Composite figure (sector + triangle, parallelogram, kite)
First isolate the SAS triangle by drawing the diagonal/chord, then apply the cosine rule on that triangle alone.
3D vector form
|a − b|² = |a|² + |b|² − 2 a·b — the same identity with a·b = |a||b|cos C.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying the sine rule first despite having no angle-opposite-side pair — leads to an algebraic dead-end.
  • Assuming a triangle inside a parallelogram or sector is right-angled and using Pythagoras without justification.
  • Picking the wrong diagonal/triangle so that the 'included' angle is no longer between the two known sides.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error: writing +2ab cos C instead of −2ab cos C, or forgetting that cos of an obtuse angle is negative.
  • Calculator in the wrong angle mode (degrees vs radians), giving a nonsensical value of cos C.
  • Premature rounding of cos C or of an angle imported from an earlier part — keep unrounded values until the final step.
  • Forgetting to take the square root and quoting c² as the side length.
  • Mislabelling which side is opposite the included angle, putting the wrong side on the left of the equation.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P2/P3) and Mechanics scope; the cosine rule c² = a² + b² − 2ab cos C is in the formula booklet. Stay with planar triangles — extensions to spherical trigonometry or non-Euclidean geometries are out of scope.
