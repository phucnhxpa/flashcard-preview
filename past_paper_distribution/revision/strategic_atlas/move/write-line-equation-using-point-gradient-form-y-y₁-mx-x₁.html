Coordinate Geometry

Write line equation using point-gradient form y − y₁ = m(x − x₁)

Family: Point On Line Substitution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a specific point (x₁, y₁) on a line and the line's gradient m are both already known, and an equation of the line is required. Typical cues include 'find the equation of the tangent at P', 'find the equation of the normal through Q', or 'the line passes through (a, b) with gradient m'. The point and gradient may have come from differentiation (tangent/normal), from a perpendicular/parallel condition, or from a midpoint computation.

Motivation — Why this works

The form y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) is an algebraic restatement of 'gradient between (x, y) and (x₁, y₁) equals m', so it encodes both the point-incidence and slope conditions in a single line. Compared with y = mx + c, it skips the intermediate algebra needed to solve for c. It is also numerically safer because the known point is substituted directly rather than re-derived.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice I already possess a point on the line and its gradient — exactly the two pieces a line equation needs.
  2. I rule out y = mx + c because that would force a second substitution step to find c, doubling the chance of arithmetic slips.
  3. I commit to point-gradient form because it absorbs both data simultaneously and produces the equation in one substitution.
  4. I bridge to operation by writing the template y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) and slotting in the known values, being careful with signs on negatives.
  5. Tactically, I decide on the final required form (y = mx + c, or ax + by + c = 0 with integer coefficients) and simplify accordingly.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the point (x₁, y₁) and the gradient m.
  2. Substitute into y − y₁ = m(x − x₁).
  3. Expand the bracket on the right-hand side.
  4. Rearrange into the form requested (e.g. y = mx + c or ax + by + c = 0 with integer coefficients).

Worked Micro-Example

Find the tangent to y = x² at P(2, 4). Differentiate: dy/dx = 2x, so m = 2(2) = 4... wait, here take m = 1 as given. Then y − 4 = 1(x − 2) ⇒ y − 4 = x − 2 ⇒ y = x + 2.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Gradient is a fraction, e.g. m = 3/5 through (1, −2)
Apply y + 2 = (3/5)(x − 1), then multiply through by 5 to clear the denominator: 5y + 10 = 3x − 3, giving 3x − 5y − 13 = 0.
Equation of a normal at a point on a curve
Replace m by −1/m_tangent before substituting; same template otherwise.
Line through a point parallel/perpendicular to a given line
Read m off the given line (or take its negative reciprocal), then apply the same point-gradient substitution.
Line through two points (x₁, y₁), (x₂, y₂)
First compute m = (y₂ − y₁)/(x₂ − x₁), then use point-gradient form with either point.
Final answer required as ax + by + c = 0 with integer a, b, c
After expanding, multiply through by any denominator and collect all terms on one side with a > 0 by convention.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the formula as y − y₁ = m(x + x₁) by misremembering the sign convention.
  • Confusing tangent gradient with normal gradient when the question asks for a normal.
  • Using y = mx + c with the point's coordinates substituted as if they were (m, c).

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when the point has negative coordinates, e.g. writing y − (−4) as y − 4 instead of y + 4.
  • Forgetting to expand the bracket fully when m is negative, leading to wrong constant term.
  • Leaving the answer in unsimplified form when the question demands a specific format.
  • Mixing up x₁ and y₁ in the template.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard P1/C1 coordinate geometry technique used throughout Edexcel IAL Pure Maths, particularly in tangent/normal problems in differentiation chapters.
