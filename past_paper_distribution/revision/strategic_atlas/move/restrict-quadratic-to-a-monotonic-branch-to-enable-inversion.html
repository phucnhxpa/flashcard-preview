Algebra & Functions

Restrict quadratic to a monotonic branch to enable inversion

Family: Domain Restriction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a function involving an even power (typically a quadratic like $g(x) = 5 - 2x^2$ or $h(x) = (x-a)^2 + b$) is presented with a one-sided domain such as $x \geq 0$, $x \leq a$, or $x \geq a$, and the question asks for $g^{-1}$, its domain/range, or its sketch. The restriction is the examiner's signal that injectivity must be enforced before inversion. Also fires when asked to 'state the largest domain' on which a quadratic is invertible.

Motivation — Why this works

A quadratic is many-to-one on $\mathbb{R}$ and so fails the horizontal line test, meaning no inverse function exists globally. Splitting at the vertex gives two monotonic branches, each of which is one-to-one and hence invertible on its half. The chosen branch's range becomes the inverse's domain, and the chosen branch's domain becomes the inverse's range — these swap roles under reflection in $y = x$.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the function is quadratic (even power) — instinctively flag many-to-one on $\mathbb{R}$.
  2. Rule out 'no inverse exists' by reading the stated domain: a one-sided restriction is given precisely to bypass non-injectivity.
  3. Commit to working on the restricted branch; locate the vertex to confirm the restriction lies entirely on one side of it.
  4. Bridge: identify monotonicity on the branch (sign of derivative or shape argument), then read off the range to get $\text{dom}(g^{-1})$.
  5. Tactically, when solving $y = g(x)$ for $x$, choose the square-root sign that matches the original domain (e.g., $+\sqrt{\cdot}$ for $x \geq 0$).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the vertex of the quadratic and confirm the stated domain lies on one side of it.
  2. Establish strict monotonicity on that branch (e.g., $g'(x) = -4x \leq 0$ for $x \geq 0$).
  3. Compute the range of $g$ on the restricted domain by evaluating at the vertex and at the boundary/limit.
  4. Solve $y = g(x)$ for $x$, choosing the root sign consistent with the restricted domain.
  5. State $g^{-1}(x)$ together with its domain (= range of $g$) and range (= domain of $g$).

Worked Micro-Example

Let $g(x) = 5 - 2x^2$, $x \geq 0$. Vertex at $(0,5)$ and $g$ is strictly decreasing on $[0,\infty)$, so range is $(-\infty, 5]$. Solving $y = 5 - 2x^2$ gives $x^2 = \tfrac{5-y}{2}$, and since $x \geq 0$ take $x = \sqrt{\tfrac{5-y}{2}}$. Hence $g^{-1}(x) = \sqrt{\tfrac{5-x}{2}}$ with domain $x \leq 5$ and range $g^{-1}(x) \geq 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Upward parabola restricted to $x \leq a$, e.g. $h(x) = (x-2)^2 + 1$, $x \leq 2$
Branch is decreasing; choose the negative root: $h^{-1}(x) = 2 - \sqrt{x-1}$, domain $x \geq 1$.
Even quartic-type, e.g. $f(x) = x^4 + 3$, $x \geq 0$
Same logic — restrict to one side of $x=0$; inverse uses $+\sqrt[4]{\cdot}$.
Modulus function $f(x) = |x - c|$ with $x \geq c$
Restriction makes $f(x) = x-c$ on that branch, so $f^{-1}(x) = x + c$, domain $x \geq 0$.
Composite with quadratic inner, e.g. $g(x) = \sqrt{x^2+1}$, $x \geq 0$
Same one-branch reasoning before inverting; pick non-negative root when undoing the square.

Common Misreadings

  • Ignoring the stated domain and declaring 'no inverse exists because $g$ is not one-to-one'.
  • Treating the restriction as cosmetic and writing $g^{-1}(x) = \pm\sqrt{\tfrac{5-x}{2}}$ with both signs.
  • Confusing 'range of $g$' with 'codomain $\mathbb{R}$' and giving $g^{-1}$ an incorrect domain.

Pitfalls

  • Choosing the wrong sign of the square root, producing an inverse whose range contradicts the original domain.
  • Forgetting to state the domain of $g^{-1}$ — a frequent mark loss in Edexcel mark schemes.
  • Misidentifying the vertex (sign error in completing the square) and so giving the wrong range bound.
  • Writing the inverse's domain as a strict inequality when the original domain includes its endpoint (or vice versa).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (Functions: inverse functions, domain and range). Monotonicity may be argued by shape or by a simple derivative sign — both are acceptable at A-level.
