Factorise monic quadratic by finding factor pair of c summing to −b

Family: Quadratic Factorisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires on a monic quadratic $x^2 + bx + c = 0$ (or $=k$ rearranged to this form) with integer coefficients, especially when the context (sum/product word problems, 'show that' parts, or 'find the exact roots') hints at integer or rational roots. Also cued when the discriminant looks like it will be a perfect square, or when the quadratic formula would be arithmetically heavy. A strong signal is when $b$ and $c$ are both integers and $c$ has a manageable list of factor pairs.

Motivation — Why this works

If $x^2 + bx + c = (x - p)(x - q)$, then expanding gives $p + q = -b$ and $pq = c$ (Vieta's relations). So finding the integer pair $(p, q)$ with the right product and sum directly recovers the roots without the quadratic formula. This is faster, exact, and aligned with how mark schemes credit factorised form in 'show that' or sum-and-product questions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the quadratic is monic with integer $b$ and $c$, and the problem context (e.g. two numbers with given sum and product) hints at integer roots.
  2. Rule out the quadratic formula as the first move — it works but is heavier; only fall back to it if no integer factor pair exists.
  3. Commit to factorisation once a quick mental check (e.g. is $b^2 - 4c$ plausibly a perfect square?) suggests integer roots.
  4. Bridge: list factor pairs of $c$ systematically, starting from small factors, and check each pair's sum against $-b$.
  5. Tactical: track signs carefully — if $c > 0$ and $-b > 0$, both roots are positive; if $c > 0$ and $-b < 0$, both negative; if $c < 0$, the roots have opposite signs.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the quadratic in the form $x^2 + bx + c = 0$.
  2. Identify the target: two numbers $p, q$ with $pq = c$ and $p + q = -b$.
  3. List factor pairs of $|c|$ systematically and test sums, attaching signs consistent with $c$ and $-b$.
  4. Write the factorisation $(x - p)(x - q) = 0$.
  5. Apply the zero-product principle: $x = p$ or $x = q$.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $x^2 - 175x + 7350 = 0$. Need $pq = 7350$, $p + q = 175$. Testing factor pairs of $7350$: $70 \times 105 = 7350$ and $70 + 105 = 175$. So $x^2 - 175x + 7350 = (x-70)(x-105) = 0$, giving $x = 70$ or $x = 105$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Non-monic quadratic $ax^2 + bx + c$ with $a \neq 1$
Seek a pair with product $ac$ and sum $b$, then split the middle term and factor by grouping (e.g. $6x^2 + 11x - 10$: product $-60$, sum $+11$, pair $15, -4$).
Quadratic with negative constant term
The factor pair has opposite signs; choose them so their sum equals $-b$.
Quadratic in disguise, e.g. $u = x^2$ giving $u^2 - 5u + 6 = 0$
Factorise in $u$ first, then back-substitute and solve for $x$.
Discriminant is not a perfect square
Abandon factor-pair search and use the quadratic formula or completing the square.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing the sum target: searching for a pair summing to $b$ instead of $-b$, leading to wrong-signed factors.
  • Writing $(x + p)(x + q)$ when both roots should be subtracted, e.g. expanding $(x+70)(x+105)$ instead of $(x-70)(x-105)$.
  • Treating $ac$ as $c$ when $a \neq 1$, missing that the non-monic case requires the product $ac$.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips when listing factor pairs of a large constant.
  • Stopping the factor search too early and defaulting to the formula, losing time on a 'show that' or factorised-form mark.
  • Sign errors when $c < 0$, where one factor must be positive and one negative.
  • Forgetting to set each linear factor to zero after factorising.
