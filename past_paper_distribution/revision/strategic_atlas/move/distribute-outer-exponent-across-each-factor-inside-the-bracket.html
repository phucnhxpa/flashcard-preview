Algebra & Functions

Distribute outer exponent across each factor inside the bracket

Family: Power Of A Product Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A bracketed product of a constant and one or more variable powers is raised to an external exponent, e.g. (3pq^2)^4 or (2x^3y)^n. Fires whenever the whole bracket must be expanded before combining with surrounding terms (other fractions, products, or like-base simplifications). Cue phrases include 'simplify', 'express in the form ax^m y^n', or any expression where a bracketed product blocks further index-law manipulation.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity (xyz)^n = x^n y^n z^n is a direct consequence of repeated multiplication and commutativity. Distributing the outer exponent converts a compound power into separate single-variable powers, each of which obeys the basic index laws. Once unpacked, every factor — including the numerical coefficient — is in standard a x^m form and can be multiplied, divided, or cancelled cleanly with the rest of the expression.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the bracket contains a product (constant × variables) raised to a single outer power, so it is one packaged object blocking further work.
  2. Rule out trying to combine the bracket with neighbouring terms first — index laws like x^a · x^b = x^{a+b} only apply once each base is exposed.
  3. Commit to (xyz)^n = x^n y^n z^n because it is the unique rule that 'unpacks' a product power without changing its value.
  4. Bridge: rewrite the bracket as a product of separate powers, one per factor, including the numerical coefficient.
  5. Inside the move, evaluate the constant power numerically (e.g. 3^4 = 81) and apply (x^m)^n = x^{mn} to any already-powered variable factor.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify each factor inside the bracket: constant, and each variable (with its existing exponent).
  2. Raise every factor to the outer exponent: (abc)^n \to a^n b^n c^n.
  3. Evaluate the numerical part explicitly (e.g. 3^4 = 81).
  4. Apply the power-of-a-power rule to any variable that already has an exponent: (q^2)^4 = q^{2\times 4} = q^8.
  5. Write the result as a single product in standard form.

Worked Micro-Example

Simplify $(3pq^2)^4$.\\ $(3pq^2)^4 = 3^4 \cdot p^4 \cdot (q^2)^4$\\ $= 81 \cdot p^4 \cdot q^{2\times 4}$\\ $= 81p^4 q^8$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Fractional outer exponent, e.g. $(4x^2 y^6)^{1/2}$
Distribute $\tfrac{1}{2}$ to each factor: $4^{1/2} x^{2\cdot 1/2} y^{6\cdot 1/2} = 2xy^3$ (assuming positive variables).
Negative outer exponent, e.g. $(2a^3 b)^{-2}$
Distribute $-2$ to each factor: $2^{-2} a^{-6} b^{-2} = \tfrac{1}{4a^6 b^2}$.
Quotient inside bracket, e.g. $\left(\dfrac{2x}{y^3}\right)^3$
Use $(a/b)^n = a^n / b^n$ then distribute across numerator and denominator: $\dfrac{8x^3}{y^9}$.
Bracket appears inside a larger fraction or product
Unpack the bracket first, then combine like bases with the surrounding terms via $x^a x^b = x^{a+b}$ or $x^a / x^b = x^{a-b}$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the bracket as a sum and trying to expand term by term instead of using the product-power rule.
  • Believing the outer exponent only applies to the variables and not to the numerical coefficient.
  • Confusing $(q^2)^4$ with $q^2 \cdot q^4$, leading to addition of exponents rather than multiplication.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to raise the coefficient: leaving $3$ instead of $3^4 = 81$.
  • Applying the outer power to the variable but not to its existing exponent: writing $q^4$ instead of $q^8$ for $(q^2)^4$.
  • Mishandling signs with negative outer exponents: forgetting to invert as well as raise.
  • With fractional exponents, dropping domain assumptions (positivity) when taking even roots.
