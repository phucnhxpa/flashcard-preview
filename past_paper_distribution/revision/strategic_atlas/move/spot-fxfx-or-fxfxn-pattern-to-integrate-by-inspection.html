Integration

Spot f'(x)/f(x) or f'(x)В·f(x)^n pattern to integrate by inspection

Family: Reverse Chain Rule Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger вҖ” When to use

Fires when the integrand is a fraction or product whose numerator is a constant multiple of the derivative of an expression appearing in the denominator, under a root, or raised to a power. Concrete cues: a quotient like (linear or polynomial)/(polynomial) where differentiating the denominator reproduces the numerator up to a scalar; an integrand of the form x/вҲҡ(aВұxВІ), x/(aВұxВІ)^n, cos x/sin x, e^x/(e^x+c), or kf'(x)В·[f(x)]^n. Equivalent phrasings: 'numerator is essentially d/dx of denominator', 'the inside function's derivative is sitting outside', 'looks ready for u-substitution but the scalar is small enough to do by sight'.

Motivation вҖ” Why this works

By the reverse chain rule, вҲ«f'(x)/f(x) dx = ln|f(x)| + C and more generally вҲ«f'(x)[f(x)]^n dx = [f(x)]^(n+1)/(n+1) + C (for n вү  вҲ’1). Recognising this pattern collapses an apparently messy integral to a one-line antiderivative without a formal substitution write-out, which saves time and reduces algebra errors. It is the canonical tool for integrating logarithmic and root-type expressions whose 'inside' has a polynomial appearing as a factor outside.

Thought Process вҖ” The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structural cue: the integrand is a quotient (or product) where the numerator looks like a scaled derivative of an expression appearing in the denominator/root/power.
  2. Compute d/dx of the candidate inner function f(x) and compare to the numerator; check the proportionality constant k so that numerator = kВ·f'(x).
  3. Rule out alternatives: partial fractions is unnecessary (denominator may not factorise, or it's a single irreducible block); a full u-substitution would work but is overkill; standard arcsin/arctan forms don't apply because there IS an x in the numerator.
  4. Commit to the reverse-chain-rule template: identify whether the antiderivative is kВ·ln|f(x)| (denominator power 1) or kВ·[f(x)]^(n+1)/(n+1) (any other power, including вҲҡf corresponding to n = вҲ’1/2).
  5. Pin down the scalar: extract k carefully вҖ” divide the numerator by f'(x), don't just copy a coefficient. Track signs (e.g. d/dx(aвҲ’xВІ) = вҲ’2x introduces a minus).
  6. Drop |В·| if f(x) > 0 on the interval (e.g. 3xВІ+5 > 0 always).

Mechanic вҖ” Concrete operations

  1. Identify f(x) as the inner expression (denominator, radicand, or base of power) and compute f'(x).
  2. Write numerator as kВ·f'(x), solving for the constant k.
  3. Apply the appropriate template: вҲ«kВ·f'(x)/f(x) dx = k ln|f(x)| + C, or вҲ«kВ·f'(x)[f(x)]^n dx = kВ·[f(x)]^(n+1)/(n+1) + C.
  4. Simplify, drop absolute values if f(x) > 0 on the integration domain, and evaluate at limits if definite.
  5. (Sanity check: differentiate your answer mentally to confirm it returns the integrand.)

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int \frac{3x}{3x^2+5}\,dx$. Let $f(x)=3x^2+5$, so $f'(x)=6x$ and $3x = \tfrac{1}{2}f'(x)$. Thus $\int \frac{3x}{3x^2+5}\,dx = \tfrac{1}{2}\int \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)}\,dx = \tfrac{1}{2}\ln(3x^2+5)+C$ (no modulus needed since $3x^2+5>0$).

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing x/вҲҡ(aВІвҲ’xВІ) with 1/вҲҡ(aВІвҲ’xВІ); the latter integrates to arcsin(x/a) but only when there is NO x in the numerator.
  • Treating 3x/(3xВІ+5) as a 1/(ax+b) form and ignoring the x in the numerator.
  • Attempting partial fractions on an irreducible quadratic denominator instead of spotting the f'/f shape.

Pitfalls

  • Wrong scalar: e.g. writing 1/6 instead of 1/2 by carelessly dividing by the whole f'(x) rather than matching coefficients.
  • Dropping a sign: d/dx(aвҲ’xВІ) = вҲ’2x, so antiderivative of x/вҲҡ(aвҲ’xВІ) is вҲ’вҲҡ(aвҲ’xВІ), not +вҲҡ(aвҲ’xВІ).
  • Writing ln|f'(x)| instead of ln|f(x)|.
  • Forgetting to carry the constant k through, especially in definite integrals where the missing 1/2 halves the final answer.

A-level Scope Notes
