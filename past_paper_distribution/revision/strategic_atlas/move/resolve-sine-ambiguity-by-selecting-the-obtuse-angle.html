Trigonometry

Resolve sine ambiguity by selecting the obtuse angle

Family: Ambiguity Resolution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever $\sin\theta = k$ with $0 < k < 1$ is solved for an angle inside a triangle, so $\theta \in (0°, 180°)$. Both $\theta_1 = \arcsin k$ and $\theta_2 = 180° - \arcsin k$ are admissible from the equation alone. Cues include: a diagram suggesting an obtuse angle, a stated constraint that a side is the longest, an SSA configuration in the sine rule, or a mark scheme answer noticeably larger than the acute case would give.

Motivation — Why this works

Sine is positive on both $(0°, 90°)$ and $(90°, 180°)$, so $\sin\theta = k$ has two solutions in the triangle range related by $\theta_2 = 180° - \theta_1$. The two choices yield genuinely different triangles, and any downstream cosine rule or area calculation depends on the sign of $\cos\theta$, which flips between the two. Selecting the obtuse branch is justified by external geometric information (side ordering, diagram, or angle-sum feasibility), not by the sine equation itself.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that $\sin(\angle BAC) = 8/15$ is positive and less than 1, and the angle lies in $(0°, 180°)$, so two solutions are possible.
  2. Rule out treating $\arcsin(8/15)$ as the unique answer: in a triangle, the supplementary angle is equally valid from the equation.
  3. Commit to the obtuse branch because the context (e.g. expected BC the longest side, or diagram) requires $\cos(\angle BAC) < 0$.
  4. Bridge: compute $\theta_1 = \arcsin(8/15)$, then take $\theta_2 = 180° - \theta_1$ as the working angle.
  5. Tactically, carry $\cos\theta_2 = -\cos\theta_1 = -\sqrt{1 - (8/15)^2} = -\sqrt{161}/15$ exactly to avoid rounding errors when the cosine rule is applied next.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute the acute solution: $\theta_1 = \arcsin(8/15) \approx 32.23°$.
  2. Form the obtuse solution: $\theta_2 = 180° - \theta_1 \approx 147.77°$.
  3. Check feasibility: confirm $\theta_2$ is consistent with the remaining angle-sum/side constraints of the triangle.
  4. Select $\theta_2$ as $\angle BAC$ and, where useful, record $\cos\theta_2 = -\sqrt{1 - (8/15)^2} = -\sqrt{161}/15$ for the next step.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\sin(\angle BAC) = 8/15$ in $\triangle ABC$ with $AB = 7$, $AC = 25$ and BC required to exceed 25. Acute: $\arcsin(8/15) \approx 32.23°$; obtuse: $147.77°$. Choosing the obtuse angle gives $\cos(\angle BAC) = -\sqrt{161}/15$, so by the cosine rule $BC^2 = 7^2 + 25^2 - 2(7)(25)(-\sqrt{161}/15) = 674 + \tfrac{70\sqrt{161}}{3}$, yielding $BC \approx 30.6$, consistent with $BC > 25$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Sine rule SSA case: $\dfrac{\sin B}{b} = \dfrac{\sin A}{a}$ gives $\sin B = k$.
Test both $B$ and $180° - B$; reject any value making $A + B \geq 180°$, otherwise both triangles are valid and must be reported.
Solving $\sin x = k$ on $[0°, 360°]$ in a general trig equation.
Take both $x = \arcsin k$ and $x = 180° - \arcsin k$, then add $360°n$; the obtuse choice is not 'preferred' but listed alongside.
Triangle area formula $\tfrac12 ab\sin C = S$ solved for $C$.
Acute and obtuse $C$ both give the same area; external information (diagram, side lengths) selects the correct one.
Cosine rule giving $\cos\theta < 0$.
No ambiguity arises — cosine uniquely determines $\theta \in (0°, 180°)$, so prefer cosine rule when an angle's quadrant must be unambiguous.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming the angle must be acute and reporting only $\arcsin(8/15)$.
  • Believing the calculator's principal value is the 'correct' triangle angle.
  • Failing to recognise that an SSA configuration can produce two valid triangles.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to flip the sign of $\cos\theta$ when switching to the obtuse branch in the cosine rule.
  • Using the rounded acute angle and then subtracting from $180°$, accumulating rounding error — keep $\cos\theta = -\sqrt{1-k^2}$ exactly.
  • Selecting the obtuse angle without checking $\theta_2 +$ (other known angle) $< 180°$.
  • Justifying the choice purely by 'matches the answer' rather than by a stated geometric constraint.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P2/P3 content: solving $\sin\theta = k$ in a given range and the ambiguous case of the sine rule in non-right triangles.
