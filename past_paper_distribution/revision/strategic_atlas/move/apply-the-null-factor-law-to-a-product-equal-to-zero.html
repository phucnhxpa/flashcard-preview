Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an equation is presented (or has just been rearranged) as a product of expressions equal to zero, e.g. $(3x+2)(x-4)=0$, $x(x^2-5)=0$, or $(2\sin x - 1)(\cos x + 1)=0$. Cue phrases include 'hence solve', 'find the values of $x$', or a fully factorised left-hand side with $=0$ on the right. Also fires after a successful factorisation step in quadratic, cubic, or trigonometric solving. The right-hand side being exactly $0$ is the non-negotiable signal.

Motivation — Why this works

In any field (or integral domain) — including $\mathbb{R}$ and $\mathbb{C}$, the only number systems used at A-level — the product $AB=0$ forces $A=0$ or $B=0$, because there are no zero divisors. This converts a single nonlinear equation into a disjunction of simpler equations that can be solved independently. It is the structural justification for why factorisation is a legitimate solving strategy, not just a manipulation. Every root of the original equation appears as a root of at least one factor, so the method is exhaustive.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the equation has the shape (expression)(expression)$=0$ with zero isolated on one side.
  2. Rules out further algebraic manipulation (expanding, dividing through, squaring) — the factored form is already maximally exploitable, and any other move risks losing roots.
  3. Commits to the null factor law because it is the unique logical bridge from a product-equals-zero to explicit solutions.
  4. Splits the single equation into one linear (or simpler) equation per factor, each set equal to zero.
  5. Solves each sub-equation independently and unions the solution sets, retaining repeated roots with their multiplicity if the question asks for it.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Confirm the equation is in the form (factor 1)(factor 2)$\cdots =0$ with $0$ on the right-hand side.
  2. Set each factor equal to zero in turn, producing one equation per factor.
  3. Solve each resulting equation by standard linear (or trig/exponential) methods.
  4. Combine all solutions using 'or', noting any repeated roots from repeated factors.
  5. If a domain is specified (e.g. $0 \le x < 2\pi$), filter the candidate solutions accordingly.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $(3x+2)(x-4)=0$.\\ By the null factor law, $3x+2=0$ or $x-4=0$.\\ From $3x+2=0$: $x=-\tfrac{2}{3}$.\\ From $x-4=0$: $x=4$.\\ Hence $x=-\tfrac{2}{3}$ or $x=4$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Repeated factor, e.g. $(x-1)^2(x+3)=0$
Apply the law to each distinct factor; record $x=1$ as a repeated (double) root and $x=-3$ as simple.
Three or more factors, e.g. $x(x-2)(2x+5)=0$
Set each of the three factors to zero, giving $x=0,\ 2,\ -\tfrac{5}{2}$.
Factorised trigonometric equation, e.g. $(2\sin x - 1)(\cos x + 1)=0$ on $0 \le x < 2\pi$
Generate $\sin x = \tfrac{1}{2}$ and $\cos x = -1$, then solve each within the stated interval.
Factorised exponential/log equation, e.g. $(e^x - 2)(e^x - 5)=0$
Set each factor to zero, giving $e^x=2$ or $e^x=5$, then take logs: $x=\ln 2$ or $x=\ln 5$.
Cubic after factor theorem, e.g. $(x-1)(x^2+x-6)=0$
Apply the law: $x=1$ or solve the residual quadratic $x^2+x-6=0$ (factor again or use the formula).

Common Misreadings

  • Setting the two factors equal to each other (e.g. $3x+2=x-4$) instead of each equal to zero.
  • Applying the law when the right-hand side is a non-zero constant, e.g. wrongly inferring $A=6$ or $B=6$ from $AB=6$.
  • Treating $(x-a)^2=0$ as giving two distinct roots rather than a single repeated root.
  • Discarding the factor $x$ in $x(x-3)=0$ and reporting only $x=3$, missing $x=0$.

Pitfalls

  • Dividing both sides by a factor (e.g. cancelling $x$) and thereby losing the root from that factor.
  • Forgetting to list all solutions when multiple factors yield valid roots.
  • Not filtering solutions to the required interval in trigonometric problems.
  • Failing to re-factorise a residual quadratic or higher-degree factor after the first split.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1–P4) and Further Pure scope: applies to real and complex polynomial equations, factorised trigonometric equations, and factorised exponential/log equations, all over fields with no zero divisors.
