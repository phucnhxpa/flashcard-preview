Sequences & Series

Split sum to k+1 into sum to k plus the (k+1)th term

Family: Recursive Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in the inductive step of a proof by induction on a summation identity, when the goal is to evaluate or rewrite $\sum_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r)$ and the inductive hypothesis provides a closed form for $\sum_{r=1}^{k} f(r)$. Cue phrases include 'Assume true for n=k' followed by 'Show true for n=k+1' on a $\Sigma$-statement. Any time you see the upper limit increase by 1 between hypothesis and goal, this decomposition is the bridge.

Motivation — Why this works

A finite sum is recursive: $\sum_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r) = \sum_{r=1}^{k} f(r) + f(k+1)$. Splitting off the last term creates a slot where the inductive hypothesis can be substituted, converting a statement about sums into an algebraic identity in $k$. Without this peel-off, the hypothesis cannot be used and the induction stalls. It is the canonical mechanism for $\Sigma$-induction in the Edexcel IAL FP1 syllabus.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the goal $\sum_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r)$ has upper limit one more than the hypothesis $\sum_{r=1}^{k} f(r)$.
  2. Rule out trying to manipulate the full sum to $k+1$ directly or re-deriving the formula from scratch — the hypothesis is meant to be used.
  3. Commit to the split because the hypothesis only matches sums with upper limit $k$, so the $(k+1)$th term must be isolated.
  4. Write $\sum_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r) = \sum_{r=1}^{k} f(r) + f(k+1)$ explicitly, then immediately substitute the hypothesis for the first piece.
  5. Compute $f(k+1)$ carefully by substituting $r=k+1$ (not $r=k$) into the general term, and keep $(k+1)$ as a common factor in anticipation of the target form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. State the goal: show $\sum_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r) = $ target closed form with $n=k+1$.
  2. Decompose: $\sum_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r) = \sum_{r=1}^{k} f(r) + f(k+1)$.
  3. Substitute the inductive hypothesis for $\sum_{r=1}^{k} f(r)$.
  4. Compute $f(k+1)$ by replacing $r$ with $k+1$ in the general term.
  5. Simplify algebraically (typically by factoring out a common $(k+1)$) until the expression matches the target.

Worked Micro-Example

Prove $\sum_{r=1}^{n} r(r+1) = \tfrac{n(n+1)(n+2)}{3}$. Assuming true for $n=k$: $\sum_{r=1}^{k+1} r(r+1) = \sum_{r=1}^{k} r(r+1) + (k+1)(k+2) = \tfrac{k(k+1)(k+2)}{3} + (k+1)(k+2) = \tfrac{(k+1)(k+2)(k+3)}{3}$, which matches the target with $n=k+1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Product identity $\prod_{r=1}^{n} f(r)$ proved by induction
Replace addition with multiplication: $\prod_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r) = \left(\prod_{r=1}^{k} f(r)\right) \cdot f(k+1)$.
Alternating series, e.g. $\sum_{r=1}^{n} (-1)^{r+1} f(r)$
Same decomposition, but track the sign of the $(k+1)$th term as $(-1)^{k+2} = (-1)^k$.
Recurrence-defined sequence $u_{n+1} = g(u_n)$ in induction proofs
Use the recurrence itself rather than a sum-split: write $u_{k+1} = g(u_k)$ and substitute the hypothesised closed form for $u_k$.
Matrix power induction $\mathbf{M}^n$ in FP1
Decompose $\mathbf{M}^{k+1} = \mathbf{M}^k \cdot \mathbf{M}$ and substitute the hypothesised form of $\mathbf{M}^k$.
Divisibility induction, e.g. $f(n) = a^n + \ldots$ divisible by $d$
Express $f(k+1)$ in terms of $f(k)$ plus a multiple of $d$, rather than a literal sum split.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $\sum_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r)$ and $\sum_{r=1}^{k} f(r)$ as unrelated objects and re-deriving from scratch.
  • Reading the $(k+1)$th term as $f(k)$ because $k$ is the 'current' index, instead of $f(k+1)$.
  • Assuming the closed form for $n=k+1$ is what should be substituted (circular reasoning), rather than the hypothesis at $n=k$.

Pitfalls

  • Substituting $r=k$ instead of $r=k+1$ into the general term when computing the extra term.
  • Forgetting to apply the inductive hypothesis after splitting, leaving an unevaluated $\sum_{r=1}^{k}$.
  • Algebra slips when combining $\tfrac{k(k+1)(k+2)}{3} + (k+1)(k+2)$ — failing to take out the common factor $(k+1)(k+2)$.
  • Not verifying that the simplified expression matches the target closed form with $n$ replaced by $k+1$ exactly.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP1) proof by induction on $\Sigma$-identities; the same decomposition principle extends to product, divisibility, recurrence, and matrix-power inductions also in FP1.
