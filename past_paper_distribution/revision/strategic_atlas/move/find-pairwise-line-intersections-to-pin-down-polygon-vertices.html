Coordinate Geometry

Find pairwise line intersections to pin down polygon vertices

Family: Intersection Identification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a region in the plane is described by three or more boundary lines (e.g. $y=0$, $y=2$, $y=kx$, $y=10-2x$) and an area, perimeter, or coordinate of a corner is required. Cues include phrases like 'the region bounded by', 'the shape enclosed', or a labelled diagram with unspecified vertex coordinates. Also fires when one vertex is given a name (say $a$) and you need to relate it to the other unknowns. Essentially: any time area/length depends on corners that aren't yet explicit.

Motivation — Why this works

Area and length formulas need explicit coordinates, not implicit boundary equations. Solving each adjacent pair of boundary equations simultaneously converts the description 'region between these lines' into a concrete polygon with named vertices. This both fixes the geometry and often produces the algebraic links between unknowns (e.g. $a=2/k$) that the problem will later exploit. Without this step, one is guessing at the shape rather than working with it.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the region is described only by boundary equations — no coordinates of corners are stated.
  2. Rule out attempting an area formula immediately: without vertices, neither base/height nor a determinant nor an integral can be set up.
  3. Commit to identifying which pairs of boundary lines actually meet at corners (not every pair of boundaries is adjacent in the polygon).
  4. Bridge: list adjacent boundary pairs in cyclic order around the region and solve each pair simultaneously.
  5. Tactically, label the unknown intersection in the problem's notation (e.g. call $(a,2)$ the meeting of $y=kx$ and $y=2$) so the resulting relation $a=2/k$ falls out automatically.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Sketch the region and list the boundary equations.
  2. Walk around the polygon and identify each pair of lines that meet at a corner.
  3. For each adjacent pair, solve simultaneously by substitution or elimination.
  4. Record each vertex as an ordered pair, using the problem's labels for unknown coordinates.
  5. Read off any algebraic relations between unknowns that emerge (e.g. $a=2/k$).
  6. Check that the vertices, taken in order, actually form a simple (non-self-intersecting) polygon consistent with the sketch.

Worked Micro-Example

Region bounded by $y=0$, $y=2$, $y=kx$, $y=10-2x$. Intersections: $y=kx\cap y=0\Rightarrow(0,0)$; $y=kx\cap y=2\Rightarrow(2/k,2)=(a,2)$ so $a=2/k$; $y=2\cap y=10-2x\Rightarrow(4,2)$; $y=10-2x\cap y=0\Rightarrow(5,0)$. Vertices: $(0,0),(a,2),(4,2),(5,0)$ — a trapezium with parallel sides on $y=0$ and $y=2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Region bounded by a curve and a line (e.g. $y=x^2$ and $y=x+2$) for area by integration
Solve the curve and line simultaneously to get integration limits rather than polygon vertices.
Triangle defined by three lines, find area
Solve each of the three pairs of lines to get the three vertices, then apply the determinant area formula.
Two curves intersecting (e.g. circle and line, or two parabolas)
Substitute one equation into the other to get a quadratic; the roots give the $x$-coordinates of the boundary points.
Piecewise-linear region in linear programming / inequality shading
Treat each inequality as an equality on its boundary and intersect adjacent boundaries to get the feasible region's vertices.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing which lines form which side, e.g. assuming the region extends up to where $y=kx$ meets $y=10-2x$ when $y=2$ actually caps it.
  • Pairing every line with every other line and getting spurious 'vertices' that lie outside the actual region.
  • Treating a labelled point like $(a,2)$ as a free parameter instead of recognising it as a determined intersection.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to record the algebraic link between unknowns (here $a=2/k$), which then forces an unnecessary second equation later.
  • Algebra slips when solving $y=kx$ with $y=c$: writing $x=ck$ instead of $x=c/k$.
  • Listing vertices in non-cyclic order, which breaks the shoelace/trapezium-area calculation that follows.
  • Including a candidate intersection that lies outside the bounded region as a genuine vertex.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure scope: simultaneous linear equations (P1) and basic coordinate geometry of straight lines, with the same idea reused for curve–line intersections in integration (P2/P3).
