Complex Numbers

Set z = e^{iθ} to convert cosθ into the symmetric sum z + z^{-1}

Family: Exponential Parametrisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question sets z = e^{iθ} (or cosθ + isinθ) and asks to prove z^n + z^{-n} = 2cos(nθ), or to express a power like cos^n θ as a sum of multiple-angle cosines (linearisation). Also fires when an FP2/FP3 problem asks to evaluate ∫cos^n θ dθ via expansion, or to prove identities relating cos(nθ) to powers of cosθ. Cue phrases: 'show that', 'hence express cos^k θ in the form...', 'use de Moivre to deduce'.

Motivation — Why this works

Setting z = e^{iθ} turns cosθ into the symmetric algebraic quantity (z + z^{-1})/2, which behaves well under binomial expansion. By De Moivre, z^n + z^{-n} = 2cos(nθ), giving a clean return route from algebraic powers of z back to trigonometric multiple angles. This dual representation is the only practical way at A-level to linearise high powers of cosθ, because direct repeated use of double-angle formulae becomes unmanageable beyond cos^4 θ.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that the question fixes z = e^{iθ} on the unit circle, so |z| = 1 and z^{-1} = e^{-iθ} is the conjugate of z.
  2. Rules out half-angle/double-angle reduction because the target power (here cos^6 θ) would require nested substitutions and is error-prone.
  3. Commits to the exponential bridge because De Moivre guarantees z^n + z^{-n} = 2cos(nθ), turning algebra back into trig at the end.
  4. First concrete operation: write z^n = cos(nθ) + isin(nθ) and z^{-n} = cos(nθ) − isin(nθ), then add to cancel sines.
  5. Tactically, plans to expand (z + z^{-1})^n by the binomial theorem and pair terms z^k + z^{-k} symmetrically before converting back to 2cos(kθ).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write z = e^{iθ}, so z^n = e^{inθ} = cos(nθ) + isin(nθ) by De Moivre.
  2. Write z^{-n} = e^{-inθ} = cos(nθ) − isin(nθ).
  3. Add the two: the imaginary parts cancel, giving z^n + z^{-n} = 2cos(nθ).
  4. When linearising cos^n θ, raise (z + z^{-1}) = 2cosθ to the nth power and expand binomially.
  5. Pair terms equidistant from the centre as z^k + z^{-k} and replace each with 2cos(kθ); divide by 2^n.

Worked Micro-Example

Let z = e^{iθ}. Then $(2\cos\theta)^4 = (z+z^{-1})^4 = z^4 + 4z^2 + 6 + 4z^{-2} + z^{-4} = (z^4+z^{-4}) + 4(z^2+z^{-2}) + 6 = 2\cos 4\theta + 8\cos 2\theta + 6$. Hence $\cos^4\theta = \tfrac{1}{8}\cos 4\theta + \tfrac{1}{2}\cos 2\theta + \tfrac{3}{8}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Linearising powers of sinθ
Use z − z^{-1} = 2isinθ and the companion identity z^n − z^{-n} = 2isin(nθ); track the factors of i carefully (i^n cycles through ±1, ±i).
Expressing cos(nθ) as a polynomial in cosθ
Run the bridge in reverse: expand (cosθ + isinθ)^n by the binomial theorem and take real parts, using sin^2θ = 1 − cos^2θ.
Evaluating ∫cos^n θ dθ on FP2/FP3
Linearise first via z = e^{iθ}, then integrate the resulting sum of cos(kθ) term-by-term.
Mixed products like sin^a θ cos^b θ
Use both (z+z^{-1})^b and (z−z^{-1})^a, multiply, then pair symmetric terms.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing z^n + z^{-n} with z^n − z^{-n}, producing a sine identity when cosine was needed.
  • Treating (z + z^{-1})^n as equal to z^n + z^{-n} (forgetting the binomial cross-terms).
  • Forgetting the 2^n denominator when converting (2cosθ)^n back to cos^n θ.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error: z^{-n} flips the sign of the sine part only, not the cosine part.
  • Mispairing binomial coefficients when n is even — the central term is unpaired and contributes a constant.
  • Forgetting the factor of i^n when working with sine powers, leading to wrong sign on the final identity.
  • Failing to divide by 2^n at the end when extracting cos^n θ from (2cosθ)^n.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3): de Moivre's theorem, exponential form of complex numbers, and applications to trigonometric identities and integrals.
