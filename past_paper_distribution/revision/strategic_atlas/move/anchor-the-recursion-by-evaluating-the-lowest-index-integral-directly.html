Integration

Anchor the recursion by evaluating the lowest-index integral directly

Family: Base Case Evaluation Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a reduction formula expresses I_n in terms of I_{n-1}, I_{n-2}, I_{n-3}, or I_{n-4}, and you have descended the recursion as far as possible. The signal is that you are left needing a numerical (or closed-form) value for the smallest index — typically I_0, I_1, or I_2 — before the recursion can yield an explicit answer. Equivalently: 'I have a recurrence but no anchor', or 'the chain bottoms out at I_k and I now need to compute I_k directly'. Sometimes the choice of base case is forced by the step size of the reduction (step-2 needs both I_0 and I_1; step-3 needs I_2; step-4 needs I_1).

Motivation — Why this works

A reduction formula is purely recursive — it cannot produce a number without an initial condition, just as a sequence cannot be evaluated without its first term. Computing the lowest-index integral directly provides this anchor, and crucially that integral is the simplest member of the family (often a standard exponential, trig, or reverse-chain-rule integral). The principle invoked is usually direct integration or the reverse chain rule (recognising d/dx[F(g(x))] = F'(g(x))g'(x)). Sometimes a clever choice of base case exploits a vanishing coefficient in the formula itself, terminating the recursion without any extra work.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the reduction formula has been applied as many times as possible and you are left with a symbol like I_0, I_1, or I_2 with no further recursion available.
  2. Rule out applying the reduction formula again — descending further would either give a negative index (often non-elementary, e.g. ∫x^{-1}√(x³+1)dx) or simply loop.
  3. Rule out integration by parts from scratch on the base case if the integrand is already simple (e.g. ∫e^{-x}dx, ∫x cos(x²)dx) — direct integration is faster.
  4. Commit to evaluating the base integral by inspection: identify whether it is a standard form (∫e^{-x}, ∫sin x, ∫cos x) or a reverse-chain-rule pattern (f'(x)·g(f(x))).
  5. Tactical check: if the reduction formula has a coefficient like (n−k) multiplying I_{n−k}, setting n=k makes that term vanish, so I_k can be read off from the boundary term alone — pick this base case if available to skip computing an awkward integral.
  6. Substitute the limits, mind signs at 0 (where x^0 = 1, sin 0 = 0, e^0 = 1), and feed the value back up the recursion.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the smallest index I_k that the recursion requires, given the step size of the reduction formula.
  2. Write I_k = ∫_a^b [integrand with n = k] dx explicitly.
  3. Recognise the antiderivative: standard form, or reverse chain rule via d/dx[F(g(x))].
  4. Evaluate at the upper and lower limits, being careful with signs.
  5. Substitute the resulting numerical value of I_k back into the reduction chain to obtain I_n.

Worked Micro-Example

For I_n = ∫_0^1 x^n e^{-x} dx with reduction I_n = nI_{n−1} − e^{-1}, anchor at n=0: I_0 = ∫_0^1 e^{-x} dx = [−e^{-x}]_0^1 = 1 − e^{-1}. Then I_1 = 1·I_0 − e^{-1} = 1 − 2e^{-1}, and so on.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Step-2 reduction (e.g. ∫_0^{π/2} sin^n x dx with I_n in terms of I_{n−2})
Two base cases needed: I_0 = π/2 and I_1 = 1, chosen depending on whether n is even or odd.
Step-4 reduction with I_5 → I_1 (e.g. integrand x cos(x²))
Base case I_1 evaluates by reverse chain rule: ∫x cos(x²)dx = ½ sin(x²); avoids I_0 = ∫cos(x²)dx (Fresnel, non-elementary).
Step-3 reduction where (n−2) coefficient appears on I_{n−3}
Choose n=2 as the base case so the coefficient vanishes; I_2 is then read off from the boundary/non-recursive term alone, sidestepping I_{−1}.

Common Misreadings

  • Attempting to apply the reduction formula one more step into a negative index, leading to a non-elementary or undefined integral.
  • Trying integration by parts again on the base case when it is already a standard integral.
  • Thinking the reduction formula alone gives a numerical answer without an anchor.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors evaluating [−e^{-x}]_0^1 or [½ sin(x²)] at the limits.
  • Forgetting that x^0 = 1 (including at x=0) when setting n=0.
  • Arithmetic slips in the back-substitution chain, especially with nested fractions like (6/15)·(2/9) = 4/45.
  • Failing to spot a vanishing coefficient that would make a higher base case (e.g. I_2) trivially available.

A-level Scope Notes

All steps stay within Edexcel IAL P3/FP2/FP3 scope: standard integrals from the formula booklet (∫e^{-x}, ∫sin x, ∫cos x), reverse chain rule, and reduction formulae as taught in Further Pure. Avoid invoking Fresnel integrals or the Gamma function — if a base case appears non-elementary, the question is almost certainly engineered so that a vanishing coefficient or a different base index circumvents it.
