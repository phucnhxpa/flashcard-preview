Strategic Atlas / Differential Equations / Variable Substitution Reduction
Differential Equations

Substitute or back-substitute to reduce ODE to solvable form

Family: Variable Substitution Reduction Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a second-order (or higher) ODE cannot be tackled directly by the standard auxiliary-equation method because either (a) the coefficients are variable — typically involving x, √x, x², or eˣ multiplying the derivatives — or (b) a higher derivative expression (e.g. d³y/dx³ for a Maclaurin series) still contains a lower derivative (d²y/dx²) that the original ODE can express in simpler terms. Visual cues include: factors like 4x, 2(1+2√x), x² coefficients, or being asked to evaluate dⁿy/dxⁿ at a point given only y(0) and y'(0). Equivalent phrasings: 'reduce to constant coefficients', 'eliminate the second derivative using the given equation', 'rewrite the ODE in a new variable t'.

Motivation — Why this works

Variable-coefficient ODEs admit no direct auxiliary equation, so a change of variable (x = t², x = eᵗ, etc.) is engineered so that chain-rule factors cancel the variable coefficients, leaving a constant-coefficient ODE solvable by CF + PI. The same reduction philosophy underlies back-substitution for Maclaurin series: the original ODE acts as an algebraic identity allowing higher derivatives to be expressed purely in terms of y and dy/dx, so they can be evaluated from the initial conditions alone. The underlying principle is 'use the structure of the equation itself to eliminate inconvenient terms', whether they are variable coefficients or unwanted lower-order derivatives.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Inspect the ODE: identify what blocks a direct solution — variable coefficients (x, √x) or an unevaluated higher derivative.
  2. Rule out the standard auxiliary-equation route because coefficients are non-constant; rule out separable/integrating-factor methods because the equation is second order and not first order linear.
  3. Spot the algebraic pattern: 4x = 4(√x)², so t = √x (i.e. x = t²) will turn surd factors into polynomial ones; or, if doing a Maclaurin series, notice the original ODE gives d²y/dx² as a polynomial in y and y'.
  4. Commit to the substitution because the chain-rule factors of t (or eᵗ) will exactly cancel the variable coefficients — or because back-substitution will reduce d³y/dx³ to a closed expression in y and y' only.
  5. Tactical choice: decide which direction the substitution runs (x = t², not t = x²) and convert EVERY term — LHS derivatives via chain rule AND the RHS forcing function.
  6. Execute carefully: compute dy/dx and d²y/dx² in the new variable, or rearrange the original ODE to isolate d²y/dx² before back-substituting.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Variable substitution case: set x = t² (so t = √x, dt/dx = 1/(2t)).
  2. Compute dy/dx = (dy/dt)(dt/dx) = (1/(2t)) dy/dt.
  3. Compute d²y/dx² using product rule: d²y/dx² = (1/(4t²)) d²y/dt² − (1/(4t³)) dy/dt.
  4. Substitute into the ODE, replacing x → t², √x → t, and the RHS forcing term; verify all t-dependent coefficients cancel to leave a constant-coefficient ODE in t.
  5. Back-substitution case: rearrange original ODE to isolate d²y/dx² (e.g. d²y/dx² = 2y(dy/dx) − 4y³).
  6. Differentiate the original ODE to obtain d³y/dx³ in terms of y, dy/dx, d²y/dx².
  7. Replace every d²y/dx² in this expression with the rearranged formula, expand, and collect like terms to obtain d³y/dx³ purely in y and dy/dx.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $4x\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 2(1+2\sqrt{x})\frac{dy}{dx} + y = 15x$, let $x=t^2$. Then $\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{1}{2t}\frac{dy}{dt}$ and $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=\frac{1}{4t^2}\frac{d^2y}{dt^2}-\frac{1}{4t^3}\frac{dy}{dt}$. Substituting and simplifying gives $\frac{d^2y}{dt^2}+2\frac{dy}{dt}+y=15t^2$ — a constant-coefficient ODE solvable by CF + PI.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cauchy–Euler equation x²y'' + axy' + by = f(x)
Use x = eᵗ (or t = ln x); chain rule converts xy' → dy/dt and x²y'' → d²y/dt² − dy/dt, giving constant coefficients.
Maclaurin series requiring d⁴y/dx⁴ at x = 0
Differentiate once more, then back-substitute BOTH the original ODE (for d²y/dx²) AND the previously derived d³y/dx³ — a cascading chain of substitutions.
ODE with eˣ-type variable coefficients
Try t = eˣ or t = e⁻ˣ depending on which exponentials appear; same philosophy of absorbing variable factors.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying t = √x but treating dt/dx as a constant, so squaring it to get d²y/dx² — this misses the product-rule term.
  • Reading the back-substitution problem as needing to solve the ODE rather than just simplify d³y/dx³ algebraically.
  • Mistaking a Cauchy–Euler equation for one solvable by t = √x, instead of t = ln x.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to convert the RHS forcing function (e.g. 15x → 15t²) as well as the LHS derivatives.
  • Confusing the direction of the substitution: x = t² is not the same as t = x².
  • Sign and index errors when expanding 2y(2y·y' − 4y³) = 4y²y' − 8y⁴ during back-substitution.
  • Substituting an incorrect rearrangement of the original ODE (e.g. dropping the −4y³ term).

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) scope: substitutions are always given in the question (x = t², x = eᵗ, etc.), and chain-rule conversion of d²y/dx² is expected. Maclaurin series in P3 use the back-substitution mechanic with the formula booklet's Maclaurin expansion. Avoid claiming a general theory of variable-coefficient ODEs — students should only use the specific substitution provided.
