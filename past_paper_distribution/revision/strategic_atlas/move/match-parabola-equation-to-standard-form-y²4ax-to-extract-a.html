Coordinate Geometry

Match parabola equation to standard form y²=4ax to extract a

Family: Coefficient Comparison Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a parabola is given in the form y² = kx (or x² = ky) and the question asks for focus, directrix, focal chord, focal distance, or any property derivable from the parameter a. Common phrasings: 'find the focus S', 'write down the directrix', 'show that PS = ...', or any setup mentioning the parametric points (at², 2at). The cue is a squared variable equal to a linear multiple of the other variable, with no shifts.

Motivation — Why this works

The standard form y² = 4ax encodes the entire geometry of a right-opening parabola in the single parameter a: focus at (a, 0), directrix at x = −a, and parametric points (at², 2at). Extracting a once by equating the coefficient of x to 4a unlocks every subsequent calculation without re-derivation. This is the canonical entry-point for any A-level parabola question and aligns directly with the formula book conventions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation has the shape (squared variable) = (linear multiple of other variable), with no constant or linear shifts — so it is a parabola in standard position.
  2. Rule out needing to complete the square or translate axes: there is no +c or shift, so the vertex is already at the origin.
  3. Commit to the standard catalogue form y² = 4ax because the formula book gives focus (a, 0) and directrix x = −a in terms of this single parameter a.
  4. Bridge: write the given equation directly above y² = 4ax and equate the coefficients of x to set up 4a = (given coefficient).
  5. Tactical decision: solve for a as a fraction (not a decimal) so subsequent substitutions into (a, 0), x = −a, or (at², 2at) stay exact.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the given equation alongside y² = 4ax.
  2. Equate coefficients of x: 4a = (given coefficient).
  3. Solve for a (keep as exact fraction).
  4. Read off focus S = (a, 0) and directrix x = −a.
  5. Record a for later use in parametric form (at², 2at) or focal-distance results.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $y^2 = 18x$, compare with $y^2 = 4ax$. Then $4a = 18$, so $a = \tfrac{9}{2}$. Hence focus $S = \left(\tfrac{9}{2},\,0\right)$ and directrix $x = -\tfrac{9}{2}$. Parametric points are $\left(\tfrac{9}{2}t^2,\,9t\right)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Upward-opening parabola x² = 4ay
Equate coefficient of y to 4a; focus is (0, a) and directrix is y = −a.
Left-opening parabola y² = −4ax (a > 0)
Match −4a to the (negative) coefficient; focus at (−a, 0), directrix x = a.
Equation given as y² = kx with k as a surd or fraction
Same comparison: a = k/4; keep exact form throughout.
Question requires parametric route (chord, tangent, normal)
After extracting a, substitute into (at², 2at) to parametrise general points on the curve.

Common Misreadings

  • Setting 2a = 18 instead of 4a = 18, confusing y² = 4ax with y² = 2ax.
  • Reading the coefficient as a directly, giving focus (18, 0) instead of (9/2, 0).
  • Treating x² = 18y as a right-opening parabola and placing the focus on the x-axis.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the factor of 4: the coefficient of x is 4a, not a or 2a.
  • Converting a to a decimal early and losing precision in later parts.
  • Sign error on the directrix: it is x = −a, not x = a, for y² = 4ax with a > 0.
  • Confusing focus (a, 0) with directrix x = −a when stating both.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard content in Edexcel IAL Further Pure (parabola in standard position y² = 4ax with focus (a, 0) and directrix x = −a). No translated or rotated parabolas are required at this level.
