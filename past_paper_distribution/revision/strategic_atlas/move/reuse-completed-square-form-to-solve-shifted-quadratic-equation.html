Reuse completed-square form to solve shifted quadratic equation

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Part (a) of a multi-part question asks you to express a quadratic f(x) = ax² + bx + c in completed square form p ± q(x − h)², and part (b) then asks you to solve an equation like f(x) = k or find exact roots. The structural cue is that part (a)'s output feeds part (b) directly. Also fires whenever a quadratic equation has irrational roots and a vertex form is already available or quick to derive. Phrasings include 'hence solve', 'hence find the exact values', or 'use your answer to part (a)'.

Motivation — Why this works

Once f(x) is written as p − q(x − h)², an equation f(x) = k rearranges to (x − h)² = (p − k)/q, which is solved by a single square root step yielding x = h ± √((p − k)/q). This bypasses the quadratic formula and its discriminant arithmetic, keeping surds exact and reducing sign errors. The method works because completing the square is an algebraic identity, so the two forms are equivalent and the vertex form exposes the symmetry of roots about x = h.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that part (a) has already produced a completed square form, and part (b) asks to solve a related equation — the word 'hence' or the question structure signals reuse.
  2. Rule out applying the quadratic formula to the original expanded form, since that ignores built structure and tends to produce messier surds.
  3. Commit to the vertex form because isolating (x − h)² lets the ± √ step deliver exact answers in one line.
  4. Substitute the completed square form into f(x) − k = 0 and combine the two constants before isolating the square.
  5. Track the sign of the leading coefficient carefully when dividing through, and remember to write both ± roots as the final answer.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Substitute the completed square form for f(x) into the given equation, e.g. f(x) − 7 = 0 becomes 21 − 2(x − 3)² − 7 = 0.
  2. Combine the constants outside the squared bracket: 14 − 2(x − 3)² = 0.
  3. Isolate the squared term: (x − 3)² = 7 (dividing by the coefficient of the bracket, watching its sign).
  4. Take square roots, including ±: x − 3 = ±√7.
  5. Solve for x: x = 3 ± √7, leaving the answer in exact surd form.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $f(x) = 21 - 2(x-3)^2$, solve $f(x) = 7$.\\ $21 - 2(x-3)^2 = 7 \Rightarrow 2(x-3)^2 = 14 \Rightarrow (x-3)^2 = 7$.\\ $x - 3 = \pm\sqrt{7}$, so $x = 3 \pm \sqrt{7}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Finding x-intercepts of a quadratic curve given in completed square form
Set f(x) = 0 directly and solve (x − h)² = p/q; same ± √ procedure.
Solving f(x) > k or f(x) < k inequalities after completing the square
Isolate (x − h)² ≥ or ≤ a constant, then convert to |x − h| inequality or read interval directly.
Range of a quadratic function or 'show that f(x) ≥ m'
Read the minimum/maximum value off the constant outside the bracket; no equation-solving needed.
Discriminant-style 'show roots are real/irrational' questions
Use vertex form to argue (x − h)² = positive constant has two real irrational roots without computing b² − 4ac.
Trigonometric or exponential equation reducible to a quadratic in u = sin x or u = eˣ
Complete the square in u, solve (u − h)² = c, then back-substitute to find x.

Common Misreadings

  • Ignoring part (a) and restarting from the expanded quadratic with the formula, losing the 'hence' marks.
  • Treating the completed square form as only a vertex-finding tool and not realising it solves equations directly.
  • Mis-handling the leading coefficient: forgetting to divide by −2 (or whatever q is) before square-rooting.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the ± when taking the square root, giving only one root instead of two.
  • Arithmetic slip combining the constants (e.g. writing 13 or 15 instead of 21 − 7 = 14).
  • Sign error when the squared term has a negative coefficient: dividing by −2 should flip the sign of the right-hand side.
  • Leaving the answer as a decimal when the question demands exact/surd form.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics P1 (quadratics, completing the square, surds). The same move recurs in P2/P3 disguised quadratics and in coordinate geometry vertex problems.
