Coordinate Geometry

Back-substitute into the simpler/parameter-free equation to find the missing coordinate

Family: Back Substitution Choice Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

This move fires once you have solved for one coordinate (typically x) of an intersection, tangent, or shared point, and you need the matching coordinate (typically y). The cue is having two original equations both containing y — for example a line and a curve, a line and a circle, or a line and a parabola — and needing to choose which to plug back into. Equivalent phrasings: 'which equation should I substitute x into?', 'how do I recover y now that I have x?', 'I have the roots from the eliminated quadratic — what next?'. The decision matters whenever the two equations differ in degree, contain unknown parameters, or involve fractions/surds.

Motivation — Why this works

Both original equations are satisfied at the intersection point, so either will return the correct y. However, substituting into the linear (or otherwise simpler/parameter-free) equation minimises arithmetic — typically just one multiplication and one addition — and avoids squaring fractions, taking square roots with ± ambiguity, or carrying an unresolved parameter forward. The principle is: extract the most information per algebraic step by choosing the equation with the fewest unknowns and the lowest degree. This keeps work sequential and reduces error propagation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice I have x-value(s) and need the corresponding y-value(s); two equations contain y, so I must pick one.
  2. I rule out the higher-degree equation (curve/circle/conic) because it forces me to square or extract roots, generating extra arithmetic and possible sign ambiguity.
  3. I also rule out any equation that still carries an unknown parameter (e.g. y = 4x + c with c unknown), because substituting there would not close the problem — it would leave two unknowns.
  4. I commit to the equation that is (a) parameter-free and (b) lowest-degree in y. Usually this is the line y = mx + k.
  5. Tactically, I keep fractions in a single common denominator from the start (e.g. write integers as n·d/d) to avoid mid-computation conversion errors.
  6. Optional verification: once y is found, plug (x, y) back into the OTHER equation as a sanity check if time allows.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two original equations containing y; classify each as linear/quadratic and as parameter-free/parameter-bearing.
  2. Select the equation that is linear AND fully numerical (no unresolved letters).
  3. Substitute the known x into that equation: y = m·x + k.
  4. Simplify with a common denominator if x is fractional; record y exactly.
  5. Write the coordinate pair (x, y). Optionally verify by substituting into the unused equation.

Worked Micro-Example

Line $y = 3x - 1$ meets circle at $x = 1$ and $x = -3/5$. Substitute into the line (linear, parameter-free): for $x=1$, $y = 3(1)-1 = 2 \Rightarrow (1,2)$; for $x=-3/5$, $y = 3(-3/5) - 1 = -9/5 - 5/5 = -14/5 \Rightarrow (-3/5, -14/5)$. No square roots, no ± ambiguity.

Variants — Same move, different surface

The line equation contains an unknown parameter (e.g. y = 4x + c with c not yet found) but the curve is fully specified.
Substitute into the curve instead — it is the parameter-free equation, so it closes y in one step. Solve for c afterwards.
Both equations are conics (e.g. ellipse and circle), so neither is linear.
Choose whichever avoids square roots or fractional coefficients; if tied, pick the one already in y = f(x) form.
Tangent problem where the gradient/derivative is also available.
Do NOT substitute into dy/dx — that gives a slope, not y. Always use the original curve or line equation.

Common Misreadings

  • Substituting into the harder equation 'to double-check', wasting time and introducing errors.
  • Substituting x into the eliminated quadratic (e.g. 5x² − 2x − 3 = 0), which trivially gives 0, not y.
  • Confusing the derivative dy/dx with y itself when a tangent is involved.

Pitfalls

  • Fraction arithmetic: forgetting to convert integers to a common denominator (e.g. writing −1 as −5/5 before adding to −9/5).
  • Sign errors when x is negative: e.g. evaluating 8(−1) as +8 instead of −8.
  • Choosing an equation that still contains an unknown parameter, leaving y expressed in terms of that parameter and stalling progress.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1–P4) for line–curve, line–circle, and tangent intersection problems. No formula booklet entry is required — this is a procedural choice. Avoid generalising to systems with three or more equations or to implicit curves where 'simpler' is ambiguous; stick to the two-equation case standard at A level.
