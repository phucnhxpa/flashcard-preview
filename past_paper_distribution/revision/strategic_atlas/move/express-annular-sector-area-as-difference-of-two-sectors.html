Coordinate Geometry

Express annular sector area as difference of two sectors

Family: Region Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The shaded region is bounded by two concentric circular arcs and two radial line segments — an annular sector or 'sector with a hole'. The diagram shows a common centre O, two radii (one inner, one outer) and a shared angle θ. Phrasings include 'the region between the two arcs', 'PQ extended to the outer arc', or giving a width x along the radius. No single A-level formula covers this directly, so it must be split.

Motivation — Why this works

Both the big sector (radius R₂) and the small sector (radius R₁) subtend the same angle θ at O, so each has a clean area ½r²θ. The annular sector is exactly the set difference of these two, giving A = ½θ(R₂² − R₁²). This converts a non-standard region into two standard sector-area applications and yields a quadratic in the unknown radial width.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the region has a curved inner boundary as well as a curved outer boundary — it is a sector with the central piece removed, not a plain sector.
  2. Rule out treating it as a single ½r²θ region, and rule out trapezium/triangle approximations or integration (overkill for A-level).
  3. Commit to the decomposition: big sector minus small sector, since both share the same central angle θ.
  4. Identify the two radii carefully from the diagram — typically the inner radius is given (e.g. OP = 1.2) and the outer is inner + width (e.g. OP + PQ = 1.2 + x).
  5. Substitute into A = ½θ(R₂² − R₁²), expand the square, simplify, then equate to the given area to form the equation in the unknown.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify inner radius R₁ and outer radius R₂ = R₁ + (radial width).
  2. Write A = ½θ(R₂² − R₁²).
  3. Expand (R₁ + w)² = R₁² + 2R₁w + w² so that R₂² − R₁² = w² + 2R₁w.
  4. Substitute to get A = ½θ(w² + 2R₁w).
  5. Equate to the given numerical area and clear fractions/decimals to reach a tidy quadratic in w.

Worked Micro-Example

With $\theta = 0.6$, inner radius $1.2$ and outer radius $1.2 + x$: $A = \tfrac{1}{2}(0.6)\big[(x+1.2)^2 - 1.2^2\big] = 0.3(x^2 + 2.4x) = 0.3x^2 + 0.72x$. Setting $A = 4.5$ gives $0.3x^2 + 0.72x - 4.5 = 0$, i.e. $x^2 + 2.4x - 15 = 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Perimeter of an annular sector
Use arc length s = rθ on each arc and add the two straight radial segments of length w: P = R₁θ + R₂θ + 2w.
Region between a chord and an arc (segment)
Decompose as sector minus triangle: A = ½r²θ − ½r²sinθ rather than sector minus sector.
Region bounded by two curves in polar form (FP further pure)
Use ∫½(r_outer² − r_inner²) dθ — same identity, integrated when radii vary with θ.
Annular sector with angle in degrees
Convert θ to radians first, or use A = (θ/360)·π(R₂² − R₁²).

Common Misreadings

  • Reading the radial width as the outer radius itself, so using R₂ = x instead of R₂ = x + R₁.
  • Treating the region as a full sector and applying ½r²θ once, ignoring the inner arc.
  • Trying to integrate or set up a trapezium when a clean subtraction is available.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to square both radii — writing ½θ(R₂ − R₁) instead of ½θ(R₂² − R₁²).
  • Leaving the angle in degrees when applying ½r²θ.
  • Algebra slip when expanding (R₁ + w)² — missing the cross term 2R₁w.
  • Not clearing decimals before solving, leading to arithmetic errors in the quadratic formula.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits within Edexcel IAL Pure (radian measure, sector area ½r²θ and arc length rθ); the polar-integration variant belongs to Further Pure.
