Differentiation

Differentiate repeatedly and evaluate at the expansion point to obtain Taylor/Maclaurin coefficients

Family: Systematic Differentiation Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks for a Maclaurin or Taylor expansion of a function that does NOT decompose neatly into standard series building blocks (e.g. e^x, sin x, ln(1+x), (1+x)^n). Typical cues: the function is an awkward composite like x²e^{tan x}, sec x, ln(cos x); the function is defined implicitly by an equation in x and y; the function is defined by a differential equation; or the question explicitly says 'use the definition' / 'find f(0), f'(0), f''(0), …'. Equivalently: whenever the only reliable way to extract the coefficient of xⁿ is to compute fⁿ(a) directly.

Motivation — Why this works

The Taylor series f(x) = Σ f⁽ⁿ⁾(a)(x − a)ⁿ/n! is the definition of the expansion: each coefficient is uniquely fixed by the n-th derivative value at the expansion point divided by n!. When no standard-series shortcut applies, systematic differentiation is the guaranteed route — it converts an abstract series into concrete numerical coefficients. The factorial denominators automatically absorb the combinatorial weight, so the student's only job is bookkeeping: clean derivatives and accurate substitution.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the function is not a product/composition of standard series in a way that makes binomial/exponential/trig shortcuts efficient — or the question forces the definition.
  2. Rule out: multiplying known Maclaurin series (would be ideal but doesn't apply here); substitution into a known series (no clean inner function); binomial expansion (not of the form (1+u)^n).
  3. Commit to direct differentiation because the formula f(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x−a) + f''(a)(x−a)²/2! + … gives every coefficient explicitly once the derivatives are known.
  4. Read off the required depth: 'up to and including x³' means I need f(0), f'(0), f''(0), f'''(0) — four evaluations, three differentiations.
  5. Plan the differentiation chain in advance: anticipate where product rule and chain rule will appear, and decide to simplify after each step so later derivatives don't blow up.
  6. Tactical choice: evaluate at x = a immediately after each differentiation when the algebra would otherwise compound; but keep the symbolic form of f⁽ⁿ⁾(x) when the next derivative needs it.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute f(a) by direct substitution.
  2. Differentiate to obtain f'(x); simplify; then substitute x = a to get f'(a).
  3. Differentiate f'(x) (product/chain rule as needed) to get f''(x); simplify; substitute to get f''(a).
  4. Continue to f'''(x), f⁽⁴⁾(x), … up to the required order, evaluating at x = a each time.
  5. Substitute the numerical values into the template f(a) + f'(a)(x−a) + f''(a)(x−a)²/2! + f'''(a)(x−a)³/3! + ⋯, dividing each by the correct factorial.

Worked Micro-Example

Maclaurin of f(x) = ln(cos x) up to x⁴. f(0) = 0. f'(x) = −tan x ⇒ f'(0) = 0. f''(x) = −sec²x ⇒ f''(0) = −1. f'''(x) = −2sec²x tan x ⇒ f'''(0) = 0. f⁽⁴⁾(x) = −2sec⁴x − 4sec²x tan²x ⇒ f⁽⁴⁾(0) = −2. Hence ln(cos x) ≈ −x²/2 − x⁴/12.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Taylor series about x = a ≠ 0
Identical procedure but evaluate every derivative at x = a and write powers as (x − a)ⁿ rather than xⁿ.
Function defined implicitly by an equation F(x, y) = 0
Differentiate the defining equation implicitly; at each stage substitute the known value of x and previously found y, y', y'', … to solve for the next derivative.
Function defined by a differential equation, e.g. y'' + y = sec x with given y(0), y'(0)
Differentiate the ODE itself repeatedly and substitute x = 0 to extract y''(0), y'''(0), …, bypassing the need for a closed form of y.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating an obvious product of standard series (e.g. e^x sin x) as needing this laborious method — multiplication of known Maclaurin series is far quicker.
  • Using direct differentiation when a substitution into a known series (e.g. replacing x by 2x² in the series for ln(1+x)) would have done the job in one line.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the n! denominator: writing f''(0)x² instead of f''(0)x²/2!.
  • Sign errors compounding through successive derivatives, especially with chain-rule factors of −1 (e.g. derivatives of cos, of 1/(1+x), of e^{−x}).
  • Failing to apply the product rule when the derivative becomes a product at higher orders (e.g. once tan x appears, the next derivative needs product rule).
  • Arithmetic slips evaluating trig functions at the expansion point (e.g. sin(π/4) = 1/√2, not 1/2).
  • For Taylor about x = a, accidentally substituting x = 0 instead of x = a.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) Taylor and Maclaurin series. The series formula is given in the formula booklet; students must still compute the derivatives themselves. Convergence/radius-of-convergence analysis is out of scope and should not be invoked.
