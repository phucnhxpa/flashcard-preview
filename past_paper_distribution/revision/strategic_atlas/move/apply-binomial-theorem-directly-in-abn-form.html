Binomial Expansion

Apply binomial theorem directly in (a+b)^n form

Family: Template Matching Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an expression of the form $(a+b)^n$ has a positive integer exponent $n$ and neither term inside the bracket is 1. Typical surface: $(2 + x/8)^{10}$, $(3 - 2x)^8$, $(1 + 2x)^7$ being asked for the first few terms or a specific term/coefficient. The presence of a positive integer power is the key cue — there is no convergence condition to enforce, so factoring is optional.

Motivation — Why this works

The binomial theorem $(a+b)^n = \sum_{r=0}^{n}\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r$ holds exactly for any two-term base raised to a positive integer, giving a finite expansion. Using the full $(a+b)^n$ template lets you read off $a$, $b$, $n$ directly without preliminary rearrangement. The alternative — factoring $a^n$ outside to convert to $(1+bx/a)^n$ — adds an extra step and a different coefficient inside, with no real benefit when $n$ is a positive integer.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the bracket has two distinct terms (neither is 1) and the exponent is a positive integer, so the full finite binomial expansion applies.
  2. I rule out the $(1+x)^n$ formula-book route: factoring out $2^{10}$ would work but is unnecessary since there is no convergence condition for integer $n$.
  3. I commit to the direct $(a+b)^n$ template because it lets me identify $a$, $b$, $n$ at sight with no algebraic preprocessing.
  4. Bridge: I write the general term $T_{r+1} = \binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r$ with the identified values substituted in.
  5. Tactically, I keep $a$ and $b$ as whole chunks (including any sign or fraction) and plan to substitute $r = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$ for the requested terms, simplifying powers carefully.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify $a$, $b$, and $n$ from the bracket, taking each full term inside as either $a$ or $b$.
  2. Write the general term $T_{r+1} = \binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r$.
  3. Substitute the required values of $r$ (typically $r = 0, 1, 2, 3$ for the first four terms).
  4. Evaluate each $\binom{n}{r}$, each power of $a$, and each power of $b$ separately, then multiply.
  5. Combine numerical coefficients and simplify any fractions or signs to give the term in standard form.

Worked Micro-Example

Expand the first four terms of $(2 + x/8)^{10}$ in ascending powers of $x$. With $a=2$, $b=x/8$, $n=10$: $T_{r+1}=\binom{10}{r}2^{10-r}(x/8)^r$. Then $T_1=2^{10}=1024$, $T_2=\binom{10}{1}2^9(x/8)=10\cdot 512 \cdot x/8=640x$, $T_3=\binom{10}{2}2^8(x/8)^2=45\cdot 256\cdot x^2/64=180x^2$, $T_4=\binom{10}{3}2^7(x/8)^3=120\cdot 128\cdot x^3/512=30x^3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$(3 - 2x)^8$ with a negative second term
Set $a=3$, $b=-2x$; keep the sign attached to $b$ so that $b^r$ alternates sign automatically.
$(1 + kx)^n$ with positive integer $n$
The direct template still works with $a=1$; this collapses to the simpler $(1+x)^n$ formula-book form.
$(2 + x/8)^{-3}$ or fractional/negative $n$
Direct $(a+b)^n$ no longer applies; you must factor $a^n$ outside to obtain $(1+bx/a)^n$ and use the infinite series with a convergence condition $|bx/a|<1$.
Asked for a specific term or coefficient (e.g. coefficient of $x^4$)
Use the general term and solve for the value of $r$ that produces the required power of $x$, rather than expanding sequentially.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the expansion as $(1+x)^n$ form when $n$ is a positive integer and unnecessarily factoring out $a^n$, then mishandling the resulting coefficient.
  • Reversing the roles of $a$ and $b$, which produces terms in descending rather than ascending powers of $x$ when ascending order is required.
  • Reading $b$ as just the coefficient (e.g. $1/8$) rather than the full term including $x$.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting that $b$ includes the variable, e.g. $b = x/8$ not $1/8$, so $b^r$ contains $x^r$.
  • Misapplying the exponent: $n$ acts on the whole bracket, so $a^{n-r}$ uses $n-r$, not $n$.
  • Dropping a negative sign when $b$ is negative — the sign must be raised to the $r$th power along with $b$.
  • Arithmetic slips when simplifying $2^{10-r}/8^r$ or similar; reducing powers to a common base early helps.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics: the finite binomial expansion for positive integer $n$ is core P2/C2 material, and the contrast with the infinite expansion for fractional/negative $n$ (P4) is an examinable scope boundary.
