Differential Equations

Differentiate the ODE implicitly to generate the next-order derivative

Family: Implicit Ode Differentiation Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question gives a second- (or first-) order ODE together with initial conditions and asks for a Maclaurin or Taylor expansion that requires a derivative of order higher than what the ODE explicitly provides (e.g. asks for y'''(0) or the x³/x⁴ term). Cue phrases include 'differentiate the equation with respect to x', 'find d³y/dx³', or 'expand y as a series up to and including the term in x³/x⁴'. The ODE is typically nonlinear or variable-coefficient and therefore not solvable in closed form by A-level methods. Equivalently: you have y, y', y'' in hand at x = 0 but need one more derivative to complete the series.

Motivation — Why this works

Differentiating both sides of the ODE with respect to x produces a new equation in which the highest-order derivative is one greater than before — the LHS d/dx(y'') becomes y''' immediately, while the RHS, treated implicitly with chain and product rules, stays in terms of x, y, y', y''. Since these lower-order quantities are already known (from initial conditions and the original ODE), y'''(0) can then be evaluated directly. The principle being exploited is that differentiation is a closed operation on smooth implicit relations, so each pass raises the order by one and yields exactly the next Taylor coefficient required.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the series asked for needs y^(n)(0) for some n exceeding the ODE's order; tally which derivatives at 0 are already obtainable (y(0), y'(0) from initial conditions; y''(0) by substituting x=0 into the ODE).
  2. Rule out solving the ODE explicitly — it is nonlinear / variable-coefficient, so separation, integrating factor, or auxiliary equation methods do not apply within the A-level toolkit.
  3. Rule out differentiating the partial Maclaurin series itself — that would be circular, since the coefficients are exactly what we are trying to find.
  4. Commit to differentiating the whole ODE with respect to x: this is the standard FP2/FP3 implicit technique and it is precisely what the question prompt usually instructs.
  5. Bridge to action: write the ODE in the form y'' = f(x, y, y'), then differentiate term-by-term, treating every occurrence of y as a function of x (chain rule) and every product of y-quantities with the product rule.
  6. Rearrange the resulting equation to make y''' the subject, ready for substitution of x = 0 with the now-known values of y, y', y''. If a higher-order term (x⁴ etc.) is needed, repeat the differentiation on this new equation.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Start from the ODE, e.g. y'' − 2y·y' + 4y³ = 0, with y as an implicit function of x.
  2. Apply d/dx to both sides: d/dx(y'') = y'''.
  3. Differentiate each RHS term: products like −2y·y' use the product rule giving −2(y')² − 2y·y''; powers like 4y³ use the chain rule giving 12y²·y'.
  4. Collect into a single equation, e.g. y''' − 2(y')² − 2y·y'' + 12y²·y' = 0.
  5. Make y''' the subject: y''' = 2(y')² + 2y·y'' − 12y²·y'.
  6. (If needed for y⁴(0)) differentiate this equation again, applying product/chain rules throughout.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $y'' = x y' + y^2$ with $y(0)=1,\ y'(0)=0$, differentiate both sides w.r.t. $x$: $y''' = y' + x y'' + 2 y y'$. At $x=0$: $y''(0) = 0\cdot 0 + 1^2 = 1$, then $y'''(0) = 0 + 0\cdot 1 + 2\cdot 1\cdot 0 = 0$, giving the cubic Maclaurin term $\tfrac{1}{2}x^2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Maclaurin series required up to x⁴ or higher
Differentiate the already-differentiated equation again to produce y⁴ in terms of lower derivatives; iterate as many times as the series demands.
Taylor series centred at x = a rather than x = 0
Same differentiation procedure; only the substitution point changes — evaluate every derivative at x = a using the given conditions there.
First-order ODE y' = f(x, y) with series required to x²+
One differentiation produces y'' in terms of x, y, y'; further differentiations give y''', y⁴, …

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to solve the ODE explicitly via separation of variables, integrating factor, or auxiliary equations — not viable for typical nonlinear/variable-coefficient FP2 ODEs.
  • Attempting to obtain y'''(0) by differentiating the truncated Maclaurin series itself — circular, since the series coefficients are unknown.
  • Reading 'differentiate the equation' as 'integrate the equation' and trying to recover y.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting that y, y', y'' are all functions of x — e.g. writing d/dx(y³) = 0 instead of 3y²·y'.
  • Sign or coefficient slips when applying the product rule to terms like −2y·y' (should give −2(y')² − 2y·y'').
  • Dropping an x-coefficient when differentiating variable-coefficient terms like x·y' (must give y' + x·y'').

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) Maclaurin and Taylor series work; uses only the product and chain rules from the core formula booklet plus the Maclaurin/Taylor expansion entries. Avoid generalising to power-series solutions of ODEs (Frobenius method) which is out of scope.
