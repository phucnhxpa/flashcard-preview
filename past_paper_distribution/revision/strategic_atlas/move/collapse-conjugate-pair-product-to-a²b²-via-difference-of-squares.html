Complex Numbers

Collapse conjugate pair product to a²+b² via difference of squares

Family: Conjugate Product Identity Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Two factors being multiplied are complex conjugates: $(a-bi)(a+bi)$. This fires whenever a conjugate root pair is combined back into a quadratic factor, when rationalising a complex denominator, or when computing $|z|^2 = zz^*$. Watch for matching real parts with opposite-sign imaginary parts, or explicit notation $z\cdot\bar{z}$.

Motivation — Why this works

By the difference-of-squares identity, $(a-bi)(a+bi) = a^2 - (bi)^2 = a^2 + b^2$, since $(bi)^2 = -b^2$. The result is always real and non-negative, so the move collapses a four-term FOIL expansion (with imaginary cancellation) into one real arithmetic step. It also encodes the geometric fact that $z\bar{z} = |z|^2$, linking algebraic conjugation to modulus.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the two factors share the same real part and have opposite-sign imaginary parts — that is the conjugate-pair signature.
  2. I rule out doing full FOIL: it works but invites sign errors on the cross terms and on $(bi)^2$.
  3. I commit to the identity $(a-bi)(a+bi) = a^2 + b^2$ because the result is guaranteed real and the computation reduces to two squares plus a sum.
  4. I read off $a$ and $b$ carefully: $a$ is the real part, $b$ is the real coefficient of $i$ (not $bi$ itself).
  5. I compute $a^2 + b^2$ and sanity-check that the answer is real and positive (zero only if $z=0$).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the factors as a conjugate pair: confirm matching real parts and opposite-sign imaginary parts.
  2. Read off $a$ (real part) and $b$ (real coefficient of $i$).
  3. Apply the identity $(a-bi)(a+bi) = a^2 + b^2$.
  4. Evaluate $a^2 + b^2$ as a single real number.
  5. Sanity-check: result is real and non-negative.

Worked Micro-Example

Compute $(1-2i)(1+2i)$.\ \ Recognise conjugate pair with $a=1$, $b=2$.\ \ Apply identity: $(1-2i)(1+2i) = 1^2 + 2^2 = 1+4 = 5$.\ \ Result is real and positive, as expected for $z\bar{z}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Rationalising a complex denominator, e.g. simplifying $\frac{3+i}{2-5i}$
Multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate $2+5i$; the new denominator becomes $2^2+5^2=29$ via the same identity.
Computing $|z|^2$ for $z=a+bi$
Write $|z|^2 = z\bar{z} = a^2+b^2$ directly without expansion.
Reconstructing a real quadratic from a conjugate root pair $a\pm bi$
The quadratic factor is $(x-(a+bi))(x-(a-bi)) = (x-a)^2 + b^2$, a shifted version of the same identity.
Product of conjugate pair in modulus-argument form $r e^{i\theta}\cdot r e^{-i\theta}$
Collapses to $r^2$ directly, matching $a^2+b^2$ since $r^2 = a^2+b^2$.

Common Misreadings

  • Writing $a^2 - b^2$ instead of $a^2 + b^2$ — forgetting that $(bi)^2 = -b^2$ flips the sign.
  • Mistaking factors with the same imaginary sign (e.g. $(a+bi)(a+bi)$) for a conjugate pair and applying the identity incorrectly.
  • Treating $(a+bi)(c-di)$ as a conjugate pair when $a\ne c$ or $b\ne d$.

Pitfalls

  • Taking $b$ to include the $i$ (e.g. for $3-5i$, using $b=5i$ giving $9+25i^2=-16$) instead of $b=5$ giving $34$.
  • Forgetting to square: writing $a+b$ instead of $a^2+b^2$.
  • Sign slip when the real part is negative, e.g. $(-2-3i)(-2+3i) = (-2)^2+3^2=13$, not $-4+9$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope: conjugate pairs, modulus, and rationalising complex fractions are core FP1 content; the identity $(a-bi)(a+bi)=a^2+b^2$ is standard.
