Coordinate Geometry

Substitute line equation into curve to find intersection coordinates

Family: Simultaneous Elimination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a point is defined as the intersection of a curve and a line (or another curve), and explicit coordinates are needed for downstream work such as tangent-finding, distance, or gradient evaluation. Cues include phrases like 'the line y = kx meets the curve at P', 'P is the point of intersection', or a constraint like x > 0 / x > a hinting at a sign choice. Also fires when a tangent or normal calculation requires a concrete point before applying y - y_1 = m(x - x_1).

Motivation — Why this works

Two simultaneous equations in x and y can be collapsed into a single-variable equation by substituting one into the other, producing a polynomial whose roots are the x-coordinates of intersection. This is the cleanest route to exact coordinates and exposes any sign or domain restriction as a choice between roots. The resulting coordinates plug directly into gradient formulas, distance formulas, or the point-gradient form of a tangent. Any stated constraint (e.g. x > 0) is exactly what disambiguates ± from a square root.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice P is defined implicitly as 'on both' a curve and a line — I have no coordinates yet, but I will need them.
  2. I rule out trying to proceed symbolically (e.g. differentiating implicitly and leaving P abstract), because the question asks for an explicit tangent equation.
  3. I commit to elimination: pick the simpler equation (the line) and substitute its expression into the curve, reducing to one variable.
  4. Concretely, replace y in xy = a² with kx, giving kx² = a², a single quadratic in x.
  5. Solve, then use the stated constraint (x > 0) to pick the correct root, and back-substitute into the line equation — not the curve — to avoid extra algebra.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the simpler equation (usually the line) and isolate one variable, e.g. y = kx.
  2. Substitute into the other equation, e.g. xy = a² becomes kx² = a².
  3. Solve the resulting single-variable equation: x² = a²/k, so x = ±a/√k.
  4. Apply any stated constraint (x > 0, x > a, etc.) to select the correct root.
  5. Back-substitute into the linear equation to get the matching y-coordinate.
  6. Record the point as an ordered pair, e.g. P = (a/√k, a√k).

Worked Micro-Example

Find P on xy = a² with y = kx and x > 0. Substitute: x(kx) = a² ⇒ kx² = a² ⇒ x² = a²/k. Since x > 0, x = a/√k. Then y = k·(a/√k) = a√k. So P = (a/√k, a√k).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line y = mx + c meets parabola y² = 4ax
Substitute y = mx + c into y² = 4ax to get a quadratic in x; two real roots give two intersection points (chord), discriminant = 0 gives tangency.
Line meets ellipse x²/a² + y²/b² = 1
Substitute the line into the ellipse equation to get a quadratic in x (or y); use the discriminant for tangency conditions.
Line meets circle (x−h)² + (y−k)² = r²
Substitute y from the line, expand, and solve the resulting quadratic; sign constraints select between the two intersection points.
Two curves intersect, e.g. y = x² and y = 2x + 3
Equate the two y-expressions to eliminate y, giving a single polynomial in x.
Parametric curve meets a line, e.g. (at², 2at) on y = mx + c
Substitute the parametric coordinates into the line to get a polynomial in the parameter t.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating P as a generic point and trying to differentiate before locating it, leading to expressions that cannot be evaluated.
  • Ignoring the x > 0 (or analogous) constraint and carrying both ± roots forward, producing two tangent lines instead of one.
  • Substituting the harder equation into the simpler one (e.g. x = a²/y into y = kx), creating unnecessary fractions.

Pitfalls

  • Mishandling surds: writing k·(a/√k) as a/√k or ak/√k without simplifying to a√k.
  • Forgetting to back-substitute for the second coordinate, leaving P only partially specified.
  • Dividing by a variable (e.g. by x) without checking it is non-zero, losing the x = 0 root in other contexts.
  • Sign errors when the constraint involves an inequality after squaring (e.g. y > 0 vs x > 0).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard technique across Edexcel IAL Pure (P1–P4) and Further Pure for intersections of lines with conics, rectangular hyperbolas, and parametric curves; no methods beyond A-level algebra are required.
