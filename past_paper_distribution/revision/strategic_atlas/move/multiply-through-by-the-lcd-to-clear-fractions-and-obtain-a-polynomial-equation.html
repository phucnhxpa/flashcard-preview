Algebra & Functions

Multiply through by the LCD to clear fractions and obtain a polynomial equation

Family: Fraction Clearing Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an equation set to zero contains denominators — either a variable in the denominator (e.g. a k/x term mixed with polynomial terms) or numerical fractional coefficients (e.g. (83/4)x). Visual cues: a single fraction sitting next to non-fractional terms, multiple terms sharing a common denominator, or coefficients written as a/b that would force nested fractions inside the quadratic formula. In short: if you cannot directly factorise, complete the square, or apply the quadratic/cubic root tools because of denominators, this move clears the obstacle.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying every term by the lowest common denominator (LCD) preserves the solution set (provided the LCD is non-zero) and converts a rational equation into an honest polynomial equation with integer or polynomial coefficients. This unlocks the standard toolkit — factor theorem, quadratic formula, factorisation by inspection — which is designed for polynomials, not rational expressions. The underlying principle is that multiplying both sides of an equation by a non-zero quantity yields an equivalent equation; the only caveat is to track values that make the LCD zero, since multiplying by them can introduce spurious roots.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the obstruction: there is at least one denominator in the equation (a 1/x term, or coefficients like 83/4) that prevents clean use of factorisation or the quadratic formula.
  2. Rule out alternatives: trying to apply the quadratic formula directly would create nested fractions under the radical; trying to factor would leave a fractional/rational expression that has no integer-root structure.
  3. Commit to clearing fractions because polynomial form is what every standard solution method (factor theorem, discriminant, completing the square) expects.
  4. Identify the LCD — for variable denominators it is the variable (or product of distinct linear factors); for numerical fractions it is the LCM of the denominators that actually appear in the equation (not, say, denominators of a parameter introduced earlier).
  5. Multiply EVERY term — including constants and any term already free of fractions — by the LCD, then collect into standard descending-powers form a_n x^n + ... + a_0 = 0.
  6. Sanity-check: confirm the LCD is non-zero in the problem's domain (e.g. x = 0 is not a solution of the new polynomial), and check that no common factor remains across all coefficients before proceeding.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify all denominators present and form the LCD (e.g. LCD = x for a 18/x term; LCD = 4 for (83/4)x and 115/4).
  2. Multiply each term of the equation by the LCD, distributing carefully across the constant term too.
  3. Simplify each product: k/x · x = k, and (a/b)·b = a; non-fractional terms gain a factor of the LCD.
  4. Rearrange into standard polynomial form, e.g. x^3 + 2x^2 - 13x - 18 = 0 or 12x^2 - 83x + 115 = 0.
  5. Check that the leading and constant coefficients are non-zero/sensible, then proceed with factor theorem or quadratic formula.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\frac{18}{x} + 13 - 2x - x^2 = 0$. Multiply every term by $x$: $18 + 13x - 2x^2 - x^3 = 0$. Rearrange: $x^3 + 2x^2 - 13x - 18 = 0$. Now the factor theorem applies — testing $x=-2$ gives $0$, so $(x+2)$ is a factor, and the cubic factorises cleanly.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Numerical fractional coefficients only (e.g. $3x^2 - \tfrac{83}{4}x + \tfrac{115}{4} = 0$)
Multiply by the LCM of the numerical denominators (here 4) to obtain integer coefficients $12x^2 - 83x + 115 = 0$ before applying the quadratic formula.
Variable in denominator (e.g. $\tfrac{k}{x}$ term)
Multiply by $x$ — raises the polynomial degree by one; verify $x=0$ is not a (spurious) root by checking the new constant term is non-zero.
Two distinct linear denominators (e.g. $\tfrac{1}{x-1} + \tfrac{1}{x+2} = \ldots$)
Multiply by $(x-1)(x+2)$; afterwards exclude $x=1$ and $x=-2$ from the candidate solutions.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating fractional coefficients as a sign that the quadratic formula must be used immediately, leading to messy surds inside surds.
  • Confusing the denominator of an external parameter (e.g. k = 59/8) with denominators actually appearing in the equation, and multiplying by the wrong LCD.
  • Trying to factor a rational expression directly without first putting it over a common denominator.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to multiply EVERY term — constants and non-fractional terms are easy to skip.
  • Introducing spurious roots when the LCD contains a variable: always check that values making the LCD zero are excluded from the final solution set.
  • Choosing an LCD that is too large (e.g. 8 instead of 4) — still gives correct roots but creates avoidable common factors and arithmetic burden.
  • Sign errors when distributing the LCD across a term that is already negative.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics scope (P1–P4): no formula booklet entry is needed, just the principle that multiplying both sides of an equation by a non-zero quantity preserves equivalence. Avoid generalising to multiplying by expressions that may equal zero without explicitly checking — this is where 'extraneous roots' appear, a topic students often mishandle.
