Substitute to bridge a disguised quadratic into standard form

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 9 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever the equation (or system) contains exactly two 'powers' of a single repeated sub-expression — typically degree 2 and degree 1 in some f(x) — plus a constant. Concrete cues: (i) a system with one linear equation x±y = S and one product/quadratic equation xy = P or x²+y² = R; (ii) an exponential equation containing both e^{2kx} and e^{kx}, or 2^{2x} and 2^x; (iii) a trigonometric equation in sin²x and sinx; (iv) any equation of the form A·f(x)² + B·f(x) + C = 0. Phrased differently: 'I see the same chunk appearing squared and unsquared' or 'I have a linear plus a non-linear constraint'.

Motivation — Why this works

The move works because a single substitution u = f(x) (or y = S − x in the system case) collapses two variables/expressions into one and converts an unfamiliar transcendental, multi-variable, or compound equation into a standard quadratic au² + bu + c = 0, which we can always solve via factorisation or the quadratic formula. The underlying principle is that the equation is structurally a quadratic 'in disguise' — only the alphabet has changed. Substitution is reversible: each valid u maps back to x via the inverse of f (logarithm, arcsin, etc.), with domain constraints (e.g. u = 2^x > 0) enforcing validity.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the equation contains a repeated chunk — either an algebraic expression like (2^x), (e^{0.3T}), sin x, or a second variable y appearing in both a linear and a product equation.
  2. Rule out direct techniques: taking logs fails because logs don't split sums; elimination by addition/subtraction fails when one equation is additive and the other multiplicative; squaring the linear equation works but is longer.
  3. Commit to substitution because the structure A·f² + B·f + C = 0 is a quadratic template — the most familiar equation type in the syllabus, with guaranteed solution methods.
  4. Choose the cleanest bridge: in a linear+product system, rearrange the linear equation for the variable that avoids fractions (y = S − x rather than y = P/x); in an exponential equation, set u equal to the lowest-power exponential so the squared term becomes u².
  5. Record any constraints on the new variable (e.g. u = 2^x > 0, u = e^{kx} > 0) so spurious roots can be discarded after solving.
  6. Solve the quadratic, then back-substitute through the inverse map (x = log_b(u), x = S − y, T = ln(u)/k) to recover the original unknown.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the repeated sub-expression f(x) (or, in a system, the variable to be eliminated).
  2. Define u = f(x) and rewrite every occurrence: f(x)² becomes u², f(x) becomes u; in a system, replace y by S − x throughout the non-linear equation.
  3. Expand and collect to obtain the standard form au² + bu + c = 0 (clear any common factor).
  4. Solve by factorisation or the quadratic formula u = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac))/(2a).
  5. Apply the constraint on u (e.g. u > 0 for exponentials) to discard invalid roots.
  6. Back-substitute: x = f⁻¹(u) (e.g. x = log_b u, T = ln(u)/k, or pair (x, S − x) for systems).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $4(2^x)^2 - 33(2^x) + 8 = 0$. Let $p = 2^x$ (so $p>0$). The equation becomes $4p^2 - 33p + 8 = 0$, which factorises as $(4p-1)(p-8)=0$, giving $p = \tfrac{1}{4}$ or $p = 8$. Back-substituting: $2^x = \tfrac{1}{4} \Rightarrow x = -2$; $2^x = 8 \Rightarrow x = 3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Linear + product system, e.g. x + y = 175, xy = 7350
Rearrange the linear equation as y = 175 − x and substitute into the product to get x² − 175x + 7350 = 0.
Exponential 'quadratic in disguise', e.g. 8(2 + e^{0.3T})² = 360 e^{0.3T}
Let u = e^{0.3T}, expand to get a quadratic in u, then recover T = ln(u)/0.3.
Trigonometric quadratic, e.g. 2sin²x − 5sin x + 2 = 0
Let u = sin x, solve the quadratic, then enforce −1 ≤ u ≤ 1 before taking arcsin.
Circle and line, e.g. x² + y² = r² with y = mx + c
Substitute the linear expression for y into the circle equation to obtain a quadratic in x.
Sum and product given (a + b = S, ab = P)
a and b are directly the roots of t² − St + P = 0 — no substitution needed, just read off.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to take logs of an exponential equation that contains a sum — log(A + B) does not simplify, so the substitution route is the only productive one.
  • Choosing the messier rearrangement in a linear+product system (e.g. y = P/x) and introducing fractions when y = S − x was available.
  • Attempting elimination by adding/subtracting equations of different algebraic types (linear vs product), which cannot cancel anything.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when moving terms to obtain standard form — e.g. writing x² − 175x − 7350 = 0 instead of x² − 175x + 7350 = 0.
  • Forgetting the cross-term when expanding a square such as (2 + u)² = 4 + 4u + u².
  • Forgetting the positivity constraint u > 0 for u = a^x and accepting a negative root as valid.
  • Failing to back-substitute, leaving the answer in terms of u rather than the original variable x or T.
  • Dividing through by a common factor incorrectly when simplifying the resulting quadratic.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics: simultaneous equations (P1), exponentials/logarithms (P2), and the quadratic formula (in the formula booklet). Do not generalise to higher-degree disguised polynomials (e.g. quartics in u) unless they factorise within syllabus methods, and do not invoke complex roots — at IAL level discard non-real outcomes after applying domain constraints.
