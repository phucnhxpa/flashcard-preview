Sequences & Series

Bridge new sum to earlier result via additive decomposition of summand

Family: Algebraic Rewriting Bridge Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires on a 'Hence' (or 'Hence, or otherwise') instruction asking for $\sum_{r=1}^{n} f(r)$ where part (a) established a closely related sum such as $\sum r(r+1)$ or $\sum r(r-1)$. The new summand $f(r)$ differs from the established one by a low-order standard sum (typically $\sum r$ or a constant times $n$). Cue words: 'hence', 'use the result of part (a)', or a part (b) immediately following a proved closed form. The discrepancy between expanded forms of $f(r)$ and the known summand is itself a standard summable expression.

Motivation — Why this works

Writing $r(r+2) = r(r+1) + r$ splits the new summand into the part (a) summand plus a standard sum, both with known closed forms in the formula book. This honours the 'hence' instruction by genuinely using the previous result, whereas expanding to $r^2 + 2r$ would force $\sum r^2$ which may not have been established or may not count as 'hence'. The underlying principle is linearity of $\sum$: any additive decomposition of the summand transfers directly to a decomposition of the sum.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the 'hence': part (a)'s closed form for $\sum r(r+1)$ must be reused, not bypassed.
  2. Rule out brute expansion $r(r+2)=r^2+2r$ followed by standard formulae — this ignores 'hence' and risks losing method marks.
  3. Commit to writing $r(r+2)$ as $r(r+1)$ plus a correction term: compute $r(r+2) - r(r+1) = r$, so $r(r+2) = r(r+1) + r$.
  4. Apply linearity: $\sum r(r+2) = \sum r(r+1) + \sum r$, substituting the part (a) result and the standard $\tfrac{1}{2}n(n+1)$.
  5. Factor out the common $n(n+1)$ early to keep the algebra clean, then combine fractions over denominator 6 to match the typical requested form $\tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)(an+b)$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute the difference $f(r) - g(r)$ where $g(r)$ is the part (a) summand; here $r(r+2)-r(r+1)=r$.
  2. Rewrite $f(r) = g(r) + (\text{simple correction})$, e.g. $r(r+2) = r(r+1) + r$.
  3. Apply $\sum_{r=1}^{n}$ termwise using linearity and quote part (a)'s closed form.
  4. Substitute the standard sum $\sum_{r=1}^{n} r = \tfrac{1}{2}n(n+1)$.
  5. Factor out $n(n+1)$, combine the bracketed fractions over a common denominator, and simplify to the requested factored form.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\sum_{r=1}^{n} r(r+1) = \tfrac{1}{3}n(n+1)(n+2)$, find $\sum_{r=1}^{n} r(r+2)$. Write $r(r+2) = r(r+1) + r$. Then $\sum_{r=1}^{n} r(r+2) = \tfrac{1}{3}n(n+1)(n+2) + \tfrac{1}{2}n(n+1) = \tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)\bigl[2(n+2)+3\bigr] = \tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)(2n+7)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Part (a) gave $\sum r(r+1)$; part (b) asks for $\sum r(r+3)$.
Decompose $r(r+3) = r(r+1) + 2r$, then add $2\cdot\tfrac{1}{2}n(n+1) = n(n+1)$.
Part (a) gave $\sum r(r-1)$; part (b) asks for $\sum r^2$.
Use $r^2 = r(r-1) + r$, then $\sum r^2 = \tfrac{1}{3}n(n-1)(n+1) + \tfrac{1}{2}n(n+1)$.
Part (a) gave $\sum r(r+1)(r+2)$; part (b) asks for $\sum r^2(r+1)$.
Write $r^2(r+1) = r(r+1)(r+2) - 2r(r+1)$, reusing part (a) plus the earlier cubic-bridge sum.
Lower limit shifted, e.g. $\sum_{r=2}^{n}$.
Apply the same decomposition then subtract the $r=1$ term from each known closed form.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'hence' as 'or otherwise' and computing $\sum r^2 + 2\sum r$ from the formula book — loses the bridge mark.
  • Attempting $r(r+2) = (r+1)^2 - 1$, which fails to involve $r(r+1)$ and so cannot invoke part (a).
  • Mistaking the correction term: writing $r(r+2) = r(r+1) + 2$ from a sign/coefficient slip.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic errors combining $\tfrac{n+2}{3} + \tfrac{1}{2}$ over the common denominator 6.
  • Failing to factor out $n(n+1)$ before combining, leading to an unwieldy cubic that is hard to match to the printed answer form.
  • Forgetting to state the use of part (a) explicitly — examiners often want the citation for the method mark.
  • Not verifying with $n=1$ or $n=2$ that the final closed form reproduces the first one or two partial sums.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP1) summation of series: uses only $\sum r$, $\sum r^2$, $\sum r^3$ standard results and linearity of $\sum$, with 'hence' bridging between consecutive parts of a structured question.
