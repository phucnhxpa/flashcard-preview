Divide equation through by GCD of coefficients before factorising

Family: Coefficient Reduction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a polynomial equation (most often a quadratic) has been collected into standard form $ax^2+bx+c=0$ and every coefficient — including the constant — shares a common integer factor greater than 1. Typical cues: all coefficients even, all divisible by 3, or all ending in 0/5. Recognised just before choosing a factorisation method, completing the square, or applying the quadratic formula. Equivalent phrasings: 'simplify the equation first', 'cancel a common factor from both sides', 'reduce to lowest terms'.

Motivation — Why this works

Dividing an equation by a non-zero constant preserves its solution set, since $kP(x)=0 \iff P(x)=0$ for $k\neq 0$. Smaller coefficients shrink the product $ac$ that drives factor-pair searches, and shrink the discriminant arithmetic if the quadratic formula is used. This reduces both cognitive load and the chance of sign or arithmetic slips. It is a pure simplification with no trade-off — the roots are unchanged.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that every coefficient in the collected equation shares an obvious common factor (e.g. all even).
  2. Rules out diving straight into factorisation or the quadratic formula with the large numbers, since the factor-pair search for $ac$ would be needlessly long.
  3. Commits to dividing through because solutions of $P(x)=0$ are identical to those of $\tfrac{1}{k}P(x)=0$, so nothing is lost.
  4. Computes the GCD of $|a|,|b|,|c|$ and divides every term — left side and right side — by it.
  5. Checks the simplified quadratic still has integer coefficients and proceeds with factorisation or the formula on the smaller numbers.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the equation in standard form $ax^2+bx+c=0$.
  2. Compute $d=\gcd(|a|,|b|,|c|)$.
  3. Divide every term by $d$: $\tfrac{a}{d}x^2+\tfrac{b}{d}x+\tfrac{c}{d}=0$.
  4. Confirm the right-hand side $0/d=0$ is unchanged.
  5. Continue with factorisation, completing the square, or the quadratic formula on the reduced equation.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $10x^2-4x-6=0$.\\ All coefficients share factor 2, so divide through: $5x^2-2x-3=0$.\\ Now $ac=-15$; the pair $(-5,3)$ works, giving $5x^2-5x+3x-3=0 \Rightarrow (5x+3)(x-1)=0$.\\ Hence $x=1$ or $x=-\tfrac{3}{5}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cubic or higher polynomial equation set to zero, e.g. $6x^3+9x^2-3x=0$
Divide through by $\gcd=3$ to obtain $2x^3+3x^2-x=0$, then factor out $x$ and continue.
Quadratic with fractional coefficients, e.g. $\tfrac{1}{2}x^2+\tfrac{3}{4}x-\tfrac{1}{4}=0$
Multiply (rather than divide) by the LCM of denominators (here 4) to clear fractions: $2x^2+3x-1=0$.
Inequality such as $4x^2-8x-12<0$
Divide by a positive common factor (here 4) to get $x^2-2x-3<0$; the inequality direction is preserved because the divisor is positive.
Linear equation $15x-25=10$
Divide every term by 5 to get $3x-5=2$ before solving.
Negative leading coefficient, e.g. $-4x^2+8x-12=0$
Divide by $-4$ to obtain $x^2-2x+3=0$, simultaneously simplifying and making the leading coefficient $+1$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the move as 'cancelling $x$' when the constant term is non-zero — confusing dividing the equation by a constant with dividing by a variable.
  • Believing the simplification changes the roots, and so avoiding it for fear of losing solutions.
  • Thinking a common factor exists when one coefficient is actually coprime to the others (e.g. assuming $6x^2+4x+3$ is divisible by 2).

Pitfalls

  • Dividing only some terms (e.g. forgetting the constant) and producing a wrong quadratic.
  • Failing to divide the right-hand side when it is non-zero, e.g. reducing $4x^2+8x=12$ to $x^2+2x=12$ instead of $x^2+2x=3$.
  • Dividing by a non-divisor and silently introducing fractions, defeating the purpose of the move.
  • When dividing an inequality by a negative common factor, forgetting to reverse the inequality sign.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely standard Edexcel IAL Pure 1 technique used throughout quadratic, cubic, and inequality work; no content beyond the specification is invoked.
