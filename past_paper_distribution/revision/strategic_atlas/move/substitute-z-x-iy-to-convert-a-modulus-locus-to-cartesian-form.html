Complex Numbers

Substitute z = x + iy to convert a modulus locus to Cartesian form

Family: Cartesian Conversion Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A locus condition expressed through moduli of linear expressions in z, such as |z - a| = k|z - b|, |z - a| = |z - b|, or |z - a| = r. The question typically asks to 'show that the locus is a circle/line' or to find its centre and radius in Cartesian form. The modulus bars obscure the geometry, so a substitution is needed to expose (x, y). Phrases like 'find the Cartesian equation of the locus' or 'show that L is a circle' are direct cues.

Motivation — Why this works

Writing z = x + iy turns |z - (p + qi)|² into the real expression (x - p)² + (y - q)², the squared distance from (x, y) to (p, q). Squaring both sides of a modulus equation eliminates the square roots and yields a polynomial equation in x and y. The result is either linear (perpendicular bisector) or quadratic with equal x² and y² coefficients (a circle), which is then recognised by completing the square. This is the standard algebraic route Edexcel mark schemes follow.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the locus is given by an equation in moduli of linear expressions in z, so direct algebra in z stalls.
  2. Rule out a purely geometric Apollonius/perpendicular-bisector argument because the question demands a Cartesian equation derivation.
  3. Commit to z = x + iy because each |z - a| then becomes √((x - Re a)² + (y - Im a)²), a familiar real object.
  4. Bridge: square both sides immediately to clear the square roots and obtain a polynomial relation in x and y.
  5. Tactically, group x² and y² terms, divide through by their common coefficient, and complete the square to read off centre and radius.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Set z = x + iy and write z - a = (x - Re a) + i(y - Im a) for each modulus.
  2. Replace each |z - a|² by (x - Re a)² + (y - Im a)².
  3. Square both sides of the given modulus equation to remove square roots.
  4. Expand brackets and collect like terms; if it is a circle, the x² and y² coefficients will match.
  5. Divide through to make the x² and y² coefficients equal to 1, then complete the square in x and in y to identify centre and radius.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the locus given by $|z - 3| = 2|z|$. Let $z = x + iy$: $(x-3)^2 + y^2 = 4(x^2 + y^2)$. Expanding: $x^2 - 6x + 9 + y^2 = 4x^2 + 4y^2$, so $3x^2 + 6x + 3y^2 = 9$, i.e. $x^2 + 2x + y^2 = 3$. Completing the square gives $(x+1)^2 + y^2 = 4$, a circle with centre $(-1, 0)$ and radius $2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Perpendicular bisector |z - a| = |z - b|
After squaring, the x² and y² terms cancel, leaving a linear equation — the perpendicular bisector of the segment joining a and b.
Single circle |z - a| = r
One application of |z - a|² = (x - Re a)² + (y - Im a)² gives the circle directly without squaring a ratio.
Argument locus arg((z - a)/(z - b)) = θ
Use z = x + iy, compute the quotient, then set Im/Re equal to tan θ to obtain the Cartesian equation (an arc of a circle or a half-line).
Half-plane inequality |z - a| < k|z - b|
Same substitution and squaring, but the final Cartesian relation is an inequality describing the interior/exterior of the circle.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating |z - a| as if it equals z - a and trying to manipulate algebraically without going to Cartesian form.
  • Attempting to 'divide through by |z|' to reduce to a single modulus, which does not simplify the problem.
  • Confusing |z - a| = k|z - b| with a perpendicular bisector when k ≠ 1 — it is a circle (Apollonius), not a line.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to square the constant factor: |z - 3| = 2|z| squares to 4(x² + y²) on the RHS, not 2(x² + y²).
  • Sign errors when expanding (x - p)² or (y - q)².
  • Failing to divide through so that x² and y² have coefficient 1 before completing the square, giving a wrong centre or radius.
  • Dropping the y² term on one side when the moduli have purely real shifts.

A-level Scope Notes

This is core Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) loci-of-complex-numbers material; only standard Cartesian circles, lines, and half-lines arise, with no need for Möbius transformations or higher-degree curves.
