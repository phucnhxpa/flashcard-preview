Differentiation

Rearrange implicit product to explicit y = f(x) before differentiating

Family: Explicit Rearrangement Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a curve is given in implicit product (or quotient) form such as $xy = c$, $xy = a^2$, or $x^2 y = k$, and a derivative or gradient at a point is required. The cue is that the equation can be solved for $y$ in one clean algebraic step, yielding a power of $x$. Particularly attractive when the resulting $y = f(x)$ reduces to a single power-rule differentiation. Alternative phrasings: 'isolate $y$ first', 'avoid implicit differentiation when unnecessary'.

Motivation — Why this works

Once $y$ is expressed as $a^2 x^{-1}$ (or similar), differentiation collapses to a one-line power-rule application, $dy/dx = -a^2 x^{-2}$. Implicit differentiation of $xy = a^2$ via the product rule yields $y + x\,dy/dx = 0$, requiring an extra rearrangement step and a substitution of $y$ in terms of $x$. The two routes are mathematically equivalent, but explicit rearrangement removes a layer of bookkeeping and reduces the chance of sign or substitution errors.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the curve is given as a product $xy = a^2$ — a form that can be solved for $y$ trivially.
  2. I rule out implicit differentiation: it works, but it leaves $dy/dx$ entangled with $y$, forcing a back-substitution.
  3. I commit to the explicit route because $y = a^2 x^{-1}$ is a single power, ideal for the power rule.
  4. I rewrite with a negative exponent ($y = a^2 x^{-1}$) so differentiation is mechanical rather than quotient-rule.
  5. After differentiating, I substitute the point's $x$-coordinate carefully, squaring denominators with attention to surds.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Solve the implicit equation for $y$: $xy = a^2 \Rightarrow y = a^2 x^{-1}$.
  2. Differentiate using the power rule: $dy/dx = -a^2 x^{-2} = -a^2/x^2$.
  3. Substitute the given $x$-coordinate to obtain the gradient at the point.
  4. Simplify, taking care with squared surds in the denominator.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the gradient of $xy = 4$ at $x = 2$. Rearrange: $y = 4x^{-1}$. Differentiate: $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = -4x^{-2} = -\dfrac{4}{x^2}$. At $x = 2$: $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = -\dfrac{4}{4} = -1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Rectangular hyperbola $xy = c^2$ in coordinate geometry questions
Same rearrangement $y = c^2/x$; gradient at $(ct, c/t)$ is $-1/t^2$.
Curves of the form $x^n y = k$
Solve $y = k x^{-n}$, then $dy/dx = -nk x^{-n-1}$ in one step.
Parabola $y^2 = 4ax$
Explicit rearrangement $y = 2\sqrt{ax}$ is possible but introduces a square root; implicit differentiation $2y\,dy/dx = 4a$ is usually cleaner here.
Parametric form $(ct, c/t)$ of $xy = c^2$
Use $dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) = (-c/t^2)/c = -1/t^2$ when parameters are supplied.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $xy = a^2$ as a product of two constants and concluding $dy/dx = 0$.
  • Trying to apply the product rule directly to $xy$ as if differentiating a function of one variable, without recognising $y$ depends on $x$.
  • Assuming implicit differentiation is mandatory whenever the equation 'looks implicit', missing the trivial rearrangement.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error: writing $dy/dx = +a^2 x^{-2}$ instead of $-a^2 x^{-2}$.
  • Mis-squaring a surd denominator: $(a/\sqrt{k})^2 = a^2/k$, not $a^2/\sqrt{k}$.
  • Forgetting to convert $1/x$ to $x^{-1}$ before applying the power rule, leading to quotient-rule slips.
  • Substituting the $y$-coordinate instead of the $x$-coordinate into $-a^2/x^2$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 differentiation and Further Pure coordinate geometry of the rectangular hyperbola; uses only the power rule and basic surd manipulation.
