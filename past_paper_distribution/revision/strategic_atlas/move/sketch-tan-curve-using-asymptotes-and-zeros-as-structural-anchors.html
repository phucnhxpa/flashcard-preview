Trigonometry

Sketch tan curve using asymptotes and zeros as structural anchors

Family: Standard Curve Recall Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question asks for a sketch of y = tan θ (or a simple transformation of it) over an interval such as [0°, 360°] or [−180°, 180°]. Cues include 'sketch y = tan θ', 'on the same axes sketch...', or a follow-up part requiring intersections with tan. Unlike sine/cosine prompts, no amplitude is given because tan is unbounded, signalling that asymptotes — not turning points — drive the shape.

Motivation — Why this works

The tangent function is unbounded and has no stationary points, so its graph is fully characterised by three structural features: period 180°, zeros at multiples of 180°, and vertical asymptotes at odd multiples of 90°. Within each period tan rises monotonically from −∞ to +∞, so once the asymptotes and zeros are placed the shape is forced. Anchoring on these features avoids the temptation to plot points and guarantees the correct branch behaviour.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the function is tan θ, so the graph is unbounded and amplitude language does not apply.
  2. Rules out a sine/cosine-style wave through max/min points, since tan has no turning points.
  3. Commits to an asymptote-and-zero framework: locate asymptotes at odd multiples of 90°, zeros at multiples of 180°.
  4. Bridges to drawing by first ruling dashed vertical asymptotes, then marking zeros on the θ-axis.
  5. Threads each branch as a strictly increasing curve from −∞ up to +∞ between consecutive asymptotes, passing through the zero in the middle of that branch.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify period 180° and count how many periods fit in the required domain.
  2. Draw dashed vertical asymptotes at θ = 90° and θ = 270° (for [0°, 360°]).
  3. Mark zeros at θ = 0°, 180°, 360° on the θ-axis.
  4. In (0°, 90°): curve rises from origin to +∞ approaching the asymptote.
  5. In (90°, 270°): curve comes up from −∞, passes through (180°, 0), continues to +∞.
  6. In (270°, 360°): curve comes up from −∞, rises to (360°, 0).
  7. Label the curve y = tan θ and label the asymptotes with their θ-values.

Worked Micro-Example

Sketch $y = \tan\theta$ for $0° \le \theta \le 360°$. Place dashed asymptotes at $\theta = 90°$ and $\theta = 270°$. Mark zeros at $0°, 180°, 360°$. Draw three increasing branches: from $(0°,0)$ up to $+\infty$ near $90°$; from $-\infty$ near $90°$ through $(180°,0)$ up to $+\infty$ near $270°$; from $-\infty$ near $270°$ up to $(360°,0)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

y = tan(2θ) on [0°, 360°]
Period halves to 90°; asymptotes at θ = 45°, 135°, 225°, 315°; zeros at 0°, 90°, 180°, 270°, 360°. Same anchoring strategy with doubled frequency.
y = tan(θ − 45°)
Shift the entire asymptote/zero lattice right by 45°: asymptotes at 135°, 315°; zeros at 45°, 225°, 405°.
y = tan θ on [−180°, 180°]
Asymptotes at θ = ±90°; zeros at −180°, 0°, 180°; same monotonic-increasing branch shape.
y = cot θ
Complementary pattern: asymptotes at multiples of 180°, zeros at odd multiples of 90°, branches strictly decreasing.
y = a tan θ (a > 0)
Asymptotes and zeros unchanged; vertical scaling steepens or flattens each branch but does not alter shape qualitatively.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating tan like sin/cos and looking for a maximum value or amplitude.
  • Reading 'period' as 360° instead of 180° and drawing only one full shape across [0°, 360°].
  • Confusing the locations of asymptotes and zeros (placing asymptotes at 0°, 180° instead of 90°, 270°).

Pitfalls

  • Drawing a branch that decreases between asymptotes instead of strictly increasing.
  • Omitting an asymptote (commonly at 270°) and joining the curve continuously through it.
  • Letting the curve cross or touch the asymptote rather than approaching it.
  • Forgetting to use dashed lines for the asymptotes, so the examiner cannot tell they were identified.
  • Failing to mark the zeros at 0°, 180°, 360° explicitly on the axis.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P2/P3 trigonometry): sketching y = tan θ and simple transformations on a stated interval, using period, zeros and asymptotes is standard examinable content.
