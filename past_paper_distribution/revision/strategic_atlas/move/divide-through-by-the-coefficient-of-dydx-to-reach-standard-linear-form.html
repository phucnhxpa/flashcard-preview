Differential Equations

Divide through by the coefficient of dy/dx to reach standard linear form

Family: Standard Form Reduction Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

A first-order ODE in which both y and dy/dx appear linearly (no y², no sin y, no (dy/dx)², no products like y·dy/dx), but the equation is not yet in the canonical form dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x). Typical surface cues: a coefficient such as a(x) multiplying dy/dx (e.g. x dy/dx + 2y = x³), terms scattered on both sides of the equals sign, or the equation presented in differential form M dx + N dy = 0. Equivalently, you see that an integrating-factor approach is intended but P(x) cannot yet be read off cleanly.

Motivation — Why this works

The integrating factor formula μ = e^{∫P(x) dx} requires P(x) to be the coefficient of y when the coefficient of dy/dx is exactly 1. Normalising the leading coefficient is therefore a non-negotiable algebraic gateway: any error here corrupts μ and hence the entire solution. The principle is simply that multiplying an ODE through by a non-zero function gives an equivalent ODE, so we exploit this freedom to reach the form in which the LHS will become an exact derivative d/dx(μy) once μ is chosen.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that y and dy/dx both appear to the first power, with coefficients that may depend on x — this signals a linear first-order ODE.
  2. Rule out separation of variables: the y-term is added to (not factorisable from) x-dependent terms, so it cannot be cast as f(y) dy = g(x) dx.
  3. Rule out homogeneous-type substitutions (v = y/x) and Bernoulli substitutions (v = y^{1−n}) because there is no y^n with n ≠ 0,1 present.
  4. Commit to the integrating factor method, which demands the canonical form dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x) so that P(x) can be read off unambiguously.
  5. Bridge to action: identify the coefficient a(x) currently multiplying dy/dx, and divide every single term in the equation (including the RHS) by a(x).
  6. Tactical check after dividing: read off P(x) as the new coefficient of y on the LHS and Q(x) as everything on the RHS, watching the sign carefully if the y-term had to be moved across the equals sign.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rearrange so all dy/dx and y terms are on the LHS and the pure-x term is on the RHS: a(x) dy/dx + b(x) y = c(x).
  2. Divide every term by a(x): dy/dx + (b(x)/a(x)) y = c(x)/a(x).
  3. Read off P(x) = b(x)/a(x) as the coefficient of y.
  4. Read off Q(x) = c(x)/a(x) as the RHS.
  5. Sanity check: the coefficient of dy/dx is now exactly 1, with no hidden factor.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $x\dfrac{dy}{dx} + 2y = x^3$. Divide through by $x$: $\dfrac{dy}{dx} + \dfrac{2}{x}y = x^2$. Hence $P(x) = \dfrac{2}{x}$ and $Q(x) = x^2$, so the integrating factor will be $\mu = e^{\int (2/x)\,dx} = e^{2\ln|x|} = x^2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

ODE given as f(x) dy/dx + g(x) y = h(x) with explicit leading coefficient
Divide every term by f(x); P(x) = g(x)/f(x), Q(x) = h(x)/f(x).
ODE given in differential form M(x,y) dx + N(x) dy = 0
Rearrange to dy/dx = −M/N, then bring the y-dependent part of M/N to the LHS to read off P(x).
ODE most naturally linear in x as a function of y, e.g. dx/dy + P(y)x = Q(y)
Same logic with roles of x and y swapped; integrating factor is e^{∫P(y) dy}.
dy/dx appears on the RHS or terms are split across the equals sign
Collect all dy/dx and y terms on the LHS first, then divide by the coefficient of dy/dx.

Common Misreadings

  • Misidentifying a Bernoulli equation (containing y^n with n ≠ 0,1) as a standard linear ODE — Bernoulli requires the substitution v = y^{1−n} before this move applies.
  • Mistaking a linear ODE for a separable one because both x and y are visible, then wasting time trying to separate.
  • Reading P(x) with the wrong sign, e.g. interpreting dy/dx − 3y = … as having P(x) = 3 instead of P(x) = −3.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to divide the RHS by the same coefficient a(x) used to normalise the LHS — this gives an incorrect Q(x).
  • Sign errors when transposing the Py term across the equals sign before dividing.
  • Dividing by a(x) without noting that a(x) might vanish at certain x-values, which can matter for the domain of validity of the solution.

A-level Scope Notes

This is the standard preparatory step for the integrating-factor method as taught in Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3); the integrating factor formula itself is in the FP formula booklet. Stay with linear first-order ODEs — do not extend this move to nonlinear cases (Bernoulli, Riccati) without the appropriate substitution first.
