Coordinate Geometry

Substitute second point's known parametric coordinates into the curve/normal equation

Family: Parametric Substitution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a curve has a parametric form and a second point on it (B) has its parameter expressed in terms of the first point's parameter (e.g. t_B = −1/(2c)), and B is also constrained to lie on a line — typically the tangent or normal at A. The brute-force alternative would solve the line simultaneously with the Cartesian curve equation, producing a quartic with one known root. Cue phrases: 'the normal at A meets the curve again at B', 'B has parameter ...', 'find c such that ...'. Whenever B's parameter is explicitly given, substitution is preferable to elimination.

Motivation — Why this works

Every point on a parametric curve has coordinates expressible in the parameter, so 'B lies on the curve' is automatic and adds no equation. The remaining condition — B lies on the normal/tangent at A — becomes a single equation in the unknown parameter once B's explicit coordinates are plugged in. This bypasses the resultant/quartic that elimination would produce, and avoids having to factor out the known root corresponding to A. The resulting equation is typically a low-degree polynomial in c (often quadratic in c²).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that B has an explicit parameter value, so its (x,y) are immediately writable in terms of c.
  2. Rule out solving the line equation simultaneously with xy = a² (or y² = 4ax, etc.), since that produces a quartic where A's parameter is a known but unwanted root.
  3. Commit to substitution because 'B on curve' is free and only 'B on normal at A' needs enforcing — exactly one equation in one unknown c.
  4. Bridge: write x_B and y_B as explicit functions of c, then plug into the normal-at-A equation.
  5. Tactically, simplify by cancelling the parameter factors that appear in numerator and denominator before introducing the numerical value of a.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify B's parameter t_B in terms of A's parameter c (here t_B = −1/(2c)).
  2. Write x_B = a·t_B, y_B = a/t_B in explicit form: x_B = −a/(2c), y_B = −2ac.
  3. Write the normal at A in the form c³x − cy = a(c⁴ − 1).
  4. Substitute x_B and y_B: c³·(−a/(2c)) − c·(−2ac) = a(c⁴ − 1).
  5. Simplify LHS: −ac²/2 + 2ac² = 3ac²/2.
  6. Cancel a (a ≠ 0): 3c²/2 = c⁴ − 1, i.e. 2c⁴ − 3c² − 2 = 0.
  7. Solve as quadratic in c²: c² = 2 (rejecting c² = −1/2), giving c = ±√2.

Worked Micro-Example

On $xy = 20$ with $a = 2\sqrt5$, the normal at $A$ (parameter $c$) is $c^3 x - cy = a(c^4 - 1)$. Given $B$ has parameter $-1/(2c)$, so $x_B = -a/(2c)$, $y_B = -2ac$. Substituting: $c^3\!\cdot\!(-a/(2c)) - c(-2ac) = a(c^4-1)$, i.e. $\tfrac{3}{2}ac^2 = a(c^4-1)$, giving $2c^4 - 3c^2 - 2 = 0$, hence $c^2 = 2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Parabola y² = 4ax: chord/normal from P(ap², 2ap) meets curve again at Q with parameter q given in terms of p (e.g. q = −p − 2/p for normal).
Substitute Q = (aq², 2aq) directly into the normal equation at P rather than solving simultaneously with y² = 4ax.
Rectangular hyperbola xy = c², tangent at (cp, c/p) meets curve again — but here it doesn't (tangent), whereas normal does.
For the normal, use the explicit second-parameter relation and substitute coordinates of the second point into the normal equation.
Ellipse in parametric form (a cosθ, b sinθ): chord through two given parameter values θ₁, θ₂ where θ₂ is fixed in terms of θ₁.
Substitute the (a cosθ₂, b sinθ₂) coordinates into the chord/normal equation at θ₁ to get a single trig equation in θ₁.
Tangent at A meets a second curve or line at B with a known parameter relationship.
Same principle: write B's coordinates from its parameter and substitute into the tangent equation rather than eliminating a variable.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to solve the normal simultaneously with xy = a² by setting y = a²/x, producing a quartic that must then be factored using A's known root.
  • Misreading B's parameter as 1/(2c) instead of −1/(2c), producing wrong signs in x_B, y_B.
  • Forgetting that 'B on the curve' is automatic in parametric form and redundantly imposing x_B y_B = a².

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when simplifying y_B = a/(−1/(2c)) = −2ac (the reciprocal of a negative fraction).
  • Arithmetic slips in c³·(−a/(2c)) — the c in the denominator cancels one factor of c³, leaving −ac²/2, not −ac³/2.
  • Dropping the factor a or √5 prematurely and then mishandling the constant on the RHS.
  • Forgetting to discard the spurious root (e.g. c² = −1/2) when solving the quadratic in c².

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure technique for rectangular hyperbolas xy = c² and parabolas y² = 4ax: parametric coordinates, equations of tangents/normals, and chords joining two parameter points are all in scope.
