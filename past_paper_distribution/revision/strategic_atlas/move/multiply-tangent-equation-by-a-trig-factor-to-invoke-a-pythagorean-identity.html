Trigonometry

Multiply tangent equation by a trig factor to invoke a Pythagorean identity

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

This move fires when deriving the equation of a tangent to a parametric conic (ellipse at (a cosθ, b sinθ), hyperbola at (a secθ, b tanθ), etc.) and the working has reached a point–gradient form cluttered with mixed trig functions (sinθ, cosθ, secθ, tanθ). The 'show that' target is a clean form like x cosθ/a + y sinθ/b = 1 or y tanθ = 2x secθ − 2, in which the right-hand side has NO θ-dependence apart from the linear terms. The visual cue is a pair of squared trig terms (or terms that will become squared after one strategic multiplication) that match a Pythagorean pair: sin²θ + cos²θ, sec²θ − tan²θ, or 1 + tan²θ = sec²θ.

Motivation — Why this works

The clean target tangent form has a pure constant (often 1 or a small integer) where messy θ-dependent terms currently sit. A Pythagorean identity is the only mechanism that can collapse two θ-dependent squared terms into a constant. Choosing the multiplier (sinθ, cosθ, or tanθ) is therefore not arbitrary — it is engineered to MANUFACTURE the squared-term pair that the relevant Pythagorean identity will annihilate. This converts a stalled rearrangement into a one-line collapse to the required form.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the target form: identify which trig function multiplies y, which multiplies x, and what the RHS constant is. This dictates the shape the LHS must take after multiplication.
  2. Rule out blind algebraic expansion — it produces long expressions with 1/cosθ or 1/sinθ that don't resolve to a constant without an identity.
  3. Rule out the 'wrong' multiplier: e.g. multiplying a hyperbola tangent by sinθ leaves stray 1/cosθ terms, whereas multiplying by tanθ produces sec²θ and tan²θ — exactly the pair sec²θ − tan²θ = 1 needs.
  4. Commit to the multiplier that creates a matched squared-pair from the Pythagorean family {sin²+cos²=1, sec²−tan²=1, 1+tan²=sec²}.
  5. Execute the multiplication, group the squared terms, apply the identity to collapse them to a constant, then divide through by any leftover factor (ab, 2, etc.) to land on the target form.
  6. Sanity-check: the final RHS should be free of θ except where the target explicitly allows it.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the tangent in point–gradient form: y − y₀ = m(x − x₀) using the parametric point and dy/dx.
  2. Inspect the target 'show that' form to determine which trig factor must multiply y (and hence both sides).
  3. Multiply both sides by that factor; simplify the gradient coefficient using sinθ/cosθ = tanθ etc.
  4. Move the constant term across so all squared trig terms sit on one side, e.g. RHS = (linear in x) − A·sec²θ + A·tan²θ.
  5. Apply the matching Pythagorean identity (sin²θ + cos²θ = 1, or sec²θ − tan²θ = 1) to collapse the pair into a constant.
  6. Divide through by any remaining numerical/parameter factor to reach the target.

Worked Micro-Example

Ellipse at $(a\cos\theta, b\sin\theta)$ with gradient $-\frac{b\cos\theta}{a\sin\theta}$: tangent is $y - b\sin\theta = -\frac{b\cos\theta}{a\sin\theta}(x - a\cos\theta)$. Multiply by $a\sin\theta$: $ay\sin\theta - ab\sin^2\theta = -bx\cos\theta + ab\cos^2\theta$. Rearrange: $bx\cos\theta + ay\sin\theta = ab(\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta) = ab$. Divide by $ab$: $\frac{x\cos\theta}{a} + \frac{y\sin\theta}{b} = 1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Hyperbola tangent at (a secθ, b tanθ) with target y tanθ = (something) secθ − k
Multiply by tanθ rather than sinθ; this manufactures sec²θ and tan²θ so that sec²θ − tan²θ = 1 collapses the constant.
Ellipse tangent at (a cosθ, b sinθ) with target x cosθ/a + y sinθ/b = 1
Multiply by sinθ (or cosθ, depending on the form of the gradient) to produce a sin²θ + cos²θ pair.
Integrals like ∫sec³θ dθ requiring a trig substitution to be cleaned up
Use 1 + tan²θ = sec²θ in the same structural role — converting a mixed expression into one collapsible to a constant or a single function.

Common Misreadings

  • Picking the multiplier from the LHS coefficient rather than from the target form — e.g. multiplying by sinθ for a hyperbola because sinθ appears in the gradient denominator, missing that tanθ is what creates the right Pythagorean pair.
  • Trying to brute-force algebraic simplification without a targeted multiplier, producing long messy expressions that obscure the identity opportunity.
  • Misreading sin²θ − cos²θ (which is −cos 2θ) as sin²θ + cos²θ and wrongly trying to collapse it to 1.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping a minus sign when moving the −A·sec²θ term across, breaking the sec²θ − tan²θ pairing.
  • Misremembering the identity as sec²θ + tan²θ = 1 instead of sec²θ − tan²θ = 1.
  • Forgetting to also multiply the constant term on the LHS by the chosen factor, losing the squared term needed for the identity.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 and FP coordinate-geometry of conics. The identities used (sin²θ + cos²θ = 1, sec²θ − tan²θ = 1, 1 + tan²θ = sec²θ) are all in the IAL formula booklet under trigonometric identities. Avoid invoking out-of-scope hyperbolic analogues (cosh² − sinh² = 1) unless the question is explicitly on hyperbolic functions.
