Algebra & Functions

Apply stated constraint to filter extraneous algebraic roots

Family: Constraint Filtering Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

This move fires whenever the algebra (a quadratic, a squared equation, a substitution, or a symmetric system) yields two or more candidate values, but the question text imposes an extra condition such as 'p is negative', 'a is a positive constant', 'x > y', 'k < 2', or an implicit domain like 'real solutions of 9^z = x' (forcing x > 0). Watch for phrases like 'given that', 'where ... is positive/negative', 'the larger root', 'the acute angle', or geometric restrictions to a quadrant. The cue is a mismatch between the number of algebraic solutions (often 2) and the unique answer the question demands. The constraint is never decoration — it is the disambiguator.

Motivation — Why this works

Many standard algebraic procedures (squaring, solving symmetric systems, factorising a polynomial in a substituted variable) produce solution sets larger than the problem actually admits. The stated constraint is the principle that breaks the symmetry or removes the spurious root, leaving a unique answer. Mathematically this reflects that operations like squaring or substituting u = f(x) are not injective, so the inverse step must be filtered against the original domain. Applying the filter is what converts a set of algebraic candidates into THE answer the examiner wants, and it almost always carries an accuracy mark.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the algebra has produced more than one candidate value (e.g. two roots from a quadratic, or a ± from a square root, or several x-values from a polynomial in a substituted variable).
  2. Re-read the question for an explicit constraint (sign, inequality, domain, geometric region) or an implicit one from the substitution used (e.g. 9^z > 0, sin θ ∈ [−1,1], lengths > 0).
  3. Rule out alternative continuations such as 'just write both answers' or 'pick the nicer-looking one' — recognise that the constraint exists precisely to disambiguate.
  4. Commit to testing each candidate against the constraint individually rather than guessing.
  5. Substitute each candidate into the constraint inequality/condition; reject any that fails; accept the unique survivor.
  6. Carry the chosen value through all subsequent parts of the question, since downstream working depends on having the right root.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. List all algebraic candidates explicitly, e.g. p = 2 + √23, p = 2 − √23.
  2. Write the stated constraint next to them, e.g. 'p < 0'.
  3. Evaluate each candidate (numerically if surds are involved) and test against the constraint.
  4. Mark each as Accept or Reject with a one-line justification.
  5. State the surviving value clearly as the final answer (or as the value to use in later parts).
  6. Where useful, sanity-check geometrically or by substitution back into the original equation.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $(2-p)^2 = 23$ given $p<0$. Then $2-p = \pm\sqrt{23}$, so $p = 2 \mp \sqrt{23}$, giving $p \approx 6.80$ or $p \approx -2.80$. Apply $p<0$: reject $p = 2+\sqrt{23}$, accept $p = 2-\sqrt{23}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Symmetric system in x, y with x > y stated
Both (a,b) and (b,a) satisfy the equations; assign the larger value to x and the smaller to y.
Substitution u = a^x or u = e^x with u > 0
Reject any non-positive u-roots before back-substituting, since a^x > 0 for real x.
Substitution u = sin θ or u = cos θ
Reject roots outside [−1, 1] before solving for θ.
Geometric problems requiring positive length or specified quadrant
Reject negative lengths, or roots that place the point in the wrong quadrant/region.
Parameter constraint carried into a later part (e.g. 'given k < 2')
Filter immediately and use only the surviving value in all subsequent computations.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the constraint ('a is a positive constant', 'p is negative', 'k < 2') as flavour text rather than a selection rule.
  • Assuming the first root produced by the quadratic formula corresponds to the 'first' variable named in the question.
  • Picking the root that gives 'nicer' numbers instead of the one that satisfies the constraint.
  • Forgetting that exponentials and squares carry implicit positivity restrictions even when the question does not restate them.

Pitfalls

  • Omitting the filtering step entirely and presenting both candidates — losing the final accuracy mark.
  • Numerical slip when estimating a surd (e.g. √23 ≈ 4.8) leading to the wrong sign decision.
  • Attempting to take a logarithm of a non-positive number and producing a spurious complex answer instead of rejecting the candidate.
  • Carrying the wrong root into a later part of the question, so all subsequent answers are silently incorrect even though internally consistent.
  • Confusing 'reject' with 'undefined' — writing z = −∞ for 9^z = 0 instead of clearly discarding it.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1–P4) and Further Pure. No formula booklet entry is required; this is a logical/reading skill applied alongside the quadratic formula or standard substitutions. Avoid invoking complex roots — at IAL, rejected roots are simply discarded, not reinterpreted in ℂ unless the question is explicitly about complex numbers (FP1).
