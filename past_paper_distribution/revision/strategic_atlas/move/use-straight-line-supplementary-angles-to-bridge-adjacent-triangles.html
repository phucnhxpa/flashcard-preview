Trigonometry

Use straight-line supplementary angles to bridge adjacent triangles

Family: Angle On A Line Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Three points are stated or shown to be collinear (e.g. A, C, D on a straight line) while a fourth point B sits off the line, creating two triangles sharing the vertex C. One triangle has been (or can be) fully solved, yielding an angle at C, but the second triangle has no angles known yet. Cue phrases include 'ACD is a straight line', 'A, C, D are collinear', or a diagram where two triangles meet along a common base.

Motivation — Why this works

Angles on a straight line sum to $180°$, so $\angle ACB$ and $\angle BCD$ form a linear pair. This single identity transfers angle information from the solved triangle to the unsolved one for free, with no extra Sine/Cosine Rule work. It is often the cheapest bridge between two triangles in a composite figure, and frequently the only way to get a foothold in the second triangle.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that A, C, D are collinear and B is off the line, so $\angle ACB$ and $\angle BCD$ sit back-to-back at C.
  2. Rule out re-solving $\triangle BCD$ from scratch with Sine/Cosine Rule on three sides — wasteful when an angle is already available next door.
  3. Commit to the supplementary-angle bridge because it is a one-line deduction directly from the collinearity hypothesis.
  4. Compute the supplement: $\angle BCD = 180° - \angle ACB$, then carry this angle into $\triangle BCD$.
  5. Decide which rule to apply next in $\triangle BCD$: with the new angle at C plus the two adjacent sides $CB$ and $CD$, the Cosine Rule gives $BD$; with an opposite side known, the Sine Rule gives another angle.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Confirm collinearity of A, C, D from the question or diagram.
  2. Identify the known angle at C inside the solved triangle, e.g. $\angle ACB = \theta$.
  3. Apply the linear-pair identity: $\angle BCD = 180° - \theta$.
  4. Insert this angle into $\triangle BCD$ and proceed with Sine or Cosine Rule as appropriate.

Worked Micro-Example

In $\triangle ABC$ I have found $\angle ACB = 127.5°$, with A, C, D collinear and B off the line. Then $\angle BCD = 180° - 127.5° = 52.5°$. With $CB = 8$, $CD = 5$ known, the Cosine Rule gives $BD^2 = 8^2 + 5^2 - 2(8)(5)\cos 52.5°$, so $BD \approx 6.36$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two triangles share a vertex on a straight line where the obtuse angle is in the second triangle, not the first.
The supplement now produces the obtuse angle; check the diagram to assign it to the correct triangle.
Bearings problem with a path turning at a point on a straight track.
The interior turn angle and its supplement give the bearings on either side; same $180°-\theta$ identity.
Quadrilateral split into two triangles by a diagonal, with one side extended through a vertex.
The exterior angle at that vertex equals $180°$ minus the interior angle; use it as the angle in the adjacent triangle.
Three collinear points with a triangle on each side (kite-like figure).
The two angles at the shared vertex on the line are supplementary; transfer between the two triangles in either direction.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $\angle ACB$ itself as an angle of $\triangle BCD$ rather than its supplement.
  • Assuming the two angles at C are equal (vertically opposite) when in fact they are supplementary on a straight line.
  • Missing the collinearity cue and trying to find $\angle BCD$ via a longer Sine/Cosine Rule route.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slip in $180° - \theta$, especially with decimal angles like $127.5°$.
  • Using the supplement in radians without converting consistently ($\pi - \theta$ vs $180° - \theta$).
  • Forgetting to check whether the original angle was the obtuse or acute solution from an earlier $\arcsin$, which determines which triangle gets which angle.

A-level Scope Notes

This is standard Edexcel IAL P2/P3 Sine and Cosine Rule territory; the only fact used beyond triangle rules is that angles on a straight line sum to $180°$.
