Algebra & Functions

Carry exact values through 'show that' to land on the printed answer

Family: Precision Management Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question is a 'show that' (or 'hence show') part with a specific numerical or decimal target (e.g. 'show that AO = 7.03' or 'show that the arc length is 35.9 cm'), and the working involves intermediate quantities that are either exact rationals (like 9/0.8 = 11.25) or contain irrationals such as π. Subsequent parts of the question then reuse the shown value in further calculations subject to an 'awrt' tolerance. The cue is: a printed target with limited significant figures, plus follow-on parts that depend on the same quantity. Equivalent phrasings: 'verify that…', 'prove the value is…', or any printed numerical answer to be reproduced.

Motivation — Why this works

A 'show that' is a verification: the examiner wants to see the algebra/arithmetic land on the stated value, so any premature rounding can corrupt the last digit and break the proof. Keeping fractions in exact form (e.g. 11.25, 7.03125) or keeping irrationals symbolic (e.g. (2π−5)/3) until the final step preserves all significant digits and guarantees the rounded answer matches. The same stored exact value is then available for later parts, avoiding cumulative drift outside the 'awrt' band. Underlying principle: rounding is a one-way lossy operation — defer it until display time.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the printed target (e.g. 7.03 to 3 s.f., or 35.9 to 1 d.p.) — this is a hard constraint that the final unrounded value must round to.
  2. Rule out the tempting shortcut of rounding intermediate quantities to match the precision of the target; this loses information and can flip the last digit.
  3. Rule out switching to decimal approximations of π or of recurring fractions early — small errors get amplified by multipliers like 84 or by further arc/area formulas.
  4. Commit to a 'symbolic/exact until the last line' policy: store every intermediate as a fraction, surd, or expression in π.
  5. Bridge to action: write the chain of equalities symbolically, then perform a single numerical evaluation at the end and round once to the requested precision.
  6. Tactical decision for downstream parts: when (b) or (c) reuses the shown quantity, fetch the stored exact value (e.g. 7.03125, not 7.03), so that the final answer sits comfortably inside the 'awrt' tolerance.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify all intermediates: separate exact rationals (terminating or not) from expressions containing π, e, or surds.
  2. Manipulate symbolically — e.g. L = 84 · (2π−5)/3 = 28(2π−5); A = (5/8) · (9/0.8) = (5/8) · 11.25 = 7.03125.
  3. Evaluate numerically only at the final display step using a high-precision value of π (≈ 3.14159265).
  4. Round once to the precision stated in the question and verify it matches the printed target.
  5. Store the unrounded value (e.g. 7.03125, or 28(2π−5)) for use in later parts; never substitute the rounded display value back into subsequent work.

Worked Micro-Example

Show that $AO = 7.03$ (3 s.f.). With $OD = \tfrac{9}{0.8} = 11.25$ exactly and $AO = \tfrac{5}{8}\,OD$, compute $AO = \tfrac{5}{8}\times 11.25 = 7.03125$. Round at the final step: $AO = 7.03$ (3 s.f.) ✓. For part (b), use the stored $7.03125$, not $7.03$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Intermediate contains π (e.g. arc length with angle (2π−5)/3)
Keep π symbolic — simplify algebraically (e.g. $84\cdot\tfrac{2\pi-5}{3}=28(2\pi-5)$), substitute π only at the very end.
Intermediate is an exact terminating decimal/fraction (e.g. 9/0.8 = 11.25)
Store the exact decimal/fraction; no rounding is ever needed for that quantity until the final display.
Follow-on part (b) or (c) reuses the 'shown' value
Substitute the unrounded stored value, not the printed rounded one, to stay inside the 'awrt' band.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the printed target ('show that AO = 7.03') as an instruction to henceforth use 7.03 in all subsequent work.
  • Believing 3 s.f. accuracy in the show-that means 3 s.f. accuracy in the inputs is sufficient.
  • Replacing π with 3.14 (or (2π−5)/3 with 1.28) early to 'simplify' the arithmetic.

Pitfalls

  • Rounding (2π−5)/3 ≈ 1.28 first, then computing 84·1.28/… and getting 35.8 instead of 35.9 — the show-that fails.
  • Using AO = 7.03 in part (b) and producing a perimeter or area that lands just outside the 'awrt' tolerance.
  • Rounding twice (intermediate then final) and accumulating a digit-shift error.
  • Forgetting to use enough digits of π on the calculator (use the π key, not 3.14).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL practice: 'show that' parts demand exact working with rounding only at the last step, and 'awrt' tolerances in later parts assume unrounded carry-over. No special formula booklet entry — this is a procedural discipline applicable across P1–P4, FP1–FP3, M1–M3, and S1–S3.
