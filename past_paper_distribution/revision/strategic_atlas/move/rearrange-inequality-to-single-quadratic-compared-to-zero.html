Algebra & Functions

Rearrange inequality to single quadratic compared to zero

Family: Reduce To Standard Inequality Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an inequality compares two different expressions across the inequality sign, and at least one side is a quadratic (or both sides combine into a quadratic). Typical cues: 'Solve f(x) < g(x)' where f is quadratic and g is linear or constant, or 'Find the values of x for which the curve lies below the line'. Also fires for word problems like 'for what x is the area less than ...' once the algebraic inequality has been set up.

Motivation — Why this works

A quadratic inequality of the form q(x) < 0 (or > 0) is solved by finding the roots of q(x) = 0 and then using the sign of the leading coefficient together with the parabola's shape to identify the intervals where q has the required sign. Keeping non-zero expressions on both sides forces an awkward pointwise comparison; collecting everything on one side converts a comparison problem into a single-sign problem with a standard algorithm (factorise / quadratic formula / sketch).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the inequality compares two different expressions, with a quadratic on one side and something simpler on the other.
  2. I rule out trying to compare graphs by inspection or solving f(x)=0 and g(x)=0 separately — neither directly answers an inequality.
  3. I commit to rearranging to the form q(x) < 0 because that is the standard form I know how to solve via roots and parabola shape.
  4. Bridge: subtract every term on the right from both sides so the right-hand side becomes 0, being careful with signs.
  5. Tactical: collect like terms, ensure the leading coefficient is positive (multiply through by −1 and flip the sign if not), then proceed to factorise or apply the quadratic formula.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two sides of the inequality and the inequality direction.
  2. Subtract every term on the right-hand side from both sides so the right-hand side becomes 0.
  3. Collect like terms to obtain a single quadratic expression q(x) compared to 0.
  4. If the leading coefficient is negative, multiply by −1 and reverse the inequality so the leading coefficient is positive.
  5. Hand off to the standard quadratic-inequality method: find roots of q(x)=0, sketch the parabola, read off the solution interval.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $x^2 - x - 2 < x + 6$.\\ Subtract $x+6$ from both sides: $x^2 - x - 2 - x - 6 < 0$.\\ Simplify: $x^2 - 2x - 8 < 0$, i.e. $(x-4)(x+2) < 0$.\\ Since the parabola opens upwards, $q(x) < 0$ between the roots: $-2 < x < 4$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Inequality with quadratic on both sides, e.g. $2x^2 + 3 \ge x^2 + x + 5$
Subtract one side fully; the $x^2$ terms partly cancel, leaving a single quadratic $\ge 0$ to solve.
Curve-versus-line problem: 'find x for which the curve lies below the line'
Form curve − line < 0, then solve as a quadratic inequality.
Negative leading coefficient, e.g. $-x^2 + 5 > 2x$
After rearranging to $-x^2 - 2x + 5 > 0$, multiply by $-1$ and flip: $x^2 + 2x - 5 < 0$.
Discriminant condition for 'two distinct real roots' giving an inequality in a parameter k
The discriminant $b^2 - 4ac > 0$ is already in 'expression > 0' form; treat it directly as a quadratic inequality in k.
Rational inequality $\dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)} < 0$
Do not multiply through by $g(x)$ (its sign is unknown); instead bring to one side over a common denominator and analyse sign of numerator and denominator on intervals.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $f(x) < g(x)$ as two separate equations $f(x)=0$ and $g(x)=0$ and comparing roots.
  • Reading the answer off a graph without algebraic working, losing method marks.
  • Believing the solution is just 'where the roots are' rather than the interval between or outside them.
  • Confusing $<$ with $\le$ and including/excluding endpoints incorrectly.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when subtracting: e.g. forgetting to change the sign of both $+x$ and $+6$.
  • Failing to flip the inequality when multiplying or dividing by a negative number.
  • Stopping at $q(x) < 0$ without solving — the rearrangement is only the first half of the method.
  • Writing the solution as $x < -2$ or $x < 4$ (union of wrong regions) instead of the interval $-2 < x < 4$ for an upward parabola less than 0.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1/P2) — quadratic inequalities solved by factorisation or the quadratic formula together with a sketch of the parabola; no calculus or sign-chart formalism beyond this is required.
