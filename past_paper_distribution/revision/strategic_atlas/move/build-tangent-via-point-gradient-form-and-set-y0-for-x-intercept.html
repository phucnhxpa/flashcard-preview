Coordinate Geometry

Build tangent via point-gradient form and set y=0 for x-intercept

Family: Tangent Line Intercept Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question gives (or lets you compute) a point P on a curve and asks where the tangent at P meets the x-axis. Typical phrasings: 'the tangent at P crosses the x-axis at Q', 'find the x-coordinate of Q', or 'show that Q has coordinates ...'. The cue is having both a point and an easily-evaluated gradient from dy/dx, with the target being a single intercept rather than the full line equation.

Motivation — Why this works

The point-gradient form y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) is the most direct vehicle when a point and gradient are already in hand — it avoids a detour through y = mx + c and the algebra of finding c. Substituting y = 0 immediately collapses the equation to a one-variable solve for x. This minimises rearrangement steps and reduces sign-error opportunities, especially when y₁ is a logarithm, surd, or other exact value that you don't want to manipulate twice.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice I already have P = (x₁, y₁) and m from dy/dx evaluated at x₁ — both ingredients of point-gradient form.
  2. I rule out converting to y = mx + c, since computing c is wasted work when only the x-intercept is asked.
  3. I commit to y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) because setting y = 0 in it gives a clean linear equation in x.
  4. I substitute y = 0 to get −y₁ = m(x − x₁), then isolate x = x₁ − y₁/m.
  5. I keep y₁ in exact form (ln, surd, fraction) throughout and simplify only at the end to preserve an exact answer.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute y₁ by substituting x₁ into the curve equation.
  2. Compute m = dy/dx evaluated at x = x₁.
  3. Write the tangent: y − y₁ = m(x − x₁).
  4. Set y = 0: −y₁ = m(x − x₁).
  5. Solve for x: x = x₁ − y₁/m, then simplify to exact form.

Worked Micro-Example

Curve y = ln(2x+1), point P = (1, \ln 9). Then dy/dx = 2/(2x+1), so m = 2/3 at x=1 (or 4/3 if y = 2\ln(2x+1)). Tangent: y - \ln 9 = \tfrac{4}{3}(x-1). Set y=0: -\ln 9 = \tfrac{4}{3}(x-1), so x = 1 - \tfrac{3\ln 9}{4}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Tangent meets the y-axis instead
Set x = 0 in the point-gradient form to get y = y₁ − m·x₁.
Normal at P meets an axis
Replace m with −1/m in the point-gradient form, then set y=0 (or x=0) as required.
Parametric curve with point P(t₀)
Get m = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) at t₀ and (x₁, y₁) from the parametric equations, then proceed identically.
Implicit curve (e.g. circle or conic)
Obtain m by implicit differentiation at P, then apply the same point-gradient + y=0 routine.
Tangent from an exponential curve y = e^{f(x)}
Same procedure; y₁ stays as e^{f(x₁)} in exact form until the final step.
Distance/length of segment between intercepts
Find both x- and y-intercepts via this method, then apply the distance formula.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'crosses the x-axis' as 'crosses the y-axis' and setting x=0 instead of y=0.
  • Using the gradient of the curve at the wrong point (e.g. at the intercept Q rather than at P).
  • Confusing tangent with normal and using −1/m by mistake.
  • Reading y₁ as a decimal approximation rather than keeping it exact (e.g. ln 9).

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic errors when dividing by a fractional gradient — forgetting to multiply by the reciprocal.
  • Sign errors when transposing −y₁ across the equality.
  • Prematurely converting ln 9 to 2 ln 3 (or vice versa) and losing the form the markscheme wants.
  • Dropping the factor of 3/4 (or m⁻¹) when isolating x − x₁.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3/P4 coordinate geometry and differentiation; uses only point-gradient form, standard derivatives (including ln, e^x, chain rule), and exact-value manipulation.
