Trigger — When to use

Fires after repeated integration by parts on a polynomial × exponential integrand, when the resulting antiderivative is a sum of 2-3 terms each containing the same exponential factor (e.g. e^{kx} or e^{-x/n}). Visual cue: every term shares an exponential, AND the rational coefficients have a clear common denominator (e.g. 1/3, 2/9, 2/27 → LCD 27) or a common integer factor (e.g. -18, -108, -324 → GCF -18). Also fires when the question phrasing demands a particular target form ('show that…', 'express in the form…', 'hence find the exact value') or when a 'hence' part requires substituting boundary values into the antiderivative.

Motivation — Why this works

Factoring out the common exponential and a rational scalar collapses several scattered terms into a single product (constant) × (exponential) × (polynomial), which is the canonical 'show that' target form in Edexcel mark schemes and the form that minimises arithmetic when substituting limits. Mathematically this is just the distributive law applied in reverse, but strategically it dramatically reduces the number of operations during definite-integral evaluation (one exponential evaluation instead of three) and makes asymptotic/limit analysis transparent, since all x-dependence is encapsulated in one product. It also makes verification by differentiation cheaper, because the product rule need only be applied once.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that every term in the antiderivative contains the same exponential factor e^{kx} — this is the obvious common factor to extract.
  2. Rule out leaving the answer expanded (technically correct but loses presentation marks on 'show that' / 'express in the form' questions, and triples the work in any subsequent limit substitution).
  3. Rule out factoring just the exponential without consolidating the rational coefficients — half-finished factorisation rarely matches the target form.
  4. Commit to the move because either (a) the question states a target form, or (b) a 'hence' part needs limit substitution and a compact form will streamline it.
  5. Tactical decision: choose the scalar to extract so the polynomial inside has integer coefficients — find the LCD of fractional coefficients (e.g. 1/3, 2/9, 2/27 → extract 1/27) or the GCF of integer coefficients (e.g. -18, -108, -324 → extract -18).
  6. Bridge to operation: rewrite each fractional/integer coefficient as (chosen scalar) × (integer), then pull the scalar and the exponential out front.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the common exponential factor e^{kx} present in every term.
  2. Identify the LCD of fractional coefficients (or GCF of integer coefficients) to determine the scalar to extract.
  3. Rewrite each term as (scalar)(e^{kx})(monomial in x): e.g. (1/3)x²e^{3x} = (1/27)e^{3x}·9x².
  4. Pull (scalar)(e^{kx}) outside a bracket containing the polynomial: result has the form (scalar)·e^{kx}·(ax² + bx + c) + C.
  5. Verify by mental re-expansion that the three original terms are reproduced (and check the sign of the middle term carefully).
  6. Restore the constant of integration C if working with an indefinite integral.

Worked Micro-Example

After IBP twice on $\int x^2 e^{3x}\,dx$ we obtain $\tfrac{1}{3}x^2 e^{3x} - \tfrac{2}{9}x e^{3x} + \tfrac{2}{27}e^{3x} + C$. The common factor is $e^{3x}$ and the LCD of $\{1/3,\,2/9,\,2/27\}$ is $27$. Rewrite as $\tfrac{e^{3x}}{27}\bigl(9x^2 - 6x + 2\bigr) + C$. Mental check: $\tfrac{1}{27}\cdot 9x^2 = \tfrac{x^2}{3}$ ✓, $-\tfrac{1}{27}\cdot 6x = -\tfrac{2x}{9}$ ✓, $\tfrac{1}{27}\cdot 2 = \tfrac{2}{27}$ ✓.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Antiderivative of the form $e^{ax}\sin(bx)$ or $e^{ax}\cos(bx)$ after the standard 'two-IBP-and-solve' trick.
Factor out $e^{ax}/(a^2+b^2)$ to leave a clean $a\sin(bx)\pm b\cos(bx)$ bracket — same consolidation principle, different surface.
Repeated IBP on $\int x^n \sin(kx)\,dx$ leaving terms with mixed $\sin$ and $\cos$ factors.
Group $\sin$-terms and $\cos$-terms separately, factor each with rational coefficients on a common denominator.
Antiderivative with a common surd or logarithmic factor (e.g. $\sqrt{x}$ or $\ln x$) appearing in every term.
Extract that factor first, then consolidate remaining rational coefficients on a common denominator before substituting limits.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the expanded form as the final answer when the question says 'express in the form $\tfrac{1}{27}e^{3x}(ax^2+bx+c)$' — losing the final accuracy mark.
  • Factoring out only the exponential but leaving fractional coefficients inside the bracket (half-finished, rarely matches the target form).
  • Misreading 'hence' in part (b) as permission to restart from scratch, rather than using the compact form from part (a).

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic errors when converting fractions to a common denominator — especially the sign of the middle term (e.g. writing $9x^2 + 6x + 2$ instead of $9x^2 - 6x + 2$).
  • Miscounting the factor pulled from the middle term (e.g. writing $2x^2 - x + 1$ instead of $2x^2 - 2x + 1$).
  • Forgetting the constant of integration $+C$ after rewriting an indefinite integral.
  • Sign errors when the GCF is negative (e.g. extracting $-18$ from $-18, -108, -324$ — the inner polynomial coefficients become positive).

A-level Scope Notes

Stays strictly inside Edexcel IAL Maths/Further Maths. The move uses only the distributive law and basic fraction arithmetic; no formula booklet entry is invoked beyond the standard IBP formula $\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$. Avoid generalising to complex-exponential representations of $\sin/\cos$ (out of scope).
