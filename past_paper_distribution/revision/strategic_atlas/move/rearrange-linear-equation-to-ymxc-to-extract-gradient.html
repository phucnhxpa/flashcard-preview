Coordinate Geometry

Rearrange linear equation to y=mx+c to extract gradient

Family: Gradient Extraction Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The line is given in general form (ax+by+c=0) or rearranged general form (ay+bx=c, 2y−3x=26, etc.) rather than slope-intercept form, and a subsequent step requires the gradient — typically to apply a perpendicularity condition (m₁m₂=−1), a parallelism condition (m₁=m₂), or to find an angle of inclination. Visual cues: x and y appear on the same side, y has a coefficient ≠ 1, or there is a constant term mixed in. Equivalent phrasings: 'find the gradient of L₁', 'a line perpendicular to L₁ passes through P', 'L₂ is parallel to L₁'.

Motivation — Why this works

In y=mx+c the gradient m is literally the coefficient of x, so once the equation is in this canonical form the gradient can be read off with no further computation. Rearranging is more transparent and less error-prone than memorising the shortcut m=−A/B from Ax+By+C=0, where sign mistakes are very common. This step unlocks every downstream tool involving gradients: perpendicularity (m₁m₂=−1), parallelism, tangent-normal relationships, and angle calculations.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the line is not in y=mx+c form — y is not isolated, so the gradient is not immediately visible.
  2. Recognise that the next step (perpendicular line, parallel line, angle, etc.) needs the gradient m₁, so extracting it is the immediate goal.
  3. Consider the shortcut m=−A/B from general form, but rule it out as sign-error-prone; rearranging fully to y=mx+c is safer and shows the working clearly.
  4. Commit to isolating y: move the x-term and constant to the other side, then divide every term by the coefficient of y.
  5. Once in y=mx+c form, read off m as the coefficient of x; the constant c gives the y-intercept (a useful bonus for sketching or checking).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Starting from the given form (e.g. 2y − 3x = 26 or 3x − 2y + 12 = 0), move all non-y terms to the right-hand side, watching signs carefully (e.g. 2y = 3x + 26 or 2y = 3x + 12).
  2. Divide every term on both sides by the coefficient of y (e.g. divide by 2): y = (3/2)x + 13 or y = (3/2)x + 6.
  3. Identify the gradient as the coefficient of x: m₁ = 3/2.
  4. (Optional) Note the y-intercept c for any later sketching or substitution checks.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $L_1: 2y - 3x = 26$, find its gradient. Add $3x$: $2y = 3x + 26$. Divide by $2$: $y = \tfrac{3}{2}x + 13$. Hence $m_1 = \tfrac{3}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line given as ax + by + c = 0 (general form with everything on one side)
Isolate by: by = −ax − c, then divide by b to get y = (−a/b)x − c/b. Same logic; just an extra sign to track.
Line given as ax + by = c
Identical procedure: isolate y, divide by b. Gradient is −a/b.
Line given parametrically or via a direction vector (a, b)
Compute gradient as dy/dx = b/a directly from the direction vector — no rearrangement needed.
Quick read-off shortcut for confident students
From Ax + By + C = 0, gradient = −A/B. Use only if comfortable with sign tracking.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading the coefficient of x directly from the unrearranged form, e.g. claiming m = −3 from 2y − 3x = 26.
  • Forgetting the negative when using the shortcut: writing m = a/b instead of −a/b from ax + by + c = 0.
  • Confusing the constant term with the gradient when y has a non-unit coefficient.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when transposing the x-term: moving −3x across gives +3x, not −3x.
  • Forgetting to divide every term (including the constant) by the coefficient of y.
  • Dividing by a negative y-coefficient and mishandling the resulting signs on both the gradient and the intercept.
  • Stopping at 2y = 3x + 26 and reading m = 3 instead of completing the division to get m = 3/2.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics P1/P3 (coordinate geometry of straight lines). No formula booklet entry is required — y = mx + c and the perpendicular-gradient relation m₁m₂ = −1 are assumed knowledge. Avoid generalising to non-linear curves; for those, gradient comes from differentiation, not rearrangement.
