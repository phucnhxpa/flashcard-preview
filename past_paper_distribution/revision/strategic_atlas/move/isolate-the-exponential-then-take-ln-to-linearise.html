Isolate the exponential, then take ln to linearise

Family: Log Extraction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever the unknown sits inside an exponential term (e.g. $e^{kt}$, $a^{x}$) that is embedded in a sum, product, or fraction, and an exact algebraic value is required. Typical cues: logistic/limited-growth models like $N = \frac{A}{B+e^{-kt}}$, Newton's law of cooling $T = T_0 + Ce^{-kt}$, or compound models $P = A - Be^{-kt}$. Phrases like 'find the exact value of $t$' or 'find $t$ when ...' confirm a logarithmic finish is expected. Numerical/graphical methods are ruled out because exact form is demanded.

Motivation — Why this works

The natural logarithm is the inverse of the exponential, so $\ln(e^{f(t)}) = f(t)$ cleanly extracts the exponent — but only when the exponential stands alone on one side. $\ln$ does not distribute over sums or differences, so any constant added to or multiplied with the exponential must first be removed by ordinary algebra (cross-multiplying, subtracting, dividing). Once the exponential is isolated, one application of $\ln$ converts an exponential equation into a linear one in $t$, which is trivial to solve.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the unknown $t$ is trapped inside $e^{-0.12t}$, which itself sits in a denominator — direct log application would hit a sum inside the log.
  2. Rule out trial/graphical methods because the question demands an exact value, and rule out applying $\ln$ immediately because $\ln(2+e^{-0.12t})$ does not simplify.
  3. Commit to the standard recipe: peel away the algebraic wrapping (fraction, constant, coefficient) until $e^{-0.12t}$ is alone, then invert with $\ln$.
  4. First concrete step is to cross-multiply to clear the fraction, converting the equation into a linear-in-exponential form.
  5. Tactically, keep fractions exact (e.g. $\tfrac{1}{7}$) and use $\ln(1/a) = -\ln a$ to manage the negative coefficient, dividing only at the very end to avoid rounding drift.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Cross-multiply to clear the fraction: $420(2 + e^{-0.12t}) = 900$, so $2 + e^{-0.12t} = \tfrac{15}{7}$.
  2. Subtract the constant: $e^{-0.12t} = \tfrac{15}{7} - 2 = \tfrac{1}{7}$.
  3. Take $\ln$ of both sides: $-0.12t = \ln(\tfrac{1}{7})$.
  4. Apply $\ln(1/7) = -\ln 7$: $-0.12t = -\ln 7$.
  5. Divide by $-0.12$: $t = \dfrac{\ln 7}{0.12} \approx 16.22$.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $420 = \dfrac{900}{2+e^{-0.12t}}$ for $t$.\\ Cross-multiply: $2 + e^{-0.12t} = \tfrac{15}{7}$, hence $e^{-0.12t} = \tfrac{1}{7}$.\\ Take $\ln$: $-0.12t = -\ln 7$, so $t = \dfrac{\ln 7}{0.12} \approx 16.22$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Coefficient times exponential, e.g. $5\cdot 2^{3x} = 40$
Divide by the coefficient first to get $2^{3x} = 8$, then take $\ln$ (or $\log_2$) and use $\ln(a^b) = b\ln a$ to extract $3x$.
Exponential inside a sum, e.g. $T = 20 + 60e^{-0.05t}$ with $T = 35$
Subtract the constant ($20$), then divide by $60$ to isolate $e^{-0.05t}$ before applying $\ln$.
Two exponentials with same base, e.g. $e^{x} + e^{-x} = k$
Substitute $u = e^{x}$ to obtain a quadratic $u^2 - ku + 1 = 0$; solve for $u$, then take $\ln$ — isolation happens after substitution.
Different base, e.g. $3^{2x+1} = 50$
Already isolated; take $\ln$ of both sides and use $\ln(3^{2x+1}) = (2x+1)\ln 3$ to linearise.

Common Misreadings

  • Taking $\ln$ before isolating, producing $\ln(420) = \ln 900 - \ln(2+e^{-0.12t})$, which leaves the unknown still trapped because $\ln$ does not distribute over a sum.
  • Believing $\ln(2 + e^{-0.12t}) = \ln 2 + (-0.12t)$, i.e. distributing $\ln$ over addition.
  • Reading $e^{-0.12t} = \tfrac{1}{7}$ as $-0.12t = \tfrac{1}{7}$, dropping the $\ln$ step entirely.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when dividing by the negative coefficient $-0.12$; forgetting that $\ln(1/7)$ is negative so $t$ comes out positive.
  • Premature rounding of $\ln 7$ before the final division, shifting the answer in the second decimal place.
  • Arithmetic slip in the fraction step: computing $\tfrac{15}{7} - 2$ as $\tfrac{13}{7}$ instead of $\tfrac{1}{7}$.
  • Confusing $\ln$ with $\log_{10}$ when the exponential base is $e$, leading to a wrong numerical answer.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P2/P3 technique for exponential models (population, cooling, logistic-style growth); uses only $\ln$ as inverse of $e^{x}$ and the law $\ln(a^b)=b\ln a$, all within the core syllabus.
