Algebra & Functions

Check candidate solutions against logarithm argument domains

Family: Domain Validation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an equation contains one or more logarithms, $\ln(\cdot)$ or $\log(\cdot)$, and algebraic manipulation (combining logs, exponentiating, squaring, clearing denominators) has produced candidate solutions. Cues include phrases like 'solve $\ln(\cdot) - \ln(\cdot) = k$', 'find $x$ such that...', or any equation where logs were merged via product/quotient laws. Also fires implicitly on Edexcel mark schemes that award a final mark for justifying the solution lies in the domain.

Motivation — Why this works

Logarithms are only defined for strictly positive arguments, so combining or exponentiating logs can introduce extraneous roots that satisfy the transformed equation but not the original. Verifying each candidate against the intersection of all argument-positivity constraints guarantees the answer is genuinely a solution. On Edexcel papers this is often the final accuracy/verification mark and is required for full credit.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the original equation contains $\ln$ terms, so each argument must be strictly positive.
  2. I rule out treating the algebraic solution as automatically valid: exponentiating can hide sign issues.
  3. I commit to listing every argument's positivity constraint and intersecting them to find the binding domain.
  4. I bridge to the concrete check by identifying the strictest inequality (here $x>1$ dominates $x>-\tfrac{1}{3}$).
  5. Tactically, I evaluate the candidate numerically (or symbolically) and confirm it lies inside the binding constraint, rejecting any root that does not.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. List the positivity requirement for each logarithm argument in the original equation.
  2. Intersect the inequalities to obtain the binding domain (the strictest constraint).
  3. Substitute or estimate each candidate solution numerically.
  4. Compare against the binding domain; accept if it lies inside, reject as extraneous if not.
  5. State the conclusion explicitly (e.g., 'since $x \approx 1.91 > 1$, the solution is valid').

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\ln(3x+1) - \ln(x-1) = 2$. Combining: $\ln\!\left(\tfrac{3x+1}{x-1}\right)=2 \Rightarrow \tfrac{3x+1}{x-1}=e^{2}$, giving $x=\tfrac{e^{2}+1}{e^{2}-3}\approx 1.91$. Domain: $3x+1>0$ and $x-1>0$ give binding $x>1$. Since $1.91>1$, the solution is valid.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic-in-$x$ produced after log manipulation gives two roots
Test each root separately against the domain; typically one is extraneous and must be explicitly rejected.
Equation involves $\ln(x^{2})$ or $\log(x^{2})$
Domain is $x \neq 0$ rather than $x>0$, so negative roots may be admissible — do not over-restrict.
Equation involves $\ln(f(x))$ with $f$ a polynomial of degree $\ge 2$
Solve $f(x)>0$ as an inequality (sign diagram or factorisation) before testing candidates.
Exponential equation obtained by taking logs of both sides
Ensure both sides were positive before logging; otherwise the log step itself is invalid for some candidates.

Common Misreadings

  • Skipping the domain check entirely and assuming any algebraic solution is valid.
  • Checking only one of several logarithm arguments rather than intersecting all constraints.
  • Confusing the domain of the combined log $\ln\!\left(\tfrac{3x+1}{x-1}\right)$ (which only needs the quotient positive) with the stricter domain of the original two separate logs.

Pitfalls

  • Using the weaker constraint $x>-\tfrac{1}{3}$ instead of the binding $x>1$ and accepting a spurious root.
  • Approximating $e^{2}\approx 7.389$ carelessly and miscomputing the candidate's decimal value.
  • Forgetting to state the verification explicitly, losing the final mark even if the answer is correct.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 logarithm and exponential content; uses only natural log laws, exponentiation, and basic inequality reasoning expected at A-level.
