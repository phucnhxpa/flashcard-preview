Trigger — When to use

Fires when counting intersections (or roots of $f(x)=g(x)$) between two curves where at least one has a fixed sign on its domain — e.g., $y=k/x^2>0$, $y=e^x>0$, $y=\sqrt{x}\geq 0$, or $y=a^x>0$. Cue phrases: 'how many solutions', 'number of intersections', 'state the number of roots'. Especially useful when the other curve (often a cubic, quadratic, or linear function) changes sign, so whole subintervals can be discarded without algebra.

Motivation — Why this works

An intersection requires both curves to take the same value at the same $x$, so they must share the same sign there. If curve A is strictly positive everywhere it is defined, any intersection $x$-value must lie in the region where curve B is also positive. This converts a global counting question into a local one on a much smaller subset, where monotonicity and end-behaviour usually pin the count exactly.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that one curve has a fixed sign throughout its domain (e.g. $k/x^2>0$ for $x\neq 0$).
  2. Rule out every $x$-interval where the other curve has the opposite sign or is zero — no intersection is possible there.
  3. Commit to working only on the surviving region(s), since algebraic equation-solving is unnecessary outside them.
  4. Identify the boundary of the surviving region (typically a root or asymptote of the sign-changing curve) and evaluate both curves there.
  5. Use end-behaviour and monotonicity on the surviving region to argue exactly how many crossings occur (often one).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. State the sign of the always-positive (or always-negative) curve on its domain.
  2. Sketch or analyse the other curve's sign chart, listing intervals where it is positive, negative, or zero.
  3. Discard every interval where the signs disagree — no intersections possible there.
  4. On each surviving interval, compare the two curves at the endpoints (including limits at $\pm\infty$ and at vertical asymptotes).
  5. Apply monotonicity / IVT: if one curve increases while the other decreases (or their difference is monotonic), conclude a unique crossing; otherwise count by sign changes of the difference.
  6. State the total number of intersections.

Worked Micro-Example

Count solutions of $y=k/x^2$ and $y=(2x-k)(x+4)^2$ for $k>0$. Since $k/x^2>0$ for all $x\neq 0$, intersections need the cubic positive, i.e. $x>k/2$. On $(k/2,\infty)$ the cubic rises from $0$ to $\infty$ while $k/x^2$ falls from $4/k$ to $0$. Their difference goes from negative to positive monotonically, so there is exactly $1$ intersection.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Intersections of $y=e^x$ with a polynomial $p(x)$
Since $e^x>0$, restrict attention to intervals where $p(x)>0$; discard intervals where $p(x)\leq 0$.
Intersections of $y=\sqrt{x}$ with a line $y=mx+c$
Domain forces $x\geq 0$ and $y\geq 0$; if $mx+c<0$ on part of $[0,\infty)$, eliminate that subinterval before squaring.
Intersections of $y=\ln x$ with a curve known to be negative on $(0,1)$
$\ln x<0$ on $(0,1)$ and $>0$ on $(1,\infty)$; match signs to localise crossings.
Roots of $f(x)=g(x)$ where $g(x)\geq 0$ (e.g. $g(x)=x^2$)
Restrict to $\{x:f(x)\geq 0\}$; equality at $f(x)=0$ requires $g(x)=0$ simultaneously.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating a touch point of the sign-changing curve (where it equals zero) as a candidate intersection, even though the always-positive curve is strictly positive there.
  • Forgetting that $y=k/x^2$ (or $y=1/x$, $\ln x$, etc.) is undefined at certain $x$, so those points cannot be intersections regardless of limits.
  • Confusing 'curves have the same sign' (necessary condition) with 'curves are equal' (sufficient condition) — sign agreement alone does not guarantee a crossing.

Pitfalls

  • Miscounting by overlooking that $x^3$ eventually dominates $1/x^2$, so after one crossing the cubic stays above the reciprocal — there is no second crossing at large $x$.
  • Forgetting to check behaviour at vertical asymptotes (e.g. as $x\to 0^+$, $k/x^2\to\infty$) when arguing about whether the curves cross.
  • Sketching the cubic with the wrong end behaviour and thereby misidentifying which interval has the cubic positive.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only sign analysis, sketching, end-behaviour and IVT/monotonicity arguments standard in Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P2–P4); no calculus beyond recognising long-term growth rates is required.
