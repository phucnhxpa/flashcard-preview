Split polynomial-over-surd into separate index-form power terms

Family: Index Form Preprocessing Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an expression to be differentiated or integrated has the form (polynomial)/x^k or (polynomial)/√x, with a single-term denominator. Cues include a sum in the numerator divided by a monomial, or a surd/root in the denominator. Also triggered by phrases like 'find dy/dx' or '∫ ... dx' on a quotient where the bottom is one power of x. The move precedes any application of the power rule.

Motivation — Why this works

The power rule d/dx(ax^n) = anx^(n−1) and ∫ax^n dx = ax^(n+1)/(n+1) + c require each term to already be in the form ax^n. Because division distributes over a sum in the numerator (a+b)/c = a/c + b/c, and because x^a/x^b = x^(a−b), a single-term denominator can be absorbed into each numerator term as a shift in index. This bypasses the quotient rule entirely and produces an additive form that calculus rules act on term-by-term.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the expression is a sum of polynomial terms divided by a single power of x (here √x = x^(1/2)).
  2. Rule out the quotient rule (overkill for three terms) and 'integrating top and bottom separately' (invalid).
  3. Commit to splitting because the denominator is a monomial, so distribution is legitimate and each piece will simplify via index laws.
  4. Rewrite the surd as a fractional index so subtraction of exponents is mechanical: √x → x^(1/2).
  5. Subtract exponents term-by-term, keeping coefficients intact, and check each resulting power (including any that become negative or zero) before applying the calculus rule.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rewrite any surds/roots in the denominator as fractional indices, e.g. √x = x^(1/2).
  2. Distribute the division: (A + B + C)/x^k = A/x^k + B/x^k + C/x^k.
  3. Apply x^a / x^b = x^(a−b) to each term, treating constants as x^0.
  4. Collect into the standard form Σ a_i x^(n_i), ready for differentiation or integration.
  5. Apply the power rule term-by-term.

Worked Micro-Example

Integrate $\dfrac{x^2 + 10x + 25}{\sqrt{x}}$. Rewrite as $x^{3/2} + 10x^{1/2} + 25x^{-1/2}$. Then $\int \left(x^{3/2} + 10x^{1/2} + 25x^{-1/2}\right)dx = \tfrac{2}{5}x^{5/2} + \tfrac{20}{3}x^{3/2} + 50x^{1/2} + c$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Polynomial over a positive integer power, e.g. $(x^3 - 4x)/x^2$
Split to give $x - 4x^{-1}$; one term has a negative index, integrate $-4x^{-1}$ as $-4\ln|x|$ rather than via the power rule.
Polynomial over a cube root, e.g. $(x^2 + 1)/\sqrt[3]{x}$
Rewrite denominator as $x^{1/3}$ and subtract $1/3$ from each numerator index, giving $x^{5/3} + x^{-1/3}$.
Numerator is a product expanded first, e.g. $(2x+1)^2/\sqrt{x}$
Expand the bracket before splitting, since the move requires an explicit sum in the numerator.
Denominator is a binomial such as $(x+1)$
Splitting is invalid; use polynomial division, partial fractions, or substitution instead.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the expression as needing the quotient rule, which is technically valid for differentiation but slow and error-prone here.
  • Attempting to 'differentiate/integrate numerator and denominator separately', which is not a valid rule.
  • Reading $\sqrt{x}$ as a constant or leaving it un-indexed, blocking the index-law step.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to subtract the denominator's index from every numerator term — e.g., writing $10x$ instead of $10x^{1/2}$ for the middle term.
  • Sign errors on negative indices, e.g., $25/x^{1/2} = 25x^{-1/2}$, not $25x^{1/2}$.
  • Mishandling a constant numerator term: a constant is $x^0$, so dividing by $x^{1/2}$ gives $x^{-1/2}$, not $x^{1/2}$.
  • After splitting, forgetting to add the constant of integration when integrating.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics: index laws (P1), differentiation and integration of $ax^n$ (P1/P2), and the special case $\int x^{-1}dx = \ln|x| + c$ (P2).
