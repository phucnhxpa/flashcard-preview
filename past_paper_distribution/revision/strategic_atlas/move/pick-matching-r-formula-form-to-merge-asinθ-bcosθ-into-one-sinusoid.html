Trigonometry

Pick matching R-formula form to merge a·sinθ ± b·cosθ into one sinusoid

Family: R Formula Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an expression of the form a·sinθ ± b·cosθ (with a, b > 0) appears and you need to either solve an equation, find a maximum/minimum, or sketch the combined wave. Strong cues: two different trig functions of the same angle θ added or subtracted, and the question hints at single-sinusoid form (e.g. 'express as R sin(θ − α)' or 'find the greatest value'). Also fires when the question gives a target form like R cos(θ + α) explicitly.

Motivation — Why this works

Any linear combination a·sinθ + b·cosθ is itself a sinusoid because {sinθ, cosθ} spans all such combinations, so it can be rewritten as R sin(θ ± α) or R cos(θ ± α) with R = √(a²+b²). Collapsing two terms into one converts an otherwise un-isolable equation into a single invertible trig equation solvable by arcsin/arccos. Choosing the form whose compound-angle expansion matches the given sign pattern keeps both R and α positive (with α acute), avoiding sign errors.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the expression mixes sinθ and cosθ with different coefficients, so θ cannot be isolated directly.
  2. Rule out squaring (introduces extraneous roots) and t-substitution (overkill at A-level here); the R-formula is the standard tool.
  3. Commit to R-form because the compound-angle identity provides an exact algebraic match to a·sinθ ± b·cosθ.
  4. Choose the specific form (sin vs cos, + vs −) so that the expansion's sign pattern matches the given expression with α acute and R > 0.
  5. For a·sinθ − b·cosθ pick R sin(θ − α), since sin(A−B) = sinA cosB − cosA sinB lines up term-for-term with the given signs.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the sign pattern of the given expression a·sinθ ± b·cosθ.
  2. Select the matching identity: a·sinθ + b·cosθ → R sin(θ + α); a·sinθ − b·cosθ → R sin(θ − α); b·cosθ + a·sinθ → R cos(θ − α); b·cosθ − a·sinθ → R cos(θ + α).
  3. Expand the chosen R-form using the compound-angle formula.
  4. Equate the sinθ coefficients to get R cosα = a (when sin-form) or R sinα = a (when cos-form).
  5. Equate the cosθ coefficients to get R sinα = b (or R cosα = b), keeping both sides positive by virtue of the matched signs.
  6. Solve: R = √(a² + b²) and tanα = (sinα coefficient)/(cosα coefficient), with α acute.

Worked Micro-Example

Express $4\sin\theta - 3\cos\theta$ as $R\sin(\theta - \alpha)$ with $R>0$, $0<\alpha<\tfrac{\pi}{2}$. Expand: $R\sin(\theta-\alpha) = R\cos\alpha\sin\theta - R\sin\alpha\cos\theta$. Match coefficients: $R\cos\alpha = 4$, $R\sin\alpha = 3$. Then $R = \sqrt{16+9} = 5$ and $\tan\alpha = \tfrac{3}{4}$, so $\alpha = \arctan\tfrac{3}{4}$. Hence $4\sin\theta - 3\cos\theta = 5\sin(\theta - \arctan\tfrac{3}{4})$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

a·sinθ + b·cosθ with a, b > 0
Use R sin(θ + α); equate R cosα = a, R sinα = b.
b·cosθ + a·sinθ written cosine-first
Use R cos(θ − α); equate R cosα = b, R sinα = a.
b·cosθ − a·sinθ
Use R cos(θ + α), since cos(A+B) = cosA cosB − sinA sinB matches the +cos −sin pattern.
Question fixes the target form (e.g. requires R cos(θ + α) even though expression is a·sinθ − b·cosθ)
Still expand the prescribed form and match; α may end up acute but R-formula identity still gives R = √(a²+b²).
Coefficients negative or expression like −a·sinθ − b·cosθ
Factor out −1 first, or pick R cos(θ − α) variant whose expansion has matching signs, so α stays acute.

Common Misreadings

  • Choosing R sin(θ + α) for a·sinθ − b·cosθ, then forcing R sinα = −b — algebraically valid but breaks the convention that α is acute and risks sign slips.
  • Reading the question's target form as optional and defaulting to a different form, leading to a correct but non-matching answer that loses marks.
  • Treating R as possibly negative; by convention R > 0 and α acute.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when equating the cosθ coefficient: writing R sinα = −b instead of +b after the two minus signs already cancelled.
  • Swapping the roles of sin and cos when equating coefficients (e.g. setting R sinα = a from the sinθ term).
  • Computing α via tanα with the ratio inverted; remember tanα = (R sinα)/(R cosα).
  • Forgetting to state the range of α (acute) and the positivity of R when presenting the final form.

A-level Scope Notes
