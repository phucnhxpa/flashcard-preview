Algebra & Functions

Combine reciprocal-paired new roots over common denominator αβ

Family: Algebraic Restructuring Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in 'form a new quadratic' problems when the new roots are rational expressions in α and β — most classically α/β and β/α, but also forms like (α+1)/β paired with (β+1)/α. The cue is that each new root is a fraction whose denominator is the *other* original root, so adding them naturally invites a common denominator of αβ. Recognise this as a restructuring task, not an evaluation task: you are not solving for α and β individually.

Motivation — Why this works

Putting α/β + β/α over αβ gives (α² + β²)/(αβ), and α² + β² = (α+β)² − 2αβ is a standard symmetric-function identity expressible in the known sum and product. Likewise the product (α/β)(β/α) collapses to 1 by cancellation. The whole point of the scaffolded structure of these exam parts is that earlier results (S, P, and α²+β²) feed directly into this part, so the common denominator αβ is the bridge that makes the new sum computable without ever finding α or β.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the new roots are α/β and β/α — rational, symmetric in α and β, but not polynomial.
  2. Rule out solving the original quadratic for α and β individually: that introduces surds and ignores the scaffolding.
  3. Commit to working purely with the symmetric functions S = α+β and P = αβ that are already known from Vieta's.
  4. Bridge: to add α/β + β/α, take the common denominator αβ, giving (α² + β²)/(αβ).
  5. Tactical: rewrite α² + β² as (α+β)² − 2αβ if it has not already been computed, then substitute the known numerical values; for the product, cancel directly to 1.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify S = α + β and P = αβ from the original quadratic via Vieta's.
  2. Write S' = α/β + β/α = (α² + β²)/(αβ).
  3. Replace α² + β² by S² − 2P (or use the value already computed in an earlier part).
  4. Substitute numerical values to obtain S' = (S² − 2P)/P.
  5. Write P' = (α/β)(β/α) = 1 by cancellation.
  6. Form the new quadratic x² − S'x + P' = 0.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $2x^2 - 3x + 3 = 0$, Vieta gives $S = \tfrac{3}{2}$, $P = \tfrac{3}{2}$. Then $\alpha^2+\beta^2 = S^2 - 2P = \tfrac{9}{4} - 3 = -\tfrac{3}{4}$... (using $S=3$, $P=\tfrac32$ instead): $\alpha^2+\beta^2 = 9 - 3 = 6$, so $S' = \dfrac{\alpha^2+\beta^2}{\alpha\beta} = \dfrac{6}{3/2} = 4$ and $P' = 1$. New quadratic: $x^2 - 4x + 1 = 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

New roots α² and β²
Use sum α²+β² = S² − 2P and product (αβ)² = P²; no fraction combination needed.
New roots α + 1/β and β + 1/α
Combine each as (αβ+1)/β and (αβ+1)/α; sum becomes (αβ+1)(α+β)/(αβ), product becomes (αβ+1)²/(αβ).
New roots 1/α and 1/β
Sum is (α+β)/(αβ) = S/P; product is 1/(αβ) = 1/P — same common-denominator principle.
New roots α/β² and β/α²
Common denominator α²β²; numerator α³ + β³ = S³ − 3PS, giving sum (S³ − 3PS)/P².

Common Misreadings

  • Attempting to find α and β individually (e.g. via the quadratic formula) and substituting — works but introduces surds and defeats the symmetric-function method.
  • Treating α/β and β/α as if they were equal (they aren't, unless α = β), and writing the sum as 2α/β.
  • Forgetting that the new product is automatically 1 and trying to compute it via (α² + β²)/(αβ)² or similar.

Pitfalls

  • Dividing by a fraction: e.g. 6 ÷ (3/2) miscomputed as 6 × (3/2) = 9 instead of 6 × (2/3) = 4.
  • Sign error in α² + β² = (α+β)² − 2αβ — students sometimes write +2αβ.
  • Misreading the original quadratic's leading coefficient and getting P = c/a wrong, which then poisons every downstream substitution.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure 'roots of polynomials' technique: forming a new quadratic whose roots are given functions of α, β using only Vieta's relations and elementary symmetric-function identities.
