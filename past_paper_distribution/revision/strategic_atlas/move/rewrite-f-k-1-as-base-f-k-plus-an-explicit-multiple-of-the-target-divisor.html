Audit note

The atlas has hypothesis-substitution and exponent-peeling as separate moves, but the deliberate strategic choice of which base to factor out—so that the remainder visibly contains the divisor—is the key decision point that students miss; it is the organising frame, not a sub-step.

Evidence: 8::Jun_2022::Q9, 8::Oct_2021::Q9, 8::Jun_2024::Q6, 8::Jan_2021::Q9, 8::Jun_2021::Q8, 8::Jan_2024::Q10

Trigger — When to use

The inductive step of a 'prove $f(n)$ is divisible by $d$' proof, where $f(n)$ is a sum of exponentials like $a^{pn+q}+b^{rn+s}$. You need $f(k+1)$ written so the hypothesis $f(k)$ can be substituted and the leftover is visibly a multiple of $d$.

Motivation — Why this works

Peeling one factor of the dominant base writes $f(k+1)=(\text{base})f(k)+(\text{base}-\text{other coefficient})\times(\text{remaining term})$; choosing the base so that the bracket is exactly $d$ makes both pieces divisible by $d$, completing the step.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Set $P(n)$: '$f(n)$ is divisible by $d$'; verify the base case $f(1)$ (or $f(0)$) is a multiple of $d$.
  2. State the hypothesis: assume $f(k)=dm$ for some integer $m$.
  3. Write $f(k+1)$ and increase each exponent by its per-step factor (e.g. $a^{p(k+1)+q}=a^p\cdot a^{pk+q}$).
  4. Decide WHICH base to factor out — usually one that makes $f(k)$ reappear cleanly.
  5. Add and subtract to form $(\text{base})f(k)$, leaving a remainder on the other exponential term.
  6. Force the remainder coefficient to equal $d$ (or a multiple), so it reads $d\times(\text{integer})$.
  7. Conclude $f(k+1)=(\text{base})\,dm + d(\dots)=d(\dots)$, hence divisible by $d$, and write the formal conclusion.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Expand exponents: e.g. $f(k+1)=2\cdot2^{k+2}+9\cdot3^{2k+1}$.
  2. Factor the chosen base over $f(k)$: $=2\big(2^{k+2}+3^{2k+1}\big)+(9-2)3^{2k+1}$.
  3. Identify the bracket as $f(k)$ and the leftover as a multiple of $d$: $=2f(k)+7\cdot3^{2k+1}$.
  4. Substitute $f(k)=dm$ if a numerical factor is wanted, or leave as $(\text{base})f(k)+d(\dots)$.
  5. Factor out $d$ explicitly: $=d\big(\dots\big)$, an integer multiple of $d$.

Worked Micro-Example

Prove $f(n)=2^{n+2}+3^{2n+1}$ is divisible by $7$. Then $f(k+1)=2\cdot2^{k+2}+9\cdot3^{2k+1}=2\big(2^{k+2}+3^{2k+1}\big)+7\cdot3^{2k+1}=2f(k)+7\cdot3^{2k+1}$. As $f(k)$ is a multiple of $7$ by hypothesis and $7\cdot3^{2k+1}$ is plainly divisible by $7$, $f(k+1)$ is divisible by $7$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two different bases, e.g. $f(n)=4^{n+2}+5^{2n+1}$ divisible by $21$.
Factor one base, then substitute $a^{k+2}=21m-5^{2k+1}$ from the hypothesis so the other base's coefficients combine to $21$: $f(k+1)=4\cdot21m+21\cdot5^{2k+1}=21(\dots)$.
Exponential plus linear/constant, e.g. $3^{2n+2}-8n-9$ divisible by $64$, or $4^n+6n-1$ divisible by $9$.
Pull out the single exponential base, substitute the hypothesis for that power, then collect the linear and constant terms — they will sum to a clean multiple of the divisor: $64(9M+k+1)$, $9(4m-2k+1)$.
Base case starts at $n=0$.
Verify $f(0)$ (e.g. $3^{2\cdot0+2}-9=0=64\times0$); $0$ counts as divisible, then proceed identically.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to compute $f(k+1)-f(k)$ directly instead of $f(k+1)-(\text{base})f(k)$, which fails to reveal a multiple of $d$.
  • Picking the wrong base to factor, so $f(k)$ never cleanly reappears and the remainder is not obviously divisible by $d$.
  • Confusing $2n$ with $2^n$ (or similar) when reading the recurrence/expression, derailing the index arithmetic.

Pitfalls

  • Index-arithmetic errors when raising exponents, e.g. miswriting $3^{2(k+1)+1}=9\cdot3^{2k+1}$.
  • Stopping at a form like $9m+9$ instead of factoring to $9(m+1)$ — the explicit multiple-of-$d$ form is required.
  • Failing to state the inductive hypothesis ($f(k)=dm$) before using it.
  • Omitting the formal conclusion referencing the base case and the principle of induction.
