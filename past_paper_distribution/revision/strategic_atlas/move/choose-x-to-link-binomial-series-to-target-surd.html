Trigger — When to use

Part (b) of a binomial-expansion question asks for a numerical approximation of a specific surd (e.g. $\sqrt{3}$, $\sqrt[3]{2}$, $\sqrt{1.01}$) using the series found in part (a). Cues include phrases like 'use your expansion to find an approximation for...' or 'by substituting a suitable value of $x$...'. The student must select an $x$ so that the exact closed form $f(x)$ collapses to an expression containing the target surd, while the truncated series gives a rational number.

Motivation — Why this works

A binomial expansion is valid as an approximation $f(x) \approx P(x)$ on a convergence interval. Substituting one value of $x$ into both sides produces an equation 'surd $\approx$ rational', which is exactly what a numerical approximation question wants. The choice of $x$ is forced by two constraints: it must lie inside the validity range so truncation error is small, and it must make the bracket inside $f$ evaluate to a number whose root is (a rational multiple of) the target surd.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that part (b) names a specific surd, so $x$ is not free — it must be reverse-engineered from $f(x)$.
  2. Rule out 'plug in any small $x$': a small $x$ gives accuracy but not necessarily the right surd; the surd dictates the choice.
  3. Commit to solving the equation 'inside-bracket of $f(x)$ = (something whose root is the target surd)' for $x$, then check $|x|$ lies in the validity range.
  4. Bridge: substitute that $x$ into the exact $f(x)$ to express it as a multiple of the target surd, and into the polynomial to get a rational number.
  5. Tactical: keep fractions exact (no decimals yet), then rearrange to isolate the surd at the very end and only then convert to a decimal.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the target surd in part (b) and the closed form $f(x)$ from part (a).
  2. Solve for $x$ so that $f(x)$ simplifies to a rational multiple of the target surd.
  3. Verify $x$ lies inside the convergence range (e.g. $|x|<2$ or $|x|<1$).
  4. Substitute $x$ into the truncated expansion to obtain a rational approximation.
  5. Equate the surd expression to the rational approximation.
  6. Rearrange to isolate the target surd; convert to a decimal at the final step.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $f(x)=(4-2x)^{-1/2} \approx \tfrac12+\tfrac{x}{8}+\tfrac{3x^2}{64}+\tfrac{5x^3}{256}$ for $|x|<2$. To approximate $\sqrt{3}$, set $4-2x=3 \Rightarrow x=\tfrac12$. Exact: $f(\tfrac12)=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt 3}$. Series: $\tfrac12+\tfrac{1}{16}+\tfrac{3}{256}+\tfrac{5}{2048}=\tfrac{1181}{2048}$. So $\sqrt 3\approx \tfrac{2048}{1181}\approx 1.7341$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Approximating $\sqrt{1.01}$ from $(1+x)^{1/2}$
Choose $x=0.01$ so the bracket is exactly $1.01$; no rearrangement needed beyond reading off the series value.
Approximating a cube root from $(1-x)^{1/3}$
Solve $1-x=$ (target cube) for $x$, check $|x|<1$, then equate as before.
Closed form $(a+bx)^{n}$ with $n$ negative or fractional
Factor out $a^{n}$ first to put the bracket as $(1+\tfrac{b}{a}x)^{n}$; the validity becomes $|\tfrac{b}{a}x|<1$, which restricts the admissible $x$.
Target surd appears in the denominator of $f(x)$
After substitution you get surd $\approx 1/\text{(rational)}$; invert at the end to isolate the surd.

Common Misreadings

  • Substituting into the polynomial only and reporting that decimal as the surd, without ever evaluating the exact form.
  • Confusing direction: if $f(x)\approx 1/\sqrt{3}$, writing $\sqrt 3\approx f(x)$ instead of $\sqrt 3\approx 1/f(x)$.
  • Treating $x$ as free and picking a 'nice' small value that does not actually produce the requested surd.

Pitfalls

  • Choosing $x$ outside the convergence range, making the truncated approximation unreliable.
  • Arithmetic slips combining fractions with denominators $16, 64, 256, 2048$ — losing a factor when finding a common denominator.
  • Rounding too early; converting each term to a decimal loses precision before the final inversion.
  • Forgetting to multiply by any constant pulled out front (e.g. $a^{n}$ from factoring $(a+bx)^{n}$) when reconstructing $f(x)$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 binomial-expansion topics: fractional/negative index expansions, validity range $|x|<1$ after factoring, and using the expansion to estimate roots and surds.
