Trigonometry

Rearrange arc-length formula to extract unknown radius

Family: R Formula Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a circular-arc problem supplies the arc length $s$ and the subtended angle $\theta$ (in radians) but leaves the radius $r$ unknown, and that radius is required for later parts (sector area, perimeter, chord, second arc). Common phrasings: 'arc AB has length ...', 'the angle AOB = ... rad', or a diagram labelling an arc and a central angle. Especially forced when no other equation in the problem mentions $r$.

Motivation — Why this works

The arc-length identity $s = r\theta$ (valid only with $\theta$ in radians) is the unique linear relation tying together arc, radius, and angle. With any two known, the third is a one-step rearrangement. Because radius typically gates every subsequent computation in a sector problem, extracting it first unlocks the rest of the question.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the radius is missing but both an arc length and a central angle (in radians) are given.
  2. Rule out chord-based approaches (cosine rule, $2r\sin(\theta/2)$) since the given length is along the curve, not a straight chord.
  3. Commit to $s = r\theta$ because it is the only A-level formula directly linking these three quantities.
  4. Confirm the angle is in radians (or convert if in degrees) before substituting.
  5. Rearrange to $r = s/\theta$ and compute, keeping an exact fraction if later parts demand precision.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Check $\theta$ is in radians; convert via $\theta_{\text{rad}} = \theta_{\deg}\times\pi/180$ if necessary.
  2. Write $s = r\theta$ and substitute the known values.
  3. Rearrange to $r = s/\theta$.
  4. Evaluate, retaining exact form (e.g. fraction) when downstream parts need it.
  5. Feed $r$ into any subsequent proportional or geometric relations stated in the question.

Worked Micro-Example

Arc $DC = 9$ m subtends angle $0.8$ rad at centre $O$. Find $OD$.\\ Using $s = r\theta$: $9 = OD \times 0.8$.\\ Hence $OD = 9/0.8 = 11.25$ m.\\ If additionally $AO = \tfrac{5}{8}OD$, then $AO = \tfrac{5}{8}(11.25) = 7.03125$ m.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Sector area $A$ and angle $\theta$ given, radius unknown
Use $A = \tfrac{1}{2}r^{2}\theta$ and rearrange to $r = \sqrt{2A/\theta}$.
Arc length and radius given, angle unknown
Rearrange $s = r\theta$ to $\theta = s/r$ (result in radians).
Angle supplied in degrees
Convert to radians first: $\theta_{\text{rad}} = \theta_{\deg}\pi/180$, then apply $s = r\theta$.
Perimeter of sector given instead of arc
Subtract the two radii first: $s = P - 2r$, then solve $P - 2r = r\theta$ for $r$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the given length as a chord rather than an arc and invoking $2r\sin(\theta/2)$.
  • Applying $s = r\theta$ with $\theta$ in degrees without converting.
  • Confusing arc length with sector area and using $A = \tfrac{1}{2}r^{2}\theta$ by mistake.

Pitfalls

  • Dividing in the wrong direction (computing $\theta/s$ instead of $s/\theta$).
  • Premature rounding of $r$ which then propagates error into later sector area or perimeter calculations.
  • Forgetting to attach units (metres) to the final radius.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P2/P3 radian measure and circular sectors); uses only $s = r\theta$ and $A = \tfrac{1}{2}r^{2}\theta$, both standard formulae.
