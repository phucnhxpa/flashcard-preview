Matrices

Use trace and determinant to reduce remaining eigenvalues to a quadratic

Family: Eigenvalue Shortcut Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires for a 3×3 matrix when one eigenvalue λ₁ is already known (given, guessed, or found from a stated eigenvector) and the other two are required. Also fires when the question explicitly asks for the remaining eigenvalues after verifying one. The full characteristic polynomial det(M − λI) = 0 would be a tedious cubic in λ. Cue phrases: 'show that … is an eigenvalue, hence find the others', or a problem that supplies one eigenvector then asks for the spectrum.

Motivation — Why this works

For any 3×3 matrix, tr(M) = λ₁ + λ₂ + λ₃ and det(M) = λ₁λ₂λ₃. Once λ₁ is known, the trace gives the sum S = λ₂ + λ₃ and the determinant gives the product P = λ₂λ₃. By Vieta's, λ₂ and λ₃ are then the roots of t² − St + P = 0, a single quadratic instead of a cubic. This replaces a symbolic 3×3 determinant expansion with one numerical determinant plus a quadratic solve.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that one eigenvalue λ₁ is already in hand, so the unknowns reduce from three to two.
  2. Rule out expanding det(M − λI) as a cubic in λ — algebraically heavy and sign-error prone.
  3. Commit to the trace/determinant route because two symmetric functions of the eigenvalues are exactly what's needed for two unknowns.
  4. Bridge: read tr(M) straight off the diagonal, then compute the single numerical det(M) by cofactor expansion along the easiest row.
  5. Tactically, divide det(M) by λ₁ for the product, subtract λ₁ from tr(M) for the sum, and assemble t² − St + P = 0.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Read off tr(M) from the diagonal entries and compute S = λ₂ + λ₃ = tr(M) − λ₁.
  2. Compute det(M) numerically by cofactor expansion along the row or column with the most zeros / smallest entries.
  3. Compute P = λ₂λ₃ = det(M)/λ₁.
  4. Form the quadratic t² − St + P = 0.
  5. Solve by factorisation or the quadratic formula to obtain λ₂ and λ₃.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $M$ have $\lambda_1 = -3$, with $\operatorname{tr}(M) = -17$ and $\det(M) = -156$. Then $\lambda_2 + \lambda_3 = -17 - (-3) = -14$ and $\lambda_2\lambda_3 = -156/(-3) = 52$. So $\lambda_2, \lambda_3$ satisfy $t^2 + 14t + 52 = 0$, giving $t = -7 \pm \sqrt{49 - 52} = -7 \pm i\sqrt{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

2×2 matrix with no eigenvalue known
Use t² − tr(M)·t + det(M) = 0 directly; both eigenvalues come from one quadratic with no prior root needed.
3×3 matrix with two eigenvalues known
The third follows from trace alone: λ₃ = tr(M) − λ₁ − λ₂. No determinant needed; use det as a check.
3×3 matrix where an eigenvector v is given but its eigenvalue is not
First compute Mv to read off λ₁ (since Mv = λ₁v), then proceed with the trace/determinant reduction.
Singular 3×3 matrix (det(M) = 0) with one nonzero eigenvalue known
Then 0 is an eigenvalue, so P = 0 and one of λ₂, λ₃ is 0; the other equals S = tr(M) − λ₁.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating det(M) as the sum of eigenvalues and tr(M) as the product — the two invariants swapped.
  • Thinking the method needs the full characteristic polynomial first, defeating the shortcut.
  • Assuming the quadratic must factor nicely and abandoning the method when it produces complex or irrational roots — these are still valid eigenvalues at this level.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors in the 3×3 cofactor expansion, especially with negative diagonal entries; check by expanding along a second row.
  • Forgetting the minus sign in t² − St + P (writing t² + St + P instead).
  • Dividing det(M) by the wrong known eigenvalue, or by 0 when λ₁ = 0.
  • Miscounting the trace by including off-diagonal entries.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure: 2×2 and 3×3 eigenvalue problems, characteristic equations, and the identities tr(M) = Σλᵢ, det(M) = Πλᵢ are all standard. Complex eigenvalues from the resulting quadratic are accepted in FP scope.
