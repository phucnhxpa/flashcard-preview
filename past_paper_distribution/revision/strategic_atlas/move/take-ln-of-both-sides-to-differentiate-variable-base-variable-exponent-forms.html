Strategic Atlas / Differentiation / Logarithmic Differentiation
Differentiation

Take ln of both sides to differentiate variable-base variable-exponent forms

Family: Logarithmic Differentiation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when y = f(x)^{g(x)} with BOTH base and exponent non-constant, e.g. y = x^{\sin x}, y = x^x, y = (\sin x)^{\cos x}, y = (\ln x)^x. The standard power rule fails (exponent is not constant) and the exponential rule a^x fails (base is not constant). Cue phrases: 'differentiate y = ...' where the exponent contains x. Also fires when an expression is a long product/quotient of powers whose derivative is easier after taking logs.

Motivation — Why this works

The log law \ln(a^b) = b\ln a converts a variable exponent into a product, which is differentiable by the product rule. Taking ln of both sides preserves the equation (for y > 0) and the LHS \ln y differentiates implicitly to (1/y)(dy/dx), so multiplying back by y recovers dy/dx in closed form. This bypasses the need to memorise a rule for f^g and reduces every such problem to standard A-level rules: log laws, product rule, chain rule, implicit differentiation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the function has form (variable)^{variable}, so neither d/dx(x^n) = nx^{n-1} nor d/dx(a^x) = a^x \ln a applies.
  2. Rules out direct power/exponential rules and rules out a clumsy chain rule attempt on f^g; considers but rejects rewriting as e^{g(x)\ln f(x)} since the Edexcel-standard method is logarithmic differentiation.
  3. Commits to taking ln of both sides because the log law will collapse the exponent into a product of two known differentiable pieces.
  4. Writes \ln y = g(x)\ln f(x), readying both sides for term-by-term differentiation.
  5. Plans: differentiate LHS implicitly to (1/y)\,dy/dx, apply product rule on RHS, then multiply through by y and substitute back y = f(x)^{g(x)}.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Take natural log of both sides: \ln y = \ln\bigl(f(x)^{g(x)}\bigr).
  2. Apply log law to bring exponent down: \ln y = g(x)\ln f(x).
  3. Differentiate both sides w.r.t. x; LHS becomes (1/y)(dy/dx) by implicit differentiation.
  4. Differentiate RHS by the product rule: g'(x)\ln f(x) + g(x)\cdot f'(x)/f(x).
  5. Multiply both sides by y and substitute y = f(x)^{g(x)} to express dy/dx explicitly in x.

Worked Micro-Example

Differentiate y = x^{\sin x}. Take ln: \ln y = \sin x\,\ln x. Differentiate: (1/y)(dy/dx) = \cos x\,\ln x + \sin x\cdot(1/x). Multiply by y: dy/dx = x^{\sin x}\bigl(\cos x\,\ln x + \tfrac{\sin x}{x}\bigr).

Variants — Same move, different surface

y = x^x
\ln y = x\ln x \Rightarrow (1/y)y' = \ln x + 1, so dy/dx = x^x(\ln x + 1).
y = (\sin x)^x
\ln y = x\ln(\sin x); product rule on RHS gives \ln(\sin x) + x\cot x, then multiply by y.
y = (\ln x)^{\,x}
\ln y = x\ln(\ln x); RHS derivative is \ln(\ln x) + 1/\ln x.
Heavy product/quotient e.g. y = \dfrac{(x+1)^3\sqrt{2x-1}}{(x^2+4)^2}
Take ln to convert into a sum of logs: \ln y = 3\ln(x+1) + \tfrac12\ln(2x-1) - 2\ln(x^2+4); differentiate term-by-term, then multiply by y.
Equivalent exponential rewrite y = e^{g(x)\ln f(x)}
Differentiate using chain rule directly; mathematically identical but logarithmic differentiation is the expected Edexcel presentation.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the exponent as a constant and applying the power rule, e.g. d/dx(x^{\sin x}) = \sin x\cdot x^{\sin x - 1}.
  • Treating the base as a constant and applying the exponential rule, e.g. d/dx(x^{\sin x}) = x^{\sin x}\ln x \cdot \cos x.
  • Trying a single chain rule pass on f^g without first taking logs or rewriting as an exponential.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting implicit differentiation on the LHS: writing d/dx(\ln y) = 1/y instead of (1/y)(dy/dx).
  • Failing to substitute y = f(x)^{g(x)} back at the end, leaving dy/dx in terms of y.
  • Mis-applying the product rule on g(x)\ln f(x), e.g. forgetting the f'(x)/f(x) factor from differentiating \ln f(x).
  • Using the log law incorrectly, e.g. writing \ln(x^{\sin x}) = \ln x \cdot \ln(\sin x).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 scope: relies only on log laws, product rule, chain rule, and implicit differentiation. Standard examiner expectation for any y = f(x)^{g(x)} question.
