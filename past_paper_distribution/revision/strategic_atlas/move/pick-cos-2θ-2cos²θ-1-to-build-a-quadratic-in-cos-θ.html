Trigonometry

Pick cos 2θ = 2cos²θ − 1 to build a quadratic in cos θ

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation contains cos 2θ together with cos θ (and no sin θ), e.g. cos θ + cos 2θ = 0, 3cos 2θ − cos θ + 2 = 0, or any polar/identity problem mixing first and second cosine harmonics. The cue is a single surviving simple trig term (cos θ) plus a double-angle cos 2θ. The aim is to reduce to one variable so standard quadratic factoring solves it.

Motivation — Why this works

cos 2θ has three equivalent forms; choosing 2cos²θ − 1 keeps everything in cos θ, matching the other term in the equation and producing a clean polynomial in cos θ. The other forms either introduce sin θ (cos²θ − sin²θ) or force a switch to sin θ (1 − 2sin²θ), neither of which simplifies when the rest of the equation is in cos θ. Identity selection is therefore driven by 'match the surviving variable'.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation mixes cos θ and cos 2θ — two harmonics, so it cannot be solved until reduced to one trig function.
  2. Rule out cos 2θ = cos²θ − sin²θ (introduces sin θ) and cos 2θ = 1 − 2sin²θ (forces conversion of cos θ to sin θ via Pythagoras, adding work and sign cases).
  3. Commit to cos 2θ = 2cos²θ − 1 because it preserves cos θ as the only trig function, yielding a quadratic in cos θ.
  4. Bridge: substitute and collect to standard form a·cos²θ + b·cos θ + c = 0, then factor or use the quadratic formula.
  5. Tactically, after solving for cos θ, check each root lies in [−1,1] and within the stated θ-domain before reporting answers.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify cos 2θ and replace it with 2cos²θ − 1.
  2. Collect into a quadratic in cos θ: a·cos²θ + b·cos θ + c = 0.
  3. Factor (or apply the quadratic formula) to find cos θ values.
  4. Discard any cos θ outside [−1, 1].
  5. Solve cos θ = k for θ in the given domain, taking all relevant principal and symmetric values.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\cos\theta + \cos 2\theta = 0$ for $0 \le \theta \le \tfrac{\pi}{2}$.\nReplace: $\cos\theta + (2\cos^2\theta - 1) = 0 \Rightarrow 2\cos^2\theta + \cos\theta - 1 = 0$.\nFactor: $(2\cos\theta - 1)(\cos\theta + 1) = 0$.\nSo $\cos\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}$ giving $\theta = \tfrac{\pi}{3}$, or $\cos\theta = -1$ giving $\theta = \pi$ (rejected, outside domain).\nAnswer: $\theta = \tfrac{\pi}{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation mixes sin θ and cos 2θ, e.g. sin θ + cos 2θ = 0.
Choose cos 2θ = 1 − 2sin²θ to obtain a quadratic in sin θ instead.
Equation mixes sin 2θ and cos θ (or sin θ).
Use sin 2θ = 2 sin θ cos θ and factor by grouping rather than forming a single-variable quadratic.
Equation has cos 2θ alone equated to a constant or to cos θ on both sides with sin θ also present.
Use cos 2θ = cos²θ − sin²θ together with sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 to eliminate one variable, then form the quadratic.
Polar curve r = 1 + cos θ intersected with another curve producing cos θ + cos 2θ = 0.
Same identity choice; after solving for θ, substitute back to compute r.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating cos 2θ as 2 cos θ (a linear scaling) rather than as a double-angle expression.
  • Selecting cos 2θ = cos²θ − sin²θ by default and then being unable to eliminate sin θ cleanly.
  • Believing sum-to-product is required; it works but produces half-angle equations that are harder to enumerate.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error: writing 2cos²θ + 1 or 1 − 2cos²θ instead of 2cos²θ − 1.
  • Forgetting to test each root of the quadratic against the given θ-domain.
  • Dropping the cos θ = −1 (or other) root prematurely without checking it lies in the domain.
  • Mis-factoring 2cos²θ + cos θ − 1; verify by expansion.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the standard double-angle identity cos 2θ = 2cos²θ − 1 and quadratic factoring, both within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 trigonometry.
