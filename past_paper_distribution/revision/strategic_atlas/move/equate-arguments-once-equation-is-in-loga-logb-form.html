Algebra & Functions

Equate arguments once equation is in log(A) = log(B) form

Family: Log Injectivity Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when both sides of the equation have been condensed into a single logarithm of the same base, e.g. log_b(A(x)) = log_b(B(x)). Typical cues: previous moves combined sums/differences of logs via product/quotient laws, or absorbed a constant k as log_b(b^k). Also fires when one side is a 'bare' constant that has just been rewritten as a log of the same base. The equation must be of the form 'one log = one log' with matching base before this move is legal.

Motivation — Why this works

The function log_b is strictly monotonic on (0, ∞), hence injective: log_b(A) = log_b(B) ⟹ A = B. This collapses the transcendental equation to a purely algebraic one (typically polynomial or rational), which can then be solved by standard A-level techniques. The preceding manipulation steps (combining logs, converting constants) exist solely to enable this injectivity step. Domain conditions A > 0 and B > 0 must still be checked at the end against any candidate roots.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices both sides are now single logarithms with the same base — the structural precondition for injectivity.
  2. Rules out further log manipulation (no more combining is needed) and rules out exponentiating both sides as base^(…), since that would give the same result more clumsily.
  3. Commits to dropping the logs because log_b is one-to-one on its domain, so the equation A = B is equivalent on the log-domain.
  4. Bridges to the algebraic equation by writing A(x) = B(x) explicitly and preparing to expand or rearrange.
  5. Decides tactically whether to expand fully, factor, or match a target form (e.g. divide through by a common factor to hit a 'show that' cubic).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Verify both sides are single logs with the same base: log_b(A) = log_b(B).
  2. Apply injectivity: drop the logs to obtain A = B.
  3. Expand and simplify both sides to a polynomial (or rational) equation.
  4. Rearrange to standard form (e.g. = 0) and divide out any common factor to match a required target.
  5. Flag the domain constraints A > 0 and B > 0 for later checking of candidate roots.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\log_3((2x-1)^3) = \log_3(9(14x-25))$.\nBy injectivity of $\log_3$: $(2x-1)^3 = 9(14x-25)$.\nExpand: $8x^3 - 12x^2 + 6x - 1 = 126x - 225$.\nRearrange: $8x^3 - 12x^2 - 120x + 224 = 0$, divide by 4: $2x^3 - 3x^2 - 30x + 56 = 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Exponential equation a^{f(x)} = a^{g(x)} with same base a > 0, a ≠ 1
By injectivity of the exponential, equate exponents: f(x) = g(x).
Different bases, e.g. log_2(A) = log_4(B)
First use change-of-base (or rewrite log_4 as ½log_2) so both sides share a base, then equate arguments.
ln(A) = ln(B)
Same move with natural log: A = B, subject to A, B > 0.
log_b(A) = c (a constant)
Rewrite c as log_b(b^c), then equate arguments to get A = b^c (equivalent to exponentiating both sides).
log_b(A) = log_b(B) + k
Absorb k as log_b(b^k) on the right, combine to a single log, then equate arguments.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating log_b(A) + log_b(B) = log_b(C) as if one could 'cancel logs' immediately, without first combining the LHS into log_b(AB).
  • Assuming log_b(A) = log_c(B) (different bases) permits dropping logs directly.
  • Reading log_b(A) = k as A = k rather than A = b^k.
  • Believing the move introduces extraneous solutions on its own, when in fact extraneous roots arise only from the domain conditions A, B > 0.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping logs when bases differ, producing an incorrect equation.
  • Forgetting to check A > 0 and B > 0 against candidate roots, accepting extraneous solutions.
  • Algebra slips on expansion, e.g. mis-cubing (2x-1)^3 or losing the factor 9 outside the bracket.
  • Failing to spot a common numerical factor and producing a cubic that doesn't match the 'show that' target form.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure (P2/P3) technique for solving logarithmic equations; relies only on log laws and the injectivity of log_b on (0, ∞).
