Differential Equations

Apply two boundary conditions to solve simultaneously for A and B

Family: Boundary Condition Resolution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a second-order ODE has been solved to its general form $y = \text{CF} + \text{PI}$ containing exactly two arbitrary constants $A$ and $B$, and the question supplies two conditions — typically $y(x_0) = k_1$ together with $y'(x_0) = k_2$, or two values $y(x_1)$ and $y(x_2)$. The cue phrases are 'given that', 'initially', 'when $x = 0$', or 'particular solution'. Whenever the count of unknowns equals the count of conditions, a 2×2 linear system is the closing move.

Motivation — Why this works

The general solution describes an entire family of curves; each boundary condition selects one constraint, collapsing one degree of freedom. Substituting a value of $x$ into $y$ or $y'$ converts the condition into a linear equation in $A$ and $B$. Two such equations uniquely pin down the family member that the problem demands, by the existence-and-uniqueness principle for linear ODEs with constant coefficients.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the general solution still contains two arbitrary constants and the question gives two pieces of data — this is the matching count for closure.
  2. I rule out trying to fix $A$ and $B$ from the ODE itself or from the PI alone; the constants only constrain via the supplied conditions.
  3. I commit to producing two linear equations in $A,B$ because each condition, once $x$ is plugged in, becomes algebraically linear.
  4. I begin by substituting the condition on $y$ first (it is usually the simpler equation), then differentiate $y$ in full to handle the condition on $y'$.
  5. Tactically I keep $A$ and $B$ symbolic until both equations are formed, then solve by elimination or substitution and report the particular solution explicitly.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the general solution $y = \text{CF} + \text{PI}$ clearly, identifying $A$ and $B$.
  2. Substitute $x = x_0$ and $y = k_1$ to obtain Equation (1) in $A$ and $B$.
  3. Differentiate the FULL general solution (CF and PI) to get $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$.
  4. Substitute $x = x_0$ and $y' = k_2$ into $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ to obtain Equation (2) in $A$ and $B$.
  5. Solve the simultaneous pair (1),(2) by elimination or substitution.
  6. Re-write $y$ with the numerical values of $A$ and $B$ to give the particular solution.

Worked Micro-Example

General solution: $y = Ae^{2x} + Be^{-x} + 3$, with $y(0)=4$ and $y'(0)=1$.\\ Condition 1: $A + B + 3 = 4 \Rightarrow A+B=1$.\\ Differentiate: $y' = 2Ae^{2x} - Be^{-x}$, so $2A - B = 1$.\\ Adding: $3A = 2 \Rightarrow A = \tfrac{2}{3},\ B = \tfrac{1}{3}$.\\ Particular solution: $y = \tfrac{2}{3}e^{2x} + \tfrac{1}{3}e^{-x} + 3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two-point boundary conditions, e.g. $y(0)=1$ and $y(\pi)=0$
Substitute each $x$-value directly into $y$ (no differentiation needed); the system is still 2×2 but trig/exp evaluations at the second point can be messier.
Condition given on $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ at a non-zero point
Differentiate first, then evaluate at the specified $x$; watch for non-trivial values of $e^{ax}$, $\sin$, $\cos$.
First-order ODE with one constant $A$ and one condition
Reduces to a single equation; same logic but no simultaneous solving required.
Coupled system reduced to a single second-order ODE in one variable
After finding the particular solution for one variable, back-substitute to find the second; conditions may apply to either variable.
Complex-root CF written as $e^{\alpha x}(A\cos\beta x + B\sin\beta x)$
Differentiation requires the product rule; substituting $x=0$ usually simplifies via $\cos 0=1,\ \sin 0=0$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $A$ and $B$ as if they could be read off from the PI rather than determined by the conditions.
  • Assuming the conditions apply to the CF only, ignoring the PI when substituting.
  • Confusing 'initial' with 'boundary' and applying both conditions at the same $x$ when the problem specifies two different $x$-values.
  • Believing that one condition suffices because the ODE is 'simple'.

Pitfalls

  • Differentiating only the CF and omitting the derivative of the PI when forming the equation in $y'$.
  • Arithmetic slips evaluating $e^0 = 1$, $\cos 0 = 1$, $\sin 0 = 0$ — particularly sign errors on $-Be^{-x}$ type terms.
  • Swapping which condition is for $y$ and which is for $y'$.
  • Forgetting to write the final particular solution after solving for $A,B$ — losing the presentation mark.
  • Algebraic errors when eliminating between the two equations, especially with fractions or surds.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope (FP2/FP3 second-order linear ODEs with constant coefficients). The 2×2 linear system is solved by elementary elimination — no matrix inversion required.
