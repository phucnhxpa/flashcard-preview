Factor out common exponential before evaluating definite-integral limits

Family: Strategic Factorisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

After completing integration by parts (often twice) on x^n e^{kx}, the antiderivative appears as a sum of several terms each carrying a factor of e^{kx}. Before substituting limits, scan the terms for a shared exponential factor and the smallest rational coefficient. The cue is multiple terms with identical e^{kx} and fractional coefficients like ½, ¼. This also fires for antiderivatives with shared sin(kx), cos(kx) or ln(x) factors across all terms.

Motivation — Why this works

Pulling out the smallest coefficient times e^{kx} leaves an integer-coefficient polynomial bracket, which is dramatically easier to evaluate at each limit and reduces the chance of arithmetic slips. The underlying principle is the distributive law: F(b) − F(a) = c·e^{kb}·P(b) − c·e^{ka}·P(a), where P is a polynomial. At x = 0 the exponential collapses to 1, so the answer often reduces to a clean form like c(e^{kb} − 1) or c(αe^{kb} − β).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice every term in my antiderivative shares the factor e^{kx}, and the coefficients are all rational multiples of a common base (e.g. ¼).
  2. I rule out substituting limits term-by-term because that means evaluating 2n separate sub-expressions and tracking signs across them.
  3. I commit to factorising because consolidating into c·e^{kx}·P(x) turns the limit substitution into two clean evaluations of one expression.
  4. I identify the smallest coefficient (here ¼) and pull out ¼e^{2x} so the bracket has integer coefficients only.
  5. Inside the bracket I check signs term-by-term and verify the polynomial expands back to the original sum before substituting limits.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the common exponential factor e^{kx} present in every term.
  2. Identify the smallest rational coefficient c so that factoring c·e^{kx} leaves integer coefficients.
  3. Rewrite the antiderivative as c·e^{kx}·P(x) where P(x) is an integer-coefficient polynomial.
  4. Expand mentally to verify the factorisation reproduces the original sum (sign-check).
  5. Substitute the upper limit, then the lower limit, into the single factored expression.
  6. Subtract and simplify, factoring further if a common factor like (e^{kb} − 1) appears.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_0^1 x^2 e^{2x}\,dx$. By parts twice gives antiderivative $\tfrac12 x^2 e^{2x} - \tfrac12 x e^{2x} + \tfrac14 e^{2x}$. Factor: $\tfrac14 e^{2x}(2x^2 - 2x + 1)$. At $x=1$: $\tfrac14 e^2(1)$; at $x=0$: $\tfrac14(1)$. Result: $\tfrac14(e^2 - 1)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Antiderivative of the form a·x·sin(kx) + b·cos(kx) after integrating x cos(kx) by parts
No single common factor across both terms; instead group by trig function and use cos(0)=1, sin(0)=0 to simplify the lower limit, then factor any shared rational coefficient at the end.
Antiderivative containing multiple terms all with ln(x), e.g. after integrating x ln(x) or x²ln(x) by parts
Factor out the highest power of x or the ln(x) term that appears in all relevant terms before substituting; remember ln(1)=0 makes the upper or lower limit collapse.
Antiderivative of x² e^{−kx} (decay)
Same factorisation strategy with c·e^{−kx}·P(x); be careful with signs of derivatives in the by-parts chain so the integer polynomial inside is correct.
Reduction-formula style result giving sum of e^{kx} terms with growing coefficients
Factor the smallest coefficient × e^{kx}; the resulting polynomial bracket often telescopes when limits are inserted.

Common Misreadings

  • Substituting limits term-by-term without factoring — correct in principle but error-prone, especially with multiple ½ and ¼ coefficients.
  • Treating the factorisation as an algebraic requirement rather than a tactical simplification, leading students to skip it when factoring looks awkward.
  • Believing the factorisation must produce a 'nice' polynomial; settling for fractional coefficients inside the bracket and losing the benefit.

Pitfalls

  • Pulling out ½e^{2x} instead of ¼e^{2x}, leaving fractional coefficients inside the bracket and partially defeating the purpose.
  • Sign error when combining the second by-parts result with the first — the minus sign must distribute through every term before factoring.
  • Forgetting that e^{0} = 1 (not 0) when substituting the lower limit, leading to dropped constants.
  • Misexpanding to verify: writing 2x² − 2x + 1 when the correct bracket should be checked against the original three terms term-by-term.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully inside Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P3/P4) and Further Pure: integration by parts on x^n e^{kx}, x^n sin/cos(kx), x^n ln(x), and definite integrals over standard intervals like [0,1] or [0,π/2].
