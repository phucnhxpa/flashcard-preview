Algebra & Functions

Reduce improper rational function via polynomial long division before partial fractions

Family: Improper Fraction Reduction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a rational function $N(x)/D(x)$ has $\deg N \ge \deg D$ and the task requires partial fractions, integration, or expansion. Concrete cues include a target form like $Ax + B + \tfrac{P}{x+\alpha} + \tfrac{Q}{x+\beta}$, an instruction to 'express in the form...', or a question asking to integrate/expand a rational function whose numerator degree is at least as large as the denominator's. Alternative phrasing: 'write as a polynomial plus partial fractions', 'divide out first', or 'reduce to proper form'.

Motivation — Why this works

Partial fraction decomposition is only valid for proper rational functions, because the assumed form $\sum \tfrac{A_i}{(x-r_i)^{k}}$ tends to $0$ as $|x|\to\infty$, while an improper fraction does not. Polynomial long division produces a unique identity $N(x) = Q(x)D(x) + R(x)$ with $\deg R < \deg D$, so $N/D = Q + R/D$ where $R/D$ is now proper and decomposable. Skipping this step forces the partial-fraction identity to be inconsistent at large $x$, yielding contradictory simultaneous equations.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the degree comparison: $\deg(\text{numerator}) \ge \deg(\text{denominator})$, flagging the fraction as improper.
  2. Rule out direct partial fractions — the standard template $\tfrac{A}{x+a}+\tfrac{B}{x+b}$ cannot represent a function that grows like a polynomial.
  3. Commit to long division (or the equivalent identity-matching method) to peel off the polynomial part first.
  4. Set up the division carefully, inserting $0x^k$ placeholders for any missing powers in the dividend to keep columns aligned.
  5. Anticipate the form of the quotient from the degree difference: difference $d$ gives a degree-$d$ quotient, then decompose the remainder over the factored denominator.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Confirm $\deg N \ge \deg D$ and, if needed, factor $D(x)$ for the later partial-fraction stage.
  2. Write the dividend with all powers present, using $0x^k$ for missing terms.
  3. Divide leading term of current dividend by leading term of divisor to get the next quotient term.
  4. Multiply the divisor by that quotient term and subtract from the current dividend, taking care with signs.
  5. Repeat until the remainder has degree strictly less than $\deg D$.
  6. Write $\dfrac{N(x)}{D(x)} = Q(x) + \dfrac{R(x)}{D(x)}$ and apply partial fractions to $R(x)/D(x)$ only.

Worked Micro-Example

Express $\dfrac{2x^3 - 4x^2 + 15}{(x+1)(x+2)}$ in the form $Ax+B+\dfrac{P}{x+1}+\dfrac{Q}{x+2}$. Divide $2x^3+0x^2\cdots$ wait — divide $2x^3 - 4x^2 + 0x + 15$ by $x^2+3x+2$: first quotient term $2x$ gives remainder $-10x^2 - 4x + 15$; next term $-10$ gives remainder $26x + 35$. So $f(x) = 2x - 10 + \dfrac{26x+35}{(x+1)(x+2)}$, and the proper part decomposes as $\dfrac{9}{x+1} + \dfrac{17}{x+2}$ (after the standard cover-up step).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quartic over quadratic, e.g. $\dfrac{x^4+1}{x^2-1}$
Degree difference $2$ produces a quadratic quotient $Ax^2+Bx+C$; long division runs for three stages before the remainder becomes linear or constant.
Numerator degree equals denominator degree
Quotient is a single constant; one division step suffices, after which the proper remainder is decomposed normally.
Repeated linear factor in denominator, e.g. $\dfrac{x^3}{(x-1)^2}$
Divide first, then decompose the remainder using the template $\tfrac{A}{x-1}+\tfrac{B}{(x-1)^2}$.
Coefficient-matching alternative
Write $N(x) \equiv (Ax+B)D(x) + (Cx+D)$ (or appropriate templates), expand, and equate coefficients — avoids long-division layout but still requires the proper-form template.
Improper integrand in integration question
Same reduction is required before integrating term-by-term using $\int \tfrac{1}{x+a}\,dx = \ln|x+a|+c$.

Common Misreadings

  • Applying partial fractions directly to the improper fraction and obtaining an inconsistent system (often a contradiction like $0 = $ nonzero).
  • Misreading the target form $Ax+B+\cdots$ as merely a stylistic choice rather than a signal that division is required.
  • Omitting the $0x$ placeholder in the dividend, causing misaligned subtraction.
  • Treating the quotient $Q(x)$ as if it should also be split into partial-fraction-like terms.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when subtracting the product at each stage, especially with negative leading coefficients.
  • Halting division prematurely while the remainder still has degree $\ge \deg D$.
  • Forgetting to factor the denominator before the partial-fraction stage on the proper remainder.
  • Carrying the polynomial quotient into the cover-up step and contaminating the constants $P,Q$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL C34/P3–P4 partial fractions and integration; denominators are products of distinct or repeated linear factors (and at most one irreducible quadratic in Further Pure), so long division stays at GCSE/AS algebraic manipulation level.
