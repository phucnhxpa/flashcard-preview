Trigonometry

Substitute to map part (b) onto part (a)'s proven identity

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in multi-part structured questions where part (b) starts with 'Hence' (or 'Hence solve') and contains a trig expression structurally parallel to the identity/result proved in part (a), but with shifted or scaled arguments. Concrete cues: same coefficients and same trig functions appear in both parts; arguments differ by a constant offset (e.g. x − 10° vs x + 20°, both 30° apart) or by a constant scale factor (e.g. 4θ = 2(2θ), or 6θ = 2(3θ)). Phrased differently: 'part (b)'s trig arguments can be relabelled by a single substitution θ = (something in x) so that part (a)'s LHS appears verbatim'. The 'hence' keyword is itself a strong textual trigger that an earlier result must be redeployed.

Motivation — Why this works

A 'hence' instruction tells you the examiner has engineered part (b) so that part (a)'s identity, once you make the right substitution, immediately collapses the messy expression into something elementary (e.g. tan θ = k or a single linear trig equation). The underlying principle is that any proven identity in a variable x holds for any expression substituted for x, so renaming x → f(θ) is a free move that transports the identity into a new context. This avoids re-deriving compound-angle or double-angle expansions from scratch, which is algebraically intensive and error-prone. Spotting the substitution is the intended efficiency: it converts a hard equation into a one-line application of part (a).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the word 'hence' and check that the trig structures in (a) and (b) share the same coefficients and the same functions (sin/cos pairing).
  2. Compare the arguments: is part (b)'s argument a constant multiple of part (a)'s (suggesting x = kθ), or do part (b)'s two arguments differ by the same offset that appeared in part (a) (suggesting a shift substitution)?
  3. Rule out brute-force expansion (compound-angle on every term) — it works but wastes time and ignores the 'hence' signal; rule out treating (b) as independent.
  4. Commit to the substitution that makes the simpler argument in (b) match the bare variable in (a)'s identity; this fixes the substitution direction uniquely.
  5. Execute: write θ = (the chosen expression in x), verify that the OTHER argument in (b) becomes the corresponding shifted/scaled argument from (a), and then quote part (a)'s conclusion directly.
  6. Finally, transfer back: replace θ in the answer by its expression in x and solve the resulting elementary equation over the stated domain, remembering to translate the domain through the substitution.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify part (a)'s identity in the form LHS(x) ≡ RHS(x).
  2. Choose substitution: let x = g(θ) so that LHS(g(θ)) matches the expression appearing in part (b). Typical choices: x = 2θ (scale) or x = θ + c / θ = x + c (shift).
  3. Substitute throughout the identity to obtain LHS(g(θ)) ≡ RHS(g(θ)) — this is the bridge identity.
  4. Replace the corresponding sub-expression in part (b)'s equation by RHS(g(θ)), reducing it to a simple equation (often tan(g(θ)) = k).
  5. Solve for g(θ) over the domain induced by the original range, then unpick g to recover θ (or x).

Worked Micro-Example

Part (a) proved $\dfrac{\sin 2x}{1+\cos 2x} \equiv \tan x$. Part (b): solve $\dfrac{\sin 4\theta}{1+\cos 4\theta} = \sqrt{3}$ for $\theta \in [0°,180°]$. Set $x = 2\theta$: the identity becomes $\dfrac{\sin 4\theta}{1+\cos 4\theta} \equiv \tan 2\theta$. Hence $\tan 2\theta = \sqrt{3}$, so $2\theta = 60°, 240°$, giving $\theta = 30°, 120°$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Scale substitution when part (b) has arguments k× those in part (a)
Let x = kθ (e.g. x = 2θ when (b) shows 4θ and (a) shows 2x); the identity carries through unchanged.
Shift substitution when part (b) has two arguments offset by the same constant as in (a)
Let θ = x + c (where x + c is the 'bare' argument in (b)); verify the other argument becomes θ − (offset).
R-formula linkage: (a) writes a sin θ + b cos θ as R sin(θ+α); (b) gives a shifted version
Substitute φ = θ + (shift) so the (b) expression matches (a)'s form, then quote R and α.
Identity reused inside an integral with u = f(x)
Same logic in integration: the proven identity becomes the integrand after the substitution u = f(x).

Common Misreadings

  • Ignoring 'hence' and re-expanding sin/cos of compound or multiple angles from scratch — correct but wasteful and error-prone.
  • Treating part (b) as an independent problem and missing the structural parallel entirely.
  • Spotting that a substitution is needed but choosing the wrong direction (e.g. matching the harder argument instead of the simpler one), so part (a)'s exact form fails to appear.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to translate the domain: if θ ∈ [0°,180°], then 2θ ∈ [0°,360°] (or x + 20° ∈ [20°,200°]), and ALL solutions in the new range must be found before unpicking.
  • Forgetting domain restrictions inherited from part (a) (e.g. cos x ≠ 0 becomes cos 2θ ≠ 0), which can exclude otherwise-valid θ.
  • Substituting in only one place — the substitution must be applied to every occurrence of the variable in the identity.
  • Arithmetic slip when unpicking: e.g. solving tan(x + 20°) = k and forgetting to subtract 20° from each principal solution.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 and Further Pure scope. Invokes double-angle and compound-angle identities from the formula booklet (sin 2x, cos 2x, sin(A±B), cos(A±B)). Avoid generalising to substitutions that change the nature of the identity (e.g. complex substitutions or non-linear g(θ) beyond simple scaling/shifting), which are out of scope.
