Coordinate Geometry

Parameterise quadratic by roots and fix scale via a third point

Family: Root Form Parameterisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a quadratic's two roots (or x-intercepts) are known and one additional point on the curve is given — typically the turning point, the y-intercept, or any labelled coordinate. Cues include phrases like 'the curve crosses the x-axis at x = … and x = …' together with 'passes through' or 'has minimum/maximum at'. Also fires when a sketch shows two x-intercepts plus one extra marked point. The task is to find the full equation y = ax² + bx + c or an equivalent factored form.

Motivation — Why this works

Any quadratic with roots r₁ and r₂ can be written y = A(x − r₁)(x − r₂), where A is the single unknown leading coefficient controlling vertical scale and orientation. This collapses the three-unknown problem (a, b, c) to a one-unknown problem, so a single substitution of the third point yields a linear equation in A. It exploits the factor theorem directly and avoids the algebraic overhead of solving a 3×3 system.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice two roots are given, so (x − r₁) and (x − r₂) must be factors of the quadratic.
  2. I rule out starting from y = ax² + bx + c because that would force me to set up three simultaneous equations from three points — wasteful when factors are already visible.
  3. I commit to y = A(x − r₁)(x − r₂) because it encodes both roots automatically and leaves only A to determine.
  4. To pin down A, I bridge to the third known point (turning point, y-intercept, or any other given coordinate) and substitute it into the factored form.
  5. Tactically, I evaluate each bracket numerically before multiplying to minimise sign errors, then divide to isolate A and write the final equation with A explicit.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two roots r₁, r₂ and the third known point (x₀, y₀).
  2. Write y = A(x − r₁)(x − r₂).
  3. Substitute (x₀, y₀): y₀ = A(x₀ − r₁)(x₀ − r₂).
  4. Evaluate each bracket carefully (watch signs), then solve the resulting linear equation for A.
  5. Write the final equation y = A(x − r₁)(x − r₂), expanding only if the question requires y = ax² + bx + c form.

Worked Micro-Example

A curve has x-intercepts at x = 4 and x = 10 and turning point (7, −18). Write y = A(x − 4)(x − 10). Substitute (7, −18): −18 = A(3)(−3) = −9A, so A = 2. Therefore y = 2(x − 4)(x − 10), or equivalently y = 2x² − 28x + 80.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Turning point given but roots unknown
Switch to vertex form y = A(x − h)² + k and substitute any other known point (e.g. a root or y-intercept) to find A.
Cubic with three known roots plus one extra point
Use y = A(x − r₁)(x − r₂)(x − r₃) and substitute the extra point to solve for A.
Repeated root (curve touches x-axis at x = r) plus one extra point
Use y = A(x − r)² and substitute the extra point to find A.
Only y-intercept given as the third point
Substitute x = 0 directly: y₀ = A(−r₁)(−r₂) = A·r₁·r₂, then solve for A.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming A = 1 because the roots alone seem to determine the quadratic — forgetting the vertical scaling factor.
  • Confusing 'turning point' with 'root' and trying to set y = 0 at the vertex.
  • Treating the factored form as the final answer when the question explicitly asks for y = ax² + bx + c.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when evaluating brackets like (7 − 10) = −3, producing the wrong sign for A.
  • Forgetting to include A in the final equation, leaving y = (x − r₁)(x − r₂).
  • Expanding prematurely before solving for A, which reintroduces the very algebra the move is designed to avoid.
  • Mis-identifying a y-intercept as a point of the form (x, 0) instead of (0, y).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1) — quadratic functions, factor theorem, and curve sketching. Extends naturally to cubics in P2/P3 using the same parameterisation principle.
