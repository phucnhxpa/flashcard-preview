Vectors

Parametrise point on line and square distance to solve for parameter

Family: Parametric Distance Equation Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

An unknown point E must lie on a given line r = a + λd AND satisfy a fixed-distance condition |PE| = k from a known point P (often where P itself sits on the line, or is the foot of perpendicular). Visual cues: 'find the point(s) on l such that the distance from … is …', 'find E on the line with PE = …'. Equivalent phrasings: 'where does the line meet a sphere of radius k centred at P', 'find points on l equidistant from P at distance k'. The hallmark is one geometric constraint (distance) plus one positional constraint (on the line).

Motivation — Why this works

Any point on r = a + λd has position vector entirely controlled by the single scalar λ, so the displacement from a reference point on the line reduces to λd. Then |PE|² = λ²|d|², collapsing a three-coordinate distance equation into a single-variable quadratic in λ. Squaring removes the square root, and the identity |λd|² = λ²|d|² (from the orthogonality of i, j, k components) does the heavy lifting. The ± from the square root naturally yields the two points on the line equidistant from P, one on each side.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice E is constrained two ways: it lies on a known line (one parameter of freedom) and is at a fixed distance from a known point P (one scalar equation). One unknown, one equation — perfect.
  2. Rule out setting up three simultaneous coordinate equations or solving geometrically with projections; those over-complicate a problem that has only one degree of freedom.
  3. Commit to the parametric route: write E = a + λd so every coordinate of E is linear in λ.
  4. Choose the reference origin for the displacement carefully — if P is itself on the line (e.g. P = a, or the foot of perpendicular), then PE = λd directly, and |PE|² = λ²|d|² without cross terms.
  5. Square the distance constraint to avoid surds: set λ²|d|² = k², giving λ² = k²/|d|² and λ = ±k/|d|.
  6. Recognise the ± gives two points; substitute each value of λ back into a + λd to get the coordinates of both candidate points E.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the general point on l as E = a + λd, with coordinates linear in λ.
  2. Form PE = E − P. If P lies on l at parameter 0, this simplifies to PE = λd.
  3. Compute |PE|² = λ²|d|² (or expand component-wise if P is off the line).
  4. Set |PE|² equal to the squared target distance k², obtaining a quadratic (or pure square) in λ.
  5. Solve for λ — keep both signs.
  6. Substitute each λ back into E = a + λd to obtain the coordinate(s) of E.

Worked Micro-Example

Line $l: \mathbf{r} = (1,5,2) + t(-5,4,3)$, with $P=(1,5,2)$ on $l$. Find $E$ on $l$ with $|PE| = 2\sqrt5$. Then $\vec{PE} = t(-5,4,3)$, so $|PE|^2 = t^2(25+16+9) = 50t^2$. Setting $50t^2 = 20$ gives $t^2 = \tfrac{2}{5}$, so $t = \pm\sqrt{2/5}$. Substituting: $E = (1\mp\sqrt{10},\, 5\pm\tfrac{4\sqrt{10}}{5},\, 2\pm\tfrac{3\sqrt{10}}{5})$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line meets a sphere |r − c|² = R²
Substitute the parametric line into the sphere equation; expanding gives a quadratic in λ — same parametric-substitution principle, with cross terms when c is not on the line.
Reference point P is NOT on the line
PE = (a − P) + λd, so |PE|² expands into a full quadratic Aλ² + Bλ + C = k², not a pure square.
Constraint is perpendicularity instead of distance (e.g. AE ⊥ AP)
Replace |PE|² = k² with the dot-product equation AE · AP = 0; still a single linear/quadratic equation in λ.
2D coordinate geometry: line meets a circle
Same parametric substitution into (x−h)² + (y−k)² = r² yields a quadratic in the parameter.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating E's three coordinates as three independent unknowns and writing three simultaneous equations rather than recognising the one-parameter structure.
  • Confusing 'distance from P' with 'distance along the line from a' — these coincide only when P is the basepoint a.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the ± when taking the square root of λ², thus reporting only one point E when two exist.
  • Sign errors when squaring negative direction-vector components (e.g. (−5)² written as −25).
  • Squaring the target distance incorrectly: |PE|² = (2√5)² = 20, not 2√5; similarly (2√2)² = 8.
  • Simplification slips with surds: 5√(2/5) = √(25·2/5) = √10, not √(10/5).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 vectors: uses |v|² = v·v and the parametric form r = a + λd from the formula booklet. No need for Lagrange multipliers, calculus minimisation, or any 3D geometry beyond standard dot-product methods.
