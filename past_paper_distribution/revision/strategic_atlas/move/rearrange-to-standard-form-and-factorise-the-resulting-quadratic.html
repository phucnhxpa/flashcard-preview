Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation reduces (after expansion or cancellation) to a monic quadratic $x^2+bx+c=0$ with small integer coefficients. Strong cues: leading coefficient $1$, modest $|c|$ with few factor pairs, and $b,c\in\mathbb{Z}$. Also triggers when a problem says 'show that' or 'hence solve' after rearrangement, signalling that clean integer roots are intended. Equivalent phrasings: 'solve by factorising', 'find the roots', 'hence determine $x$'.

Motivation — Why this works

For a monic quadratic, $x^2+bx+c=(x-p)(x-q)$ where $p+q=-b$ and $pq=c$, so finding two integers with the right sum and product yields the roots immediately. This is faster and less error-prone than the quadratic formula when such a pair exists, and avoids surd arithmetic. Trying factorisation first is rational because integer-root quadratics are common in exam settings, and the search is short when $|c|$ is small.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation, once expanded and collected, becomes a monic quadratic with smallish integer constant — a strong factorisation candidate.
  2. Rule out the quadratic formula and completing the square as overkill: both introduce extra arithmetic when integer roots are likely available.
  3. Commit to factorisation by scanning factor pairs of $c$ for one summing to $b$; if the pair exists, the roots come out exactly with no surds.
  4. Bridge: rearrange the original equation into $x^2+bx+c=0$ form, then list factor pairs of $c$ in a controlled order.
  5. Tactically, fix signs first — if $c<0$ the factors have opposite signs; if $c>0$ they share the sign of $b$ — then test pairs by sum until one matches $b$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Expand and collect like terms to produce $x^2+bx+c=0$.
  2. Determine the sign pattern of the factor pair from the signs of $b$ and $c$.
  3. List factor pairs of $|c|$ and test which sums to $b$.
  4. Write the factorisation $(x-p)(x-q)=0$.
  5. Apply the zero-product principle to read off $x=p$ and $x=q$.
  6. If no integer pair works, fall back to the quadratic formula or completing the square.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $x^2+3x+27=5x+75$.\\ Rearrange: $x^2-2x-48=0$.\\ Need integers with product $-48$ and sum $-2$: take $-8$ and $+6$.\\ Factorise: $(x-8)(x+6)=0$.\\ Hence $x=8$ or $x=-6$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Non-monic quadratic $ax^2+bx+c=0$ with $a\neq 1$
Use the AC-method: find factors of $ac$ summing to $b$, split the middle term, then factor by grouping.
Disguised quadratic, e.g. $e^{2x}-2e^{x}-48=0$ or $u-2\sqrt{u}-48=0$
Substitute $y=e^x$ (or $y=\sqrt{u}$) to reduce to $y^2-2y-48=0$, factorise, then back-substitute and check domain (e.g. $y>0$).
Quadratic in trig, e.g. $\sin^2\theta-2\sin\theta-48k=0$ style after manipulation
Let $s=\sin\theta$, factorise in $s$, then solve $\sin\theta=p$ within the given range.
Discriminant is not a perfect square
Abandon factorisation and switch to the quadratic formula or completing the square; surd roots are expected.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the equation as already in standard form and factorising before collecting terms from both sides.
  • Assuming integer roots must exist and persisting with factor-pair search when the discriminant is non-square.
  • Confusing the sign rule: with $c<0$ the factors have opposite signs, not the same.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when $c<0$: forgetting one factor must be positive and the other negative.
  • Arithmetic slips while collecting like terms during the rearrangement step.
  • Reflexively applying the quadratic formula and mismanaging the discriminant.
  • Forgetting to set the equation to zero before factorising (e.g. factorising $x^2-2x=48$ directly).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure content (P1): solving quadratics by factorisation, with the formula and completing the square as named alternatives. The disguised-quadratic variants connect to exponentials/logarithms (P2) and trigonometric equations (P2/P3) within scope.
