Trigonometry

Linearise cos²θ or sin²θ via the half-angle identity before integrating

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an integrand contains cos²θ or sin²θ (often after expanding a bracket like (a + cosθ)² or (a − sinθ)²). These squared trig terms have no entry in the standard antiderivative table, so they must be linearised. A clear cue is seeing cos²(kθ) or sin²(kθ) sitting beside terms that are already directly integrable. Also fires when computing areas, arc lengths, or mean values that produce a squared sine or cosine.

Motivation — Why this works

The double-angle identity cos2θ = 2cos²θ − 1 rearranges to cos²θ = ½(1 + cos2θ), and similarly sin²θ = ½(1 − cos2θ). This converts a squared trig function into a constant plus a single cosine of a multiple angle — both directly integrable. The move trades a non-elementary form for a sum of standard forms, so the integration becomes term-by-term and routine.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand contains cos²θ (or sin²θ) — a squared trig term that is not on the standard integration list.
  2. Rule out substitution u = cosθ (would need a sinθ dθ factor, which isn't here) and integration by parts (overkill for a polynomial-in-cos integrand).
  3. Commit to the half-angle identity because it converts the squared term into directly integrable pieces without introducing new variables.
  4. Substitute cos²θ = ½(1 + cos2θ), then collect constants and cosine terms before integrating.
  5. Watch the chain factor when integrating cos2θ: ∫cos2θ dθ = ½sin2θ, so a coefficient of ½ becomes ¼sin2θ.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the squared trig term: cos²θ or sin²θ.
  2. Replace using cos²θ = ½(1 + cos2θ) or sin²θ = ½(1 − cos2θ).
  3. Distribute and collect constant terms with any other constants in the integrand.
  4. Integrate term-by-term, dividing by the inner coefficient when integrating cos(kθ) or sin(kθ).
  5. Add +C (indefinite) or apply limits (definite).

Worked Micro-Example

Compute $\int (2+\cos\theta)^2\,d\theta$. Expand: $4 + 4\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta$. Replace $\cos^2\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}(1+\cos 2\theta)$ to get $\tfrac{9}{2} + 4\cos\theta + \tfrac{1}{2}\cos 2\theta$. Integrate: $\tfrac{9\theta}{2} + 4\sin\theta + \tfrac{1}{4}\sin 2\theta + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Integrand contains sin²θ
Use sin²θ = ½(1 − cos2θ); note the minus sign on the cos2θ term.
Integrand contains cos²(kθ) or sin²(kθ)
Use cos²(kθ) = ½(1 + cos2kθ); when integrating, divide by 2k, giving sin(2kθ)/(4k).
Higher even power like cos⁴θ
Apply the identity once to get [½(1+cos2θ)]², expand, then apply the identity again to the resulting cos²2θ term.
Mixed product such as sin²θ cos²θ
Either apply both half-angle identities, or use sinθcosθ = ½sin2θ first to get ¼sin²2θ, then linearise.
Definite integral for area/volume of revolution
Same linearisation; substitute limits after integrating, taking care that sin(2θ) at standard limits like π or 2π vanishes.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating cos²θ as if it integrates to cos³θ/3 (confusing it with a power-of-x rule).
  • Trying u-substitution u = cosθ without a matching sinθ dθ factor.
  • Confusing cos²θ = ½(1+cos2θ) with cos2θ = 2cos²θ (forgetting the −1).
  • Reading cos²θ as cos(2θ) — they are different functions.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error: writing sin²θ = ½(1 + cos2θ) instead of ½(1 − cos2θ).
  • Forgetting the chain factor when integrating cos2θ: ∫½cos2θ dθ = ¼sin2θ, not ½sin2θ.
  • Failing to combine the new constant ½ with existing constants before integrating.
  • Mis-applying the identity to cosθ² that was meant as cos(θ²) (rare, but check notation).

A-level Scope Notes

Half-angle/double-angle identities and integration of cos(kθ), sin(kθ) are core Edexcel IAL Pure content (P3/P4); this move is the standard route for integrating squared trig functions in area, volume, and arc-length problems.
