Coordinate Geometry

Substitute one relation into another to collapse a two-variable system to one variable

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever you have two equations in two unknowns where one equation is already (or trivially) solved for one variable in terms of the other. Typical visual cues: a line given as y = mx + c paired with a circle/conic equation; a directrix or focus condition giving e in terms of a (or vice versa) alongside the structural identity b² = a²(e² ± 1); any pairing of a 'simple' linear/explicit relation with a 'complicated' quadratic/polynomial relation. In short: when one equation is a clean isolation and the other is a degree-2 (or higher) constraint, substitute the clean one into the messy one.

Motivation — Why this works

Substitution converts a 2-variable simultaneous system into a single-variable polynomial equation, which can then be solved by standard quadratic/polynomial techniques. It works because any point satisfying both equations must in particular satisfy the messy one when the explicit relation is enforced — substitution simply enforces that constraint algebraically. Choosing which variable to eliminate is a tactical decision: keep the variable you ultimately want as your unknown, and substitute away the other. This is the 'bridge' that links a geometric simultaneity problem to ordinary algebra.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the system has two equations in two unknowns and observe that one equation is already (or easily) solved for one variable — e.g. y = 3x − 1, or e = 3a/4.
  2. Rule out alternatives: full elimination by addition/subtraction would mix degrees badly; rearranging the messy equation first doubles the algebra; expanding before substituting is wasteful.
  3. Decide which variable to eliminate. Prefer to eliminate the one NOT asked for in the final answer, so the resulting single-variable equation is in your target unknown.
  4. Commit: replace every occurrence of the eliminated variable in the messy equation with its expression from the clean equation. Use brackets to avoid sign errors.
  5. Anticipate the degree of the result: substituting a linear into a quadratic gives a quadratic; substituting a linear in a into a²(e² − 1) gives a quartic in a. Knowing the expected degree is a sanity check.
  6. Simplify the resulting single-variable equation, solve it, then back-substitute into the clean equation to recover the eliminated variable.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Label the two equations (1) and (2); identify (1) as the explicit/isolated one.
  2. From (1), write the eliminated variable as an explicit expression, e.g. y = 3x − 1 or e = 3a/4.
  3. In (2), replace every occurrence of the eliminated variable with that expression, keeping it in brackets.
  4. Simplify any small inner cancellations BEFORE expanding (e.g. (3x − 1) + 1 = 3x).
  5. Expand and collect terms to obtain a single polynomial equation in the remaining variable.
  6. Solve the polynomial (factorise / quadratic formula); back-substitute solutions into (1) to obtain the paired values.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the intersections of $y = 3x - 1$ with $(x-2)^2 + (y+1)^2 = 10$. Substitute: $(x-2)^2 + (3x-1+1)^2 = 10 \Rightarrow (x-2)^2 + 9x^2 = 10$. Expand: $10x^2 - 4x - 6 = 0 \Rightarrow 5x^2 - 2x - 3 = 0 \Rightarrow (5x+3)(x-1)=0$. So $x = 1$ or $x = -3/5$, giving $y = 2$ or $y = -14/5$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line given implicitly as ax + by = c (not solved for either variable)
First rearrange for whichever variable is easier (avoiding fractions if possible), then substitute. Same strategy, one extra preliminary step.
Two circles instead of line + circle
Subtract the two circle equations first to obtain the linear radical axis, then substitute that line into one circle. Substitution still finishes the job; a preliminary elimination produces the line.
Ellipse with directrix/eccentricity relation
Use the structural identity $b^2 = a^2(1 - e^2)$ instead of $b^2 = a^2(e^2 - 1)$. Substitute e in terms of a (or vice versa) exactly as before; only the sign in the bracket changes.
Parametric curve meeting a Cartesian curve
Substitute the parametric expressions x(t), y(t) into the Cartesian equation to get a single equation in t.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the line and circle as independent and solving the circle alone, ignoring the constraint from the line.
  • Expanding the messy equation fully first, then trying to substitute — this multiplies the algebra unnecessarily.
  • Eliminating the wrong variable so the final equation is in the variable you don't actually want.

Pitfalls

  • Sign/bracket errors when substituting: e.g. writing $(3x - 1 + 1)^2 = (3x - 2)^2$ instead of $(3x)^2$.
  • Squaring errors on rational coefficients: $(3a/4)^2 = 9a^2/16$, not $9a/16$ or $3a^2/16$.
  • Using the wrong structural identity for the conic ($b^2 = a^2(1 - e^2)$ for ellipse vs $b^2 = a^2(e^2 - 1)$ for hyperbola) — sign error propagates everywhere.
  • Forgetting to back-substitute to find the second coordinate / second unknown after solving the single-variable equation.
  • Discarding a valid root because it 'looks ugly' — both algebraic roots usually correspond to genuine geometric solutions.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL Pure (line–circle intersections, P2) and Further Pure (conic sections, FP1/FP3) using the formula booklet entries for circle equations and the conic identities $b^2 = a^2(1 \pm e^2)$. No need for resultants, Gröbner bases, or other out-of-scope elimination machinery — direct substitution is always sufficient at this level.
