Algebra & Functions

Divide polynomial by known linear factor via coefficient comparison

Family: Inspection Division Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A polynomial (typically cubic) is to be factorised or divided after a linear factor (x − a) or (x + a) has been confirmed by the factor theorem. Cue words include 'hence factorise', 'hence find the quotient', or 'show that f(x) = (x ± a)(...)'. The quotient's degree and leading/constant coefficients are immediately predictable, so coefficient matching is faster than long division. Especially efficient when the leading and constant coefficients of f(x) read off A and C directly.

Motivation — Why this works

If (x − a) is a factor of a cubic f(x) = px³ + qx² + rx + s, then f(x) = (x − a)(Ax² + Bx + C) for unique A, B, C. Expanding the right side and equating coefficients gives a small linear system: A = p (from x³) and −aC = s (from constant), which fix A and C instantly, leaving B determined by any one cross-term equation. This works because polynomial identity forces equality coefficient-by-coefficient, and the structure of the product makes most coefficients depend on only two unknowns.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that part (a) or the factor theorem has confirmed a linear factor, and the word 'hence' signals to reuse it rather than restart.
  2. Rule out brute trial-and-error factorisation and rule out long division as unnecessarily mechanical given the small number of unknown coefficients.
  3. Commit to the ansatz f(x) = (linear factor)(quadratic with unknowns A, B, C) because the quotient's degree is forced.
  4. Bridge: read off A from the leading x³ coefficient and C from the constant term — these require no algebra.
  5. Tactically, pick the simplest remaining coefficient (usually x² or x) to solve for B, then optionally cross-check with the other to catch arithmetic errors.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write f(x) = (x + a)(Ax² + Bx + C) with A, B, C unknown.
  2. Equate x³ coefficients to read off A.
  3. Equate constant terms to read off C.
  4. Equate one middle coefficient (x² or x) to solve for B.
  5. Cross-check using the other middle coefficient.
  6. State the quadratic factor and, if asked, factorise it further.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $f(x) = 2x^3 - 3x^2 - 11x + 6$ with $(x+2)$ a factor, write $f(x) = (x+2)(Ax^2+Bx+C)$. Comparing $x^3$: $A=2$. Comparing constants: $2C=6 \Rightarrow C=3$. Comparing $x^2$: $B+2A=-3 \Rightarrow B=-7$. So $f(x)=(x+2)(2x^2-7x+3)=(x+2)(2x-1)(x-3)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quartic divided by a known linear factor
Ansatz becomes a cubic Ax³+Bx²+Cx+D; A and D read off, two middle coefficients require comparison.
Quartic with a known quadratic factor
Ansatz is another quadratic Ax²+Bx+C; compare extreme coefficients first, then one middle term.
Division by (ax + b) rather than (x + a)
Leading coefficient of the quotient is p/a (must divide cleanly); constant of quotient is s/b.
Long division alternative
Same answer obtained more mechanically; preferred if the student is unsure about cross-terms.
Synthetic division (Horner) for (x − a)
Compact tabular method giving the quotient coefficients directly with the remainder as a check.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the quotient as having unknown degree rather than recognising it must be one less than f(x).
  • Trying to guess all linear factors by trial rather than exploiting the known factor structurally.
  • Confusing the sign in the factor: writing (x − 2) when the factor is (x + 2).

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting that middle coefficients receive contributions from two cross-terms (e.g. the x² coefficient comes from x·Bx and a·Ax²).
  • Sign errors when the linear factor is (x − a) — the constant comparison gives −aC = s, not aC = s.
  • Verifying with only one coefficient and missing an arithmetic slip in B.
  • Stopping at the quadratic factor when the question asks for full factorisation into linear factors.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure technique (P2/P3) used after the factor theorem; applies to cubics and quartics with integer or rational coefficients within syllabus scope.
