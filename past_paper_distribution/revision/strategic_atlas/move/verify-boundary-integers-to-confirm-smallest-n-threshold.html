Sequences & Series

Verify boundary integers to confirm smallest-n threshold

Family: Boundary Check Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a sequence/series problem asks for the smallest (or largest) integer n satisfying an inequality, and a logarithmic or algebraic manipulation has produced a non-integer critical value such as n > 38.60. Typical phrasings: 'find the smallest n such that S_n > M', 'how many terms are needed before…', 'least value of N for which…'. Also fires when the bound came from log inequalities where rounding could shift the boundary across an integer.

Motivation — Why this works

Logarithmic manipulations chain several rounded quantities, so the computed threshold can sit very close to an integer and end up on the wrong side after rounding. Substituting the two candidate integers into the original expression gives an exact (or tightly bounded) check that the inequality flips between them. Edexcel mark schemes for 'least integer' questions reserve a mark for this verification, so it both secures accuracy and earns the method/answer mark explicitly.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the algebra produced a non-integer critical value (e.g. k > 38.60), so the answer must be the next integer up — but the rounding chain is suspicious.
  2. Rule out simply quoting ⌈38.60⌉ = 39 without justification: the exam expects evidence that k = 38 fails.
  3. Commit to a two-point check at the integers immediately below and above the critical value, evaluated in the original (un-logged) inequality.
  4. Bridge: identify the original quantity (S_n, a_n, or sum total) and prepare to substitute both k = 38 and k = 39.
  5. Tactically keep r and r^k to 4+ s.f. during substitution to avoid the same rounding hazard ruining the verification.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the integer candidates straddling the critical value (here k = 38 and k = 39).
  2. Substitute the lower candidate into the original expression and show the inequality FAILS (e.g. total ≈ 2989 < 3000).
  3. Substitute the upper candidate and show the inequality HOLDS (e.g. total ≈ 3008 > 3000).
  4. State the conclusion explicitly, converting back to the question's variable if shifted (e.g. N = 20 + 39 = 59).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve gives $k > 38.60$. Check $k=38$: $(30/31)^{38}\approx 0.2877$, GP sum $\approx 1922(1-0.2877)=1369$, total $\approx 2989<3000$ ✗. Check $k=39$: $(30/31)^{39}\approx 0.2784$, GP sum $\approx 1922(1-0.2784)=1388$, total $\approx 3008>3000$ ✓. Hence smallest $k=39$, so $N=20+39=59$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Geometric series sum exceeding a target ($S_n > M$ with $0<r<1$)
Substitute both candidate n's into $S_n=a(1-r^n)/(1-r)$ directly rather than the log form.
Term of a GP first dropping below a tolerance ($ar^{n-1}<\varepsilon$)
Evaluate the term itself at both integers; inequality direction reverses for decreasing sequences.
Compound interest / investment threshold ($P(1+r)^n > T$)
Substitute the two integer year values into the compound formula and compare to T.
Arithmetic series threshold ($S_n>M$ with linear $S_n$)
Solve quadratic in n, then verify the two integer roots/candidates in $S_n=\tfrac{n}{2}(2a+(n-1)d)$.
Shifted indexing where the question's N differs from the working variable k
After verifying k, apply the index shift (e.g. $N=k+\text{offset}$) before stating the final answer.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the non-integer bound as exact and just rounding up without verification.
  • Verifying in the logged inequality rather than the original sum/term, which propagates the same rounding error.
  • Forgetting to convert k back to the question's variable when an index shift was used earlier.

Pitfalls

  • Only checking the upper candidate (k = 39) and not showing the lower (k = 38) fails — typically loses the verification mark.
  • Premature rounding of the common ratio r before raising to a high power, distorting both checks.
  • Mixing strict (>) and non-strict (≥) inequalities between the question and the verification step.
  • Stating the answer as the critical decimal (e.g. 38.6) instead of the integer.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 sequences and series: arithmetic/geometric sums, log-based inequality solving, and explicit verification of integer thresholds as required by standard mark schemes.
