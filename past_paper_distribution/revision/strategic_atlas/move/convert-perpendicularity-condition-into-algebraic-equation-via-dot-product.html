Vectors

Convert perpendicularity condition into algebraic equation via dot product

Family: Perpendicularity To Equation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a problem states two vectors are perpendicular (or asks for the value of a parameter making them so), and at least one component contains an unknown. Cue phrases include 'OC is perpendicular to BC', 'find the value of a such that...', or an explicit 'OC · BC = 0'. Also fires when a right angle is stated geometrically (e.g. 'angle OCB = 90°') and the natural translation is a zero dot product. The unknown appearing in multiple components signals that the resulting equation will be polynomial (typically quadratic).

Motivation — Why this works

The scalar product identity $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=a_1b_1+a_2b_2+a_3b_3$ converts a geometric perpendicularity statement into a purely algebraic equation. Setting this equal to zero is the standard A-level criterion for perpendicularity, and embedding an unknown in the components turns the criterion into a solvable equation in that unknown. Because each component product is at most linear in the unknown, the sum is at most quadratic — a form familiar and tractable for IAL students.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the condition 'perpendicular' is given and both vectors contain the parameter $a$, so a dot product will produce an equation in $a$.
  2. Rule out using $|\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}|=0$ (that is parallelism) and rule out using $\cos\theta$ formulas (unnecessary when $\theta=90°$ collapses the RHS to zero).
  3. Commit to $\mathbf{OC}\cdot\mathbf{BC}=0$ because it is the cleanest equation-generator: one scalar equation in one unknown.
  4. Bridge: write each vector explicitly in component form in terms of $a$, then prepare to compute the three component products carefully.
  5. Tactically, expand $(a)(a-6)$ as $a^2-6a$ rather than $a\cdot a$, and track the sign on the $\mathbf{k}$-component product to avoid losing constants.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write both vectors in component form with the unknown embedded, e.g. $\mathbf{OC}=(5,a,-1)$, $\mathbf{BC}=(0,a-6,1)$.
  2. Form the dot product componentwise: $(5)(0)+(a)(a-6)+(-1)(1)$.
  3. Simplify each term: $0+a^2-6a-1$.
  4. Set the sum equal to zero: $a^2-6a-1=0$.
  5. Solve the resulting quadratic (here by the quadratic formula) to obtain values of $a$.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\mathbf{OC}=(5,a,-1)$ and $\mathbf{BC}=(0,a-6,1)$ with $\mathbf{OC}\perp\mathbf{BC}$: $\mathbf{OC}\cdot\mathbf{BC}=0+a(a-6)+(-1)(1)=a^2-6a-1=0$. Hence $a=\dfrac{6\pm\sqrt{36+4}}{2}=3\pm\sqrt{10}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Angle specified as $\theta\neq 90°$ between two vectors containing a parameter
Use $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\cos\theta$; the RHS is non-zero, and squaring may be needed to clear the magnitudes.
Perpendicularity of a line to a plane's direction vectors (Further Maths)
Apply two dot-product-equals-zero conditions, one per direction vector, generating a small simultaneous system.
Finding the foot of perpendicular from a point to a line
Parametrise the point on the line, then impose $(\text{displacement})\cdot(\text{direction})=0$ to get a linear equation in the parameter.
Unknown component in only one vector, the other fixed
Equation is linear in the unknown rather than quadratic; solve directly without the quadratic formula.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the condition as parallelism and trying to set components proportional, instead of using the zero dot product.
  • Dotting the wrong pair of vectors (e.g. $\mathbf{OC}\cdot\mathbf{OB}$ rather than $\mathbf{OC}\cdot\mathbf{BC}$) because the diagram suggests $O$ as a hub.
  • Reading 'perpendicular at $C$' as a magnitude condition $|\mathbf{OC}|=|\mathbf{BC}|$ rather than an angle condition.

Pitfalls

  • Expanding $(a)(a-6)$ as $a^2$ instead of $a^2-6a$, losing the linear term.
  • Sign error on the $\mathbf{k}$-component: $(-1)(1)=-1$, not $+1$.
  • Forgetting to set the dot product equal to zero and instead simplifying it as if it were a magnitude.
  • Discarding one root of the quadratic without checking whether the problem requires both values or imposes a contextual restriction.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure/Core vectors: 3D component dot products, perpendicularity criterion, and solving a resulting quadratic via the quadratic formula. No matrix, plane-equation, or cross-product machinery is required at this level.
