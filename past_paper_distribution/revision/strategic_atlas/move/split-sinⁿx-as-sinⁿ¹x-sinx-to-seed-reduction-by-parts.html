Strategic Atlas / Integration / Parts Decomposition
Integration

Split sinⁿx as sinⁿ⁻¹x · sinx to seed reduction by parts

Family: Parts Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an integral involves a single high power of a trig function, e.g. ∫sinⁿx dx or ∫cosⁿx dx, and a reduction formula linking Iₙ to Iₙ₋₂ is required. Also triggers when a question explicitly asks to 'establish' or 'prove' such a recurrence. The cue is a pure power of one trig function with no other algebraic partner, and the target is to drop the exponent by 2.

Motivation — Why this works

Integration by parts needs a 'dv' with a clean antiderivative; peeling off a single sinx gives v = −cosx instantly. The remaining sinⁿ⁻¹x serves as 'u', whose derivative produces (n−1)sinⁿ⁻²x cosx. The cosx from du multiplied by the cosx from v yields cos²x, which the Pythagorean identity cos²x = 1 − sin²x converts back into a combination of Iₙ and Iₙ₋₂. Any other split breaks this chain because sin²x or sinⁿ⁻²x lack a tidy antiderivative.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand is a pure power sinⁿx with no other factor — a reduction formula is the standard target.
  2. Rule out u-substitution u = sinx: du = cosx dx leaves an unmatched cosine, so it stalls.
  3. Rule out the split sinⁿ⁻²x · sin²x: sin²x has no clean antiderivative for dv, and integrating sinⁿ⁻²x for dv is even harder.
  4. Commit to sinⁿ⁻¹x · sinx because dv = sinx dx integrates immediately to v = −cosx, the smallest workable dv.
  5. Anticipate that du · v will generate cos²x, then plan to apply cos²x = 1 − sin²x to recover Iₙ and Iₙ₋₂ for the recurrence.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write sinⁿx = sinⁿ⁻¹x · sinx and set u = sinⁿ⁻¹x, dv = sinx dx.
  2. Differentiate: du = (n−1)sinⁿ⁻²x cosx dx; integrate: v = −cosx.
  3. Apply IBP: Iₙ = −sinⁿ⁻¹x cosx + (n−1)∫ sinⁿ⁻²x cos²x dx.
  4. Substitute cos²x = 1 − sin²x to obtain Iₙ = −sinⁿ⁻¹x cosx + (n−1)Iₙ₋₂ − (n−1)Iₙ.
  5. Solve algebraically: nIₙ = −sinⁿ⁻¹x cosx + (n−1)Iₙ₋₂, then divide by n (and apply limits if definite).

Worked Micro-Example

For Iₙ = ∫₀^{π/2} sinⁿx dx, set u = sinⁿ⁻¹x, dv = sinx dx. Then du = (n−1)sinⁿ⁻²x cosx dx, v = −cosx. IBP gives Iₙ = [−sinⁿ⁻¹x cosx]₀^{π/2} + (n−1)∫₀^{π/2} sinⁿ⁻²x cos²x dx = 0 + (n−1)(Iₙ₋₂ − Iₙ). Hence Iₙ = ((n−1)/n) Iₙ₋₂.

Variants — Same move, different surface

∫ cosⁿx dx
Split as cosⁿ⁻¹x · cosx with dv = cosx dx, v = sinx; identical structure with sin ↔ cos.
∫ xⁿ eˣ dx
Take u = xⁿ, dv = eˣ dx so v = eˣ; each IBP drops the polynomial power by 1, giving Iₙ = xⁿeˣ − nIₙ₋₁.
∫ xⁿ ln x dx or ∫ (ln x)ⁿ dx
For (ln x)ⁿ, take u = (ln x)ⁿ, dv = dx so v = x; reduction drops the log power by 1.
∫ tanⁿx dx
Split as tanⁿ⁻²x · tan²x and use tan²x = sec²x − 1 instead of IBP — the recurrence comes from the identity, not parts.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating sinⁿx dx as suitable for u = sinx substitution, ignoring the missing cosx factor.
  • Believing any split will work because IBP is 'just a formula' — the choice of dv is what makes the recurrence close.
  • Confusing this with the tanⁿx reduction, which uses an identity rather than parts.

Pitfalls

  • Choosing dv = sinⁿ⁻¹x dx (the larger piece), which has no elementary antiderivative.
  • Dropping the (n−1) chain-rule factor when differentiating sinⁿ⁻¹x.
  • Forgetting to convert cos²x via 1 − sin²x, leaving the integral unreduced.
  • Sign slip from v = −cosx when evaluating the boundary term.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure reduction-formula technique; only requires basic IBP, the Pythagorean identity, and algebraic rearrangement, all within the FP syllabus.
