Algebra & Functions

Substitute a known point to calibrate the leading coefficient

Family: Parameter Determination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The functional form is fully determined up to a single multiplicative constant — typically after factoring out known roots, e.g. f(x) = A(x − r_1)(x − r_2), or after writing vertex form f(x) = A(x − h)^2 + k with (h, k) known. A specific point on the curve (a maximum, an intercept, a stated coordinate) is supplied. The phrase 'the curve passes through' or 'has maximum value' alongside known zeros is the canonical cue. One unknown, one usable equation — substitution is the natural next step.

Motivation — Why this works

Once the structural skeleton (roots, vertex, asymptotes) is fixed, the only remaining freedom is the vertical scale factor A. Any additional point on the curve must satisfy the equation, so plugging it in produces one linear equation in A. This works because A appears linearly in the form, guaranteeing a unique solution. It is the most efficient route compared to expanding and using simultaneous equations.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that the factored/vertex form contains exactly one unknown constant A after the roots or vertex are pinned down.
  2. Rules out expanding to ax^2 + bx + c and solving simultaneous equations — that reintroduces unknowns already determined by structure.
  3. Commits to substitution because a specific (x, y) point is given, which yields one equation in the one unknown A.
  4. Bridges to the operation by writing f(x_0) = y_0 with the chosen point, keeping the factored form intact rather than expanding.
  5. Inside the move: chooses the most convenient point (often the maximum or an explicitly stated coordinate), evaluates each factor carefully with sign tracking, and solves the resulting linear equation for A.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the form with one unknown, e.g. f(x) = Ax(x − 4).
  2. Identify a known point (x_0, y_0) on the curve, e.g. (2, 20).
  3. Substitute: y_0 = A·(factor evaluations at x_0).
  4. Simplify the right-hand side carefully, tracking signs.
  5. Solve the linear equation for A.
  6. Write the final function with A inserted.

Worked Micro-Example

Given f(x) has roots 0 and 4 and maximum (2, 20), write f(x) = Ax(x − 4). Substitute (2, 20): $20 = A(2)(2-4) = A(2)(-2) = -4A$. Hence $A = -5$, so $f(x) = -5x(x-4)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Vertex form with known vertex (h, k) and one other point
Write f(x) = A(x − h)^2 + k, substitute the other point to solve for A.
Cubic with three known roots and one extra point
Write f(x) = A(x − r_1)(x − r_2)(x − r_3), substitute the extra point for A.
Exponential model y = A e^{kx} with k already known and one data point
Substitute the point to solve the resulting linear equation in A.
Trig model y = A sin(Bx) + C with B, C fixed and one point given
Substitute to recover A directly.
Reciprocal/rational form with known asymptotes and one point
Write the form preserving asymptote structure, substitute the point to fix the scale.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating A as already 1 and concluding f(x) = x(x − 4) without using the given point.
  • Reading the maximum value 20 as the y-coordinate of an intercept rather than f(2).
  • Assuming a downward parabola requires building in a minus sign manually rather than letting A absorb it.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error in evaluating (2 − 4) = −2; dropping the negative flips A and produces the wrong orientation.
  • Expanding before substituting and making arithmetic slips in the expansion.
  • Forgetting to write the final answer with A substituted in — leaving f(x) in terms of A.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure technique for determining quadratic, cubic, or simple transformed function equations from given features (roots, vertex, intercepts, stated points).
