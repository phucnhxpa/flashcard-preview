Vectors

Build plane parametrically from anchor on contained line plus two spanning directions

Family: Parametric Plane Construction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks for a plane that 'contains line l₁ and is parallel to line l₂' (or contains one line and is parallel to another, or passes through a point and is parallel to two given directions). Visual cues: two skew lines given in vector form, the word 'contains' attached to one and 'parallel to' attached to the other, or a request for the plane in form r = a + su + tv. Equivalent phrasings include 'the plane through l₂ parallel to l₁', 'the plane containing both directions d₁ and d₂ passing through P', or 'find a parametric equation of the plane'. Often paired with a follow-up part asking for the scalar/Cartesian form r·n = p, which signals that d₁ × d₂ should also be computed.

Motivation — Why this works

A plane is uniquely determined by one point and two non-parallel direction vectors. 'Containing l₂' forces the anchor point to lie on l₂ and forces d₂ to be a spanning direction; 'parallel to l₁' forces d₁ to also lie in (be parallel to) the plane, hence be the second spanning direction. Because the lines are skew, d₁ and d₂ are non-parallel and genuinely span a 2D plane. Writing r = a + sd₁ + td₂ directly encodes this geometric specification with no algebra needed. The same parametric setup feeds the Cartesian form via n = d₁ × d₂.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structural pattern: one line is to be CONTAINED in the plane, the other is to be PARALLEL to the plane — this is the hallmark of the 'point + two directions' construction.
  2. Rule out alternatives: I do not need a normal vector yet (that's for the Cartesian form), I do not need to solve simultaneous equations, and I do not need the lines to intersect — skewness is fine because I only need their direction vectors.
  3. Commit to r = a + sd₁ + td₂ because a point and two independent directions uniquely determine a plane, and all three ingredients are visible in the question.
  4. Critical tactical decision: the anchor point a must come from the CONTAINED line (l₂), not the parallel line (l₁). Choosing a point on l₁ gives a different plane (parallel to both lines but containing neither).
  5. Read the anchor point off l₂ as the constant vector in its equation; read d₁ and d₂ off as the coefficients of the parameters in l₁ and l₂.
  6. If a Cartesian form is requested next, recognise that n = d₁ × d₂ is the plane's normal, and p = n · a is found by substituting the same anchor a.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify a point a on the line that must be CONTAINED (e.g. a = 2i − 4j + 4k from l₂).
  2. Identify the two direction vectors: d₁ from the parallel line, d₂ from the contained line.
  3. Check d₁ ∦ d₂ (non-parallel) so they span a plane.
  4. Write r = a + s d₁ + t d₂ — this is the parametric equation.
  5. (If Cartesian form needed) Compute n = d₁ × d₂.
  6. (If Cartesian form needed) Compute p = n · a, then write r · n = p; optionally divide through by a common factor of n to simplify.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $l_1: \mathbf{r}=\mathbf{i}+\mathbf{j}+s(5\mathbf{i}+\mathbf{j})$ and $l_2: \mathbf{r}=2\mathbf{i}-4\mathbf{j}+4\mathbf{k}+t(8\mathbf{i}-2\mathbf{j}+3\mathbf{k})$. Plane contains $l_2$, parallel to $l_1$. Anchor $\mathbf{a}=2\mathbf{i}-4\mathbf{j}+4\mathbf{k}$ (from $l_2$), spanning directions $\mathbf{d}_1=5\mathbf{i}+\mathbf{j}$, $\mathbf{d}_2=8\mathbf{i}-2\mathbf{j}+3\mathbf{k}$. So $\mathbf{r}=(2\mathbf{i}-4\mathbf{j}+4\mathbf{k})+s(5\mathbf{i}+\mathbf{j})+t(8\mathbf{i}-2\mathbf{j}+3\mathbf{k})$. For Cartesian: $\mathbf{n}=\mathbf{d}_1\times\mathbf{d}_2=3\mathbf{i}-15\mathbf{j}-18\mathbf{k}$, $p=\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{a}=6+60-72=-6$, giving $\mathbf{r}\cdot(\mathbf{i}-5\mathbf{j}-6\mathbf{k})=-2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Plane through three points A, B, C
Take a = A and spanning directions AB = B − A, AC = C − A; same r = a + s(AB) + t(AC) structure.
Plane through a point P parallel to two given vectors u, v
Anchor a = P, spanning directions u and v directly: r = P + su + tv.
Convert the resulting parametric plane to Cartesian form r · n = p
Compute n = (spanning vector 1) × (spanning vector 2), then p = n · a; if the cross product was already done in an earlier part, reuse it rather than recomputing.

Common Misreadings

  • Using a point on the PARALLEL line as the anchor instead of a point on the CONTAINED line — this gives a plane parallel to both lines but containing neither.
  • Interpreting 'parallel to l₁' as 'normal parallel to d₁' rather than 'd₁ lies in the plane'.
  • Not recognising that an earlier part computing d₁ × d₂ has already supplied the normal for a Cartesian-form follow-up.

Pitfalls

  • Using a normal vector as one of the spanning directions instead of d₁ and d₂ themselves.
  • Forgetting the scalar p when writing the Cartesian form (writing r · n = 0 as if the plane passes through the origin).
  • Sign errors in computing p = n · a, especially when the anchor has negative components producing double negatives like (−15)(−4).
  • Failing to verify d₁ ∦ d₂ — if they were parallel, no plane is uniquely determined.

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 vectors: parametric form of a plane r = a + su + tv and scalar form r · n = p are both in scope; cross product (formula booklet) is used to find n. Avoid bringing in matrix/determinant theory or general n-dimensional generalisations.
