Trigger — When to use

The integrand is a proper rational function P(x)/Q(x) where Q(x) factorises into linear factors (possibly repeated) and the combined fraction has no direct antiderivative in the standard table. Visual cues: a denominator like (1 − x²), u(u+2), or u³(u−2) — i.e. a product/factorisable polynomial — sitting under a constant or low-degree numerator. Equivalently phrased: 'integrate k/[(ax+b)(cx+d)]', 'integrate over a denominator that is a difference of squares', or 'integrate over a denominator with a repeated linear factor'. If you cannot write the integrand as f'(x)/f(x) or in another standard form, partial fractions is the canonical next step.

Motivation — Why this works

Partial fractions rewrites P(x)/Q(x) as a sum of simple terms — A/(ax+b), B/(ax+b)², (Cu+D)/(quadratic) — each of which integrates by elementary rules: ∫1/(ax+b) dx = (1/a)ln|ax+b|, and ∫(ax+b)^(−n) dx via the power rule for n ≥ 2. The underlying principle is that the field of rational functions over ℝ admits a unique decomposition determined by the factorisation of Q(x). Without this decomposition there is no standard integration rule for the combined fraction; with it, every piece becomes a textbook integral.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand is a single rational function whose denominator factors (or already is factored) into linear pieces — this is the visual fingerprint of partial fractions.
  2. Check the fraction is proper (deg numerator < deg denominator); if not, do polynomial long division first.
  3. Rule out alternatives: substitution fails because the numerator isn't proportional to the derivative of the denominator; integration by parts won't simplify a rational function; trig substitution is overkill for real linear factors.
  4. Commit to the partial fraction template dictated by the factorisation: one term per distinct linear factor, and for a repeated factor (ax+b)^n include terms for every power 1, 2, ..., n; for an irreducible quadratic use a (Cu+D) numerator.
  5. Tactical choice for finding constants: use the cover-up (substitution) method when factors are distinct linear — substitute the root that kills all but one term. For repeated factors, mix cover-up (for the highest power) with equating coefficients of remaining powers.
  6. After decomposition, integrate each term: 1/(ax+b) → (1/a)ln|ax+b|; 1/(ax+b)^n → (1/a)·(ax+b)^(1−n)/(1−n). Watch chain-rule signs (e.g. ∫1/(1−x)dx = −ln|1−x|).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Factor the denominator Q(x) fully into linear (and irreducible quadratic) factors.
  2. Write the partial fraction template: one A/(linear) per distinct simple factor, terms B₁/(linear) + B₂/(linear)² + ... + Bₙ/(linear)ⁿ for a repeated factor of multiplicity n, and (Cu+D)/(quadratic) for irreducible quadratics.
  3. Multiply both sides by Q(x) to clear denominators, obtaining a polynomial identity.
  4. Find unknowns by cover-up (substitute the root of each linear factor) and/or by equating coefficients of like powers of x.
  5. Substitute the constants back, splitting the integrand into a sum of elementary terms.
  6. Integrate term-by-term: ∫A/(ax+b) dx = (A/a)ln|ax+b| + C; ∫B/(ax+b)^n dx via the power rule for n ≥ 2.
  7. Combine logs (using ln a − ln b = ln(a/b)) where the question expects a tidy form, and remember the constant of integration.

Worked Micro-Example

Compute $\int \frac{8}{u(u+2)}\,du$. Write $\frac{8}{u(u+2)} = \frac{A}{u} + \frac{B}{u+2}$, so $8 = A(u+2) + Bu$. Cover-up: $u=0 \Rightarrow A=4$; $u=-2 \Rightarrow B=-4$. Hence $\int\!\left(\frac{4}{u} - \frac{4}{u+2}\right)du = 4\ln|u| - 4\ln|u+2| + C = 4\ln\left|\frac{u}{u+2}\right| + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Denominator is a difference of squares, e.g. 1 − x² or a² − x²
Factor as (1−x)(1+x); decomposition gives 1/(1−x) + 1/(1+x); remember ∫1/(1−x)dx = −ln|1−x| due to the inner derivative −1.
Repeated linear factor, e.g. denominator u³(u−2)
Use the full template B/u + C/u² + D/u³ + A/(u−2). The u^(−2) and u^(−3) terms integrate by the power rule, not as logarithms.
Irreducible quadratic factor (in scope only via simple cases)
Use numerator (Cu+D) over that quadratic; split into a logarithmic piece (after recognising f'/f) plus possibly an arctan piece.
Improper fraction (deg numerator ≥ deg denominator)
Do polynomial long division first, then apply partial fractions to the proper remainder.

Common Misreadings

  • Attempting integration by parts on a rational function — it does not simplify the structure.
  • Writing only D/(ax+b)ⁿ for a repeated factor and omitting the lower-power terms — this under-determines the system.
  • Forgetting to long-divide first when the fraction is improper, leading to an inconsistent system.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error from the chain rule: ∫1/(1−x)dx = −ln|1−x|, not +ln|1−x|.
  • Sign errors in cover-up when substituting negative roots, e.g. u = −2 giving 8 = −2B.
  • Dropping absolute values inside logarithms (only safe when the argument is known positive on the integration interval).
  • For repeated factors, omitting intermediate-power terms or mishandling the −1 in (ax+b)^(1−n)/(1−n).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 (Core Pure for Further Maths). Partial fractions with distinct linear factors, repeated linear factors, and a single irreducible quadratic factor are in scope; the formula booklet provides ∫1/(ax+b) dx = (1/a)ln|ax+b|. Avoid Heaviside-style derivatives for repeated factors and avoid complex-root decompositions — these are out of scope.
