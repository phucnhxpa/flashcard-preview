Differentiation

Discard strictly-positive factors when solving product = 0

Family: Sign Analysis Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation f'(x) = 0 (or f(x) = 0) is presented as a product of factors and at least one factor is manifestly never zero — typically an exponential e^{g(x)}, a non-zero constant, or an always-positive expression like x^2+1 or \sqrt{x^2+1}. Cue phrases include 'find stationary points' after a product/quotient rule differentiation that produced an e^{kx} factor. Also fires when checking sign of f'(x) on an interval: a strictly positive factor can be ignored without affecting the sign.

Motivation — Why this works

A product equals zero iff at least one factor equals zero (zero-product property). Factors that are strictly positive on \mathbb{R} contribute no roots and do not affect the sign of the product, so they can be cleanly removed. Exponentials satisfy e^{g(x)} > 0 for every real input, and sums-of-squares-plus-positive-constant are always positive. Eliminating these factors reduces a transcendental equation to a polynomial one that A-level techniques can solve directly.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices f'(x) is presented as a product of several factors after applying product/chain rule.
  2. Rules out the exponential factor e^{g(x)} and any non-zero constant on the grounds that they cannot equal zero for any real x.
  3. Commits to the reduced algebraic equation built only from the factors that can vanish.
  4. Bridges to concrete work by writing the residual polynomial/linear equation explicitly and stating the justification for the discard.
  5. Inside the move, decides whether to keep a squared factor (which gives a repeated root) or split it; flags any always-positive non-exponential factors (e.g. x^2+1) for the same dismissal.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write f'(x) as a product of clearly identified factors.
  2. Identify each factor that is strictly positive for all real x (constants \ne 0, e^{g(x)}, x^2+c with c>0, etc.).
  3. State explicitly: 'e^{g(x)} > 0 for all x \in \mathbb{R}, so this factor is never zero.'
  4. Cancel/discard those factors from the equation f'(x) = 0.
  5. Solve the remaining polynomial or linear equation for x.
  6. If sign analysis is needed, note that the discarded factors do not change the sign of f'(x).

Worked Micro-Example

Given f(x) = (2x+1)^3 e^{-4x}, differentiate to get f'(x) = 2(2x+1)^2(1-4x)e^{-4x}. Since e^{-4x} > 0 for all x \in \mathbb{R} and 2 \ne 0, f'(x) = 0 \iff (2x+1)^2(1-4x) = 0. Hence x = -\tfrac{1}{2} or x = \tfrac{1}{4}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

f(x) = x^2 e^{3x}, solving f'(x) = 0
f'(x) = x(2+3x)e^{3x}; discard e^{3x} > 0 to get x(2+3x) = 0.
Quotient with exponential denominator, e.g. f(x) = \frac{x}{e^{x}}
After differentiation the e^{x} factors collapse but any residual e^{-x} can be discarded when setting numerator = 0.
Factor like (x^2+1) appearing in f'(x)
Since x^2+1 \ge 1 > 0 for all real x, dismiss it in the same way as an exponential.
Sign analysis of f'(x) for monotonicity
Strictly positive factors do not affect the sign; reduce sign question to the remaining factors only.
Trigonometric factor 2 + \cos x or 1 + \sin^2 x
Bounded strictly positive expressions can be discarded with a brief justification of the bound.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating e^{-4x} \to 0 as x \to \infty as meaning e^{-4x} = 0 has a solution.
  • Believing the exponential contributes a stationary point 'at infinity'.
  • Cancelling a factor without checking it is genuinely never zero (e.g. cancelling x, which IS zero at x=0).

Pitfalls

  • Failing to state the justification 'e^{g(x)} > 0 for all real x' — examiners often require this line.
  • Discarding a squared factor (2x+1)^2 entirely instead of solving (2x+1)^2 = 0 to get the repeated root.
  • Mistakenly cancelling a factor that can equal zero (e.g. cancelling (1-4x)) and losing a root.
  • In sign analysis, forgetting that (2x+1)^2 \ge 0 changes the sign behaviour at x = -1/2 (touch, not cross).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P3/P4 reasoning for stationary points of products involving e^{kx}; the strict positivity of e^{g(x)} and of sums like x^2+1 is assumed knowledge and routinely cited in mark schemes.
