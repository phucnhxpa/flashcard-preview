Trigonometry

Substitute the point whose x-value zeroes the trig argument

Family: Strategic Substitution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a transformed trig curve y = A f(x − α) (or A f(Bx − C)) is given together with a labelled point — typically a stated maximum, minimum, or intercept — whose x-coordinate makes the argument of the trig function equal to 0 (or a clean multiple of 90°). Cue phrases include 'the minimum point has coordinates (α, k)' or 'the curve passes through (α, k)' where x = α matches the phase shift inside the bracket. The alignment of the given x with the phase shift is the signal.

Motivation — Why this works

Setting the argument to 0 collapses cos(0°) = 1 (or sin(90°) = 1, etc.) so the trig factor becomes a known constant and the unknown amplitude A is read off in one line. This works because extrema and axis-crossings of sin/cos occur at arguments that are integer multiples of 90°, and the question is usually engineered so that a labelled feature sits exactly at one such argument. Substituting elsewhere would force you to solve an equation involving an awkward cosine value; substituting here makes the algebra trivial.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the bracket (x − α) inside the trig and a given point whose x-coordinate equals α.
  2. Rule out trying to find A by reading the graph's range or by simultaneous equations — both are unnecessary work here.
  3. Commit to direct substitution because the argument will vanish and cos(0°) = 1 is immediate.
  4. Plug x = α and y = k into y = A cos(x − α)° to get k = A · 1.
  5. Use the 'minimum/maximum' label to sanity-check the sign of A: a minimum on y = A cos forces A and k to share sign (here both negative).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the phase shift α inside the trig bracket.
  2. Match it against the given point (α, k).
  3. Substitute x = α, y = k into the equation.
  4. Evaluate the trig factor at argument 0 (cos 0° = 1, sin 0° = 0).
  5. Solve the resulting linear equation for A and check sign against the max/min label.

Worked Micro-Example

The curve $y = A\cos(x - 30)^\circ$ has minimum point $P(30, -3)$. Substitute: $-3 = A\cos(0^\circ) = A \cdot 1$, so $A = -3$. The negative value is consistent with $P$ being a minimum (a positive $A$ would make $x=30$ a maximum).

Variants — Same move, different surface

y = A sin(x − α)° with point at x = α + 90°
Argument becomes 90°, sin(90°) = 1, so y-coordinate equals A directly.
y = A cos(Bx − C)° with point at x = C/B
Argument is 0, cos = 1, A equals the y-coordinate of that point.
y = A sin(x − α)° + d with point at x = α
sin(0°) = 0 kills the A term and isolates the vertical shift d instead.
y = A cos(x − α)° + d with maximum and minimum y-values given
Use A = (max − min)/2 and d = (max + min)/2 instead of point substitution.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the point as a generic point requiring a system of equations rather than spotting the argument-zeroing alignment.
  • Reading 'minimum' as just a y-value cue and ignoring that it pins down the sign of A.
  • Confusing the role of α: thinking x = α gives cos(α) rather than cos(0).

Pitfalls

  • Writing cos(0°) = 0 by confusion with sin(0°).
  • Reporting A = 3 instead of A = −3 by assuming amplitude must be positive — in IAL questions A can be signed.
  • Working in radians by mistake when the equation is stated in degrees (or vice versa).
  • Substituting into y = A cos(x) − 30 instead of y = A cos(x − 30)° due to bracket misreading.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure (P2/P3) graph-transformation territory: amplitude, phase shift, and the values of sin/cos at 0° and 90°. No identities beyond direct evaluation are needed.
