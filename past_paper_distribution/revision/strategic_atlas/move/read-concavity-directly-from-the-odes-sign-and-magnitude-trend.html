Differential Equations

Read concavity directly from the ODE's sign and magnitude trend

Family: Qualitative Curve Analysis Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a sketch of a solution curve is required and the ODE gives dy/dt (or dr/dt) explicitly in terms of the dependent variable. Cues include phrases like 'sketch the curve', 'show the shape between two points', or a follow-up after solving an ODE. Especially useful when the ODE is a simple rational or power expression in the dependent variable, so monotonicity of |dy/dt| as the variable changes is easy to read off.

Motivation — Why this works

The sign of dy/dt tells you whether the curve rises or falls; the trend of |dy/dt| as the variable evolves tells you whether the slope is steepening or flattening, which is exactly concavity. If a decreasing curve's slope becomes more negative, it is concave down; if the slope becomes less negative, it is concave up. This bypasses an explicit d²y/dt² computation, which for a quotient like −k/r² would require the chain rule and is more error-prone.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question asks for a sketch and I already have dr/dt = −k/r² with k > 0.
  2. I rule out computing d²r/dt² explicitly — it's unnecessary work when the ODE structure makes the trend obvious.
  3. I commit to a qualitative argument: track how |dr/dt| changes as r changes along the trajectory.
  4. First concrete step: at the initial r = 12, r² is large so |dr/dt| is small — the curve starts shallow.
  5. Tactical step: as r → 0, r² → 0 so |dr/dt| → ∞ — slope becomes increasingly negative, hence concave down, with a vertical tangent at the t-axis crossing.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify dr/dt from the ODE and confirm its sign on the interval (here negative, so r decreasing).
  2. Track how |dr/dt| changes as r evolves: here r decreases, so r² decreases, so |dr/dt| = k/r² increases.
  3. Translate slope trend into concavity: slope becoming more negative ⇒ concave down; slope becoming less negative ⇒ concave up.
  4. Mark endpoints (0, 12) and (120/7, 0) and the tangent behaviour at each (shallow at start, near-vertical at the t-axis).
  5. Draw a smooth monotone-decreasing curve with the deduced concavity joining the endpoints.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\dfrac{dr}{dt}=-\dfrac{k}{r^2}$, $k>0$, sketch $r$ vs $t$ from $(0,12)$ to $(120/7,0)$. At $r=12$: $|dr/dt|=k/144$ (small, shallow start). As $r\downarrow 0$: $|dr/dt|=k/r^2\to\infty$ (steepens to vertical). Slope becomes more negative throughout, so the curve is concave down, dropping gently then plunging to meet the $t$-axis vertically at $t=120/7$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Exponential decay $dN/dt=-kN$
As $N$ decreases, $|dN/dt|=kN$ also decreases — slope becomes less negative, so the curve is concave up (and asymptotes to $N=0$).
Newton's law of cooling $d\theta/dt=-k(\theta-\theta_a)$
Same logic as exponential decay relative to $\theta_a$: $|d\theta/dt|$ shrinks as $\theta\to\theta_a$, giving a concave-up approach to the asymptote.
Logistic growth $dP/dt=kP(M-P)$
$|dP/dt|$ peaks at $P=M/2$, so concavity changes sign there — the sketch has an inflection at $P=M/2$.
Power-law inflow $dV/dt=k\sqrt{h}$ with $V$ rising
As $h$ grows, $dV/dt$ grows, so slope becomes more positive — concave up while increasing.

Common Misreadings

  • Drawing a straight-line decrease, ignoring concavity entirely.
  • Drawing a concave-up decay curve by analogy with exponential decay, without checking the ODE's actual dependence on r.
  • Treating 'steeper' as automatically meaning 'concave up' regardless of the sign of the slope.

Pitfalls

  • Confusing 'gradient becoming more negative' with 'concave up'; in fact decreasing slope (more negative) means concave down.
  • Forgetting to mark the near-vertical tangent at $r=0$, which is a key visual feature when $|dr/dt|\to\infty$.
  • Applying the trend argument without first confirming the sign of dr/dt on the interval.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Maths/Further Maths: uses only first-derivative sign analysis and the standard interpretation of concavity, with no formal use of d²y/dt² required for the sketch.
