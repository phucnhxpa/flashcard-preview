Eliminate one variable to reduce a 2×2 system to a single equation

Family: Simultaneous Elimination Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

Two simultaneous equations in two unknowns appear, and the goal is to find both unknowns or to derive a single equation in one of them (e.g. a 'show that' quadratic in r). Visual cues: matching coefficients on one variable (e.g. both equations contain '+q' or '2a'), one equation that is trivially solvable for a variable (e.g. ar = 18), or a line equated to a curve y = f(x) producing a rational/polynomial equation. Equivalent phrasings: 'eliminate q', 'reduce to one variable', 'cancel a between the equations'.

Motivation — Why this works

Eliminating one variable collapses a 2×2 system to a single equation in the remaining unknown, which is then solvable directly (linear → divide; quadratic → factorise/discriminant). The underlying principle is that any linear combination of two true equations is also true, so subtracting (or substituting) preserves the solution set while removing a degree of freedom. Choosing the cheapest elimination route — matching coefficients ⇒ subtract; one equation easily isolated ⇒ substitute; equation pair like uₙ ⇒ divide — minimises algebraic clutter and reduces error risk in 'show that' tasks where a target form must be matched exactly.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structural feature: do coefficients of one variable already match? Is one equation trivially solvable for a variable? Is one side a fraction that should be cleared?
  2. Rule out brute substitution when matching coefficients exist — subtraction is one step and avoids fractions; rule out isolating from the messier equation when a simpler one is available.
  3. Commit to the cheapest route: subtract if coefficients match (possibly after scaling), substitute a = expression(r) from the simpler equation, or equate y-values when a line meets a curve.
  4. Order the operation to keep arithmetic clean: subtract so the leading coefficient stays positive; clear denominators only after confirming the denominator is non-zero (e.g. x ≠ 2 at the asymptote).
  5. Execute the elimination, expand and rearrange to standard form (ax² + bx + c = 0 or linear), then back-substitute the recovered value into the simpler original equation to find the second unknown.
  6. If the question is 'show that', divide through by any common factor to match the target form exactly.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the elimination route: match-and-subtract, isolate-and-substitute, or equate-and-clear.
  2. Apply the operation: e.g. (Eq2) − (Eq1) to cancel a shared term, or substitute a = 18/r into the second equation.
  3. Simplify: clear fractions by multiplying through by any denominator (noting domain restrictions).
  4. Rearrange to standard polynomial form with positive leading coefficient.
  5. Solve the resulting single-variable equation (linear divide, or quadratic factor/formula/discriminant).
  6. Back-substitute into the simpler original equation to recover the eliminated variable.
  7. If 'show that', divide by any common factor so the result matches the printed target.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $9p+q=8$ and $25p+q=13.824$. Subtract: $(25p+q)-(9p+q)=13.824-8 \Rightarrow 16p=5.824 \Rightarrow p=0.364$. Back-substitute: $9(0.364)+q=8 \Rightarrow q=4.724$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Coefficients of the target variable do not match
Scale one or both equations by suitable constants to equalise a coefficient, then subtract.
Line y = mx + c meets a rational curve y = 1/(2−x)
Equate the y-expressions and multiply by (2−x) to clear the fraction, producing a quadratic in x for discriminant analysis.
Geometric series with two given terms (e.g. u₃ and u₆) plus an S∞ condition
Divide one equation by the other to eliminate a directly via the ratio, avoiding substitution.
3×3 linear system
Apply elimination twice to reduce 3×3 → 2×2, then again to a single equation.

Common Misreadings

  • Reaching for substitution reflexively when coefficients already match — this introduces unnecessary fractions.
  • Trying to apply the quadratic formula directly to a/(1−r) = 75 without first eliminating a, treating (1−r) as a single unknown.
  • Substituting y from the curve into the line equation (solving for x first) when equating y-expressions is more direct.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when subtracting equations — failing to subtract both sides, or subtracting in the wrong order so coefficients flip sign.
  • Premature rounding of the first variable before back-substituting — use the exact value or full decimal to avoid propagated error.
  • Forgetting domain restrictions (e.g. x ≠ 2) when multiplying through by a denominator, risking spurious roots.
  • Missing a common factor in a 'show that' final form (e.g. failing to divide 75r² − 75r + 18 = 0 by 3 to reach 25r² − 25r + 6 = 0).
  • Sign errors when rearranging −Ar² + Br − C = 0 into standard Ar² − Br + C = 0.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (P1–P4) scope; no formula-booklet entry required as elimination is a foundational technique. Do not generalise to matrix/row-reduction notation — that is FP territory and unnecessary here.
