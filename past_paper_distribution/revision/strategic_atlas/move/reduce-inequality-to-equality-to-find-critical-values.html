Reduce inequality to equality to find critical values

Family: Critical Value Method Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A polynomial or rational inequality such as $2x^2+5x-3>0$ is presented and the solution must be expressed as a set of intervals. The expression is continuous, so it can only change sign at points where it equals zero (or, for rational expressions, where the denominator is zero). Phrases like 'solve the inequality', 'find the set of values of $x$' or 'find the range of values' are strong cues. This contrasts with equation problems where only discrete roots are required.

Motivation — Why this works

A continuous polynomial can only change sign at its real roots; between consecutive roots its sign is constant. Therefore solving the associated equation locates every possible sign change, partitioning $\mathbb{R}$ into intervals on which the inequality has uniform truth value. Once the critical values are known, the original inequality reduces to a finite check of sign on each interval (or to reading off intervals using the shape of the parabola). This converts a continuous condition into a finite, mechanical procedure.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the problem asks for a set of $x$-values, not isolated solutions, so the answer will be one or more intervals.
  2. Rules out completing the square and reading off $(x+a)^2>k$, since that route is clumsier when the leading coefficient is not $1$ and the inequality is not already a perfect square.
  3. Commits to the critical-value method: roots of the quadratic are the only places where its sign can flip, so locating them is the essential first step.
  4. Bridges to the concrete operation by replacing '$>$' with '$=$' and solving the resulting quadratic.
  5. Plans to follow up with a sign analysis (sketch of the parabola or sign table) and to translate the answer back into interval/inequality notation, mindful of strict vs non-strict.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Replace the inequality sign with '$=$' to form the associated equation, e.g. $2x^2+5x-3=0$.
  2. Solve the equation by factorising, formula, or completing the square to obtain the critical values.
  3. Mark the critical values on a number line; they partition $\mathbb{R}$ into intervals.
  4. Determine the sign of the original expression on each interval (using the parabola's shape or a test value).
  5. Select the intervals matching the original inequality, taking care over whether endpoints are included ($\le,\ge$) or excluded ($<,>$).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $2x^2+5x-3>0$. Set $2x^2+5x-3=0\Rightarrow(2x-1)(x+3)=0$, giving critical values $x=-3$ and $x=\tfrac{1}{2}$. Since the parabola opens upwards, the expression is positive outside the roots. Hence the solution set is $x<-3$ or $x>\tfrac{1}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Rational inequality such as $\dfrac{x-1}{x+2}>0$
Find critical values from both numerator $=0$ and denominator $=0$; the denominator's zero is a vertical asymptote (excluded), and a sign table on the resulting intervals is used rather than parabola shape.
Cubic or higher polynomial inequality, e.g. $x^3-x<0$
Factorise fully to find all real roots, then build a sign table across all resulting intervals; the curve's shape is no longer parabolic so test values are safer.
Modulus inequality such as $|2x-1|<3$
First convert to a polynomial inequality (e.g. by squaring or splitting into cases), then apply the critical-value method to the resulting quadratic or linear pieces.
Quadratic inequality with parameter, e.g. find $k$ such that $x^2+kx+1>0$ for all $x$
Critical values are replaced by a discriminant condition; the move shifts from finding roots to ensuring no real roots exist.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the problem as an equation and reporting the critical values themselves as the final answer.
  • Choosing the wrong pair of intervals by assuming the answer is always 'between the roots' regardless of the direction of the inequality or the sign of the leading coefficient.
  • For rational inequalities, multiplying both sides by the denominator without considering its sign, which can flip the inequality.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting that strict inequality ($<,>$) excludes the critical values, while ($\le,\ge$) includes them (except where the expression is undefined).
  • Mis-sketching the parabola when the leading coefficient is negative, leading to the wrong choice of intervals.
  • Writing the solution as a single compound inequality like $-3>x>\tfrac{1}{2}$, which is logically impossible, instead of using 'or' between two separate intervals.
  • Algebraic slips when factorising a quadratic with a non-unit leading coefficient.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1/P2): solving quadratic and simple polynomial/rational inequalities via critical values and sign analysis, expressed in interval or inequality notation.
