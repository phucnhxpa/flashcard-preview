Coordinate Geometry

Substitute a linear equation into a curve equation to reduce to a single-variable solve

Family: Point On Line Substitution Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever you need the intersection point(s) of a straight line with another curve (a conic such as xy = c², y² = 4ax, an ellipse, or even another line). Visual cues: one equation is linear (e.g. x + y = k, ty = x + at², or a vertical/horizontal line x = c), and the other expresses a product or higher-degree relation in x and y. Equivalent phrasings: 'find where the tangent/normal/chord meets the directrix/asymptote/curve', or 'show the line cuts the curve at points P and Q'. Whenever one equation can be rearranged to express one variable explicitly, this move applies.

Motivation — Why this works

Substitution collapses a two-equation system into a single equation in one unknown, which is always easier to handle. If the linear equation gives x or y directly (vertical/horizontal line), the substitution yields a linear equation — one solve. If it gives y = mx + k, substituting into a degree-2 curve yields a quadratic, whose two roots correspond to the two intersection points (or a repeated root if the line is tangent). The underlying principle is that degree(line) × degree(curve) bounds the number of intersections, so the resulting polynomial has predictable, manageable degree.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structure: I have two simultaneous equations, one of which is linear in x and y.
  2. Consider alternatives: I could parameterise the curve (e.g. x = ct, y = c/t for xy = c²) and substitute the parameter form into the line, but that introduces a new variable and an extra back-substitution — less direct.
  3. Commit to substitution because the linear equation is the 'cheapest' to rearrange: solving it for one variable costs nothing and immediately reduces dimension.
  4. Decide which variable to isolate: pick the one that produces the cleaner expression (often whichever has coefficient 1, or whichever the second equation depends on more simply).
  5. Substitute and identify the resulting equation's degree: linear if the line was vertical/horizontal, quadratic if both equations are general — anticipate using the quadratic formula or factoring.
  6. After solving for one coordinate, back-substitute into the linear equation (not the curve) to recover the other coordinate cleanly.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the linear equation and rearrange for one variable, e.g. y = k − x, or read off x = c directly if vertical.
  2. Substitute this expression into the second (curve) equation, replacing every occurrence of that variable.
  3. Simplify and rearrange to standard form: ax² + bx + c = 0 (or simply a linear equation if the line was axis-parallel).
  4. Solve: factor, complete the square, or apply the quadratic formula x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac))/(2a); simplify any surds (e.g. √800 = 20√2).
  5. Back-substitute each x-value into the linear equation to obtain the corresponding y-value.
  6. State each intersection point as an ordered pair (x, y), making sure both coordinates are explicit.

Worked Micro-Example

Find where $x + y = 30$ meets $xy = 25$. From the line: $y = 30 - x$. Substitute: $x(30-x) = 25 \Rightarrow x^2 - 30x + 25 = 0$. Quadratic formula: $x = \frac{30 \pm \sqrt{800}}{2} = 15 \pm 10\sqrt{2}$. Then $y = 30 - x = 15 \mp 10\sqrt{2}$, giving the two intersection points.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line is vertical (x = c) or horizontal (y = c), e.g. tangent meeting directrix x = −a
Substitute the fixed coordinate directly into the other equation; the result is linear in the remaining variable, solved in one step.
Line meets a parabola y² = 4ax
Substitute y = mx + k into y² = 4ax to get a quadratic in x (or substitute x = (y² )/(4a) into the line to get a quadratic in y) — same logic.
Line meets an ellipse or circle
Substitute the linear relation into the conic; the resulting quadratic's discriminant tells you whether the line is a secant, tangent, or non-intersector.
Rectangular hyperbola in parametric form (x = ct, y = c/t)
Substitute the parametric coordinates into the line equation to get a quadratic in t; each root gives a parameter value of an intersection point.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to solve the system by elimination via addition/subtraction when one equation is non-linear — this fails because xy or y² cannot be cancelled linearly.
  • Parameterising the curve when the line equation is already linear and easy to rearrange — adds an unnecessary variable.
  • Treating the curve as a function y = f(x) when it is implicit (e.g. xy = 25 must be rearranged to y = 25/x carefully, watching for x = 0).

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when rearranging to standard form (e.g. 30x − x² = 25 becomes x² − 30x + 25 = 0, not x² + 30x − 25 = 0).
  • Failing to simplify surds (e.g. leaving √800 instead of 20√2) — costs accuracy marks.
  • Forgetting to compute the second coordinate after solving for the first; an intersection 'point' must be a full (x, y) pair.
  • Algebraic slip when simplifying expressions like −a + at² to a(t² − 1), occasionally written as a(t² + 1).
  • Pairing the x-values with the wrong y-values when the quadratic gives ±-paired roots; back-substitute carefully.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (P1–P4) and Further Pure scope. Uses only the quadratic formula (formula booklet) and basic algebraic manipulation. Avoid generalising to systems of degree ≥ 3 in both variables (e.g. cubic curves) — those are out of A-level scope and may produce equations not solvable by elementary methods.
