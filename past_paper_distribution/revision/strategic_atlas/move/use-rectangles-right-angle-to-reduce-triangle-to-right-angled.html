Coordinate Geometry

Use rectangle's right angle to reduce triangle to right-angled

Family: Geometric Construction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a triangle is formed using two adjacent sides of a rectangle (or a side and a segment lying along an adjacent side). Typical cue: a point $O$ sits on edge $DC$ of rectangle $ABCD$, and the triangle in question has $D$ (or another rectangle corner) as a vertex with the other two vertices along perpendicular edges. Also fires for squares, and for any quadrilateral whose vertex angle is stated to be $90^\circ$. The phrase 'ABCD is a rectangle' is itself the cue — it silently gives four right angles.

Motivation — Why this works

Every rectangle vertex is a $90^\circ$ angle, so any triangle that uses two edges meeting at that vertex is automatically right-angled there. This converts an otherwise generic triangle (needing cosine rule, sine rule, or coordinates) into a SOH-CAH-TOA / Pythagoras problem. The right angle is 'free' geometric information that students often overlook because it is implied rather than marked. Recognising it collapses several lines of working into one.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the triangle's three vertices: two are corners of the rectangle, one lies on a side.
  2. Rule out cosine rule and coordinate setup — these are wasteful if a right angle is already present.
  3. Commit once I confirm the two sides meeting at the rectangle corner lie along perpendicular edges of the rectangle.
  4. Identify which vertex carries the right angle, then label opposite/adjacent/hypotenuse relative to the angle I actually want.
  5. Choose between $\sin$, $\cos$, $\tan$, or Pythagoras based on which two sides are known.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. State: $ABCD$ is a rectangle $\Rightarrow \angle ADC = 90^\circ$.
  2. Note that $O$ lies on $DC$, so segment $DO$ lies along $DC$ and $\angle ADO = 90^\circ$.
  3. Conclude $\triangle AOD$ is right-angled at $D$.
  4. Label sides relative to the target angle (e.g. for $\angle AOD$: opposite $= AD$, adjacent $= OD$, hypotenuse $= OA$).
  5. Apply the appropriate trig ratio or Pythagoras.

Worked Micro-Example

Rectangle $ABCD$ has $AD = 8\sqrt{2}$, and $O$ lies on $DC$ with $OD = 4$, $OA = 12$. Since $\angle ADC = 90^\circ$ and $O \in DC$, $\triangle AOD$ is right-angled at $D$. Then $\cos(\angle AOD) = \dfrac{OD}{OA} = \dfrac{4}{12} = \dfrac{1}{3}$, so $\angle AOD = \arccos\!\left(\tfrac{1}{3}\right) \approx 70.5^\circ$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Square $ABCD$ with point on a side
Same right-angle argument; additionally all sides equal, often simplifying ratios.
Point $O$ on extension of $DC$ beyond $C$ or $D$
Right angle at $D$ still holds since $O$ is collinear with $DC$; just check $OD$ length sign/direction.
Diagonal of rectangle as hypotenuse
Triangle formed by two adjacent sides and the diagonal is right-angled at the shared corner; use Pythagoras for the diagonal length.
Cuboid problem (3D trig)
A face of the cuboid is a rectangle, so the same right-angle reduction works inside that face before lifting to 3D.
Circle inscribed/triangle inside rectangle
Drop perpendiculars to rectangle sides to recreate right triangles using the rectangle's $90^\circ$ corners.

Common Misreadings

  • Defaulting to cosine rule on $\triangle AOD$ without noticing the rectangle gives a right angle at $D$.
  • Believing the right angle sits at $O$ (where the point lies on the side) rather than at the rectangle corner $D$.
  • Assuming $\triangle AOC$ or $\triangle AOB$ is right-angled at $O$ — it generally is not unless $AO \perp DC$.

Pitfalls

  • Mislabelling opposite vs adjacent relative to the wrong angle (using $\angle OAD$ instead of $\angle AOD$).
  • Confusing $OD$ with $OC$ when $O$ is given by its distance from the wrong endpoint.
  • Forgetting that the rectangle's right angle is at the corner, not at the interior point on the side.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure (P1/P2) trigonometry and geometry; uses only Pythagoras and SOH-CAH-TOA, no advanced theorems.
