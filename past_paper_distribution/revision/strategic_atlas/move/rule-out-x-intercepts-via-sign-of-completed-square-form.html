Algebra & Functions

Rule out x-intercepts via sign of completed square form

Family: Discriminant Alternative Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a quadratic is already in completed square form $y = a(x+p)^2 + q$ (or can be cheaply put there) and you need to decide whether the curve meets the x-axis — typically for a sketch, range statement, or 'show that the equation has no real roots' task. Especially natural when the discriminant computation would involve awkward arithmetic but the vertex $(-p, q)$ is visible at a glance. Cues include phrases like 'hence sketch', 'show that ... has no real solutions', or 'state the minimum value'.

Motivation — Why this works

If $a > 0$ then $a(x+p)^2 \ge 0$ for all real $x$, so $y \ge q$ with equality at the vertex; symmetrically if $a < 0$ then $y \le q$. Hence the sign of $q$ (relative to $0$, when $a > 0$) decides whether $y = 0$ is attainable. Setting $y = 0$ and isolating the square produces $(x+p)^2 = -q/a$, which has no real solution exactly when $-q/a < 0$. This is logically equivalent to $b^2 - 4ac < 0$ but reads off the structure directly without arithmetic.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the quadratic is already in completed square form $a(x+p)^2 + q$ with $a$ and $q$ both positive (or both negative).
  2. Rule out factorising or quadratic formula — the form already exposes the vertex and hence the minimum (or maximum) value, so a discriminant detour is wasteful.
  3. Commit to a sign argument: the minimum value of $y$ is $q$, attained at $x = -p$, and $q > 0$ means the entire graph lies strictly above the x-axis.
  4. Bridge to operation: set $y = 0$ and isolate the squared term to expose the impossibility $(x+p)^2 = \text{negative}$.
  5. Inside the move, decide whether to phrase the conclusion via 'minimum value is $q > 0$' or via 'square cannot be negative' — both are acceptable; pick whichever the question wording invites.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify $a$, $p$, $q$ in $y = a(x+p)^2 + q$ and check the sign of $a$.
  2. Set $y = 0$ to test for x-intercepts: $a(x+p)^2 + q = 0$.
  3. Rearrange to $(x+p)^2 = -q/a$.
  4. Observe that the right-hand side is negative (when $a$ and $q$ share sign), while $(x+p)^2 \ge 0$.
  5. Conclude no real $x$ satisfies the equation, so the curve has no x-intercepts.
  6. Reflect the conclusion in the sketch: the parabola sits entirely on one side of the x-axis, with vertex $(-p, q)$.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $y = 3(x+2)^2 + 1$, set $y = 0$: $3(x+2)^2 = -1$, so $(x+2)^2 = -\tfrac{1}{3}$. Since $(x+2)^2 \ge 0$ for all real $x$, no real solution exists. Equivalently, the minimum value of $y$ is $1 > 0$, so the curve lies entirely above the x-axis with vertex $(-2, 1)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic with $a > 0$ but $q < 0$ (e.g. $y = 2(x-1)^2 - 8$)
Sign analysis now confirms two x-intercepts; solve $(x-1)^2 = 4$ directly to get $x = -1, 3$ instead of using the quadratic formula.
Quadratic with $a < 0$ and $q < 0$ (downward parabola entirely below x-axis)
Maximum value is $q < 0$, so by the same sign argument no x-intercepts exist; phrase as 'maximum is $q$, which is negative'.
'Show that $x^2 + bx + c > 0$ for all real $x$' style proofs
Complete the square to $(x + b/2)^2 + (c - b^2/4)$ and argue the constant term is positive, so the whole expression is positive — no need to invoke the discriminant explicitly.
Discriminant comparison check
Verify with $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$ if the question explicitly asks for it; for $y = 3(x+2)^2 + 1 = 3x^2 + 12x + 13$, $\Delta = 144 - 156 = -12 < 0$.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming every parabola must cross the x-axis and attempting to factorise or apply the quadratic formula despite the completed square already revealing the vertex above the axis.
  • Confusing the minimum value $q$ with the y-intercept (which is $ap^2 + q$), and then arguing about the wrong quantity.
  • Treating $-q/a < 0$ as a sign requiring a discriminant computation rather than a direct read-off.

Pitfalls

  • Concluding 'no real roots' algebraically but then sketching the curve touching or crossing the x-axis — the diagram must match the conclusion.
  • Forgetting to check the sign of $a$: if $a < 0$, the vertex is a maximum, and a positive $q$ would mean the curve does cross the x-axis, opposite to the $a > 0$ case.
  • Dropping the factor $a$ when isolating the square: writing $(x+p)^2 = -q$ instead of $(x+p)^2 = -q/a$.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths P1 (completing the square, quadratic graphs, discriminant); the sign-of-a-square argument is a standard alternative to the discriminant and is fully acceptable in 'show that' questions.
