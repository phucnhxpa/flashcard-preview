Algebra & Functions

Apply polynomial long division to decompose an improper rational expression

Family: Polynomial Division Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

The move fires whenever you face a rational expression P(x)/D(x) where deg(P) ≥ deg(D), or equivalently whenever the question demands a 'quotient + remainder/divisor' form. Concrete cues: (i) the question explicitly asks for the form A(x) + R/D(x) with stated unknown constants/coefficients; (ii) you need an oblique/horizontal asymptote of a rational function (deg numerator − deg denominator = 1 or 0); (iii) you are told a polynomial factor of a higher-degree polynomial and must extract the complementary factor (remainder must be zero); (iv) you need to differentiate or integrate an improper rational function and want each piece in standard form. In all these cases the structural signal is the same: the fraction is improper, and a clean polynomial-plus-proper-fraction split is required.

Motivation — Why this works

Polynomial long division is the unique algorithm that writes P(x) = Q(x)·D(x) + R(x) with deg(R) < deg(D), giving P(x)/D(x) = Q(x) + R(x)/D(x). This is the polynomial analogue of the integer identity 17/5 = 3 + 2/5: it isolates the 'whole part' Q(x) (which carries the dominant/asymptotic behaviour) from a proper-fraction tail (which → 0 as x → ±∞). The decomposition makes downstream calculus (differentiation, integration, limits) routine and exposes structural information — oblique asymptotes, missing factors, third roots of cubics — that is otherwise hidden in the original fraction.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structural mismatch: the numerator's degree is at least the denominator's, so the fraction is improper, OR a known factor must be divided out of a higher-degree polynomial.
  2. Identify the target form demanded by the question: 'P(x) + Q/(x − a)', 'oblique asymptote y = …', or 'remaining factor after dividing by …'. All three are outputs of the same algorithm.
  3. Rule out alternatives: partial fractions (only relevant once the fraction is proper); Vieta/sum-of-roots (works only when one unknown root remains and the sum is clean); equating coefficients via P(x) ≡ (Ax+B)D(x) + C (valid but less systematic for higher degrees); guessing with the Factor Theorem (not guaranteed in one pass).
  4. Commit to long division because it is mechanical, guaranteed to terminate in deg(P) − deg(D) + 1 steps, and produces both Q(x) and R simultaneously — and crucially, it shows method marks.
  5. Set up the dividend with explicit zero placeholders for any missing powers of x to keep columns aligned during subtraction.
  6. At each stage, divide leading terms to get the next quotient term, multiply the entire divisor by it, subtract carefully (watch signs), and continue until the running remainder has degree strictly less than deg(D).
  7. Interpret the output according to context: read off P and Q for an algebraic identity; declare y = Q(x) as the asymptote (justifying R/D → 0 as x → ±∞); or, if extracting a factor, verify the remainder is exactly zero.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the dividend P(x) in descending powers of x, inserting 0·x^k placeholders for any missing terms.
  2. Divide the leading term of the current dividend by the leading term of D(x); record this as the next term of the quotient Q(x).
  3. Multiply the entire divisor D(x) by that new quotient term and subtract the result from the current dividend, carefully tracking signs.
  4. Repeat steps 2–3 with the new (lower-degree) dividend until the running remainder has degree strictly less than deg(D).
  5. Assemble the answer as P(x)/D(x) = Q(x) + R(x)/D(x) and read off whatever the question asks: coefficients of Q, the constant remainder R, the asymptote y = Q(x), or the complementary factor (with R = 0).

Worked Micro-Example

Decompose $\dfrac{4x^2 - 10x - 1}{x - 3}$. Divide $4x^2 \div x = 4x$; multiply: $4x(x-3) = 4x^2 - 12x$; subtract: $2x - 1$. Divide $2x \div x = 2$; multiply: $2(x-3) = 2x - 6$; subtract: remainder $5$. Hence $\dfrac{4x^2 - 10x - 1}{x - 3} = 4x + 2 + \dfrac{5}{x - 3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Divisor with leading coefficient ≠ 1 (e.g. 2x − 8)
Each quotient term acquires a fractional coefficient (e.g. x² ÷ 2x = ½x); the algorithm is unchanged but arithmetic care is needed.
Finding an oblique asymptote
After dividing, state explicitly that R(x)/D(x) → 0 as x → ±∞, so y = Q(x) is the asymptote.
Extracting the complementary factor when a quadratic factor of a cubic is known
Divide the cubic by the known quadratic; the quotient is the remaining linear factor, and the remainder must be exactly 0 — use this as a check.
Identity-matching alternative
Write P(x) ≡ (Ax + B)D(x) + C and equate coefficients to solve for A, B, C — same decomposition, no division layout.
Synthetic division with a linear divisor (x − a)
Use the streamlined coefficient-only tableau; faster but only works when the divisor is linear.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing this with partial fractions: partial fractions split a proper fraction over factored denominators; long division must precede partial fractions whenever the fraction is improper.
  • For oblique asymptotes, taking a 'leading coefficients' shortcut and missing the constant term that only emerges from full division (e.g. writing y = ½x instead of y = ½x + 2).
  • Reporting the remainder as the asymptote (e.g. y = 11) instead of the quotient.
  • Dividing by an individual complex linear factor instead of the real quadratic factor, generating unnecessary complex intermediate coefficients.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when subtracting the product back, especially with negative coefficients in the divisor.
  • Forgetting to insert zero placeholders for missing powers of x, which throws off column alignment.
  • Mishandling fractional quotient terms when the divisor's leading coefficient is not 1.
  • Stopping too early (remainder still has degree ≥ deg(D)) or too late (carrying the algorithm into the proper-fraction regime).
  • When extracting a known factor, failing to verify the remainder is exactly zero — a nonzero remainder signals an arithmetic slip earlier.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (P3/P4) and Further Pure scope; no formula-booklet entry is needed — long division is an assumed algorithmic skill. Avoid invoking general ring-theoretic division algorithms or formal power-series long division, which are out of scope.
