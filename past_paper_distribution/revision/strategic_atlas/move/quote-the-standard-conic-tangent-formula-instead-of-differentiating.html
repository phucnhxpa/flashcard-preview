Coordinate Geometry

Quote the standard conic tangent formula instead of differentiating

Family: Formula Deployment Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an FP3 question asks for the tangent (or normal, via the partner formula) at a named point on a standard conic — ellipse x²/a² + y²/b² = 1, hyperbola x²/a² − y²/b² = 1, or parabola y² = 4ax — given either parametrically (a cos θ, b sin θ), (a sec t, b tan t), (at², 2at) or as a general point (x₁, y₁). Cue phrases include 'find the equation of the tangent at the point P with parameter…', 'show that the tangent at P meets the axes at…', or anything that will immediately need axis intercepts. If the question does not say 'using differentiation', this is the efficient route.

Motivation — Why this works

The tangent equations are bookwork results in FP3, derived once via implicit differentiation and then reusable. Quoting them avoids re-running the implicit-differentiation → gradient → point-slope → rearrange chain, which is fraction-heavy and error-prone under time pressure. The quoted form is also already in symmetric intercept-friendly shape, so setting x = 0 or y = 0 reads off the axis intercepts directly — useful when the next part asks for an area or a midpoint.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the point is given parametrically on a standard conic and the tangent is wanted — this is exactly the bookwork case.
  2. I rule out implicit differentiation: it produces dy/dx = −b²x/(a²y), then needs evaluation at (a cos θ, b sin θ) and a point-slope rearrangement — four fraction-algebra steps for a known result.
  3. I commit to quoting the standard formula because subsequent parts almost certainly want intercepts or chord properties, which the quoted form exposes immediately.
  4. I identify the conic type and parametrisation, then write down the matching tangent template.
  5. I substitute the parameter values carefully — particularly watching the minus sign in the hyperbola version and the ty = x + at² shape for the parabola — and present the line in the form the next part will need.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the conic and the parametrisation given.
  2. For ellipse x²/a² + y²/b² = 1 at (a cos θ, b sin θ): write (x cos θ)/a + (y sin θ)/b = 1.
  3. For hyperbola x²/a² − y²/b² = 1 at (a sec t, b tan t): write (x sec t)/a − (y tan t)/b = 1.
  4. For parabola y² = 4ax at (at², 2at): write ty = x + at².
  5. If point is Cartesian (x₁, y₁): use xx₁/a² ± yy₁/b² = 1 (sign matches the conic).
  6. Sanity-check by substituting the point: both sides should reduce to 1 (or the parabola identity).

Worked Micro-Example

Find the tangent to $\tfrac{x^2}{9}+\tfrac{y^2}{4}=1$ at $P(3\cos\theta,\,2\sin\theta)$. Quote the standard form: $\tfrac{x\cos\theta}{3}+\tfrac{y\sin\theta}{2}=1$. Intercepts read off immediately: $x$-axis at $(3\sec\theta,0)$, $y$-axis at $(0,2\csc\theta)$ — ready for any area-of-triangle follow-up.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Tangent at Cartesian point (x₁,y₁) on ellipse
Use xx₁/a² + yy₁/b² = 1 — the 'replace one x with x₁, one y with y₁' rule.
Tangent to hyperbola at (a sec t, b tan t)
Use (x sec t)/a − (y tan t)/b = 1; note the MINUS, mirroring the conic's sign.
Tangent to parabola y² = 4ax at (at², 2at)
Use ty = x + at²; not in symmetric '= 1' form.
Normal at the same parametric point
Use the partner normal formula (e.g. ax sec θ − by csc θ = a² − b² for the ellipse) rather than re-deriving.
Rectangular hyperbola xy = c² at (ct, c/t)
Tangent is x + t²y = 2ct — a different bookwork template.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing the tangent formula with the normal formula and quoting the wrong one.
  • Misremembering the hyperbola sign as plus instead of minus.
  • Using the parametric tangent template with Cartesian coordinates (or vice versa), getting algebraic nonsense.
  • Reading 'the point with parameter t' as a Cartesian point and substituting into xx₁/a² + yy₁/b² = 1.

Pitfalls

  • If the question says 'using differentiation, find…' then quoting loses method marks — derive instead.
  • Sign slip on the hyperbola: it is (x sec t)/a − (y tan t)/b = 1.
  • Forgetting that a, b in the formula are the semi-axis lengths, not a², b² — easy to over-divide.
  • Mixing up sec t / tan t with cos t / sin t when the conic has switched between ellipse and hyperbola in adjacent question parts.

A-level Scope Notes

All formulas here (ellipse, hyperbola, parabola, rectangular hyperbola tangents and normals) are explicit bookwork in Edexcel IAL Further Pure 3; quoting them is fully accepted unless the question demands derivation.
