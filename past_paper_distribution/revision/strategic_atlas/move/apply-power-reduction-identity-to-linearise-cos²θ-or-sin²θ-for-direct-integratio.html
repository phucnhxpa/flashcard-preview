Trigonometry

Apply power-reduction identity to linearise cos²θ or sin²θ for direct integration

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

After squaring r = f(θ) in a polar area calculation (or after expanding any trigonometric expression), the integrand contains an even power such as cos²θ, sin²θ, cos²(nθ), or sin²(nθ). These squared trig terms have no direct antiderivative in the formula booklet and block term-by-term integration. The cue is visual: you see a 'cos²' or 'sin²' sitting inside an integral sign, possibly alongside already-integrable terms like 1, cosθ, or sinθ. Equivalently, the trigger fires whenever expanding (1 + cosθ)², (a + b sinθ)², or similar produces a squared trig term.

Motivation — Why this works

The power-reduction (linearisation) identities cos²θ = ½(1 + cos2θ) and sin²θ = ½(1 − cos2θ) replace a squared trig term with a constant plus a single cosine of double angle — both of which integrate to standard forms (∫c dθ = cθ, ∫cos(kθ)dθ = sin(kθ)/k). This is the only A-level-viable route: integration by parts loops back on itself, and direct substitution does not simplify the square. The identities are derived from the double-angle formula cos2θ = 2cos²θ − 1 = 1 − 2sin²θ, rearranged.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand contains cos²θ or sin²θ (or cos²(nθ)) — a squared trig term that has no entry in the standard integration table.
  2. Rule out integration by parts: ∫cos²θ dθ via parts produces ∫sin²θ dθ and loops, requiring a cleverer trick anyway. Rule out u-substitution: setting u = cosθ gives du = −sinθ dθ, but there is no sinθ factor to absorb.
  3. Commit to the power-reduction identity because it is the unique A-level tool that converts cos²θ into a sum of immediately-integrable pieces.
  4. Recall the exact form carefully: cos²θ = ½(1 + cos2θ), with the ½ factor outside — a frequent slip is to drop the ½ or to mis-sign the sin² version.
  5. Substitute the identity into the integrand, distribute any constants from the area formula (e.g. the ½ from ½∫r² dθ), and combine like terms before integrating.
  6. Integrate term by term using ∫cos(kθ)dθ = (1/k)sin(kθ), being careful with the 1/k coefficient on the double-angle term.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Expand r² fully, e.g. r = a(1 + cosθ) ⇒ r² = a²(1 + 2cosθ + cos²θ).
  2. Identify each squared trig term and replace using cos²θ = ½(1 + cos2θ) or sin²θ = ½(1 − cos2θ).
  3. If cross-products like cosθ·cos2θ appear, apply product-to-sum: cosA cosB = ½[cos(A−B) + cos(A+B)].
  4. Collect constants: e.g. 1 + 2cosθ + ½(1 + cos2θ) = 3/2 + 2cosθ + ½cos2θ.
  5. Integrate term by term: ∫(3/2)dθ = 3θ/2, ∫2cosθ dθ = 2sinθ, ∫½cos2θ dθ = ¼sin2θ.
  6. Apply limits and multiply by any outer factor (e.g. ½ from the polar area formula).

Worked Micro-Example

Compute $\int_0^{\pi} (1+\cos\theta)^2\,d\theta$. Expand: $1 + 2\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta$. Linearise: $\cos^2\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}(1+\cos 2\theta)$, so integrand $= \tfrac{3}{2} + 2\cos\theta + \tfrac{1}{2}\cos 2\theta$. Integrate: $\tfrac{3\theta}{2} + 2\sin\theta + \tfrac{1}{4}\sin 2\theta$. Evaluate $0 \to \pi$: $\tfrac{3\pi}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Integrand contains sin²θ instead of cos²θ
Use sin²θ = ½(1 − cos2θ) — identical structure but with a minus sign; everything else proceeds the same way.
Integrand contains cos²(nθ) or sin²(nθ) for some integer n
Use cos²(nθ) = ½(1 + cos2nθ); the doubled angle becomes 2nθ, and ∫cos(2nθ)dθ = sin(2nθ)/(2n).
Higher even power, e.g. cos⁴θ
Apply the identity twice: cos⁴θ = [½(1 + cos2θ)]² = ¼(1 + 2cos2θ + cos²2θ), then linearise cos²2θ = ½(1 + cos4θ).
Cubic term cos³θ from expanding (1 + cosθ)³
Use cos³θ = cosθ(1 − sin²θ) and substitute u = sinθ, or apply the triple-angle formula cos3θ = 4cos³θ − 3cosθ.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying integration by parts on ∫cos²θ dθ — technically possible but loops and is far slower.
  • Treating cos²θ as (cosθ)² and attempting u = cosθ, which fails because no sinθ dθ factor is present.
  • Linearising r itself rather than r² in polar area problems.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the ½ factor: writing cos²θ = 1 + cos2θ instead of ½(1 + cos2θ).
  • Sign error in the sin² version: confusing sin²θ = ½(1 − cos2θ) with ½(1 + cos2θ).
  • Forgetting the 1/k chain factor when integrating: ∫½cos2θ dθ = ¼sin2θ, not ½sin2θ.
  • Forgetting the outer ½ from the polar area formula A = ½∫r² dθ when combining with the ½ from the identity.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (P3 trig integration) and Further Pure (FP2/FP3 polar area). The double-angle identities cos2θ = 1 − 2sin²θ = 2cos²θ − 1 appear in the formula booklet; the rearranged power-reduction forms are expected to be derived or recalled. Avoid invoking complex-exponential derivations (e^{iθ}) — out of scope.
