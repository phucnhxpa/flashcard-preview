Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question says 'express in partial fractions', 'decompose', or asks for a form like A/(...) + B/(...) and supplies (or expects you to factorise) a denominator. The first cue is a rational expression whose denominator is — or can be — written as a product of factors. Before writing any template, check (i) the fraction is proper (numerator degree < denominator degree) and (ii) the factor types: distinct linear, repeated linear, or irreducible quadratic. The template is dictated entirely by these factor types.

Motivation — Why this works

The partial fractions theorem guarantees that any proper rational function decomposes uniquely into a sum determined by its denominator's factor structure: each distinct linear factor contributes a constant-over-linear term, each repeated linear factor (ax+b)^k contributes one term per power 1..k, and each irreducible quadratic contributes a (Cx+D)-over-quadratic term. Matching the template to the factor types ensures the resulting linear system in the unknown constants has a unique solution. A wrong template either under-parameterises (no solution) or over-parameterises (indeterminate).

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the instruction is 'express in partial fractions' and inspect the denominator's factorisation — here (2x+1)(x−2), two distinct linear factors.
  2. Rule out long division by comparing degrees: numerator degree 1 < denominator degree 2, so the fraction is proper and no quotient polynomial is needed.
  3. Commit to the distinct-linear template because there are no repeated factors and no irreducible quadratic — each linear factor earns exactly one constant numerator.
  4. Bridge to operation: write the identity (11x−1)/[(2x+1)(x−2)] ≡ A/(2x+1) + B/(x−2), introducing exactly two unknowns matching the two factors.
  5. Tactically, defer the choice of solving method (cover-up vs substitution vs equating coefficients) until after clearing denominators; the template itself is fixed by structure alone.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Confirm the fraction is proper: deg(numerator) < deg(denominator). If not, perform polynomial long division first and decompose the remainder fraction.
  2. Factorise the denominator fully (if not already given) and classify each factor: distinct linear, repeated linear (ax+b)^k, or irreducible quadratic.
  3. Assign template terms: A/(linear) for each distinct linear factor; A_1/(ax+b) + A_2/(ax+b)^2 + ... + A_k/(ax+b)^k for a repeated linear factor; (Cx+D)/(quadratic) for each irreducible quadratic.
  4. Write the identity with ≡ (identical for all x), e.g. (11x−1)/[(2x+1)(x−2)] ≡ A/(2x+1) + B/(x−2).
  5. Multiply through by the full denominator to obtain a polynomial identity ready for cover-up, substitution of convenient x-values, or equating coefficients.

Worked Micro-Example

Decompose $\dfrac{11x-1}{(2x+1)(x-2)}$. Degrees: $1<2$, proper. Factors: two distinct linears. Template: $\dfrac{11x-1}{(2x+1)(x-2)} \equiv \dfrac{A}{2x+1} + \dfrac{B}{x-2}$. Clearing denominators: $11x-1 \equiv A(x-2) + B(2x+1)$, ready to solve by substituting $x=2$ and $x=-\tfrac12$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Repeated linear factor, e.g. denominator $(2x+1)^2(x-2)$
Use $\dfrac{A}{2x+1} + \dfrac{B}{(2x+1)^2} + \dfrac{C}{x-2}$ — one term per power up to the multiplicity.
Irreducible quadratic factor, e.g. denominator $(x^2+1)(x-2)$
Use $\dfrac{Cx+D}{x^2+1} + \dfrac{A}{x-2}$ — linear numerator over the quadratic.
Improper fraction, e.g. numerator degree $\ge$ denominator degree
Polynomial long-divide first, then apply the partial fractions template to the proper remainder.
Denominator not pre-factorised
Factorise first (looking for rational roots, difference of squares, or grouping) before classifying factor types.
Three distinct linear factors, e.g. $(x-1)(x+2)(2x-3)$
Template extends naturally: $\dfrac{A}{x-1} + \dfrac{B}{x+2} + \dfrac{C}{2x-3}$, one constant per factor.

Common Misreadings

  • Skipping the proper-fraction check and applying the template directly to an improper fraction, producing a decomposition that cannot reproduce the polynomial part.
  • Treating a repeated factor $(ax+b)^2$ as if it were two distinct factors, writing only $A/(ax+b)$ and omitting the $B/(ax+b)^2$ term.
  • Treating an irreducible quadratic as if it were linear, putting just a constant $A$ over it instead of $(Cx+D)$.
  • Adding a stray constant term '+C' to the template, confusing the proper-fraction case with the improper-case quotient.

Pitfalls

  • Placing $(Ax+B)$ over a linear factor instead of just $A$, over-parameterising the system.
  • Forgetting to fully factorise — leaving a quadratic that actually factors over the rationals leads to an unnecessarily complex template.
  • Mislabelling the multiplicity of a repeated factor and writing too few or too many tower terms.
  • Using $=$ instead of $\equiv$ and then 'solving' for $x$ rather than treating the relation as an identity in $x$.

A-level Scope Notes

Edexcel IAL P4 covers partial fractions with denominators consisting of distinct linear factors, repeated linear factors, and (in Further Pure where applicable) irreducible quadratic factors; improper fractions are handled by prior long division. No complex-factor or higher-multiplicity quadratic cases are required.
