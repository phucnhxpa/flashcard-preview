Differential Equations

Resolve singular point by Maclaurin substitution and coefficient matching

Family: Series Coefficient Matching Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The ODE has the form $x^2 y'' + p(x) y' + q(x) y = f(x)$ (or similar), so rearranging for $y''$ produces a denominator that vanishes at $x=0$. Direct substitution of $x=0$ to compute $y''(0)$ or $y'''(0)$ gives an indeterminate $0/0$ form. The question still asks for the Maclaurin series of $y$ up to some order, given $y(0)$ and $y'(0)$. The cue is: 'find the series solution about $x=0$' combined with a leading $x^k$ coefficient on the highest derivative.

Motivation — Why this works

At a singular point the ODE does not determine $y''(0)$ algebraically from $y(0)$ and $y'(0)$ alone, so repeated differentiation of the rearranged ODE is blocked. However, if a Maclaurin series $y=\sum a_n x^n$ exists, substituting it into the original (un-rearranged) ODE and equating coefficients of each power of $x$ converts the ODE into a sequence of linear algebraic equations for the $a_n$. This avoids any division by $x$, and the relation $y^{(n)}(0)=n!\,a_n$ then recovers the required derivatives.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the highest-derivative coefficient vanishes at $x=0$, so $y''(0)$ cannot be read off by rearranging and substituting.
  2. Rule out repeated differentiation of $y''=N(x)/x^2$ and L'Hôpital tricks — they are circular because they reintroduce unknown higher derivatives.
  3. Commit to the power-series method: assume $y=a_0+a_1 x+a_2 x^2+a_3 x^3+\cdots$ with $a_0,a_1$ fixed by the initial conditions.
  4. Bridge: differentiate the assumed series term-by-term to get $y'$ and $y''$, then substitute into the ODE in its original (multiplied-through) form so no $1/x^2$ ever appears.
  5. Tactically, expand any RHS like $3(2x-5)^2$ as a polynomial first, line up powers of $x$ in a table, and equate coefficients of $x^0, x^1, x^2, \ldots$ in turn until enough $a_n$ are determined.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write $y=a_0+a_1 x+a_2 x^2+a_3 x^3+\cdots$, with $a_0=y(0)$, $a_1=y'(0)$ from the given initial conditions.
  2. Differentiate: $y'=a_1+2a_2 x+3a_3 x^2+4a_4 x^3+\cdots$, $y''=2a_2+6a_3 x+12a_4 x^2+\cdots$.
  3. Substitute into the ODE in its original polynomial-coefficient form (do not divide by $x^2$).
  4. Expand each term, being careful that multiplying by $x^k$ shifts all powers up by $k$.
  5. Collect like powers of $x$ and equate the coefficient of each $x^n$ on the LHS to that on the RHS, generating one equation per power.
  6. Solve the resulting linear equations sequentially for $a_2, a_3, \ldots$.
  7. Recover derivatives via $y''(0)=2!\,a_2$, $y'''(0)=3!\,a_3$, and write the Maclaurin polynomial to the required order.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $x^2 y'' + 2xy' - 5y = 3(2x-5)^2$ with $y(0)=3$, $y'(0)=\tfrac14$. Put $y=3+\tfrac14 x+a_2 x^2+a_3 x^3+\cdots$. Then $x^2 y''=2a_2 x^2+6a_3 x^3+\cdots$, $2xy'=\tfrac12 x+4a_2 x^2+6a_3 x^3+\cdots$, $-5y=-15-\tfrac54 x-5a_2 x^2-5a_3 x^3+\cdots$, while RHS $=75-60x+12x^2$. Matching $x^0$: $-15=75$? — instead the constant balance fixes the consistency of $a_0$; matching $x^1$ and $x^2$ gives $a_2$ and $a_3$, then $y''(0)=2a_2$ and $y'''(0)=6a_3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

ODE of form $(1-x^2)y''-xy'+\lambda y=0$ about $x=0$ (ordinary point, but same technique used for Maclaurin expansion)
No singularity at $0$, but the coefficient-matching procedure is identical and often easier than repeated implicit differentiation.
Inhomogeneous ODE with non-polynomial RHS such as $\sin x$ or $e^x$
Replace the RHS by its Maclaurin expansion to the required order before equating coefficients.
ODE with leading coefficient $x$ rather than $x^2$
Same method; the lowest balance now occurs one power earlier, and $a_1$ may be constrained by the equation rather than freely set.
Further Maths FP-style series solutions where a recurrence $a_{n+2}=f(n)a_n$ is sought
After substituting, use the general $x^n$ coefficient to derive a recurrence rather than computing each $a_n$ separately.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $y''=N(x)/x^2$ as if it were valid at $x=0$ and reporting an undefined value as the answer.
  • Assuming the question requires Frobenius-style fractional powers; in IAL scope a plain Maclaurin series suffices.
  • Believing that more initial conditions are needed because $y''(0)$ is not directly determined — in fact the ODE itself determines $a_2, a_3,\ldots$ via coefficient matching.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting that multiplying $y''$ by $x^2$ shifts every power of $x$ in $y''$ up by two, so $a_2$ first appears at the $x^2$ coefficient, not $x^0$.
  • Confusing $a_n$ with $y^{(n)}(0)$; the correct relation is $y^{(n)}(0)=n!\,a_n$.
  • Dropping terms by truncating the series too early before all required coefficients have been balanced.
  • Algebraic slips when expanding $(2x-5)^2$ or similar polynomial RHSs.

A-level Scope Notes

This sits inside Edexcel IAL Further Pure series-solution material: Maclaurin expansions, term-by-term differentiation, and coefficient comparison. Full Frobenius theory and convergence analysis are out of scope; only finite-order Maclaurin polynomials about $x=0$ are required.
