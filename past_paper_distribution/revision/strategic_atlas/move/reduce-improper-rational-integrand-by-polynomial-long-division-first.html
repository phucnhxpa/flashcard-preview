Integration

Reduce improper rational integrand by polynomial long division first

Family: Long Division Reduction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an integrand is a rational function p(x)/q(x) with deg(p) ≥ deg(q), i.e. an improper algebraic fraction. Typical cues: a quartic over a quadratic, a cubic over a linear or quadratic, or any numerator whose leading power matches/exceeds the denominator. Recognise also when partial fractions are attempted and yield inconsistent coefficient equations — that is a symptom of skipping this reduction. The move is mandatory whenever standard techniques (partial fractions, completing the square, ln/arctan templates) require a proper fraction.

Motivation — Why this works

Every improper rational function can be uniquely written as quotient(x) + remainder(x)/q(x), where deg(remainder) < deg(q). The quotient is a polynomial that integrates termwise via the power rule, and the remainder fraction is now proper, so the standard A-level toolkit (partial fractions, ln-of-linear, k/(x²+a²) → arctan, numerator-as-derivative-of-denominator) applies. Skipping this step leaves no valid technique because partial fractions assumes properness as a structural premise. The reduction is purely algebraic and lossless.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the degrees: deg(numerator) ≥ deg(denominator), so the fraction is improper.
  2. Rules out direct partial fractions, u-substitution, or completing-the-square on the whole integrand — none accept an improper fraction.
  3. Commits to long division as the only structural reduction that produces an integrable form.
  4. Sets up the division carefully, inserting 0-coefficient placeholders for any missing powers in the denominator or numerator.
  5. Plans the post-division route: integrate the quotient by the power rule, then apply the appropriate proper-fraction technique to the remainder.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Confirm deg(numerator) ≥ deg(denominator).
  2. Perform algebraic long division, including zero placeholders for missing powers.
  3. Write integrand as quotient(x) + remainder(x)/q(x) with deg(remainder) < deg(q).
  4. Integrate the quotient termwise using ∫xⁿ dx = xⁿ⁺¹/(n+1).
  5. Apply partial fractions, completing the square, or numerator-splitting to the proper remainder and integrate.
  6. Combine results and add a single constant of integration.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int \frac{x^3}{x^2+1}\,dx$. Long division: $x^3 = x(x^2+1) - x$, so $\frac{x^3}{x^2+1} = x - \frac{x}{x^2+1}$. Then $\int x\,dx - \int \frac{x}{x^2+1}\,dx = \tfrac{1}{2}x^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}\ln(x^2+1) + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quartic over quadratic, e.g. $\int \frac{x^4+1}{x^2-1}\,dx$
Division yields a quadratic quotient plus a proper fraction; finish with partial fractions on $\frac{2}{x^2-1}$.
Cubic over linear, e.g. $\int \frac{x^3}{x-2}\,dx$
Division gives a quadratic quotient plus a constant/(x−2); finish with a single ln term.
Improper fraction in curve sketching or limits (Further Pure)
Same division reveals oblique/polynomial asymptote y = quotient(x) as x → ∞.
Equal degrees, e.g. $\int \frac{2x^2+3}{x^2+1}\,dx$
Division gives constant 2 plus $\frac{1}{x^2+1}$, integrating to $2x + \arctan x + C$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the improper fraction as if partial fractions can be applied directly, leading to over-determined or inconsistent coefficient equations.
  • Attempting a u-substitution on the whole integrand and getting stuck because the substitution does not simplify the improper structure.
  • Mistaking 'numerator is the derivative of denominator' shortcut as applicable when degrees do not actually permit it.

Pitfalls

  • Omitting zero-coefficient placeholders during long division when powers are missing, causing alignment errors.
  • Stopping division too early so the 'remainder' is not actually of lower degree than the denominator.
  • Integrating only the remainder fraction and forgetting the quotient polynomial.
  • Sign slips when subtracting partial products line by line in the division.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure 4 / Further Pure: polynomial long division and integration of proper rational functions via partial fractions, ln, and arctan templates are all standard syllabus tools.
