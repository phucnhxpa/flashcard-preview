Reflect below-axis portion in x-axis to sketch y = |f(x)|

Family: Graph Transformation Analysis Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks for a sketch of y = |f(x)| and the graph of y = f(x) (or its key features: vertex, intercepts, asymptotes) is already known or just sketched. Common phrasings: 'On the same axes sketch y = |f(x)|', 'Hence sketch...', or a follow-up to solving f(x) = 0. Also fires when an equation |f(x)| = k must be solved graphically and the modulus graph is needed first.

Motivation — Why this works

By definition |f(x)| = f(x) where f(x) ≥ 0 and |f(x)| = −f(x) where f(x) < 0, so the modulus leaves the above-axis part fixed and reflects the below-axis part in the x-axis. The x-intercepts of f are fixed points of the reflection, so they become the join points; if f had a smooth turning point below the axis it becomes a smooth maximum above, and if it had a sharp vertex below it stays sharp after reflection.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the request is y = |f(x)|, not y = f(|x|) or |y| = f(x), so the rule is 'reflect parts where y < 0'.
  2. Rule out reflecting the whole graph or reflecting in the y-axis — only the sub-axis portion moves.
  3. Commit because every x where f(x) < 0 must give a positive y, and the cleanest geometric realisation is reflection in y = 0.
  4. Bridge to operation: locate the x-intercepts of f (the hinge points) and identify which arcs/segments lie below the axis.
  5. Tactically, reflect each below-axis feature: a vertex (a, b) with b < 0 maps to (a, −b); arms/branches keep their shape but flip sign of gradient; intercepts stay put and become cusps (local minima) of |f(x)|.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Sketch (or recall) y = f(x), marking x-intercepts, turning points, and asymptotes.
  2. Identify the regions where f(x) < 0 (the parts strictly below the x-axis).
  3. Leave the parts where f(x) ≥ 0 unchanged.
  4. Reflect each below-axis portion in the x-axis: point (x, y) ↦ (x, −y), so minima below become maxima above.
  5. Mark the new key features: reflected turning points, the original x-intercepts as cusps/local minima of |f(x)|, and any horizontal asymptote y = c < 0 reflected to y = −c.
  6. Check the graph is continuous, lies entirely in y ≥ 0, and is non-smooth precisely at the original x-intercepts.

Worked Micro-Example

Let f(x) = x + 7/2 − ... actually take f(x) = |x + 7/2| − 10 (a V with vertex (−7/2, −10), x-intercepts −27/2 and 13/2). Below the axis on (−27/2, 13/2). Reflecting that segment: vertex (−7/2, −10) ↦ (−7/2, 10), giving a local max; intercepts (−27/2, 0) and (13/2, 0) become cusp local minima. Outer arms (gradients ±1) stay as they were.

Variants — Same move, different surface

f is a quadratic with two real roots, e.g. y = x² − 4
Reflect the arch lying below the axis to get a bump above; the parabola's smooth minimum becomes a smooth maximum, roots become cusps.
f is a cubic crossing the axis at three points
Reflect each below-axis arc separately; smooth turning points below become smooth turning points above, three roots become three cusps.
f is a reciprocal/exponential with horizontal asymptote y = c < 0
The asymptote reflects to y = −c > 0; branches below the axis flip above and approach the new asymptote.
f is trigonometric, e.g. y = sin x giving y = |sin x|
Each negative half-wave flips up, producing a periodic sequence of arches with cusps at multiples of π.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing y = |f(x)| with y = f(|x|): the latter reflects the right half of the graph in the y-axis, not the below-axis part in the x-axis.
  • Confusing y = |f(x)| with |y| = f(x), which reflects the above-axis part downward instead.
  • Treating |f(x)| as |x| inside f, e.g. writing |f(x)| = f(|x|) for a linear f.

Pitfalls

  • Smoothing out the join at an original x-intercept — the modulus produces a sharp cusp there, not a rounded minimum.
  • Reflecting a sharp vertex into a smooth maximum (or vice versa) — the local geometry at the reflected point matches the original up to sign of y.
  • Forgetting to reflect asymptotes that lie below the x-axis, or leaving part of the new graph below y = 0.
  • Failing to label the new coordinates of reflected turning points and the unchanged x-intercepts.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure content (P2/P3 graph transformations); applies to modulus sketches of linear, quadratic, cubic, reciprocal, exponential, logarithmic and trigonometric functions encountered in IAL Maths and Further Maths.
