Integration

Evaluate IBP boundary brackets at engineered limits to kill or simplify them

Family: Boundary Evaluation Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

After applying integration by parts inside a definite integral (typically while deriving a reduction formula), you face a boundary bracket of the form [u(x)v(x)] evaluated between two specific limits. The limits are usually 'engineered' — 0 and π/2, 0 and √(π/2), 0 and 1, or 0 and √π — so that trig factors hit 0 or 1, and power factors x^k hit 0 at the lower bound. Cues: limits chosen so that cos or sin lands on a multiple of π/2; products of the form x^n·cos x, x^n·sin x, sin^{n−1}x·cos x; the question asks you to derive a recurrence I_n = ... I_{n−2} or I_{n−4}.

Motivation — Why this works

Reduction formulas only become 'clean' if the boundary bracket either vanishes entirely or collapses to a simple constant. Boundary terms vanish via two complementary mechanisms: (i) at the upper limit a trig factor like cos(π/2) or sin(0) is exactly zero, and (ii) at the lower limit a positive power of x or sin x is exactly zero. Recognising this immediately means you don't carry dead expressions through the algebra. The principle is just careful evaluation of the FTC bracket [F(x)]_a^b = F(b) − F(a), exploiting that the question setter has aligned the limits with zeros of the trig/power factors.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice that after IBP I have a boundary bracket [u·v]_a^b plus a remaining integral, and the remaining integral is what should become I_{n−2} (or I_{n−4}).
  2. I considered just plugging both endpoints in mechanically, but I first scan whether the bracket is structurally killed — this saves algebra and reveals the intended shape of the recurrence.
  3. I commit to checking each endpoint factor-by-factor: at the upper limit, look for a trig factor that is 0 (cos(π/2)=0, sin(0)=0, sin(π)=0); at the lower limit, look for a power x^k or sin^k x that vanishes when k≥1.
  4. Bridge to operation: substitute x=b first, then x=a, into u(x)v(x); if any single factor is 0, the whole product is 0 — no need to evaluate the others.
  5. Tactical decision: track which endpoint contributes a non-zero constant (often only one does), since this constant becomes the inhomogeneous term in the recurrence I_n = (constant) + (coefficient)·I_{n−k}.
  6. Finally, note the range of n for which the vanishing argument is valid (e.g. need n ≥ 1 for x^n or sin^{n−1}x to be 0 at x=0); flag base cases that need separate treatment.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the boundary bracket explicitly: [u(x)v(x)]_a^b = u(b)v(b) − u(a)v(a).
  2. At the upper limit b: identify any factor that is exactly 0 (e.g. cos(π/2)=0, sin(π)=0). If found, that whole term is 0.
  3. At the lower limit a (usually 0): identify any factor x^k, sin^k x with k≥1, which is 0. If found, that whole term is 0.
  4. Combine the surviving contributions (often a single clean constant such as n(π/2)^{n−1} or ½(π/2)^{(n−1)/2}).
  5. Substitute the simplified bracket into the IBP equation and read off the recurrence I_n in terms of I_{n−2} (or I_{n−4}).

Worked Micro-Example

For $I_n = \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^n x\, dx$, IBP gives $I_n = [-\sin^{n-1}x \cos x]_0^{\pi/2} + (n-1)\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^{n-2}x\cos^2 x\,dx$. At $x=\pi/2$: $\cos(\pi/2)=0$, so the term is 0. At $x=0$: $\sin^{n-1}(0)=0$ for $n\ge 1$, so the term is 0. Hence the bracket vanishes and $I_n = (n-1)\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^{n-2}x(1-\sin^2 x)\,dx$, leading to $I_n = \tfrac{n-1}{n}I_{n-2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Limits 0 to π/2 with integrand x^n sin x or x^n cos x
x^n kills the lower limit for n≥1; cos(π/2)=0 or sin(π/2)=1 controls the upper — typically one boundary term survives, giving an inhomogeneous recurrence.
Limits 0 to √(π/2) with integrand x^n sin(x²) or x^n cos(x²)
Use x²=π/2 at the upper limit so cos(x²)=0, sin(x²)=1; lower limit killed by x^k. Yields recurrences relating I_n to I_{n−4}.
Beta-type integral ∫_0^1 x^n(1−x)^m dx
Same idea: x=0 kills x^n at lower limit, (1−x)=0 kills the other factor at upper limit; both boundary terms vanish.
Limits 0 to √π giving x²=π
sin(π)=0 and cos(π)=−1 — different factors vanish, so re-check which bracket survives.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the boundary bracket as 'just an evaluation' and grinding through both endpoints without spotting the structural zero — leads to messy algebra and lost marks.
  • Confusing this with a substitution opportunity — the move is purely an evaluation, not a change of variable.
  • Assuming the boundary bracket always vanishes; sometimes one endpoint contributes a non-zero constant that is the key inhomogeneous term.

Pitfalls

  • Boundary vanishing at x=0 requires the exponent k≥1 in x^k or sin^k x; for n=0 or n=1, base cases must be computed directly.
  • Forgetting the minus sign in v=−cos x (from ∫sin x dx), which flips the sign of the surviving boundary term.
  • Misreading x^{n−1} at x=√(π/2) as (π/2)^{n−1} instead of the correct (π/2)^{(n−1)/2}.
  • Stating a recurrence without recording the range of n for which it is valid.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) reduction-formula questions; uses only IBP from the formula booklet plus standard values sin(0), cos(0), sin(π/2), cos(π/2). Avoid invoking Gamma/Beta function identities — stay with elementary IBP and direct endpoint evaluation.
