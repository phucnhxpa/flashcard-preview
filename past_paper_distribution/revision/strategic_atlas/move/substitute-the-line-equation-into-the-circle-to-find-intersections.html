Coordinate Geometry

Substitute the line equation into the circle to find intersections

Family: Line Circle Intersection Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Use this move whenever you need the intersection point(s) of a straight line and a circle (or another locus given by a Cartesian equation), and the line is given — or can easily be rearranged — as y = mx + c, x = k, or y = k. Typical cues: a circle equation in completed-square form (x−a)² + (y−b)² = r², plus a line through given points, a tangent/chord candidate, or a vertical/horizontal directrix-style line. Equivalent phrasings: 'find where the line meets the circle', 'show the line is a tangent', 'find the points of intersection', 'find the chord'. The move also fires for line-meets-asymptote/parabola/ellipse problems where one equation is linear.

Motivation — Why this works

Substituting the linear equation into the quadratic curve eliminates one variable, collapsing the simultaneous system into a single quadratic in the remaining variable. The number of real roots of that quadratic (via the discriminant) directly classifies the geometric situation: two roots ↔ secant, one repeated root ↔ tangent, no real roots ↔ miss. This is the standard, most reliable method on the syllabus and avoids the algebraic mess of squaring or solving two non-linear equations together.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that one equation is linear (y = mx + c, or x = k for a vertical line) and the other is quadratic in x and y — this asymmetry is the cue that substitution will reduce the problem to a single-variable quadratic.
  2. Rule out alternatives: solving the system by elimination of squared terms, parametrising the circle, or using distance-from-centre would all work but are heavier — substitution is the direct route on A-level.
  3. Commit because after substitution I get a quadratic ax² + bx + c = 0 whose discriminant or roots immediately answer the geometric question (intersection coordinates, tangency, chord length).
  4. Bridge to operation: replace y in the circle equation with the linear expression (or set x equal to the constant for a vertical line), then expand carefully.
  5. Tactical choice: keep the substitution in the variable that gives the cleaner expansion — usually substitute y in terms of x, but if the line is x = k or x = f(y), substitute the other way.
  6. After solving, back-substitute into the line (not the circle) to recover the partner coordinate — it's linear and avoids spurious roots.
  7. Finally, filter solutions against any geometric constraints in the question (half-line, x > 0, point on a specified arc, etc.).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the circle (or curve) equation and the line equation; rearrange the line to y = mx + c or x = k if not already.
  2. Substitute the linear expression into the curve equation, replacing y (or x) everywhere.
  3. Expand the squared brackets fully: (x − p)² = x² − 2px + p², being careful with signs.
  4. Collect like terms to obtain a quadratic Ax² + Bx + C = 0; divide through by any common factor.
  5. Solve via factorisation, completing the square, or the quadratic formula x = (−B ± √(B² − 4AC))/(2A); simplify the surd (e.g. √32 = 4√2).
  6. Substitute each x back into y = mx + c to obtain the corresponding y-coordinates.
  7. Discard any solution that violates a stated geometric restriction in the problem.

Worked Micro-Example

Find where y = x − 2 meets (x−8)² + (y−4)² = 18. Substitute: (x−8)² + (x−6)² = 18 ⇒ 2x² − 28x + 82 = 0 ⇒ x² − 14x + 41 = 0. Then x = (14 ± √32)/2 = 7 ± 2√2, giving y = 5 ± 2√2. Intersection points: (7 + 2√2, 5 + 2√2) and (7 − 2√2, 5 − 2√2).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line is vertical (x = k), e.g. a directrix meeting asymptotes or a circle.
Substitute the constant k for x directly into the curve; this leaves a quadratic (or pair of values) in y only — no expansion of (x − a)² needed beyond plugging in the number.
Showing the line is a tangent to the circle.
After substitution, do NOT solve — instead set the discriminant B² − 4AC = 0 and verify, or solve to find the unique repeated root which gives the point of contact.
Line meets a parabola, ellipse, or hyperbola asymptote.
Same substitution logic; the resulting equation is still a quadratic in one variable, solved identically.
Line given implicitly as ax + by + c = 0.
Rearrange to y = … (or x = …) first, choosing whichever variable has the simpler coefficient to isolate.

Common Misreadings

  • Mistaking 'shortest distance from centre to line' problems for intersection problems — those use the perpendicular-distance formula instead.
  • Treating a tangency question as needing two distinct intersection points, rather than a discriminant = 0 condition.
  • Trying to solve the system by squaring a rearranged distance equation when a clean linear substitution is available.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when expanding (x − p)² — particularly when p itself is an expression like (x − 2 − 4) reducing to (x − 6).
  • Arithmetic slips in the discriminant; e.g. failing to simplify √32 to 4√2, or miscomputing B² − 4AC.
  • Forgetting the ± when finding y from a symmetric set-up (e.g. asymptotes y = ±mx), so only one of the two intersection points is reported.
  • Back-substituting x into the circle equation (giving spurious sign choices) instead of into the line equation.
  • Ignoring a domain restriction stated in the question (e.g. half-line, x > 0) and reporting both roots when only one is valid.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1/P2 circle geometry; P3 further coordinate geometry including parabolas/ellipses/hyperbolas). Uses only the quadratic formula (formula booklet) and standard expansion; no parametric or vector methods required. Avoid invoking projective or general conic-intersection theory.
