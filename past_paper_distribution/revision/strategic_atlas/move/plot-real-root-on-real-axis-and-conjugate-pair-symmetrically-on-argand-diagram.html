Trigger — When to use

Fires when all roots of a real-coefficient polynomial (typically a cubic or quartic) have been found and the question asks to display or sketch them on an Argand diagram. The root set is a mix of real roots (on the real axis) and complex conjugate pairs. Cue phrases include 'show the roots on an Argand diagram', 'represent the roots in the complex plane', or 'plot z_1, z_2, z_3'. Also fires when verifying root structure visually after using the conjugate root theorem.

Motivation — Why this works

A complex number a + bi corresponds to the point (a, b) in the Argand plane, so a real root r = r + 0i lies on the horizontal (real) axis. Because the polynomial has real coefficients, non-real roots occur in conjugate pairs a ± bi, which map to (a, b) and (a, −b) — exact mirror images in the real axis. This forced symmetry is both a structural feature of the diagram and a self-check: if the conjugate points are not reflections, a computational error has occurred.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the root list contains one real number and a pair that are complex conjugates, so the diagram will have one point on the real axis and two symmetric points off it.
  2. I rule out treating the real root as if it has a non-zero imaginary part, and rule out plotting only the complex roots while forgetting the real one.
  3. I commit to the standard mapping a + bi ↦ (a, b) and use conjugate symmetry about the real axis as my structural anchor.
  4. First concrete operation: draw and label axes as Re and Im, then place each root using its real part as the horizontal coordinate and imaginary part as the vertical coordinate.
  5. Tactically, I label every point with its complex value (not just a dot), and visually check that the conjugate pair is reflected across the real axis.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Draw two perpendicular axes; label horizontal as Re (or Re(z)) and vertical as Im (or Im(z)).
  2. For each real root r, plot the point (r, 0) on the real axis and label it with its value.
  3. For each complex root a + bi, plot the point (a, b); for its conjugate a − bi, plot (a, −b).
  4. Label every plotted point with the corresponding complex number (e.g. z_1 = -1 + 3i).
  5. Verify visually that each conjugate pair is symmetric about the real axis; if not, recheck arithmetic.

Worked Micro-Example

Roots of $2z^3 - z^2 + 2z - 5 = 0$ are $z_1 = -1 - 3i$, $z_2 = -1 + 3i$, $z_3 = \tfrac{5}{2}$. On the Argand diagram, plot $(-1, -3)$, $(-1, 3)$, and $(2.5, 0)$. The first two are reflections of each other in the Re-axis, and the third lies on the Re-axis itself.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quartic with two distinct conjugate pairs (no real roots)
Plot four points forming two symmetric pairs about the real axis; no point lies on the real axis.
Quartic with two real roots and one conjugate pair
Plot two points on the real axis and one symmetric pair off it.
Repeated real root
Plot a single point on the real axis but annotate multiplicity (e.g. 'double root').
Purely imaginary conjugate pair ±bi
The pair lies on the imaginary axis at (0, b) and (0, −b), still symmetric about the real axis.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading the real root as having non-zero imaginary part and plotting it off the axis (e.g. 5/2 placed at (0, 2.5)).
  • Treating the Argand diagram as an (x, y) graph and swapping the roles of real and imaginary parts.
  • Assuming the conjugate of a + bi is −a − bi rather than a − bi, which destroys the real-axis symmetry.

Pitfalls

  • Omitting the real root entirely — focusing only on the complex roots.
  • Labelling axes as x and y instead of Re and Im, which can lose presentation marks.
  • Swapping real and imaginary parts when plotting, e.g. placing −1 − 3i at (−3, −1).
  • Failing to label points with their complex values, leaving the marker unable to identify which root is which.
  • Distorting scale so the conjugate symmetry is not visually apparent.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope: Argand diagrams, the conjugate root theorem for real-coefficient polynomials, and the mapping a + bi ↦ (a, b) are standard FP1/FP2 content.
