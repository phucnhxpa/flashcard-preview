Trigonometry

Scale per-cycle solution count by LCM-period repetitions over extended domain

Family: Periodicity Scaling Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A later part of a question extends the domain from a single fundamental interval (typically [0°, 360°]) to a much larger interval such as [0°, 2160°] and asks for the total number of solutions to the same trig equation. The per-cycle count has already been established (often via a sketch in an earlier part). Cues include 'how many solutions in [0°, k·360°]' or 'state the number of solutions' after a sketch part. The equation typically involves two periodic functions with different periods (e.g. tan θ and sin θ).

Motivation — Why this works

Solutions of f(θ) = g(θ) repeat whenever both f and g return to the same configuration, which happens with period equal to LCM of their individual periods. So the intersection pattern in any LCM-length window is identical, and the count over a domain that is a whole number of LCM-windows is just (per-cycle count) × (number of windows). This replaces a tedious global sketch by a single cycle count plus arithmetic.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Student notices the new domain is a clean multiple of 360° (here 2160° = 6 × 360°), signalling a scaling argument rather than a fresh sketch.
  2. They rule out re-sketching across [0°, 2160°] and rule out simply multiplying by the ratio of any single period (e.g. using tan's 180°).
  3. They commit to the LCM-of-periods principle because both functions must simultaneously return to the same values for the intersection pattern to repeat.
  4. They identify periods: tan θ has period 180°, 1 + sin θ has period 360°, so LCM = 360°.
  5. They compute 2160° ÷ 360° = 6 full repetitions and multiply by the per-cycle count from the earlier sketch part.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify periods of each side: period of tan θ = 180°, period of 1 + sin θ = 360°.
  2. Compute LCM of the periods: LCM(180°, 360°) = 360°.
  3. Confirm the extended domain is a whole multiple: 2160° ÷ 360° = 6.
  4. Read off the per-cycle solution count N from the earlier sketch part (here N = 6).
  5. Total solutions = N × 6 = 36.

Worked Micro-Example

Given tan θ = 1 + sin θ has 6 solutions in [0°, 360°] (from a sketch). For [0°, 2160°]: periods are 180° and 360°, so LCM = 360°. Number of full LCM-cycles = 2160° ÷ 360° = 6. Total solutions = 6 × 6 = 36.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation sin 2θ = cos 3θ on an extended domain.
Periods are 180° and 120°; LCM = 360°. Count solutions in one 360° window, then scale by (domain length)/360°.
Extended domain not a whole multiple of the LCM, e.g. [0°, 2000°].
Take floor(2000°/360°) = 5 full cycles giving 5N, then count solutions of the residual interval [1800°, 2000°] directly from the sketch and add.
Domain in radians, e.g. equation in sin x and cos 2x on [0, 6π].
Use LCM in radians: LCM(2π, π) = 2π; cycles = 6π/2π = 3; total = 3N.
Equation reducible to one trig function, e.g. 2 sin²θ = sin θ.
Only one period (360°) is involved, so 'LCM' is just that period; scaling proceeds identically.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the relevant period as that of just one side (e.g. using tan's 180° to get 12 cycles) and then trying to compensate.
  • Reading 2160° as 'about 6 times' and guessing a multiplier without verifying exact divisibility.
  • Confusing 'number of solutions' with 'number of cycles' and reporting 6 instead of 36.

Pitfalls

  • Using a single function's period instead of the LCM, giving the wrong cycle count.
  • Arithmetic slip in 2160 ÷ 360; the answer must be exactly 6 for clean scaling.
  • Double-counting an endpoint: if θ = 0 and θ = 2160° both satisfy the equation, ensure the per-cycle count convention matches the inclusive/exclusive endpoints used.
  • Forgetting to recheck that the per-cycle count from part (i) was over a full LCM-window (not a half-period).

A-level Scope Notes

Sits within Edexcel IAL Pure (P2/P3) trigonometric equations on extended domains; uses only periodicity of standard trig functions and basic LCM arithmetic, with no content beyond the IAL Maths/Further Maths syllabus.
