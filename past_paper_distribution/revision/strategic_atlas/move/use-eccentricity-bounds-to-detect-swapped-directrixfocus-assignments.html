Coordinate Geometry

Use eccentricity bounds to detect swapped directrix/focus assignments

Family: Constraint Checking Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires immediately after computing e from two given conic measurements (typically the semi-major axis-related quantity a/e and the focal distance ae, or directrix and focus data). The cue is a numerical value of e that violates its defining range: e > 1 is required for a hyperbola, 0 < e < 1 for an ellipse. Also fires when the problem states the conic type explicitly but your computed e contradicts it. Phrasings like 'the directrix is x = k and a focus is at (h,0)' often trigger this risk.

Motivation — Why this works

The two equations a/e = p and ae = q are symmetric under swapping p and q: this exchange replaces e with 1/e while leaving a² = pq unchanged. So if you assign the given values to the wrong sides, you obtain the reciprocal eccentricity. The conic-type constraint (e > 1 vs e < 1) acts as an unambiguous diagnostic that pins down the correct assignment without redoing algebra. This works because eccentricity and its reciprocal lie on opposite sides of 1.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the computed e fails the conic-type constraint (e.g. e = 12/13 < 1 but the problem demands a hyperbola).
  2. I rule out arithmetic error by recognising the structural symmetry: the system in a/e and ae is invariant under swap up to e ↔ 1/e.
  3. I commit to swapping the assignments rather than restarting, because a² = (a/e)(ae) is preserved so a is already correct.
  4. Concretely, I take the reciprocal of my current e and re-verify it now satisfies the bound.
  5. I double-check: the product of the two given quantities equals a² regardless of assignment, so only e flips.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute e from the initial assignment of given values to a/e and ae.
  2. Test e against the required bound: hyperbola needs e > 1; ellipse needs 0 < e < 1.
  3. If the bound fails, replace e by 1/e (equivalent to swapping which given value is a/e and which is ae).
  4. Confirm a² = (a/e)(ae) is unchanged, so a need not be recomputed.
  5. State the corrected e and proceed to find b² via b² = a²(e² − 1) for hyperbola or b² = a²(1 − e²) for ellipse.

Worked Micro-Example

Hyperbola has directrix x = 13/12 and focus (12,0), so a/e = 13/12 and ae = 12 give e² = 144/13·12/13... initial assignment yields e = 12/13 < 1, impossible. Swap: take a/e = 12, ae = 13/12, so e² = (13/12)/12 ... instead simply invert: e = 13/12 > 1. ✓ Then a² = (a/e)(ae) = (13/12)(12) ... wait, a² = 13, so b² = 13(169/144 − 1) = 13·25/144.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Ellipse with given directrix x = a/e and focus (ae, 0)
Reject any e > 1 and invert; check 0 < e < 1 holds after swap.
Conic given by semi-latus rectum l = a(1−e²) (ellipse) or a(e²−1) (hyperbola) plus another datum
Use the sign of (1 − e²) or (e² − 1) as the diagnostic; a negative b² flags wrong conic-type assumption.
Problem states 'a conic' without naming type
No swap needed — accept whichever e comes out; the value of e itself classifies the conic.

Common Misreadings

  • Accepting e < 1 for a stated hyperbola without checking and proceeding with wrong formulae.
  • Assuming the larger given number must be ae and the smaller a/e (or vice versa) without justification.
  • Confusing the directrix equation x = a/e with x = ae.

Pitfalls

  • Restarting the full calculation after the swap instead of simply inverting e — wastes time since a² is invariant.
  • Forgetting to update b² with the corrected e when computing the equation of the conic.
  • Inverting e but then mis-substituting into b² = a²(e² − 1), e.g. using the ellipse formula by habit.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard for Edexcel IAL Further Pure conic sections (ellipse and hyperbola in standard position), where eccentricity, foci (±ae, 0) and directrices x = ±a/e are core content.
