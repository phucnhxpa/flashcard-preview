Trigger — When to use

Fires on multi-part numerical integration questions where part (b) estimates ∫f(x) dx (typically by trapezium rule) and a later part says 'hence' find ∫g(x) dx over the same limits, with g(x) algebraically related to f(x). Cue words: 'hence', 'use your answer to', or matching limits with a structurally similar integrand. Especially common when both integrands share a transcendental term (like log₁₀(sin x), eˣ, or √(x)) with the same or opposite sign.

Motivation — Why this works

Definite integration is linear: ∫(af(x) + b) dx = a∫f(x) dx + b(upper − lower). If the new integrand can be written as af(x) + b, the new integral collapses into a constant plus a scalar multiple of the already-computed estimate. The mark scheme's 'hence' explicitly rewards this reuse — recomputing from scratch wastes time and forfeits method marks tied to the algebraic link.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice both integrands share the same non-trivial sub-expression (e.g. log₁₀(sin x)) — this signals an intended algebraic bridge.
  2. Rule out redoing the trapezium rule with fresh y-values: the word 'hence' and matching limits indicate reuse is mandatory.
  3. Commit to letting y = (original integrand) and solving for the shared sub-expression in terms of y.
  4. Substitute that expression into the new integrand and simplify to the form a − y or a + by.
  5. Split the new integral by linearity: ∫(constant) dx becomes constant × (b − a), and ∫y dx is the known estimate.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Let y denote the original integrand (e.g. y = 1 − log₁₀(sin x)).
  2. Solve for the shared sub-expression: log₁₀(sin x) = 1 − y.
  3. Substitute into the new integrand: 3 + log₁₀(sin x) = 3 + (1 − y) = 4 − y.
  4. Apply linearity: ∫ₐᵇ(4 − y) dx = 4(b − a) − ∫ₐᵇ y dx.
  5. Insert the known numerical estimate from part (b) for ∫ₐᵇ y dx and the limits' difference for the constant term.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose part (b) gave $\int_{0.5}^{3}(1-\log_{10}(\sin x))\,dx \approx 2.3$. For part (c): let $y = 1-\log_{10}(\sin x)$, so $\log_{10}(\sin x) = 1-y$. Then $3+\log_{10}(\sin x) = 4-y$, giving $\int_{0.5}^{3}(4-y)\,dx = 4(2.5) - 2.3 = 10 - 2.3 = 7.7$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Part (c) asks for ∫(af(x) + b) dx with a ≠ 1.
Result is a(b − a)·... no — apply linearity: a × (estimate) + b × (upper − lower).
New integrand has same sign on shared term, e.g. f(x) = 1 + log(sin x) and g(x) = 5 + log(sin x).
Then log(sin x) = y − 1, so g(x) = 5 + (y − 1) = 4 + y; integral becomes 4(b−a) + (estimate).
Shared term is eˣ, √x, or another non-log expression.
Same algebraic isolation; the bridge is independent of the function type.
Question uses Simpson's rule estimate instead of trapezium (Further Maths).
Identical reasoning — linearity of ∫ doesn't depend on which numerical method produced the estimate.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the two parts as independent and re-tabulating y-values for a fresh trapezium rule.
  • Identifying log₁₀(sin x) directly with y instead of with 1 − y, dropping the constant.
  • Forgetting that ∫(constant) dx over [a,b] equals constant × (b − a), not just the constant.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when isolating the shared term: writing log₁₀(sin x) = y − 1 instead of 1 − y.
  • Confusing y (the whole integrand) with just the transcendental piece inside it.
  • Multiplying the constant by the wrong interval length, e.g. using upper limit alone instead of (upper − lower).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 trapezium-rule questions and Further Pure Simpson's-rule extensions; relies only on linearity of the definite integral, which is standard A-level content.
