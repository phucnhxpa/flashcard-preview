Differentiation

Sign-analyze derivative components to conclude monotonicity

Family: Sign Analysis Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks to show f is increasing/decreasing on an interval or its domain, and f'(x) has already been computed (or is easy to compute) as a quotient, product, or square whose sign is structurally determined. Typical cues: 'show that f is an increasing function', 'prove f is decreasing for x > a', or derivatives of the form k/(x+a)², k·e^(g(x)), or a perfect square. The move applies when no inequality-solving is needed — the sign follows by inspecting positive/negative factors.

Motivation — Why this works

Monotonicity on an interval is equivalent to f'(x) keeping a constant sign there. If f'(x) decomposes into factors whose signs are individually obvious (positive constants, squares, exponentials, known-sign denominators), the sign of the whole follows by the rules of sign multiplication/division. This avoids solving f'(x) > 0 as an inequality and produces a short, examiner-friendly argument.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice f'(x) is a quotient/product whose pieces have structurally fixed signs (a constant numerator, a squared denominator).
  2. Rule out solving f'(x) > 0 as an inequality — unnecessary when each component's sign is transparent.
  3. Commit to a factor-by-factor sign argument: identify each piece as >0, <0, or ≥0 and combine.
  4. Bridge: state the domain restriction so the denominator/factors are guaranteed non-zero and signed.
  5. Tactically write the conclusion as 'numerator > 0 and denominator > 0, hence f'(x) > 0, so f is increasing'.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write f'(x) in a factored/quotient form making signs visible.
  2. Identify the sign of each factor on the given domain (constant, square, exponential, etc.).
  3. Combine signs using +/+ = +, +/− = −, etc., to get the sign of f'(x).
  4. Verify no factor is zero on the interval (so the inequality is strict).
  5. Conclude: f'(x) > 0 (or < 0) on the interval, hence f is increasing (or decreasing).

Worked Micro-Example

Let $f(x)=\dfrac{15x}{x+3}$ for $x>0$. Then $f'(x)=\dfrac{15(x+3)-15x}{(x+3)^2}=\dfrac{45}{(x+3)^2}$. Numerator $45>0$; denominator $(x+3)^2>0$ since $x+3>3>0$. Hence $f'(x)>0$ for all $x>0$, so $f$ is increasing on its domain.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Derivative of the form $-k/(x+a)^2$ with $k>0$
Negative numerator, positive denominator gives $f'(x)<0$, so f is decreasing.
$f'(x)=g(x)\,e^{h(x)}$
Use $e^{h(x)}>0$ always; sign of f' is sign of $g(x)$.
$f'(x)$ is a perfect square plus a positive constant, e.g. $(x-1)^2+2$
Square ≥0 plus positive ⇒ strictly positive everywhere; f increasing on ℝ.
$f'(x)$ is a quadratic with negative discriminant and positive leading coefficient
No real roots and opens upward ⇒ $f'(x)>0$ for all x; conclude increasing.
$f'(x)=\dfrac{p(x)}{q(x)^2}$ with $p$ of mixed sign
Denominator squared is positive; reduce to sign analysis of numerator $p(x)$ only.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $(x+a)^2$ as possibly negative or undefined without checking the domain.
  • Confusing 'f'(x) ≥ 0' with strict monotonicity — assuming non-strict suffices when the question demands strictly increasing.
  • Reading the move as requiring a full inequality solve rather than a sign inspection.

Pitfalls

  • Omitting the domain reference that guarantees the denominator is non-zero.
  • Stating 'f is increasing' without the explicit link 'f'(x) > 0 ⇒ f increasing'.
  • Algebra slip when simplifying f'(x) before inspecting signs (e.g. dropping a minus sign in the quotient rule).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 differentiation: uses only the principle that f'(x)>0 on an interval implies f is increasing there, with sign analysis via positive constants, squares, and exponentials.
