Algebra & Functions

Apply natural logarithm to invert the exponential and isolate the variable

Family: Inverse Function Application Frequency: 6 questions

Trigger — When to use

The equation has been reduced to the form e^{f(x)} = k where k is a positive constant and the unknown x sits trapped inside the exponent. Typical surface cues: a single isolated exponential term equal to a number (e.g. e^x = 3, e^{1-0.05t} = 4/3), or this form arising after a substitution u = e^x has been resolved back. No purely algebraic rearrangement (dividing, factoring, subtracting) can free a variable from an exponent — this is the visual signal that an inverse-function step is required. The question may explicitly request the answer in the form 'ln a' or as an exact value, reinforcing the choice of ln rather than log₁₀.

Motivation — Why this works

The natural logarithm is the inverse function of e^x, satisfying the identity ln(e^{f(x)}) = f(x). Applying ln to both sides of e^{f(x)} = k therefore collapses the left side to f(x) and converts the equation to f(x) = ln k, which is now an ordinary (often linear) equation in x. The step is valid precisely because k > 0 — non-positive values of k were already rejected at the earlier substitution stage since e^{anything} > 0. This is the canonical and essentially unique elementary technique for extracting a variable from a natural exponential.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the unknown is locked inside an exponent of base e, and the rest of the equation has already been reduced to a numerical constant on the right.
  2. I rule out further algebraic manipulation: dividing or rearranging cannot pull x out of the exponent. I also rule out log₁₀ because the base is e, so ln is the cleaner inverse and matches any 'ln a' form requested.
  3. I commit to applying ln to both sides because ln and exp are mutual inverses: ln(e^{f(x)}) = f(x) is an identity, so this single operation linearises the equation.
  4. Before applying it, I confirm the right-hand side is positive so ln is defined — this also justifies why any earlier negative root of a substituted quadratic was discarded.
  5. I apply ln to both sides and immediately use ln(e^{f(x)}) = f(x) to obtain f(x) = ln k, then solve the resulting linear equation in x.
  6. Finally, I decide on the required form: exact (e.g. x = ln 3, or simplified to −ln 2 via ln(1/a) = −ln a) versus a decimal to a stated precision, based on the wording of the question.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Confirm the equation is in the form e^{f(x)} = k with k > 0 and the exponential term isolated.
  2. Apply ln to both sides: ln(e^{f(x)}) = ln(k).
  3. Use the inverse identity ln(e^{f(x)}) = f(x) to obtain f(x) = ln(k).
  4. Solve the resulting (typically linear) equation for x by standard algebra.
  5. Simplify exact forms where appropriate (e.g. ln(1/2) = −ln 2) and/or evaluate numerically to the requested precision.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $e^{1-0.05t} = \tfrac{4}{3}$. Apply $\ln$ to both sides: $1 - 0.05t = \ln\tfrac{4}{3}$. Rearrange: $0.05t = 1 - \ln\tfrac{4}{3}$, so $t = 20\bigl(1 - \ln\tfrac{4}{3}\bigr) \approx 14.247$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Base is not e, e.g. 2^x = 5 or a^x = b
Take ln of both sides and use the power rule: x ln a = ln b, hence x = ln b / ln a (equivalently log_a b via change of base).
Base 10 exponential, e.g. 10^x = k
Apply log₁₀ instead of ln: x = log₁₀(k).
Linear exponent, e.g. e^{2x+1} = 5
Take ln to obtain 2x + 1 = ln 5, then solve the linear equation: x = (ln 5 − 1)/2.
Quadratic-in-exponential, e.g. e^{2x} − 5e^x + 6 = 0
First substitute u = e^x to get a quadratic in u, solve for u (discarding non-positive roots), then apply ln to each positive u to recover x.
Reciprocal right-hand side, e.g. e^x = 1/2
Apply ln then simplify ln(1/a) = −ln a to give the cleanest exact form, x = −ln 2.

Common Misreadings

  • Using log₁₀ when the base is e — gives a correct but messy x = log₁₀(k)/log₁₀(e) and fails to match an 'ln a' answer form.
  • Treating the exponential as if it could be 'cancelled' by dividing rather than recognising that an inverse function is needed.
  • Forgetting to first isolate the exponential term before taking logs (e.g. taking ln while a constant is still added to e^x).

Pitfalls

  • Confusing ln(a/b) with ln a / ln b — they are not equal; ln(a/b) = ln a − ln b.
  • Failing to simplify exact forms when required: leaving ln(1/2) instead of −ln 2, or leaving x·ln e instead of x.
  • Giving a decimal approximation when the mark scheme demands an exact value (e.g. writing 1.099 instead of ln 3).
  • Premature rounding of ln k in multi-step problems, propagating error into later arithmetic.
  • Mishandling a constant in the exponent: in 1 − 0.05t = ln(4/3), forgetting the leading 1 when rearranging.
  • Writing x = ln(negative) for a rejected branch of a substitution rather than correctly stating 'no solution'.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P2/P3) — uses the inverse relationship between e^x and ln x and the basic log laws ln(ab)=ln a+ln b, ln(a/b)=ln a−ln b, ln(a^n)=n ln a from the formula booklet's logarithm section. Avoid invoking complex logarithms or principal branches — at IAL, ln is only defined for positive real arguments, which is why non-positive candidate values must be rejected before this step.
