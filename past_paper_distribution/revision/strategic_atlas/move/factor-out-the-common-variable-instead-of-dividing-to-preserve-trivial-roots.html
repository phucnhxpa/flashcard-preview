Algebra & Functions

Factor out the common variable instead of dividing to preserve trivial roots

Family: Solution Preservation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation of the form $xf(x) = xg(x)$ or $x \cdot P(x) = 0$ arises — typically from equating two curves that both pass through the origin, or from setting up intersection/turning-point equations where every term shares a common factor (often $x$, $\sin\theta$, or $e^x$). Cues include: a 'show that' instruction producing a reduced polynomial of lower degree than expected, or a follow-up part asking about the number of intersections. The temptation to divide by the common factor must be resisted whenever that factor can vanish.

Motivation — Why this works

Dividing by an expression that may equal zero discards the root where it vanishes and is logically invalid at that value. Factoring instead yields a product equal to zero, and the zero-product principle preserves every solution: the trivial root from the common factor and the non-trivial roots from the remaining factor. This cleanly separates 'always-true' intersections (like the origin) from the conditional ones controlled by the reduced equation, which is usually exactly what later parts of the question rely on.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that after equating and rearranging, every term shares a factor of $x$ (or another expression).
  2. Rule out dividing through by $x$: this would lose the root $x = 0$ and is undefined there anyway.
  3. Commit to factoring because the 'show that' target is a reduced equation of lower degree than the full polynomial — this signals the examiner expects the trivial factor split off.
  4. Rearrange so one side is zero, then extract the common factor explicitly.
  5. State both branches: $x = 0$ separately, and the reduced equation as the required result, ready for use in subsequent parts.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Equate the two expressions and bring everything to one side: $x(4 - x^2) - Ax = 0$.
  2. Identify the common factor across all terms (here $x$).
  3. Factor it out: $x(x^2 - 4 + A) = 0$ (after sign-tidying $-x^3 + 4x - Ax = -x(x^2 - 4 + A)$).
  4. Apply the zero-product principle: $x = 0$ or $x^2 - 4 + A = 0$.
  5. Present the quadratic factor as the 'show that' result and retain $x = 0$ for later parts.

Worked Micro-Example

Equate $y = x(4 - x^2)$ with $y = Ax$: $x(4 - x^2) = Ax \Rightarrow 4x - x^3 - Ax = 0 \Rightarrow x(4 - x^2 - A) = 0$. Hence $x = 0$ or $x^2 - 4 + A = 0$. The trivial intersection at the origin is preserved, and the non-origin intersections are governed by the quadratic.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Trig equation $\sin\theta\cos\theta = \sin\theta$
Factor as $\sin\theta(\cos\theta - 1) = 0$ rather than dividing by $\sin\theta$, preserving $\theta = 0, \pi, 2\pi,\ldots$
Stationary points where $f'(x) = x \cdot g(x)$
Factor to record $x = 0$ as a stationary point and solve $g(x) = 0$ for the others.
Two curves through origin intersecting a line through origin
Always factor out $x$ — the origin is automatic and the reduced equation governs the remaining intersections.
Exponential equation like $e^x \cdot h(x) = e^x \cdot k(x)$
Here $e^x \neq 0$, so dividing is safe — but factoring is still cleaner and the same principle applies whenever the common factor can vanish.

Common Misreadings

  • Dividing by $x$ silently and then being unable to explain the origin intersection in a follow-up part.
  • Reading 'show that $x^2 - 4 + A = 0$' as the only equation governing all intersections, forgetting $x = 0$ is also a solution.
  • Treating the factored form as if $x = 0$ were extraneous rather than a genuine intersection point.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when rearranging: writing $x^2 + 4 + A = 0$ or $x^2 - 4 - A = 0$ instead of $x^2 - 4 + A = 0$.
  • Forgetting to bring all terms to one side before factoring, leading to $x(4 - x^2) = Ax$ being 'cancelled' on each side.
  • Stating only the quadratic factor in the final answer and omitting the $x = 0$ branch when later parts require it.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P1–P4 technique for curve intersections, stationary points, and trig equations; the zero-product principle and awareness of division by zero are core algebraic skills assessed throughout.
