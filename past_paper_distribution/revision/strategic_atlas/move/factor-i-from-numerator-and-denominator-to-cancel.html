Complex Numbers

Factor i from numerator and denominator to cancel

Family: Common Factor Extraction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a complex fraction has every term in both numerator and denominator carrying a factor of i (i.e. each term is purely imaginary). Typical cue: after substituting a value like z = i(1+√3) into an expression, you see sums such as i(1+√3) + pi or i(1+√3) + 3i where p, 3 are real. Equivalent phrasing: 'numerator and denominator are both purely imaginary'. Recognise this before reaching for the conjugate trick.

Motivation — Why this works

If N = i·A and D = i·B with A, B real, then N/D = A/B is real, because the i factors cancel. This is just the distributive law plus i/i = 1, and it converts a complex-over-complex quotient into a real-over-real quotient in one line. It is far cleaner than multiplying by the conjugate of D, which would expand into four terms and require collecting real and imaginary parts. The move exploits structure that conjugate multiplication ignores.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that every term in the numerator carries an i, and likewise every term in the denominator carries an i — both sums are purely imaginary.
  2. Rules out the default 'multiply by conjugate of denominator' reflex, since that would unnecessarily expand a structure that already shares a common factor.
  3. Commits to factoring i out of both because the resulting bracketed expressions are real, so the quotient simplifies immediately.
  4. Bridges to the operation by writing N = i·(real bracket) and D = i·(real bracket), then cancelling i.
  5. Tactically checks that all parameters (here p) are declared real before relying on the bracket being real-valued.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Confirm each term in the numerator is a real multiple of i; factor i out: N = i(1+√3+p).
  2. Confirm each term in the denominator is a real multiple of i; factor i out: D = i(4+√3).
  3. Cancel the common factor i: N/D = (1+√3+p)/(4+√3), which is real.
  4. Substitute back into the original equation and equate real/imaginary parts as required.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\dfrac{i(1+\sqrt{3}) + pi}{i(1+\sqrt{3}) + 3i} = k$ for real $p, k$.\nNumerator $= i(1+\sqrt{3}+p)$, denominator $= i(4+\sqrt{3})$.\nCancel $i$: LHS $= \dfrac{1+\sqrt{3}+p}{4+\sqrt{3}}$, which is real, so $k = \dfrac{1+\sqrt{3}+p}{4+\sqrt{3}}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Numerator and denominator share a common real factor (e.g. both divisible by 2 or by (1+√3))
Same idea — extract the shared real factor and cancel before any conjugate work.
Polar/exponential form quotient $\dfrac{r_1 e^{i\theta_1}}{r_2 e^{i\theta_2}}$
Factor $e^{i\theta}$ pieces and divide moduli; the cancellation principle is the same but expressed via subtraction of arguments.
Both num and denom are purely real multiples of a shared complex number $w$
Factor $w$ out of each; quotient reduces to a ratio of the real coefficients.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $pi$ as a generic complex term rather than recognising that, with $p$ real, $pi$ is purely imaginary and shares the i factor.
  • Believing the conjugate method is mandatory whenever a denominator looks complex, missing the shortcut.
  • Reading $i(1+\sqrt{3}) + 3i$ as already simplified and not factoring further to $i(4+\sqrt{3})$.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to verify that all parameters in the bracket (e.g. $p$) are real — if $p$ were complex, the bracket would not be real and the cancellation would not produce a real quotient.
  • Cancelling $i$ but forgetting to also cancel signs or coefficients (e.g. $-i$ vs $i$).
  • Arithmetic slip when collecting like real terms inside the bracket, e.g. $1+\sqrt{3}+3 \ne 4+\sqrt{3}$ if not careful (here it is correct because $1+3=4$).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope: uses only basic properties of $i$, distributivity, and cancellation in fractions — no advanced complex analysis required.
