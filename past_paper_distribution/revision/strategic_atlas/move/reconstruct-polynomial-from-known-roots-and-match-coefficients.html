Reconstruct polynomial from known roots and match coefficients

Family: Coefficient Comparison Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a polynomial equation has two or more unknown coefficients (e.g. $p$ and $q$) but you have already determined all roots and the leading coefficient. Typical cues: a complex root is given so its conjugate is forced, or a real root has been found by inspection and the remaining quadratic factor is in hand. Equivalent phrasings: 'find $p$ and $q$ given that $z = a+bi$ is a root', or 'given the roots are $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$, find the coefficients'.

Motivation — Why this works

A monic-up-to-leading-coefficient polynomial is uniquely determined by its roots, so $A(x-x_1)(x-x_2)(x-x_3)$ must be identical to the given $4x^3 - 19x^2 + px + q$. Expanding the factored form yields every coefficient simultaneously, and matching powers of $x$ reads off $p$ and $q$ in one pass. The known $x^3$ and $x^2$ coefficients also act as a free consistency check on the expansion.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that all roots and the leading coefficient are now known, so the polynomial is fully determined as a product of linear (and quadratic) factors.
  2. Rule out solving Vieta's relations as separate equations for $p$ and $q$ — workable but it duplicates effort when the factor expansion already produces every coefficient.
  3. Commit to expanding the factored form because the conjugate-pair quadratic and the remaining linear factor are already in hand.
  4. Bridge: multiply the quadratic factor by the linear factor carefully, collecting like powers of $x$.
  5. Tactically check that the $x^3$ and $x^2$ coefficients reproduce the given $4$ and $-19$ before reading off $p$ from the $x$-coefficient and $q$ from the constant term, being careful with signs.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Form the polynomial as $A \prod (x - x_i)$ using the leading coefficient $A$ and all known roots (combine complex conjugates into a real quadratic factor first).
  2. Expand the product fully, collecting like powers of $x$.
  3. Compare with the given polynomial $4x^3 - 19x^2 + px + q$ term by term.
  4. Verify the highest-degree coefficients match (sanity check on the expansion).
  5. Read off the unknowns from the remaining coefficients, taking care with the sign of the constant term.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $4x^3 - 19x^2 + px + q = 0$ has roots $\tfrac{3\pm i\sqrt{71}}{8}$ and $4$. The conjugate pair gives the quadratic factor $4x^2 - 3x + 5$, so the polynomial is $(4x^2 - 3x + 5)(x - 4) = 4x^3 - 19x^2 + 17x - 20$. Matching coefficients: $p = 17$ and $q = -20$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quartic with one complex conjugate pair plus two real roots, two unknown coefficients
Multiply the conjugate-pair quadratic by both linear factors; expansion yields all five coefficients at once.
Cubic where one real root is found by inspection and the other two are unknown but symmetric expressions appear
Divide out the known linear factor, then compare the quotient quadratic's coefficients with Vieta on the remaining roots.
Polynomial with repeated root
Include the repeated factor $(x-\alpha)^2$ in the reconstruction before expanding.
Substitute-a-known-root alternative
Plug each known root into the original equation to get linear equations in the unknowns; cleaner when only one unknown remains, heavier with complex roots.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $q$ as $-q$ (or vice versa) because the equation is written as $+ q = 0$, leading to a sign flip.
  • Trying to apply Vieta's formulas separately for each unknown when the factor expansion delivers both coefficients in one step.
  • Forgetting to include the leading coefficient $A$ in front of $\prod(x - x_i)$, producing a monic polynomial that disagrees with the given one.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error in the constant term: $(4x^2 - 3x + 5)(x - 4)$ gives $-20$, so $q = -20$, not $+20$.
  • Arithmetic slips when combining the three $x^2$ contributions or the two $x$ contributions during expansion.
  • Dropping the leading $4$ when forming the quadratic factor from the complex conjugate pair.

A-level Scope Notes
