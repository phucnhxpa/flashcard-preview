Merge same-base logs by product law then equate arguments

Family: Log Merge And Equate Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation, after homogenizing to a single base, takes the form log_a(M) + log_a(N) = log_a(P) — a sum of same-base logs on one side and a single same-base log on the other. Equivalent surfaces include log_a(M) − log_a(N) = log_a(P) (use quotient law) or log_a(M) + log_a(N) = k where k can be rewritten as log_a(a^k). The cue is: 'two logs on one side, one log (or a constant convertible to a log) on the other, all in the same base'.

Motivation — Why this works

The product law log_a(M) + log_a(N) = log_a(MN) compresses a sum of logs into a single log. Once each side is a single log_a, the strict monotonicity (one-to-one property) of log_a guarantees that log_a(P) = log_a(Q) ⟺ P = Q (with P, Q > 0). This eliminates the transcendental layer and reduces the problem to a polynomial or rational equation solvable by standard A-level techniques.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices both sides are pure log_a expressions in the same base — no stray constants or mixed bases remain.
  2. Rules out taking exponentials of both sides directly: that would work but is clumsier than merging first; also rules out switching to natural logs since the structure is already same-base.
  3. Commits to the product law because the LHS is literally log_a(·) + log_a(·), which is the exact pattern the law collapses.
  4. Bridges by writing log_a(MN) = log_a(P), then invokes one-to-one to drop the logs and write MN = P.
  5. Tactically checks domain (M, N, P all positive) so any spurious roots from the resulting polynomial can be discarded at the end.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Confirm all logs share the same base; if not, change base first.
  2. Apply the product law: log_a(M) + log_a(N) = log_a(MN).
  3. Rewrite the equation as log_a(MN) = log_a(P).
  4. Equate arguments: MN = P.
  5. Solve the resulting algebraic equation and reject any root that violates the original log domain (argument > 0).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\log_3(81) + \log_3(b) = \log_3(5b^2)$.\\ Merge LHS: $\log_3(81b) = \log_3(5b^2)$.\\ Equate arguments: $81b = 5b^2 \Rightarrow b(5b - 81) = 0$.\\ Reject $b=0$ (log undefined); so $b = \tfrac{81}{5}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Difference of logs equals a single log: $\log_a(M) - \log_a(N) = \log_a(P)$.
Use quotient law $\log_a(M/N) = \log_a(P)$, then equate $M/N = P$.
Sum of logs equals a constant: $\log_{10}(x) + \log_{10}(x+3) = 1$.
Rewrite the constant as a log: $1 = \log_{10}(10)$, merge LHS to $\log_{10}(x(x+3))$, then equate $x(x+3)=10$.
Coefficient in front of a log: $2\log_a(x) + \log_a(y) = \log_a(z)$.
First absorb the coefficient via power law $2\log_a(x) = \log_a(x^2)$, then merge by product law before equating arguments.
Mixed bases such as $\log_2(x) + \log_4(x) = 3$.
Change base first (e.g. $\log_4(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}\log_2(x)$) so all logs share a base, then merge and equate.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $\log_a(M) + \log_a(N)$ as $\log_a(M+N)$ instead of $\log_a(MN)$.
  • Believing a constant on the RHS blocks the technique, when in fact $k = \log_a(a^k)$ converts it.
  • Reading $\log_a(M)\cdot\log_a(N)$ as something the product law applies to (it does not).

Pitfalls

  • Equating arguments while bases differ — the one-to-one step requires identical bases.
  • Forgetting to enforce positivity of every original log argument, leading to acceptance of extraneous roots.
  • Dropping a coefficient in front of a log without first applying the power law.
  • Sign slip when combining via quotient law: writing $\log(M) - \log(N) = \log(N/M)$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P2/P3) coverage of logarithm laws and exponential equations; no results beyond product, quotient, power, and change-of-base laws are required.
