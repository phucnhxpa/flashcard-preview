Integration

Use total-area decomposition to replace awkward sub-region with complement

Family: Area Complement Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a diagram shows two adjacent regions R1 and R2 that together partition a region whose total area is a single clean integral, but one of the sub-regions (typically R2) has mixed boundaries — part bounded by a line, part by a curve, part by an axis — making direct integration require splitting at an awkward x-value. Cues include: 'the line cuts the curve at x = 1 − m', 'R1 = R2', or any prompt asking for an equation in a parameter rather than a numerical area. Alternative phrasing: 'shaded regions have equal area' where one shaded region is geometrically simple and the other isn't.

Motivation — Why this works

If R1 ∪ R2 exactly fills a region whose area A is easy to compute as a single integral, then R2 = A − R1 by additivity of area, with no new integration needed. When the problem also states R1 = R2, substitution gives 2·R1 = A, a single equation in the unknown parameter. This converts a two-integral problem (one of which is awkward) into one easy integral plus algebra, exploiting the same complement principle that underlies P(A') = 1 − P(A).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice R2 has mixed boundaries (line on one part, curve on another, x-axis below) — direct integration would need a split at x = 1 − m.
  2. I rule out computing R2 directly: it would require two integrals and careful bookkeeping of which boundary is active where.
  3. I commit to the complement approach because the diagram shows R1 + R2 fills the whole region under the parabola from 0 to 1, whose area is a single clean integral.
  4. Bridge: compute A = ∫₀¹(x − x²)dx = 1/6, then use R1 = R2 to get 2·R1 = A.
  5. Tactically, I only need a closed form for R1 (the easier region between curve and line) in terms of m, then solve 2·R1 = 1/6 for m.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the total region R1 ∪ R2 and compute its area as a single integral: A = ∫₀¹(x − x²)dx = [x²/2 − x³/3]₀¹ = 1/6.
  2. Express R1 in terms of the parameter m: R1 = (1 − m)³/6 (from the earlier part of the question).
  3. Apply R1 + R2 = A together with R1 = R2 to obtain 2·R1 = A.
  4. Substitute: 2 · (1 − m)³/6 = 1/6.
  5. Simplify to (1 − m)³ = 1/2, then solve for m: m = 1 − (1/2)^{1/3}.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose the line $y = m$ and curve $y = x - x^2$ partition the region under the curve on $[0,1]$ into $R_1$ (above the line) and $R_2$ (below). Total area $A = \int_0^1 (x - x^2)\,dx = \tfrac{1}{6}$. Given $R_1 = R_2$, we have $2R_1 = \tfrac{1}{6}$. With $R_1 = (1-m)^3/6$, we get $(1-m)^3 = \tfrac{1}{2}$, so $m = 1 - 2^{-1/3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two shaded regions inside a rectangle bounded by a curve, where one region is between curve and a chord and the other fills the rest of the rectangle
Total area A is the rectangle's area; R2 = A_rectangle − R1 with no integration of R2 needed.
Region between two curves split by a vertical line, where one sub-region has a clean integral and the other doesn't
Compute total ∫(upper − lower)dx over the full interval, then subtract the easy sub-region.
Volume of revolution problems where a solid is split into two parts and one part is awkward
Compute total volume by a single ∫πy²dx and subtract the easier piece.
Equal-area conditions in mensuration where one region is a triangle/trapezium
Use elementary area formula for the easy piece and total-minus-easy for the curved piece.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating R2 as just ∫_{1−m}^{1}(x − x²)dx, which gives the area under the curve on that sub-interval but ignores that R2 actually extends from the line down to the x-axis on a different sub-interval.
  • Assuming R1 + R2 equals the area of a bounding rectangle rather than the area under the curve — misreading which region is being partitioned.
  • Forgetting the condition R1 = R2 and trying to solve with only the total-area equation.

Pitfalls

  • Misidentifying the total region: if the line crosses the x-axis or the curve dips negative on the interval, R1 + R2 may not equal ∫(curve)dx and the decomposition fails.
  • Algebraic slip when solving (1 − m)³ = 1/2 — students sometimes write 1 − m = 1/2 instead of taking the cube root.
  • Using the wrong expression for R1 from the earlier part (e.g. forgetting a factor or sign) propagates into a wrong m.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 integration: polynomial integration, area between curve and line, and solving a cubic equation by taking a cube root. No techniques beyond standard A-level are required.
