Trigonometry

Decompose a compound angle into a known sum or difference to unlock identities

Family: Angle Decomposition Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an angle in the problem is not directly usable, but can be written as a sum or difference of angles that ARE usable. Two flavours: (a) a non-standard multiple like 3x, 4x, or (A+B) where the addition/double-angle formulae apply once you split it; (b) a geometric configuration where a total angle is partitioned into sub-angles, one known and one unknown, so the unknown is just (total − known). Visual cues include 'prove sin 3x = …', 'find the angle subtended by arc CD given the section angle is 2π/3', or any expression where the displayed angle does not match a formula booklet entry but a near neighbour (2x, x, π/2 − x, …) does.

Motivation — Why this works

The standard trig identities (sin(A±B), cos(A±B), double-angle, etc.) and the geometric identity 'angles on a partition sum to the whole' all require the angle to be expressed as a combination of simpler pieces. Decomposing the awkward angle into pieces you can name lets you invoke those identities directly. Mathematically this is just associativity of addition applied to the argument of a trig function, or to a partitioned angular region — but it is the indispensable first move that converts an unworkable expression into a chain of formula-book steps.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the target angle (3x, θ_CD, etc.) is not a standard formula-book angle and cannot be tackled head-on.
  2. Scan for nearby angles that ARE workable: 2x and x are in the formula book; the total fan angle and one sub-angle are given in the diagram.
  3. Rule out alternatives: writing 3x = x + x + x needs the addition formula twice (longer); guessing the triple-angle identity from patterns loses proof marks; assuming inner and outer arcs share an angle is geometrically wrong.
  4. Commit to the two-piece split — 3x = 2x + x for identities, or θ_total = θ_known + θ_unknown for geometry — because it matches exactly one application of the relevant identity/principle.
  5. Choose the split direction: for proofs, pick the split that leads to the side of the identity you must reach (e.g. 2x + x because the answer is in sin x, cos x); for geometry, isolate the unknown on one side: θ_unknown = θ_total − θ_known.
  6. Execute the substitution and immediately apply the named identity to convert the decomposed form into something operable.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the awkward angle θ and a known decomposition θ = α + β (or θ = total − γ).
  2. Substitute: replace the trig/angle expression with its decomposed form, e.g. sin 3x = sin(2x + x), or θ_CD = (2π/3) − θ_AB.
  3. Apply the matching identity: sin(A+B) = sin A cos B + cos A sin B, or simply combine fractions over a common denominator in the geometric case.
  4. Substitute any further sub-identities (double-angle: sin 2x = 2 sin x cos x, cos 2x = 1 − 2sin²x) to reduce to the target variables.
  5. Simplify and collect terms to match the required form.

Worked Micro-Example

Prove $\sin 3x = 3\sin x - 4\sin^3 x$. Write $\sin 3x = \sin(2x+x) = \sin 2x\cos x + \cos 2x \sin x$. Substitute $\sin 2x = 2\sin x\cos x$ and $\cos 2x = 1 - 2\sin^2 x$: $= 2\sin x\cos^2 x + (1-2\sin^2 x)\sin x$. Replace $\cos^2 x = 1 - \sin^2 x$: $= 2\sin x(1-\sin^2 x) + \sin x - 2\sin^3 x = 3\sin x - 4\sin^3 x.$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Triple angle for cosine: prove cos 3x = 4cos³x − 3cos x
Write cos 3x = cos(2x + x) and apply cos(A+B) = cos A cos B − sin A sin B; same skeleton, different identity.
Triple angle for tangent
Write tan 3x = tan(2x + x) and use the tangent addition formula tan(A+B) = (tan A + tan B)/(1 − tan A tan B).
Compound sector with three or more sub-arcs at the same centre
Use the partition principle as a single sum constraint: θ_total = θ₁ + θ₂ + θ₃; solve for the unknown by subtracting the known angles.
Non-standard angle like 75° = 45° + 30° for exact values
Same sin(A+B) expansion, with A, B chosen from the special-angle table.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 3x as a single 'unsplittable' angle and trying to manipulate sin 3x directly with no identity available.
  • Assuming inner and outer arcs in a compound sector subtend equal angles at the centre — they do not when the radii differ in this kind of problem.
  • Guessing the triple-angle identity from memory in a 'prove' question instead of deriving it from the decomposition.

Pitfalls

  • Confusing sin(A+B) = sin A cos B + cos A sin B with cos(A+B) = cos A cos B − sin A sin B — the sign and product structure differ.
  • Forgetting to also substitute the double-angle identities after the addition formula, leaving a mixed sin/cos expression instead of reducing to one variable.
  • Arithmetic slip when combining over a common denominator: e.g. (2π/3) − (5/3) = (2π − 5)/3, not (2π − 5)/6.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL P3 (compound and double-angle formulae, formula booklet entries for sin(A±B), cos(A±B), tan(A±B), sin 2A, cos 2A, tan 2A) and in P2 sector geometry (arc length s = rθ). Avoid generalising to product-to-sum identities or complex-exponential derivations of multiple-angle formulae — these are out of IAL scope.
