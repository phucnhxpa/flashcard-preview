Coordinate Geometry

Apply point-gradient form then rearrange to required line-equation format

Family: Line Equation Construction Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

You need the equation of a straight line and you have either (i) two known points, or (ii) one known point together with a gradient (e.g. a tangent gradient from differentiation, or a normal gradient from the negative reciprocal). The question typically asks for the answer in a specific format: y = mx + c, or ax + by + c = 0 with integer coefficients. Phrasings include 'find the equation of the tangent/normal at P', 'find the equation of the line through A and B', or 'show that the line has equation ...'.

Motivation — Why this works

The point-gradient form y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) is the most direct route from a (point, gradient) pair to a line equation — it requires no simultaneous equations and no separate computation of the y-intercept. When two points are given, computing m = (y₂ − y₁)/(x₂ − x₁) first reduces the situation to the (point, gradient) case. Rearranging to the requested format (clearing fractions by multiplying through by the denominator of m, then collecting all terms on one side) is mechanical and produces the unique standard form the mark scheme expects.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question gives either two points or a (point, gradient) pair, and demands a line equation in a specified format.
  2. If two points are given, compute the gradient first via m = (y₂ − y₁)/(x₂ − x₁); if a gradient is already given (tangent/normal), skip to the next step.
  3. Rule out y = mx + c with simultaneous equations — it works but adds an unnecessary step compared to point-gradient form.
  4. Commit to y − y₁ = m(x − x₁); when choosing which point to substitute, prefer the one with smaller/integer coordinates to minimise arithmetic errors.
  5. Read the required output format: if y = mx + c is asked, expand and isolate y; if ax + by + c = 0 with integer coefficients is asked, multiply through by the denominator of m to clear fractions, then move all terms to one side.
  6. Verify the result: check coefficients are integers (if required), check a chosen point satisfies the equation, and check the gradient is recoverable.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. If two points (x₁, y₁), (x₂, y₂): compute m = (y₂ − y₁)/(x₂ − x₁), being careful with sign order.
  2. Substitute into y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) using a chosen point.
  3. If m is a fraction p/q, multiply both sides by q to clear the denominator.
  4. Expand brackets fully, distributing to every term on each side.
  5. Collect all terms on one side to obtain ax + by + c = 0, or isolate y to obtain y = mx + c, as required.
  6. Check: substitute the original point back into the final equation to confirm it holds.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the normal at P(1/2, 3) given tangent gradient 5/2, in form ax+by+c=0. Normal gradient = −2/5. Point-gradient: $y - 3 = -\tfrac{2}{5}(x - \tfrac{1}{2})$. Multiply by 5: $5y - 15 = -2x + 1$. Rearrange: $2x + 5y - 16 = 0$. Check (1/2, 3): $1 + 15 - 16 = 0$ ✓.

Variants — Same move, different surface

One of the two points is the y-intercept (0, c)
After computing m, write y = mx + c directly — no rearrangement needed.
Gradient is an integer
Skip the multiply-through step; coefficients are already integer after expanding.
Gradient is 0 (horizontal tangent at a stationary point)
Equation collapses to y = y₁, i.e. y − y₁ = 0; no x-term appears.
Gradient is undefined (vertical line)
Cannot use point-gradient form; write x = x₁ directly, i.e. x − x₁ = 0.
Normal line at a point on a curve
Replace m by −1/m_tangent (negative reciprocal) before applying point-gradient form.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating a horizontal tangent (m = 0) as a vertical line and writing x = x₁ instead of y = y₁.
  • Leaving the answer as y = mx + c when the question specifies ax + by + c = 0 with integer coefficients — loses a presentation mark.
  • Confusing tangent gradient with normal gradient when the question asks for the normal.

Pitfalls

  • Inconsistent subtraction order in the gradient formula: using (y₂ − y₁) on top with (x₁ − x₂) on the bottom flips the sign.
  • Sign errors with negative coordinates: e.g. 28 − (−4) must give +32, not 24.
  • Forgetting to distribute the multiplier across every term when clearing fractions, especially the constant term.
  • Sign errors when moving terms across the equals sign to reach the '= 0' form — a quick substitution check catches this.
  • Leaving fractional coefficients when integer form is explicitly required.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 coordinate geometry. The point-gradient form y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) is given in the formula booklet. Negative-reciprocal relationship for perpendicular gradients (m₁m₂ = −1) is also booklet-standard. No vector or parametric line forms needed at this level for these questions.
