Trigger — When to use

Fires when a Cartesian function y = f(x) is built from parametric equations x = g(t), y = h(t) on a restricted t-interval, AND g(t) has a discontinuity (typically tan t, sec t, cot t, or 1/(t−a)) inside that interval. The question asks for the range of y as a function of x, or equivalently for the set of attained y-values. Cue phrases include 'find the range of f', 'state the range', or 'show that f(x) ≥ k'. Also fires when the t-interval straddles a vertical asymptote of x(t).

Motivation — Why this works

The Cartesian formula alone does not determine the range — only the x-values actually attained as t sweeps its domain do. A discontinuity of x(t) splits the t-interval into branches, each mapping to a sub-interval of x-values; their union is the true x-domain induced by the parametrisation. Once the induced x-set is known, the range of y = f(x) follows by minimising/maximising f over that set. Skipping this trace risks excluding or including x-values incorrectly, even when the completed-square answer happens to coincide.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that x(t) involves a function (tan t, sec t, etc.) with an asymptote, and check whether that asymptote lies inside the given t-interval.
  2. Rule out the shortcut of reading the range directly from the completed-square form of f(x), because that assumes x ranges over all of ℝ — which must be justified, not assumed.
  3. Commit to splitting the t-interval at the discontinuity and tracking x(t) on each piece separately.
  4. On each subinterval, evaluate x(t) at the closed endpoint and take the limit at the open (asymptotic) endpoint, producing a half-line or interval of x-values.
  5. Union the pieces; if the union is ℝ (or contains the vertex of the quadratic), the global minimum/maximum of f is attained, otherwise restrict to the attained x-set and optimise f there.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify any t-values in the given interval where x(t) is undefined (asymptotes of tan, sec, cot, or rational singularities).
  2. Partition the t-interval at each such discontinuity into closed/open subintervals.
  3. On each subinterval, compute x at the closed endpoint(s) and the one-sided limit at the asymptotic endpoint to get the image interval for x.
  4. Take the union of these x-image intervals to obtain the induced Cartesian domain D ⊆ ℝ.
  5. Optimise f(x) over D: locate stationary points / vertex inside D and compare with boundary/limit values.
  6. State the range as the resulting interval, with correct open/closed endpoints.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $x = \tan t$, $y = 2(x-5)^2 + 8$ for $-\tfrac{3\pi}{4} \le t \le \tfrac{\pi}{4}$. Since $\tan t$ has an asymptote at $t = -\tfrac{\pi}{2}$, split: on $(-\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \tfrac{\pi}{4}]$, $x \in (-\infty, 1]$; on $[-\tfrac{3\pi}{4}, -\tfrac{\pi}{2})$, $x \in [1, +\infty)$. The union is $\mathbb{R}$, so $x = 5$ is attained and the minimum of $y$ is $8$. Range: $y \ge 8$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Parametric x = sin t or x = cos t on a restricted interval (bounded, no asymptote)
No splitting needed; map t-endpoints and any interior turning points of x(t) to get the x-image, then optimise f on that closed interval.
x = sec t or x = cot t with asymptote inside t-interval
Split at the asymptote exactly as for tan; sec t produces image |x| ≥ 1 on each branch, so the induced x-set may be (−∞,−1] ∪ [1,∞) rather than ℝ.
x = 1/(t − a) with a inside the t-interval
Split at t = a; each branch gives a one-sided unbounded x-interval, union typically covers ℝ \ {0}.
Restricted t-domain with no discontinuity
Skip splitting; evaluate x(t) at endpoints and at any stationary points of x(t) to determine the closed image interval.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading the range straight from the completed-square form $a(x-h)^2 + k$ without checking whether the vertex x = h is actually attained by the parametrisation.
  • Treating the discontinuity in t as if it removed an x-value, when in fact the two branches together cover all (or most) of ℝ.
  • Confusing the t-domain with the x-domain and reporting the range as f evaluated only at the t-endpoints.

Pitfalls

  • Getting open/closed endpoints wrong: the asymptotic side gives an open limit, the included t-endpoint gives a closed x-value.
  • Forgetting to take the union — reporting only one branch's image.
  • Using $y = 8\sec^2 t \ge 8$ as a shortcut without verifying that $\sec^2 t$ attains its minimum 1 within the given t-interval (it does at t = 0, but this needs a check).
  • Mis-evaluating $\tan(-3\pi/4) = 1$ (sign and reference-angle errors).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure / Further Pure parametric-equations material: converting to Cartesian form, handling tan/sec asymptotes on closed t-intervals, and stating ranges of quadratic functions via completed-square form.
