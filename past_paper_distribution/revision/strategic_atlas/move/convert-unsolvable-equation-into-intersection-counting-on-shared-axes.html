Algebra & Functions

Convert unsolvable equation into intersection-counting on shared axes

Family: Graphical Solution Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

An equation of the form f(x) = g(x) cannot be solved by standard algebra — typically because it mixes a transcendental function (tan, sin, e^x, ln) with a polynomial, rational, or linear expression. The question structure is a strong cue: earlier parts have asked you to sketch y = f(x) and y = g(x), often on the same axes, and the final part asks 'how many solutions' or 'state the number of roots' on a given interval. Phrasings to watch for: 'use your sketches to find the number of solutions', 'hence state the number of roots of the equation', 'deduce the number of solutions of f(x) = g(x)'.

Motivation — Why this works

The number of real solutions of f(x) = g(x) equals the number of intersection points of the curves y = f(x) and y = g(x). When neither side admits an exact algebraic resolution (no factoring, no identity, no inverse function trick), the graphical method converts an intractable algebraic problem into a visual counting problem. The principle is simply that x = a satisfies f(x) = g(x) iff (a, f(a)) = (a, g(a)) lies on both curves. Because earlier parts of the question have already produced the two sketches, the examiner has built the diagram for you — counting crossings is then the intended final step.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the equation mixes a transcendental function with a non-transcendental one (e.g. tan x = 1/(x+1)) — there is no identity, substitution, or factoring that will isolate x.
  2. I rule out algebraic methods: no rearrangement gives a polynomial, no inverse-function move works because the two functions are of different types, and numerical/iterative methods are not what the question asks for.
  3. I look back at the question structure: parts (a) and (b) asked me to sketch y = tan x and y = 1/(x+1). This staging is a deliberate signal that the final part should be answered by overlaying those sketches.
  4. I commit to the graphical method: the number of solutions equals the number of intersections of the two curves on the stated interval.
  5. I bridge to execution by redrawing both curves carefully on the same axes, paying attention to asymptotes, periodicity, and the endpoints of the interval.
  6. Tactically, I check behaviour near each asymptote of tan x and the asymptote x = −1 of 1/(x+1) — these are the regions where intersections are easiest to miscount.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rewrite the equation as the system y = f(x) and y = g(x).
  2. Draw both curves on the same set of axes over the specified interval, using the sketches from earlier parts.
  3. Mark all asymptotes (vertical and horizontal) clearly — these are where curves change branch.
  4. Identify each branch of f and check, branch by branch, whether g crosses it.
  5. Count every crossing point; this count is the number of solutions to the original equation.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\tan x = \tfrac{1}{x+1}$ on $[-2\pi, 2\pi]$. Sketch $y=\tan x$ (asymptotes at $x = \pm\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \pm\tfrac{3\pi}{2}$) and $y = \tfrac{1}{x+1}$ (asymptote at $x=-1$, hyperbolic shape). On each of the four tan-branches in $[-2\pi, 2\pi]$, the rational curve crosses exactly once, giving $4$ intersections, hence $4$ solutions.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation mixes exponential and linear, e.g. $e^x = 3 - x$
Sketch $y = e^x$ and $y = 3 - x$ on the same axes; count intersections. Same overlay-and-count logic.
Equation must first be split, e.g. $x^2 + x - 4 = 0$ rewritten as $x^2 = 4 - x$
Rearrange so each side matches a standard graph you can sketch (parabola vs line), then overlay and count.
Question asks for number of solutions of $f(x) = k$ for a constant $k$
Draw the horizontal line $y = k$ across the sketch of $y = f(x)$ and count crossings.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the equation as solvable algebraically and attempting identities or substitutions when the mixed-function structure makes this impossible.
  • Ignoring the structural hint of earlier sketch parts and starting the final part from scratch.
  • Confusing 'number of solutions' with 'values of solutions' — the graphical method gives a count, not exact roots.

Pitfalls

  • Miscounting intersections near vertical asymptotes where both curves diverge — careful sign and limit analysis is needed on each branch.
  • Failing to restrict counting to the stated interval (e.g. counting crossings outside $[-2\pi, 2\pi]$).
  • Missing an intersection on a tan branch that is squeezed between two close asymptotes — sketch each branch with enough vertical extent.
  • Double-counting a tangential touch as two crossings, or dismissing a tangential touch as none.

A-level Scope Notes

This is standard Edexcel IAL P2/P3 technique for trig and rational/exponential equations; no formula booklet entry is required. Avoid invoking numerical root-finding (e.g. Newton-Raphson) here — that belongs to a different family of moves and is not what this question type asks for.
