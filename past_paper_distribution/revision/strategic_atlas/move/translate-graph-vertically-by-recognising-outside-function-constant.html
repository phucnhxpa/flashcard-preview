Trigonometry

Translate graph vertically by recognising outside-function constant

Family: Graph Transformation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the target curve has the form y = f(x) + k or y = f(x) − k, with the constant added or subtracted OUTSIDE the function bracket. Typical cues: 'sketch y = g(x) − 2', 'on the same axes draw y = f(x) + 3', or a follow-up part to a sketch of y = f(x). Distinguished from y = f(x ± k), where the constant sits inside the bracket and produces a horizontal shift.

Motivation — Why this works

Adding k to every output value moves every point (x, y) to (x, y + k), so the entire graph translates by the vector (0, k) with no change to x-coordinates, period, or shape. For a sinusoid this just relocates the midline from y = 0 to y = k while preserving amplitude. Because nothing depends on x, no recomputation of zeros, turning-point x-values, or period is needed — only the y-coordinates of named features change.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the constant sits OUTSIDE the function: y = g(x) − 2 means 'evaluate g, then subtract 2', so this is a transformation of the output.
  2. Rule out a horizontal shift (which would require the form g(x − 2)) and rule out a stretch (no multiplicative factor on g(x) or x).
  3. Commit to a translation by the vector (0, −2): every labelled feature keeps its x-coordinate and has its y-coordinate decreased by 2.
  4. Bridge to operation: list the named features of g (max, min, midline, intercepts) and subtract 2 from each y-value.
  5. Tactical check: if the original curve crossed the x-axis, the translated curve may no longer do so; verify by comparing the new max and min to 0.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the form y = f(x) + k and read off k (here k = −2).
  2. Translate midline from y = 0 to y = k.
  3. Translate max from y = M to y = M + k and min from y = m to y = m + k.
  4. Translate each labelled point (a, b) on the original to (a, b + k).
  5. Redraw the identical shape (same period, same amplitude) centred on the new midline.
  6. Re-evaluate axis-crossings: keep, relabel, or remove x-intercepts as appropriate; relabel the y-intercept.

Worked Micro-Example

Given y = \sin x with max 1, min −1, midline y = 0, sketch y = \sin x − 2. New midline: y = −2; new max: y = −1 at x = \tfrac{\pi}{2}; new min: y = −3 at x = \tfrac{3\pi}{2}; y-intercept: (0, −2). The curve lies entirely below the x-axis, so no x-intercepts.

Variants — Same move, different surface

y = f(x) + c with c > 0
Translate upward by c; same procedure with positive shift on every y-coordinate.
y = f(x − a) (constant inside bracket)
Horizontal translation by +a; x-coordinates change, y-coordinates fixed — do NOT confuse with the vertical case.
y = a f(x) + k (combined stretch and shift)
Apply vertical stretch by factor a first (max/min scale by a), then shift by k; order matters because the stretch is also outside.
Non-trig surfaces, e.g. y = e^x − 2 or y = \ln x + 1
Same vertical-shift logic; asymptotes also translate (e.g. y = 0 becomes y = −2 for the exponential).

Common Misreadings

  • Reading y = g(x) − 2 as y = g(x − 2) and shifting right instead of down.
  • Treating −2 as a vertical stretch factor rather than a translation.
  • Assuming x-intercepts simply shift down by 2 in the y-direction (intercepts are points where y = 0, not features that translate vertically).

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to relabel the y-intercept, which the question almost always requires.
  • Leaving the original x-intercepts on the sketch when the translated curve no longer crosses the x-axis.
  • Failing to translate asymptotes (when present, e.g. for tan or exponential surfaces).
  • Drawing the new curve with altered amplitude or period — only the vertical position changes.

A-level Scope Notes

Single vertical translation y = f(x) + k is core Edexcel IAL Pure content (P2 graph transformations) and applies uniformly across trig, polynomial, exponential and logarithmic graphs within the syllabus.
