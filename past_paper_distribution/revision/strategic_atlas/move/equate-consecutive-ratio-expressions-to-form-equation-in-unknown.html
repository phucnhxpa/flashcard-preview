Sequences & Series

Equate consecutive ratio expressions to form equation in unknown

Family: Common Ratio Consistency Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when three (or more) consecutive terms of a geometric sequence are given as algebraic expressions in an unknown parameter (often k), and the question asks to derive an equation — typically a quadratic — in that unknown. Look for phrasings like 'the first three terms of a geometric sequence are...' or 'show that k satisfies...'. Also fires when a problem states that three given quantities 'form a geometric progression' without using subscript notation.

Motivation — Why this works

In a geometric sequence the common ratio r = u_{n+1}/u_n is constant for all n. Hence u₂/u₁ = u₃/u₂, which is a single algebraic constraint linking the three expressions. Setting these two ratio expressions equal converts the geometric structure into a polynomial equation in the unknown, which is the only way to extract numerical information about the parameter.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice three consecutive GP terms given as expressions in k — the defining feature is a constant common ratio.
  2. I rule out treating it as arithmetic (would use common difference) and rule out trying to find r numerically first — r itself is unknown.
  3. I commit to the ratio-equality route because it directly encodes the geometric property and produces one equation in one unknown.
  4. Concretely, I write r = u₂/u₁ and r = u₃/u₂ as two fractions in k, then set them equal.
  5. Tactical choice: I can either keep the fraction form and cross-multiply, or use the equivalent geometric-mean form u₂² = u₁·u₃ to skip straight to a polynomial; for 'show that' questions, cross-multiplying makes the derivation more transparent.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Label the three terms: u₁, u₂, u₃ as the given expressions in k.
  2. Write the common ratio two ways: r = u₂/u₁ and r = u₃/u₂.
  3. Equate the two expressions: u₂/u₁ = u₃/u₂.
  4. Cross-multiply to clear fractions: u₂² = u₁·u₃.
  5. Expand both sides and collect terms to obtain the target quadratic (or polynomial) in k.
  6. Note implicit restrictions u₁ ≠ 0 and u₂ ≠ 0 so the ratios are defined.

Worked Micro-Example

The first three terms of a GP are $k+4,\ k,\ 2k-15$. Then $\dfrac{k}{k+4}=\dfrac{2k-15}{k}$, so $k^2=(k+4)(2k-15)$. Expanding: $k^2 = 2k^2 - 7k - 60$, giving $k^2 - 7k - 60 = 0$, hence $(k-12)(k+5)=0$ so $k=12$ or $k=-5$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Geometric-mean shortcut
Use u₂² = u₁·u₃ directly, skipping the fraction stage and going straight to a polynomial equation — faster but less explicit about the GP definition.
Four consecutive GP terms with one unknown
Equate u₂/u₁ = u₃/u₂ = u₄/u₃; typically two of these suffice to pin down k, with the third acting as a consistency check.
Arithmetic sequence analogue
Replace constant ratio with constant difference: u₂ − u₁ = u₃ − u₂, i.e. 2u₂ = u₁ + u₃, giving a linear equation in k.
Three terms with a known r
If r is given numerically, set u₂ = r·u₁ (single equation) rather than equating two ratios.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the three expressions as an arithmetic sequence and using a common difference.
  • Writing the ratios inverted (u₁/u₂ instead of u₂/u₁); algebraically harmless if done consistently but a frequent source of sign slips.
  • Mistaking 'three terms in GP' for 'three terms whose product/sum is given', leading to an unrelated equation.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the implicit domain restrictions u₁ ≠ 0 and u₂ ≠ 0; a candidate k-value that makes a denominator zero must be rejected.
  • Algebraic slips when cross-multiplying — e.g. expanding (k+4)(2k−15) incorrectly.
  • Stopping after finding k without checking which root is admissible (e.g. if the problem requires r > 0 or |r| < 1 for a sum to infinity).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 (geometric sequences and series); uses only the definition of common ratio and basic quadratic manipulation.
