Trigger — When to use

The curve is given by an implicit equation F(x,y) = 0 — typically a circle (x−a)² + (y−b)² = r², an ellipse x²/a² + y²/b² = 1, a hyperbola x²/a² − y²/b² = 1, or a more exotic relation like xy = c² or x³ + y³ = 6xy — and the question asks for the gradient, tangent, or normal at a specified point. Solving for y explicitly would introduce ± square roots, branch choices, or messy chain-rule work. Equivalent phrasings: 'find the equation of the tangent/normal at P', 'show that dy/dx = …', or any setup where y cannot cleanly be made the subject. The move is also forced when the examiner specifically asks for differentiation rather than a geometric shortcut.

Motivation — Why this works

Implicit differentiation applies d/dx term-by-term to F(x,y) = 0, using the chain rule on every y-term so that d/dx[g(y)] = g'(y)·(dy/dx). This produces a linear equation in dy/dx that can be solved as a clean rational expression in x and y, evaluable directly at the target point. It bypasses the ± branch issue from solving for y explicitly and works uniformly for any differentiable implicit curve, including those with no explicit form. Underlying principle: the chain rule, treating y as a differentiable function of x along the curve.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the equation mixes x and y — typically with y appearing inside a square or product — so y cannot be cleanly isolated without introducing ± or surds.
  2. Rule out solving for y explicitly: although possible for a circle/ellipse, the resulting square root forces a branch choice and a messier chain-rule differentiation.
  3. Rule out (or note as alternative) any geometric shortcut — e.g. perpendicular-radius for circles — because it does not generalise and the question may demand a calculus method.
  4. Commit to implicit differentiation: differentiate both sides w.r.t. x, attach a (dy/dx) factor to every y-term via the chain rule, then solve algebraically for dy/dx.
  5. Tactical step: keep the expression in terms of both x and y rather than re-substituting — this makes the substitution of the specific point (Cartesian or parametric) at the end completely mechanical.
  6. If the result is needed for a normal rather than tangent, take the negative reciprocal −1/m before applying point-slope form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Differentiate every term of F(x,y) = 0 with respect to x, using the chain rule on y-terms: d/dx[y^n] = n·y^(n−1)·(dy/dx); use the product rule on mixed terms like xy.
  2. Collect all (dy/dx) terms on one side and the rest on the other.
  3. Factor out dy/dx and divide to obtain dy/dx = (expression in x and y).
  4. Substitute the coordinates of the given point P(x₁, y₁) (or parametric P(a cos θ, b sin θ)) to get the numerical/parametric gradient m.
  5. For a tangent: y − y₁ = m(x − x₁). For a normal: y − y₁ = (−1/m)(x − x₁).
  6. Clear fractions if a tidy form ax + by + c = 0 is requested.

Worked Micro-Example

Find dy/dx on $(x-2)^2 + (y+1)^2 = 25$ at $P(5,3)$. Differentiate: $2(x-2) + 2(y+1)\tfrac{dy}{dx} = 0$. Solve: $\tfrac{dy}{dx} = -\tfrac{x-2}{y+1}$. At $P$: $\tfrac{dy}{dx} = -\tfrac{3}{4}$. Tangent: $y - 3 = -\tfrac{3}{4}(x-5)$, i.e. $3x + 4y - 27 = 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Ellipse x²/a² + y²/b² = 1 with parametric point (a cos θ, b sin θ)
Implicit differentiation gives dy/dx = −b²x/(a²y); substituting parametric coordinates yields dy/dx = −(b cos θ)/(a sin θ).
Hyperbola x²/a² − y²/b² = 1
The minus sign flips the result: dy/dx = +b²x/(a²y). Watch the sign carefully.
Rectangular hyperbola xy = c²
Use the product rule: y + x(dy/dx) = 0, so dy/dx = −y/x.
Non-conic implicit curve (e.g. x³ + y³ = 6xy)
Implicit differentiation is essentially the only practical route — apply the product rule to 6xy and solve: dy/dx = (6y − 3x²)/(3y² − 6x).
Question asks for the normal, not the tangent
Compute m as above, then use gradient −1/m in point-slope form.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to rearrange the equation for y first (e.g. y = −1 ± √(25 − (x−2)²)) and then differentiate — works but invites errors and forces a branch choice.
  • Confusing 'tangent at a point on the curve' with 'tangent from an external point' and using a discriminant-equals-zero approach instead.
  • Treating y as a constant and writing d/dx[y²] = 0 — fundamental misuse of implicit differentiation.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the chain rule factor (dy/dx) on y-terms — writing 2y instead of 2y·(dy/dx).
  • Sign errors, especially when migrating from ellipse (+) to hyperbola (−), or when forming the negative-reciprocal gradient for a normal.
  • Dividing by zero: if y₁ = 0 (e.g. end of minor axis) or y + 1 = 0 in a shifted circle, the tangent is vertical (x = constant) — handle geometrically.
  • Forgetting the product rule on mixed terms like xy or x²y when the curve is not a standard conic.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 (implicit differentiation) and FP1/FP3 (conics). No formula-booklet entry is needed — the chain rule and product rule are assumed. Avoid invoking partial derivatives or the formula dy/dx = −F_x/F_y, which is out of scope; instead, perform the term-by-term differentiation explicitly.
