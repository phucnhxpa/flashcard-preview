Trigger — When to use

Fires immediately after differentiating a product (or applying chain/quotient rule) when the resulting expression is a sum of two or more terms sharing a visible common factor, and the next task is to solve f'(x) = 0 for stationary points. Typical cues: 'find the x-coordinate(s) of the stationary point(s)', 'show that f'(x) = 0 implies...', or any equation-solving step on the derivative. Especially strong when the shared factor is an exponential e^{kx} or a trig function like sec, cos, or sin that has known sign behaviour.

Motivation — Why this works

A sum equal to zero is hard to solve, but a product equal to zero splits into independent factor equations by the zero-product property. Pulling out the common factor reorganises f'(x) into A(x)·B(x) = 0, where often A(x) is a function (like e^{kx} or sec(2x)) that is never zero, leaving a single simpler equation B(x) = 0 to solve. This is the standard Edexcel route from 'derivative computed' to 'stationary points found'.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the differentiated expression is a sum of terms with a visibly repeated factor (e.g. e^{3x} sec(2x) appears in both).
  2. Rule out solving the sum directly — setting a sum to zero forces messy algebra or transcendental manipulation.
  3. Commit to factoring because the upcoming part asks for stationary points, where a product form is decisively easier.
  4. Identify the largest common factor (constants, exponentials, trig pieces, powers of x) and bracket what remains.
  5. Write f'(x) = [common factor] · [bracket], then note which factors can never vanish so the stationary-point equation reduces to the bracket = 0.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the differentiated expression as a sum of terms.
  2. Identify the greatest common factor across all terms (constants, e^{kx}, trig, power of x, etc.).
  3. Extract the common factor, leaving the residual sum inside square brackets.
  4. State (or note silently) which factors are never zero on the given domain.
  5. Set the remaining bracket equal to zero to obtain the stationary-point equation.

Worked Micro-Example

Let g(x) = e^{3x} sec(2x). Product rule gives g'(x) = 3e^{3x} sec(2x) + e^{3x} · 2 sec(2x) tan(2x). Factor: g'(x) = e^{3x} sec(2x)[3 + 2 tan(2x)]. Since e^{3x} > 0 and sec(2x) ≠ 0, stationary points satisfy 3 + 2 tan(2x) = 0, i.e. tan(2x) = -3/2.

Variants — Same move, different surface

y = x^n e^{kx}
Factor out x^{n-1} e^{kx} after product rule, leaving a linear bracket (kx + n) = 0.
y = e^{kx} (trig)
Factor out e^{kx}; reduces to a trig equation in the bracket only.
y = (ax+b)^m (cx+d)^n via product/chain rule
Factor out (ax+b)^{m-1}(cx+d)^{n-1}; bracket is linear, solve directly.
Quotient rule output with shared numerator factor
Factor the numerator; denominator is automatically nonzero on the domain, so set factored numerator = 0.
Implicit differentiation giving dy/dx · P(x,y) + Q(x,y) = 0
After isolating dy/dx, factor numerator/denominator before solving dy/dx = 0.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the sum form as already simplified and trying to solve it directly with trig identities or numerical methods.
  • Believing e^{kx} = 0 contributes a solution (it never does).
  • Cancelling sec(2x) or e^{kx} from both sides without first factoring, losing track of domain restrictions.

Pitfalls

  • Pulling out only part of the common factor (e.g. e^{3x} but not sec(2x)), leaving an unnecessarily complicated bracket.
  • Sign errors when the residual term has a coefficient absorbed into the factor.
  • Forgetting to state that the extracted factor is never zero, leaving the solution incomplete in 'show that' style questions.
  • Mishandling the chain rule constant (e.g. factor of 2 from differentiating sec(2x)).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P3/P4 technique for stationary points of products of exponentials, trig, and polynomial factors; uses only product/chain rule and the zero-product property.
