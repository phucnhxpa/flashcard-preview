Integration

Assign u to the algebraic factor via LIATE for integration by parts

Family: Parts Assignment Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integrand is a product of two different function families — typically an algebraic factor (x, x², polynomial) multiplied by a transcendental factor (e^{kx}, sin(kx), cos(kx), sec²(kx), etc.) — and no substitution simplifies it directly. Integration by parts is therefore forced, requiring a choice of u and dv/dx. A 'Hence' cue referring to a previously found antiderivative (e.g. part (a) gave ∫sec²(2x) dx) is a strong signal that the trigonometric/exponential factor should be dv/dx. Phrasings like 'use your answer to part (a)' or 'show that' followed by a clean closed form also fire this move.

Motivation — Why this works

Integration by parts converts ∫u(dv/dx)dx into uv − ∫v(du/dx)dx, so the assignment is chosen to make the residual integral ∫v·du/dx dx STRICTLY simpler than the original. The LIATE hierarchy (Logarithmic, Inverse trig, Algebraic, Trigonometric, Exponential) ranks u-candidates by how much they simplify under differentiation: differentiating x or x² lowers the polynomial degree (and eventually kills it), while integrating e^{kx}, sin(kx), or sec²(kx) merely returns a function of the same complexity. Choosing u = algebraic therefore guarantees monotone progress; the reverse assignment would raise the polynomial degree at each round and spiral.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand is a product of two different types of function — an algebraic factor (x or x²) and a transcendental factor (e^{kx}, sec²(kx), sin x, etc.) — so no single substitution will collapse it.
  2. Rule out direct antidifferentiation, u-substitution, and partial fractions: none addresses the product structure. Integration by parts is the remaining tool.
  3. Apply LIATE: the algebraic factor outranks the trigonometric/exponential factor as the choice for u, because differentiating it lowers (and eventually eliminates) the polynomial degree.
  4. Cross-check by imagining the reverse: u = e^{3x} or u = sec²(2x) would give du with the SAME or HIGHER complexity, while v would now be a polynomial of higher degree — the residual integral becomes worse, confirming the rejection.
  5. If the question said 'Hence', identify v from the earlier part of the question (e.g. v = (1/2)tan(2x) was given by part (a)); this locks in dv/dx as the trigonometric/exponential factor.
  6. Commit: write u = (algebraic), dv/dx = (transcendental); compute du/dx by differentiating, and v by integrating; substitute into uv − ∫v·du/dx dx and verify the new integral is simpler before continuing.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two factors of the integrand and label the algebraic one u, the transcendental one dv/dx.
  2. Differentiate: du/dx = (derivative of the algebraic factor), e.g. u = x ⇒ du/dx = 1; u = x² ⇒ du/dx = 2x.
  3. Integrate: v = ∫(dv/dx) dx, carefully tracking constant factors, e.g. dv/dx = sec²(2x) ⇒ v = (1/2)tan(2x); dv/dx = e^{3x} ⇒ v = (1/3)e^{3x}.
  4. Apply the formula ∫u(dv/dx)dx = uv − ∫v(du/dx)dx, substituting all four pieces.
  5. Evaluate any boundary term [uv] at the limits if it is a definite integral, then simplify the residual integral (which now has a lower-degree algebraic factor or none at all).
  6. If the residual integral still has an algebraic × transcendental product (e.g. after one round on x²·e^{3x}), repeat the move.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_0^{\pi/6} x\sec^2(2x)\,dx$. Set $u=x$, $\frac{dv}{dx}=\sec^2(2x)$, so $\frac{du}{dx}=1$ and $v=\tfrac{1}{2}\tan(2x)$. Then $I=\left[\tfrac{x}{2}\tan(2x)\right]_0^{\pi/6}-\int_0^{\pi/6}\tfrac{1}{2}\tan(2x)\,dx = \tfrac{\pi\sqrt{3}}{12}-\tfrac{1}{2}\int_0^{\pi/6}\tan(2x)\,dx$, and the residual integral is a standard form.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Integrand is x·e^{kx}, x·sin(kx), or x·cos(kx)
Same LIATE choice: u = x (algebraic), dv/dx = transcendental; one round of by-parts suffices because du/dx = 1.
Integrand is x²·e^{kx} or x²·sin(kx)
Same assignment u = x² but two rounds of by-parts are required (or the tabular method); each round lowers the polynomial degree by one.
Integrand is ln(x) alone or ln(x)·(polynomial)
LIATE puts Logarithmic above Algebraic, so u = ln(x), dv/dx = 1 (or the polynomial); differentiation converts ln(x) into 1/x.
'Hence' cue referencing a part (a) antiderivative
The function whose antiderivative was found in part (a) MUST be taken as dv/dx; this overrides any ambiguity in LIATE.

Common Misreadings

  • Ignoring a 'Hence' cue and attempting substitution or a fresh integration instead of using the part (a) antiderivative as v.
  • Choosing u = transcendental because it 'looks harder' — confusing 'hardest to integrate' with 'correct u'.
  • Treating x·sec²(2x) as a candidate for u-substitution (e.g. u = 2x) without noticing the standalone x factor blocks that approach.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping a constant factor when integrating dv/dx: v = (1/3)e^{3x}, not e^{3x}; v = (1/2)tan(2x), not tan(2x).
  • Sign error in the formula: writing uv + ∫v du instead of uv − ∫v du.
  • Forgetting to apply the constant factor of v to BOTH the boundary term [uv] and the residual integral.
  • Stopping after one round when the residual integral is still a product (e.g. for x²·e^{3x}).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3 integration by parts; the formula ∫u(dv/dx)dx = uv − ∫v(du/dx)dx appears in the formula booklet. LIATE is a heuristic, not an examinable rule — students should justify their choice by showing the residual integral is simpler, not by quoting LIATE. Avoid generalising to repeated by-parts with reduction formulae, which is Further Maths territory.
