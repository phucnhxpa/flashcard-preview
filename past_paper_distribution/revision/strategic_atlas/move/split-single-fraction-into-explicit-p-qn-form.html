Algebra & Functions

Split single fraction into explicit p + q√n form

Family: Target Form Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a final answer sits as a single fraction of the type (A + B√n)/k but the question specifies the form p + q√n with p and q as separate rationals (or integers). Common phrasings: 'express in the form p + q√3', 'where p and q are rational', 'find the exact values of p and q'. Also fires after rationalising a denominator leaves a combined surd fraction. Does not fire if the question is content with a single combined exact value.

Motivation — Why this works

Division distributes over addition, so (A + B√n)/k ≡ A/k + (B/k)√n is an identity, not an approximation. Presenting the answer in two additive terms makes the rational coefficients of 1 and √n directly readable, matching the demanded structural template. Edexcel marks the final A-mark on form, not just numerical equivalence, so a combined fraction can lose the presentation mark even when correct. The move is purely cosmetic algebra — no new computation is introduced.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the answer is a single fraction (A + B√n)/k, but the question demands the explicit shape p + q√n.
  2. Rule out rationalising: the denominator k is already rational, so no conjugate is needed — the surd is only in the numerator.
  3. Commit to fraction splitting because distributing the denominator across the two numerator terms is an identity that exposes p and q.
  4. Write (A + B√n)/k = A/k + (B√n)/k, then pull √n to the right to get A/k + (B/k)√n.
  5. Read off p = A/k and q = B/k, simplifying each fraction to lowest terms if possible.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the target form p + q√n and the current single-fraction form (A + B√n)/k.
  2. Distribute the denominator: (A + B√n)/k = A/k + (B√n)/k.
  3. Factor √n out of the second term: = A/k + (B/k)√n.
  4. Simplify A/k and B/k to lowest terms.
  5. State p = A/k and q = B/k explicitly.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $y = \dfrac{3 + 9\sqrt{3}}{7}$, express in the form $p + q\sqrt{3}$.\ \ Split: $\dfrac{3 + 9\sqrt{3}}{7} = \dfrac{3}{7} + \dfrac{9\sqrt{3}}{7} = \dfrac{3}{7} + \dfrac{9}{7}\sqrt{3}$.\ \ Hence $p = \dfrac{3}{7}$, $q = \dfrac{9}{7}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Post-rationalisation surd answer
After multiplying by the conjugate, e.g. $\dfrac{6 + 4\sqrt{5}}{10}$, split to get $\dfrac{3}{5} + \dfrac{2}{5}\sqrt{5}$.
Complex numbers in x + yi form (FP1)
Apply the same split: $\dfrac{a + bi}{k} = \dfrac{a}{k} + \dfrac{b}{k}i$ to read off real and imaginary parts.
Logarithm answers in form a + b ln k
If a single expression like $\dfrac{2 + 6\ln 3}{4}$ is required as $p + q\ln 3$, split identically to get $\tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{3}{2}\ln 3$.
Integration answer demanded as a + b·e^c
Same distributive split applied to the constant term and the exponential term.

Common Misreadings

  • Believing rationalisation is needed because a surd appears, even though the surd is in the numerator and the denominator is already rational.
  • Treating (A + B√n)/k as if it cannot be split unless k divides A and B exactly — missing that p and q are allowed to be rational.
  • Reading 'p and q rational' as 'p and q integer' and trying to force whole-number answers.

Pitfalls

  • Writing p = A and q = B, forgetting to divide both by k.
  • Leaving the answer as the combined fraction (A + B√n)/k — equivalent but not in the demanded form, costing the final accuracy mark.
  • Failing to simplify A/k or B/k to lowest terms when a common factor exists.
  • Mis-attaching the √n: writing q = B/k but then presenting it as (B/k)/√n or similar slip.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure Maths surd manipulation (P1) and a direct analogue for complex numbers in FP1; no content beyond IAL scope is invoked.
