Polar Coordinates

Bridge polar to parametric via x=r cosθ, y=r sinθ for dy/dx

Family: Parametric Bridge Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question asks for dy/dx (a Cartesian gradient, tangent slope, or horizontal/vertical tangent) on a curve given in polar form r = f(θ). Cues include phrases like 'find the gradient at θ = ...', 'tangent parallel to the initial line', or 'tangent perpendicular to the initial line'. Since r = f(θ) is not itself a Cartesian relation, a coordinate bridge is needed before differentiation can produce a slope.

Motivation — Why this works

Substituting r = f(θ) into x = r cosθ and y = r sinθ rewrites the curve as a parametric pair x(θ), y(θ), reducing the problem to standard parametric differentiation: dy/dx = (dy/dθ)/(dx/dθ). This is mathematically identical to the compact polar formula (r' sinθ + r cosθ)/(r' cosθ − r sinθ), but writing x(θ) and y(θ) explicitly removes reliance on a memorised formula and makes product-rule structure visible. It also generalises cleanly to finding horizontal tangents (dy/dθ = 0) and vertical tangents (dx/dθ = 0).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that dy/dx is Cartesian, but the curve is described radially as r = f(θ).
  2. Rules out treating dr/dθ as the gradient — that is a radial rate, not a slope in the xy-plane.
  3. Commits to the parametric bridge because product-rule differentiation of x(θ), y(θ) is more transparent than the memorised polar gradient formula.
  4. Writes x = f(θ)cosθ and y = f(θ)sinθ, expanding before differentiating to keep terms organised.
  5. Differentiates with the product rule, simplifies using double-angle identities where helpful, then evaluates (dy/dθ)/(dx/dθ) at the required θ.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Substitute r = f(θ) into x = r cosθ and y = r sinθ to get x(θ), y(θ) explicitly.
  2. Expand each product so the derivative structure is clear.
  3. Differentiate x(θ) and y(θ) using the product rule (and chain rule where needed).
  4. Simplify using identities such as 2 sinθ cosθ = sin 2θ and cos²θ − sin²θ = cos 2θ.
  5. Form dy/dx = (dy/dθ)/(dx/dθ) and substitute the given θ-value.

Worked Micro-Example

For $r = 2 + \cos\theta$ at $\theta = \pi/3$: $x = 2\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta$, $y = 2\sin\theta + \sin\theta\cos\theta$. Then $\dfrac{dx}{d\theta} = -2\sin\theta - \sin 2\theta$ and $\dfrac{dy}{d\theta} = 2\cos\theta + \cos 2\theta$. At $\theta = \pi/3$: $\dfrac{dy}{d\theta} = 1 - \tfrac12 = \tfrac12$, $\dfrac{dx}{d\theta} = -\sqrt3 - \tfrac{\sqrt3}{2} = -\tfrac{3\sqrt3}{2}$, so $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = -\dfrac{1}{3\sqrt3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Tangent parallel to the initial line (horizontal tangent)
Solve dy/dθ = 0 (with dx/dθ ≠ 0) instead of forming the full ratio.
Tangent perpendicular to the initial line (vertical tangent)
Solve dx/dθ = 0 (with dy/dθ ≠ 0).
Curve given as r = a(1 + cosθ) or other cardioid/limaçon
Same bridge; expansion produces extra cosθ·cosθ and cosθ·sinθ terms requiring the product rule.
Tangent at the pole (r = 0)
At θ-values where r = 0, the tangent direction is simply θ itself; the parametric ratio still works but reduces to tan θ.
Direct polar gradient formula
Use dy/dx = (r' sinθ + r cosθ)/(r' cosθ − r sinθ) in one line; equivalent but more error-prone from memory.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating dr/dθ as the gradient of the curve — it is the radial rate of change, not a Cartesian slope.
  • Thinking the tangent at θ = α has slope tan α in general (true only when r = 0 at that θ).
  • Confusing 'tangent parallel to initial line' (horizontal, dy/dθ = 0) with 'θ = 0' (a specific point).

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the product rule when differentiating f(θ)cosθ or f(θ)sinθ.
  • Sign errors in d/dθ(cos²θ) = −2 sinθ cosθ = −sin 2θ (not +sin 2θ).
  • Failing to simplify with double-angle identities, leaving a messy expression that hides the answer.
  • Evaluating (dy/dθ)/(dx/dθ) at the wrong θ, or substituting before differentiating.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits within Edexcel IAL Further Pure polar coordinates: parametric differentiation, product rule, and standard trig identities are all in-scope; no implicit differentiation or vector calculus is required.
