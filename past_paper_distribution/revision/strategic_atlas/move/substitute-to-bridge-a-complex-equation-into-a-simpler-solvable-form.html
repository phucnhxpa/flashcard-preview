Complex Numbers

Substitute to bridge a complex equation into a simpler solvable form

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fire this move whenever a complex equation contains a repeated structural block that can be renamed as a single variable. The classic cues are: (i) a quartic in z with only even powers (z⁴ and z² and a constant) — 'quadratic in disguise'; (ii) a Möbius/linear-fractional transformation w = (az+b)/(cz+d) where z is constrained to a parametric locus such as z = iy, z = x, or z = re^{iθ}. In short: if the equation/transformation is awkward in its current variable but becomes a familiar object after one rename, substitute.

Motivation — Why this works

Substitution exploits hidden structure: the original problem is algebraically heavy but structurally identical to a problem we already know how to solve (a quadratic, a real rational expression, etc.). By introducing a bridge variable (w = z² for even-power quartics, or z = iy for an imaginary-axis locus), we collapse a degree-4 equation to degree-2, or convert a complex transformation into a real-parameter family. The underlying principle is variable economy: trade one complicated object for two simpler ones (a substitution step plus a back-substitution step).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice a structural redundancy: either the equation has only even powers of z (so z² appears as a 'block'), or the transformation involves z constrained to a parametrised locus.
  2. Rule out direct attacks: factorising a complex quartic by inspection is unreliable, and trying to manipulate w = (z+2+i)/(z−i) symbolically without parametrising z leaves two unknowns mixed.
  3. Commit to a substitution because it isolates the repeated block as a single new variable, reducing the number of unknowns or the degree of the polynomial.
  4. Choose the bridge variable carefully: w = z² for the disguised quadratic, or z = iy (real parameter y) for the imaginary-axis locus. The choice is dictated by what gets simplified.
  5. Execute the substitution cleanly, grouping real and imaginary parts (or coefficients of w² and w) without sign errors.
  6. Plan the back-substitution in advance: each w from the quadratic gives two z's via z = ±√w; each y gives one point on the w-locus, traced as y varies over ℝ.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the repeated block (e.g. z² in z⁴ + az² + b = 0, or z in a Möbius map with z = iy).
  2. Introduce the bridge variable: let w = z² (quartic case) or substitute z = iy (locus case).
  3. Rewrite the equation/transformation entirely in the new variable, simplifying coefficients.
  4. Solve the reduced equation: apply the quadratic formula w = (−a ± √(a²−4b))/2, or separate w into u + iv to study its locus.
  5. Back-substitute to recover the original variable: solve z² = w for each root (giving ±√w), or eliminate the real parameter y to obtain a Cartesian relation in (u,v).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $z^4 + 3z^2 - 4 = 0$. Let $w = z^2$, so $w^2 + 3w - 4 = 0 \Rightarrow (w+4)(w-1) = 0$, giving $w = -4$ or $w = 1$. Back-substitute: $z^2 = 1 \Rightarrow z = \pm 1$, and $z^2 = -4 \Rightarrow z = \pm 2i$. All four roots recovered.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Trigonometric equation with only even powers of sin x or cos x
Let u = sin²x (or cos²x); solve the resulting quadratic in u, then invert via arcsin/arccos with attention to range.
Exponential equation like e^{2x} + 3e^x − 4 = 0
Let u = e^x; solve quadratic in u; reject non-positive u before taking ln.
Möbius transformation with z on a circle z = re^{iθ}
Substitute and use double-angle identities to separate w = u + iv into real-parameter form before eliminating θ.
Möbius transformation with z on a real line z = x
Substitute z = x (real); rationalise the denominator by multiplying by its conjugate to separate u and v.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to factorise a complex quartic directly by inspection when even-powered structure makes substitution far cleaner.
  • Attempting to find complex roots by trial substitution of real values.
  • Treating w = (z+2+i)/(z−i) as having two independent variables, instead of parametrising z first.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting that each w = z² yields TWO values of z (±√w) — losing half the roots.
  • Mishandling a negative or complex discriminant when applying the quadratic formula to w.
  • Sign errors when grouping real and imaginary parts after substituting z = iy (e.g. confusing the real part 2 with the imaginary part i(y+1) in the numerator).
  • Failing to eliminate the parameter y at the end, leaving the w-locus in parametric rather than Cartesian form.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP1/FP2) for complex numbers and loci, and Pure (P2/P3) for disguised quadratics. The quadratic formula is in the formula booklet; substitution itself is not. Avoid invoking general theory of polynomial resolvents or conformal mapping — those are beyond IAL scope.
