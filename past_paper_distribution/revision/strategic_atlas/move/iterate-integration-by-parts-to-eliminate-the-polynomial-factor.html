Integration

Iterate integration by parts to eliminate the polynomial factor

Family: Repeated Ibp Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an integrand has the form x^n·e^{kx} (or x^n·sin(kx), x^n·cos(kx)) with n ≥ 2, so a single application of IBP leaves a residual integral of the same polynomial×transcendental shape but with degree n−1. Recognise the cue: 'after one IBP I still have a polynomial times the same kind of function'. The number of IBP rounds equals the original polynomial degree n. Equivalent phrasings include 'apply IBP repeatedly' or 'reduce the polynomial degree to zero'.

Motivation — Why this works

Choosing u = polynomial in LIATE/LIPET means du differentiates the polynomial, dropping its degree by exactly one each round, while dv = e^{kx} (or sin/cos) keeps v in the same family. After n rounds the polynomial becomes a constant, leaving a pure exponential or trigonometric integral that evaluates directly. The method terminates precisely because polynomials have finite degree — this is what makes IBP the natural (and unavoidable) tool for these products.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the first IBP has produced a boundary term plus a residual ∫(lower-degree polynomial)·e^{kx} dx — same structural shape, just smaller degree.
  2. I rule out switching technique (substitution u = e^{3x} fails because the polynomial factor has no matching derivative; partial fractions and standard results don't apply).
  3. I commit to repeating IBP because each round is guaranteed to drop the polynomial degree by 1, so after n total rounds I will reach ∫e^{kx} dx.
  4. I bridge in by re-applying LIPET to the residual: u = x, dv/dx = e^{3x}, keeping the outer coefficient (e.g. −2/3) factored out so I work on a clean sub-integral.
  5. Tactically I solve the inner IBP separately, then substitute the whole result back into the outer expression and finally distribute the coefficient and add a single +C.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the residual integral after the first IBP, e.g. ∫xe^{3x} dx, and pull any outer coefficient out front.
  2. Apply IBP again with u = x, du/dx = 1; dv/dx = e^{3x}, v = (1/3)e^{3x}.
  3. Compute ∫xe^{3x} dx = (1/3)xe^{3x} − ∫(1/3)e^{3x} dx = (1/3)xe^{3x} − (1/9)e^{3x}.
  4. Substitute back into the outer expression: I = (1/3)x²e^{3x} − (2/3)[(1/3)xe^{3x} − (1/9)e^{3x}] + C.
  5. Distribute and simplify: I = (1/3)x²e^{3x} − (2/9)xe^{3x} + (2/27)e^{3x} + C.
  6. Optionally factor e^{3x} to present a tidy answer: I = e^{3x}[9x² − 6x + 2]/27 + C.

Worked Micro-Example

Compute $\int x^2 e^{3x}\,dx$. First IBP ($u=x^2$, $dv=e^{3x}dx$): $=\tfrac{1}{3}x^2 e^{3x}-\tfrac{2}{3}\int x e^{3x}\,dx$. Second IBP ($u=x$, $dv=e^{3x}dx$): $\int x e^{3x}\,dx=\tfrac{1}{3}x e^{3x}-\tfrac{1}{9}e^{3x}$. Substituting: $\int x^2 e^{3x}\,dx=\tfrac{1}{3}x^2 e^{3x}-\tfrac{2}{9}x e^{3x}+\tfrac{2}{27}e^{3x}+C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

∫x^3 e^{2x} dx
Three rounds of IBP needed; degree drops 3→2→1→0, then integrate e^{2x} directly.
∫x^2 sin(kx) dx or ∫x^2 cos(kx) dx
Same iteration with u = polynomial; v cycles through sin/cos with sign changes, so track signs carefully across both rounds.
Definite integral ∫_a^b x^2 e^{3x} dx
Carry the limits through each IBP, evaluating boundary terms [uv]_a^b at every stage rather than only at the end.
∫x^2 ln x dx (single IBP suffices)
Here u = ln x, dv = x^2 dx — only one IBP, because differentiating ln x removes the transcendental factor; iteration is not needed.

Common Misreadings

  • Believing one IBP should always finish the problem and abandoning the method when a residual integral appears.
  • Switching to substitution u = e^{3x} or u = x^2 mid-problem, neither of which simplifies a polynomial × exponential product.
  • Treating the residual ∫xe^{3x} dx as a 'standard result' and quoting an incorrect closed form instead of doing the second IBP.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping or mishandling the outer coefficient (e.g. −2/3) when performing the second IBP — keep it factored outside the inner work.
  • Sign errors when expanding: (−2/3)(−1/9) = +2/27 is commonly miscomputed.
  • Forgetting the +C only at the very end (not after each IBP), and not simplifying coefficients like 2/9, 2/27 to lowest terms.
  • Mismatching u and dv on the second round (e.g. switching to u = e^{3x}), which undoes the previous progress and creates a circular integral.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P3/P4) integration techniques: integration by parts applied to polynomial × exponential or polynomial × trigonometric integrands, including iterated application. Tabular/DI shortcut is not required by the syllabus but the underlying repeated-IBP method is standard.
