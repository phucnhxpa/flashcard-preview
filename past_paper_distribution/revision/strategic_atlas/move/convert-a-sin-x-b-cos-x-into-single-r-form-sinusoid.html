Trigonometry

Convert a sin x + b cos x into single R-form sinusoid

Family: Graph Transformation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an expression is a linear combination of $\sin x$ and $\cos x$ with constant coefficients, e.g. $12\sin x - 5\cos x$ or $a\cos\theta + b\sin\theta$. Typical prompts: 'express in the form $R\sin(x\pm\alpha)$', 'find the maximum value of...', 'solve $a\sin x + b\cos x = k$', or any question that needs the amplitude/phase explicitly. Also fires implicitly when a later part asks for extrema or general solutions of such a combination.

Motivation — Why this works

The compound angle identity $\sin(x\pm\alpha) = \sin x\cos\alpha \pm \cos x\sin\alpha$ (and the cosine analogue) shows that any $a\sin x + b\cos x$ is itself a single shifted sinusoid of amplitude $\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$. Collapsing two terms into one exposes the amplitude $R$ and phase $\alpha$ directly, so maxima/minima are read off as $\pm R$ and equations reduce to $\sin(\text{stuff})=\text{const}$. Matching coefficients gives two equations $R\cos\alpha$, $R\sin\alpha$ from which $R$ (Pythagoras) and $\alpha$ (arctan) follow uniquely once the quadrant is fixed by the signs.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the expression is exactly one $\sin x$ term plus/minus one $\cos x$ term with constant coefficients — the textbook R-form signal.
  2. Rule out trying to solve directly or to square the equation: those create extraneous roots and lose the amplitude information the rest of the question needs.
  3. Commit to R-form because subsequent parts (max/min, equation solving) become one-line consequences once $R$ and $\alpha$ are known.
  4. Choose the form ($R\sin(x\pm\alpha)$ vs $R\cos(x\pm\alpha)$) so that the sign pattern of the expansion matches the given expression and $\alpha$ lies in the first quadrant.
  5. Expand the chosen form, equate $\sin x$ and $\cos x$ coefficients, then get $R$ from $R^2=a^2+b^2$ and $\alpha$ from $\tan\alpha = (\text{cos-coeff})/(\text{sin-coeff})$, checking signs to fix the quadrant.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Pick the form whose expansion sign-pattern matches the given expression, e.g. for $12\sin x - 5\cos x$ choose $R\sin(x-\alpha)$.
  2. Expand: $R\sin(x-\alpha) = R\cos\alpha\,\sin x - R\sin\alpha\,\cos x$.
  3. Equate coefficients: $R\cos\alpha = 12$ and $R\sin\alpha = 5$.
  4. Square and add: $R^2 = 12^2 + 5^2 = 169$, so $R = 13$ (take the positive root).
  5. Divide: $\tan\alpha = 5/12$, giving $\alpha = \arctan(5/12) \approx 0.3948$ rad.
  6. Check quadrant: both $R\cos\alpha>0$ and $R\sin\alpha>0$, so $\alpha$ is genuinely first-quadrant — no $\pi$ adjustment needed.
  7. State the result: $12\sin x - 5\cos x \equiv 13\sin(x-0.3948)$.

Worked Micro-Example

Express $12\sin x - 5\cos x$ as $R\sin(x-\alpha)$, $R>0$, $0<\alpha<\pi/2$. Expanding: $R\cos\alpha\,\sin x - R\sin\alpha\,\cos x$, so $R\cos\alpha=12$, $R\sin\alpha=5$. Then $R=\sqrt{12^2+5^2}=13$ and $\tan\alpha = 5/12 \Rightarrow \alpha \approx 0.3948$ rad. Hence $12\sin x - 5\cos x \equiv 13\sin(x-0.3948)$, with maximum $13$ when $x = \alpha + \pi/2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$5\cos x + 12\sin x$ (both coefficients positive, sin-led)
Use $R\sin(x+\alpha)$ since expansion $R\sin(x+\alpha)=R\cos\alpha\,\sin x + R\sin\alpha\,\cos x$ matches the $+$ sign.
$a\cos\theta + b\sin\theta$ with cosine dominant or question requests cosine form
Use $R\cos(\theta-\alpha)=R\cos\alpha\,\cos\theta + R\sin\alpha\,\sin\theta$, so $R\cos\alpha=a$, $R\sin\alpha=b$.
$a\cos\theta - b\sin\theta$
Use $R\cos(\theta+\alpha)=R\cos\alpha\,\cos\theta - R\sin\alpha\,\sin\theta$ to keep $\alpha$ acute and positive.
Coefficients involving the same angle but inside, e.g. $3\sin 2x + 4\cos 2x$
Treat $2x$ as the variable: write as $R\sin(2x+\alpha)$ with $R=5$, $\tan\alpha=4/3$.
Solving $a\sin x + b\cos x = k$ on a given interval
After R-form conversion, solve $R\sin(x\pm\alpha)=k$ as a standard sine equation, remembering to shift the interval by $\mp\alpha$ before listing solutions.

Common Misreadings

  • Picking $R\sin(x+\alpha)$ for $12\sin x - 5\cos x$ without checking the sign of the cosine term, then getting a negative $\alpha$ or contradictory equations.
  • Reading $\tan\alpha$ as (sin-coefficient)/(cos-coefficient) by reflex, instead of the ratio dictated by the specific expansion (it depends on the form chosen).
  • Treating $R$ as possibly negative; by convention $R>0$ and any sign issues are absorbed into the choice of form or quadrant of $\alpha$.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to check the quadrant of $\alpha$: if either $R\cos\alpha$ or $R\sin\alpha$ comes out negative, $\alpha$ is not in the first quadrant and arctan must be adjusted.
  • Mixing up which equation is for $\cos\alpha$ and which for $\sin\alpha$, leading to a reciprocal value of $\tan\alpha$.
  • Dropping or mis-rounding $\alpha$ early — keep it exact (as $\arctan(\cdot)$) until the final answer to avoid accumulated error in later parts.
  • When solving equations afterwards, forgetting to shift the interval for $x\pm\alpha$ before reading off all solutions in the required range.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P3): the R-form $a\sin x + b\cos x \equiv R\sin(x\pm\alpha)$ or $R\cos(x\pm\alpha)$ with $R>0$ and $\alpha$ acute is a standard examinable technique, used for amplitude/phase, extrema, and equation solving.
