Complex Numbers

Convert roots between Cartesian and polar forms as the question demands

Family: Polar Conversion Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger â€” When to use

The output form requested differs from the form in which the roots were naturally produced. Cues include phrases like 'express in the form $a+bi$', 'give exact values', 'state the modulus and argument', or 'write in the form $re^{i\theta}$'. This also fires when a later sub-part needs Cartesian roots (e.g. for plotting or algebraic manipulation) after De Moivre produced polar roots, or vice versa.

Motivation â€” Why this works

De Moivre's theorem and $n$th-root formulas produce roots naturally as $r^{1/n}(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)$, but many sub-parts demand Cartesian form for further algebra or an Argand diagram. Conversely, roots found by factorising a polynomial in Cartesian form must be repackaged as $(r,\theta)$ when modulusâ€“argument data is required. The conversion rests on the identities $a=r\cos\theta$, $b=r\sin\theta$, $r=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ and $\theta=\arctan(b/a)$ adjusted for quadrant, and works cleanly when $\theta$ is a standard angle with known exact trig values.

Thought Process â€” The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the form I have (polar / Cartesian / exponential) does not match the form the question is asking for.
  2. I rule out leaving the answer in its current form, even if mathematically equivalent, because the mark scheme rewards the requested form.
  3. I commit to conversion once I check whether the arguments are standard angles ($k\pi/6, k\pi/4, k\pi/3$) so exact surd values are available.
  4. I bridge to the first operation by writing $r$ and $\theta$ explicitly for each root, then substituting $\cos\theta$ and $\sin\theta$.
  5. Tactically, I track quadrant signs carefully and keep the modulus factor $r$ (or $r^{1/n}$) outside the bracket until the very last simplification.

Mechanic â€” Concrete operations

  1. Identify each root's modulus $r$ and argument $\theta$ (or its real/imaginary parts $a,b$).
  2. For polar $\to$ Cartesian: substitute exact values of $\cos\theta$ and $\sin\theta$, then distribute $r$ to obtain $a+bi$.
  3. For Cartesian $\to$ polar: compute $r=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ and $\theta=\arctan(b/a)$, adjusting by $\pm\pi$ for the correct quadrant and reducing to the principal range $(-\pi,\pi]$.
  4. Simplify surds, rationalise denominators, and ensure the final expression matches the requested template exactly.
  5. Check by squaring/computing $|z|$ or by re-evaluating $z=re^{i\theta}$ numerically against the original.

Worked Micro-Example

A cube root is found as $z=2\bigl(\cos\tfrac{2\pi}{3}+i\sin\tfrac{2\pi}{3}\bigr)$, but the question asks for $a+bi$ form. Substituting $\cos\tfrac{2\pi}{3}=-\tfrac{1}{2}$ and $\sin\tfrac{2\pi}{3}=\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ gives $z=2\bigl(-\tfrac{1}{2}+\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}i\bigr)=-1+\sqrt{3}\,i$. Check: $|z|=\sqrt{1+3}=2$. \checkmark

Variants â€” Same move, different surface

Question asks for exponential form $re^{i\theta}$ instead of $r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)$
Same modulus and argument; just rewrite the bracket as $e^{i\theta}$ and keep $\theta$ in the principal range.
Cartesian root $a+bi$ in a non-standard quadrant must be given in modulusâ€“argument form
Use $r=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ and compute $\theta$ from a sketch or by adding/subtracting $\pi$ to $\arctan(b/a)$ to land in the correct quadrant.
Roots of unity multiplied by a Cartesian factor (e.g. $z^n=c$ with $c$ given as $a+bi$)
First convert $c$ to polar to apply De Moivre, then convert each $n$th root back to Cartesian for the final answer.
Argument is not a standard angle
Leave the answer in polar/exponential form, or give decimal approximations to the accuracy specified â€” do not invent surd values.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'modulusâ€“argument form' and '$a+bi$ form' as interchangeable and answering in whichever is easier.
  • Reading 'exact form' as permission to give decimals from a calculator.
  • Assuming the modulus of each $n$th root equals the modulus of the original number, rather than its $n$th root.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when $\theta$ lies in the second or third quadrant, e.g. writing $\cos\tfrac{3\pi}{4}=+\tfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$.
  • Forgetting to multiply the trig values by the modulus $r$ (or $r^{1/n}$ for $n$th roots).
  • Using $\arctan(b/a)$ blindly without quadrant adjustment, giving an argument off by $\pi$.
  • Leaving the argument outside the principal range $(-\pi,\pi]$ when that range is specified.
