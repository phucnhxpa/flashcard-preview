Coordinate Geometry

Substitute parametric coordinates directly into the Cartesian equation of the other curve

Family: Parametric Elimination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when two curves must be intersected and one is given parametrically (e.g. x = f(t), y = g(t)) while the other is given by a Cartesian relation (e.g. xy = c², y = mx + c, x² + y² = r²). Cue phrases include 'find the points of intersection', 'where the line meets the curve', or 'the parabola crosses the hyperbola'. The asymmetry of forms — one parameterised, one Cartesian — is the key signal.

Motivation — Why this works

Direct substitution collapses the system into a single equation in the single unknown t, because the Cartesian equation is a constraint that the parametric coordinates must satisfy. This avoids the detour of eliminating t to convert the parametric curve to Cartesian form and then solving two Cartesian equations simultaneously. The resulting polynomial in t is typically lower-degree and has roots that immediately give the parameter values of the intersection points, from which (x, y) follow by back-substitution.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice one curve is parametric in t and the other is a Cartesian relation in x and y.
  2. I rule out converting the parametric curve to Cartesian and solving a 2×2 Cartesian system, since that introduces redundant elimination work.
  3. I commit to substitution because the Cartesian equation is already a single scalar constraint — feeding x(t) and y(t) into it gives one equation in t.
  4. First concrete operation: replace every x and y in the Cartesian equation by their parametric expressions in t, then simplify to a polynomial in t.
  5. Tactically, I solve for t first (rational roots, factoring, or quadratic formula), then back-substitute into x(t), y(t) to recover each intersection point.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the parametric curve x = f(t), y = g(t) and the Cartesian equation F(x, y) = 0.
  2. Substitute: form F(f(t), g(t)) = 0.
  3. Simplify to a polynomial (or other solvable equation) in t alone.
  4. Solve for all real values of t.
  5. Back-substitute each t into x = f(t), y = g(t) to obtain the intersection points.
  6. Check each point lies on both original curves (sanity check).

Worked Micro-Example

Find where the parabola $x = 10t^2,\ y = 20t$ meets the hyperbola $xy = 25$. Substitute: $(10t^2)(20t) = 25 \Rightarrow 200t^3 = 25 \Rightarrow t^3 = \tfrac{1}{8} \Rightarrow t = \tfrac{1}{2}$. Then $x = 10(\tfrac{1}{2})^2 = \tfrac{5}{2}$ and $y = 20(\tfrac{1}{2}) = 10$, giving the intersection $(\tfrac{5}{2}, 10)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line meets a parabola given parametrically as $x = at^2,\ y = 2at$
Substitute into the line equation $y = mx + c$ to obtain a quadratic in t; its two roots give the two intersection points (or a repeated root for tangency).
Parametric curve meets a circle $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$
Substitute $x(t)$ and $y(t)$ into $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$; use trig identities such as $\cos^2 t + \sin^2 t = 1$ if the parametrisation is trigonometric.
Tangent/normal at parameter $t_0$ meets the curve again
Substitute the parametric form back into the tangent's Cartesian equation; the resulting polynomial has $t = t_0$ as a (repeated) root, factor it out to find the second intersection.
Parametric curve meets a rectangular hyperbola $xy = c^2$
Multiply $x(t) \cdot y(t)$ and set equal to $c^2$, yielding a single polynomial in t.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to parameterise the Cartesian curve as well (e.g. writing $x = 5c,\ y = 5/c$ for $xy = 25$) and then equating parameters, producing a harder two-parameter system.
  • Eliminating t first to get a Cartesian form of the parametric curve, then solving simultaneously — correct but unnecessarily long.
  • Treating the parameter t of the parametric curve as if it were also a parameter on the Cartesian curve.

Pitfalls

  • Algebraic slip in forming the product or substitution, e.g. miscomputing $10t^2 \cdot 20t$ as $200t^2$ instead of $200t^3$.
  • Solving $t^n = k$ and forgetting to take all real roots (e.g. $t^2 = 4$ gives $t = \pm 2$, not just $t = 2$).
  • Introducing spurious complex roots when only real intersections are required at A-level.
  • Forgetting to back-substitute t into both x(t) and y(t), or substituting into the wrong equation.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard technique in Edexcel IAL Pure (parametric equations, P3/P4) and Further Pure (conics: parabola $y^2 = 4ax$, rectangular hyperbola $xy = c^2$, ellipse). Resulting equations are typically linear, quadratic, or simple cubics solvable by inspection or factoring.
