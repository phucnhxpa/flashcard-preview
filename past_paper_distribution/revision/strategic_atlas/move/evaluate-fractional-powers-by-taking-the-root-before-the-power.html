Evaluate fractional powers by taking the root before the power

Family: Computational Ordering Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

An expression of the form k^(p/q) must be evaluated without a calculator, where k is an integer (or simple rational) admitting a clean q-th root. Typical surfaces: simplifying 8^(2/3), 27^(4/3), 16^(3/4), or evaluating definite values inside surd/index manipulation, integration limits, or binomial-expansion coefficients. The cue is a fractional index whose denominator matches a recognisable perfect power of the base.

Motivation — Why this works

By the index law k^(p/q) = (k^(1/q))^p = (k^p)^(1/q), the two orders give the same value, but root-first keeps intermediate numbers small and exploits memorised perfect powers (2³=8, 3³=27, 2⁵=32, 2⁴=16). Power-first inflates the base into an unfamiliar integer whose q-th root must then be guessed. In a non-calculator paper the strategic choice of order is the difference between a one-line evaluation and an arithmetic dead-end.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the exponent is a fraction p/q and the base is an integer with a familiar q-th root.
  2. Rule out power-first: computing k^p produces a large integer whose q-th root is not immediately recognisable.
  3. Commit to root-first because the q-th root of k is a small known integer, making the subsequent p-th power trivial.
  4. Rewrite k^(p/q) as (k^(1/q))^p and replace k^(1/q) by its known integer value.
  5. Raise that small integer to the power p mentally; check the sign convention if p or q is even and k is negative or if the question asks for the principal root.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify q (denominator) and check that k^(1/q) is a known integer.
  2. Rewrite k^(p/q) = (k^(1/q))^p.
  3. Evaluate the q-th root: k^(1/q) = m.
  4. Compute m^p to obtain the final value.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $8^{2/3}$ without a calculator.\ $8^{2/3} = (8^{1/3})^{2} = (\sqrt[3]{8})^{2} = 2^{2} = 4.$\ Power-first would give $8^{2}=64$, then $\sqrt[3]{64}=4$ — same answer, but $64$ is harder to recognise as $4^{3}$ on sight.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$27^{4/3}$
Root first: $\sqrt[3]{27}=3$, then $3^{4}=81$, avoiding $27^{4}=531441$.
$16^{3/4}$
Root first: $\sqrt[4]{16}=2$, then $2^{3}=8$, avoiding $16^{3}=4096$.
$32^{3/5}$
Root first: $\sqrt[5]{32}=2$, then $2^{3}=8$, avoiding $32^{3}=32768$.
Negative fractional index $8^{-2/3}$
Apply reciprocal first: $8^{-2/3}=1/8^{2/3}=1/4$, then root-first inside as usual.
Rational base $(\tfrac{8}{27})^{2/3}$
Take cube root of numerator and denominator separately: $(\tfrac{2}{3})^{2}=\tfrac{4}{9}$.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading $k^{p/q}$ as $k^{p}/k^{q}$ or as $k^{p}/q$, confusing a fractional index with a quotient.
  • Treating $8^{2/3}$ as $8 \times \tfrac{2}{3}$ (multiplying instead of exponentiating).
  • Believing the order of root and power changes the value rather than just the difficulty.

Pitfalls

  • Choosing power-first on a large base (e.g. $64^{2/3}$) and getting stuck cube-rooting $4096$.
  • Forgetting to apply the reciprocal first when the index is negative, e.g. writing $8^{-2/3}=-4$.
  • Mis-identifying the denominator's matching root, e.g. trying $\sqrt{8}$ when the index is $2/3$ (should be cube root).
  • Dropping a sign with negative bases under even-order roots where the principal root is required.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1) under laws of indices and surds, and recurs whenever fractional powers appear in differentiation, integration, or binomial expansions.
