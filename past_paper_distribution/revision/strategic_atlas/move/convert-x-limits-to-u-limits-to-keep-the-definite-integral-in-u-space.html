Integration

Convert x-limits to u-limits to keep the definite integral in u-space

Family: Limit Transformation Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a definite integral undergoes a substitution u = f(x) and the original bounds are x-values. Visual cues: integral signs with numerical/expression bounds in x (e.g. ∫₀^{π/2}, ∫₁^e) together with a chosen substitution. Also fires when the question explicitly states a target form with new limits in u (e.g. 'show that I = ∫₁² g(u) du'), which signals limit conversion is mandatory. Equivalently phrased: 'change the limits' or 'transform the bounds'.

Motivation — Why this works

Converting limits keeps the entire problem in u-space, so the antiderivative can be evaluated directly without the extra algebra and error-risk of back-substituting u → x. Mathematically this rests on the substitution rule for definite integrals: ∫_{a}^{b} f(g(x)) g'(x) dx = ∫_{g(a)}^{g(b)} f(u) du, provided g is differentiable on [a,b]. It also matches Edexcel mark schemes, which typically award a B mark for the correctly converted limits, and produces cleaner exact answers.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integral is definite (bounds visible) and that a substitution u = f(x) has been (or will be) made — so the bounds must be handled, not ignored.
  2. Rule out back-substitution: integrating in u and then reverting to x just to use the original x-limits doubles the algebra and invites sign/notation slips, especially when the final answer is just a number.
  3. Commit to converting the limits because (a) it is shorter, (b) it is what the mark scheme rewards, and (c) if the question shows a target integral with u-bounds, conversion is required for the 'show that' step.
  4. Bridge to action: evaluate u = f(x) at each x-bound to get the corresponding u-bound — i.e. compute f(a) and f(b).
  5. Tactical check: confirm f is monotonic on [a,b] so the new bounds keep their order; if f were decreasing, the new lower bound would exceed the upper, requiring a swap (with a sign change) or careful retention of order.
  6. Rewrite the integral entirely in u with the new bounds, then proceed to integrate.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the substitution u = f(x) and the original bounds x = a, x = b.
  2. Compute the new lower limit: u_lower = f(a).
  3. Compute the new upper limit: u_upper = f(b).
  4. Replace the integrand and dx with their u-expressions (using du = f'(x) dx).
  5. Write the transformed integral as ∫_{f(a)}^{f(b)} (integrand in u) du and evaluate.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $I = \int_{0}^{\pi/2} \frac{\cos x}{(3+\sin x)^2}\,dx$. Let $u = 3 + \sin x$, so $du = \cos x\,dx$. Limits: $x=0 \Rightarrow u=3$; $x=\pi/2 \Rightarrow u=4$. Then $I = \int_{3}^{4} u^{-2}\,du = \left[-\tfrac{1}{u}\right]_{3}^{4} = -\tfrac{1}{4}+\tfrac{1}{3} = \tfrac{1}{12}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Logarithmic substitution, e.g. u = 1 + ln x with x ∈ [1, e]
Compute u(1) = 1 and u(e) = 2; new bounds are 1 to 2.
Trigonometric substitution x = a sin θ for ∫ involving √(a² − x²)
Convert x-bounds to θ-bounds via θ = arcsin(x/a); the new limits are angles.
Decreasing substitution (e.g. u = cos x on [0, π/2])
New lower limit exceeds upper limit; either keep the swapped order or flip with a sign change.
Indefinite integral
This move does not apply — there are no limits to convert; back-substitute u → x in the antiderivative instead.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the integral as if the x-limits still apply to the u-integrand and evaluating the u-antiderivative at x = a, x = b.
  • Assuming limit conversion is optional when the question says 'show that' with new bounds — it is then required to earn the method/B mark.

Pitfalls

  • Swapping the new limits accidentally (e.g. writing ∫₄³ instead of ∫₃⁴), producing a sign error.
  • Miscomputing trig/log values at the bounds, e.g. sin(π/2) as 0 or ln(e) as 0.
  • Forgetting to change limits entirely and mixing x-bounds with a fully u-form integrand.
  • Ignoring non-monotonicity of the substitution on the interval, which can require splitting the integral.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P3/P4 integration-by-substitution technique; uses the substitution rule listed implicitly in the formula booklet under 'Integration'. Stay within elementary substitutions (algebraic, trig, exponential/log); avoid invoking change-of-variables theorems for improper or multivariable integrals.
