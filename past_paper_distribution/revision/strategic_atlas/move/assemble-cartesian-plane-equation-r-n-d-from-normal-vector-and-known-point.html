Trigger — When to use

Use this as the closing step of almost any FP3 plane-finding question: once you have a normal vector $\mathbf{n}=(a,b,c)$ (usually from a cross product) and any point in the plane, you need the scalar $d$ to write $ax+by+cz=d$. Recognition cue: 'find/show an equation for the plane $\Pi$' or 'show that $\Pi$ is $3x+y-z=3$'.

Motivation — Why this works

Every point $\mathbf{r}$ in the plane satisfies $\mathbf{r}\cdot\mathbf{n} = d$ where $d$ is constant, because $\mathbf{n}$ is perpendicular to the plane. Substituting one known point fixes $d = \mathbf{r}_0\cdot\mathbf{n}$, completing the equation.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Obtain the normal $\mathbf{n}=(a,b,c)$, typically as the cross product of two vectors lying in the plane (two direction vectors, or a direction and a joining vector).
  2. Simplify $\mathbf{n}$ by dividing out any common factor to keep the numbers small.
  3. Write the plane in the form $ax+by+cz = d$ with $d$ unknown.
  4. Pick a known point on the plane (a point on a contained line, or a given point).
  5. Substitute its coordinates to evaluate $d = a x_0 + b y_0 + c z_0$.
  6. State the finished Cartesian (or scalar-product) equation; if it is a 'show that', confirm it matches the target.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute $\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\ u_1&u_2&u_3\\ v_1&v_2&v_3\end{vmatrix}$.
  2. Reduce $\mathbf{n}$ to lowest terms, e.g. $(-42,14,-21) = -7(6,-2,3)$.
  3. Form $ax+by+cz = d$.
  4. Substitute the known point: $d = \mathbf{r}_0\cdot\mathbf{n}$.
  5. Write the result, e.g. $6x-2y+3z = 5$ (or $\mathbf{r}\cdot\mathbf{n} = d$).

Worked Micro-Example

With $\overrightarrow{AP}=(-2,3,6)$ and direction $\mathbf{d}=(5,3,-8)$, $\mathbf{n}=\overrightarrow{AP}\times\mathbf{d}=(-42,14,-21)=-7(6,-2,3)$, so take $\mathbf{n}=(6,-2,3)$. Substituting $P(1,2,1)$: $6(1)-2(2)+3(1)=5$, giving the plane $6x-2y+3z=5$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two lines lie in the plane (normal from the two direction vectors).
Take $\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{d}_1\times\mathbf{d}_2$, then substitute a point from either line, e.g. $(1,1,1)$ gives $3(1)+1-1=3$ for $3x+y-z=3$.
Scalar-product form requested instead of Cartesian.
Write $\mathbf{r}\cdot\mathbf{n} = p$ with $p = \mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{a}$; e.g. $\mathbf{n}=(3,-15,-18)$, point $(2,-4,4)$ gives $p=6+60-72=-6$, so $\mathbf{r}\cdot(3,-15,-18)=-6$ (or $\mathbf{r}\cdot(1,-5,-6)=-2$).
Parametric plane $\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}+s\mathbf{b}+t\mathbf{c}$ given first.
Find $\mathbf{n}=\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c}$, then convert to $\mathbf{r}\cdot\mathbf{n}=\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{n}$ using the base point $\mathbf{a}$.

Common Misreadings

  • Using a point that is not actually in the plane (e.g. an arbitrary point rather than one on a contained line) when fixing $d$.
  • Thinking the plane equation needs both lines' points or a second condition, when one normal plus one point fully determines it.
  • Confusing the constant $d$ in $ax+by+cz=d$ with the $d$ in the point-to-plane distance formula $ax+by+cz+d=0$ (opposite sign).

Pitfalls

  • Sign error in the cross product, especially the $\mathbf{j}$-component which carries a minus in the determinant expansion.
  • Forgetting to substitute a point at all, leaving $d$ undetermined.
  • Substituting the point into the wrong side or mis-pairing coordinates with $(a,b,c)$.
  • Not simplifying $\mathbf{n}$, leading to heavy arithmetic and a non-matching 'show that' answer.
