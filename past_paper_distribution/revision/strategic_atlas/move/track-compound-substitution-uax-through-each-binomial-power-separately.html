Binomial Expansion

Track compound substitution u=ax through each binomial power separately

Family: Compound Substitution Tracking Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever you expand (1+u)^n where u is not plain x but a compound expression like 2x, −x/2, 3x, or x/4. The cue is any bracketed binomial whose 'second term' carries a numerical coefficient and/or a sign, combined with a fractional or negative index n. Equivalently: any time you see (1+ax)^n or (1−ax/b)^n with a≠1, you must plan to handle the constant a and its powers a^k as a separate bookkeeping stream. The trigger is sharpest when n is fractional or negative, because then the falling-factorial numerator already alternates in sign and you risk double-counting or cancelling sign changes.

Motivation — Why this works

The generalised binomial expansion (1+u)^n = 1 + nu + n(n−1)u²/2! + n(n−1)(n−2)u³/3! + … is only clean when u is a single symbol; substituting u=ax means every u^k silently injects an extra a^k factor that must be combined with the falling factorial and the k! denominator. Splitting the work into three independent streams — the falling-factorial numerator, the k! denominator, and the power a^k from u^k — eliminates the single most common source of lost marks: forgetting to raise the constant to the same power as x, or dropping a sign when a is negative. The underlying principle is simply the substitution rule applied carefully to a generating series; the discipline is bookkeeping, not new mathematics.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the bracket has the form (1+ax)^n with a≠1 (e.g. (1+2x)^{-1/2} or (1−x/2)^{-1}), so the 'inner variable' is u=ax, not x.
  2. Rule out: a direct Pascal's-triangle expansion (n is fractional/negative), and rule out trying to factor a out of the bracket unless the question explicitly needs the radius of convergence — that's a different move.
  3. Commit to the generalised binomial series (1+u)^n with u=ax, planning to substitute u^k = a^k x^k at the very end of each term so that a^k is visible and not absorbed into the coefficient prematurely.
  4. Bridge to operation: write each term schematically as [falling factorial / k!] · [a^k] · x^k, computing the three pieces in that fixed order so signs and powers can be checked independently.
  5. Tactical decision: track signs from two independent sources — the falling factorial n(n−1)(n−2)… (which alternates when n<0) and the constant a^k (which alternates if a<0) — and combine them only at the last step. This prevents the 'double-negative cancels what I expected to flip' error.
  6. Sanity-check the leading correction against the geometry: e.g. (1+2x)^{-1/2} must decrease for small x>0, so the x-coefficient must be negative.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify n and a from (1+ax)^n; write the template T_k = [n(n−1)…(n−k+1)/k!] · a^k · x^k.
  2. For k=1: coefficient = n; multiply by a; attach x. Sign from n only.
  3. For k=2: compute n(n−1)/2; multiply by a²; attach x². Note a² is always positive.
  4. For k=3: compute n(n−1)(n−2)/6; multiply by a³; attach x³. Sign of a³ matches sign of a.
  5. Simplify each term as a single rational number times x^k; only then combine and, if required, multiply by any external scalar.
  6. Verify by checking the k=1 term against the linear approximation (1+ax)^n ≈ 1 + nax.

Worked Micro-Example

Expand $(1+2x)^{-1/2}$ to $x^3$. Template $T_k = \binom{-1/2}{k}\cdot 2^k\cdot x^k$. $k=1$: $(-1/2)\cdot 2\cdot x = -x$. $k=2$: $\frac{(-1/2)(-3/2)}{2}\cdot 4\cdot x^2 = \frac{3}{8}\cdot 4\cdot x^2 = \frac{3}{2}x^2$. $k=3$: $\frac{(-1/2)(-3/2)(-5/2)}{6}\cdot 8\cdot x^3 = -\frac{15}{48}\cdot 8\cdot x^3 = -\frac{5}{2}x^3$. So $(1+2x)^{-1/2} \approx 1 - x + \tfrac{3}{2}x^2 - \tfrac{5}{2}x^3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Negative compound substitution, e.g. (1−x/2)^{-1}
Set a=−1/2; then a^k = (−1)^k/2^k contributes its own alternating sign, which may cancel the falling-factorial sign. For n=−1 the two sources cancel every term, recovering the geometric series 1 + x/2 + x²/4 + …
Large positive constant, e.g. (1+3x)^{-2}
No sign issue from a, but a^k = 3^k grows quickly; keep 3^k visible until the final simplification to avoid arithmetic slips.
Fractional shrinking constant, e.g. (1+x/4)^{-2}
a^k = 1/4^k shrinks the terms; same template, but track the denominator 4^k carefully alongside k!.
Both fractional n and negative a, e.g. (1−3x)^{1/3}
Track signs from falling factorial (sign pattern + − + − for n=1/3) and from (−3)^k separately, then combine at the last step.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating u=ax as if it were x and forgetting a entirely after the first term.
  • Substituting only 'a' (not 'a^k') into the kth term, e.g. writing (2x)² as 2x² instead of 4x².
  • Believing that a negative a always makes the expansion alternate, without checking the falling-factorial signs that may already alternate.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping a negative inside the falling factorial: writing (n−1) when n=−1/2 gives −3/2, not −1/2.
  • Computing (ax)^k incorrectly: (2x)^3 = 8x³, not 6x³ or 2x³.
  • Arithmetic errors when combining fractions like (−15/8)/6 — safest to write as −15/(8·6) = −15/48 then simplify to −5/16.
  • Combining the two sign sources in one mental step and losing track; always write the unsigned magnitudes first, then attach the overall sign.

A-level Scope Notes

This is the standard generalised binomial expansion in the Edexcel IAL P3/P4 formula booklet: (1+x)^n = 1 + nx + n(n−1)x²/2! + …, valid for |x|<1. With u=ax the validity becomes |ax|<1, i.e. |x|<1/|a|. Do not extend to non-real n or to multivariate expansions — those are out of scope.
