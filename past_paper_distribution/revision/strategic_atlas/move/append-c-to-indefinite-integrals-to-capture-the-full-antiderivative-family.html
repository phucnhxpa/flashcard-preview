Integration

Append +C to indefinite integrals to capture the full antiderivative family

Family: Integration Constant Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integral sign ∫ appears with NO limits of integration (no upper/lower bounds), or the question asks you to 'find ∫f(x) dx', 'integrate f(x)', or 'find the general solution' of dy/dx = f(x). Equivalent phrasings: 'find an expression for y in terms of x given dy/dx = ...', or any integration step where you have not yet been given an initial/boundary condition. The structural cue is simple: bounds absent ⇒ +C required.

Motivation — Why this works

Two functions with the same derivative differ by a constant (a corollary of the Mean Value Theorem, since d/dx(C) = 0). So if F(x) is one antiderivative of f(x), then F(x) + C is the most general antiderivative — without +C you have written down only one specific member of an infinite family. On Edexcel IAL mark schemes the constant of integration carries its own dedicated mark (typically B1), so omission loses a mark even when every other manipulation is flawless. This is the single most commonly dropped 'easy' mark in integration questions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I scan the integral: are there limits on the ∫ symbol? No bounds visible, so this is an indefinite integral, not a definite one.
  2. I rule out evaluating at endpoints (which would apply to a definite integral) and rule out the assumption that '+C' is implicit or optional — it is neither.
  3. I commit to producing a general antiderivative: every term integrated by the power/standard rules, plus a single additive constant at the end.
  4. Tactical decision: I use one combined constant C, not a separate constant per term — all the per-term constants merge into one arbitrary C.
  5. After writing the antiderivative expression, my final action before boxing the answer is to append '+ C' so the answer represents the entire family of antiderivatives.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Integrate each term using the standard rule ∫xⁿ dx = x^{n+1}/(n+1) (for n ≠ −1), or other standard antiderivatives.
  2. Combine the integrated terms into a single expression F(x).
  3. Append '+ C' (a single arbitrary constant) to give the final answer F(x) + C.
  4. If a boundary/initial condition is later supplied, substitute it into F(x) + C to solve for the specific value of C.

Worked Micro-Example

Find $\int (4x^3 - 5x^{-2})\,dx$.\\ Integrate term-by-term: $\frac{4x^4}{4} - \frac{5x^{-1}}{-1} = x^4 + 5x^{-1}$.\\ Append the constant: $\boxed{x^4 + 5x^{-1} + C}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Initial-value / boundary-condition problem (e.g. 'the curve passes through (1, 3)')
Still introduce +C immediately after integrating, then substitute the given point to solve for C and produce the specific antiderivative.
Differential equation dy/dx = f(x) with no condition given
+C remains arbitrary; the answer is the general solution y = F(x) + C.
Definite integral ∫_a^b f(x) dx
No +C is written — the constants cancel when evaluating F(b) − F(a).

Common Misreadings

  • Treating an indefinite integral as if it were definite because the answer 'looks complete' without +C.
  • Assuming +C is implied or that examiners will overlook its absence — they will not; it is an explicit B1 mark.
  • Thinking +C is only needed in differential-equation contexts, not in routine 'find ∫...' questions.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting +C entirely — loses the dedicated B1 mark even if all integration is correct.
  • Writing multiple constants C₁ + C₂ + C₃ (one per integrated term) instead of a single combined C.
  • Including +C on a definite integral (where it would be redundant since it cancels).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard for Edexcel IAL P2/P3 integration questions; the rule ∫xⁿ dx = x^{n+1}/(n+1) + C (n ≠ −1) is in the formula booklet. Stay within elementary antiderivatives covered in IAL — no need to invoke abstract antiderivative theory or general measure-theoretic constants.
