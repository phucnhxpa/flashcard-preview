Algebra & Functions

Raise both sides to reciprocal power to isolate x

Family: Inverse Exponent Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the equation has been reduced to the form x^{p/q} = k (or x^{-p/q} = k) with a single power of x equal to a constant. Typical cues: a fractional or negative exponent on x after dividing through by a coefficient, and no other x-terms remaining. Phrasings include 'solve x^{-3/2} = 8', 'find x given x^{2/3} = 9', or appearing as the final step after differentiating and setting derivative to zero in optimisation problems involving powers.

Motivation — Why this works

If x^{p} = k, then by the index law (a^m)^n = a^{mn}, raising both sides to the reciprocal power 1/p yields x^{p \cdot 1/p} = x^1 = k^{1/p}. This is the direct algebraic inverse of exponentiation by p, analogous to square-rooting x^2 = k. Choosing the exact reciprocal (including its sign) converts the left side to x in a single step, avoiding intermediate rearrangements via reciprocals or radicals.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation has the form x^{p/q} = k with a single power of x and a constant on the right.
  2. Rule out rewriting as a radical (e.g. 1/\sqrt{x^3} = 8) since that introduces an extra reciprocation step and risks sign/domain confusion.
  3. Commit to raising both sides to the reciprocal exponent because (p/q) \cdot (q/p) = 1 collapses the left to x in one move.
  4. Bridge: identify the reciprocal exponent precisely — for x^{-3/2} the reciprocal is -2/3, keeping the sign.
  5. Tactically evaluate k^{q/p} by splitting into a root then a power: e.g. 8^{-2/3} = (8^{1/3})^{-2} = 2^{-2} = 1/4, which is cleaner than (8^{-2})^{1/3}.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Isolate the power: get the equation into the form x^{p/q} = k by dividing/multiplying away any coefficient.
  2. Identify the reciprocal exponent q/p, preserving its sign.
  3. Raise both sides to that reciprocal: x = k^{q/p}.
  4. Evaluate k^{q/p} by taking the p-th root first then raising to the q-th power (or vice versa, whichever gives integer arithmetic).
  5. Check the result is consistent with any domain restriction (e.g. x > 0 if a fractional power was involved).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve \tfrac{3}{4}x^{-3/2} = 6.\\ Multiply by \tfrac{4}{3}: x^{-3/2} = 8.\\ Raise to the power -\tfrac{2}{3}: x = 8^{-2/3} = (8^{1/3})^{-2} = 2^{-2} = \tfrac{1}{4}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

x^{2/3} = 9
Raise both sides to 3/2: x = 9^{3/2} = 27.
x^{-1/2} = 5 (e.g. from rearranging 1/\sqrt{x} = 5)
Raise to -2: x = 5^{-2} = 1/25.
Optimisation: dy/dx = kx^{p} - c = 0 leads to x^{p} = c/k
Apply the same reciprocal-power move to extract x at the stationary point.
Exponential/decay style t^{-1/2} = c
Raise to -2: t = c^{-2}; same mechanic with integer reciprocal exponent.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the reciprocal of -3/2 as -3/2 itself (forgetting to flip numerator and denominator).
  • Confusing 'reciprocal of the exponent' with 'negative of the exponent', e.g. using +3/2 instead of -2/3.
  • Reading x^{-3/2} = 8 as -x^{3/2} = 8 and proceeding with a sign error.

Pitfalls

  • Mis-evaluating fractional powers: writing 8^{2/3} as (8/3)^2 or similar; correct route is (8^{1/3})^2 = 2^2 = 4.
  • Dropping the negative sign in the exponent and reporting x = 4 instead of x = 1/4.
  • Forgetting that even-denominator fractional powers require x > 0, so spurious negative roots should not be introduced.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics indices and surds (P1) and reappears in differentiation/optimisation (P2–P4) when solving f'(x)=0 for power functions; only real, positive bases are considered for fractional exponents.
