Trigger — When to use

This move fires whenever the working contains a trig expression that is structurally incompatible with the next step — for example, you have sin θ but the cosine rule or a dot product needs cos θ, or you have sin²x / cos²x inside an integral that has no direct antiderivative. Visual cues include: (i) a squared trig function (sin²x, cos²x) sitting inside an integral, especially multiplied by a polynomial factor, (ii) only one of {sin θ, cos θ, tan θ} is given but the next formula needs another, (iii) a request for an EXACT answer where going via arcsin/arctan would force decimals. In short: 'I have the wrong trig function for the formula I want to apply, and I need an exact algebraic bridge.'

Motivation — Why this works

Identity selection works because the Pythagorean identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 and the double-angle identity cos 2x = 1 − 2sin²x = 2cos²x − 1 each provide an EXACT algebraic link between trig functions, eliminating the need to compute the angle numerically. Pythagoras converts between sin and cos at a single angle (preserving exactness, surd form retained); the double-angle identity trades a power-reduction for a frequency-doubling, replacing sin²x or cos²x with a linear cosine that is amenable to integration by parts or direct antidifferentiation. The price paid (sign ambiguity for Pythagoras, doubled frequency for the double-angle form) is small compared to the structural simplification gained.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the mismatch: the next step (cosine rule, IBP, dot product, etc.) requires a specific trig function, but the given/derived expression supplies a different one — or a squared one with no elementary antiderivative.
  2. Rule out the numerical route: computing θ = arcsin(...) then taking cos(θ) introduces rounding and loses exact surd form; for integrals, attempting IBP directly on x·sin²x leads to circular or messy recursion.
  3. Rule out substitution where it fails: u = sin x cannot absorb a polynomial factor x; squaring both sides of an identity loses sign information.
  4. Commit to the identity that DIRECTLY isolates the unwanted term: use sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 to swap sin↔cos at one angle; use cos 2x = 1 − 2sin²x ⇒ sin²x = (1 − cos 2x)/2 to linearize a squared trig in an integrand; use cos 2x = 2cos²x − 1 ⇒ cos²x = (1 + cos 2x)/2 for the cos² version.
  5. Decide the sign / choose the variant: for Pythagoras, the sign of the square root is fixed by quadrant information (acute ⇒ +, obtuse ⇒ −). For double-angle, pick the rearrangement that isolates exactly the squared term you have.
  6. Substitute and simplify, keeping the result in exact form (surds, fractions of π) until the final numerical evaluation if any.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the target form needed by the next step (cos θ, or a linear trig function under the integral sign).
  2. Select the identity: Pythagorean sin²θ + cos²θ = 1, or double-angle sin²x = (1 − cos 2x)/2, or cos²x = (1 + cos 2x)/2.
  3. Rearrange algebraically: e.g. cos²θ = 1 − sin²θ, then cos θ = ±√(1 − sin²θ).
  4. Resolve the sign using contextual information (quadrant, obtuse/acute, given range of θ).
  5. Substitute the result back into the original expression and simplify before proceeding to the next technique (cosine rule, IBP, etc.).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\sin(\angle BAC) = \tfrac{8}{15}$ with $\angle BAC$ obtuse, find $\cos(\angle BAC)$ exactly. Apply $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$: $\cos^2\theta = 1 - \tfrac{64}{225} = \tfrac{161}{225}$, so $\cos\theta = \pm\tfrac{\sqrt{161}}{15}$. Since $\theta$ is obtuse, $\cos\theta = -\tfrac{\sqrt{161}}{15}$ — exact, no decimals introduced.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Given cos θ, need sin θ (e.g. trig substitution in integration)
Use sin θ = ±√(1 − cos²θ); sign from the substitution range (e.g. sin θ ≥ 0 for θ ∈ [−π/2, π/2]).
Integrand contains cos²x (with or without polynomial factor)
Use cos²x = (1 + cos 2x)/2 — same identity family, opposite sign rearrangement.
Integrand contains sin⁴x or cos⁴x
Apply the double-angle identity twice, generating cos 2x and cos 4x terms.
Given tan θ, need sec θ or vice versa
Use 1 + tan²θ = sec²θ — same Pythagorean family, derived by dividing by cos²θ.
Vector / dot-product geometry where only sin of angle is known
Same Pythagorean conversion to obtain cos θ for the dot product a·b = |a||b|cos θ.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying integration by parts directly on x·sin²x without first linearising — leads to a circular integral.
  • Reaching for a calculator: computing arcsin then cos numerically when the question asks for an EXACT answer.
  • Picking the wrong variant of cos 2x (e.g. using 2cos²x − 1 when the integrand has sin²x), forcing extra algebra.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting that √(cos²θ) = |cos θ|, not cos θ — the sign must be set from quadrant/obtuse-acute information.
  • Defaulting to the positive root when the angle is obtuse, giving the wrong cosine and a wrong final length in the cosine rule.
  • Dropping the factor of ½ when rearranging the double-angle identity: writing sin²x = 1 − cos 2x instead of (1 − cos 2x)/2.
  • Mis-doubling the argument: forgetting that ∫cos 2x dx = ½ sin 2x, not sin 2x.

A-level Scope Notes

All identities used are in the Edexcel IAL formula booklet (Pure Maths): sin²θ + cos²θ = 1, cos 2A = 1 − 2sin²A = 2cos²A − 1, and 1 + tan²θ = sec²θ (P3/P4). Stay within these; do not invoke complex-exponential derivations or product-to-sum identities beyond the booklet without justification.
