Vectors

Take absolute value of normals' dot product for acute dihedral angle

Family: Dot Product Angle Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question asks for the acute angle between two planes (or the dihedral angle) and normal vectors n₁, n₂ are available or readily extracted from Cartesian equations ax+by+cz=d. Phrasings include 'acute angle between the planes', 'angle of intersection', or implicit cues when two plane equations are given. Also fires when normals are obtained from a cross product of two direction vectors lying in each plane.

Motivation — Why this works

The angle between two planes equals the angle between their normals or its supplement, since reversing a normal flips the sign of the cosine. The dot product gives cos θ = (n₁·n₂)/(|n₁||n₂|), which can be negative depending on chosen orientations. Taking |n₁·n₂| forces a non-negative cosine, guaranteeing θ ∈ [0°, 90°] — the acute answer the question demands. This avoids ambiguity from arbitrary normal direction choices.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question wants the acute angle between two planes, and I can read normals directly from the Cartesian equations.
  2. Rule out projecting onto a single plane or using direction vectors of lines — the planes' angle is governed by their normals.
  3. Commit to cos θ = |n₁·n₂|/(|n₁||n₂|) because the modulus collapses both possible normal orientations into the acute case.
  4. Bridge: write down n₁ and n₂ as column vectors, then compute the scalar dot product and the two magnitudes separately.
  5. Tactically keep surds exact (e.g. √11·√6 = √66) until the final inverse cosine to preserve accuracy.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Read off normals n₁ and n₂ from the plane equations (coefficients of x, y, z).
  2. Compute the scalar dot product n₁·n₂.
  3. Compute |n₁| and |n₂| as square roots of sums of squares.
  4. Form cos θ = |n₁·n₂| / (|n₁|·|n₂|), keeping the modulus to enforce acuteness.
  5. Apply θ = cos⁻¹ of that value and give the answer to the required precision (degrees or radians).

Worked Micro-Example

Planes $3x+y-z=4$ and $x+y-2z=1$ have normals $\mathbf{n_1}=(3,1,-1)$, $\mathbf{n_2}=(1,1,-2)$. Then $\mathbf{n_1}\cdot\mathbf{n_2}=3+1+2=6$, $|\mathbf{n_1}|=\sqrt{11}$, $|\mathbf{n_2}|=\sqrt{6}$. So $\cos\theta=\dfrac{|6|}{\sqrt{66}}=\dfrac{6}{\sqrt{66}}$, giving $\theta=\cos^{-1}\!\left(\tfrac{6}{\sqrt{66}}\right)\approx 42.4°$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Acute angle between two lines with directions d₁, d₂
Use cos θ = |d₁·d₂|/(|d₁||d₂|); same modulus trick because direction vectors can be reversed.
Acute angle between a line (direction d) and a plane (normal n)
Use sin θ = |d·n|/(|d||n|), since the line–plane angle is the complement of the line–normal angle.
Plane given by three points instead of an equation
First obtain n via a cross product of two in-plane vectors, then proceed with |n₁·n₂|/(|n₁||n₂|).
Normals already perpendicular (n₁·n₂ = 0)
Modulus is irrelevant; θ = 90° immediately and the planes are perpendicular.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the angle between planes as the angle between in-plane direction vectors rather than between normals.
  • Reading the question's 'acute' as merely a hint about size rather than as a directive to apply the modulus.
  • Mistaking a line–plane angle problem for a plane–plane one and using cos instead of sin.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the absolute value and reporting the obtuse supplement.
  • Sign errors when extracting normal components from equations like $x - y + 2z = 5$.
  • Rounding |n₁| or |n₂| early, causing the final angle to be off in the last decimal.
  • Giving the answer in radians when the question expects degrees (or vice versa).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Core Pure / FP3 vector geometry: dot product, magnitudes, and angles between lines and planes in 3D using Cartesian or vector equations.
