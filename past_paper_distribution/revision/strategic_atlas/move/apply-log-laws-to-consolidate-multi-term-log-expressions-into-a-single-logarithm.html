Algebra & Functions

Apply log laws to consolidate multi-term log expressions into a single logarithm

Family: Log Law Simplification Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever you see two or more logarithmic terms with the same base joined by + or − signs, possibly with integer coefficients, e.g. \(\ln 4 - \ln 2\), \(2\ln 4 + \ln 3\), or \(\log_2(16x) + \log_2(x+1)\). Equivalent cues: an expression of the form \(a\ln p \pm b\ln q\) inside a final answer; a question demanding the answer 'in the form \(\ln a / \ln b\)' or 'as a single logarithm'; an equation with several log terms that must be reduced before equating arguments. The move is also signalled when a definite-integral evaluation produces \(\ln(\text{int}) - \ln(\text{int})\) where the arguments share a common factor.

Motivation — Why this works

The three log laws — power \(a\ln b = \ln(b^a)\), product \(\ln A + \ln B = \ln(AB)\), and quotient \(\ln A - \ln B = \ln(A/B)\) — collapse multi-term log expressions into a single \(\ln(\cdot)\). This achieves three things: (i) it produces the simplest exact form expected by mark schemes, (ii) it reduces calculator evaluations and rounding error, and (iii) it is the prerequisite step for solving equations by equating arguments of logs on both sides. The underlying principle is that logarithms turn multiplication/division/exponentiation into addition/subtraction/multiplication, so running the laws in reverse compresses sums/differences back into a product/quotient inside a single log.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the expression contains two or more log terms with the same base, possibly with integer coefficients in front — a clear log-law signal.
  2. Rule out alternatives: numerical evaluation would lose exactness; change-of-base alone won't combine the terms; factorising the arguments without the laws won't simplify the structure.
  3. Commit to the laws because the trigger pattern (sums/differences of same-base logs) maps exactly onto the product/quotient rules, and any leading coefficient can be absorbed first by the power rule.
  4. Bridge to action: deal with coefficients first (power rule reversed: \(2\ln 4 \to \ln 16\)), then combine pairs using product or quotient rule depending on the sign between them.
  5. Tactical choice: if the final form required is \(\ln a / \ln b\) (a quotient of logs, not a log of a quotient), keep numerator and denominator separately consolidated; if a single \(\ln\) is wanted, push all the way to one term and simplify integer arithmetic inside the argument.
  6. Sanity check: confirm the simplified argument is still positive and the simplification matches the form requested by the question.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Apply the power rule in reverse to absorb coefficients: \(k\ln a = \ln(a^k)\) (e.g. \(2\ln 4 = \ln 16\), \(2\ln 3 = \ln 9\)).
  2. Apply the product rule to any sum of same-base logs: \(\ln A + \ln B = \ln(AB)\).
  3. Apply the quotient rule to any difference of same-base logs: \(\ln A - \ln B = \ln(A/B)\).
  4. Simplify the resulting integer/rational argument (e.g. \(16 \times 3 = 48\), \(4/2 = 2\)).
  5. If the question requests the form \(\log_b a\), recognise \(\ln a / \ln b = \log_b a\) by change of base.

Worked Micro-Example

Simplify \(x = \dfrac{2\ln 4 + \ln 3}{2\ln 3 - \ln 4}\). Power rule: \(2\ln 4 = \ln 16\), \(2\ln 3 = \ln 9\). Product rule on numerator: \(\ln 16 + \ln 3 = \ln 48\). Quotient rule on denominator: \(\ln 9 - \ln 4 = \ln(9/4)\). Therefore \(x = \dfrac{\ln 48}{\ln(9/4)} = \log_{9/4} 48 \approx 4.77\).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation with several log terms on each side, e.g. \(\log_2(16x) + \log_2(x+1) = \log_2(x+6) + 3\)
Combine each side into a single log first (use product/quotient rules), then convert any constant \(k\) into a log via \(k = \log_a(a^k)\), then equate arguments.
Definite integral yields \(\ln 4 - \ln 2\) plus rational pieces
Apply quotient rule to log part to get \(\ln 2\); handle rational arithmetic separately over a common denominator; final answer is \(\ln 2 + \text{fraction}\).
Answer required 'in the form \(\ln a / \ln b\)' or as \(\log_b a\)
Stop the consolidation when numerator and denominator are each a single log; do not combine across the division line.
Surface uses \(\log_{10}\) or general \(\log_a\) instead of \(\ln\)
Same three laws apply identically — only the base symbol changes.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating \(\ln A / \ln B\) as if the quotient rule applies, writing it as \(\ln(A/B)\) — the rule is for \(\ln A - \ln B\), not \(\ln A \div \ln B\).
  • Reading \(\ln(A) + \ln(B)\) as \(\ln(A+B)\) by analogy with distribution.
  • Subtracting arguments directly: \(\ln 4 - \ln 2 = \ln(4-2)\) — gives the right answer here by coincidence and entrenches a wrong method.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to apply the power rule to coefficients before combining (e.g. trying to product-rule \(2\ln 4 + \ln 3\) directly).
  • Splitting \(\log_2(16x)\) as \(\log_2(16)\cdot \log_2(x)\) instead of \(\log_2 16 + \log_2 x\).
  • Sign errors when the difference of logs comes from an evaluated bracket (missing the outer minus from a definite integral).
  • Arithmetic slips inside the consolidated argument (e.g. \(4^2 = 16\), \(16 \times 3 = 48\)).

A-level Scope Notes

All three laws (power, product, quotient) are core Edexcel IAL P2/P3 content and are NOT in the formula booklet — they must be memorised. Stay with positive arguments only; do not extend to complex logarithms. Change of base \(\log_b a = \ln a / \ln b\) is in scope and often the bridge to a final numerical answer.
