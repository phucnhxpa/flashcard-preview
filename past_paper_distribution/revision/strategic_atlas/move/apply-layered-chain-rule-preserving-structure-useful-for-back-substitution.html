Differentiation

Apply layered chain rule preserving structure useful for back-substitution

Family: Chain Rule Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever the expression to differentiate is a composition of three or more layers — typically an outer power wrapping a trig/exp function wrapping a linear inner function (e.g. $\sin^2(2y)$, $\cos^3(3x)$, $e^{f(t)^2}$), OR when a derivative already obtained in one variable must itself be re-differentiated with respect to another variable (so the chain rule must be re-applied with a product rule inside). Visual cues: nested brackets, exponents on trig functions, parametric/substitution problems where $\frac{dy}{dx}$ is expressed as a product of $t$-dependent factors. Phrased alternatively: 'differentiate a composite of three things' or 'differentiate $\frac{dy}{dx}$ again when $x$ and $y$ are both functions of a parameter'.

Motivation — Why this works

The chain rule says $\frac{d}{dy}f(g(h(y))) = f'(g(h))\cdot g'(h)\cdot h'(y)$ — each nested layer contributes one factor. Working layer-by-layer prevents the most common error (skipping the innermost factor) and keeps the derivative in a structurally useful form. The strategic refinement is to NOT immediately collapse the result via identities (e.g. double-angle) when the un-collapsed factored form will be needed later for back-substitution into the original variable, or for combining with terms from an ODE. This same operator-thinking, $\frac{d}{dx} = \frac{dt}{dx}\cdot\frac{d}{dt}$, also generates higher derivatives correctly via the product rule.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the expression has more than two nested layers — e.g. an outer power, a middle trig, and an inner linear function — or that I'm being asked to differentiate something already expressed as a product in a different variable.
  2. Rule out shortcuts: I could try to simplify first (e.g. $\sin^2(2y) = \tfrac12(1-\cos 4y)$), but doing so may destroy structure that the rest of the question needs (such as factors of $\sin(2y)$ and $\cos(2y)$ I can replace using the original equation and Pythagoras).
  3. Commit to peeling layers one at a time, writing each derivative factor explicitly so no inner factor (like the innocuous $2$ from $\frac{d}{dy}(2y)$) is dropped.
  4. For a second derivative under substitution: recognise that $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} \neq \left(\frac{dt}{dx}\right)^2 \frac{d^2y}{dt^2}$. Instead apply the operator $\frac{d}{dx} = \frac{dt}{dx}\cdot\frac{d}{dt}$ to the whole expression for $\frac{dy}{dx}$, which is itself a product, so the product rule activates inside.
  5. Tactical decision: keep the answer in factored/product form rather than applying identities, because the next step of the question typically requires substituting the original variable back in piece-by-piece.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the layers: write the function as $F(G(H(y)))$ with $H$ innermost.
  2. Outer derivative: bring down the power / differentiate the outer function, evaluated at the inner pieces.
  3. Middle derivative: differentiate the middle function with respect to its argument.
  4. Inner derivative: differentiate the innermost (often linear) function — this is the factor most often missed.
  5. Multiply all factors together; do NOT simplify away product structure if subsequent steps need the individual factors.
  6. For iterated chain rule (second derivative under substitution $x = x(t)$): write $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{dt}{dx}\cdot\frac{d}{dt}\!\left[\frac{dy}{dx}\right]$, expand the inner $\frac{d}{dt}$ using the product rule, then multiply through by $\frac{dt}{dx}$.

Worked Micro-Example

Differentiate $x = 6\sin^2(2y)$ w.r.t. $y$. Layers: power, sine, linear. $\frac{dx}{dy} = 6\cdot 2\sin(2y)\cdot \cos(2y)\cdot 2 = 24\sin(2y)\cos(2y)$. Keep this product form: since $\sin^2(2y) = x/6$ and $\cos^2(2y) = 1 - x/6$, we get $\left(\frac{dx}{dy}\right)^2 = 576\cdot\frac{x}{6}\cdot\left(1-\frac{x}{6}\right)$ — impossible to obtain cleanly if we had collapsed to $12\sin(4y)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Differentiating $\cos^3(3x)$, $\tan^2(\tfrac12 x)$, or similar three-layer trig powers
Same layered procedure: outer power gives $n\cdot(\text{inner})^{n-1}$, then derivative of trig, then derivative of linear inner — three factors multiplied.
Second derivative under Euler substitution $x = e^{t}$
$\frac{dt}{dx} = e^{-t}$, so iterated chain rule gives $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = e^{-2t}\!\left(\frac{d^2y}{dt^2} - \frac{dy}{dt}\right)$ — same operator + product rule logic.
Parametric second derivative with $x = t^2$
$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{1}{4t^2}\frac{d^2y}{dt^2} - \frac{1}{4t^3}\frac{dy}{dt}$, obtained from product rule on $\frac{1}{2t}\frac{dy}{dt}$ then multiplying by $\frac{1}{2t}$.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ as $\left(\frac{dt}{dx}\right)^2\frac{d^2y}{dt^2}$ — squaring the first-derivative operator instead of applying it twice.
  • Reading $\sin^2(2y)$ as $(\sin 2y)\cdot 2$ in some hybrid way, or treating $\frac{d}{dy}\sin^2(2y)$ as $(\cos(2y))^2$.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the innermost factor — e.g. forgetting the extra $\times 2$ from $\frac{d}{dy}(2y)$.
  • Forgetting the product rule inside the iterated chain rule — missing the term coming from differentiating the $1/(2t)$ factor.
  • Sign slip on $\frac{d}{dt}\!\left[\frac{1}{2t}\right] = -\frac{1}{2t^2}$.
  • Collapsing the result via a double-angle identity too early, destroying factors needed for back-substitution into $x$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 (chain rule, parametric and implicit differentiation) and FP-level second-order ODE substitutions. No formula-booklet entry — chain rule and product rule are assumed. Avoid invoking Faà di Bruno's formula or general operator theory; stay with explicit layer-by-layer differentiation.
