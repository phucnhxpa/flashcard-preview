Integration

Apply secondary linear shift to bypass partial fractions on repeated linear denominators

Family: Linear Shift Simplification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an integrand has the form (linear)/(u−c)^n, where the denominator is a power of a single linear factor. Typical cue: after a first substitution you land on something like ∫(u+1)/(u−1)² du or ∫(2u+3)/(u−4)³ du. Recognising that the denominator is already 'one term' (not a product of distinct factors) signals that a shift w = u − c will collapse the fraction into pure power-of-w terms.

Motivation — Why this works

The shift w = u − c rewrites the numerator as a linear function of w, so (au+b)/(u−c)^n becomes (aw + (ac+b))/w^n, which splits termwise into a·w^(1−n) + (ac+b)·w^(−n). Each piece is a standard power-rule or logarithm integral, requiring no system of equations. This is essentially partial fractions 'by inspection': because the only factor is (u−c), there is nothing to decompose — only to re-centre.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the denominator is a pure power of a single linear factor (u−1)², not a product of distinct factors.
  2. Rule out formal partial fractions A/(u−1) + B/(u−1)² as unnecessarily heavy — it would require solving for A, B.
  3. Commit to the shift w = u−1 because it makes the denominator a monomial w² and rewrites the numerator linearly in w with no algebraic cost.
  4. Bridge: compute u+1 in terms of w by substituting u = w+1, giving numerator w+2; note du = dw and shift the limits by 1.
  5. Inside the move, split (w+2)/w² = 1/w + 2w⁻² and integrate to ln|w| − 2/w, then back-substitute or evaluate at shifted limits.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the linear factor (u−c) in the denominator and set w = u − c, so du = dw.
  2. Express the numerator au+b as a(w+c)+b = aw + (ac+b).
  3. Rewrite the integral as ∫(aw + (ac+b))/w^n dw.
  4. Split into a·w^(1−n) + (ac+b)·w^(−n) and integrate each by the power rule (or ln|w| when the exponent is −1).
  5. Shift the limits (u = α → w = α−c) for definite integrals, or back-substitute w = u−c for indefinite ones.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_{7/2}^{6} \frac{u+1}{(u-1)^2}\,du$. Let $w=u-1$, so $u+1=w+2$, $du=dw$, limits $5/2 \to 5$. Then $\int_{5/2}^{5} \frac{w+2}{w^2}\,dw = \int_{5/2}^{5} \left(\frac{1}{w}+\frac{2}{w^2}\right)dw = \left[\ln|w| - \frac{2}{w}\right]_{5/2}^{5} = \left(\ln 5 - \tfrac{2}{5}\right) - \left(\ln\tfrac{5}{2} - \tfrac{4}{5}\right) = \ln 2 + \tfrac{2}{5}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

∫(au+b)/(u−c)^n du for n ≥ 2
Shift w = u−c; numerator becomes aw + (ac+b); split into a·w^(1−n) + (ac+b)·w^(−n) and apply the power rule.
∫(quadratic)/(u−c)^n du
Same shift w = u−c; expand the quadratic numerator in (w+c) to get a polynomial in w, then divide each term by w^n.
Formal partial fractions A/(u−c) + B/(u−c)²
Equivalent outcome but requires solving simultaneous equations; the shift skips that algebra.
After a trig or exponential substitution leaving (e^t + k)/(e^t − c)^2 type form
A further shift w = e^t − c (or its analogue) achieves the same simplification on the inner rational expression.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating (u−1)² as if it required full partial fractions decomposition with two unknown constants A and B.
  • Mistaking the denominator for an irreducible quadratic and reaching for an arctan form.
  • Trying long polynomial division on (u+1)/(u−1)² when the degree of the numerator is already lower than the denominator.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to shift the limits when changing variable to w = u − c in a definite integral.
  • Sign error on ∫2w⁻² dw: the antiderivative is −2/w, not +2/w.
  • Arithmetic slip when evaluating −2/(5/2): correctly equals −4/5, not −5/4.
  • Failing to rewrite the numerator fully in terms of w (e.g. leaving u+1 unchanged after substituting in the denominator).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths / Further Pure scope: linear substitution, the power rule, and ∫1/w dw = ln|w|. Offered as a faster alternative to partial fractions with repeated linear factors (C4/FP).
