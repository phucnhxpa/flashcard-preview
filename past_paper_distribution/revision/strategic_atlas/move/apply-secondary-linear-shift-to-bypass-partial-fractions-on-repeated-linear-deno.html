Integration

Apply secondary linear shift to bypass partial fractions on repeated linear denominators

Family: Linear Shift Simplification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger ŌĆö When to use

Fires when an integrand has the form (linear)/(uŌłÆc)^n, where the denominator is a power of a single linear factor. Typical cue: after a first substitution you land on something like Ōł½(u+1)/(uŌłÆ1)┬▓ du or Ōł½(2u+3)/(uŌłÆ4)┬│ du. Recognising that the denominator is already 'one term' (not a product of distinct factors) signals that a shift w = u ŌłÆ c will collapse the fraction into pure power-of-w terms.

Motivation ŌĆö Why this works

The shift w = u ŌłÆ c rewrites the numerator as a linear function of w, so (au+b)/(uŌłÆc)^n becomes (aw + (ac+b))/w^n, which splits termwise into a┬Ęw^(1ŌłÆn) + (ac+b)┬Ęw^(ŌłÆn). Each piece is a standard power-rule or logarithm integral, requiring no system of equations. This is essentially partial fractions 'by inspection': because the only factor is (uŌłÆc), there is nothing to decompose ŌĆö only to re-centre.

Thought Process ŌĆö The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the denominator is a pure power of a single linear factor (uŌłÆ1)┬▓, not a product of distinct factors.
  2. Rule out formal partial fractions A/(uŌłÆ1) + B/(uŌłÆ1)┬▓ as unnecessarily heavy ŌĆö it would require solving for A, B.
  3. Commit to the shift w = uŌłÆ1 because it makes the denominator a monomial w┬▓ and rewrites the numerator linearly in w with no algebraic cost.
  4. Bridge: compute u+1 in terms of w by substituting u = w+1, giving numerator w+2; note du = dw and shift the limits by 1.
  5. Inside the move, split (w+2)/w┬▓ = 1/w + 2wŌü╗┬▓ and integrate to ln|w| ŌłÆ 2/w, then back-substitute or evaluate at shifted limits.

Mechanic ŌĆö Concrete operations

  1. Identify the linear factor (uŌłÆc) in the denominator and set w = u ŌłÆ c, so du = dw.
  2. Express the numerator au+b as a(w+c)+b = aw + (ac+b).
  3. Rewrite the integral as Ōł½(aw + (ac+b))/w^n dw.
  4. Split into a┬Ęw^(1ŌłÆn) + (ac+b)┬Ęw^(ŌłÆn) and integrate each by the power rule (or ln|w| when the exponent is ŌłÆ1).
  5. Shift the limits (u = ╬▒ ŌåÆ w = ╬▒ŌłÆc) for definite integrals, or back-substitute w = uŌłÆc for indefinite ones.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_{7/2}^{6} \frac{u+1}{(u-1)^2}\,du$. Let $w=u-1$, so $u+1=w+2$, $du=dw$, limits $5/2 \to 5$. Then $\int_{5/2}^{5} \frac{w+2}{w^2}\,dw = \int_{5/2}^{5} \left(\frac{1}{w}+\frac{2}{w^2}\right)dw = \left[\ln|w| - \frac{2}{w}\right]_{5/2}^{5} = \left(\ln 5 - \tfrac{2}{5}\right) - \left(\ln\tfrac{5}{2} - \tfrac{4}{5}\right) = \ln 2 + \tfrac{2}{5}$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating (uŌłÆ1)┬▓ as if it required full partial fractions decomposition with two unknown constants A and B.
  • Mistaking the denominator for an irreducible quadratic and reaching for an arctan form.
  • Trying long polynomial division on (u+1)/(uŌłÆ1)┬▓ when the degree of the numerator is already lower than the denominator.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to shift the limits when changing variable to w = u ŌłÆ c in a definite integral.
  • Sign error on Ōł½2wŌü╗┬▓ dw: the antiderivative is ŌłÆ2/w, not +2/w.
  • Arithmetic slip when evaluating ŌłÆ2/(5/2): correctly equals ŌłÆ4/5, not ŌłÆ5/4.
  • Failing to rewrite the numerator fully in terms of w (e.g. leaving u+1 unchanged after substituting in the denominator).
