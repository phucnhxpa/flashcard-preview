Cross-multiply to clear denominators and reduce to a polynomial equation

Family: Fraction Clearing Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever you face an equation of the form a/b = c/d (two fractions equated) or a single fraction equal to a known expression, e.g. k = N(x,y)/D(x,y). Visual cues: variables (or unknowns) trapped in denominators; the target/expected form is polynomial (no fractions); the denominator is a single product or simple expression you can multiply away. Equivalent phrasings: 'clear the fraction', 'multiply through by the denominator', 'apply ad = bc'. Particularly natural in locus problems (where the goal is a polynomial curve equation) and in solving for a single unknown trapped on one side of a quotient.

Motivation — Why this works

Cross-multiplying converts a rational equation into a polynomial equation in one stroke, eliminating denominators that obstruct expansion, comparison of coefficients, or recognition of a standard curve. The underlying identity is simply: if b, d ≠ 0, then a/b = c/d ⇔ ad = bc. This is the cleanest path because the resulting polynomial equation is what downstream work (factoring, completing the square, identifying conics, equating real/imaginary parts) requires. It also avoids the bookkeeping of carrying denominators through several lines of algebra.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structural cue: I have a fraction (or a ratio of two fractions) and the target form is polynomial / fraction-free.
  2. Consider alternatives — partial fractions (no, only one term), substitution (no, doesn't remove the fraction), or expanding numerator first (premature). None match as cleanly as clearing the denominator.
  3. Check that the denominator is nonzero on the relevant domain (e.g. (4+√3) is a nonzero real; in a locus problem note any excluded points where y = 0 or x = 5).
  4. Commit to cross-multiplication: write ad = bc, or equivalently multiply both sides by the denominator(s).
  5. Decide carefully on signs and which factor multiplies which side — a minus sign in a numerator (e.g. −y) is a common slip point.
  6. Expand the resulting products fully and rearrange into the canonical target form (e.g. standard conic, linear equation in the unknown).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify denominators D₁, D₂ and confirm they are nonzero.
  2. Apply ad = bc (for two-fraction case) or multiply both sides by D (single-fraction case).
  3. Expand each product carefully, tracking signs.
  4. Collect like terms and rearrange to the desired canonical form (polynomial = 0, or unknown isolated).
  5. Note any domain restrictions inherited from the original denominators.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\dfrac{2x}{y} = \dfrac{-y}{x-5}$, cross-multiply: $2x(x-5) = (-y)(y) = -y^2$. Expand: $2x^2 - 10x = -y^2$. Rearrange: $2x^2 + y^2 = 10x$ — a polynomial (ellipse) equation, with restriction $y \neq 0,\; x \neq 5$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Single fraction equal to a constant or known expression, e.g. k = N/D
Multiply both sides by D once: kD = N. Equivalent to cross-multiplication with denominator 1 on the other side.
Complex-number locus problems with |z − a|/|z − b| = k
Cross-multiply to get |z − a| = k|z − b|, then square both sides to clear the moduli — produces a circle (or line if k = 1).
Surd denominators
Rationalise first by multiplying by the conjugate; then cross-multiply if a fraction remains.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating a/b + c/d = e as cross-multipliable directly — it is not; you must combine LHS over a common denominator first.
  • Assuming the denominator is automatically nonzero and forgetting to record domain restrictions for a locus.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when a numerator carries a minus, e.g. (−y)(y) = −y², not +y².
  • Failing to fully expand a product like 2x(x−5), leaving 2x² − 5 instead of 2x² − 10x.
  • Losing solutions or introducing extraneous ones by ignoring the cases where a denominator equals zero.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1–P4) and Further Pure scope; no formula booklet entry needed — this is a basic algebraic manipulation. Avoid generalising to multiplying by expressions that may be zero or change sign without case analysis (relevant when the denominator contains the unknown).
