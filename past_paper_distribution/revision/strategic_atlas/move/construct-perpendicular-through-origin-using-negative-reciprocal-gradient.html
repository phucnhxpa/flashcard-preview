Coordinate Geometry

Construct perpendicular through origin using negative reciprocal gradient

Family: Line Equation Construction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a second line $l_2$ must be perpendicular to a known line $l_1$ (often a tangent or chord) AND passes through the origin or another explicitly named point. Cues include phrases like 'the line through O perpendicular to...', 'the normal from the origin', or a setup where $l_1$ has a known gradient $m_1$. The origin condition is the simplifying feature: no $y$-intercept needs solving for.

Motivation — Why this works

Two non-vertical lines are perpendicular iff $m_1 m_2 = -1$, so $m_2 = -1/m_1$. A line through the origin with gradient $m$ has equation $y = mx$, with no constant term to determine. This produces the cleanest possible substitutable form for finding the intersection point with $l_1$, since $y$ is already isolated as a single term in $x$.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice $l_2$ has two pieces of data: a perpendicularity constraint and a known point (the origin).
  2. Rule out using $y - y_0 = m(x - x_0)$ in full generality — the origin collapses it to $y = mx$.
  3. Commit to negative-reciprocal gradient because $m_1$ is already in hand from the tangent/given line.
  4. Write $m_2 = -1/m_1$ and simplify the compound fraction carefully, tracking signs.
  5. Decide presentation: leave $y = m_2 x$ in the cleanest algebraic form ready to substitute into $l_1$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify $m_1$, the gradient of $l_1$.
  2. Compute $m_2 = -1/m_1$, simplifying any compound fraction (flip and negate).
  3. Write $l_2: y = m_2 x$ since it passes through $O(0,0)$.
  4. Hand off to substitution into $l_1$ to locate the intersection.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose $l_1$ has gradient $m_1 = -\dfrac{2\cos\theta}{3\sin\theta}$. Then $m_2 = -\dfrac{1}{m_1} = \dfrac{3\sin\theta}{2\cos\theta}$. Since $l_2$ passes through $O$, $l_2: y = \dfrac{3\sin\theta}{2\cos\theta}\,x$. This is now ready to substitute into $l_1$'s equation to solve for the foot of the perpendicular.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Perpendicular through a non-origin point $(x_0,y_0)$
Use $y - y_0 = m_2(x - x_0)$; expand only if substitution requires it.
$l_1$ is horizontal ($m_1 = 0$)
Negative reciprocal undefined; $l_2$ is the vertical line $x = x_0$ (here $x = 0$).
$l_1$ is vertical (undefined gradient)
$l_2$ is horizontal: $y = y_0$ (here $y = 0$).
Normal to a curve at a point (Pure 1/2 calculus context)
Compute $m_1 = \dfrac{dy}{dx}$ at the point first, then apply the same negative-reciprocal step.
Perpendicular bisector of a chord
Combine this move with the midpoint formula to obtain $(x_0,y_0)$ before applying point-gradient form.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 'perpendicular' as 'parallel' and reusing $m_1$ instead of $-1/m_1$.
  • Assuming the perpendicular passes through the same point as $l_1$'s defining point rather than the origin.
  • Treating $m_1 m_2 = -1$ as $m_1 + m_2 = 0$.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the sign: writing $m_2 = 1/m_1$ instead of $-1/m_1$.
  • Mis-simplifying $-1 \big/ \left(-\tfrac{2\cos\theta}{3\sin\theta}\right)$ — the two negatives cancel to give a positive result.
  • Leaving $y = m_2 x$ with an unsimplified compound fraction, making later substitution error-prone.
  • Adding a spurious '+ c' constant despite the origin condition forcing $c=0$.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics content (P1 coordinate geometry, extended in P2/P3 for normals to curves). Uses only the perpendicularity criterion $m_1 m_2 = -1$ and point-gradient form of a straight line.
