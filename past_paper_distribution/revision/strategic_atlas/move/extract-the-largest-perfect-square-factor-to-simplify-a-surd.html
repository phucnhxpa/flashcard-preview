Extract the largest perfect square factor to simplify a surd

Family: Surd Simplification Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a surd √n appears with n not itself a perfect square but containing a perfect square factor (e.g. √24, √27, √192, √432). Typical structural cues include: the question demands an 'exact value', a final answer must match a prescribed form such as a√b or (P ± √Q)/R, the surd appears in the output of the quadratic formula, or the surd sits in a numerator/denominator where cancellation with rational coefficients is needed. Equivalently: if you see √n where n has any factor that is a square (4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100, 121, 144, …), the surd is not yet in simplest form. The presence of decimal-looking surds left over after applying a formula is also a strong cue.

Motivation — Why this works

The move uses the identity √(ab) = √a · √b, choosing a to be the largest perfect square dividing n so that √a becomes a rational integer. This converts √n into k√m with m square-free, which is the standard 'simplest surd form' required by Edexcel mark schemes. Beyond cosmetic simplification, the extracted integer factor k frequently shares a common factor with adjacent rational terms (numerators, denominators, coefficients), unlocking cancellation that is otherwise hidden. Without the extraction, fractions of the form (P ± √Q)/R cannot be reduced and marks for 'fully simplified' or 'exact' answers are lost.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice a surd √n where n is not a perfect square (e.g. √432), and the question demands an exact / fully simplified answer or a specific form like a√b or (P ± √Q)/R.
  2. I rule out a decimal approximation (would lose the 'exact' mark) and rule out rationalisation-by-conjugate (no surd denominator of the form a ± √b is present yet).
  3. I commit to surd simplification because n contains a perfect square factor — this is the only way to produce an integer coefficient outside the radical that can interact with the rest of the expression.
  4. To find the largest perfect square factor, I either prime-factorise n and pair the primes (e.g. 432 = 2⁴·3³ = (2²·2²·3²)·3 = 144·3) or scan known squares 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100, 121, 144 from the largest downward.
  5. I write n = k²·m with m square-free, then √n = k√m. I always check m has no remaining square factor (a common error is stopping at √432 = 4√27, which still simplifies).
  6. Finally, I substitute k√m back into the full expression and look for a common factor between k and surrounding rational terms (HCF cancellation) to reach the prescribed form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify n inside √n.
  2. Find the largest perfect square k² dividing n by prime factorisation or by trial against the list of squares; write n = k²·m with m square-free.
  3. Apply √n = √(k²·m) = √(k²)·√m = k√m.
  4. Substitute k√m back into the original expression.
  5. Identify the HCF of k and any other rational coefficients/denominators, and divide top and bottom by that HCF to fully simplify.

Worked Micro-Example

Simplify $\sqrt{432}$ inside $x = \dfrac{48 \pm \sqrt{432}}{18}$. Factorise: $432 = 144 \times 3$. Then $\sqrt{432} = \sqrt{144}\,\sqrt{3} = 12\sqrt{3}$. Substitute: $x = \dfrac{48 \pm 12\sqrt{3}}{18}$. Divide numerator and denominator by HCF $= 6$: $x = \dfrac{8 \pm 2\sqrt{3}}{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Surd appears in (P ± √Q)/R form from the quadratic formula
After extracting k√m, look for an HCF between k, P and R; divide all three by it to match the prescribed answer form.
Cube root √[3]{n} (Further Maths / occasional A-level)
Extract the largest perfect cube factor instead, e.g. √[3]{54} = √[3]{27·2} = 3√[3]{2}.
Surd in a denominator of a coefficient, e.g. y = 3/(√27 · x^{3/2})
Simplify √27 = 3√3 first; this exposes a common factor of 3 with the numerator that can then be cancelled.
Surd from a distance/length, e.g. √(4a² + 4b²)
Factor out the perfect square coefficient first: √(4(a²+b²)) = 2√(a²+b²).
Radicand has no perfect square factor > 1 (e.g. √6, √15)
Already in simplest form — leave it.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'exact value' as 'decimal to 3 s.f.' and writing √24 ≈ 4.899 instead of 2√6.
  • Reading √n as already simplest just because n is not a square, missing that n still has a square factor.
  • Confusing √(ab) = √a·√b with √a + √b (illegal split).

Pitfalls

  • Not extracting the LARGEST square factor: writing √432 = 4√27 or √192 = 2√48 and stopping, leaving the surd unsimplified.
  • Forgetting to take the square root of the perfect square factor: writing √24 = √4·√6 = 4√6 instead of 2√6.
  • Cancelling only part of the common factor in the surrounding fraction, e.g. (16 ± 8√3)/32 → (8 ± 4√3)/16 instead of (2 ± √3)/4.
  • Arithmetic slips in factorisation (e.g. claiming 27 = 4 × ...) leading to wrong extracted coefficient.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1 surds chapter) and reused throughout P2–P4 wherever the quadratic formula, distance formula, or 'exact value' instructions occur. No formula booklet entry is invoked — the identity √(ab)=√a·√b is assumed knowledge. Avoid generalising to nested radicals like √(a+√b) or to imaginary surds (√(−n) belongs to FP1 complex numbers and is treated separately).
