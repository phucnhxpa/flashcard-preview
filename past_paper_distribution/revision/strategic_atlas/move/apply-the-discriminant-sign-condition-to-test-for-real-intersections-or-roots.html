Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a quadratic equation $ax^2+bx+c=0$ has been produced (often by substituting a line into a curve, or by rearranging a case in a 'find all solutions' problem) and the question asks about the EXISTENCE or NUMBER of real roots/intersections. Cue phrases include 'does not intersect', 'meets at exactly one point', 'two distinct points', 'show that there are no real solutions', or any sub-case whose validity depends on whether real $x$ exist. Also fires when the question contains a parameter $k$ and asks for the range of $k$ ensuring some intersection condition.

Motivation — Why this works

The discriminant $\Delta = b^2-4ac$ classifies the roots of a quadratic without solving it: $\Delta>0$ gives two distinct real roots, $\Delta=0$ a repeated (tangent) root, $\Delta<0$ no real roots. Because the real roots of the derived quadratic are exactly the $x$-coordinates of intersection (or the valid solutions of a case), the geometric/existence question converts into a clean algebraic (in)equality. This is faster and more conclusive than attempting the quadratic formula, and when a parameter is present it produces a quadratic inequality in that parameter.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the problem reduces to a quadratic in $x$ and the question is about how many (if any) real $x$ satisfy it, not about the actual values.
  2. Rule out solving by formula or factorisation: those are unnecessary work if only existence/count is required, and may lead into complex arithmetic.
  3. Commit to the discriminant test because it directly classifies real-root behaviour using only the coefficients.
  4. Translate the question's wording into the correct sign: 'no intersections' $\Rightarrow \Delta<0$; 'does not cross' often $\Rightarrow \Delta\le 0$ (tangency allowed); 'exactly one' $\Rightarrow \Delta=0$; 'two distinct' $\Rightarrow \Delta>0$.
  5. Read off $a,b,c$ carefully (watching signs, especially when $b$ is itself a negated expression in $k$), then compute $b^2-4ac$.
  6. Apply the sign condition: either conclude immediately (numeric case) or simplify the resulting inequality in the parameter and solve it as a quadratic inequality.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify coefficients $a$, $b$, $c$ from the quadratic, keeping brackets around any signed parameter expression.
  2. Compute $b^2$ (square the whole bracket so the outer sign disappears).
  3. Compute $4ac$.
  4. Form $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$.
  5. Impose the appropriate sign: $<0$, $\le 0$, $=0$, $\ge 0$, or $>0$ as dictated by the question wording.
  6. If numeric: state the conclusion (real roots exist / do not exist). If parametric: simplify to a tidy (in)equality in the parameter and solve.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $2x^2 - x + 4 = 0$ (a sub-case): identify $a=2,\ b=-1,\ c=4$. Then $\Delta = (-1)^2 - 4(2)(4) = 1 - 32 = -31 < 0$, so there are no real solutions and this case is dismissed. Parametric variant: from $16x^2-(16k-40)x+9=0$, $\Delta\le 0$ gives $(16k-40)^2 - 576 \le 0$, i.e. $256k^2-1280k+1024\le 0$, a quadratic inequality in $k$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line meets curve at exactly one point (tangency).
Use $\Delta = 0$ and solve for the parameter; the resulting equation typically gives discrete values of $k$.
Two distinct intersection points required.
Use $\Delta > 0$ and solve a strict quadratic inequality in the parameter (sketch the parabola in $k$ to read off the range).
Dismissing a case in a 'find all real $x$' problem (e.g. after splitting on $|f(x)|$ or squaring).
Compute $\Delta$ numerically; if $\Delta<0$, state 'no real roots' and close the case without further work.
The derived equation is not quadratic (cubic, quartic, surd).
The discriminant test does not directly apply; reduce to a quadratic in a substitute variable first, or use a different existence argument.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing $\Delta<0$ (strictly no intersections) with $\Delta\le 0$ (no intersections OR tangency) — the question wording must be read carefully.
  • Treating 'does not intersect' as needing $\Delta>0$ because the student has memorised 'discriminant positive = good'.
  • Reaching for the quadratic formula in a numeric case and getting tangled in $\sqrt{\text{negative}}$ instead of just checking $\Delta$.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error in $b$: when $b = -(16k-40)$, students may write $b^2 = -(16k-40)^2$. The square removes the sign, so $b^2=(16k-40)^2$.
  • Arithmetic slip in the cross term when expanding $(\alpha k + \beta)^2$, e.g. $(16k-40)^2$ — the $-1280k$ middle term is a frequent error site.
  • For $b=-1$, writing $b^2=-1$ instead of $+1$.
  • Forgetting to divide through by a common factor before solving the quadratic inequality in $k$, leading to messy boundary values.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P1/P3 content: discriminant of $ax^2+bx+c$ is $b^2-4ac$ (not in the formula booklet — must be memorised). Stay with real-coefficient quadratics; do not invoke complex roots or higher-degree discriminants, which are out of scope.
