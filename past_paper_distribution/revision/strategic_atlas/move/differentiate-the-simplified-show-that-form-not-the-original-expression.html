Differentiation

Differentiate the simplified show-that form, not the original expression

Family: Strategic Form Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

An earlier part (typically 'show that') establishes that f(x) equals a simpler equivalent form, and a later part asks for f'(x), f''(x), a stationary point, or a tangent. The original expression is a sum of fractions, a product with a removable factor, or an unsimplified algebraic combination. The simplified form is a single rational, polynomial, or standard composite that fits one differentiation rule cleanly. Cue phrases include 'Hence find f'(x)' or 'Use part (a) to...'.

Motivation — Why this works

A 'show that' result is offered precisely because it is the differentiation-friendly form: it collapses cancellations the examiner wants you to exploit. Differentiating the original would force quotient rule on quadratic-over-quadratic expressions or repeated product/quotient combinations, multiplying the chance of sign and expansion errors. The simplified form usually yields a tidy derivative (often a constant numerator), which the next part will exploit further. The word 'Hence' makes this strategy mandatory for full method marks.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that part (a) gave a clean equivalent form f(x) = 5x/(x+3) and the current part asks for f'(x).
  2. Rule out differentiating the original two-fraction expression — it would need two quotient rules plus a combine step.
  3. Commit to the simplified form because 'Hence' signals the examiner expects it, and it fits one quotient rule.
  4. Bridge: identify u = 5x, v = x+3 ready for the quotient rule.
  5. Inside the move, expand the numerator carefully and check for cancellation that simplifies the final derivative.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Quote the simplified form from the earlier 'show that' part.
  2. Identify u, v (or inner/outer) and compute u', v'.
  3. Apply the quotient rule f'(x) = (vu' − uv')/v².
  4. Expand and simplify the numerator, looking for cancellation.
  5. Leave the denominator factored as (x+3)² (do not expand).

Worked Micro-Example

Given f(x) = \frac{5x}{x+3}, let u = 5x, v = x+3, so u' = 5, v' = 1. Then f'(x) = \frac{(x+3)(5) - (5x)(1)}{(x+3)^2} = \frac{5x+15-5x}{(x+3)^2} = \frac{15}{(x+3)^2}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Logarithmic expression like ln((2x+1)/(x-1)) before differentiation
Split using log laws to ln(2x+1) − ln(x−1), then differentiate term-by-term instead of using quotient rule inside the log.
Parametric problem where 'show that' gives dy/dx in a tidy form
Use the simplified dy/dx for the next part (tangent, stationary point) rather than recomputing from x(t), y(t).
Integration follow-up where part (a) simplifies the integrand
Integrate the simplified form; the original would require partial fractions or substitution unnecessarily.
Product simplifying to a polynomial after expansion
Expand first, then differentiate term-by-term rather than using the product rule.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating part (a) as independent and re-differentiating the original expression with two quotient rules.
  • Believing 'Hence' is optional and choosing whichever form looks familiar, losing the method-mark route.
  • Trying to combine the original fractions from scratch instead of quoting the part (a) result.

Pitfalls

  • Quotient rule sign error: writing (uv' − vu')/v² instead of (vu' − uv')/v².
  • Expanding (x+3)² in the denominator unnecessarily, then failing to refactor for later parts.
  • Dropping the bracket on (x+3)(5) and writing 5x+3 instead of 5x+15.
  • Forgetting to square the denominator.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P3/P4 technique: 'show that' parts in differentiation questions are deliberately structured to feed a clean derivative in the next part.
