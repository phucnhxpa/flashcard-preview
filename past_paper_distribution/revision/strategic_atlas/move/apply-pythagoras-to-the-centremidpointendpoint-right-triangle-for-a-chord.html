Coordinate Geometry

Apply Pythagoras to the centre–midpoint–endpoint right triangle for a chord

Family: Chord Perpendicular Bisector Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A circle has a known centre and radius, and a chord of known length lies on (or is constrained by) a given line — most commonly a horizontal line y = p or vertical line x = q. You are asked to find the line's position, the chord length, or the perpendicular distance from the centre to the chord. The phrase 'chord of length L' together with a line equation is the strongest cue. Equivalent phrasings include 'the line cuts the circle at A and B with AB = L'.

Motivation — Why this works

The perpendicular from the centre of a circle to a chord bisects the chord, so the centre, the chord's midpoint, and either endpoint form a right triangle. The legs are the perpendicular distance d from the centre to the chord and half the chord length L/2; the hypotenuse is the radius r. Pythagoras then gives d² + (L/2)² = r², a single equation linking the three quantities. This is far cleaner than substituting the line into the circle equation and invoking the difference-of-roots formula on the resulting quadratic.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the chord lies on a specified line and that the circle's centre and radius are known, so a right triangle is implicit in the configuration.
  2. Rule out substituting the line into the circle equation and using sum/product or difference of roots — it works but is algebraically heavier and error-prone.
  3. Commit to the geometric route because the perpendicular-bisector theorem instantly supplies a right triangle with two known sides (radius, half-chord) and one unknown (perpendicular distance).
  4. Bridge: write d as the perpendicular distance from the centre to the line (for a horizontal line y = p with centre (h, k), simply |k − p|), and write the half-chord as L/2.
  5. Inside the move, square once, isolate the unknown, and remember to keep both ± roots since the line can sit on either side of the centre.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify centre (h, k), radius r, chord length L, and the chord's line.
  2. Compute the perpendicular distance d from (h, k) to the line (for y = p, d = |k − p|; for ax + by + c = 0, d = |ah + bk + c|/√(a² + b²)).
  3. Write Pythagoras: d² + (L/2)² = r².
  4. Substitute known values and solve for the unknown (d, L, r, or the line parameter).
  5. If solving for a line parameter, take ± when square-rooting and report both solutions.

Worked Micro-Example

Circle: $(x+3)^2 + (y-2)^2 = 27$, centre $(-3, 2)$, $r = 3\sqrt{3}$. The line $y = p$ cuts it in a chord of length $4$. Then $d = |2 - p|$, half-chord $= 2$, so $(2-p)^2 + 2^2 = 27$, giving $(2-p)^2 = 23$ and $p = 2 \pm \sqrt{23}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Vertical chord on x = q
Use d = |h − q| where (h, k) is the centre; everything else is identical.
Oblique chord on ax + by + c = 0
Compute d = |ah + bk + c|/√(a² + b²), then apply the same d² + (L/2)² = r².
Find chord length given the line and circle
Solve for L: L = 2√(r² − d²).
Find radius given chord length and distance from centre
Solve for r: r = √(d² + (L/2)²).
Tangent as limiting case
When L = 0, d = r, recovering the tangency condition.

Common Misreadings

  • Using the full chord length L instead of the half-chord L/2 in the Pythagoras relation.
  • Mistaking the chord's length for the radius, or the perpendicular distance for the radius.
  • Assuming the chord passes through the centre (i.e. that it is a diameter) and dropping the d² term.

Pitfalls

  • Expanding (k − p)² incorrectly, especially with negative coordinates.
  • Forgetting the ± when square-rooting and losing one of the two valid line positions.
  • Forgetting absolute value or to square the perpendicular distance from a general line (omitting the √(a² + b²) denominator).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics: circle equation in the form (x − a)² + (y − b)² = r², the perpendicular-from-centre-bisects-chord property, and Pythagoras' theorem.
