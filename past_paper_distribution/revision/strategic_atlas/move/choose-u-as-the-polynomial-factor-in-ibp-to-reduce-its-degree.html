Integration

Choose u as the polynomial factor in IBP to reduce its degree

Family: Integration By Parts Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integrand is a product of a polynomial factor (x, x², or xⁿ) and a function that integrates cleanly to itself or to its own family — typically an exponential e^(kx), a sine/cosine sin(kx)/cos(kx), or similar. No straightforward substitution simplifies the product, and no standard antiderivative covers it directly. Visual cue: 'algebraic × exponential' or 'algebraic × trig' sitting side-by-side under one ∫ sign. The polynomial has finite degree n, so n applications of integration by parts will eliminate it entirely.

Motivation — Why this works

Integration by parts, ∫u dv = uv − ∫v du, transfers a derivative from one factor to the other — it is essentially the product rule reversed. By assigning u to the polynomial, du lowers the polynomial's degree by one each round, while dv is chosen as the factor whose antiderivative v stays in the same family (e^(kx) → (1/k)e^(kx); cos(kx) → (1/k)sin(kx)). After enough applications the polynomial vanishes and only an elementary integral of pure exponential or trigonometric type remains. The reverse assignment would integrate the polynomial to a higher degree (xⁿ → xⁿ⁺¹/(n+1)) — strictly making the next integral harder.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand is a product of two functions from different families — a polynomial times an exponential or trig function — so no substitution and no standard antiderivative apply directly.
  2. Rule out u-substitution: there is no inner function whose derivative appears as a factor; only IBP is the natural tool for products of unrelated families.
  3. Apply the LIATE heuristic (Logarithmic > Inverse trig > Algebraic > Trig > Exponential) — Algebraic outranks Trig and Exponential, so the polynomial becomes u.
  4. Check by simulation: differentiating xⁿ gives nxⁿ⁻¹ (simpler), while integrating e^(kx) or cos(kx) keeps the same family (no complication). The reverse assignment would raise the polynomial degree — clearly worse.
  5. Anticipate the number of rounds: degree-n polynomial requires n applications of IBP to fully eliminate; for n ≥ 2, plan to chain IBP (or use the tabular method).
  6. Commit: set u = polynomial, dv = exponential/trig dx; compute du and v carefully (watch the 1/k factor when integrating e^(kx) or cos(kx)).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two factors and label u = polynomial (xⁿ), dv = remaining factor dx (e.g. e^(kx) dx or cos(kx) dx).
  2. Differentiate to get du = nxⁿ⁻¹ dx; integrate to get v (e.g. v = (1/k)e^(kx) or v = (1/k)sin(kx)).
  3. Substitute into ∫u dv = uv − ∫v du, producing a new integral with polynomial degree reduced by one.
  4. If the residual integral still contains a polynomial factor, repeat IBP with u = new (lower-degree) polynomial.
  5. Continue until the polynomial is eliminated; evaluate the final pure exponential/trig integral and add +C (or apply limits for a definite integral).

Worked Micro-Example

Compute $\int x e^{x/2}\,dx$. Let $u=x$, $dv=e^{x/2}dx$, so $du=dx$, $v=2e^{x/2}$. Then $\int x e^{x/2}\,dx = 2x e^{x/2} - \int 2e^{x/2}\,dx = 2x e^{x/2} - 4e^{x/2} + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Polynomial × trigonometric, e.g. ∫x cos(2x) dx
Same choice u = x; now dv = cos(2x) dx gives v = (1/2)sin(2x). The 1/k factor comes from integrating the trig function rather than the exponential.
Higher-degree polynomial, e.g. ∫x² e^(2x) dx
Apply IBP twice (or use the tabular/DI method): first round reduces x² to 2x, second round reduces 2x to a constant, leaving a pure exponential.
Polynomial × logarithm, e.g. ∫x ln x dx
Roles reverse: LIATE puts Logarithmic above Algebraic, so u = ln x (because differentiating gives 1/x, which simplifies the product) and dv = x dx.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying a substitution like t = kx first — this only rescales the variable and leaves the polynomial × exponential/trig structure intact.
  • Choosing u = e^(kx) because 'it differentiates easily' — but the goal is to simplify the integral, not just to differentiate; this assignment makes the next integral harder.
  • Abandoning the method after one round when a product still remains — for degree-n polynomials, n rounds are required.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the 1/k factor when integrating e^(kx) or cos(kx): v = (1/k)e^(kx), not e^(kx).
  • Sign errors in the formula uv − ∫v du, especially when the minus sign compounds across multiple IBP applications.
  • When chaining IBP, accidentally reusing the original u (e.g. x²) instead of the new lower-degree polynomial (x) on the second round.
  • Mishandling constants (e.g. the factor 2 from differentiating x²) when they meet the subtraction in the IBP formula.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P3 integration by parts) and Further Pure. The IBP formula ∫u (dv/dx) dx = uv − ∫v (du/dx) dx is given in the IAL formula booklet. Avoid mentioning the tabular/DI method by name as it is not standard in IAL — for repeated IBP, students should chain the formula explicitly.
