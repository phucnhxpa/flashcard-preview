Coordinate Geometry

Evaluate using factored form rather than expanded form

Family: Strategic Evaluation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a curve, polynomial, or expression is available in two equivalent forms — typically a factored form like $y=(x-a)(x-b)(x-c)$ and an expanded polynomial — and you need a specific numerical value at a given $x$. Cues include 'find the y-coordinate when x = ...', 'evaluate f(k)', or substituting into a previously derived equation. Especially relevant when the chosen $x$-value makes individual factors small integers.

Motivation — Why this works

Substitution into a factored form reduces evaluation to a small product of simple integers, while substitution into the expanded form forces computation of large powers and a long alternating sum. Both forms give the same value (they are algebraically identical), so the choice is purely about arithmetic efficiency and error reduction. Picking the form whose factors are smallest at the target $x$ is a deliberate, low-cost optimisation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that the curve/expression is available in more than one equivalent form (factored and expanded).
  2. Rules out blindly using whichever form was last written; checks which form yields the smaller arithmetic at the target $x$.
  3. Commits to the factored form because each factor evaluates to a single-digit or small integer.
  4. Bridges to action by substituting $x$ into each bracket separately rather than expanding first.
  5. Inside the move: evaluates each factor, then multiplies; cross-checks sign of each factor before multiplying.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify all equivalent forms of the expression available (factored, expanded, completed-square, etc.).
  2. Mentally evaluate each factor at the target $x$ to gauge arithmetic load.
  3. Choose the form whose components are smallest/cleanest at that $x$.
  4. Substitute and evaluate each factor separately.
  5. Multiply the factor values together (mind signs and powers).
  6. Record the resulting coordinate or value.

Worked Micro-Example

Find $y$ on $y=(x-2)(x-4)^2$ at $x=6$. Factored: $y=(6-2)(6-4)^2 = 4 \times 2^2 = 4 \times 4 = 16$. Expanded form $y=x^3-10x^2+32x-32$ gives $216-360+192-32=16$ — same answer, but with much heavier arithmetic. Point: $(6,16)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Evaluating a remainder via the factor/remainder theorem on a polynomial given in partly-factored form
Substitute the root-candidate into whichever form (factored portion times linear, or expanded) keeps numbers small.
Finding a turning point's $y$-value after solving $\frac{dy}{dx}=0$ on a cubic given in factored form
Plug the stationary $x$ back into the original factored $y$, not the expanded version used for differentiation.
Computing a definite-integral boundary value where the integrand or antiderivative factorises
Use the factored antiderivative at the limits when the limits make factors small integers.
Evaluating a completed-square quadratic $y=a(x-h)^2+k$ at a specific $x$
Prefer the completed-square form when $x$ is close to $h$, since $(x-h)$ is then small.

Common Misreadings

  • Thinking the expanded form is 'more correct' or 'more final' and so must be used for substitution.
  • Believing the two forms might give different answers and so distrusting the cleaner factored evaluation.
  • Discarding the factored form after expanding it earlier in the question.

Pitfalls

  • Mishandling the squared factor, e.g. computing $(6-4)^2$ as $2 \cdot 2 = 4$ correctly but then writing $-4$ by carrying a sign error.
  • Forgetting that a factor like $(x-4)^2$ is always non-negative, leading to spurious sign flips.
  • Multiplying factor values in the wrong grouping when there are three or more factors and losing track of signs.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1–P4) — applies to polynomials, factor theorem work, and curve sketching. No content beyond standard substitution into factored polynomial forms.
