Trigger — When to use

The expression contains BOTH tan²θ (or tan² inside an integrand) AND sec θ / sec^k θ, with no obvious cancellation between them. Visual cues: a quadratic-style equation like a·tan²θ + b·sec θ + c = 0, or — in calculus — a leftover integral of the form ∫sec^(n-2)x · tan²x dx after integration by parts on ∫sec^n x dx. Equivalent phrasings: 'mixed tan² and sec', 'I need everything in one trig function', 'I have a Pythagorean pair'.

Motivation — Why this works

The Pythagorean identity 1 + tan²θ ≡ sec²θ, rearranged as tan²θ = sec²θ − 1, is the unique tool that converts tan² into a polynomial in sec without introducing fractions. In equation-solving, this collapses two trig functions into one variable u = sec θ, producing a solvable quadratic. In integration, it splits ∫sec^(n-2)x·tan²x dx cleanly into I_n − I_{n-2}, which is exactly the recursive structure needed for a reduction formula. Converting to sin/cos instead would create rational expressions and increase algebraic complexity.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the expression mixes tan² with sec — two functions, one of them squared. Quadratic-in-one-variable solving (or matching to I_k form) requires a single trig function.
  2. Recall the three Pythagorean identities and pick the one that links tan and sec: 1 + tan²θ = sec²θ. The cot/csc and sin/cos versions don't match the surface.
  3. Rule out converting everything to sin/cos: this produces a rational equation with cos²θ in the denominator and adds steps. Rule out double-angle identities: they don't simplify into sec-powers.
  4. Commit to the substitution tan²θ = sec²θ − 1 because it preserves polynomial structure, keeps the highest power at 2 (in equations) or generates exactly I_n and I_{n-2} (in integration).
  5. Substitute, then expand carefully — distribute any leading coefficient across BOTH the sec²θ term and the −1 to avoid losing the constant.
  6. After substitution, set u = sec θ (for equations) and solve the quadratic, OR identify the I_n / I_{n-2} pieces and rearrange for the reduction formula.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Locate every tan²θ in the expression.
  2. Replace tan²θ with (sec²θ − 1) literally, keeping brackets.
  3. Distribute any coefficient: a(sec²θ − 1) = a·sec²θ − a.
  4. Collect like terms in sec θ to form a quadratic in sec θ — or, in integration, recognise ∫sec^n x dx − ∫sec^(n-2)x dx as I_n − I_{n-2}.
  5. Solve the quadratic (let u = sec θ) for sec θ, then recover θ; or rearrange the integral equation to isolate I_n on the LHS.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $3\tan^2\theta + 7\sec\theta - 3 = 0$. Replace $\tan^2\theta = \sec^2\theta - 1$: $3(\sec^2\theta - 1) + 7\sec\theta - 3 = 0 \Rightarrow 3\sec^2\theta + 7\sec\theta - 6 = 0$. Factorise: $(3\sec\theta - 2)(\sec\theta + 3) = 0$, giving $\sec\theta = -3$ (since $|\sec\theta|\ge 1$ rules out $2/3$), so $\cos\theta = -1/3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation mixes cot²θ and csc θ
Use cot²θ ≡ csc²θ − 1 — same Pythagorean family, complementary pair.
Equation mixes sin²θ and cos θ (or vice versa)
Use sin²θ = 1 − cos²θ to unify in terms of cos θ; the identity is the same Pythagorean tool, oriented to eliminate the squared function.
Reduction formula for ∫sec^n x dx
After IBP, the leftover ∫sec^(n-2)x·tan²x dx becomes ∫sec^n x dx − ∫sec^(n-2)x dx = I_n − I_{n-2}; move I_n to the LHS to extract the reduction formula.
∫tan^n x dx reduction
Factor tan^(n-2)x · tan²x and replace tan²x = sec²x − 1 directly — no IBP needed.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to convert tan and sec separately into sin/cos, producing a rational equation that's harder to manage.
  • Reaching for a double-angle identity (e.g. tan²θ = (1 − cos2θ)/(1 + cos2θ)) which doesn't unify with sec.
  • Misremembering the identity as tan²θ = sec²θ (dropping the −1) and losing the constant term.

Pitfalls

  • Distribution error: writing 3(sec²θ − 1) = 3sec²θ − 1 instead of 3sec²θ − 3.
  • Sign error when rearranging in integration: −(n−2)(I_n − I_{n-2}) = −(n−2)I_n + (n−2)I_{n-2}, NOT −(n−2)I_n − (n−2)I_{n-2}.
  • Forgetting the constraint |sec θ| ≥ 1 when solving the resulting quadratic, leading to spurious roots.
  • Losing solutions by dividing through by sec θ instead of factorising.
