Sequences & Series

Divide two GP term equations to eliminate a and isolate r

Family: Ratio Elimination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Two equations of the form $ar^{n-1}$ are known for distinct positions in a geometric progression (e.g. the 2nd and 5th terms), and the task is to find the common ratio $r$ (and possibly $a$ afterwards). The shared unknown $a$ appears as a multiplicative factor in both. Phrasings include 'the second term is..., the fifth term is...' or 'given $u_p$ and $u_q$ of a GP, find $r$'. Whenever you see two GP terms specified numerically, this move should be the default reflex.

Motivation — Why this works

Every term of a GP has the multiplicative form $ar^{n-1}$, so the ratio of any two terms is purely a power of $r$ — the factor $a$ cancels exactly. This reduces a two-unknown system to a single equation $r^{q-p} = u_q/u_p$, which is solved by a single root extraction. Substitution would also work but typically forces a fraction $a = u_p/r^{p-1}$ into the second equation, producing avoidable algebra. Division exploits the geometric (multiplicative) structure directly, mirroring how the GP itself is defined.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice both given equations share the factor $a$ and differ only in the power of $r$ — this is the GP fingerprint.
  2. I rule out subtracting the equations (that's an AP/linear instinct) and rule out substitution because it would introduce a fraction $a = 12/r$.
  3. I commit to dividing the higher-index equation by the lower one because $a$ cancels in one stroke, leaving a clean power of $r$.
  4. Concretely: $\frac{ar^4}{ar} = \frac{324}{12}$ gives $r^3 = 27$.
  5. I check the exponent difference (here 3, odd) so only one real root applies; if it had been even I would pause to consider $\pm$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Label the two given terms with their GP formula, e.g. $u_2 = ar = 12$ and $u_5 = ar^4 = 324$.
  2. Form the ratio (higher index)/(lower index): $\frac{ar^4}{ar} = \frac{324}{12}$.
  3. Simplify the left side using index laws to $r^{q-p}$ (here $r^3$).
  4. Simplify the right side numerically (here $27$).
  5. Take the appropriate root of both sides: $r = \sqrt[3]{27} = 3$ (consider $\pm$ if the index is even).
  6. Back-substitute $r$ into either original equation to find $a$ if required.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $u_2 = ar = 12$ and $u_5 = ar^4 = 324$ in a GP. Dividing: $\dfrac{ar^4}{ar} = \dfrac{324}{12}$, so $r^3 = 27$ and $r = 3$. Then $a = 12/r = 4$, giving the GP $4, 12, 36, 108, 324, \dots$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

GP with 3rd and 7th terms given
Dividing yields $r^4 = u_7/u_3$; since the exponent is even, include $r = \pm\sqrt[4]{\cdot}$ unless the context (e.g. all positive terms) rules out the negative root.
GP given by sum conditions like $u_1 + u_2 = S_1$ and $u_3 + u_4 = S_2$
Factor each side first: $a(1+r)$ and $ar^2(1+r)$; dividing cancels both $a$ and $(1+r)$, leaving $r^2$.
GP defined recursively with $u_{n+1}/u_n = r$ given two non-adjacent values
Same division principle — ratio of any two terms is $r$ raised to the index gap.
Finding $r$ in a GP from $S_n$ formulae where $a$ is shared
Divide two $S_n$ expressions to cancel $a$, leaving an equation in $r$ alone (often non-trivial polynomial).

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the two GP equations like simultaneous linear equations and subtracting them, which does not eliminate $a$.
  • Mislabelling the term: writing $u_5 = ar^5$ instead of $ar^4$ (off-by-one in the exponent $n-1$).
  • Assuming $r$ must be positive and silently dropping a valid negative root when the exponent difference is even.

Pitfalls

  • When the exponent difference is even (e.g. $r^4 = 16$), forgetting $r = -2$ if the problem allows it.
  • Arithmetic slip in reducing the numerical ratio (e.g. $324/12 = 27$ — verify by factoring).
  • Forgetting to back-substitute to find $a$ when the question also asks for $u_1$ or $S_n$.
  • Dividing the lower term by the higher term, producing $r^{-3}$ and an awkward reciprocal.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P1/P2 geometric series technique; relies only on the explicit formula $u_n = ar^{n-1}$ and basic index laws, fully within A-level scope.
