Trigger — When to use

The function under transformation has its argument modified, e.g. f(x + a), f(x − a), f(kx), or f(−kx). Whenever the constant or multiplier is INSIDE the bracket (acting on x before f is applied), the transformation is HORIZONTAL and behaves counter-intuitively: opposite sign for translations, reciprocal factor for stretches. Visual cues: a sketch problem asking you to draw y = f(x + 3), y = f(−3x), y = f(2x), etc., usually with named points (intercepts, touches, cusps) on the original graph. Equivalent phrasings: 'sketch y = f(...)', 'state the coordinates of the image of P under...', 'describe the transformation taking f(x) to f(...)'.

Motivation — Why this works

An inside-bracket modification changes WHICH x-value produces a given output, not the output itself, so the graph deforms horizontally while y-values are preserved. If f(c) = d, then f(x + a) = d when x = c − a, hence the LEFT shift for +a; similarly f(kx) = d when x = c/k, producing a compression by factor 1/|k| (and a y-axis reflection if k < 0). The 'opposite/reciprocal' rule is just the algebra of solving for the new x. Decomposing compound arguments like −3x into a reflection plus a stretch keeps the point-mapping systematic and avoids sign or scale-factor errors.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the constant/multiplier sits INSIDE the f(...) bracket, acting on x — so this is a horizontal transformation, not vertical.
  2. I rule out outside transformations like f(x) + a or a·f(x), which would change y-coordinates; here the y-values of all named points must stay fixed.
  3. I commit to the inside-bracket rule: translations go in the OPPOSITE direction to the sign, and stretches use the RECIPROCAL of the multiplier; a negative multiplier additionally reflects in the y-axis.
  4. I write the argument in the form k(x − h) if needed, then read off the mapping x ↦ (x/k) + h, equivalently (x, y) ↦ ((x − h_old shift)/k_correction, y). For simple cases: f(x + a) gives (x, y) ↦ (x − a, y); f(kx) gives (x, y) ↦ (x/k, y); f(−kx) gives (x, y) ↦ (−x/k, y).
  5. I apply this mapping to every named point (intercepts, turning points, touches/cusps) and crucially preserve the NATURE of each feature — a touch stays a touch, a crossing stays a crossing, asymptotes transform too.
  6. Sanity check: any point on the y-axis is fixed by a pure horizontal stretch/reflection (good invariance test); any point originally at x = −a maps to the y-axis under f(x + a).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Read the argument of f and identify whether you have a translation (x ± a), a stretch (kx), or a compound (−kx, k(x − h), etc.).
  2. Write the point-mapping rule: f(x + a) → (x, y) ↦ (x − a, y); f(x − a) → (x, y) ↦ (x + a, y); f(kx) → (x, y) ↦ (x/k, y); f(−kx) → (x, y) ↦ (−x/k, y).
  3. Apply the mapping to each named point on the original curve, keeping y-coordinates unchanged.
  4. Transfer the geometric NATURE of each feature with the point (touch ↔ touch, cross ↔ cross, max ↔ max).
  5. Transform any asymptotes using the same rule (vertical asymptote x = c moves to the new x-coordinate; horizontal asymptotes are unaffected by inside-bracket changes).
  6. Sketch the new curve through the transformed points, preserving overall shape, and label the new coordinates.

Worked Micro-Example

Original curve passes through $(-1,0)$, $(0,2)$, and touches the x-axis at $(3,0)$. For $y = f(x+3)$: mapping $(x,y) \mapsto (x-3, y)$ gives $(-4,0)$, $(-3,2)$, and a touch at $(0,0)$. For $y = f(-3x)$: mapping $(x,y) \mapsto (-x/3, y)$ gives $(1/3,0)$, $(0,2)$ (invariant, on the mirror line), and a touch at $(-1,0)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Pure horizontal translation f(x ± a)
Shift LEFT by a for +a, RIGHT by a for −a; mapping (x, y) ↦ (x ∓ a, y).
Pure horizontal stretch f(kx) with k > 0
Stretch by reciprocal scale factor 1/k (compression if k > 1, stretch if 0 < k < 1); mapping (x, y) ↦ (x/k, y).
Pure y-axis reflection f(−x)
Reflect in the y-axis; mapping (x, y) ↦ (−x, y). Special case k = −1.
Compound inside transformation f(−kx)
Decompose into reflection (sign) and stretch (reciprocal of |k|); combined mapping (x, y) ↦ (−x/k, y).
Mixed form f(k(x − h)) or f(kx + b)
Factorise the argument first as k(x − h) with h = −b/k; apply translation by h then horizontal stretch by 1/k.
Outside transformations like f(x) + a or −f(x)
These are VERTICAL (change y-coordinates) — do NOT confuse with inside transformations. Mapping (x, y) ↦ (x, y + a) or (x, −y).

Common Misreadings

  • Shifting RIGHT for f(x + a) by following the '+' sign literally instead of applying the opposite-direction rule.
  • Treating f(x + a) as a vertical shift (confusing inside vs outside operations).
  • Multiplying x-coordinates by k instead of 1/k for f(kx) — forgetting the reciprocal rule.
  • Reading the negative in f(−x) as an x-axis reflection (which is −f(x)) rather than a y-axis reflection.
  • Applying an inside multiplier to y-coordinates instead of x-coordinates.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to preserve the NATURE of features: a touch at (3, 0) must become a touch at the image point, not a crossing.
  • Forgetting to transform asymptotes alongside the named points.
  • On compound arguments like f(−3x), applying only the stretch or only the reflection and forgetting the other.
  • Mis-factorising forms like f(2x − 4): must rewrite as f(2(x − 2)), giving a translation right by 2 then stretch by 1/2 — not a translation by 4.
  • Altering y-coordinates for an inside transformation — y-values must stay fixed.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P2/P3) graph transformations. No formula booklet entry is required — these are standard transformation rules. Avoid out-of-scope generalisations such as transformations of complex-valued functions or matrix representations of transformations beyond what FP1 covers.
