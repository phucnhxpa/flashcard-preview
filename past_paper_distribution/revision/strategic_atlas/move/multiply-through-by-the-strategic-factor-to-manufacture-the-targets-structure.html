Trigonometry

Multiply through by the strategic factor to manufacture the target's structure

Family: Strategic Clearing Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

After converting tan/sec/cosec into sin/cos, you arrive at an equation containing a fraction (e.g. sinα/cosα = ... or a·cosθ = b/sinθ) AND the 'show that' target contains a specific product or power (sin²α, sinθcosθ, sin2θ). The visual cue is a mismatch: the working equation has sin and cos separated by a division, while the target has them multiplied or squared together. Equivalently: you can see an obvious 'easy cancellation' (e.g. dividing by sinα) that would simplify the equation but destroy a factor the target explicitly requires. Whenever the target dictates a specific surface form, the clearing factor must be chosen to BUILD that form, not collapse it.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying both sides by a chosen trig factor (cosα, sinθ, etc.) clears denominators while simultaneously creating the exact product or power needed in the target identity. This works because multiplication preserves equivalence (provided the factor is non-zero on the given interval) and converts a quotient sinα/cosα into the product sinα·cosα or similar — directly setting up double-angle identities (sin2θ = 2sinθcosθ) or Pythagorean rearrangements (sin²α = 1 − cos²α). The deeper principle: in 'show that' problems, the form of the answer dictates the algebraic operation, not raw simplicity. Cancelling a common factor is tempting but throws away the very structure being asked for.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the working equation has sinα (or sinθ) appearing on both sides, or a fraction like sinα/cosα — there's an obvious cancellation/division available.
  2. Before cancelling, I check the target: it contains sin²α (or the product sinθcosθ for sin2θ). If I divide by sinα, that factor disappears from the equation and I can never recover the target form.
  3. I rule out dividing by sinα — it gives a correct but useless equation (e.g. 2 = 3cosα) that doesn't match the requested 'show that' identity.
  4. I rule out squaring — that introduces sin² and cos² terms but multiplies the complexity unnecessarily and risks extraneous solutions.
  5. I commit to multiplying by cosα (or sinθ): this clears the fraction AND keeps every sin factor intact, producing terms like sinα·cosα or sin²α that align with the target.
  6. Tactical choice of factor: pick the denominator's complement — if the fraction is sinα/cosα, multiply by cosα; if the equation is a·cosθ = b/sinθ, multiply by sinθ to manufacture sinθcosθ on the left.
  7. Finally, verify the chosen factor is non-zero on the stated interval (so the multiplication is reversible).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the denominator/divisor in the working equation (e.g. cosα in sinα/cosα = ...).
  2. Identify the target's signature sub-expression (e.g. sin²α, sinθcosθ, sin2θ).
  3. Choose the clearing factor that simultaneously kills the denominator and builds the target sub-expression.
  4. Multiply BOTH sides by that factor: e.g. (sinα/cosα = (3/2)sinα) × cosα ⟹ sinα = (3/2)sinα·cosα.
  5. Rearrange algebraically — collect terms, factor, or apply an identity (e.g. sin2θ = 2sinθcosθ, sin²α = 1 − cos²α) to reach the stated form.
  6. Confirm cosα ≠ 0 (or sinθ ≠ 0) on the given interval so the step is valid.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\tan\alpha = \tfrac{3}{2}\sin\alpha$, write as $\tfrac{\sin\alpha}{\cos\alpha} = \tfrac{3}{2}\sin\alpha$. Multiplying by $2\cos\alpha$ (rather than dividing by $\sin\alpha$) gives $2\sin\alpha = 3\sin\alpha\cos\alpha$. Since the target involves $\sin^2\alpha$, multiply through by $\sin\alpha$ form: rearranging $2\cos\alpha = 3\sin^2\alpha/\sin\alpha \cdot \sin\alpha$ — or equivalently, from $8\cos\theta = 3/\sin\theta$, multiply by $\sin\theta$ to get $8\sin\theta\cos\theta = 3$, i.e. $4\sin 2\theta = 3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation a·sinθ = b/cosθ with target involving sin2θ
Multiply by cosθ to create sinθcosθ on the left, then apply sin2θ = 2sinθcosθ.
Equation a·tanθ = b·secθ
Multiply by cosθ to convert to a·sinθ = b — the product route is bypassed because the target is linear in sinθ.
Target contains sin²α via Pythagoras
After multiplying to create sinα·cosα, substitute sin²α = 1 − cos²α (or vice versa) to match the target's variable.

Common Misreadings

  • Cancelling sinα from both sides because it 'simplifies' — yields a correct but unusable equation that doesn't match the 'show that' target.
  • Squaring both sides to eliminate the fraction — introduces sin²θcos²θ, overcomplicates, and risks extraneous roots.
  • Dividing by cosα to get tanα — moves AWAY from the target structure when the target involves sin² or a product.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to verify the multiplying factor (cosα or sinθ) is non-zero on the given interval — required to justify the operation.
  • Multiplying by the wrong factor: if the target is just 'find cosα', dividing by sinα is actually correct — context determines the strategy.
  • Stopping too early after multiplication without rearranging into the exact target form (e.g. leaving 2sinα = 3sinα·cosα instead of dividing/rearranging to 2cosα = 3sin²α).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 trig identity work. Uses the standard identities sin²θ + cos²θ = 1, tanθ = sinθ/cosθ, and the double-angle formula sin2θ = 2sinθcosθ (formula booklet). Avoid invoking sum-to-product or Weierstrass substitutions — these are out of scope.
