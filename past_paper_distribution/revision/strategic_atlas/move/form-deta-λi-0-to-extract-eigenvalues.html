Matrices

Form det(A − λI) = 0 to extract eigenvalues

Family: Characteristic Polynomial Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question explicitly asks for eigenvalues of a square matrix, or asks for any downstream object that requires eigenvalues first — diagonalisation A = PDP⁻¹, computing high powers Aⁿ, or finding eigenvectors. Visual cues: a 2×2 or 3×3 matrix is given with the words 'eigenvalues', 'characteristic equation', 'diagonalise', or 'P and D such that P⁻¹AP = D'. Alternative phrasings include 'find the values of λ for which Ax = λx has non-trivial solutions' or 'show that λ = k is an eigenvalue of A'. If you see any of these, the very first action is to form (A − λI) and set its determinant to zero.

Motivation — Why this works

An eigenvalue λ is, by definition, a scalar for which Ax = λx admits a non-zero vector x. Rearranging gives (A − λI)x = 0, and a homogeneous system has a non-trivial solution exactly when its coefficient matrix is singular, i.e. det(A − λI) = 0. This converts an abstract matrix question into a polynomial equation in λ — a quadratic for 2×2, a cubic for 3×3 — which can be tackled by standard algebraic techniques (factor theorem, polynomial division, quadratic formula). It is the universal entry point to every eigenvalue calculation in the syllabus.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question mentions eigenvalues, diagonalisation, or matrix powers — all of which depend on knowing λ first.
  2. Recall the definition Ax = λx and rearrange it to (A − λI)x = 0; rule out trying to 'guess' eigenvectors directly or solving Ax = λx component-wise (these are slower and error-prone for 3×3).
  3. Commit to the singularity criterion: non-trivial x exists iff det(A − λI) = 0. This is the only systematic route.
  4. Bridge to operation: write A − λI by subtracting λ from each diagonal entry of A (every diagonal entry — do not miss one).
  5. Tactical choice for 3×3: expand the determinant along the row or column with the most zeros to minimise cofactor work; for 2×2 use the shortcut λ² − tr(A)λ + det(A).
  6. After obtaining the polynomial, plan the factorisation: for cubics, test small integers ±1, ±2, ±3 via the factor theorem, then divide out the linear factor and solve the residual quadratic.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Form A − λI by subtracting λ from every diagonal entry of A, leaving off-diagonal entries unchanged.
  2. Compute det(A − λI) by cofactor expansion along the row/column with the most zeros (or by the 2×2 trace–determinant formula λ² − tr(A)λ + det(A)).
  3. Set det(A − λI) = 0; this is the characteristic equation.
  4. Solve: for a cubic, find an integer root by inspection (factor theorem on ±1, ±2, …), perform polynomial division to extract a quadratic factor, then solve the quadratic to obtain λ₁, λ₂, λ₃.
  5. List the eigenvalues, noting any repeated roots (algebraic multiplicity).

Worked Micro-Example

Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$. Then $A - \lambda I = \begin{pmatrix} 2-\lambda & 1 \\ 1 & 2-\lambda \end{pmatrix}$, so $\det(A - \lambda I) = (2-\lambda)^2 - 1 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = 0$. Factorising: $(\lambda - 1)(\lambda - 3) = 0$, giving eigenvalues $\lambda = 1, 3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

2×2 matrix
Use the shortcut det(A − λI) = λ² − tr(A)λ + det(A); no cofactor expansion needed.
Triangular matrix (upper or lower) or diagonal matrix
Eigenvalues are simply the diagonal entries — no expansion required, since det(A − λI) is the product of (aᵢᵢ − λ).
Question states 'show that λ = k is an eigenvalue'
Substitute λ = k into det(A − λI) and verify it equals zero, rather than solving from scratch.
Cubic with no obvious integer root
Look for rational roots given by leading-coefficient/constant-term factors, or check whether the question gives one eigenvalue as a hint to factor out.

Common Misreadings

  • Setting up det(A + λI) = 0 or det(λA − I) = 0 instead of det(A − λI) = 0.
  • Computing det(A) (a number) instead of det(A − λI) (a polynomial in λ).
  • Confusing the characteristic equation with Cayley–Hamilton: the latter says A satisfies its own characteristic polynomial, not that one substitutes the matrix back into the scalar equation at this stage.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when subtracting λ from diagonal entries — particularly when the diagonal entry is already negative (e.g. −3 − λ, not −3 + λ).
  • Forgetting to subtract λ from every diagonal entry (a frequent slip in 3×3 matrices, often missing the (3,3) position).
  • Sign errors in the alternating +/−/+ cofactor pattern during 3×3 expansion.
  • Arithmetic slips inside the 2×2 sub-determinants of a 3×3 cofactor expansion.
  • Missing a repeated root when factorising — always check whether the quadratic factor has a double root or shares a root with the linear factor.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP1/FP3) scope for 2×2 and 3×3 real matrices. The characteristic equation is not in the formula booklet — students must derive det(A − λI) = 0 from first principles. Avoid generalisations to complex eigenvalues with non-real entries beyond what the question presents, and do not invoke Cayley–Hamilton unless explicitly asked.
