Coordinate Geometry

Locate circle centre as midpoint of given diameter

Family: Diameter Midpoint Property Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The problem explicitly labels a chord as a diameter (e.g. 'QR is a diameter of circle C') and provides the coordinates of its two endpoints. Equivalent phrasings include 'PQ is a diameter', 'AB passes through the centre', or being told that a right angle subtended at a third point on the circle implies the opposite side is a diameter (converse of angle-in-semicircle). Once a diameter is identified, the centre is automatically the midpoint of its endpoints — no further machinery is needed.

Motivation — Why this works

By definition a diameter is a chord passing through the centre, and the centre is equidistant from the two endpoints lying on the circle. Hence the centre is exactly the midpoint of the diameter segment. This bypasses the three-equation system one would otherwise solve for $(a,b,r^2)$ in $(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2=r^2$, and avoids constructing perpendicular bisectors of chords.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the keyword 'diameter' attached to a named segment whose endpoints are given — this is the dominant structural cue.
  2. Rule out the heavier route of substituting three points into the general circle equation, or intersecting two perpendicular bisectors of chords, since both are unnecessary here.
  3. Commit to the midpoint route because the diameter property collapses the centre to a single midpoint computation.
  4. Bridge: write down the two endpoint coordinates and prepare the midpoint formula $\left(\tfrac{x_1+x_2}{2},\tfrac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)$.
  5. Tactically, after obtaining the centre, get $r^2$ as the squared distance from the centre to either endpoint (or as $\tfrac14|QR|^2$), then assemble the circle equation.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two endpoints of the stated diameter, e.g. $Q=(x_1,y_1)$ and $R=(x_2,y_2)$.
  2. Compute the centre $M=\left(\tfrac{x_1+x_2}{2},\tfrac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)$.
  3. Compute $r^2=(x_1-M_x)^2+(y_1-M_y)^2$ using one endpoint (or $r=\tfrac12|QR|$).
  4. Write the circle equation $(x-M_x)^2+(y-M_y)^2=r^2$.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $Q=(-2,10)$ and $R=(8,-14)$ are endpoints of a diameter of circle $C_2$. Centre $M=\left(\tfrac{-2+8}{2},\tfrac{10+(-14)}{2}\right)=(3,-2)$. Then $r^2=(-2-3)^2+(10-(-2))^2=25+144=169$. So $C_2:(x-3)^2+(y+2)^2=169$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Three points on the circle are given but no diameter is named
Use perpendicular bisectors of two chords (or solve three simultaneous equations from the general circle form) to find the centre.
A point $P$ on the circle subtends a right angle at the chord $QR$
By the converse of the angle-in-semicircle theorem, $QR$ is a diameter; then apply the midpoint move.
The diameter is given by an equation of a line plus one endpoint on the circle
Find where the line meets the circle to get the second endpoint, then take the midpoint.
Centre is given and one endpoint of a diameter is required
Reflect the known endpoint through the centre: the other endpoint is $2M-Q$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the named segment as an arbitrary chord and unnecessarily setting up perpendicular bisectors.
  • Confusing 'diameter' with 'radius' and halving coordinates instead of averaging them.
  • Assuming any long chord through two given points is a diameter without the question stating it.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors in the midpoint formula when coordinates are negative.
  • Forgetting to halve after summing, giving twice the centre's coordinates.
  • Using $r=|QR|$ instead of $r=\tfrac12|QR|$ when computing the radius from the diameter length.
  • Mixing up which coordinate pairs go together (pairing $x_1$ with $y_2$).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P2 coordinate geometry of the circle): uses only the midpoint formula, distance formula, and standard circle equation $(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2=r^2$.
