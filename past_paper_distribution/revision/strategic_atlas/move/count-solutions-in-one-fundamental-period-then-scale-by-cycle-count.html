Trigonometry

Count solutions in one fundamental period then scale by cycle count

Family: Periodic Scaling Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The interval given (e.g. 0 ≤ x ≤ 20π, or 0 ≤ θ ≤ 720°) is many times larger than the natural period of the trig equation. The equation involves one or more periodic functions whose combined period can be identified (LCM of individual periods). The question asks for the *number* of solutions rather than their explicit values. Phrasings like 'how many solutions', 'state the number of roots', or 'find n such that...' over a wide interval are strong cues.

Motivation — Why this works

A trig equation built from functions with a common fundamental period T has a solution set that is invariant under translation by T. So the count of solutions in [0, kT) is exactly k times the count in [0, T). This converts a potentially huge enumeration into a small base count plus a multiplication, exploiting periodicity directly rather than solving the general solution and filtering.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the interval length is a clean multiple of a recognisable period (e.g. 20π = 10 × 2π), signalling a periodicity argument.
  2. Rules out brute-force listing of all solutions across the full interval and rules out using the smaller period (e.g. π) before checking that *both* sides repeat over it.
  3. Commits to finding the LCM of the periods of all trig terms as the true fundamental period of the equation.
  4. Reduces the equation on one period to a simple form (e.g. via a double-angle identity) and solves it inside [0, T) to get the base count n.
  5. Multiplies n by the number of complete periods k = (interval length)/T, then audits the endpoints to ensure no boundary solution is missed or double-counted.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the period of each trig term and take the LCM to get the fundamental period T.
  2. Reduce the equation to a single-variable form on [0, T) using identities (double-angle, Pythagorean, etc.).
  3. Solve on [0, T) and record the base count n of distinct solutions.
  4. Compute k = (length of given interval) / T, the number of complete cycles.
  5. Multiply: total = n × k.
  6. Check endpoints: if the interval is closed, test whether the right endpoint satisfies the equation and is not already counted as the start of the next cycle.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\cos 2x = \sin x$ on $0 \le x \le 20\pi$. Period of $\cos 2x$ is $\pi$, of $\sin x$ is $2\pi$, so $T = 2\pi$. Using $\cos 2x = 1 - 2\sin^2 x$: $2\sin^2 x + \sin x - 1 = 0 \Rightarrow \sin x = \tfrac{1}{2}$ or $\sin x = -1$. On $[0,2\pi)$ this gives $x = \tfrac{\pi}{6}, \tfrac{5\pi}{6}, \tfrac{3\pi}{2}$ — 3 solutions. With $20\pi/2\pi = 10$ cycles, total $= 10 \times 3 = 30$. Endpoint $x=20\pi$: $\sin(20\pi)=0$, not a solution.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation $\sin 3x = \tfrac{1}{2}$ on $0 \le x \le 4\pi$.
Period of $\sin 3x$ is $2\pi/3$; count solutions on $[0, 2\pi/3)$ (which is 2), then multiply by $4\pi \div (2\pi/3) = 6$ to get 12.
Equation in degrees, e.g. $\tan 2\theta = 1$ on $0° \le \theta \le 720°$.
Period of $\tan 2\theta$ is $90°$; one solution per period; $720/90 = 8$ cycles gives 8 solutions.
Mixed-period equation $\sin 2x = \cos 3x$ on a wide interval.
Take LCM of $\pi$ and $2\pi/3$, which is $2\pi$; count base solutions on $[0,2\pi)$ then scale.
Interval not a clean multiple of $T$, e.g. $0 \le x \le 7\pi$ with $T = 2\pi$.
Use $\lfloor 7\pi / 2\pi \rfloor = 3$ complete cycles, then individually count solutions in the leftover $[6\pi, 7\pi]$ tail.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the smaller of the two individual periods (e.g. $\pi$ for $\cos 2x$) as the period of the whole equation, instead of the LCM.
  • Assuming the equation has the same number of solutions per period as one of its component equations, ignoring the coupling.
  • Reading 'number of solutions' as 'general solution' and producing a formula rather than an integer count.

Pitfalls

  • Double-counting the boundary when both endpoints of $[0, kT]$ satisfy the equation (closed interval pitfall).
  • Miscounting base-period solutions by missing the second-quadrant root of $\sin x = c$ or the symmetric root of $\cos x = c$.
  • Applying the multiplication when the interval length is not an integer multiple of $T$ without handling the partial cycle.
  • Forgetting to convert between radian and degree period when the equation is posed in degrees.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 trig: uses standard periods, double-angle identities, and exact values on $[0, 2\pi)$. No tools beyond identities and quadratic-in-$\sin x$ techniques are required.
