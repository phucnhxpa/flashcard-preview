Integration

Recognise part (b) integrand as I_n from part (a)'s reduction formula

Family: Cross Referencing Parts Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in a multi-part FP3 question where part (a) establishes a reduction formula for I_n = ∫_0^{π/2} sin^n x dx (or cos^n x, or similar), and part (b) asks for an arc length, surface area, or volume of revolution. After applying the standard formula and simplifying (often via 1+(dy/dx)^2 collapsing through a Pythagorean identity), the integrand reduces to sin^k x or cos^k x on [0, π/2]. The structural hint is decisive: an unused part (a) result almost always means it is needed in part (b).

Motivation — Why this works

Edexcel FP3 structured questions are deliberately scaffolded so the reduction formula is the intended evaluation tool. The recurrence I_n = ((n−1)/n) I_{n−2} chains down to a known base case (I_0 = π/2 or I_1 = 1), giving an exact closed-form value without ad hoc integration by parts repeated from scratch. Recognising the link converts a daunting high-power trig integral into a short product of fractions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. After simplifying the arc length/surface area integrand, I notice it is exactly sin^k x (or cos^k x) integrated over [0, π/2] — the same shape as I_n in part (a).
  2. I rule out direct integration by parts from scratch and rule out substitution: both would replicate work already done in part (a).
  3. I commit because part (a)'s reduction formula would be redundant otherwise — the question is engineered for this link.
  4. I rewrite the integral as (constant prefactor) × I_k, identifying k precisely from the exponent.
  5. I decide the parity of k to fix the base case (I_0 if k even, I_1 if k odd) and chain the recurrence downwards, multiplying fractions carefully.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Simplify the arc length/surface area integrand using the relevant Pythagorean identity until it has the form c · sin^k x (or cos^k x).
  2. Match the limits to those defining I_n in part (a) (typically 0 to π/2).
  3. Write the integral as c · I_k.
  4. Apply I_k = ((k−1)/k) · I_{k−2} repeatedly until reaching I_0 or I_1.
  5. Substitute I_0 = π/2 or I_1 = 1.
  6. Multiply through by all prefactors (e.g. 2π for surface area, π for volume) and present the exact answer.

Worked Micro-Example

Given I_n = ∫_0^{π/2} sin^n x dx with I_n = ((n−1)/n) I_{n−2}, suppose part (b) yields surface area S = 2π ∫_0^{π/2} sin^4 x dx = 2π · I_4. Then I_4 = (3/4) I_2 = (3/4)(1/2) I_0 = (3/8)(π/2) = 3π/16. Hence S = 2π · 3π/16 = 3π^2/8.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Reduction formula given for cos^n x
Identical logic; ensure the simplified integrand is in cosine form, possibly via x → π/2 − x if needed.
Volume of revolution in part (b)
Prefactor becomes π (not 2π), and the integrand typically arises from y^2 rather than √(1+(dy/dx)^2).
Reduction formula of mixed form, e.g. ∫ x^n e^x dx or ∫ tan^n x dx
Same cross-referencing logic; chain down to the explicit base case stated in part (a) (often n=0 or n=1).
Odd power giving base case I_1
Chain stops at I_1 = 1 (for sin) or I_1 = 0 evaluated explicitly for other recurrences — check the definition in part (a).

Common Misreadings

  • Treating part (a) and part (b) as independent and attempting fresh integration by parts in (b).
  • Misreading the exponent k after simplification, leading to the wrong I_k.
  • Assuming the reduction formula applies on limits other than [0, π/2] without checking.

Pitfalls

  • Applying the reduction formula in the wrong direction (raising n instead of lowering it).
  • Choosing the wrong base case: I_0 = π/2 for even n, I_1 = 1 for odd n (with sin definition).
  • Forgetting the constant prefactor (2π for surface area, π for volume) when writing the final answer.
  • Arithmetic slip when multiplying the chain of fractions (e.g. (5/6)(3/4)(1/2)).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure 3 (FP3): reduction formulae, arc length, surface area of revolution, and volumes of revolution. No techniques outside IAL FM are required.
