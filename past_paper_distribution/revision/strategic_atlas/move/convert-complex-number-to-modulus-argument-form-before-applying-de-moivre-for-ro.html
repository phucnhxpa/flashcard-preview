Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever the question asks for the nth roots of a complex number — typically an equation of the form z^n = w with n ≥ 2, where w is given in Cartesian form (a + bi), as a pure negative real (e.g. z^4 = -16), as a pure imaginary (e.g. z^3 = 8i), or with surd entries (e.g. 1 + i√3). Equivalent phrasings: 'solve z^n = w', 'find all nth roots of w', 'express the roots of unity scaled by w', or 'find the square roots of a complex number'. The visual cue is a complex number on the right-hand side that is not already a perfect nth power, paired with a request for multiple roots.

Motivation — Why this works

De Moivre's theorem for roots states that the nth roots of r(cos θ + i sin θ) are r^{1/n}(cos((θ + 2kπ)/n) + i sin((θ + 2kπ)/n)) for k = 0, 1, ..., n-1. This formula requires the input in modulus-argument form: only then do exponentiation and root-taking become a clean operation on r alone and an angle division on θ. Cartesian-form approaches (expanding (x+iy)^n and equating real/imaginary parts) work for n=2 but become algebraically intractable for n ≥ 3. Polar conversion thus unlocks the systematic, equally-spaced root structure on the Argand circle of radius r^{1/n}.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the equation has the shape z^n = w where w is a complex number and I am asked for all n roots — this is a textbook de Moivre roots problem.
  2. I rule out brute-force expansion of (x+iy)^n with real/imaginary comparison: feasible only for n=2, hopeless for n ≥ 3, and it does not naturally produce all n distinct roots.
  3. I commit to de Moivre's root formula because it generates all n roots systematically and the modulus and arguments are easy to compute.
  4. The bridge to action: de Moivre needs r and θ, so my first concrete operation must be to convert w from a + bi to r(cos θ + i sin θ).
  5. Tactical decision: I sketch w on an Argand diagram to fix the correct quadrant for θ, and I check whether to use the principal range (-π, π] (Edexcel convention) or [0, 2π) depending on the question phrasing.
  6. I also pattern-match standard angles (e.g. 1 + i√3 → 2 cis(π/3); -1 → 1·cis(π); 8i → 8 cis(π/2)) to avoid unnecessary arctan computations.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify w = a + bi. Compute the modulus r = |w| = √(a² + b²) (always the positive root).
  2. Compute the argument θ = arg(w). Start from arctan(b/a), then adjust by ±π based on the quadrant determined by the signs of a and b; for a = 0 use ±π/2; for b = 0, a < 0 use π.
  3. Place θ in the required range (typically the principal range (-π, π] for Edexcel).
  4. Rewrite: w = r(cos θ + i sin θ), or equivalently w = r e^{iθ}, ready for de Moivre's theorem.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $z^4 = -8 + 8i\sqrt{3}$. Convert RHS: $r = \sqrt{(-8)^2 + (8\sqrt{3})^2} = \sqrt{64 + 192} = 16$. Argument: point is in the second quadrant, so $\theta = \pi - \arctan(\sqrt{3}) = \pi - \tfrac{\pi}{3} = \tfrac{2\pi}{3}$. Hence $-8 + 8i\sqrt{3} = 16\bigl(\cos\tfrac{2\pi}{3} + i\sin\tfrac{2\pi}{3}\bigr)$, ready for de Moivre to give $z = 2\,\mathrm{cis}\bigl(\tfrac{2\pi/3 + 2k\pi}{4}\bigr)$, $k = 0,1,2,3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

RHS is a pure negative real, e.g. z^n = -c with c > 0
Modulus is c, argument is π (NOT 0). A common slip is to treat the argument as 0 because the imaginary part is zero.
RHS is purely imaginary, e.g. z^n = ki
Modulus is |k|; argument is π/2 if k > 0, -π/2 if k < 0. No arctan needed.
RHS contains standard surds like 1 + i√3 or √3 - i
Pattern-match to a known exact angle (π/3, π/6, etc.) instead of computing arctan numerically.
RHS already given in exponential form re^{iθ}
Skip the conversion — read off r and θ directly, but verify θ lies in the required range.
Square roots specifically (n = 2)
Polar form still preferred; the two roots are √r·cis(θ/2) and √r·cis(θ/2 + π). Cartesian simultaneous-equation method is viable but slower.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to factor or expand z^n = a + bi algebraically by setting z = x + iy and equating real/imaginary parts — intractable for n ≥ 3.
  • Treating the problem as if w were already in polar form and reading 'a' as the modulus when w = a + bi.
  • Guessing roots by inspection when no obvious factorisation exists.

Pitfalls

  • Placing θ in the wrong quadrant: arctan(b/a) gives a principal value that may need ±π adjustment when a < 0.
  • Forgetting that the modulus must be the positive square root of a² + b².
  • Forgetting to express arg in the required range (usually (-π, π] for Edexcel) — answers outside this range may lose marks.
  • For negative reals, mistakenly writing arg(w) = 0 instead of π.
  • Mixing degrees and radians — de Moivre works in either but must be consistent.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2) scope: de Moivre's theorem and nth roots of complex numbers. The relevant formula booklet entry is de Moivre's theorem (cos θ + i sin θ)^n = cos nθ + i sin nθ. Avoid generalising to fractional/irrational n or to roots of multivalued functions like complex logarithms — those are out of scope.
