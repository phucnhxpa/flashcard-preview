Algebra & Functions

Solve algebraically for the reappearing integral after cyclic IBP

Family: Algebraic Rearrangement Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires after applying integration by parts twice (typically to ∫ e^{ax} sin(bx) dx, ∫ e^{ax} cos(bx) dx, or similar products) and finding that the original integral I has reappeared on the right-hand side, multiplied by some constant k. The defining cue is an equation of the form I = (known expression) + kI with k ≠ 1. Phrasings like 'notice the original integral has come back' or 'we now have an equation for I' signal the move. It also fires in reduction-formula derivations where In appears on both sides.

Motivation — Why this works

Once I appears linearly on both sides, the integral has effectively become an algebraic unknown in a one-variable linear equation, which can be solved by collecting like terms and dividing. This works because integration is linear, so the equation I − kI = (expression) is a valid identity in I. The cyclic IBP strategy is designed precisely to engineer this self-reference; without solving algebraically, the IBP would loop forever. The payoff converts a non-elementary-looking manipulation into a one-line rearrangement.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the integral I has reappeared on the right-hand side, so further IBP would just repeat the cycle.
  2. Rule out continuing IBP or trying a substitution — the structure is now algebraic, not analytic.
  3. Commit to treating I as an unknown in a linear equation: bring all I terms to one side.
  4. Bridge: write I − kI = (expression), factor (1−k) from the left.
  5. Tactically, factor common exponential/trig terms on the right before dividing, and remember to add +C since the original was indefinite.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the post-substitution equation in the form I = E(x) + kI where E(x) is the known elementary expression.
  2. Subtract kI from both sides: (1−k) I = E(x).
  3. Divide both sides by (1−k) to isolate I.
  4. Simplify by factoring common terms (e.g. e^{ax}) on the right.
  5. Append the constant of integration +C if the original integral was indefinite.

Worked Micro-Example

For $I = \int e^{2x}\cos x\,dx$, two applications of IBP give $I = \tfrac12 e^{2x}\sin x - \tfrac14 e^{2x}\cos x - \tfrac14 I$. Adding $\tfrac14 I$ to both sides: $\tfrac{5}{4} I = \tfrac14 e^{2x}(2\sin x - \cos x)$. Multiplying by $\tfrac45$: $I = \tfrac15 e^{2x}(2\sin x - \cos x) + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

∫ e^{ax} sin(bx) dx with general a, b
After two IBPs the coefficient becomes k = −b²/a² (or similar); solve (1+b²/a²) I = E(x) and divide.
Reduction formulae where I_n reappears on the right (e.g. ∫ sin^n x dx)
Same algebra: collect I_n terms, divide by (1−k), to get the standard reduction identity.
Definite version ∫_a^b e^{2x} cos x dx
Carry the limits through the IBP, then solve algebraically; no +C needed, just evaluate the bracketed term at the limits.
∫ sec^3 x dx in Further Pure
After one IBP, sec³ reappears via tan²x = sec²x − 1; collect the sec³ terms and divide by 2.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the reappearance of I as a sign that IBP failed and restarting with a different u, dv choice.
  • Misreading kI as a separate new integral rather than the original I.
  • Thinking the equation can only be solved if k = 0, missing that any k ≠ 1 works.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when distributing a negative through a bracket like −½(… + ½I), giving the wrong coefficient on I.
  • Arithmetic slips when combining fractions such as 1 + ¼ = 5/4.
  • Forgetting +C in the indefinite case.
  • Dividing only part of the right-hand side by (1−k) instead of every term.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard technique in Edexcel IAL P3/P4 integration by parts and in Further Pure reduction formulae; uses only linear-equation algebra and the linearity of the integral.
