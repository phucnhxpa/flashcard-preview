Algebra & Functions

Isolate the square root then square to match required squared form

Family: Form Matching Rearrangement Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires after a separable-variables integration yields an equation of the form $k\sqrt{A} = f(t) + C$, while the question demands the answer in the form $A = (pt^q + r)^2$ (or any explicit squared bracket). Cue phrases include 'express in the form', 'show that y can be written as', or a target equation containing a squared bracket. The integrated form contains a square root that must be eliminated.

Motivation — Why this works

If $\sqrt{A}$ equals a single expression $E(t)$, then squaring both sides gives $A = E(t)^2$ exactly — this is a reversible operation provided $\sqrt{A} \ge 0$. Dividing first by the coefficient of $\sqrt{A}$ ensures the squared bracket has the leading coefficient demanded by the target form. Comparing coefficients then reads off $p, q, r$ directly.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the integrated equation has a $\sqrt{A}$ term with a numeric coefficient and the target form is a perfect square in $t$.
  2. Rule out expanding the bracket in the target form (that would force factorising back later) and rule out squaring before isolating $\sqrt{A}$ (that introduces stray coefficients).
  3. Commit to: isolate $\sqrt{A}$ as a clean linear expression in $t^q$, substitute the known constant of integration, then square.
  4. Bridge: divide both sides by the coefficient of $\sqrt{A}$ to get $\sqrt{A} = \tfrac{f(t)+C}{k}$.
  5. Tactical: absorb the constant into a single $r$ before squaring; if the target uses integer $p$, factor inside the bracket so the form matches exactly.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the integrated equation in the form $k\sqrt{A} = f(t) + C$.
  2. Use the boundary condition to determine $C$.
  3. Divide both sides by $k$: $\sqrt{A} = \tfrac{1}{k}f(t) + \tfrac{C}{k}$.
  4. Square both sides: $A = \left(\tfrac{1}{k}f(t) + \tfrac{C}{k}\right)^2$.
  5. Tidy the bracket to match the prescribed form $(pt^q + r)^2$ and read off $p, q, r$.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose $\int \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{A}}\,dA = \int t^{-2/5}\,dt$ gives $2\sqrt{A} = \tfrac{5}{3}t^{3/5} + C$. With $A=4$ when $t=0$: $2(2) = C$, so $C=4$. Divide by 2: $\sqrt{A} = \tfrac{5}{6}t^{3/5} + 2$. Squaring: $A = \left(\tfrac{5}{6}t^{3/5} + 2\right)^2$, giving $p=\tfrac{5}{6}$, $q=\tfrac{3}{5}$, $r=2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Target form $y = e^{f(x)}$ after integrating to $\ln y = f(x)+C$
Exponentiate both sides instead of squaring; the constant becomes a multiplicative factor $e^C = A$.
Target form $y = \tfrac{1}{ax+b}$ after integrating to $-\tfrac{1}{y} = \dots$
Take reciprocals (with a sign tidy) rather than squaring.
Target form $(at+b)^3$ after integrating to $3y^{1/3} = \dots$
Isolate $y^{1/3}$, then cube both sides.
Target form involving $\sqrt{\cdot}$ on the answer side
Do not square — keep the root and just rearrange algebraically.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $(pt^q + r)^2$ as $p^2 t^{2q} + r^2$ and trying to match term-by-term without the cross term.
  • Reading the target as $pt^q + r$ (linear) rather than the full squared bracket.
  • Believing $C$ must be absorbed inside the bracket as a $t^0$ coefficient rather than as the constant $r$ after dividing by $k$.

Pitfalls

  • Squaring before dividing by the coefficient of $\sqrt{A}$, producing $k^2 A = (\dots)^2$ rather than $A = (\dots)^2$.
  • Forgetting to apply the boundary condition before squaring, leaving $C$ unresolved inside the bracket.
  • Sign errors when $C$ is negative — the constant $r$ inherits the sign and is sometimes dropped.
  • Failing to simplify fractions inside the bracket so the stated form $(pt^q+r)^2$ is not recognisable.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard final step in Edexcel IAL P3/P4 differential-equations questions that demand the solution 'in the form' of a squared (or otherwise structured) expression; involves only GCSE-level squaring and basic algebra.
