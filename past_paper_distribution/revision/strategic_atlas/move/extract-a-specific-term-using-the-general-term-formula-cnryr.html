Binomial Expansion

Extract a specific term using the general-term formula C(n,r)y^r

Family: Direct Term Extraction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks for one specific term (e.g. 'the term in x^k', 'the coefficient of x^k', or 'the 7th term') of a binomial expansion, especially when n is large enough that writing out every term is wasteful. Also fires in multi-part questions where part (b) needs a term beyond those listed in part (a)'s ascending expansion. Cue phrases include 'find the term independent of x', 'find the coefficient of x^k', and 'the rth term of...'. Once the required power index r is identified (often via a power-matching step), the general term formula delivers it in one shot.

Motivation — Why this works

The expansion of (1+y)^n has general term T_{r+1} = C(n,r)y^r, and (a+b)^n has T_{r+1} = C(n,r)a^{n-r}b^r. These formulas are exact closed forms for every individual term, so once r is known the term is just an arithmetic evaluation. This avoids expanding every preceding term and isolates the algebra to a single binomial coefficient and a single power. It is the standard efficient route for 'particular term' questions in IAL binomial work.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question asks for one specific term or coefficient, not the whole expansion.
  2. Rule out writing out the full expansion term by term — n is too large or the index is too far along.
  3. Commit to the general term T_{r+1} = C(n,r)y^r because it gives the required term directly once r is fixed.
  4. Determine the correct r by matching the power of x (or other variable) demanded by the question to the exponent inside y^r.
  5. Evaluate C(n,r) and the power piece carefully, then multiply in any external prefactor and simplify the coefficient.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the general term: T_{r+1} = C(n,r) y^r (or C(n,r)a^{n-r}b^r for (a+b)^n).
  2. Substitute the variable form (e.g. y = x/4) so the power of x is visible: C(n,r)(x/4)^r = C(n,r) x^r / 4^r.
  3. Set the exponent of x equal to the target k and solve for r.
  4. Evaluate C(n,r) using n!/(r!(n-r)!) or the falling-product form n(n-1)...(n-r+1)/r!.
  5. Compute the numerical power (e.g. 4^r) and combine with C(n,r).
  6. Multiply by any external prefactor from the original expression and simplify to a single fraction or surd.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the coefficient of $x^5$ in $8(1+\tfrac{x}{4})^{12}$. General term: $T_{r+1}=\binom{12}{r}(x/4)^r=\binom{12}{r}\tfrac{x^r}{4^r}$. Set $r=5$: $\binom{12}{5}=792$ and $4^5=1024$, giving $\tfrac{792}{1024}x^5=\tfrac{99}{128}x^5$. Multiplying by the prefactor $8$ yields coefficient $\tfrac{99}{16}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Expansion of (a+b)^n with both letters varying
Use T_{r+1}=C(n,r)a^{n-r}b^r and track powers of both a and b; the chosen r must satisfy the required exponent on b (or equivalently n-r on a).
Find the term independent of x in (ax^p + b/x^q)^n
Form the general term, collect the net power of x as p(n-r)-qr, set this equal to 0, solve for r, then evaluate.
Find the coefficient of x^k in (1+ax)^n with fractional/negative n (Maclaurin-style binomial series)
Use the generalised binomial coefficient n(n-1)...(n-r+1)/r!; otherwise the extraction step is identical.
Find the rth term (e.g. '7th term')
Use r = (term number) − 1 inside C(n,r), since T_{r+1} is the (r+1)th term.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing T_r with T_{r+1}: the (r+1)th term uses C(n,r), so the 7th term corresponds to r=6, not r=7.
  • Reading 'coefficient of x^k' as 'kth term' — these are generally different indices.
  • Forgetting the external prefactor multiplying the bracket and quoting only the internal coefficient.

Pitfalls

  • Miscalculating C(n,r) by truncating the falling product, e.g. using 12·11·10·9·8/5! correctly gives 792 but stopping early gives wrong values.
  • Arithmetic errors in powers, e.g. taking 4^5=4096 instead of 1024.
  • Forgetting to apply the power to a numerical denominator inside the bracket, e.g. writing (x/4)^r as x^r/4 instead of x^r/4^r.
  • Sign errors when the bracket is (1−y)^n: the general term carries (−1)^r.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure scope: binomial expansion for positive integer n (P2) and the generalised binomial series for rational n with |x|<1 (P4). No tools beyond C(n,r) evaluation and basic index laws are required.
