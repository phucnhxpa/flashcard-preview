Trigonometry

Read negative amplitude as x-axis reflection to locate max/min

Family: Graph Transformation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a sinusoid y = A cos(x − c)° or y = A sin(x − c)° has an unknown constant A and a labelled stationary point identifies whether it is a maximum or minimum. Cue phrases include 'A is a constant' (not 'positive constant'), a marked minimum point at the natural max-location of the un-reflected curve, or a graph drawn upside-down relative to the standard shape. Also fires when extracting A from a coordinate yields a negative value and the student must reconcile this with the labelling.

Motivation — Why this works

For y = A cos θ with A > 0, the maximum is at θ = 0 (where cos θ = 1) and the minimum is at θ = ±180° (where cos θ = −1). If A < 0 the whole curve is reflected in the x-axis, so the roles of max and min swap: max now occurs where cos θ = −1, min where cos θ = +1. Recognising this is essential for correctly reading off coordinates of stationary points and for solving subsequent equations like A cos(x − c)° = k.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the labelled point P is a minimum but sits at the x-value that would normally give a maximum of a cosine curve.
  2. Rule out A > 0: if A were positive, the natural max-location would not be a minimum, contradicting the diagram.
  3. Commit to A < 0, treating the curve as a reflection of |A|cos(x − c)° in the x-axis.
  4. Substitute the coordinates of P into y = A cos(x − c)° to solve for A, expecting a negative value.
  5. Use the reflection insight to re-map: maxima now occur where the inner cosine equals −1, minima where it equals +1, and adjust all later part-(b) work accordingly.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify from the diagram whether the labelled stationary point is a max or min, and at what argument of the inner function it occurs.
  2. Substitute the point's coordinates into y = A cos(x − c)° (or sin) and solve for A; accept negative values.
  3. If A < 0, state that the graph is the reflection of y = |A| cos(x − c)° in the x-axis.
  4. Re-deduce: maximum y-value = |A|, achieved when cos(x − c)° = −1; minimum y-value = −|A|, achieved when cos(x − c)° = +1.
  5. Use these corrected conditions when answering follow-up parts (e.g. finding x-coordinates of further max/min, or solving y = k).

Worked Micro-Example

Curve y = A cos(x − 30)° has a minimum at P(30, −3). Sub in: −3 = A cos(0°) = A, so A = −3. Since A < 0, the curve is y = 3 cos(x − 30)° reflected in the x-axis. Hence the maximum y = 3 occurs when cos(x − 30)° = −1, i.e. x − 30 = 180°, giving x = 210°.

Variants — Same move, different surface

y = A sin(x − c)° with a labelled maximum where standard sine would have a minimum
Same logic: A < 0 reflects in x-axis, so maxima of y occur where sin(x − c)° = −1.
y = A cos(x − c)° + d with vertical shift
Reflection still swaps max/min roles; max y = −A·(−1) + d = |A| + d (if A<0), min y = A + d.
Determining A from a maximum point that gives a positive value
Standard case A > 0; no reflection reasoning needed, but check the diagram orientation to be sure.
y = A cos(B(x − c))° with stretch in x
B affects period only; sign of A still controls reflection and max/min interchange.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming 'A is a constant' implies A > 0 because the word 'amplitude' is positive in physical contexts.
  • Treating the labelled minimum as a maximum and obtaining A = 3, contradicting the diagram.
  • Believing a negative A reflects in the y-axis rather than the x-axis.

Pitfalls

  • After finding A = −3, still searching for max where cos(x − c)° = +1, leading to the wrong x-coordinate.
  • Forgetting to flip max/min conditions when solving A cos(x − c)° = k for boundary values.
  • Sign errors when substituting negative A back into the equation for later parts.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P2/P3 graph-transformation content: reflections of sinusoidal curves in the x-axis via a negative coefficient, with stationary points read from a sketch.
