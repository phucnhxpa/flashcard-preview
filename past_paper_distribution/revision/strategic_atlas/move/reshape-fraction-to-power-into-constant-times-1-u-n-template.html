Trigger — When to use

The expression is a fractional or reciprocal power such as $\left(\frac{a}{b+cx}\right)^n$, $\frac{1}{(b+cx)^n}$, or $(b+cx)^{-n}$, which does not directly match the formula-booklet template $(1+u)^n$. The bracket contains a constant term other than 1, so the generalised binomial series cannot be applied as written. Equivalent phrasings include 'expand in ascending powers of $x$' or 'find the first few terms of the expansion of a rational expression'.

Motivation — Why this works

The generalised binomial series in the Edexcel formula booklet is stated only for $(1+u)^n$ with $|u|<1$. Any constant other than 1 inside the bracket must be factored out so that the leading term becomes 1, leaving a small perturbation $u$ in $x$. Distributing powers across a fraction and converting denominators to negative exponents preserves equality while producing the exact template the formula expects, with the extracted constants becoming a single multiplicative prefactor.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the expression is a power of a fraction (or a reciprocal of a binomial), so it is not in $(1+u)^n$ form as required by the formula booklet.
  2. I rule out applying the binomial series directly to $(b+cx)^n$ because the leading term inside is $b$, not $1$, so convergence and the standard coefficients fail.
  3. I rule out factoring $cx$ out of the bracket because that would give an expansion in descending powers of $x$ (or $1/x$), not the ascending powers required.
  4. I commit to factoring the constant $b$ out of the bracket, which produces $b^n(1+\tfrac{cx}{b})^n$ with $\tfrac{cx}{b}$ small for the relevant $x$-range.
  5. Tactically, I distribute the outer power across numerator and denominator first, convert any denominator to a negative exponent, then factor the constant — combining all extracted constants into one prefactor that scales every term of the expansion.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Distribute the outer power: $\left(\frac{a}{b+cx}\right)^n = \frac{a^n}{(b+cx)^n}$.
  2. Convert the denominator to a negative exponent: $= a^n(b+cx)^{-n}$.
  3. Factor the constant $b$ from the bracket: $(b+cx)^{-n} = b^{-n}\left(1+\tfrac{cx}{b}\right)^{-n}$.
  4. Combine constants into a single prefactor: $\dfrac{a^n}{b^n}\left(1+\tfrac{cx}{b}\right)^{-n}$.
  5. Identify $u = \tfrac{cx}{b}$ and exponent $-n$, ready for the generalised binomial formula.

Worked Micro-Example

Prepare $\left(\frac{2}{5+2x}\right)^8$ for expansion: $\left(\frac{2}{5+2x}\right)^8 = \frac{2^8}{(5+2x)^8} = \frac{256}{5^8\left(1+\frac{2x}{5}\right)^8} = \frac{256}{390625}\left(1+\frac{2x}{5}\right)^{-8}.$ Now $u=\tfrac{2x}{5}$, $n=-8$, and the formula applies directly with validity $|x|<\tfrac{5}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$(3-x)^{-2}$ — negative exponent on a binomial with subtraction
Factor $3$: $3^{-2}\left(1-\tfrac{x}{3}\right)^{-2}$; here $u=-\tfrac{x}{3}$ carries the negative sign.
$\sqrt{4+x}$ — fractional positive exponent
Write as $(4+x)^{1/2}=4^{1/2}(1+\tfrac{x}{4})^{1/2}=2(1+\tfrac{x}{4})^{1/2}$.
$\dfrac{1}{(a+bx)^n}$ — pure reciprocal power
Factor $a$: $a^{-n}\left(1+\tfrac{bx}{a}\right)^{-n}$ with prefactor $a^{-n}$.
$\dfrac{p+qx}{(a+bx)^n}$ — non-trivial numerator
Apply the move to the denominator only, then multiply the resulting expansion by $(p+qx)$ term-by-term afterwards.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to expand $(b+cx)^n$ directly with the $(1+u)^n$ formula, treating $b$ as if it were $1$.
  • Factoring $cx$ instead of $b$, producing an expansion in negative powers of $x$ (descending) instead of ascending powers.
  • Treating $\left(\frac{a}{b+cx}\right)^n$ as $\frac{a^n}{b^n+c^nx^n}$ — incorrectly distributing the power across the sum in the denominator.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to raise the factored constant to the full power, e.g. writing $5$ instead of $5^8$ in the prefactor.
  • Sign error when the bracket contains subtraction, e.g. $(5-2x)$: the new $u$ must be $-\tfrac{2x}{5}$, and odd powers of $u$ flip sign.
  • Losing or mis-multiplying the constant prefactor when writing the final expansion — every term of $(1+u)^n$ must be scaled by it.
  • Forgetting to update the validity range: $|u|<1$ becomes $\left|\tfrac{cx}{b}\right|<1$, i.e. $|x|<\tfrac{b}{|c|}$.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard preparation step for Edexcel IAL P3/P4 generalised binomial expansion of rational and surd expressions; uses only index laws and the formula-booklet series for $(1+x)^n$ with $n\in\mathbb{Q}$.
