Parameterise quadratic in vertex form when turning point is given

Family: Vertex Form Parameterisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question states (or has already established) that a quadratic has its turning point/vertex at a specific coordinate (h, k), e.g. 'maximum at (−2, 12)' or 'minimum value k attained at x = h'. Equivalent phrasings include 'stationary point of the curve y = f(x) is …', 'least value of f(x) is k, occurring at x = h', or a graph sketch labelling the vertex. The move fires whenever the vertex is directly available and one further condition (a point on the curve, a root, an intercept) remains to pin down the leading coefficient.

Motivation — Why this works

The vertex form f(x) = a(x − h)² + k encodes the turning point (h, k) directly as two of its three parameters, leaving only the leading coefficient a unknown. Starting from ax² + bx + c instead would require either differentiation plus solving f'(h) = 0 or invoking the axis-of-symmetry formula x = −b/(2a), introducing a second equation for no gain. Vertex form thus collapses a three-unknown fit into a one-unknown fit, exploiting the completed-square identity a(x − h)² + k = ax² − 2ahx + (ah² + k).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the question hands over the turning-point coordinates explicitly — this is the strongest structural cue available.
  2. Rule out standard form ax² + bx + c (three unknowns, needs differentiation or symmetry condition) and factored form a(x − r₁)(x − r₂) (needs both roots, only one is implied at best).
  3. Commit to vertex form a(x − h)² + k because it absorbs the given vertex into its very structure, leaving only a to determine.
  4. Substitute the vertex coordinates immediately to write f(x) = a(x − h)² + k with numerical h, k, taking care with the sign of h.
  5. Decide the sign of a from max/min language (max ⇒ a < 0, min ⇒ a > 0) and plan to fix |a| using the remaining datum (an extra point, a root, or a y-intercept).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Read off the vertex (h, k) from the question.
  2. Write f(x) = a(x − h)² + k, being careful that x = h gives the bracket (x − h), so a vertex at x = −2 produces (x + 2)².
  3. Record the sign constraint on a: a < 0 for a maximum, a > 0 for a minimum.
  4. Substitute the remaining given condition (e.g. a known point on the curve, or f(0) = c) to obtain a linear equation in a and solve.
  5. If the final answer is required in expanded form, expand a(x − h)² + k to ax² − 2ahx + (ah² + k).

Worked Micro-Example

Find f(x) given a maximum turning point at (−2, 12) and f(0) = 8. Write f(x) = a(x + 2)² + 12. Substitute x = 0: 8 = 4a + 12, so a = −1 (consistent with a < 0 for a maximum). Hence f(x) = −(x + 2)² + 12 = −x² − 4x + 8.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Minimum turning point at (h, k) plus a known point
Same form a(x − h)² + k, but enforce a > 0; solve for a using the extra point.
Two roots given instead of a vertex
Switch to factored form a(x − r₁)(x − r₂) and find a from one further point.
Three non-vertex points given
Use standard form ax² + bx + c and solve a 3×3 simultaneous system.
'Least value of f(x) is k' without coordinates of the vertex
First complete the square on the given expression to extract h, then read off vertex form directly.
Curve passes through the vertex and a tangent/line condition is given
Use vertex form for the curve, then impose the tangency/intersection condition to determine a.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the vertex coordinates as a generic point on the curve and substituting into ax² + bx + c, ignoring that this is the turning point.
  • Starting with standard form and using b² − 4ac or differentiation to impose the turning-point condition, creating unnecessary algebraic complexity.
  • Confusing the y-coordinate of the vertex k with the y-intercept c of the expanded quadratic.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error in (x − h): vertex at x = −2 gives (x − (−2))² = (x + 2)², not (x − 2)².
  • Choosing the wrong sign for a (using a > 0 for a maximum or vice versa).
  • Forgetting to expand back to ax² + bx + c when the question asks for that form, or conversely expanding when vertex form was required.
  • Arithmetic slip when expanding a(x − h)² + k, particularly with the cross term −2ah.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL P1/Pure 1 'completing the square and quadratic graphs'; relies only on vertex form, sign of leading coefficient, and substitution of a known point.
