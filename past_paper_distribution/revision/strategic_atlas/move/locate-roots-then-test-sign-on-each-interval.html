Algebra & Functions

Locate roots, then test sign on each interval

Family: Sign Diagram Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A polynomial (or factorisable) inequality of the form f(x) > 0, < 0, ≥ 0 or ≤ 0, especially when f(x) is already presented as a product of linear factors. Also fires when the question says 'find the set of values of x for which...' and a quick factorisation is available. Common phrasings: 'solve the inequality', 'find the values of x', 'state the range of x'. Particularly efficient when sketching the curve would be slower than a sign table.

Motivation — Why this works

A polynomial is continuous, so it can only change sign at its real roots. Once the roots partition ℝ into open intervals, the sign of f is constant on each interval, so a single test value per interval determines the sign everywhere. The factored form makes both the roots (each factor = 0) and the sign of the product (product of signs of factors) immediate, avoiding any need to expand or sketch.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the expression is already factored into linear pieces, so the roots are readable directly with no algebraic work.
  2. I rule out expanding to a cubic and solving from scratch, and I rule out a full curve sketch — both are slower than a sign diagram.
  3. I commit to a sign-diagram approach because three linear factors give three roots and four intervals, each needing only one test value.
  4. I bridge to action by setting each factor to zero, then ordering the resulting roots on a number line so the intervals are correctly labelled.
  5. Tactically, I pick simple test values (integers or 0 where possible) and use the sign of the leading coefficient as a sanity check on the leftmost/rightmost intervals.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Set each linear factor to zero to read off the roots.
  2. Order the roots on a number line, taking care with negative fractions.
  3. Mark the resulting open intervals (one more interval than there are roots).
  4. Choose a convenient test value in each interval and compute the sign of each factor at that value.
  5. Multiply the signs of the factors to get the sign of f on that interval.
  6. Collect the intervals matching the required inequality, using open or closed endpoints according to whether the inequality is strict.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $(3x+20)(x+6)(2x-3) > 0$. Roots: $x=-\tfrac{20}{3},\ -6,\ \tfrac{3}{2}$, ordered as $-\tfrac{20}{3}<-6<\tfrac{3}{2}$. Test $x=-7$: $(-)(-)(-)<0$; $x=-6.5$: $(+)(-)(-)>0$; $x=0$: $(+)(+)(-)<0$; $x=2$: $(+)(+)(+)>0$. Solution: $-\tfrac{20}{3}\tfrac{3}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Repeated factor, e.g. $(x-a)^2(x-b)>0$
The sign does not change at the double root; treat it as a 'touch' so adjacent intervals share the same sign.
Rational inequality $\dfrac{p(x)}{q(x)}>0$
Use roots of both numerator and denominator to partition ℝ, but exclude denominator roots from the solution set (open circles).
Quadratic inequality $ax^2+bx+c>0$
Factorise (or use the formula) to get two roots, then sign-test the three intervals; the sign of $a$ gives an instant check.
Inequality involving $|f(x)|$ or squared factor always $\ge 0$
Cancel/ignore the always-nonnegative factor (noting where it is zero), and apply the sign diagram to the remaining factors.

Common Misreadings

  • Expanding the product into a cubic and trying to solve from scratch, throwing away the free root information.
  • Treating $\ge$ and $>$ as interchangeable and including (or excluding) the roots incorrectly.
  • Reading the factored form as already telling you the answer is 'all factors positive', forgetting that an even number of negatives also gives a positive product.

Pitfalls

  • Mis-ordering the roots when negatives or fractions are involved (e.g. $-\tfrac{20}{3}\approx-6.67$ is less than $-6$, not greater).
  • Picking a test value that coincidentally equals a root, giving zero instead of a sign.
  • Forgetting to flip the inequality direction when multiplying through by a negative quantity earlier in the working.
  • Writing the final answer as a single interval when the solution set is a union of two disjoint intervals.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure technique for solving polynomial and rational inequalities (P1–P4); sign diagrams are the expected method alongside or in place of curve sketches.
