Algebra & Functions

Cite range of f as domain of f⁻¹ via inverse duality

Family: Inverse Function Application Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a multi-part question first asks for the range of f and then asks for f⁻¹(x) together with its domain. Also triggers when the inverse turns out to be a rational function with an obvious denominator restriction that 'happens' to match the excluded value from the range. Phrases like 'hence find', 'state the domain of f⁻¹', or 'using your answer to part (a)' are strong cues that the duality is intended.

Motivation — Why this works

For any bijection, domain(f⁻¹) = range(f) and range(f⁻¹) = domain(f); this is the defining symmetry of inverse functions. Citing the previously found range gives the domain of f⁻¹ instantly and earns the 'hence' mark, whereas re-deriving it from the algebraic form of f⁻¹ duplicates work and risks missing implicit restrictions. The denominator restriction in f⁻¹(x) is not a coincidence — it is forced by which y-values f never attains.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that part (a) already produced range(f) = ℝ \ {2}, and part (b) now asks for f⁻¹ and its domain.
  2. Rule out re-computing the domain from scratch by inspecting f⁻¹(x)'s denominator alone — that misses the 'hence' connection the examiner wants.
  3. Commit to the inverse-duality principle: domain(f⁻¹) is exactly what range(f) was, by definition of inverse.
  4. Bridge: carry out the algebraic inversion (swap-and-solve) to get an explicit formula for f⁻¹(x).
  5. Tactically, after writing f⁻¹(x), state its domain by quoting part (a) — verify consistency with the denominator restriction, but cite the range result as the justification.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write y = f(x) and swap x and y to get x = f(y).
  2. Solve for y algebraically (clear denominators, collect y-terms, factor).
  3. Write f⁻¹(x) = (resulting expression).
  4. State domain of f⁻¹ as range of f from the previous part, e.g. x ∈ ℝ, x ≠ 2.
  5. Optionally check that this matches the natural algebraic restriction of f⁻¹(x) (denominator ≠ 0).

Worked Micro-Example

Given f(x) = (2x+3)/(x−1), x ≠ 1, with range ℝ \ {2} from part (a). Set x = (2y+3)/(y−1) ⇒ x(y−1) = 2y+3 ⇒ y(x−2) = x+3 ⇒ f⁻¹(x) = (x+3)/(x−2). Hence domain of f⁻¹ is x ∈ ℝ, x ≠ 2 (= range of f).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Logarithm/exponential pair, e.g. f(x) = e^x + k with range (k, ∞)
domain of f⁻¹(x) = ln(x − k) is x > k, quoted directly from range(f).
Square-root function f(x) = √(x−a) + b with range [b, ∞)
domain of f⁻¹ is x ≥ b, taken from range of f rather than re-deriving from the squared expression.
Restricted trig inverse, e.g. arcsin as inverse of sin on [−π/2, π/2]
domain of arcsin = range of sin on the restricted interval = [−1, 1].
Rational f with horizontal asymptote y = c
the excluded value c in range(f) becomes the excluded value c in domain(f⁻¹), matching the denominator zero of f⁻¹(x).

Common Misreadings

  • Stating the domain of f⁻¹ by looking only at where its denominator vanishes, without referencing range of f — loses the 'hence' link.
  • Confusing domain and range of f⁻¹: writing the original domain of f as the domain of f⁻¹.
  • Forgetting that the result requires f to be one-to-one on its stated domain before invoking the duality.

Pitfalls

  • Algebra slip when collecting y-terms during the swap-and-solve, leading to a wrong f⁻¹ and an inconsistent domain.
  • Writing the domain as 'x ≠ 1' (copying f's restriction) instead of 'x ≠ 2'.
  • Omitting the set notation (e.g. just writing 'x ≠ 2' without 'x ∈ ℝ') when the mark scheme expects both.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure (P3) Functions topic: inverse functions exist only on a domain where f is one-to-one, and the duality domain(f⁻¹) = range(f) is the expected justification in 'hence' style questions.
