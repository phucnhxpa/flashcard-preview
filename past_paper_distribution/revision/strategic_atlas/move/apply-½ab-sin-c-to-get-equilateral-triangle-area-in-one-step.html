Trigonometry

Apply ½ab sin C to get equilateral triangle area in one step

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a problem requires the area of an equilateral (or any known SAS) triangle expressed purely in terms of a side length, especially as a building block for surface areas of solids with triangular faces (tetrahedron, octahedron, icosahedron) or for decomposing regular polygons. Cues include 'equilateral triangular faces of side x', 'regular hexagon of side a', or any 'find the area in exact form' phrasing where two sides and the included angle are known. Also triggers when avoiding a separate height computation would streamline the working.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity Area = ½ab sin C is the SAS area formula and applies directly whenever two sides and the included angle are known. For an equilateral triangle, a = b = side and C = 60°, so sin 60° = √3/2 produces the exact surd (√3/4)x² in a single line. This avoids the auxiliary step of computing the perpendicular height by Pythagoras or by x sin 60°, and is cleaner than Heron's formula, which introduces s = 3x/2 and a square root.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice I need the area of an equilateral triangle with side x in exact form, not a numerical value.
  2. I rule out ½ × base × height because the height isn't given and would need a separate Pythagoras/trig step, and I rule out Heron's formula as algebraically heavier than needed.
  3. I commit to ½ab sin C because all three inputs (a = x, b = x, C = 60°) are immediate from the equilateral property.
  4. Bridging to operation: substitute a = b = x and use sin 60° = √3/2 to obtain (√3/4)x² per face.
  5. Tactically, I keep the result in surd form and multiply by the number of faces (e.g. 20 for an icosahedron) at the end to avoid rounding.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the triangle as equilateral with side x, giving a = b = x and included angle C = 60°.
  2. Apply Area = ½ab sin C = ½ · x · x · sin 60°.
  3. Substitute sin 60° = √3/2 to get Area = (√3/4)x² per triangle.
  4. Multiply by the number of congruent triangular faces (or sub-triangles) to obtain the required total.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the surface area of a regular icosahedron with edge length $x$. Each face is equilateral, so its area is $\tfrac{1}{2}x^2\sin 60° = \tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{4}x^2$. With 20 faces, total surface area $= 20 \cdot \tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{4}x^2 = 5\sqrt{3}\,x^2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Regular tetrahedron or octahedron with edge x
Same per-face formula (√3/4)x²; multiply by 4 or 8 faces respectively.
Regular hexagon of side a
Decompose into 6 equilateral triangles to get area = 6·(√3/4)a² = (3√3/2)a².
General SAS triangle (two sides and included angle known)
Use ½ab sin C directly with the given C; no equilateral simplification.
Regular n-gon of side a inscribed about centre
Split into n isoceles triangles with apex angle 2π/n and apply ½r² sin(2π/n) per triangle (circumradius form).

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing the number of faces (icosahedron has 20 triangular faces; dodecahedron has 12 pentagonal faces).
  • Treating the triangle as right-angled and using ½ × base × height with height = x, instead of x√3/2.
  • Reading 'equilateral' as merely 'isoceles' and leaving the angle unspecified.

Pitfalls

  • Writing sin 60° = ½ (which is sin 30°) instead of √3/2.
  • Misremembering the formula as ½ab cos C.
  • Forgetting to square x, or dropping the factor ¼ when simplifying ½ · √3/2.
  • Rounding √3 prematurely when an exact-form answer is required.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the SAS area formula and exact value sin 60° = √3/2, both standard in Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P2 trigonometry). No further-maths content required.
