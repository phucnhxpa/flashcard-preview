Matrices

Expand along the row or column whose entries are parameter-free

Family: Expansion Row Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A 3×3 determinant must be evaluated where one or more entries depend on a parameter (e.g. k), and no row or column contains a zero to exploit. One row (or column) consists entirely of constants while the others mix constants with the parameter. The task is to choose which row/column to expand along for cofactor expansion. Phrasings include 'find the values of k for which det(A) = 0' or 'show that det(A) = ...' with a symbolic matrix.

Motivation — Why this works

Cofactor expansion multiplies each chosen row/column entry by a 2×2 minor. If the chosen entries are constants, the parameter k appears only inside the minors, never as both a multiplier and inside a minor at once. This keeps the resulting polynomial in k linear-in-structure and minimises bracket expansions. A shared constant factor (like 2) can also be pulled out, simplifying the algebra further.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the matrix is symbolic: some entries depend on k, others are pure numbers.
  2. Rule out rows/columns containing k as multipliers, since they would force k to appear both outside and inside the 2×2 minors.
  3. Commit to the row (or column) of all constants, because constant multipliers keep the expansion clean and any common factor can be extracted.
  4. Bridge: write det(A) = a·C₁ − b·C₂ + c·C₃ with the correct sign pattern (+, −, +) for that row/column.
  5. Inside each 2×2 minor, expand carefully; collect like powers of k at the end rather than mid-computation.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the row or column whose entries are all constants (or contain the most constants/zeros).
  2. Write det(A) as the signed sum of (entry)·(2×2 minor) using the checkerboard sign pattern.
  3. Form each 2×2 submatrix by deleting the chosen row and the relevant column.
  4. Evaluate each 2×2 minor as ad − bc, keeping k-terms tidy.
  5. Combine, factor out any common constant, and simplify to a polynomial in k.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 2 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 & 2 \\ 2 & k & k \end{pmatrix}$. Expand along row 1: $\det(A) = 2\begin{vmatrix}1 & 2 \\ k & k\end{vmatrix} - 2\begin{vmatrix}2 & 2 \\ 2 & k\end{vmatrix} + 2\begin{vmatrix}2 & 1 \\ 2 & k\end{vmatrix}$. This gives $2(k - 2k) - 2(2k - 4) + 2(2k - 2) = -2k - 4k + 8 + 4k - 4 = -2k + 4$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

A row or column contains a zero entry
Prefer that row/column instead — the zero kills one cofactor term entirely, beating the 'all constants' heuristic.
Symbolic 3×3 with parameter spread evenly across all rows
Choose the row/column with the fewest distinct symbolic terms, or use row operations first to create a zero before expanding.
A column (not row) is the all-constant one
Expand along that column using the same sign pattern (+, −, +) read vertically; the logic is identical.
The constant row shares a common factor
Factor it out of the determinant first via the scalar-row property: replacing row by 1, multiplying det by the factor.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming row 1 is always the right choice by default, even when row 2 or a column has zeros.
  • Thinking 'mostly constant' beats 'all constant' — any single k in the chosen row reintroduces the multiplier-and-minor entanglement.
  • Confusing 'fewer parameter entries' with 'shorter computation'; the issue is parameter placement, not raw count.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the alternating sign pattern: the (1,2) cofactor carries a minus sign.
  • Deleting the wrong row or column when forming the 2×2 minor.
  • Dropping a factor of 2 (or other common multiplier) when extracting it from the row.
  • Sign errors inside ad − bc when one of the entries is itself negative or contains −k.

A-level Scope Notes

Cofactor expansion of 3×3 determinants is standard in Edexcel IAL Further Pure (matrices chapter); the row-choice heuristic is implicit in worked examples and exam efficiency.
