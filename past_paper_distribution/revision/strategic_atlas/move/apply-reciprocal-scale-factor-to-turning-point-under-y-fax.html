Algebra & Functions

Apply reciprocal scale factor to turning point under y = f(ax)

Family: Graph Transformation Analysis Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question presents y = f(ax) (e.g. f(¼x), f(2x), f(−3x)) and asks for the image of a labelled point — turning point, intercept, or stationary point. The cue is a coefficient inside the function argument, not outside. The decision point is recognising that the horizontal scale factor is 1/a, not a. Common phrasings: 'state the coordinates of the image of …', 'find the new turning point of y = f(ax)', or 'sketch y = f(½x)'.

Motivation — Why this works

If (p, q) lies on y = f(x), then on y = f(ax) the same output q occurs when ax = p, i.e. x = p/a. So x-coordinates are divided by a, equivalently multiplied by 1/a — a horizontal stretch from the y-axis with scale factor 1/a. The y-coordinate is unaffected because the transformation acts only inside the argument. This 'reciprocal' behaviour is the inside-the-function counterpart to the outside-the-function rule, where y = af(x) scales y directly by a.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the coefficient sits inside the bracket: y = f(ax), so this is a horizontal (x-direction) transformation, not vertical.
  2. I rule out multiplying x-coordinates by a directly — that would be the outside-the-function rule; inside-the-function transformations behave 'oppositely'.
  3. I commit to the reciprocal rule: horizontal scale factor is 1/a, applied from the y-axis, with the y-coordinate untouched.
  4. Bridge: take the given point (p, q) and compute the new x-coordinate as p/a = p × (1/a); keep y = q.
  5. Tactical check: substitute the new x back into f(ax) to confirm it returns q, e.g. f(a · (p/a)) = f(p) = q.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the transformation form: y = f(ax) ⇒ horizontal stretch, scale factor 1/a, from the y-axis.
  2. Read off the original point (p, q).
  3. Compute new x-coordinate: p/a (equivalently p × 1/a).
  4. Keep new y-coordinate equal to q.
  5. State the image point (p/a, q) and, if useful, verify by substitution into f(ax).

Worked Micro-Example

The curve y = f(x) has turning point (4, 9). Find the turning point of y = f(¼x).\nHere a = ¼, so the horizontal scale factor is 1/a = 4.\nNew x-coordinate: 4 × 4 = 16; y-coordinate unchanged: 9.\nCheck: f(¼ · 16) = f(4) = 9. ✓ Image: (16, 9).

Variants — Same move, different surface

y = f(2x) with point (6, −5)
Scale factor 1/2; new point is (3, −5) — a horizontal compression.
y = f(−x) with point (3, 7)
Reflection in the y-axis (scale factor −1); new point is (−3, 7).
y = af(x) with point (p, q)
Outside transformation: y is multiplied by a directly, giving (p, aq); no reciprocal involved.
y = f(2x − 6) with point (p, q)
Rewrite as f(2(x − 3)): horizontal stretch by 1/2 then translation +3 in x; new x = p/2 + 3, y = q.
Asked for x-intercept or asymptote rather than turning point
Same rule applies — divide x-coordinate by a; vertical asymptote x = k becomes x = k/a.

Common Misreadings

  • Multiplying x-coordinates by a instead of 1/a (e.g. using ¼ rather than 4).
  • Treating f(ax) as if it were af(x) and scaling the y-coordinate instead.
  • Applying the stretch from the x-axis rather than from the y-axis.
  • For f(ax + b), forgetting to factor out a before identifying the translation.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to rewrite f(ax + b) as f(a(x + b/a)) before separating stretch and translation.
  • Mishandling negative a — overlooking the reflection component alongside the stretch.
  • Stating the scale factor without the centre ('from the y-axis'), losing a mark on 'fully describe the transformation'.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics graph transformations (P2/P3), covering single transformations of the form y = f(ax) and their effect on key features such as turning points, intercepts, and asymptotes.
