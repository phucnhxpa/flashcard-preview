Substitute the required point to solve for a translation constant

Family: Point Substitution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a transformed curve (typically y = f(x) − a, y = f(x) + c, or similar) is required to pass through a specified point, and the original graph supplies the corresponding value of f at that x-coordinate. Common phrasings: 'find the value of a such that the curve passes through the origin', 'the transformed graph crosses the y-axis at...', or 'the new curve has y-intercept 0'. The unknown is a single transformation constant, and one point condition is given.

Motivation — Why this works

A vertical translation y = f(x) − a shifts every y-coordinate down by a, so requiring the new curve to pass through (x₀, y₀) gives the scalar equation y₀ = f(x₀) − a. Since f(x₀) can be read directly off the original graph (often at an intercept or labelled feature), the geometric condition collapses to a one-line algebraic solve. This is the canonical way to pin down any single transformation parameter from a single point constraint.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question gives a point the new curve must pass through, and asks for a single unknown constant in the transformation.
  2. Rule out re-deriving the equation of f or solving simultaneously — only one unknown and one condition are present.
  3. Commit to substituting the point's coordinates directly into the transformed equation, treating f(x₀) as a number to be read from the graph.
  4. Bridge: identify which graph feature gives f(x₀) — typically a y-intercept (for x₀ = 0), a labelled point, or a turning point.
  5. Tactically, plug in, isolate the constant, and sanity-check by mentally applying the translation to confirm the named point maps correctly.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the equation of the transformed curve, e.g. y = f(x) − a.
  2. Substitute the required point (x₀, y₀) into this equation: y₀ = f(x₀) − a.
  3. Read f(x₀) from the original graph at the relevant feature.
  4. Solve the resulting linear equation for the unknown constant.
  5. Verify by checking that the translation sends the original point to the required image point.

Worked Micro-Example

The curve $y = f(x)$ has y-intercept $(0, 6)$. Find $a$ so that $y = f(x) - a$ passes through the origin. Substitute $(0,0)$: $0 = f(0) - a = 6 - a$, hence $a = 6$. Check: the translation shifts $(0,6)$ down by $6$ to $(0,0)$ ✓.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Horizontal translation y = f(x − a) passing through a given point
Substitute the point and solve f(x₀ − a) = y₀; you must locate the x-value on the original graph where f attains y₀, then equate it to x₀ − a.
Vertical stretch y = k f(x) passing through a known point
Substitute to get y₀ = k f(x₀) and divide; reading f(x₀) from the graph gives k = y₀ / f(x₀).
Reflection combined with translation, e.g. y = −f(x) + c through a point
Substitute and solve c = y₀ + f(x₀); the sign flip of f must be handled before isolating c.
Two-parameter transformation y = f(x + b) + c with two point conditions
Form two equations from the two points and solve simultaneously rather than in one line.

Common Misreadings

  • Using a labelled turning point or other prominent feature instead of the y-intercept when the condition specifies x = 0.
  • Confusing a vertical translation y = f(x) − a with a horizontal one y = f(x − a) and substituting into the wrong slot.
  • Reading the x-intercept as f(0) instead of the y-intercept.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error: solving 0 = f(0) + a instead of 0 = f(0) − a, giving a = −6.
  • Misreading the graph value of f(x₀), especially when scales on the axes differ.
  • Forgetting to verify, so a sign or direction error in the translation goes undetected.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P1/P2 graph transformations content: single-parameter vertical/horizontal translations, stretches and reflections of y = f(x), with parameters determined from given points or intercepts read off a sketch.
