Algebra & Functions

Combine rational terms over a common denominator for an exact fraction

Family: Arithmetic Consolidation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires at the end of a calculation when several rational terms with different denominators must be presented as a single fraction p/q. Typical cues: a binomial/Taylor approximation evaluated at a rational point producing terms like 1, 4/27, −16/729; a question explicitly asking for the answer 'as a fraction' or 'in the form p/q'; partial-fraction reconstructions; or summing the first few terms of a series. Any phrase like 'give your answer as an exact fraction in its simplest form' is a direct flag.

Motivation — Why this works

A single fraction is the canonical exact form, and mark schemes typically reserve a final accuracy mark for it. The lowest common denominator is found by taking the highest power of each prime appearing in any denominator (here 3^6 = 729 dominates 3^0 and 3^3). Converting every term to that LCD lets the numerators be added as ordinary integers, after which a divisibility check confirms the fraction is in lowest terms.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the terms share a prime base in their denominators (1, 27, 729 are all powers of 3), so a clean LCD exists.
  2. I rule out converting to decimals — that would forfeit the 'exact fraction' mark — and I rule out pairwise addition, which is wasteful when one denominator already dominates.
  3. I commit to using the largest denominator, 729 = 3^6, as the LCD because every other denominator divides it.
  4. First concrete step: rewrite each term with denominator 729 by multiplying numerator and denominator by the appropriate power of 3.
  5. Tactically, I keep signs explicit during the integer addition, then test divisibility (digit sum for 3, parity for 2) to check whether the result reduces.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the LCD: 729 = 3^6 dominates 1 and 27 = 3^3.
  2. Rewrite each term: 1 = 729/729; 4/27 = 108/729; −16/729 unchanged.
  3. Add numerators: 729 + 108 − 16 = 821.
  4. Check reduction: digit sum of 821 is 11, not divisible by 3, so 821/729 is already in lowest terms.
  5. State the final answer as 821/729.

Worked Micro-Example

Combine $1 + \tfrac{4}{27} - \tfrac{16}{729}$. LCD $= 729$, so $1 = \tfrac{729}{729}$ and $\tfrac{4}{27} = \tfrac{108}{729}$. Sum: $\tfrac{729+108-16}{729} = \tfrac{821}{729}$. Since $8+2+1=11$ is not divisible by $3$, the fraction is already in simplest form.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Binomial expansion evaluated at x = 1/k giving terms in 1/k, 1/k^2, 1/k^3
Use k^n (highest power) as LCD; multiply earlier terms by k^{n-i}/k^{n-i}.
Sum of partial fractions A/(x−a) + B/(x−b) reassembled into a single rational function
LCD becomes the product (x−a)(x−b); combine numerators algebraically rather than numerically.
First few terms of a geometric series with rational ratio
LCD is the highest power of the ratio's denominator appearing.
Mixed denominators with distinct primes, e.g. 1/6 + 1/10
LCD is the product of primes to their highest powers (lcm), not just the largest denominator.
Question demands 'exact form' rather than 'fraction'
A single fraction is still the safest presentation; surds or π may be retained in the numerator.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the largest denominator as the LCD when denominators share no common base (e.g. 1/6 and 1/10 — LCD is 30, not 10).
  • Believing that a fraction with a non-prime denominator must always reduce, and 'simplifying' incorrectly.
  • Reading 'exact' as permitting a decimal approximation.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slip when scaling numerators (e.g. 4/27 × 27 = 108 misread as 104 or 112).
  • Sign error when one of the terms is negative, especially after an alternating binomial expansion.
  • Forgetting the final reduction check, leaving an answer like 1642/1458 instead of 821/729.
  • Writing the answer as a mixed number when the question expects an improper fraction (or vice versa).

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure scope: appears in C12/P2 binomial expansion 'use to estimate' parts, partial fractions in P3/P4, and series questions in FP1. No tools beyond integer arithmetic and a divisibility check are required.
