Coordinate Geometry

Recognise tangent intercept as a repeated root (perfect square)

Family: Discriminant Interpretation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when finding the intersection of a circle (or conic) with a coordinate axis or line known to be tangent. After substituting y = 0 (or x = 0, or the line equation) into the circle equation, you obtain a quadratic that should yield exactly one solution. Cues include: the problem already stated/established tangency, the centre's distance to the line equals the radius, or the resulting quadratic visibly factors as a perfect square such as x² − 6x + 9.

Motivation — Why this works

Geometric tangency between a line and a circle corresponds algebraically to the substituted quadratic having discriminant zero, i.e. a repeated root. Spotting the perfect-square form (x − a)² = 0 immediately gives the unique contact point x = a without invoking the quadratic formula. This also serves as a self-consistency check on any earlier parameter (e.g. the constant k that made the circle tangent to the axis).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the tangency context — the line/axis touches the circle at exactly one point, so expect one intersection.
  2. Rule out using the full quadratic formula or computing a non-zero discriminant: if tangency holds, the discriminant must be 0.
  3. Commit to looking for a perfect-square factorisation of the resulting quadratic, since b² = 4ac is forced.
  4. Substitute y = 0 (or x = 0) into the circle equation and simplify to a quadratic in one variable.
  5. Read off (x − a)² = 0 to obtain the contact point, then sanity-check that it lies directly above/below/beside the centre as the geometry of a horizontal/vertical tangent demands.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Substitute the axis/line equation into the circle equation to eliminate one variable.
  2. Simplify to a quadratic of the form x² + bx + c = 0 (or in y).
  3. Verify or expect discriminant = 0; rewrite as (x − h)² = 0 by completing the square or inspection.
  4. Take the unique root x = h; pair with the substituted value to give the tangent point.
  5. Check the point's position relative to the centre as a geometric consistency test.

Worked Micro-Example

Circle $(x-3)^2 + (y+4)^2 = 16$ is tangent to the x-axis. Setting $y=0$: $(x-3)^2 + 16 = 16 \Rightarrow (x-3)^2 = 0$. Hence the unique contact point is $R(3, 0)$, directly below the centre $(3,-4)$, consistent with radius $4$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Circle tangent to the y-axis
Set x = 0; the resulting quadratic in y is a perfect square (y − k)² = 0, giving the contact point (0, k).
Circle tangent to a non-axis line y = mx + c
Substitute the line into the circle, expand to a quadratic in x, and expect discriminant 0; factor as a perfect square to find the single point of contact.
Determining the value of a parameter k that makes the circle tangent
Impose discriminant = 0 on the substituted quadratic to solve for k, then factor to locate the tangent point.
Parabola or other conic tangent to a line (Further Pure context)
Same logic: substitute the line into the conic and require the resulting quadratic to be a perfect square.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the substitution result as a generic quadratic with two distinct roots and reporting two intercepts.
  • Believing tangency implies no intersection (confusing with discriminant < 0) rather than a repeated intersection.
  • Thinking the circle 'crosses' the axis at the tangent point and looking for a second intercept.

Pitfalls

  • Using the quadratic formula unnecessarily when (x − a)² is visible by inspection.
  • Sign errors when completing the square, e.g. writing (x + 3)² instead of (x − 3)² for x² − 6x + 9.
  • Forgetting to pair the x-value with the correct substituted y-value (or vice versa) when stating the contact point.
  • Failing to verify the contact point lies on the line of tangency, missing arithmetic slips.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics: circle equations, intersections of lines and circles, and the discriminant condition for tangency. No tools beyond completing the square and basic factorisation are required.
