Algebra & Functions

Take logs of exponential inequality, flip when base is between 0 and 1

Family: Log Inequality Solve Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The unknown sits in an exponent inside an inequality of the form $a^k < b$ (or $>$, $\le$, $\ge$) and the base $a$ satisfies $0 < a < 1$. Cues include fractional bases like $30/31$, decay factors, or expressions like $(0.97)^n$. The presence of an inequality (not an equation) is what makes the sign of $\ln a$ critical. Equivalent phrasings: 'find the smallest $n$ such that …', 'how many terms before the value drops below …'.

Motivation — Why this works

Taking $\ln$ of both sides is valid because $\ln$ is strictly increasing on $(0,\infty)$, so the direction of the inequality is preserved at this step. The exponent is brought down by the law $\ln(a^k) = k\ln a$, converting the exponential inequality into a linear one in $k$. However, isolating $k$ requires dividing by $\ln a$, and when $0 < a < 1$ this quantity is negative, which forces a sign-flip. Recognising the sign of $\ln a$ is the entire point of the move.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice $k$ is in the exponent, so logs are the natural tool to extract it.
  2. Rule out trial-and-error or repeated multiplication; logs give a closed-form bound.
  3. Commit because $\ln$ is monotonic increasing, so applying $\ln$ keeps the inequality intact.
  4. Bridge: rewrite $k\ln a < \ln b$ and prepare to divide by $\ln a$.
  5. Tactical: check the sign of $\ln a$. Since $0 < a < 1$, $\ln a < 0$, so dividing reverses the inequality.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Apply $\ln$ to both sides: $k\ln a < \ln b$ (direction preserved).
  2. Identify sign: since $0 < a < 1$, $\ln a < 0$.
  3. Divide by $\ln a$ and reverse the inequality: $k > \dfrac{\ln b}{\ln a}$.
  4. Evaluate the right-hand side numerically with full calculator precision.
  5. Take the smallest integer satisfying the strict inequality (ceiling if non-integer).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $(30/31)^k < 271/961$. Apply $\ln$: $k\ln(30/31) < \ln(271/961)$. Since $\ln(30/31) \approx -0.032789 < 0$, dividing flips: $k > \dfrac{\ln(271/961)}{\ln(30/31)} \approx \dfrac{-1.2657}{-0.032789} \approx 38.60$. Smallest integer: $k = 39$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Base $a > 1$, e.g. compound growth $(1.05)^n > 2$
$\ln a > 0$, so dividing preserves direction: $n > \ln 2/\ln 1.05$.
Geometric series tail bound, e.g. find smallest $n$ with $ar^{n-1} < \varepsilon$ where $|r|<1$
Same flip applies after isolating $r^{n-1}$ and taking logs.
Decay model $A_0 e^{-\lambda t} < c$ with $\lambda > 0$
Dividing by $-\lambda$ flips, giving $t > \tfrac{1}{\lambda}\ln(A_0/c)$.
Reverse inequality $a^k > b$ with $0 < a < 1$
Same logic; sign-flip yields $k < \ln b/\ln a$, so seek the largest integer.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $\ln(30/31)$ as positive and concluding $k < 38.60$, giving the wrong integer.
  • Reading the problem as an equation and ignoring inequality direction entirely.
  • Confusing 'smallest $k$' with 'largest $k$' once the direction has flipped.

Pitfalls

  • Rounding $\ln$ values too early; the boundary value can shift across an integer.
  • Mixing $\ln$ and $\log_{10}$ in the same calculation due to calculator-mode error.
  • Forgetting to convert back to the originally requested quantity (e.g. $N = 20 + k$).
  • Applying $\ln$ to a negative or zero side without checking positivity first.
