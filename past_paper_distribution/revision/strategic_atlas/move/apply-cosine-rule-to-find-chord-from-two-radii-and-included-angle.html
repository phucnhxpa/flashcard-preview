Trigonometry

Apply cosine rule to find chord from two radii and included angle

Family: Cosine Rule Chord Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a chord length is needed inside a circle and the two radii to its endpoints, together with the angle they subtend at the centre, are known or computable. Typical cues: a sector/segment perimeter problem asking for the straight edge across the arc, an isosceles triangle with two equal sides being radii, or an angle at the centre given (often in radians) with the chord required. Equivalent phrasings include 'find the length of the chord AB' or 'find the length of the third side of triangle OAB' where O is the centre.

Motivation — Why this works

The cosine rule c² = a² + b² − 2ab cos C is the direct identity for two sides and the included angle, which is exactly the data available in triangle (centre, endpoint, endpoint). Because both sides equal r, it simplifies to c² = 2r²(1 − cos θ), giving the chord without auxiliary constructions. It avoids introducing perpendiculars or coordinates and works for any θ ∈ (0, 2π), including obtuse angles where cos θ < 0.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I need a straight-line distance between two points that both lie on the circle, and I already know the radius and the central angle.
  2. Sine rule is ruled out because I don't have an opposite angle paired with a known side; dropping a perpendicular from the centre works but adds an unnecessary step.
  3. Two sides + included angle is the canonical cosine-rule signature, so I commit to c² = a² + b² − 2ab cos C with a = b = r and C = θ.
  4. Substitute the radii and the central angle, being careful about whether θ is acute, obtuse, or expressed as π − (something).
  5. Compute cos θ with the calculator in radian mode (or degrees, matching the problem), then take the positive square root for the chord length.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the triangle (centre O, endpoints B, D) with OB = OD = r and ∠BOD = θ.
  2. Write BD² = r² + r² − 2r² cos θ = 2r²(1 − cos θ).
  3. Substitute the numerical values of r and θ, ensuring radian/degree mode matches.
  4. Evaluate cos θ (watch sign if θ obtuse, e.g. cos(π − α) = −cos α).
  5. Take BD = √(BD²), keeping the positive root, and round to the required accuracy.

Worked Micro-Example

In a circle centred at $O$ with radius $3$, points $B$ and $D$ satisfy $\angle BOD = \pi - 1.4$. Then $BD^2 = 3^2 + 3^2 - 2(3)(3)\cos(\pi - 1.4) = 18 + 18\cos(1.4) \approx 18 + 3.0595 = 21.0595$. Hence $BD \approx \sqrt{21.0595} \approx 4.589\text{ m}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Sector perimeter problem where the chord closes a segment
Use the same cosine rule on the two radii bounding the sector; add to the arc length rθ for the segment perimeter.
Central angle given as π − α or 2π − α (reflex situation)
Apply cos(π − α) = −cos α or cos(2π − α) = cos α before substituting; sign handling is the only change.
Equivalent half-angle form for chord
Use BD = 2r sin(θ/2), derived from 2r²(1 − cos θ) via the identity 1 − cos θ = 2 sin²(θ/2).
Triangle with two equal sides not radii (isosceles, included angle known)
Same cosine-rule template c² = 2a²(1 − cos C); only the side label changes.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating ∠BOD as the angle at B or D rather than at the centre, then misapplying the cosine rule.
  • Assuming the triangle is right-angled because the radii look perpendicular in the diagram, and using Pythagoras instead.
  • Reading the given angle as the arc measure in degrees when the problem is stated in radians (or vice versa).

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting that cos θ is negative when θ is obtuse, e.g. computing 18 − 18 cos(π − 1.4) as 18 − (positive) instead of 18 + (positive).
  • Mixing degree and radian modes on the calculator when evaluating cos θ.
  • Dropping the square root and quoting BD² as the chord length.
  • Algebra slip in 2r²(1 − cos θ): writing 2r² − cos θ or r²(1 − 2 cos θ).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics: cosine rule (P2) and radian-measure circle work including arc length and sector area (P2). No content beyond standard A-level trigonometry is required.
