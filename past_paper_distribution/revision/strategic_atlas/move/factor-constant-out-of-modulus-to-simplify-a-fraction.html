Factor constant out of modulus to simplify a fraction

Family: Modulus Factoring Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a final answer contains a modulus expression of the form |ax + b| (or |ak + c|) where a and b share a common factor, especially when that factor also shares a divisor with a denominator. Typical cues: expressions like |16k − 8|/6, |6x − 12|/9, or |10t + 4|/8 appearing as a length, area, volume, or distance. The move is also indicated when the mark scheme rewards a 'simplified' or 'fully reduced' final form.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity |ab| = |a||b| lets a positive constant be pulled outside a modulus cleanly: |c·f(x)| = c|f(x)| when c > 0. Once extracted, the constant can cancel with factors in a numerator or denominator, reducing the fraction to lowest terms. This produces a tidier expression that is easier to interpret (e.g., locating where the quantity is zero) and aligns with examiner expectations for fully simplified answers.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the integers inside the modulus share a common factor (here 16 and 8 share 8).
  2. Rule out cancelling terms across the modulus directly with the denominator, since modulus is not distributive over addition/subtraction in that way.
  3. Commit to factoring because |c·g| = c|g| for c > 0 is a standard, safe identity, and the resulting constant will simplify with the denominator.
  4. Pull the positive constant outside: |16k − 8| = |8(2k − 1)| = 8|2k − 1|.
  5. Reduce the resulting numerical fraction (8/6 → 4/3) and present the answer in cleanest form, optionally noting where the modulus equals zero.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the largest positive common factor c of the coefficients inside the modulus.
  2. Rewrite |ax + b| = |c(a'x + b')| = c|a'x + b'| (valid because c > 0).
  3. Substitute back into the larger expression and reduce any numerical fraction with the denominator.
  4. Check the result is in simplest integer/coprime form.

Worked Micro-Example

Simplify $V = \dfrac{|16k - 8|}{6}$. Factor: $|16k - 8| = |8(2k - 1)| = 8|2k - 1|$. Then $V = \dfrac{8|2k - 1|}{6} = \dfrac{4|2k - 1|}{3}$, which is zero exactly when $k = \tfrac{1}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Distance/length expression like $\dfrac{|6x - 12|}{9}$
Factor $|6x-12| = 6|x-2|$ then reduce $6/9$ to $2/3$, giving $\tfrac{2}{3}|x-2|$.
Modulus inside an area formula, e.g. $A = \tfrac{1}{2}|10t + 4|$
Factor $|10t+4| = 2|5t+2|$, so $A = |5t+2|$.
Negative leading coefficient, e.g. $|-6x + 9|$
Factor the positive 3: $|-6x+9| = 3|-2x+3| = 3|2x-3|$, since $|-u|=|u|$.
Modulus in a vector magnitude or scalar triple product giving $\tfrac{|6\lambda - 3|}{2}$
Factor $|6\lambda-3| = 3|2\lambda-1|$, then $\tfrac{3}{2}|2\lambda-1|$.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to cancel a coefficient inside the modulus directly with the denominator without first factoring (e.g. cancelling the 16 with the 6).
  • Treating |16k − 8| as though it splits as |16k| − |8|.
  • Believing the constant must be removed by squaring or by case analysis when a simple factorisation suffices.

Pitfalls

  • Pulling out a negative factor without taking its absolute value: |−3x| = 3|x|, not −3|x|.
  • Forgetting to reduce the resulting numerical fraction after extraction (leaving 8/6 instead of 4/3).
  • Dropping the modulus brackets around the remaining linear expression once the constant is outside.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the standard property |ab| = |a||b| for real numbers, well within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (modulus function in P2/P3) and applicable wherever modulus expressions appear in mechanics or further pure contexts.
