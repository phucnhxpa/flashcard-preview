Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a term in the expression is a product of two factors that each depend on x — typically one explicit (like x, x², or e^{kx}) and one implicit (y, where y = y(x)), or two distinct explicit functions of x (like x·e^{2x} or x²·ln x). Visual cues: terms such as xy, 4xy, x²y, ye^{-2x}, xe^{2x}, x sin y written as a single juxtaposed product with no obvious composition structure. Equivalently: 'two variable things multiplied', or 'I cannot rewrite this as a single composite function f(g(x))'. This is the most error-prone differentiation step in implicit differentiation, ODE manipulation, and Maclaurin/Taylor series construction.

Motivation — Why this works

The product rule (uv)' = u'v + uv' is the only correct way to differentiate a product of two non-constant functions; it generates two separate contributions, one from each factor varying. Treating either factor as constant collapses the derivative to a single term and silently loses a contribution. In implicit differentiation it is exactly this rule that creates both the 'free' algebraic term and the dy/dx term needed to later isolate dy/dx. The underlying identity is the Leibniz product rule, applied with the chain rule whenever a factor like y or e^{kx} carries its own internal differentiation.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the term has the shape (factor 1)·(factor 2) where both factors change with x — e.g. 4xy has the explicit factor 4x and the implicit factor y(x).
  2. Rule out alternatives: it is not a composition f(g(x)), so chain rule alone is insufficient; it is not a quotient, so the quotient rule is irrelevant; expanding/rewriting as a single power doesn't apply.
  3. Commit to the product rule because both factors are x-dependent, so both must be differentiated and both contributions retained.
  4. Choose the split u = (simpler/explicit factor), v = (factor that itself needs a chain rule), so that u' is trivial and v' is a single clean chain-rule application.
  5. Compute u' and v' separately — remembering that v' = dy/dx if v = y, and v' = k·e^{kx} if v = e^{kx} (chain rule on the exponent).
  6. Assemble (uv)' = u'v + uv', writing both terms explicitly before substituting back into the larger equation, so neither is dropped during algebra.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two x-dependent factors and label them u and v.
  2. Compute u' = du/dx (often a constant or simple power).
  3. Compute v' = dv/dx, applying the chain rule if v itself is composite (e.g. d/dx(e^{-2x}) = −2e^{-2x}, d/dx(y) = dy/dx, d/dx(y³) = 3y²·dy/dx).
  4. Write d/dx(uv) = u'v + uv', keeping both terms intact.
  5. Substitute the expanded derivative back into the full differentiated equation, then collect dy/dx terms in the next step.

Worked Micro-Example

Differentiate $4xy$ implicitly with respect to $x$. Set $u=4x$, $v=y$, so $u'=4$, $v'=\tfrac{dy}{dx}$. Then $\tfrac{d}{dx}(4xy) = 4\cdot y + 4x\cdot\tfrac{dy}{dx} = 4y + 4x\tfrac{dy}{dx}$. Both terms must survive into the next line.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Mixed term with a power of y, e.g. x²y³
Combine product rule with chain rule on y³: d/dx(x²y³) = 2xy³ + x²·3y²·(dy/dx).
Implicit factor times exponential, e.g. ye^{-2x}
u = y, v = e^{-2x}; d/dx = e^{-2x}(dy/dx) − 2ye^{-2x}. Don't forget the −2 from chain rule on the exponent.
Explicit polynomial times transcendental, e.g. xe^{2x} or x²ln x
Same product rule structure with no implicit dy/dx; chain rule still needed on e^{2x}: dy/dx = e^{2x} + 2xe^{2x}.
Differentiating an ODE term like xy to build a Maclaurin series
d/dx(xy) = y + x(dy/dx); the y term is the one most often forgotten, breaking all subsequent derivatives.
Product with a function of y, e.g. x·sin y
d/dx(x sin y) = sin y + x cos y·(dy/dx); chain rule needed inside the v' step because sin y is composite in x.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading xe^{2x} as a composition and applying only the chain rule, giving 2xe^{2x} instead of e^{2x} + 2xe^{2x}.
  • Reading 4xy as 'a constant times y' (treating x as fixed), producing only 4·dy/dx.
  • Reading 4xy as 'a constant times x' (treating y as fixed), producing only 4y.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping one of the two terms — most commonly the 'free' term that comes from differentiating the explicit factor (e.g. losing the 4y in d/dx(4xy)).
  • Forgetting the chain-rule factor when differentiating e^{kx} (writing e^{-2x} instead of −2e^{-2x}) or when differentiating y^n (writing ny^{n−1} instead of ny^{n−1}·dy/dx).
  • Applying product rule but forgetting to attach dy/dx to the v' term when v = y.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P2/P3 differentiation) and Further Pure (FP differential equations, Maclaurin series). The product rule is given in the formula booklet under 'Differentiation'. Stay with single-variable y = y(x); do not invoke partial derivatives or multivariable Leibniz extensions, which are out of scope.
