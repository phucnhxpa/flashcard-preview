Algebra & Functions

Reflect intercepts in y = x to sketch the inverse graph

Family: Graphical Inverse Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks for a sketch of both $f$ and $f^{-1}$ on the same axes with labelled intercepts/key points, but does NOT demand an explicit formula for $f^{-1}$. Also triggered when inverting $f$ algebraically would be messy (restricted-domain quadratic, cubic, exponential/log pair). Phrases like 'on the same diagram, sketch $y = f(x)$ and $y = f^{-1}(x)$, indicating clearly the coordinates of any points where the curves meet the axes' are strong cues.

Motivation — Why this works

The graph of $y = f^{-1}(x)$ is the reflection of $y = f(x)$ in the line $y = x$, since $(a,b) \in f \iff (b,a) \in f^{-1}$. Reflecting in $y = x$ swaps coordinates, so axis intercepts of $f$ become axis intercepts of $f^{-1}$ with $x$ and $y$ exchanged. This bypasses any algebraic inversion and exploits the fact that domain/range also swap, which fixes the correct branch automatically.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question only requires a sketch with labelled intercepts, not an explicit $f^{-1}(x)$.
  2. Rule out algebraic inversion (solving $x = f(y)$ for $y$) as unnecessary work, especially with a restricted domain.
  3. Commit to the reflection-in-$y=x$ method because intercepts of $f$ map directly to intercepts of $f^{-1}$ by swapping coordinates.
  4. Find the intercepts of $f$ first, then swap each $(a,b) \to (b,a)$ to get the intercepts of $f^{-1}$.
  5. Use the swapped domain/range to choose the correct branch and orientation when sketching $f^{-1}$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the intercepts of $y = f(x)$: set $x=0$ for the $y$-intercept and $y=0$ for the $x$-intercept(s).
  2. Reflect each intercept in $y = x$ by swapping coordinates: $(0,b) \to (b,0)$ and $(a,0) \to (0,a)$.
  3. State the domain and range of $f^{-1}$ as range and domain of $f$ respectively, to determine the correct branch.
  4. Sketch $f$ on its restricted domain through its intercepts, then sketch $f^{-1}$ as its mirror image through the swapped intercepts.
  5. Optionally draw $y = x$ as a dashed line to make the symmetry visible and label all four intercepts.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $g(x) = 5 - 2x^2$, $x \ge 0$. Intercepts: $(0,5)$ and $(\sqrt{5/2},0)$. Reflecting in $y=x$ gives $g^{-1}$ intercepts $(5,0)$ and $(0,\sqrt{5/2})$. Sketch $g$ as the right half of a downward parabola from $(0,5)$ to $(\sqrt{5/2},0)$; sketch $g^{-1}$ as its mirror image from $(5,0)$ rising to $(0,\sqrt{5/2})$, with domain $x \le 5$ and range $y \ge 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Exponential/logarithm pair, e.g. $f(x)=e^x+k$ and $f^{-1}(x)=\ln(x-k)$
Reflect the $y$-intercept $(0,1+k)$ to the $x$-intercept $(1+k,0)$ of $f^{-1}$; reflect the horizontal asymptote $y=k$ to the vertical asymptote $x=k$.
Restricted-domain cubic or piecewise function with awkward inverse formula
Plot a few key points on $f$ (endpoints, intercepts, turning point on the boundary) and reflect them rather than inverting algebraically.
Modulus or piecewise-linear function on a restricted domain
Reflect each linear piece's endpoints in $y=x$ and join with straight segments; the kink reflects to a kink.
Curves meeting at a point on $y=x$
Solve $f(x)=x$ to find the intersection of $f$ and $f^{-1}$, since any fixed point of $f$ lies on $y=x$ and is shared by both graphs.

Common Misreadings

  • Reflecting in the $x$-axis or $y$-axis instead of in $y = x$.
  • Sketching $f^{-1}$ for the full unrestricted function rather than the branch consistent with the restricted domain of $f$.
  • Treating $f^{-1}(x)$ as $1/f(x)$ and trying to plot a reciprocal graph instead.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the domain restriction on $f$, so the wrong half of the parabola/curve is drawn and then reflected.
  • Mislabelling swapped intercepts, e.g. writing $\sqrt{2/5}$ instead of $\sqrt{5/2}$, or putting the swapped value on the wrong axis.
  • Not making the two curves visibly symmetric about $y=x$ in the sketch (e.g. crossing $y=x$ at different points).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 inverse-function work: domain/range swap, reflection in $y=x$, and sketches with labelled intercepts; no calculus or transformation theory beyond the syllabus is required.
