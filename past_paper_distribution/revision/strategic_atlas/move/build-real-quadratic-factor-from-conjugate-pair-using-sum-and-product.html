Trigger — When to use

Fires when a polynomial with real coefficients has a known complex root α = a + bi, so its conjugate ā = a − bi is automatically also a root, and you need a real quadratic factor to continue factorising (typically a cubic or quartic). Cues include phrases like 'show that … is a root' followed by 'find the other roots', or being given one complex root and asked to fully factorise. Also fires when matching coefficients against an unknown linear factor (z − k) requires a clean real quadratic to divide out.

Motivation — Why this works

If α and ā are both roots, then (z − α)(z − ā) expands to z² − (α + ā)z + αā. The sum α + ā = 2 Re(α) is real, and the product αā = |α|² = a² + b² is real and non-negative. So the quadratic has real coefficients automatically, and you never have to multiply two complex linear factors directly — you just read off 2a and a² + b².

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the given root is non-real and the polynomial has real coefficients, so the conjugate must also be a root.
  2. Rules out substituting α into f(z) = 0 to solve for unknown coefficients — workable but algebraically heavier than building a factor.
  3. Commits to forming the quadratic factor (z − α)(z − ā) because it will have real coefficients and divide cleanly into the cubic/quartic.
  4. Bridges to the shortcut: sum = 2 Re(α), product = |α|² = a² + b², avoiding any (a+bi)(a−bi) expansion by hand.
  5. Inside the move, decides whether to then use polynomial division or coefficient comparison to extract the remaining linear (or quadratic) factor.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the conjugate pair α = a + bi and ā = a − bi.
  2. Compute sum S = α + ā = 2a.
  3. Compute product P = αā = a² + b².
  4. Write the real quadratic factor as z² − Sz + P.
  5. Use this factor to divide the original polynomial (or match coefficients) to find remaining factors.

Worked Micro-Example

Given that 5 + 4i is a root of f(z) = z³ − 11z² + 51z − 41 (real coefficients), the conjugate 5 − 4i is also a root.\nSum: (5+4i) + (5−4i) = 10. Product: (5+4i)(5−4i) = 5² + 4² = 41.\nQuadratic factor: z² − 10z + 41.\nDivide: f(z) = (z² − 10z + 41)(z − 1), so the third root is z = 1.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Conjugate surd roots of a real polynomial, e.g. 3 + √2 and 3 − √2
Same sum/product idea: S = 6, P = 9 − 2 = 7, giving rational quadratic factor z² − 6z + 7.
Complex root given in modulus-argument form, e.g. 2 cis(π/3)
Either convert to Cartesian first, or use S = 2|α|cos(arg α) and P = |α|² directly.
Quartic with two known complex roots forming one conjugate pair
Build the real quadratic factor as above, then divide the quartic by it to leave a real quadratic whose roots are found by the formula.
Polynomial with unknown real coefficients p, q to determine
Form the quadratic factor from the conjugate pair, write f(z) = (z² − Sz + P)(z − k), expand, and compare coefficients to find p, q, k.

Common Misreadings

  • Substituting the complex root into f(z) = 0 and separating real/imaginary parts to solve — valid but much more laborious than the sum/product shortcut.
  • Trying to multiply (z − (a+bi))(z − (a−bi)) by full FOIL expansion and getting bogged down in i² terms instead of recognising the difference-of-squares pattern.
  • Assuming the conjugate is automatically a root even when the polynomial has non-real coefficients — the conjugate root theorem requires real coefficients.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error in the product: computing (a+bi)(a−bi) as a² − b² instead of a² + b² (forgetting (bi)² = −b²).
  • Sign error in the linear term: writing z² + Sz + P instead of z² − Sz + P.
  • Using just Re(α) instead of 2 Re(α) for the sum.
  • Forgetting to verify real coefficients before invoking the conjugate root theorem.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure content: conjugate root theorem for real-coefficient polynomials, factorisation of cubics and quartics over ℝ, and the identity (a+bi)(a−bi) = a² + b² = |a+bi|².
