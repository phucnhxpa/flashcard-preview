Algebra & Functions

Unify mixed integer and fractional terms over a common denominator

Family: Common Denominator Combination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires after an evaluation step (e.g. substituting a value into f'(x) or f(x), or simplifying a surd expression) leaves a sum of an integer and two or more fractions sharing a denominator. The instruction asks for a single exact fraction, often 'in the form (a + b√c)/d' or 'as a single fraction'. Recognise it whenever rational and surd terms coexist with mismatched denominators but a clear LCD is visible.

Motivation — Why this works

Addition of fractions requires a common denominator; rewriting the integer k as kd/d preserves value while aligning denominators. Once aligned, numerators combine linearly, and rational and surd parts stay separate because √c is irrational and cannot merge with rationals. This produces the compact exact form examiners expect and confirms the answer is fully simplified.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the expression mixes an integer with fractions over the same denominator (here, 4).
  2. Rule out simplifying the surd separately or rationalising — there is no surd in the denominator, only in a numerator.
  3. Commit to the common-denominator route because it directly yields the single-fraction form the mark scheme requires.
  4. Bridge: rewrite the integer as a fraction over the shared denominator (3 = 12/4).
  5. Inside the move, keep rational and surd numerator parts grouped separately so sign errors are visible: (12 − 17) − 3√2.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the common denominator d shared by the fractional terms.
  2. Rewrite each integer/whole term k as kd/d.
  3. Place all terms over d as a single fraction with combined numerator.
  4. Group like parts in the numerator: rationals together, surd terms together.
  5. Simplify the rational part arithmetically; leave surd term as is.
  6. Check for any common factor between the numerator coefficients and d; if none, the form is final.

Worked Micro-Example

Simplify $3 - \tfrac{3\sqrt{2}}{4} - \tfrac{17}{4}$.\\ Rewrite: $3 = \tfrac{12}{4}$, so the expression becomes $\tfrac{12}{4} - \tfrac{3\sqrt{2}}{4} - \tfrac{17}{4}$.\\ Combine: $\tfrac{12 - 3\sqrt{2} - 17}{4} = \tfrac{-5 - 3\sqrt{2}}{4}$.\\ No common factor among 5, 3, 4, so the final exact form is $\dfrac{-5 - 3\sqrt{2}}{4}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Evaluated expression has denominator 2, e.g. $5 + \tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} - \tfrac{7}{2}$
Rewrite 5 as 10/2; combine to $(3 + \sqrt{3})/2$.
Two different denominators, e.g. $\tfrac{a}{2} + \tfrac{b}{3}$
Use LCD 6: rewrite as $\tfrac{3a + 2b}{6}$.
Integration constant evaluation $F(b) - F(a)$ producing mixed surd/rational terms
Same routine after substitution; align over LCD before stating the exact value.
Binomial expansion coefficient like $1 + \tfrac{x}{2} - \tfrac{x^2}{8}$ evaluated at a value
Convert the leading 1 to 8/8 to merge into a single fraction.
Complex number in form $a + \tfrac{b\,\mathrm{i}}{d}$
Treat real and imaginary parts analogously to rational and surd parts; align denominators separately.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the surd term as if it can merge with the rationals (e.g. writing 12 − 3√2 − 17 = −8√2).
  • Believing the answer must be rationalised, when there is no surd denominator to clear.
  • Thinking a negative numerator means the form is wrong and forcing a sign flip.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when combining: 12 − 17 = −5, not +5 or −29.
  • Forgetting to convert the integer (writing $3 - \tfrac{3\sqrt{2} + 17}{4}$ then mishandling the 3).
  • Unnecessary factoring of −1 to write $-\tfrac{5 + 3\sqrt{2}}{4}$ when the mark scheme expects $\tfrac{-5 - 3\sqrt{2}}{4}$ (or vice versa) — match the requested form.
  • Cancelling a factor that only divides part of the numerator (e.g. dividing only the −5 or only the 3√2 by a common factor with 4).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure technique used throughout C12/P1–P4 when stating exact values from differentiation, integration, surd manipulation, or binomial evaluation; no content beyond GCSE-level fraction arithmetic and surd handling is invoked.
