Differentiation

Rewrite surds and reciprocals as index powers before applying the power rule

Family: Index Law Rewrite Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

The function to be differentiated contains terms that are NOT in the standard $ax^n$ form — typically surds like $\sqrt{x}$, $\sqrt[3]{x}$, $x\sqrt{x}$, or reciprocals like $1/x$, $a/x^k$, $1/\sqrt{x}$. Visually, look for radical signs, fraction bars with $x$ in the denominator, or mixed roots-and-fractions in a polynomial-style expression. Equivalent phrasings: 'Find $\frac{dy}{dx}$ where $y$ involves $\sqrt{x}$ and $1/x$', 'Differentiate $f(x) = \ldots$' with mixed surd/reciprocal terms. The move fires BEFORE any calculus is attempted.

Motivation — Why this works

The power rule $\frac{d}{dx}(ax^n) = anx^{n-1}$ applies directly only when each term is written as $ax^n$ with an explicit numerical exponent. By the index laws $\sqrt[k]{x} = x^{1/k}$ and $1/x^k = x^{-k}$, every surd or reciprocal term can be converted into this canonical form. This avoids invoking the quotient rule or special root-derivative formulas unnecessarily, makes the differentiation purely mechanical, and keeps the algebraic manipulation clearly separated from the calculus step — which dramatically reduces sign and coefficient errors.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the expression mixes ordinary polynomial terms (already in $ax^n$ form) with surds (e.g. $\sqrt{x}$, $\sqrt[3]{x}$) and/or reciprocals (e.g. $1/x$, $4/x^2$, $3/\sqrt{x}$).
  2. Rule out heavier tools: the quotient rule on $a/x^k$ is overkill when the numerator is constant, and there is no need for the chain rule on $\sqrt{x}$ alone — a rewrite suffices.
  3. Commit to a uniform strategy: convert EVERY non-standard term to $ax^n$ first, so a single application of the power rule handles the whole expression.
  4. Bridge to operation: apply the index laws — $\sqrt[k]{x} \to x^{1/k}$, $1/x^k \to x^{-k}$, $x\sqrt{x} \to x^{3/2}$, $a/(b\sqrt{x}) \to (a/b)x^{-1/2}$ — being careful to carry the original sign and coefficient through.
  5. Tactical check: rewrite the entire function on one line BEFORE differentiating, then differentiate term-by-term; this prevents mixing algebraic and calculus errors.
  6. After differentiating, optionally convert back to surd/fraction form if the question or mark scheme prefers that presentation.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify each non-standard term (surd or reciprocal) in the expression.
  2. Apply index laws: $\sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}$, $\sqrt[3]{x} = x^{1/3}$, $1/x = x^{-1}$, $1/x^2 = x^{-2}$, $1/\sqrt{x} = x^{-1/2}$, $x\sqrt{x} = x^{3/2}$.
  3. Rewrite the full expression in the form $\sum a_i x^{n_i}$, preserving all signs.
  4. Apply the power rule term-by-term: $\frac{d}{dx}(ax^n) = anx^{n-1}$.
  5. Simplify each new coefficient (e.g. $20 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2} = 10$, $\tfrac{5}{3} \cdot \tfrac{1}{3} = \tfrac{5}{9}$) and exponent.
  6. (Optional) Convert back to surd/fraction notation if required by the question.

Worked Micro-Example

Differentiate $y = 3x^2 - 20\sqrt{x} - \dfrac{2}{x} + 7$.\\ Rewrite: $y = 3x^2 - 20x^{1/2} - 2x^{-1} + 7$.\\ Apply power rule: $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 6x - 20\cdot\tfrac{1}{2}x^{-1/2} - 2\cdot(-1)x^{-2} = 6x - 10x^{-1/2} + 2x^{-2}$.\\ Equivalently: $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 6x - \dfrac{10}{\sqrt{x}} + \dfrac{2}{x^2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Higher-order roots, e.g. $\sqrt[3]{x}$ or $\sqrt[4]{x}$
Rewrite as $x^{1/3}$ or $x^{1/4}$ — same principle, fractional exponent denominator changes.
Reciprocal of a higher power, e.g. $1/x^3$
Rewrite as $x^{-3}$; the power rule then gives $-3x^{-4}$.
Mixed root-product, e.g. $5x\sqrt{x}$ or $3/(2\sqrt{x})$
Combine indices first: $5x\sqrt{x} = 5x^{3/2}$; $3/(2\sqrt{x}) = \tfrac{3}{2}x^{-1/2}$, then apply the power rule.
Integration instead of differentiation
Exactly the same rewrite step is needed before applying the reverse power rule $\int x^n\,dx = \tfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C$ (provided $n \neq -1$).
Second derivative required
Keep results in index form after the first derivative so the power rule can be applied a second time without re-converting.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to differentiate $\sqrt{x}$ directly and writing $\frac{d}{dx}(\sqrt{x}) = \frac{1}{2x}$ instead of $\frac{1}{2}x^{-1/2} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$.
  • Reaching for the quotient rule on a constant-over-monomial term like $4/x^2$, which is unnecessarily heavy and error-prone.
  • Treating $1/x$ as a special case requiring a logarithm — at IAL P-pure level it is simply $x^{-1}$ for differentiation by the power rule.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the negative sign when rewriting $1/x^k$ as $x^{-k}$ (writing $x^k$ by mistake).
  • Sign errors with subtracted reciprocals: $-2/x$ becomes $-2x^{-1}$, whose derivative is $-2 \cdot (-1) x^{-2} = +2x^{-2}$ — the double negative is a frequent error source.
  • Coefficient slips on fractional powers: $20 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2} = 10$ (not $20$); $\tfrac{5}{3} \cdot \tfrac{1}{3} = \tfrac{5}{9}$ (not $\tfrac{5}{3}$).
  • Off-by-one exponent error: after differentiating $x^{-2}$ writing $x^{-1}$ instead of $x^{-3}$.
  • Forgetting the derivative of a constant term is zero (e.g. dropping the $+7$ correctly but accidentally carrying it forward).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1/P2 introduce the power rule with fractional and negative indices; P3 reuses it). No formula booklet entry is required — both the index laws and the power rule are assumed knowledge. Avoid invoking $\frac{d}{dx}(\ln x) = 1/x$ here: at this stage $1/x$ is differentiated as $x^{-1}$ via the power rule, not via logarithms.
