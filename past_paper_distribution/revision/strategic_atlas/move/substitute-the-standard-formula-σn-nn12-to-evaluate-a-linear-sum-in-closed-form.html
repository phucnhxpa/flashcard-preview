Sequences & Series

Substitute the standard formula Σn = N(N+1)/2 to evaluate a linear sum in closed form

Family: Standard Series Substitution Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when, after factoring out constants, you are left with a sum of the form Σ_{n=1}^{N} n — i.e. the first N positive integers added together. Visual cues: the summand is just 'n' (or reduces to n after splitting Σ(an+b) into aΣn + bΣ1), the lower limit is n=1, and the upper limit is a specific integer N. Equivalent phrasings: 'sum of first N natural numbers', 'arithmetic progression with a=1, d=1, last term N'. If the summand is more complex (n², n³, or starts at n≠1), this trigger does not fire directly — split or shift first.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity Σ_{n=1}^{N} n = N(N+1)/2 collapses an N-term addition into a single multiplication, which is essential for any closed-form answer. It is the simplest case of the AP sum formula S_N = N/2·(a₁ + a_N) with a₁=1, a_N=N. Recognising this standard result is faster than re-deriving via S_N = N/2·(2a+(N−1)d), and it is the form most often expected in exam mark schemes. Once Σn is evaluated, multiplying back any factored-out constant completes the calculation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the summation has been reduced (after splitting/factoring) to Σ_{n=1}^{N} n — a pure sum of consecutive integers from 1 to N.
  2. Rule out direct enumeration (too many terms), and rule out the heavier general AP formula S_N = N/2·(2a+(N−1)d) — it works but adds substitution overhead when a=d=1.
  3. Commit to the standard result Σn = N(N+1)/2 because it is the cleanest, is in the formula booklet, and matches the expected exam method.
  4. Bridge: substitute the upper limit directly into N(N+1)/2; do NOT use N+1 or N−1 as the count of terms — the upper limit IS N when starting at n=1.
  5. Tactical check: after computing N(N+1)/2, remember to multiply back by any constant that was factored out earlier (e.g. the 5 in Σ5n, or the k in Σkn).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Reduce the original summation to the form c · Σ_{n=1}^{N} n by factoring out constants and/or splitting Σ(an+b) = aΣn + bΣ1.
  2. Identify N as the upper limit of the sum.
  3. Apply Σ_{n=1}^{N} n = N(N+1)/2.
  4. Compute N(N+1)/2 carefully (often the arithmetic step that costs marks).
  5. Multiply by the factored-out constant c to obtain the final closed-form value.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\sum_{n=1}^{59} 5n$. Factor: $5\sum_{n=1}^{59} n$. Apply standard result: $\sum_{n=1}^{59} n = \tfrac{59 \cdot 60}{2} = 1770$. Multiply: $5 \times 1770 = 8850$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Sum starts at n=k>1, e.g. Σ_{n=5}^{50} n
Use Σ_{n=1}^{50} n − Σ_{n=1}^{4} n = 50·51/2 − 4·5/2, i.e. subtract the unwanted initial segment.
Summand is an+b with a≠1 or b≠0
Split: Σ(an+b) = aΣn + bN, then apply Σn = N(N+1)/2 and Σ1 = N.
Summand is n² or n³
Use the analogous standard formulae Σn² = N(N+1)(2N+1)/6 or Σn³ = [N(N+1)/2]² — same substitution strategy, different identity.
AP with general first term a and common difference d
Use S_N = N/2·(2a+(N−1)d) directly rather than reducing to Σn.

Common Misreadings

  • Mistaking Σn (arithmetic) for Σaⁿ (geometric) when both appear in the same chapter and applying the wrong formula.
  • Treating an upper limit such as 59 as 'the number of terms minus 1' rather than as N itself.

Pitfalls

  • Off-by-one error: using N=60 (or N=49 for a 1-to-50 sum) by confusing the upper limit with 'number of terms+1'.
  • Arithmetic slips in N(N+1)/2 — e.g. 59×60/2 = 1770 or 50×51/2 = 1275 are common error spots.
  • Forgetting to multiply by the constant that was factored out at the start.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 (arithmetic series) and FP1 (Σn, Σn², Σn³ standard results, listed in the formula booklet). Avoid invoking generating functions or induction-style derivations in a routine evaluation — the formula is quoted directly.
