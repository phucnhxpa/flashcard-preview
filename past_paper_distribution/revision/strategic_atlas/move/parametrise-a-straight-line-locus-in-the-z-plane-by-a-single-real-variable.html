Complex Numbers

Parametrise a straight-line locus in the z-plane by a single real variable

Family: Locus Parametrisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question fixes z to a straight line in the Argand diagram (e.g. the imaginary axis Re(z)=0, the real axis Im(z)=0, or a vertical/horizontal line Re(z)=k or Im(z)=c) and asks for the image under a Möbius transformation w = f(z). Cues include phrases like 'z lies on the imaginary axis', 'z = iy where y is real', or 'find the locus of w as z varies on this line'. The aim is to reduce the two real degrees of freedom in z to one, so w can be studied as a function of a single real parameter.

Motivation — Why this works

A straight line in the z-plane is intrinsically one-dimensional, so it can be encoded by exactly one real parameter. Writing z = iy (or the analogous form for the line concerned) turns w = f(z) into a parametric pair (u(y), v(y)), which is exactly the setup needed to eliminate y and recover a Cartesian relation between u and v. Choosing the parameter to match the geometry of the line (rather than retaining z = x+iy with a side-condition) keeps the algebra minimal and the excluded points easy to track.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the locus is a straight line characterised by a single linear condition on Re(z) or Im(z), e.g. Re(z) = 0 here.
  2. Rule out polar form z = r e^{iθ}: it would force θ = ±π/2 and split the line into two rays, doubling the casework.
  3. Commit to the Cartesian form aligned with the line: z = iy with y ∈ ℝ captures the whole imaginary axis as one parameter.
  4. Bridge: substitute z = iy into w = f(z) so that w becomes a single complex-valued function of the real variable y.
  5. Tactically, identify and exclude any y that makes the denominator of f vanish (here y = 1 since z = i is the pole), and keep y real throughout so that later separating w into u + iv is straightforward.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the linear condition defining the locus (e.g. Re(z) = 0).
  2. Choose the matching parametrisation: z = iy with y ∈ ℝ for the imaginary axis.
  3. State the excluded values of y arising from poles of f or other domain restrictions (e.g. y ≠ 1 if z = i is a pole).
  4. Substitute z = iy into w = f(z), ready to be simplified into u(y) + i v(y).

Worked Micro-Example

Let $w = \dfrac{z+1}{z-i}$ and let $z$ lie on the imaginary axis. Set $z = iy$, $y \in \mathbb{R}$, $y \neq 1$. Then $w = \dfrac{iy+1}{iy-i} = \dfrac{1+iy}{i(y-1)}$, giving a single-parameter expression in $y$ ready to split into real and imaginary parts.

Variants — Same move, different surface

z lies on the real axis (Im(z) = 0)
Parametrise z = x with x ∈ ℝ, excluding any x that is a pole of f.
z lies on a vertical line Re(z) = k
Parametrise z = k + iy with y ∈ ℝ, excluding y values causing poles.
z lies on a horizontal line Im(z) = c
Parametrise z = x + ic with x ∈ ℝ, excluding x values causing poles.
z lies on the circle |z| = r
Switch family: parametrise z = r(cos θ + i sin θ) with θ ∈ [0, 2π), excluding θ corresponding to any pole on the circle.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'imaginary axis' as |z| = something rather than as Re(z) = 0.
  • Keeping z = x + iy generic and only imposing x = 0 at the end, which inflates the algebra.
  • Confusing the imaginary axis (z = iy) with the line y = 1 or some other vertical line.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to exclude the parameter value that makes the denominator zero (here y = 1).
  • Dropping the condition 'y ∈ ℝ' so that later real/imaginary separation becomes invalid.
  • Writing z = iy but then accidentally treating y as complex when simplifying.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard FP Further Pure technique for loci under Möbius transformations in the Edexcel IAL Further Maths syllabus; uses only Argand-plane parametrisation and basic complex arithmetic.
