Vectors

Equate parametric position vectors componentwise to test 3D line intersection

Family: Parametric Intersection Test Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

Two lines in 3D are given in vector parametric form r₁ = a₁ + λd₁ and r₂ = a₂ + μd₂ with INDEPENDENT parameters, and the question asks to (a) show they intersect, (b) find the point of intersection, or (c) find an unknown constant given that they intersect. Phrasings include: 'show that l₁ and l₂ meet', 'find the point of intersection', 'given the lines intersect, find p', or 'determine whether the lines are skew'. The structural cue is two parametric lines side-by-side with different Greek letters for parameters.

Motivation — Why this works

If a common point exists on both lines, there must exist specific λ and μ for which r₁(λ) = r₂(μ). Equating the i-, j-, k-components (using the orthogonality/independence of the standard basis) converts one vector equation into three independent scalar equations in two unknowns. This is an overdetermined system: solving any two equations pins down λ and μ, and substituting into the third acts as a consistency check that distinguishes intersecting lines from skew lines. This componentwise route is the only direct algebraic test for 3D line intersection at A-level.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question involves two 3D lines in vector parametric form — in 3D they may be parallel, intersecting, or skew, so simple inspection won't do.
  2. Rule out alternatives: equating direction vectors only tests parallelism; substituting one parameter into the other's equation is meaningless because the parameters are independent labels.
  3. Commit to setting r₁(λ) = r₂(μ): a common point requires the position vectors to coincide for some specific (λ, μ) pair.
  4. Critical tactical decision: keep the two parameters DIFFERENT (λ for l₁, μ for l₂). Using one letter for both falsely assumes the meeting point is at 'equal parameter values'.
  5. Bridge to algebra: write each line in column/component form, then equate i-, j-, k-components separately to produce three labelled scalar equations (i), (ii), (iii).
  6. Plan the solve: pick the two simplest equations to solve for λ and μ, then substitute both values into the third equation. If it balances, the lines intersect (and you can compute the meeting point); if not, they are skew. If the question contains an unknown constant p, the third equation becomes the one that determines p.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write l₁: r = a₁ + λd₁ and l₂: r = a₂ + μd₂ in component (column-vector) form, using DIFFERENT parameters λ and μ.
  2. Set r₁ = r₂ as a single vector equation, then split into three scalar equations by equating i-, j-, k-components: label them (i), (ii), (iii).
  3. Choose the two equations that look algebraically cleanest (often those without the unknown constant) and solve simultaneously for λ and μ.
  4. Substitute the obtained λ, μ into the third equation: if it is satisfied, the lines intersect; if not, they are skew (state this explicitly for full marks).
  5. If intersection is confirmed, substitute λ into r₁ (or μ into r₂ as a double-check) to obtain the position vector of the point of intersection.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $l_1: \mathbf{r}=(1,0,-1)+\lambda(1,2,3)$ and $l_2: \mathbf{r}=(0,1,3)+\mu(2,1,-1)$. Equate components: (i) $1+\lambda=2\mu$, (ii) $2\lambda=1+\mu$, (iii) $-1+3\lambda=3-\mu$. From (i),(ii): $\lambda=1$, $\mu=1$. Check (iii): $-1+3=2$ and $3-1=2$ ✓. Lines meet at $(2,2,2)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line meets a plane given in Cartesian form ax+by+cz=d
Substitute the parametric components of the line directly into the plane equation — yields a single equation in one parameter, always solvable unless the line is parallel to the plane.
Question contains an unknown constant p in one line and states the lines DO intersect
Solve two of the three equations (those free of p) for λ and μ, then substitute into the equation containing p to determine p.
Two lines given in Cartesian symmetric form
Convert each to parametric form first by introducing parameters λ, μ, then proceed identically.
Two lines in 2D
Same technique gives two equations in two unknowns — always solvable unless parallel; no consistency check needed since 2D lines cannot be skew.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 'show they intersect' as 'show they are not parallel' and only comparing direction vectors.
  • Equating direction vectors instead of position vectors — this tests parallelism, not intersection.
  • Treating the question as if one parameter governs both lines.

Pitfalls

  • Using the same parameter letter (e.g. λ) for both lines — this falsely constrains the system and is a fatal conceptual error.
  • Solving only two of the three component equations and declaring intersection without verifying the third — skew lines satisfy two equations but fail the third.
  • Sign errors when rearranging to standard form, particularly with negative direction-vector components.
  • Forgetting to state the conclusion explicitly ('the third equation is satisfied, hence the lines intersect') — examiners require this.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics P3/P4 (Vectors). Uses vector equation of a line r = a + λd from the formula booklet. Avoid invoking cross-product or scalar-triple-product skew-line tests beyond Further Pure scope; the componentwise equate-and-verify method is the expected A-level technique.
