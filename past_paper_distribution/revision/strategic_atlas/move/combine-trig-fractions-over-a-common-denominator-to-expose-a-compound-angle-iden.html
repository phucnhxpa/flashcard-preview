Trigonometry

Combine trig fractions over a common denominator to expose a compound-angle identity

Family: Fraction Combination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The expression is a difference (or sum) of two fractions whose denominators are different trig functions, e.g. cos 2x/sin x − sin 2x/cos x, or cot A − cot B written as cosA/sinA − cosB/sinB. The target is a single compact trig expression such as cosec x, sec x, or sin(A−B)/(sinA sinB). No independent simplification of either fraction is possible, so structural progress requires merging them first.

Motivation — Why this works

Once combined over the LCD, the numerator becomes a linear combination of products like cosA cosB − sinA sinB or sinA cosB − cosA sinB, which are exactly the compound-angle expansions of cos(A+B) and sin(A−B). The denominator sin x cos x conveniently equals ½ sin 2x, often matching a factor in the numerator and producing the targeted single trig function. The move converts a structurally opaque difference into a recognisable identity pattern.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice two trig fractions with different denominators and a target expression that is a single trig function — neither fraction alone can reach that form.
  2. I rule out manipulating each fraction independently (e.g. converting to tan or applying double-angle to one term) because the structural mismatch with the target persists.
  3. I commit to combining over a common denominator because the resulting numerator will be a sum/difference of products — exactly the shape of compound-angle formulas.
  4. First concrete step: write the LCD as sin x cos x (and remember this equals ½ sin 2x if needed later).
  5. Inside the move, after combining I scan the numerator for cos(A+B), cos(A−B), sin(A±B) patterns, and match the denominator factor against what the target requires.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the LCD of the two fractions (e.g. sin x · cos x).
  2. Rewrite each fraction with the LCD: multiply numerator and denominator of each term by the missing factor.
  3. Combine into a single fraction; expand the numerator carefully.
  4. Match the numerator to a compound-angle or double-angle identity (cos(A+B), sin(A−B), etc.).
  5. Simplify the resulting fraction against the denominator and reconcile with the target form.

Worked Micro-Example

Prove $\dfrac{\cos 2x}{\sin x} - \dfrac{\sin 2x}{\cos x} \equiv \dfrac{\cos 3x}{\tfrac12 \sin 2x}$. Combine: $\dfrac{\cos 2x \cos x - \sin 2x \sin x}{\sin x \cos x}$. Numerator $= \cos(2x+x) = \cos 3x$; denominator $= \tfrac12 \sin 2x$. Hence the identity.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Proving $\cot A - \cot B \equiv \dfrac{\sin(B-A)}{\sin A \sin B}$
LCD is $\sin A \sin B$; numerator collapses via $\sin(B-A) = \sin B \cos A - \cos B \sin A$.
Sum of fractions like $\dfrac{1}{\sin x} + \dfrac{1}{\cos x}$ in a 'show that' problem
LCD $\sin x \cos x = \tfrac12 \sin 2x$; numerator $\cos x + \sin x$ may be combined using R-form.
$\tan A + \tan B$ rewritten as $\dfrac{\sin A \cos B + \cos A \sin B}{\cos A \cos B} = \dfrac{\sin(A+B)}{\cos A \cos B}$
Same combine-then-recognise pattern, but with cos·cos as LCD and sin(A+B) emerging in the numerator.
Difference of secant-type fractions, e.g. $\dfrac{1}{1-\cos x} - \dfrac{1}{1+\cos x}$
LCD is $1-\cos^2 x = \sin^2 x$; numerator simplifies to a constant multiple of $\cos x$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating each fraction separately and trying to convert to tan x, missing that the target requires merging.
  • Assuming the numerator after combining must factorise algebraically rather than via a compound-angle identity.
  • Reading the LCD sin x cos x as needing immediate conversion to ½ sin 2x before the numerator simplification, obscuring the compound-angle match.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to multiply both numerator and denominator of each fraction by the missing factor when finding the LCD.
  • Sign error when expanding cos 2x · cos x − sin 2x · sin x and failing to recognise it as cos(2x+x).
  • Losing or duplicating a factor of 2 when toggling between sin x cos x and ½ sin 2x.
  • Stopping at the combined fraction without identifying the compound-angle identity in the numerator.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only compound-angle and double-angle identities in the Edexcel IAL P3/P4 specification; no calculus or non-syllabus identities are required.
