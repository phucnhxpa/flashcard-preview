Coordinate Geometry

Decompose composite perimeter into arcs plus radial segments

Family: Perimeter Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a closed region is bounded by a mixture of circular arcs and straight line segments — most commonly an annular sector (two concentric arcs joined by two radial sides) or a 'sector-with-a-bite' shape. Verbal cues include 'find the perimeter of region OPQRS', a diagram showing two arcs at different radii sharing the same central angle, or descriptions of paths traced along radii and curves. Anytime the boundary cannot be traversed by one formula, this move applies.

Motivation — Why this works

Perimeter is additive along disjoint boundary pieces, so the total length equals the sum of each edge computed by its own appropriate formula. Each circular arc contributes $r\theta$ (in radians) at its own radius, and each radial/straight segment contributes its own length. Listing edges in traversal order guarantees no piece is missed and no piece is double-counted, while collecting like terms (e.g. the two radii of equal length) before substituting numerics keeps the arithmetic clean.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the region's boundary is not a single circle or polygon — it has both curved and straight portions.
  2. Rule out single-formula approaches (circumference, polygon perimeter) and rule out using area/sector-area formulas for length.
  3. Commit to a 'walk-the-boundary' decomposition: list each edge once in order and assign the right length formula to each.
  4. Translate each edge to a length: straight radial side → its length; arc at radius $r$ subtending angle $\theta$ → $r\theta$.
  5. Group like contributions (e.g. two equal radii give $2x$; two arcs at the same angle give $\theta(r_1+r_2)$) before substituting any numerical value.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Trace the closed boundary in order, labelling each edge as 'arc' or 'straight'.
  2. For each arc, identify its radius $r$ and central angle $\theta$ (in radians) and write its length as $r\theta$.
  3. For each straight edge, write down its length directly (often the annulus width $x$ or a given radius).
  4. Sum all pieces: $P = \sum (\text{arc lengths}) + \sum (\text{straight lengths})$.
  5. Collect like terms in any unknown, then substitute the numerical value and round to the required precision.

Worked Micro-Example

An annular sector $OPQRS$ has inner radius $1.2$, outer radius $1.2+x$, and angle $0.6$ rad. Boundary = inner arc + outer arc + two radial sides: $P = 0.6(1.2) + 0.6(1.2+x) + 2x = 0.72 + 0.72 + 0.6x + 2x = 1.44 + 2.6x$. With $x \approx 26.165$, $P \approx 1.44 + 68.03 \approx 69.5$ m.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Circular segment (arc + chord)
Replace one straight side with a chord of length $2r\sin(\theta/2)$; arc length still $r\theta$.
Sector OAB (one arc + two radii)
Only one arc contributes; perimeter $= r\theta + 2r$.
Stadium / shape with semicircular ends
Two straight parallel sides plus two semicircular arcs of length $\pi r$ each.
Shape with arc removed (e.g. rectangle minus a quarter-disc)
Subtract the removed straight edges and add the new arc length; tracing order still applies.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading the question as asking for area when it asks for perimeter, then applying $\tfrac12 r^2\theta$.
  • Assuming both arcs share the same radius and writing $2r\theta$ instead of $\theta(r_1+r_2)$.
  • Missing one of the radial sides because the diagram suggests symmetry.

Pitfalls

  • Using degrees in $r\theta$ without converting to radians.
  • Forgetting the inner arc and treating the region as bounded by only three pieces.
  • Using the outer radius (or inner radius) for both arcs.
  • Substituting the numerical value of $x$ before simplifying, leading to arithmetic slips.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the radian arc-length formula $s = r\theta$ and elementary length addition, both core Edexcel IAL P2/P3 (radian measure) content.
