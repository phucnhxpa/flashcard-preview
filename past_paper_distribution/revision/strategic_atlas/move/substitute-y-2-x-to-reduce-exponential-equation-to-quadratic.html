Trigger — When to use

Fires when, after base-unification, the equation contains exactly two exponential terms whose exponents differ by a factor of 2 — typically $(2^x)^2$ (or $4^x$, $2^{2x}$) alongside $2^x$, plus constants. The structural cue is $[\text{something}]^2 + a[\text{something}] + b = 0$ where 'something' is a single exponential. Phrases like '$4^x - 12\cdot 2^x + 32 = 0$' or '$e^{2x} - 5e^x + 6 = 0$' are textbook signals. No other exponential structures (e.g. $3^x$ mixed with $2^x$) should be present.

Motivation — Why this works

The substitution $y = 2^x$ converts a transcendental equation into a polynomial one, because $(2^x)^2 = (2^2)^x = 4^x = y^2$ by index laws. Once recast, the full machinery of quadratic theory — factorisation, completing the square, the discriminant — becomes available. After solving for $y$, we recover $x$ via $x = \log_2 y$, exploiting the bijection of $2^x$ on $\mathbb{R} \to (0, \infty)$. The strict positivity of $2^x$ also lets us discard any non-positive roots cleanly.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice two exponential terms whose exponents are in 2:1 ratio — e.g. $(2^x)^2$ and $2^x$ — plus constants, matching the disguised-quadratic template.
  2. I rule out taking logarithms, because $\log$ does not distribute over the sum on the RHS; I also rule out dividing through by $2^x$, since that would lose a root.
  3. I commit to $y = 2^x$ because the equation has the exact shape $y^2 + ay + b = 0$ once I clear denominators — a quadratic substitution is the standard reduction.
  4. I write down $y = 2^x$ and replace every exponential occurrence, ensuring $(2^x)^2$ becomes $y^2$ (not $2y$ or $y\cdot 2^x$).
  5. I keep the constraint $y > 0$ in mind from the outset, so that any spurious non-positive root from the quadratic can be rejected before back-substituting via $x = \log_2 y$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Let $y = 2^x$, so $(2^x)^2 = y^2$ and any $4^x$ or $2^{2x}$ also becomes $y^2$.
  2. Substitute into the equation, e.g. $\tfrac{y^2}{4} = 3y - 8$.
  3. Clear denominators (multiply through by 4): $y^2 = 12y - 32$.
  4. Rearrange to standard form: $y^2 - 12y + 32 = 0$.
  5. Factorise (or use the quadratic formula): $(y-4)(y-8) = 0$, giving $y = 4$ or $y = 8$.
  6. Check $y > 0$ for each root, then back-substitute: $2^x = 4 \Rightarrow x = 2$ and $2^x = 8 \Rightarrow x = 3$.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\tfrac{(2^x)^2}{4} = 3\cdot 2^x - 8$. Let $y = 2^x$, giving $\tfrac{y^2}{4} = 3y - 8$, i.e. $y^2 - 12y + 32 = 0$. Factorising: $(y-4)(y-8) = 0$, so $y = 4$ or $y = 8$ (both positive, accepted). Hence $2^x = 4$ or $2^x = 8$, giving $x = 2$ or $x = 3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$e^{2x} + a e^{x} + b = 0$
Substitute $y = e^x$; reject $y \le 0$ and recover $x = \ln y$.
$3^{2x} - 5\cdot 3^x + 6 = 0$
Substitute $y = 3^x$; same template, with $x = \log_3 y$ at the end.
$\sin^2 x + a\sin x + b = 0$
Substitute $y = \sin x$; impose $-1 \le y \le 1$ instead of $y > 0$.
$9^x - 4\cdot 3^x + 3 = 0$
Rewrite $9^x = (3^x)^2$, then substitute $y = 3^x$.
$x - 5\sqrt{x} + 6 = 0$
Substitute $y = \sqrt{x}$ (so $y \ge 0$); same disguised-quadratic family.

Common Misreadings

  • Letting $y = 4^x$ or $y = 2^{2x}$ separately, missing that $(2^x)^2 = (2^x)\cdot(2^x)$ is the squared substitution variable.
  • Treating the equation as a single exponential and trying $\log$ on both sides, which fails because $\log$ does not split sums.
  • Dividing both sides by $2^x$ to reduce 'degree', which destroys one of the roots and reduces a quadratic to a linear equation.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to clear denominators (e.g. multiply by 4) before factorising, leading to algebraic slips.
  • Forgetting $y = 2^x > 0$, and accepting a negative root that has no real $x$ solution.
  • Stopping at $y = 4, 8$ without back-substituting to find $x$.
  • Mis-applying index laws: writing $(2^x)^2 = 2^{x^2}$ instead of $2^{2x}$.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P2/P3 technique for exponential equations; the substitution and quadratic solution use only index laws, factorisation, and $\log_a$/$\ln$, all within syllabus.
