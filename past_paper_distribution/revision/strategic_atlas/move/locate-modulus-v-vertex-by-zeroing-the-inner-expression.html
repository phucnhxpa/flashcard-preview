Locate modulus V-vertex by zeroing the inner expression

Family: Modulus Vertex Finding Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the function has the form $f(x) = |L(x)| + c$ with $L$ linear, and the task asks for the vertex, minimum point, corner of the V-shape, or key features needed to sketch $y = |L(x)| + c$. Also triggers when setting up case boundaries for solving $|L(x)| + c = $ something, or when identifying where the graph changes slope. Phrasings include 'state the coordinates of the minimum point', 'find the vertex of the graph', or 'sketch, showing the coordinates of the cusp'.

Motivation — Why this works

Since $|L(x)| \ge 0$ with equality iff $L(x) = 0$, the minimum of $|L(x)|$ occurs precisely at the root of the inner linear expression. Adding a constant $c$ outside the modulus translates the entire V-shape vertically by $c$ without altering the $x$-coordinate of the corner. Therefore the vertex of $y = |L(x)| + c$ is exactly $(x_0, c)$ where $L(x_0) = 0$. This separates the horizontal location (root of inner) from the vertical level (outer shift), making vertex extraction a one-line calculation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the outer modulus wraps a linear expression, so the graph is a V with a single corner.
  2. Rules out calculus or solving $f(x) = 0$ — the vertex is about the modulus hitting zero, not the whole function.
  3. Commits to the principle $|L(x)| \ge 0$ with equality at $L(x) = 0$, so that root gives the $x$-coordinate of the cusp.
  4. Sets the inside of the modulus to zero and solves the linear equation for $x$.
  5. Substitutes back into the full $f$, remembering to add any outer constant, and reports the vertex as an ordered pair.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the inner linear expression $L(x)$ inside the modulus and any outer additive constant $c$.
  2. Solve $L(x) = 0$ to get $x = x_0$.
  3. Compute $f(x_0) = |0| + c = c$.
  4. State the vertex as $(x_0, c)$.
  5. If parameters are present, leave the answer in exact form involving those parameters.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $f(x) = |3x + a| + a$ with $a > 0$. Set $3x + a = 0 \Rightarrow x = -\tfrac{a}{3}$. Then $f(-\tfrac{a}{3}) = |0| + a = a$. Hence the vertex is $\left(-\tfrac{a}{3},\, a\right)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$y = |L(x)|$ with no outer constant
Vertex is $(x_0, 0)$ where $L(x_0) = 0$.
$y = a|L(x)| + c$ with $a \ne 0$
Vertex still at $x_0$ where $L(x_0)=0$; $y$-coordinate is $c$. If $a < 0$ the V opens downward (becomes a $\Lambda$) and the vertex is a maximum.
$y = |L(x) - k|$ written without an explicit outer shift
Solve $L(x) = k$ for the $x$-coordinate of the cusp; $y$-coordinate is $0$.
$y = |f(x)|$ where $f$ is quadratic
Replaces vertex-finding with finding roots of $f$ and reflecting the portion where $f<0$ above the $x$-axis; cusps occur at the roots of $f$.
Transformations $y = |L(x)| + c$ followed by further shifts $y \mapsto y + d$ or $x \mapsto x - h$
Apply the translation to the located vertex: new vertex is $(x_0 + h,\, c + d)$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the outer constant as if it were inside the modulus, e.g. solving $3x + a + a = 0$ for the vertex.
  • Setting the whole function $f(x) = 0$ instead of just the inner expression, confusing roots of $f$ with the cusp.
  • Assuming the vertex lies on the $x$-axis even when an outer constant shifts the V vertically.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the vertical shift and reporting $(x_0, 0)$ instead of $(x_0, c)$.
  • Sign slips when solving $L(x) = 0$ for $x$ in terms of a parameter (e.g. writing $x = a/3$ instead of $-a/3$).
  • Misreading a downward-opening modulus graph ($a<0$ coefficient) as still having a minimum at the cusp.
