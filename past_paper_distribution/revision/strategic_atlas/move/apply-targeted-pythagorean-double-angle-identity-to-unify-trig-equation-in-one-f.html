Trigger — When to use

The equation, integrand, or expression contains TWO or more different trig functions (e.g. sin and cos, or sin² and cos², or mixed arguments like θ and 2θ) so it cannot be solved/integrated/simplified directly. Visual cues: cos²x sitting next to sin x; sin 2θ appearing alongside sin θ or cos θ; cos²2θ next to sin²2θ; a target expression in a single named function (e.g. 'show that ... in cosec 2θ' or 'show that integrand = 32 sin²t cos t'). The equation must be reduced to a single trig function (or single argument) before standard algebraic/integration techniques apply.

Motivation — Why this works

A single trig variable is required before factorising, solving a quadratic, or integrating by reverse chain rule. The Pythagorean identity sin²x + cos²x = 1 (and its rearrangements) trades one squared function for the other; the double-angle identities sin 2θ = 2 sin θ cos θ and cos 2θ ∈ {1−2sin²θ, 2cos²θ−1, cos²θ−sin²θ} unify mismatched arguments. The CHOICE of which form to use is deliberate: pick the rearrangement that matches the OTHER terms already present, so the resulting equation becomes a clean polynomial in one trig function with no surds and no extra terms.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation/integrand contains more than one trig function (different functions, different powers, or different arguments) — algebraic methods cannot proceed yet.
  2. Rule out blunt alternatives: dividing by a trig function risks losing roots or creates fractions; using cos²x = (1+cos 2x)/2 may introduce a NEW argument; squaring to eliminate a function introduces surds and extraneous solutions.
  3. Identify the 'target' trig function — usually dictated by which term is already linear, or by what the question asks you to show (e.g. cosec 2θ in the answer ⇒ target is sin 2θ).
  4. Commit to the identity that converts the squared/double-angle term into the target function with NO leftover mixed terms (e.g. cos²x → 1−sin²x when sin x is already present; cos 2θ → 1−2sin²θ when sin²θ will appear after cancellation).
  5. Tactical choice for cos 2θ: if the other side reduces to sin²θ, pick 1−2sin²θ; if it reduces to cos²θ, pick 2cos²θ−1; only pick cos²θ−sin²θ if a difference-of-squares factorisation is wanted.
  6. Substitute, expand carefully (watch the distributed factor), and rearrange to a quadratic-in-one-function form a·u² + b·u + c = 0 with positive leading coefficient, ready for factorisation.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Spot the offending mixed term (e.g. cos²x, cos 2θ, sin 2t, cos²2θ).
  2. Select the identity form whose output matches the OTHER term: cos²x = 1−sin²x; sin 2θ = 2 sin θ cos θ; cos 2θ = 1−2sin²θ (or 2cos²θ−1).
  3. Substitute and expand, distributing every coefficient (e.g. 2(1−sin²x) = 2 − 2sin²x).
  4. If a common factor (like cos θ) appears on both sides, cancel it — but first check it is non-zero on the given domain.
  5. Collect terms into standard polynomial form in the single chosen function, with positive leading coefficient.
  6. Solve the resulting quadratic (or higher) by factorising or using the quadratic formula; then return to angles.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $2\cos^2 x + \sin x = 1$ for $x$.\n Replace $\cos^2 x = 1 - \sin^2 x$: $\;2(1-\sin^2 x) + \sin x = 1$.\n Expand and rearrange: $\;2\sin^2 x - \sin x - 1 = 0$.\n Factorise: $(2\sin x + 1)(\sin x - 1) = 0$, giving $\sin x = -\tfrac12$ or $\sin x = 1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation mixes sin 2θ, cos 2θ with sin θ, cos θ (e.g. 2 cos 2θ cos θ = sin 2θ sin θ)
Expand sin 2θ = 2 sin θ cos θ to surface a common cos θ factor; choose cos 2θ = 1−2sin²θ so the equation becomes linear/quadratic in sin²θ after cancelling cos θ.
Integrand of form sin t · sin 2t (mismatched arguments)
Expand sin 2t = 2 sin t cos t to get sin²t · cos t, ready for reverse chain rule ∫ sin²t cos t dt = sin³t/3 + C.
Equation in sin²2θ and cos²2θ where target form is cosec 2θ
Use cos²2θ = 1 − sin²2θ to reduce to a quadratic in sin 2θ; then divide by sin 2θ if cosec 2θ is required.
tan²x appears with sec x
Use 1 + tan²x = sec²x to convert tan²x = sec²x − 1, producing a quadratic in sec x.
sin 3θ mixed with sin θ
Use the triple-angle identity sin 3θ = 3 sin θ − 4 sin³θ to unify in sin θ.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing cos²x with cos 2x and applying cos 2x = 1 − 2sin²x in the wrong context.
  • Applying a product-to-sum formula when a double-angle expansion would give a single clean term suited to reverse chain rule.
  • Trying to convert sin x to cos x (or vice versa) via square roots when only the squared version of one function is present — squaring is unnecessary.

Pitfalls

  • Choosing the wrong form of cos 2θ — e.g. using cos²θ − sin²θ when the rest of the equation is purely in sin²θ, leaving a mixed equation that needs a second substitution.
  • Forgetting to distribute a coefficient: 2(1 − sin²x) becomes 2 − 2sin²x, not 2 − sin²x.
  • Cancelling a common trig factor (e.g. cos θ) without first verifying it is non-zero on the stated domain — this loses solutions.
  • Sign errors when rearranging to standard quadratic form; ensure the leading coefficient is positive before factorising.
  • Dividing by sin x or cos x to 'simplify' instead of substituting an identity — this removes valid roots.

A-level Scope Notes

All identities used (sin²x + cos²x = 1, double-angle formulas for sin 2θ and cos 2θ, 1 + tan²x = sec²x) are listed in the Edexcel IAL formula booklet under Pure Mathematics. Avoid Weierstrass (t = tan(x/2)) substitution and complex-exponential derivations — these are out of scope for IAL P2/P3/P4.
