Integration

Unroll reduction formula iteratively to a base case

Family: Reduction Descent Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Part (b) of an integration question asks you to evaluate a specific definite integral $I_n$ for a particular numerical value of $n$ (e.g. $I_4$, $I_6$), immediately after part (a) has asked you to derive a reduction formula linking $I_n$ to $I_{n-2}$ (or $I_{n-1}$). The cue is the explicit pairing: 'Hence, find $I_4$' or 'Use your result in (a) to evaluate $\int_0^{\pi/4}\sec^6 x\,dx$'. The word 'Hence' is the structural signal — the reduction formula is not just a derivation exercise; it is the engine for part (b). The same trigger fires whenever you have a recurrence relation in $I_n$ and a target index that can be reached in finitely many steps.

Motivation — Why this works

A reduction formula has no value standing alone — it must be unrolled by repeatedly substituting smaller indices until you hit a base integral that integrates directly. For step-by-2 recurrences, even $n$ descends to $I_0$ and odd $n$ descends to $I_1$; for step-by-1 recurrences, descent terminates at $I_0$. The base case is always a standard integral ($\int\cos x$, $\int\sec^2 x$, $\int 1\,dx$, $\int e^x\,dx$, etc.) computed in one line. Each application of the recurrence is pure arithmetic, so the entire payoff of deriving the formula is realised by this mechanical descent.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structure: part (a) gave a recurrence $I_n = f(n)\cdot I_{n-2} + g(n)$ (or similar), and part (b) names a specific $n$. The word 'Hence' confirms the recurrence is the intended tool.
  2. Rule out direct integration: expanding $\sin^6 x$ or $\sec^6 x$ via identities is possible but tedious and error-prone, and the question structure is steering you away from it.
  3. Determine the descent chain by parity. For step-by-2: even $n \to I_0$, odd $n \to I_1$. For $\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^n x\,dx$ the bases are $I_0=\pi/2$, $I_1=1$; for $\int_0^{\pi/2}\cos x \cdot x^n$ type problems it may be $I_0=1$ or require a one-off IBP at $I_1$.
  4. Compute the base case directly and exactly, keeping $\pi$ and surds in symbolic form (do not decimal-approximate).
  5. Apply the recurrence bottom-up: compute $I_2$ from $I_0$, then $I_4$ from $I_2$, etc. This is cleaner than top-down substitution because each $I_k$ is a concrete number before being used.
  6. At each step, evaluate any boundary terms $[\,\cdot\,]_a^b$ using exact trig values (e.g. $\sec(\pi/4)=\sqrt 2$, $\tan(\pi/4)=1$), and combine fractions carefully before the next step.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the target index $n$ and the parity of the descent chain.
  2. Compute the base case directly: e.g. $I_0 = \int_0^{\pi/2}\cos x\,dx = 1$, or $I_2 = [\tan x]_0^{\pi/4} = 1$, or $I_0 = \pi/2$, as appropriate.
  3. Substitute into the recurrence with the next index up. Evaluate any boundary term $[\,\cdot\,]_a^b$ using exact values at this stage.
  4. Simplify to a single closed-form value before proceeding (combine fractions, collect $\pi$ powers).
  5. Repeat: feed the result into the recurrence at the next index, until you reach the target $I_n$.
  6. Multiply in any external constant (e.g. from an arc-length, surface-area, or volume formula) at the end.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $5I_6 = [\tan x\sec^4 x]_0^{\pi/4} + 4I_4$ and $3I_4 = [\tan x\sec^2 x]_0^{\pi/4} + 2I_2$ with $I_2=1$: first $3I_4 = (1)(\sqrt 2)^2 - 0 + 2(1) = 4$, so $I_4 = 4/3$. Then $5I_6 = (1)(\sqrt 2)^4 - 0 + 4(4/3) = 4 + 16/3 = 28/3$, giving $I_6 = 28/15$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Even target index with step-by-2 recurrence on $\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^n x\,dx$ or $\int_0^{\pi/2}\cos^n x\,dx$
Descend to $I_0 = \pi/2$; the answer will contain $\pi$.
Odd target index on the same Wallis-type integral
Descend to $I_1 = 1$; the answer is purely rational.
Recurrence steps by 1 (e.g. $I_n = nI_{n-1} + \text{boundary}$ from $\int x^n e^x$)
More steps in the chain but each is simpler; descend to $I_0 = \int e^x\,dx$.
Mixed integrand $\int_0^{\pi/2} x^n\cos x\,dx$ where odd descent ends at $I_1$
Compute $I_1$ via a single IBP rather than direct integration.

Common Misreadings

  • Applying the recurrence only once and leaving an intermediate $I_k$ unevaluated, not realising a second or third application is required.
  • Treating part (b) as a fresh integration problem and ignoring the 'Hence' link to part (a)'s formula.
  • Using the indefinite form of the formula and forgetting to evaluate the boundary term at each stage.

Pitfalls

  • Using the wrong base case: e.g. $I_0 = 1$ when the integrand is $\sin^0 x = 1$ on $[0,\pi/2]$ gives $\pi/2$, not $1$.
  • Confusing $\sec(\pi/4) = \sqrt 2$ with $\cos(\pi/4) = 1/\sqrt 2$, leading to wrong boundary values.
  • Arithmetic slips combining fractions with $\pi$ powers (e.g. $\pi^4/16 - 12(\pi^2/4 - 2)$ must be expanded carefully to $\pi^4/16 - 3\pi^2 + 24$).
  • Forgetting a constant prefactor from the geometric formula that wraps the integral (arc length $2\pi$, surface area, etc.).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) reduction-formula questions. No formula-booklet entry — students must derive the recurrence in part (a) (typically via integration by parts) and then apply it. Avoid invoking generalised Wallis product results or Beta/Gamma functions; stay with finite explicit descent.
