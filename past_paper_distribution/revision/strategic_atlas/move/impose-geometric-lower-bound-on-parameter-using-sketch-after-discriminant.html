Coordinate Geometry

Impose geometric lower bound on parameter using sketch after discriminant

Family: Graphical Constraint Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

After reducing an intersection problem (line meets curve in n distinct points) to a discriminant or algebraic inequality, the algebra yields only one side of the bound (e.g. A < 4) but the answer requires a two-sided range (e.g. 0 < A < 4). The missing bound must come from reading the sketch — checking sign of gradient, whether the line is non-trivial, or whether the configuration matches the diagram. Cue phrases: 'using your sketch', 'three distinct solutions', 'state the range of values'.

Motivation — Why this works

Discriminants only test whether a quadratic factor has real roots; they cannot detect whether the geometric configuration matches the picture. The sketch encodes which branch/lobe of the curve is crossed, the sign of the gradient, and whether the line is degenerate (e.g. coincides with an axis). For y = Ax cutting y = x(4 − x²), only A > 0 produces a line through the origin sweeping through the upper-right and lower-left lobes to give the intended three crossings; A ≤ 0 either degenerates to the x-axis or fails the configuration the diagram depicts.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the discriminant gave A < 4 but the mark scheme answer is 0 < A < 4, so one bound is missing.
  2. I rule out the bound coming from more algebra — the cubic factor x² + (A−4) has been fully exploited; any extra constraint must be geometric.
  3. I commit to reading the sketch from part (a): the line y = Ax pivots about the origin, and only certain gradients reproduce the three-crossing picture.
  4. Bridge: test the boundary A = 0 (line becomes the x-axis, intersections collapse onto the cubic's own roots — degenerate) and A < 0 (wrong configuration).
  5. Tactical: state the geometric reason briefly ('from the sketch, A must be positive') and combine with the algebraic bound to write 0 < A < 4.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Solve the intersection equation algebraically and extract the discriminant/real-root condition (gives one bound, e.g. A < 4).
  2. Test the boundary value A = 0: substitute and check whether the resulting intersections are distinct and match the sketch.
  3. Test a representative A < 0 against the sketch: does the line still produce the required number/type of intersections?
  4. Identify which sign of A reproduces the diagram in part (a).
  5. Combine the algebraic bound and the geometric bound into a single inequality, e.g. 0 < A < 4.

Worked Micro-Example

Line $y = Ax$ meets $y = x(4-x^2)$ where $x(4-x^2) = Ax \Rightarrow x(x^2 - 4 + A) = 0$. Three distinct roots require $4 - A > 0$, i.e. $A < 4$. From the sketch the line through $O$ must have positive gradient to cut both lobes, so $A > 0$. Hence $0 < A < 4$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line $y = mx + c$ tangent to or crossing a parabola/cubic with fixed $c$
Discriminant gives bound on $m$; sketch fixes whether $m$ must be positive/negative or above a critical slope.
Circle and line intersection with parameter in the line's intercept
Discriminant in $x$ gives $|c| < r\sqrt{1+m^2}$ style bound; sketch may further restrict sign of $c$ if only one side of the circle is intended.
Modulus graph $y = |f(x)|$ meeting $y = kx$ in a prescribed number of points
Algebra on each branch gives bounds; sketch determines which sign of $k$ and which branch contributes, fixing the missing inequality.
Quadratic $y = x^2 + bx + c$ meeting horizontal line $y = k$ with constraint on number of positive roots
Discriminant gives existence; sketch/Vieta fixes sign conditions giving the second bound.

Common Misreadings

  • Reporting only $A < 4$ from the discriminant and ignoring the lower bound entirely.
  • Treating $A = 0$ as admissible because the algebra still yields three $x$-values, missing that the line degenerates to the x-axis.
  • Assuming symmetry means negative $A$ also works without checking the sketch's specific configuration.

Pitfalls

  • Writing $\le$ instead of $<$ at the geometric boundary, including a degenerate case.
  • Justifying the lower bound by algebra alone when the examiner expects an explicit reference to the sketch.
  • Confusing 'three real roots of the combined equation' with 'three distinct intersection points of two curves' — the latter excludes degenerate overlaps.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure technique: combining discriminant analysis (P1/P2) with curve sketching (P1) to state a parameter range. No calculus or advanced theory required.
