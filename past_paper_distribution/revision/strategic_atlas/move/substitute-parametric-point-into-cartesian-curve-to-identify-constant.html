Coordinate Geometry

Substitute parametric point into Cartesian curve to identify constant

Family: Parametric Identification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question states that a general parametric point such as $(at, a/t)$ lies on a Cartesian curve like $xy = 20$, and asks for the constant $a$. The parametric form must satisfy the Cartesian equation for every value of the parameter $t$. Cue phrases include 'the point $(at, a/t)$ lies on the curve' or 'show that $P(at,a/t)$ is on $xy=k$'. The parameter must cancel, leaving an equation purely in the unknown constant.

Motivation — Why this works

Because the parametric point lies on the curve for all admissible $t$, substitution must produce an identity in $t$. For $xy=c^2$ with point $(at,a/t)$, the product $at\cdot a/t = a^2$ is independent of $t$, so matching gives $a^2 = 20$ immediately. This is the canonical way to align a parametric family $(at,a/t)$ with the standard rectangular hyperbola $xy=c^2$, where $a$ plays the role of $c$.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the parametric form $(at,a/t)$ has the same shape as the standard $(ct,c/t)$ for $xy=c^2$, so $a$ is the scale parameter.
  2. Rule out trying to eliminate $t$ via simultaneous equations or differentiation — the identity must hold for all $t$, so direct substitution is enough.
  3. Commit to substituting $x=at$, $y=a/t$ into $xy=20$ because $t$ will cancel, leaving a clean equation in $a$.
  4. Compute the product: $xy = at\cdot a/t = a^2$, independent of $t$ as expected.
  5. Solve $a^2 = 20$ and select the positive root since $a$ is a positive scale constant, giving $a = 2\sqrt{5}$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write $x = at$ and $y = a/t$.
  2. Compute $xy = at\cdot \tfrac{a}{t} = a^2$.
  3. Equate to the curve constant: $a^2 = 20$.
  4. Take the positive root: $a = 2\sqrt{5}$.

Worked Micro-Example

The point $P(at, a/t)$ lies on the curve $xy = 20$. Then $xy = at\cdot\tfrac{a}{t} = a^2$, so $a^2 = 20$. Hence $a = 2\sqrt{5}$ (taking the positive root, since $a>0$).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Rectangular hyperbola $xy=c^2$ with point $(ct,c/t)$
Substitution gives $c^2$ identically; identification of $c$ is immediate from the RHS.
Parabola $y^2 = 4ax$ with point $(at^2, 2at)$
Substitute to confirm $(2at)^2 = 4a\cdot at^2$ holds identically, then match $4a$ to the given coefficient to read off $a$.
Curve given as $xy = k$ for unspecified $k$ with point $(at,a/t)$
Identification yields $k = a^2$; use whichever of $a$ or $k$ is unknown.
General curve $f(x,y)=0$ with parametric point depending on a constant
Substitute and require the resulting expression to be independent of the parameter; coefficients of each power of $t$ must match.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading $a$ as a variable rather than a fixed constant to be determined.
  • Treating the substitution as giving a single value of $t$ instead of an identity in $t$.
  • Confusing the standard form: thinking $xy=c^2$ is parametrised by $(c/t, ct)$ with a different constant role.

Pitfalls

  • Taking $a = \pm 2\sqrt{5}$ when the problem context requires $a>0$.
  • Writing $a = 20$ by misremembering the standard form $xy=c^2$ as $xy=c$.
  • Failing to simplify $\sqrt{20}$ to $2\sqrt{5}$ in final answer.
  • Cancelling $t$ incorrectly or losing track of it when $t=0$ is excluded.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Core/Pure scope: rectangular hyperbola $xy=c^2$ with parametric form $(ct,c/t)$ and the parabola $y^2=4ax$ with $(at^2,2at)$ are standard parametric curves in C3/C4 and FP coordinate geometry.
