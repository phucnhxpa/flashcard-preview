Trigonometry

Chain modulus then vertical shift to update range incrementally

Family: Transformation Chaining Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The target curve has the form y = |f(x)| + k (or similar layered transformation) where f(x) is a trig function whose range is read directly from a given graph. You need the new maximum, minimum, or a specific y-value but have no explicit formula for f(x). Cues include phrases like 'find the value of k', 'state the range of the transformed curve', or 'write down the coordinates of the image of a given point'. Also fires when a question asks for the y-coordinate at an x where f(x) = 0.

Motivation — Why this works

Transformations compose from the inside out, so applying them one layer at a time updates the range without needing f(x) algebraically. The modulus collapses the range [m, M] to [0, max(|m|, |M|)] by reflecting the negative portion above the x-axis. A subsequent vertical shift +k rigidly translates both endpoints, preserving the spread. Tracking endpoints through each step is exact and avoids errors from trying to combine layers in one move.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the curve is built from f(x) by stacking a modulus and a vertical shift, and the range of f(x) is visible on the graph.
  2. Rule out finding an explicit formula for f(x) — unnecessary since only range/endpoints are required.
  3. Commit to peeling transformations one at a time from the innermost outward: f(x) → |f(x)| → |f(x)| + k.
  4. Read off the range [m, M] of f(x) from the graph's max and min, then compute the modulus's effect: new minimum is 0 (since f crosses zero), new maximum is max(|m|, |M|).
  5. Add k to both endpoints to get the final range, and read off the requested quantity (k, max, min, or a specific point's image).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Read range of f(x) from the graph as [m, M].
  2. Apply modulus: range of |f(x)| is [0, max(|m|, |M|)], assuming f attains both signs (or zero).
  3. Apply vertical shift: range of |f(x)| + k is [k, max(|m|, |M|) + k].
  4. Identify k as the new minimum and the new maximum as max(|m|, |M|) + k.
  5. For a specific point (x₀, f(x₀)), image is (x₀, |f(x₀)| + k).

Worked Micro-Example

Given f(x) has range $[-4, 4]$ from the graph, find the range of $y = |f(x)| + 3$. Step 1: $|f(x)|$ has range $[0, 4]$. Step 2: add 3 to get range $[3, 7]$. So the maximum is 7 and the minimum is 3. At $x = 2\pi$ where $f(2\pi) = 0$, the image point is $(2\pi, 3)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

y = a|f(x)| + k with a vertical stretch
Insert a stretch step: range of a|f(x)| is [0, a·max(|m|,|M|)] for a > 0, then add k.
y = |f(x − c)| + k with a horizontal shift
Horizontal shift leaves range unchanged; only feature x-coordinates move by +c.
y = |f(x) + k| (modulus outside the shift)
Shift first to range [m+k, M+k], then modulus gives [0, max(|m+k|,|M+k|)] only if the shifted interval crosses zero; otherwise [min(|m+k|,|M+k|), max(|m+k|,|M+k|)].
Asymmetric range like f(x) ∈ [−5, 3]
Modulus gives [0, 5] since |−5| > |3|; the original negative extreme dominates.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading |f(x)| + 3 as |f(x) + 3| and applying the modulus after the shift instead of before.
  • Assuming the maximum of |f(x)| equals the original maximum M, ignoring that |m| may be larger.
  • Treating the modulus as not changing the minimum, leaving it at m instead of 0.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting that the modulus produces a new minimum of 0 whenever f crosses the x-axis.
  • On asymmetric ranges, picking the wrong endpoint as the new maximum after the modulus.
  • Adding k only to one endpoint instead of both when applying the vertical shift.
  • Confusing the order of stacked transformations (modulus vs shift vs stretch).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (P2/P3) graph transformations and modulus function content; no calculus or further-maths machinery required.
