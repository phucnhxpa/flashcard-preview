Strategic Atlas / Differentiation / Form Conversion For Power Rule
Differentiation

Expand factored polynomial before differentiating term by term

Family: Form Conversion For Power Rule Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when $f(x)$ is presented as a product of two or more linear (or low-degree) factors and a derivative is required. Typical phrasings: 'Given $f(x)=(x+a)(x+b)(x+c)$, find $f'(x)$', or 'find the gradient of the curve $y=(2x-9)(x+4)(x-2)$ at...'. The cue is a factored polynomial whose expansion remains a manageable polynomial in standard form. Especially common in Edexcel IAL P1/P2 questions on stationary points or tangents to cubics.

Motivation — Why this works

The power rule $\frac{d}{dx}(ax^n)=anx^{n-1}$ is the most reliable tool for differentiating polynomials, but it requires the function to be a sum of power terms. A factored cubic, once expanded, has only four terms, so differentiation reduces to four single-term applications of the power rule. The alternative — repeated product rule on three factors — produces nine sub-products and is error-prone. Expansion trades a small amount of algebra for a much cleaner differentiation step.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the function is a product of linear factors, so it is fundamentally a polynomial in disguise.
  2. Rules out applying the product rule to three factors because of the bookkeeping cost (nine partial products) and the high chance of sign errors.
  3. Commits to expansion because the resulting cubic has only four terms, each of which differentiates trivially.
  4. Plans a pairwise expansion: pick the two factors that multiply most cleanly first (e.g. those with leading coefficient 1), then multiply the result by the remaining factor.
  5. Inside the move, decides to collect like terms carefully before differentiating, and double-checks signs on constant–constant products.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Choose two factors to multiply first, preferring the pair with simplest coefficients.
  2. Expand that pair to obtain a quadratic $x^2+px+q$.
  3. Multiply the quadratic by the remaining factor, distributing each term across the binomial.
  4. Collect like terms to obtain the polynomial in standard form $ax^3+bx^2+cx+d$.
  5. Differentiate term by term using the power rule: $f'(x)=3ax^2+2bx+c$.

Worked Micro-Example

Differentiate $f(x)=(2x-9)(x+4)(x-2)$. First $(x+4)(x-2)=x^2+2x-8$. Then $(x^2+2x-8)(2x-9)=2x^3-5x^2-34x+72$. Hence $f'(x)=6x^2-10x-34$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Product of two quadratics, e.g. $f(x)=(x^2+1)(x^2-3x+2)$
Expand to a quartic in standard form, then apply the power rule to each of the (up to) five terms.
Linear factor times a quadratic, e.g. $f(x)=(x-1)(x^2+2x+5)$
A single distribution gives a cubic; differentiate term by term.
Factored expression divided by a power of $x$, e.g. $\frac{(x+1)(x-2)}{x}$
Expand the numerator first, then split the fraction into separate $x^n$ terms before differentiating.
Repeated factor, e.g. $f(x)=(x-3)^2(x+1)$
Expand $(x-3)^2$ first, then multiply by $(x+1)$, then differentiate term by term.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $(uvw)'$ as $u'v'w'$ — differentiating each factor separately and multiplying the derivatives.
  • Believing the product rule must be used because the function 'looks like a product', not recognising that expansion converts it to a polynomial.
  • Differentiating one factor while leaving the others untouched, e.g. writing $f'(x)=(2)(x+4)(x-2)$.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when multiplying two negative constants, e.g. $(-8)(-9)=+72$, not $-72$.
  • Missing a cross-term in the second expansion, particularly contributions to the $x^2$ and $x$ coefficients where multiple products combine.
  • Forgetting to collect like terms before differentiating, which leads to an incorrect coefficient on $x$ or $x^2$.
  • Dropping the constant term during expansion (it vanishes under differentiation, but its absence often signals a sign mistake elsewhere).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1/P2: expansion of polynomials and differentiation of $x^n$ for integer $n$. No product rule, chain rule, or non-integer exponents are required for this move.
