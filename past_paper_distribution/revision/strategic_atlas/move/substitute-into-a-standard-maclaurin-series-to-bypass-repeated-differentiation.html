Sequences & Series

Substitute into a standard Maclaurin series to bypass repeated differentiation

Family: Standard Series Substitution Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The function to be expanded is not a bare elementary function but a composition or product built from them — e.g. e^(x²), sin(2x), ln(1 + x²), cos(√x), e^(sin x), x ln(1+x), or (1+x²)^(1/2). The outer function (e^u, sin u, cos u, ln(1+u), (1+u)^n) has a standard Maclaurin series in the IAL formula booklet, and the inner expression g(x) is simple enough that g(x)², g(x)³ etc. remain manageable. Equivalent phrasings: 'find the series expansion of f(g(x)) up to xⁿ', 'expand the product f(x)g(x) up to terms in xᵏ', 'use a known result to deduce the series for ...'.

Motivation — Why this works

Power series can be composed and multiplied: if g(u) = Σaₙuⁿ converges, then g(h(x)) = Σaₙ[h(x)]ⁿ on the appropriate interval, and similarly two series can be multiplied term-by-term like polynomials. Differentiating a composite or product four or five times by chain/product rule produces increasingly unwieldy expressions and is a common source of arithmetic slips under exam pressure. By substituting the inner expression directly into a pre-computed standard series, you replace heavy calculus with cheap algebra, and you can discard high-order terms early. This exploits the fact that the standard results in the formula book ARE the Maclaurin coefficients — pre-differentiated for you.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that f(x) is built from a recognisable outer function (e^u, sin u, cos u, ln(1+u), (1+u)^n) applied to a simple inner expression g(x), or is a product of two such functions.
  2. Rule out direct repeated differentiation: computing f⁽ⁿ⁾(0) via chain/product rule four or more times would mean nested derivatives with many terms — slow and error-prone.
  3. Rule out finding f⁽ⁿ⁾(0) numerically term-by-term: the standard-series route gives the same coefficients with far less work.
  4. Commit to substitution: write the standard series for the outer function with a dummy variable u, then set u = g(x).
  5. Decide the cut-off: identify the highest power of x required (say xᴺ); any term whose total degree in x will exceed N can be dropped immediately, including inside expansions of u², u³, etc.
  6. If the function is a product g(x)h(x), expand each factor's series only up to xᴺ and multiply like polynomials, again discarding terms of degree > N as they appear.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the relevant standard series from the formula book, e.g. e^u = 1 + u + u²/2! + u³/3! + ··· or ln(1+u) = u − u²/2 + u³/3 − ··· or (1+u)^n = 1 + nu + n(n−1)u²/2! + ···.
  2. Identify the inner expression u = g(x) (e.g. u = x², u = 2x, u = −x³, or u = sin x = x − x³/6 + ···).
  3. Substitute: replace every u in the standard series with g(x).
  4. Expand each power [g(x)]^k carefully, raising the WHOLE expression (e.g. (2x)² = 4x², not 2x²); include cross-terms when squaring/cubing a multi-term series.
  5. Truncate: drop any term of degree higher than the requested N as soon as it appears.
  6. Collect like powers of x and simplify the coefficients.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the Maclaurin series of $e^{x^2}$ up to $x^4$. Standard result: $e^u = 1 + u + \tfrac{u^2}{2!} + \tfrac{u^3}{3!} + \cdots$. Set $u = x^2$: $e^{x^2} = 1 + x^2 + \tfrac{(x^2)^2}{2} + \tfrac{(x^2)^3}{6} + \cdots = 1 + x^2 + \tfrac{x^4}{2} + O(x^6)$. So up to $x^4$, $e^{x^2} \approx 1 + x^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}x^4$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Composition with a non-monomial inner series, e.g. ln(1 + sin x) or e^(sin x).
First write the inner series sin x = x − x³/6 + ···, then substitute that ENTIRE truncated series as u into the outer series, expanding u², u³ as polynomial squares/cubes (mind the cross-terms) and truncating at the target degree.
Product of two functions, e.g. x ln(1+x) or sin x · cos x.
Write each factor's series up to the required degree and multiply term-by-term like polynomials, dropping products whose combined degree exceeds N.
Binomial-type composite, e.g. (1+x²)^(1/2) or 1/√(1−2x).
Use (1+u)^n = 1 + nu + n(n−1)u²/2! + ··· with u set to the inner expression; check |u| < 1 for the validity range.
Quotient, e.g. (sin x)/(1+x).
Either multiply the numerator series by the reciprocal series 1/(1+x) = 1 − x + x² − ···, or perform formal long division of series, truncating throughout.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating f(x) = e^(x²) as if it required four chain-rule differentiations instead of recognising the substitution shortcut.
  • Substituting the entire infinite series instead of truncating early, then drowning in algebra.
  • Mistaking ln(1+g(x)) for a case requiring ln(1−u): missing the sign on the inner expression.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to raise the WHOLE inner expression to the power: (2x)² = 4x², (−x³)² = x⁶, not 2x² or −x⁶.
  • When squaring or cubing a multi-term inner series like x − x³/6, omitting cross-terms (e.g. (x − x³/6)² has a 2·x·(−x³/6) cross-term).
  • Sign errors with alternating series (ln(1+x), cos x), especially when the inner expression itself is negative.
  • Failing to truncate consistently — keeping a degree-6 term in one place but dropping it in another leads to a corrupted coefficient.
  • Ignoring the radius of convergence when a validity statement is asked for (e.g. ln(1+u) needs |u| < 1, so ln(1+x²) is valid for |x| < 1).

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL P3/FP2 series work; uses the Maclaurin expansions for e^x, sin x, cos x, ln(1+x), and (1+x)^n given in the IAL formula booklet. Avoid invoking general analytic-function composition theorems or formal power-series rings — at this level, justify by 'substituting into a known series and collecting terms up to the required order', and state the validity interval inherited from the standard result.
