Vectors

Reverse one vector so both emanate from the angle's vertex

Family: Direction Alignment Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when computing an angle ABC (or any angle named by three points) using the dot product, and the supplied/derived vectors do not both start at the named vertex. Typical cue: position vectors of A, B, C are given, and you compute →AB and →BC — but the angle is at B, so →AB points the wrong way. Also fires when one vector is given as →PQ and you need →QP for consistency. Any phrasing like 'angle at B', 'find ∠ABC', or 'the angle between BA and BC' should prompt this check.

Motivation — Why this works

The formula cos θ = (u·v)/(|u||v|) returns the angle between two vectors placed tail-to-tail. If the two vectors do not share a common tail at the vertex, the value returned is the supplement (180° − θ) rather than θ itself, because reversing one vector flips the sign of the dot product. Negating the offending vector (→AB → →BA = −→AB) realigns both tails at the vertex without changing magnitudes, so the formula then yields the correct interior angle directly.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the angle is named ABC, so the vertex is the middle letter B — both arms must radiate outward from B.
  2. Rule out using →AB directly: although →AB and →BA have the same magnitude, →AB points into B rather than away from it, which would give the supplementary angle.
  3. Commit to negating →AB to obtain →BA, since →BC already starts at B and only one vector needs reversing.
  4. Bridge to the first operation: flip the sign of every component of →AB to get →BA = −→AB.
  5. Tactically, only reverse one vector (not both) to minimise sign-error risk, and double-check that the chosen pair both have their tails at B before substituting into the dot-product formula.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the vertex of the named angle (middle letter of ∠ABC is B).
  2. List the two vectors currently in hand and check whether each starts at the vertex.
  3. For any vector pointing toward the vertex instead of away from it, negate every component to reverse its direction (e.g. →BA = −→AB).
  4. Confirm both resulting vectors emanate from the vertex.
  5. Substitute the corrected pair into cos θ = (u·v)/(|u||v|).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\vec{AB} = 6\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j} + 3\mathbf{k}$ and $\vec{BC} = 2\mathbf{i} + 5\mathbf{j} + 8\mathbf{k}$, find $\angle ABC$. Reverse: $\vec{BA} = -6\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j} - 3\mathbf{k}$. Then $\vec{BA}\cdot\vec{BC} = -12 + 10 - 24 = -26$, with $|\vec{BA}| = 7$, $|\vec{BC}| = \sqrt{93}$. So $\cos\angle ABC = \frac{-26}{7\sqrt{93}}$, giving $\angle ABC \approx 112.65°$ (not the supplement $67.35°$).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Angle at A in triangle ABC, with →BA and →CA given
Negate both to get →AB and →AC, so both arms emanate from A.
Angle between two lines at their intersection, with direction vectors given in arbitrary orientations
No reversal needed for the acute angle — take |u·v| in the numerator; reversal only matters when a specific directed angle is required.
Angle in a parallelogram or polygon at a labelled vertex, given position vectors
Form the two edge vectors as (neighbour − vertex), which automatically places tails at the vertex without needing to negate.
2D angle problems with coordinates
Same logic: form vertex-to-neighbour vectors by subtracting the vertex's position vector from each neighbour's.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating ∠ABC as the angle at A or C rather than at B (the middle letter is always the vertex).
  • Using →AB · →BC directly and reporting the supplement as the answer.
  • Assuming |cos θ| or taking the acute angle automatically, even when the question asks for the specific interior angle which may be obtuse.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to negate every component when reversing a 3D vector (e.g. flipping only the i-component).
  • Reversing both vectors unnecessarily — the answer is still correct but it doubles the chance of sign errors.
  • Substituting the un-reversed vector into the formula and then taking 180° minus the result, which works but is error-prone and signals weak understanding.
  • Mis-computing the magnitude of the negated vector (magnitudes are unchanged by sign reversal).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Core Pure / FP scalar-product material — uses only 3D dot product, magnitude, and cos θ = (u·v)/(|u||v|). No cross product or projection theory required.
