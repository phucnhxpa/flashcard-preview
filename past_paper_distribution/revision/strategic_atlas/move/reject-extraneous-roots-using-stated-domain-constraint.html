Algebra & Functions

Reject extraneous roots using stated domain constraint

Family: Solution Validation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever solving an equation (typically a quadratic) yields multiple candidate values for an unknown that the question has explicitly constrained — e.g. 'k is a positive constant', 'a > 0', 'n is a positive integer', 'where 0 < x < π'. Cues include the phrase 'positive constant', a stated inequality on the variable, or a contextual setup (length, probability, common ratio for convergence). Also fires when the equation-solving step (factorising, quadratic formula, squaring) is known to introduce candidates that may not satisfy the original conditions.

Motivation — Why this works

Algebraic solving processes — factorising a quadratic, squaring both sides, clearing denominators — produce all algebraic candidates but cannot enforce contextual restrictions. The stated condition (k > 0, integer, in range) is part of the problem's definition, so any candidate violating it is not a solution to the original problem. Examiners reward an explicit rejection statement because it shows the student has read the question fully. This is the standard final gate before quoting an answer.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that solving has produced two (or more) candidate values for the unknown.
  2. Recalls the constraint flagged in the stem (e.g. 'k is a positive constant') that was earmarked at the outset for end-of-problem filtering.
  3. Commits to the filter: any candidate violating the stated condition must be discarded with a one-line reason.
  4. Bridges to operation by listing each candidate against the constraint and marking accept/reject.
  5. Decides whether to additionally substitute the surviving candidate back into the original equation as a sanity check, especially if squaring or denominator-clearing was used.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. List all candidate values from the solved equation.
  2. Restate the domain constraint from the question stem.
  3. Test each candidate against the constraint; mark ✓ or ✗.
  4. Write a brief justification for any rejection (e.g. 'k = −5 < 0, so rejected since k is a positive constant').
  5. Quote the surviving value(s) as the final answer.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $k$ is a positive constant and $2k^2+3k-35=0$, factorise: $(2k-7)(k+5)=0$, so $k=\tfrac{7}{2}$ or $k=-5$. Since $k>0$, reject $k=-5$. Therefore $k=\tfrac{7}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Geometric series with sum to infinity
Constraint becomes $|r|<1$; reject any candidate ratio with $|r|\ge 1$.
Length, area, or probability context
Reject negative candidates (lengths/areas) or any outside $[0,1]$ (probabilities).
Trigonometric equation in a stated interval
Reject solutions outside the given range, e.g. $0\le\theta<360°$.
Logarithmic equation
Reject any candidate making an argument of $\log$ non-positive.
Equation solved by squaring both sides
Substitute each candidate into the original (unsquared) equation to detect spurious roots.
Discrete context (number of terms $n$)
Require $n$ to be a positive integer; reject non-integer or non-positive candidates.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating both quadratic roots as valid answers despite the stated 'positive' or range condition.
  • Assuming the larger (or positive) root is automatically correct without referencing the actual stated constraint.
  • Confusing 'positive constant' with 'non-zero', and accepting negative candidates.

Pitfalls

  • Rejecting silently — failing to write a one-line reason such as 'k > 0, so k = −5 is rejected'.
  • Forgetting to re-check after a squaring or rationalising step that may have introduced extraneous roots.
  • Discarding the wrong root through a sign slip when the constraint is misread.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL practice across P1–P4: quadratic constants, geometric ratios with $|r|<1$, trig solutions in a stated interval, and log/surd equations where domain or squaring introduces extraneous candidates.
