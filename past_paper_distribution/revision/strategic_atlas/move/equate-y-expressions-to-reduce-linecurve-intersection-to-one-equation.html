Coordinate Geometry

Equate y-expressions to reduce line–curve intersection to one equation

Family: Intersection Elimination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a problem gives a line and a curve (or two curves) both in the form y = f(x) and asks about their intersections — number of intersection points, conditions on a parameter for tangency, finding coordinates of intersection, or showing they meet/do not meet. Cue phrases include 'find the points where l meets C', 'show that l intersects C at two points', 'find the values of k for which the line meets the curve', or 'find the range of values of k such that...'. Also fires when a chord, tangent, or normal must be checked against a curve.

Motivation — Why this works

If a point lies on both graphs, its coordinates satisfy both equations simultaneously, so its y-value computed from each formula must agree: f(x) = g(x). This eliminates y and collapses a two-variable system into a single equation in x, whose roots are exactly the x-coordinates of intersection points. The number and nature of these roots (controlled by the discriminant when the equation is quadratic) then directly answer questions about how many times the graphs meet.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that both objects are given as y = (expression in x), so y can be eliminated immediately without algebraic prep.
  2. Rules out solving the system by substitution-of-x or by graphing, since a parametric/algebraic answer (often involving a parameter k) is required.
  3. Commits to equating because the resulting equation in x will be a polynomial — typically quadratic — exactly the form needed for root-counting via the discriminant.
  4. Plans to bring everything to one side as '... = 0' so coefficients in x can be read off cleanly for later discriminant or factorisation work.
  5. Decides which variable to keep (x here, since the curve is quadratic in x) and tracks the parameter k symbolically inside the coefficients.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Set f(x) = g(x), e.g. write k(2x − 1) = x² + 2x + 11.
  2. Expand any brackets: 2kx − k = x² + 2x + 11.
  3. Move all terms to one side to obtain a polynomial = 0: x² + (2 − 2k)x + (11 + k) = 0.
  4. Identify coefficients a, b, c (as expressions in the parameter) ready for discriminant or factorisation.

Worked Micro-Example

Line $y = k(2x-1)$ meets curve $y = x^2 + 2x + 11$. Equate: $k(2x-1) = x^2 + 2x + 11$. Expand and rearrange: $x^2 + (2-2k)x + (11+k) = 0$. Intersections correspond to roots of this quadratic; discriminant $(2-2k)^2 - 4(11+k)$ then governs how many.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two curves both of the form y = f(x), e.g. quadratic meets quadratic or cubic meets line
Equate f(x) = g(x); x² terms may cancel, leaving a lower-degree equation — proceed accordingly.
Curve given implicitly, e.g. circle x² + y² = r² meeting line y = mx + c
Substitute the explicit y = mx + c into the implicit equation to eliminate y, yielding a quadratic in x.
Line given as x = h (vertical) meeting y = f(x)
No equating needed; substitute x = h directly into f to find the unique intersection y-value.
Parametric curve (x(t), y(t)) meeting a line ax + by = c
Substitute x(t), y(t) into the line equation to get a single equation in t whose roots give intersection parameters.
Tangency condition for a parameter
After equating and rearranging to a quadratic = 0, set the discriminant equal to zero rather than just non-negative.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the problem graphically and trying to read intersections from a sketch instead of producing the algebraic intersection equation.
  • Solving f(x) = 0 and g(x) = 0 separately (finding x-intercepts) instead of f(x) = g(x).
  • Setting x-values equal instead of y-values when both equations are y = ...

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when transposing terms — particularly with parameter-bearing terms like −2kx and +k — leading to a wrong coefficient (2 − 2k) or constant (11 + k).
  • Forgetting to expand brackets fully before collecting like terms, e.g. leaving k(2x − 1) unexpanded.
  • Dropping the parameter from a coefficient when rearranging, or absorbing it into the wrong term.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure technique (P1/P2): used routinely for line–curve intersection, tangency, and parameter-range problems, and underpins discriminant-based reasoning in later coordinate-geometry questions.
