Complex Numbers

Invoke Conjugate Root Theorem to obtain second root for free

Family: Theorem Invocation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A polynomial equation (quadratic, cubic, quartic, ...) with all real coefficients is given, together with one non-real complex root, and the question asks to state, find, or 'show that' another root exists. Cue phrases include 'given that z = a+bi is a root', 'write down another root', or 'hence find the other roots'. Also fires when factorising a real polynomial knowing one complex root, since the conjugate pair gives a real quadratic factor.

Motivation — Why this works

If P(z) has real coefficients and P(α) = 0, then taking conjugates of P(α)=0 gives P(α*) = 0 because conjugation distributes over sums and products and fixes every real coefficient. Hence non-real roots of real polynomials always come in conjugate pairs {a+bi, a−bi}. This converts a one-mark 'show/state' into a single line and, more powerfully, lets the conjugate pair be combined into a real quadratic factor (z−α)(z−α*) = z² − 2Re(α)z + |α|² for subsequent factorisation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Student notices the polynomial's coefficients are all real numbers (no i appearing) and that the given root 1−2i is non-real.
  2. They rule out brute-force substitution of a candidate root or expansion of (z−(1−2i))(z−?) as unnecessary effort for a 1-mark write-down.
  3. They commit to the Conjugate Root Theorem because its hypothesis (real coefficients, non-real root) is exactly satisfied.
  4. They bridge to the operation by forming the conjugate of 1−2i, namely 1+2i, by flipping the sign of the imaginary part only.
  5. If subsequent parts ask for remaining roots, they pair the conjugates into the real quadratic factor z² − 2z + 5 and divide the polynomial by it.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Verify explicitly that every coefficient of the polynomial is real.
  2. Cite the Complex Conjugate Root Theorem: non-real roots of real polynomials occur in conjugate pairs.
  3. Write down the conjugate of the given root by negating its imaginary part.
  4. (If needed for further parts) form the real quadratic factor (z−α)(z−α*) = z² − 2Re(α)z + |α|², then polynomial-divide to find remaining root(s).

Worked Micro-Example

Given that $z_1 = 1-2i$ is a root of $z^3 - 3z^2 + 7z - 5 = 0$. Coefficients $-3, 7, -5$ are real, so by the Conjugate Root Theorem $z_2 = \overline{1-2i} = 1+2i$ is also a root. Then $(z-(1-2i))(z-(1+2i)) = z^2 - 2z + 5$, and dividing gives $z^3 - 3z^2 + 7z - 5 = (z^2 - 2z + 5)(z - 1)$, so the third root is $z_3 = 1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quartic with real coefficients and one complex root given
Conjugate gives a second root immediately; pair them into a real quadratic factor and divide to reduce to a quadratic in the remaining two roots.
Odd-degree real polynomial with no roots given
Use the theorem in reverse: complex roots pair off, so the polynomial must have at least one real root — useful for existence arguments.
Factorising a real polynomial when a complex root is found by other means (e.g. via de Moivre)
Pair each non-real root with its conjugate to assemble real quadratic factors of the polynomial.
Polynomial with at least one non-real coefficient
Theorem does NOT apply; the conjugate need not be a root, so substitution or other methods are required.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to verify the second root by substituting (1+2i) directly into the cubic — correct but wasteful for a one-mark 'state' question.
  • Believing the theorem requires the polynomial to be monic or to have integer coefficients; only realness is needed.
  • Assuming the theorem produces a third complex root automatically — it only pairs each non-real root with its own conjugate.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to state explicitly 'since the coefficients are real' — examiners require this justification for the mark.
  • Confusing the conjugate a−bi with the negative −a−bi or with the reciprocal 1/(a+bi).
  • Applying the theorem to a polynomial that secretly contains an i in a coefficient (e.g. after a substitution), where it is invalid.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope (FP1/FP2 complex numbers and roots of polynomials); commonly examined on cubics and quartics with one given complex root.
