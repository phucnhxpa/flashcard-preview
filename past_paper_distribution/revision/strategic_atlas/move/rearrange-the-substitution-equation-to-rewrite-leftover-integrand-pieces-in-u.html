Integration

Rearrange the substitution equation to rewrite leftover integrand pieces in u

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a chosen substitution u = f(x) names part of the integrand (typically a denominator base or bracketed expression) but a separate factor in the numerator still sits in the original variable. The cue is that u-substitution doesn't fully translate the integrand on its own — there is a residual sin x, x, e^x or similar that must be expressed in u. Common phrasings: 'use u = 3 + 4 sin x', 'let u = 1 + x²', where after handling du and the bracket there is still a stray algebraic/trig piece.

Motivation — Why this works

If u = f(x) is invertible on the interval (or at least algebraically rearrangeable), then any occurrence of the inner function f(x) inside the integrand can be replaced by u, and any occurrence of a simple piece of f(x) (like 4 sin x or x²) can be isolated by rearranging u = f(x). This turns a mixed expression into a pure function of u, which is the whole point of substitution. The differential du = f'(x) dx simultaneously absorbs the derivative factor, so the residual algebraic piece is handled by rearrangement, not by extra trig identities.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that u = 3 + 4 sin x already matches the denominator (3 + 4 sin x)² = u², so the bracket is handled.
  2. Rule out trying to rewrite cos x via cos²x = 1 − sin²x — that would introduce a square root and is unnecessary because cos x will cancel with du.
  3. Commit to the substitution because du = 4 cos x dx exactly absorbs the cos x in the integrand, leaving only sin x to deal with.
  4. Bridge: rearrange u = 3 + 4 sin x to get 4 sin x = u − 3, so the leftover 16 sin x becomes 4(u − 3).
  5. Tactically, keep track of constants: 16 sin x dx → 4(u − 3) · (du / 4 cos x) · cos x, so the cos x's cancel and a factor of 1/4 emerges, giving (u − 3)/u² du.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write u = f(x) and isolate the residual piece (e.g. 4 sin x = u − 3).
  2. Differentiate: du = f'(x) dx, then identify which factor in the integrand pairs with f'(x) dx to become du.
  3. Replace the bracket by u, the residual piece by its expression in u, and dx · f'(x)-factor by du.
  4. Simplify constants and collect into a clean rational/polynomial integrand in u.
  5. Integrate in u, then back-substitute u = f(x) at the end.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int \dfrac{16 \sin x \cos x}{(3 + 4\sin x)^2}\,dx$. Let $u = 3 + 4\sin x$, so $du = 4\cos x\,dx$ and $4\sin x = u-3$. Then $16\sin x\,dx = 4(u-3)\cdot\dfrac{du}{4\cos x}$, and the integral becomes $\int \dfrac{4(u-3)}{u^2}\cdot\dfrac{1}{4}\,du = \int \dfrac{u-3}{u^2}\,du = \ln|u| + \dfrac{3}{u} + C$. Back-substitute: $\ln|3+4\sin x| + \dfrac{3}{3+4\sin x} + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Integrand has x·(1+x²)^n with u = 1 + x²
Rearrange u = 1 + x² to x² = u − 1 if a stray x² appears beyond the one absorbed by du = 2x dx.
Integrand involves e^x and (1 + e^x)^n with u = 1 + e^x
Use e^x = u − 1 to handle a residual e^x not paired with the differential.
Trig substitution u = cos x with sin x dx
If sin²x or sin³x remains, write sin²x = 1 − u² before integrating.
Definite integral version
Convert the x-limits to u-limits using u = f(x) and avoid back-substitution at the end.
u = tan(x/2) (Weierstrass-style, FP scope only at light level)
Rearrange to express sin x and cos x in u; this is the same rearrangement principle applied symmetrically.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to express cos x in terms of u via cos²x = 1 − sin²x when cos x already cancels with du.
  • Believing that once the bracket is replaced by u the job is done, ignoring the residual sin x (or x, e^x) that still needs rewriting.
  • Mistaking the residual piece for something that requires a second substitution rather than a simple algebraic rearrangement of u = f(x).

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the constant factor when isolating, e.g. writing 16 sin x = u − 3 instead of 4(u − 3).
  • Forgetting dx ≠ du and omitting the 1/f'(x) factor when solving for dx.
  • Sign errors when rearranging u = a − f(x) (e.g. u = 1 − cos x) and getting cos x = 1 − u with the wrong sign.
  • In a definite integral, failing to change the limits when switching to u.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure (C34/P3) integration-by-substitution technique; the algebraic rearrangement of u = f(x) to handle a residual factor is explicitly expected and appears regularly in exam questions.
