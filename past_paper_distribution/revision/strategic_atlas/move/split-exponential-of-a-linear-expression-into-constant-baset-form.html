Split exponential of a linear expression into constant × base^(±t) form

Family: Index Law Decomposition Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever you have a single exponential whose exponent is a linear expression in t, e.g. 10^(c + kt), 10^(c − kt), or e^(a + bt), and the target form is a product like p·q^t, a·b^t, A·e^(bt), or p·q^(−t) where p/a/A and q/b are pure numerical constants. Visual cues: the exponent contains both a constant term and a t-term joined by + or −, while the required answer separates a leading constant from a t-dependent power. Equivalently, you have just exponentiated both sides of a log-linear equation log N = kt + c (or ln N = kt + c) and now need N as a product. Alternative phrasing: 'convert from sum-in-the-exponent to product-of-powers'.

Motivation — Why this works

The index law x^(A+B) = x^A · x^B converts addition inside an exponent into multiplication of separate powers, peeling off the constant part as a standalone factor. A second index law x^(kt) = (x^k)^t then collapses the coefficient of t into the base, so −t (or t) sits cleanly as the sole exponent of one factor. Together these two identities are the only direct route from 10^(c+kt) to the product structure p·q^t demanded by the target form, exposing p and q as t-free numerical constants ready to be evaluated.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the exponent is linear in t: it contains a constant term and a t-term combined additively (e.g. 2.74 − 0.079t or 0.0646t + 1.478).
  2. Notice the target form is a PRODUCT (a·b^t or p·q^(−t)), so addition in the exponent must become multiplication of two factors.
  3. Rule out direct substitution or taking logs again — these don't expose p and q. Rule out trying to read off q = 10^(coefficient) before extracting t from the exponent, which leaves the wrong structure.
  4. Commit to the index law x^(A+B) = x^A · x^B as the bridge from additive-exponent to multiplicative-factors structure.
  5. First operation: split the exponent at the + or − sign into two separate powers, putting the constant-only power first (this becomes p or a).
  6. Second operation (if target needs base^t with t alone): apply (x^k)^t = x^(kt) in reverse to absorb the coefficient of t into the base, giving q = x^k.
  7. Read off p (or a) and q (or b) as numerical constants, evaluating to required precision.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the exponent as A + B (or A − B) where A is the constant term and B is the t-term, e.g. 10^(2.74 − 0.079t) has A = 2.74, B = −0.079t.
  2. Apply x^(A+B) = x^A · x^B: 10^(2.74 − 0.079t) = 10^(2.74) · 10^(−0.079t).
  3. If target is p·q^(±t), apply (x^k)^t = x^(kt) in reverse: 10^(−0.079t) = (10^(0.079))^(−t), or 10^(0.0646t) = (10^(0.0646))^t.
  4. Identify and evaluate the constants: p = 10^(2.74) ≈ 550, q = 10^(0.079) ≈ 1.20 (or a = 10^(1.478), b = 10^(0.0646)).
  5. State the final form, e.g. N = p·q^(−t) or N = a·b^t with the numerical values.

Worked Micro-Example

Convert $N = 10^{0.0646t + 1.478}$ into the form $N = ab^t$. Step 1: split the exponent — $N = 10^{1.478} \cdot 10^{0.0646t}$. Step 2: collapse the coefficient of $t$ into the base — $10^{0.0646t} = (10^{0.0646})^t$. Step 3: read off $a = 10^{1.478} \approx 30.06$ and $b = 10^{0.0646} \approx 1.16$, giving $N \approx 30.06 \times 1.16^t$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Natural-log model: ln N = kt + c, target N = A e^(kt)
Exponentiate to get N = e^(kt+c), then split as N = e^c · e^(kt) so A = e^c. Same identity, base e instead of 10.
Target requires q^(−t) rather than q^t (decay model)
Keep the minus sign attached to t when applying (x^k)^t in reverse: x^(−kt) = (x^k)^(−t), so q = x^k (not x^(−k)).
Reverse direction: given x = pq^t, set up a straight-line graph
Take logs to get log x = log p + t log q, a linear graph in t with gradient log q and intercept log p.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to match q directly to 10^(coefficient of t) without first extracting −t from the exponent, which leaves the sign or exponent structure wrong.
  • Reading 10^(c+kt) as if c and kt were separate terms to be added rather than combined inside one exponent — leading to the false 'simplification' 10^c + 10^(kt).

Pitfalls

  • Misapplying the sum law as 10^(A+B) = 10^A + 10^B (confusing it with distributivity) — it must be a PRODUCT.
  • Misapplying the power law: writing (10^(0.079))^(−t) as 10^(0.079 − t) instead of 10^(−0.079t).
  • Placing the wrong factor as p/a — the constant is the one with NO t in its exponent.
  • Rounding p and q too early or to too few significant figures, losing accuracy marks (especially in 'show that' questions where 3 s.f. is typically required).

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 exponentials and logarithms. Uses standard index laws a^(m+n) = a^m·a^n and a^(mn) = (a^m)^n (assumed knowledge, not in formula booklet). No need for complex bases or non-real exponents — bases are always positive real constants like 10 or e.
