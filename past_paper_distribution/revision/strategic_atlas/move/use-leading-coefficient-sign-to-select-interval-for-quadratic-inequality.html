Use leading coefficient sign to select interval for quadratic inequality

Family: Sign Analysis Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a quadratic inequality $q(x) < 0$, $q(x) > 0$, $q(x) \le 0$, or $q(x) \ge 0$ has been reduced to factored form $(x - \alpha)(x - \beta)$ with two distinct real roots. Cues include: 'solve $x^2 + bx + c < 0$', 'find the set of values of $x$ for which...', or a quadratic appearing after rearranging an inequality to one side. Also fires when a discriminant condition $b^2 - 4ac > 0$ has been turned into a quadratic inequality in a parameter.

Motivation — Why this works

A quadratic with positive leading coefficient is a U-shape, so it lies below the $x$-axis only between its two roots and above the $x$-axis outside them; with a negative leading coefficient the picture flips. Reading the sign directly from the parabola's shape avoids three-interval point-testing and matches the visual intuition expected in Edexcel mark schemes. The principle generalises: the sign of any factored expression alternates across simple roots, and the leading coefficient fixes the sign in the outermost region.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the inequality is quadratic in $x$ and has been (or can be) factorised into two linear factors with distinct real roots.
  2. Rule out plugging in test values in three intervals — that is slower and error-prone when the parabola's shape gives the answer immediately.
  3. Commit to the shape argument: read off the leading coefficient's sign to decide whether 'between roots' or 'outside roots' is the negative region.
  4. Bridge to operation: order the roots as $\alpha < \beta$, then match the inequality direction to the correct region.
  5. Tactical check: keep strict vs non-strict inequalities consistent with the original, and write the final answer as a single compound statement (e.g. $\alpha < x < \beta$) or a union ($x < \alpha$ or $x > \beta$).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rearrange the inequality so one side is $0$ and factorise the quadratic.
  2. Identify the two roots $\alpha < \beta$.
  3. Note the sign of the leading coefficient: positive ⇒ U-shape, negative ⇒ ∩-shape.
  4. Match shape to inequality: U-shape is negative between roots, positive outside; ∩-shape is positive between roots, negative outside.
  5. Write the solution interval(s), preserving strict vs non-strict inequalities from the original.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $x^2 - 2x - 8 < 0$. Factorise: $(x-4)(x+2) < 0$, giving roots $x = -2, 4$. The leading coefficient is $+1$ so the parabola opens upward and is negative between the roots. Hence $-2 < x < 4$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Negative leading coefficient, e.g. $-x^2 + x + 6 \ge 0$
Either factor as $-(x-3)(x+2) \ge 0$ and use the ∩-shape (solution between roots: $-2 \le x \le 3$), or multiply by $-1$ and flip the inequality first.
Same factorisation but the opposite inequality, e.g. $x^2 - 2x - 8 > 0$
Take the complementary region: $x < -2$ or $x > 4$.
Non-strict inequality $q(x) \le 0$
Include the roots: $\alpha \le x \le \beta$.
Discriminant condition for distinct real roots, e.g. $k^2 - 4k - 5 > 0$ in a parameter $k$
Apply the same shape argument to the quadratic in $k$: $(k-5)(k+1) > 0$ ⇒ $k < -1$ or $k > 5$.
Repeated root (discriminant zero), e.g. $(x-3)^2 \ge 0$
The parabola touches the axis; solution is all $\mathbb{R}$ for $\ge 0$, only $x=3$ for $\le 0$, and no solutions for strict $< 0$.
No real roots (discriminant negative)
Sign of $q(x)$ matches the leading coefficient everywhere; inequality is either all $\mathbb{R}$ or empty.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing 'less than zero' (between roots for a U-shape) with 'greater than zero' (outside the roots), giving $x < -2$ or $x > 4$ instead of $-2 < x < 4$.
  • Writing the solution as a union $x < -2 \cup x > 4$ when the correct region is the single interval between the roots.
  • Treating a negative leading coefficient as if it were positive without flipping the shape, leading to the complementary region.
  • Reading $(x-4)(x+2)$ as having roots $4$ and $2$ instead of $4$ and $-2$.

Pitfalls

  • Using $\le$ instead of $<$ (or vice versa) when the original inequality is strict — costs an accuracy mark on Edexcel.
  • Multiplying through by a negative leading coefficient without reversing the inequality sign.
  • Writing the answer as two separate statements 'x > -2 and x < 4' without combining into $-2 < x < 4$, which can be marked ambiguous.
  • Forgetting to check that the discriminant is positive before assuming two distinct roots; the method needs adjustment for repeated or no real roots.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure 1 (P1) content for solving quadratic inequalities, and reused in P3/P4 for discriminant-based parameter problems and in Further Pure for sign analysis of rational inequalities.
