Strategic Atlas / Vectors / Overdetermined Inconsistency
Vectors

Solve two equations, substitute into the third to expose contradiction

Family: Overdetermined Inconsistency Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when equating two vector line parametrisations $\mathbf{r}_1(\lambda)=\mathbf{r}_2(\mu)$ produces three scalar equations in just two unknowns $\lambda,\mu$. Typical phrasings: 'show the lines do not intersect', 'show the lines are skew' (combined with a non-parallel check), or 'show no point of intersection exists'. The cue is always 3 equations, 2 unknowns, and a demand to prove non-intersection.

Motivation — Why this works

Two linear equations in two unknowns generically pin down unique values of $\lambda$ and $\mu$. The remaining equation is then an independent constraint that the candidate solution must also satisfy. If it fails, the full system is inconsistent and no common point exists; if it holds, the lines meet and the point can be read off. This bypasses the heavier machinery of row-reducing a $3\times 2$ augmented matrix.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the system is overdetermined: 3 component equations but only 2 parameters, so a consistency check is built in.
  2. Rule out solving all three pairwise and comparing — valid but wasteful and error-prone.
  3. Commit to solving any two equations simultaneously and using the third as the test of consistency.
  4. Pick the equation whose coefficient on $\lambda$ or $\mu$ is $\pm 1$ to isolate cleanly without fractions.
  5. After finding $\lambda,\mu$, substitute into the unused equation and compare LHS with RHS; a numerical mismatch is the contradiction that closes the argument.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the three scalar equations (i), (ii), (iii) from equating $\mathbf{i}$, $\mathbf{j}$, $\mathbf{k}$ components.
  2. Pick the cleanest pair (say (i) and (ii)) and isolate one parameter from the simpler equation.
  3. Substitute to find numerical values of $\lambda$ and $\mu$.
  4. Substitute both values into the unused equation (iii) and compute LHS and RHS.
  5. If $\mathrm{LHS}\neq\mathrm{RHS}$, state the contradiction explicitly and conclude the lines do not intersect.

Worked Micro-Example

From (i) $\lambda+5\mu=6$, so $\lambda=6-5\mu$. Sub into (ii) $2\lambda-4\mu=-9$: $12-14\mu=-9$, giving $\mu=\tfrac{3}{2}$ and $\lambda=-\tfrac{3}{2}$. Test in (iii) $3\lambda-8\mu=-8$: LHS $=3(-\tfrac{3}{2})-8(\tfrac{3}{2})=-\tfrac{33}{2}\neq -8$. Contradiction, so the lines do not intersect.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Proving two lines intersect and finding the point
Same procedure, but the third equation IS satisfied; substitute $\lambda$ (or $\mu$) back into the line equation to give the intersection point.
Proving two lines are skew
First confirm direction vectors are not parallel (no scalar multiple), then run this move to show non-intersection; both together establish skewness.
Line meets a plane given parametrically (Further Maths)
Equating the line's parametrisation to the plane's two-parameter form gives 3 equations in 3 unknowns; isolate two and check the third for consistency in the same way.
Coefficients all awkward
Use elimination (add/subtract scaled equations) instead of substitution to avoid fractions, then test the unused equation.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the third equation as redundant and concluding intersection after solving only two equations.
  • Confusing 'directions are not parallel' with 'lines do not intersect' — these are independent facts and both are needed for skew.
  • Getting a near-miss numerical answer in the third equation and rounding to declare intersection.

Pitfalls

  • Isolating from an equation with awkward coefficients, introducing fractions early and increasing arithmetic risk.
  • Forgetting to perform the consistency check on the third equation at all.
  • Sign or fraction slips when combining terms like $3(-\tfrac{3}{2})-8(\tfrac{3}{2})$; convert to a common denominator before subtracting.
  • Stating 'no solution' without explicitly identifying which equation fails — examiners want the contradiction shown.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure/Further Pure technique for line–line intersection and skew-line proofs in 3D; uses only simultaneous linear equations and vector component comparison, no matrix rank arguments required.
