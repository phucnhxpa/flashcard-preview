Strategic Atlas / Integration / Reverse Substitution
Integration

Reverse the substitution to express the antiderivative in the original variable

Family: Reverse Substitution Frequency: 5 questions

Trigger — When to use

After evaluating an indefinite integral via a substitution (u = g(x), or a trig/hyperbolic substitution like x = a cosh θ), the antiderivative is currently expressed in the substitution variable but the question is in x. Cues: (i) the integral has no limits, so changing limits is not an option; (ii) the working has produced terms like ln|u|, u^n, θ, coth θ, etc., which still reference the dummy variable; (iii) the problem statement (or a follow-up part) requires the answer in x. Equivalently: 'I have F(u) + C but I need F(x) + C', or 'I have an answer in θ but x = a cosh θ was the link'.

Motivation — Why this works

An indefinite integral is a family of antiderivatives of f(x); it must therefore be a function of x, not of an internal dummy variable. Back-substitution undoes the forward substitution, restoring the variable the question was posed in, and often allows further simplification via log laws, hyperbolic identities (cosh²θ − sinh²θ = 1), or factoring out common surds. This step also prepares the antiderivative for any subsequent definite evaluation where the limits are stated in x. The underlying principle is simply that g and g⁻¹ are inverses, so replacing u by g(x) reverses the change of variable exactly.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the answer is currently in u (or θ) but the original integrand was in x — the substitution must be undone.
  2. Rule out the alternative of changing limits to u-values: this only works for definite integrals, and here there are none (or the question explicitly asks for an x-expression).
  3. Commit to back-substitution: write down the forward relation (e.g. u = g(x), or cosh θ = x/a) and any auxiliary identities needed to convert composite expressions (e.g. sinh θ = √(cosh²θ − 1), or u − 2 = √x).
  4. Replace every occurrence of the dummy variable; for trig/hyperbolic θ, convert θ itself via the inverse function (arcosh, arsinh, arctan) and convert ratios (coth, sec, etc.) by rewriting in terms of the basic pair already known in x.
  5. Tidy up: combine logs using ln a − ln b = ln(a/b), factor common surds, and ensure the constant of integration +C is present.
  6. Sanity-check by differentiating the final x-expression mentally (or checking one term) to confirm it matches the original integrand.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the forward substitution (e.g. u = 2 + √x, or u = √(x+1), or x = 4 cosh θ) and write down its inverse explicitly.
  2. For each non-trivial composite (e.g. coth θ, or u − 2), express it in x using the forward relation plus any Pythagorean/algebraic identity: e.g. sinh θ = √(x² − a²)/a, hence coth θ = x/√(x² − a²).
  3. Substitute term-by-term into the antiderivative F(u) (or F(θ)) to obtain F(x).
  4. Apply log laws to merge (1/k) ln|A| − (1/k) ln|B| → (1/k) ln|A/B| where applicable.
  5. Append + C and, if the next part is a definite evaluation, evaluate F(x) at the given x-limits.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\int \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}(2+\sqrt{x})^2}\,dx$ with $u = 2+\sqrt{x}$, suppose the working yields $\tfrac{1}{4}\ln|u-2| - \tfrac{1}{4}\ln|u| + \tfrac{1}{2u} + C$. Back-substitute $u-2 = \sqrt{x}$ and $u = 2+\sqrt{x}$, then combine logs: $\tfrac{1}{4}\ln\left|\dfrac{\sqrt{x}}{2+\sqrt{x}}\right| + \dfrac{1}{2(2+\sqrt{x})} + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Algebraic substitution u = g(x) with g a simple polynomial/surd (e.g. u = 2x²+3, u = √(x+1))
Replace u directly by g(x); use fractional exponents carefully — (√(x+1))³ = (x+1)^{3/2}, not (x+1)³.
Hyperbolic substitution x = a cosh θ (or a sinh θ) for √(x²−a²) or √(x²+a²)
Use θ = arcosh(x/a) (or arsinh(x/a)); for derived ratios like coth θ, rewrite as cosh θ / sinh θ and obtain sinh θ from cosh²θ − sinh²θ = 1.
Trig substitution x = a sin θ or a tan θ
θ = arcsin(x/a) or arctan(x/a); sec θ, tan θ etc. are recovered from a right-triangle picture or sin²+cos²=1.
Definite integral with limits already converted to u
Skip back-substitution entirely — evaluate F(u) at the u-limits directly.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating arcosh(x/a) as something that needs further simplification to cosh θ — it does not; arcosh is the inverse and is the final form.
  • Inverting the ratio when reconstructing coth θ (writing sinh θ / cosh θ instead of cosh θ / sinh θ).
  • Believing that for an indefinite integral one may legitimately leave the answer in u 'because u was defined earlier'.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting + C on an indefinite integral.
  • Substituting an auxiliary expression incorrectly: e.g. writing u − 2 = 2 + √x instead of u − 2 = √x when u = 2 + √x.
  • Dropping factors when ratios cancel: e.g. sinh θ = √(x²−16)/4, not √(x²−16); the /4 must be carried through coth θ.
  • Mishandling fractional powers when back-substituting: (√(x+1))³ becomes (x+1)^{3/2}, not (x+1)³.
  • Failing to combine logs with the same coefficient, leaving an unsimplified answer that can lose presentation marks.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL scope: P3/P4 covers algebraic u-substitution and basic trig substitution; FP2/FP3 covers hyperbolic substitutions with arcosh, arsinh in the formula booklet (including the logarithmic forms arcosh(x/a) = ln(x + √(x²−a²)) − ln a). Avoid invoking general inverse function theorems or complex-analytic continuations — they are out of scope.
