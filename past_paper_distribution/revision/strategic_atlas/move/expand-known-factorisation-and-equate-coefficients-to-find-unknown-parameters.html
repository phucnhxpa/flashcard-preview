Algebra & Functions

Expand known factorisation and equate coefficients to find unknown parameters

Family: Coefficient Matching Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a polynomial with unknown coefficients (e.g. p, q) has been fully factorised by other means — typically via known roots, conjugate pairs, or a given linear factor — and the remaining task is to recover those unknowns. Cue phrases include 'find the values of p and q', 'hence determine the constants', or an identity of the form (known factors) ≡ (polynomial with unknowns). Use this move once the factorisation is secure but at least one coefficient of the original polynomial is still symbolic.

Motivation — Why this works

Two polynomials that are identically equal must agree coefficient-by-coefficient. Expanding the known factored form produces a concrete polynomial whose z^k coefficients can be read off directly and matched against p, q in the original. This avoids substituting awkward values (complex roots, fractions) into f(z) = 0, replacing complex arithmetic with routine real expansion.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that the polynomial is now fully factorised but the original statement still contains symbolic coefficients p and q.
  2. Rules out substituting a complex or fractional root into f(z) = 0 — it works but introduces messy arithmetic in two unknowns.
  3. Commits to expanding the product because all factors have real coefficients and the expansion is short.
  4. Plans to focus only on the z^2 and z coefficients, since the leading and constant coefficients are already pinned down and serve as a check.
  5. Inside the expansion, tracks cross-terms carefully: each z^k coefficient is a small sum that must be collected before equating.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the identity: (known quadratic factor)(known linear factor) ≡ original polynomial in p, q.
  2. Expand the left-hand side term-by-term, keeping like powers grouped.
  3. Collect coefficients of each power of z on the LHS.
  4. Equate the coefficient of z^2 to p and the coefficient of z to q.
  5. Verify the leading and constant terms agree as a consistency check.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $f(z) = 2z^3 + pz^2 + qz - 41 \equiv (z^2 - 10z + 41)(2z - 1)$. Expanding: $(z^2 - 10z + 41)(2z - 1) = 2z^3 - z^2 - 20z^2 + 10z + 82z - 41 = 2z^3 - 21z^2 + 92z - 41$. Matching coefficients gives $p = -21$ and $q = 92$, with leading $2z^3$ and constant $-41$ confirming the identity.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cubic with one given linear factor and a quadratic factor having unknown coefficients
Polynomial division (or matching) recovers the quadratic factor; the same coefficient-matching idea applies in reverse.
Quartic with a known pair of complex conjugate roots and unknown real coefficients
Multiply (z - α)(z - ᾱ) into a real quadratic, then expand against the remaining real quadratic factor and equate.
Partial-fractions style identity A(x-1) + B(x+2) ≡ linear
Same equate-coefficients principle, just on a lower-degree identity; or substitute strategic x-values instead.
Only one unknown remains in the original polynomial
Substituting a known root into f(z) = 0 is faster than full expansion — one equation in one unknown.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating ≡ as a conditional equation and trying to solve for z, rather than recognising it as an identity in z.
  • Assuming p and q can be read off from a single term without expanding the full product.

Pitfalls

  • Sign slips, especially on the cross-term −10z × (−1) = +10z.
  • Missing a cross-term when expanding (e.g. forgetting 41 × 2z or 41 × (−1)).
  • Failing to use the leading and constant coefficients as a sanity check on the expansion.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL technique used in FP1/FP2 complex-roots questions and in C2/C4 polynomial identity problems; relies only on polynomial expansion and the identity principle.
