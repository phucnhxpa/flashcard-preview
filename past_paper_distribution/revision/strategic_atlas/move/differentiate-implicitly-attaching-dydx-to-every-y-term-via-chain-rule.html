Differentiation

Differentiate implicitly, attaching dy/dx to every y-term via chain rule

Family: Implicit Differentiation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The curve equation entangles x and y so that y cannot be isolated as an explicit function of x (e.g. x²ln y + 1/x + 2y = 3, x³ + y³ = 6xy, e^{xy} = x + y). Cues include y appearing inside transcendental functions, products of x and y, or in multiple terms of mixed degree. If you are asked for dy/dx but cannot get y = f(x) cleanly, implicit differentiation is the standard route.

Motivation — Why this works

Treating y as an unknown differentiable function of x lets us apply d/dx to both sides of the relation, with the chain rule producing a dy/dx factor whenever a y-term is differentiated. The resulting equation is linear in dy/dx, so it can be solved algebraically without ever needing y in closed form. This works because if F(x, y(x)) = c, then dF/dx = 0 by the chain rule, giving a usable relation between x, y, and dy/dx.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice y appears in non-invertible positions (inside ln, multiplied by powers of x, or in several terms) so y = f(x) cannot be written explicitly.
  2. Rule out direct differentiation of y as a function of x and rule out rearranging first — algebra cannot isolate y.
  3. Commit to implicit differentiation: differentiate both sides of the equation with respect to x, treating y as y(x).
  4. Bridge: scan the equation term by term, classifying each as x-only, y-only (needs chain rule), or mixed (needs product rule plus chain rule).
  5. Tactically, plan to collect all dy/dx terms on one side after differentiating, then factor and divide to isolate dy/dx.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Apply d/dx to every term on both sides of the equation.
  2. For x-only terms (e.g. x⁻¹), differentiate using standard rules.
  3. For y-only terms f(y), use the chain rule: d/dx[f(y)] = f'(y)·dy/dx.
  4. For mixed terms involving both x and y, use the product rule combined with the chain rule.
  5. Differentiate constants on the RHS to 0.
  6. Collect all dy/dx terms on one side, factor out dy/dx, and divide to obtain dy/dx in terms of x and y.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $x^2 \ln y + \tfrac{1}{x} + 2y = 3$, differentiate term by term: $2x\ln y + x^2\cdot\tfrac{1}{y}\tfrac{dy}{dx} - \tfrac{1}{x^2} + 2\tfrac{dy}{dx} = 0$. Collect: $\left(\tfrac{x^2}{y} + 2\right)\tfrac{dy}{dx} = \tfrac{1}{x^2} - 2x\ln y$. Hence $\tfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{\tfrac{1}{x^2} - 2x\ln y}{\tfrac{x^2}{y} + 2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Implicit equation with a product term such as xy or x²y
Apply the product rule to the mixed term, then chain-rule the y-factor: d/dx(xy) = y + x·dy/dx.
Trigonometric implicit relation, e.g. sin(xy) = x + y
Chain rule produces cos(xy) times d/dx(xy), where d/dx(xy) itself needs the product rule.
Exponential implicit relation, e.g. e^{xy} = x + y
Differentiate e^{xy} as e^{xy}·(y + x dy/dx); proceed as before.
Parametric form x = f(t), y = g(t)
Same chain-rule logic but use dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) instead of differentiating an implicit equation.
Finding a tangent or normal at a specified point on an implicit curve
After implicit differentiation, substitute the given (x, y) before solving for dy/dx, avoiding algebraic isolation.

Common Misreadings

  • Differentiating ln y as 1/y without appending dy/dx, forgetting that y depends on x.
  • Treating implicit equations as if y were an independent variable, producing partial-derivative-style expressions.
  • Believing the equation must be rearranged into y = f(x) before differentiation is possible.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the chain rule on standalone y-terms (e.g. differentiating 2y as 2 instead of 2·dy/dx).
  • Omitting the product rule on mixed terms like x²y or xy².
  • Algebra slips when collecting and factoring dy/dx terms, especially with negative signs.
  • Differentiating a constant such as 3 to anything other than 0.

A-level Scope Notes

Implicit differentiation is an Edexcel IAL Pure (P3/P4) technique; all derivatives used (powers, ln, exp, trig, product/chain rules) are within A-level scope and no partial derivatives are required.
