Trigonometry

Convert cosine equation to sine via co-function identity

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when one part of a question gives a sine equation sin(f(x)) = k with known smallest positive solution α, and a later part asks to solve cos(f(x)) = k (same k, same inner function). The examiner's intent is to reuse α rather than invoke arccos. Cue phrases: 'hence solve', 'using your answer to part (a)', or matching arguments and right-hand sides between the two equations.

Motivation — Why this works

The co-function identity cos(θ) = sin(π/2 − θ) converts the cosine equation into a sine equation with the same RHS k. Since sin(α/12) = k is already known, the general sine solution sin(A) = sin(B) ⇒ A = B + 2nπ or A = π − B + 2nπ produces x in terms of α directly. This bypasses arccos and keeps the answer in the symbolic form the question expects.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the second equation has the same k and same inner argument x/12 as the first, so the answer is meant to come from α, not from a fresh inverse evaluation.
  2. I rule out using sin²+cos² = 1 to get a numeric k and solving cos(x/12) = k independently — that loses the link to α.
  3. I commit to a co-function bridge because cos(θ) = sin(π/2 − θ) directly turns the new equation into sin(something) = sin(α/12).
  4. First concrete operation: rewrite cos(x/12) = k as sin(π/2 − x/12) = sin(α/12).
  5. Tactically I apply both general sine families, solve each for x, and screen for the smallest positive value, watching the sign flip from the −x/12 term.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Apply cos(θ) = sin(π/2 − θ) to rewrite cos(x/12) = k as sin(π/2 − x/12) = k.
  2. Replace k with sin(α/12) to get sin(π/2 − x/12) = sin(α/12).
  3. Family A: π/2 − x/12 = α/12 + 2nπ, giving x = 6π − α − 24nπ.
  4. Family B: π/2 − x/12 = π − α/12 + 2nπ, giving x = α − 6π − 24nπ.
  5. Tabulate small integer n in each family, discard non-positive values.
  6. Select the smallest positive candidate; here 6π − α (taking n = 0 in Family A).

Worked Micro-Example

Given sin(α/12) = k with α the smallest positive solution, solve cos(x/12) = k. Write cos(x/12) = sin(π/2 − x/12), so sin(π/2 − x/12) = sin(α/12). Family A: π/2 − x/12 = α/12 ⇒ x = 6π − α. Family B: π/2 − x/12 = π − α/12 ⇒ x = α − 6π < 0. Hence the smallest positive solution is x = 6π − α.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Sine equation given, cosine asked
Use sin(θ) = cos(π/2 − θ) to convert into a cosine equation, then apply the general cosine solution A = ±B + 2nπ.
Phase-shift form preferred
Use cos(θ) = sin(θ + π/2) instead; algebra differs but the resulting x-family is equivalent — extra care with signs.
Inner argument is bx + c rather than x/12
Same identity applies; after equating arguments, divide by b and subtract c/b to isolate x, scaling the 2nπ period accordingly.
tan ↔ cot link
Analogous co-function move tan(θ) = cot(π/2 − θ) bridges a tangent equation to a known cotangent solution within Further Maths scope.

Common Misreadings

  • Computing k numerically from sin²+cos² = 1 and solving cos(x/12) = k with arccos, missing that the answer must be expressed in terms of α.
  • Treating cos(x/12) = sin(α/12) as forcing x/12 = α/12, ignoring that cos and sin are different functions and the co-function shift is required.
  • Reading the question as asking for all solutions in [0, 2π] when it asks for the smallest positive solution (no domain restriction stated).

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when isolating x from π/2 − x/12 = ... — the leading minus on x/12 flips the sign of every term.
  • Forgetting Family B (the π − B branch) and so missing potentially smaller positive solutions.
  • Choosing α − 6π without checking it is negative (since α < 6π), or conversely rejecting 6π − α without verifying α < 6π.
  • Mis-scaling the 2nπ period: after multiplying through by 12 it becomes 24nπ, not 2nπ.

A-level Scope Notes

Co-function identity cos(θ) = sin(π/2 − θ) and the general solutions of sin(A) = sin(B) are core Edexcel IAL Pure (P3/P4) content; this move stays well within syllabus.
