Differentiation

Bridge dr/dt via chain rule using dV/dt and dV/dr

Family: Connected Rates Of Change Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks for the rate of change of one variable (e.g. dr/dt) but you only have access to two other rates that share a common variable — typically dV/dt (from a given ODE or model) and dV/dr (from a geometric formula). Look for cues like 'find the rate at which the radius is increasing' alongside a volume model V(t) and a shape (sphere, cone, cylinder). Also fires when explicitly writing r(t) would require solving a messy/transcendental equation.

Motivation — Why this works

The chain rule identity dV/dt = (dV/dr)(dr/dt) lets you isolate dr/dt = (dV/dt) ÷ (dV/dr) at a single instant without ever inverting V(t) to get r(t). Both right-hand-side rates are easy to evaluate pointwise: dV/dr from the geometric formula, dV/dt from the supplied differential equation. This converts a hard composite-differentiation problem into two pointwise evaluations and a division.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice r depends on V which depends on t — a chain r ↔ V ↔ t — and I am asked for dr/dt at a specific instant.
  2. I rule out forming r(t) explicitly: combining V(t) with r = (3V/(4π))^{1/3} gives a composite that is awkward to differentiate and prone to rounding error.
  3. I commit to the chain rule because both dV/dr and dV/dt are immediately available — one from geometry, one from the ODE.
  4. Bridge: write dV/dt = (dV/dr)(dr/dt), then rearrange to dr/dt = (dV/dt)/(dV/dr).
  5. Tactically: evaluate dV/dt at the given t first, find V at that t, back out r from the geometric relation, then compute dV/dr, and divide — keeping full accuracy until the last line.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. State the chain-rule link: dV/dt = (dV/dr)(dr/dt), so dr/dt = (dV/dt)/(dV/dr).
  2. Evaluate dV/dt at the required instant using the given ODE or V(t).
  3. Find r at that instant: compute V from V(t), then invert the geometric formula (e.g. r = (3V/(4π))^{1/3}).
  4. Evaluate dV/dr at that r using the geometric derivative (e.g. dV/dr = 4πr² for a sphere).
  5. Divide: dr/dt = (dV/dt)/(dV/dr), and state units.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $V = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3$ and $\dfrac{dV}{dt} = \dfrac{900t}{(2+3t)^2}$, find $\dfrac{dr}{dt}$ at $t=3$. At $t=3$: $V = \dfrac{300\cdot 3}{2+9} \approx 81.82$, so $r=\left(\dfrac{3V}{4\pi}\right)^{1/3}\approx 2.683$. Then $\dfrac{dV}{dr}=4\pi r^2\approx 90.45$ and $\dfrac{dV}{dt}=\dfrac{2700}{121}\approx 22.31$. Hence $\dfrac{dr}{dt}\approx \dfrac{22.31}{90.45}\approx 0.247\text{ cm s}^{-1}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Circle expanding, given dA/dt
Use dA/dt = (dA/dr)(dr/dt) with dA/dr = 2πr to get dr/dt.
Sphere with surface area rate dS/dt
Use dS/dr = 8πr; chain via dS/dt = (dS/dr)(dr/dt).
Cone with fixed semi-vertical angle, given dV/dt
Express V in terms of h alone (since r = h tan α), differentiate to get dV/dh, then dh/dt = (dV/dt)/(dV/dh).
Cylinder with fixed radius, varying height
dV/dh = πr² constant; dh/dt = (dV/dt)/(πr²).
Parametric curve y(t), x(t)
Same chain idea: dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) when dx/dt ≠ 0.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the problem as requiring an explicit r(t) and attempting to differentiate the composite directly.
  • Confusing dV/dr with dr/dV and multiplying instead of dividing.
  • Using a formula for V(t) when asked for V at a specific t (or vice versa) and forgetting to substitute.

Pitfalls

  • Rounding r prematurely before substituting into 4πr² — keep at least 3–4 s.f. through the calculation.
  • Substituting t into dV/dt that has already been simplified for a different value, or mis-squaring (2+3t)² at t=3.
  • Forgetting units (cm s⁻¹) or mixing units between V (cm³) and r (cm).
  • Inverting V to find r but using the wrong geometric formula (e.g. cone instead of sphere).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P3/P4 'connected rates of change' technique; uses only the chain rule and standard volume/area derivatives, with no integration of the ODE required.
