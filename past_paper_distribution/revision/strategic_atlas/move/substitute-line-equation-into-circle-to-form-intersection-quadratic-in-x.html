Coordinate Geometry

Substitute line equation into circle to form intersection quadratic in x

Family: Substitution Elimination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger â€” When to use

A line (often given explicitly as $y = mx + c$) and a circle $(x-a)^2 + (y-b)^2 = r^2$ appear together, and the question asks for points of intersection, a 'show that' quadratic in $x$, or conditions for tangency/two-intersection. Phrasings like 'show that the x-coordinates satisfy ...', 'find where the line meets the circle', or 'find the value(s) of $k$ for which the line is tangent' all fire this move. Any time intersection of a line with a quadratic curve is needed and one variable is already isolated, substitution-elimination is the default route.

Motivation â€” Why this works

A circle is a two-variable equation; a line gives a linear relation between $x$ and $y$. Substituting the line into the circle eliminates one variable and produces a single-variable quadratic whose roots are exactly the $x$-coordinates of intersection. The discriminant of that quadratic then encodes the geometric relationship: $\Delta>0$ (two points), $\Delta=0$ (tangent), $\Delta<0$ (no intersection). This converts a geometric question into clean algebra suitable for follow-up parts.

Thought Process â€” The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that one equation (the line) is already solved for $y$ in terms of $x$, while the other (circle) involves both variables squared.
  2. Rule out the distance-from-centre-to-line route: it gives a tangency condition but not the explicit quadratic in $x$ that 'show that' parts demand.
  3. Commit to substitution because the line is explicit in $y$, so replacing $y$ in the circle is algebraically the cleanest path.
  4. Bridge: write the circle in expanded-ready form, then substitute $y = mx + c$ into the $(y-b)^2$ bracket.
  5. Tactically expand each squared bracket separately, collect like powers of $x$, and move $r^2$ to the left to obtain the standard $Ax^2 + Bx + C = 0$ form matching the target.

Mechanic â€” Concrete operations

  1. Write the circle in centred form: $(x-a)^2 + (y-b)^2 = r^2$.
  2. Substitute the line $y = mx + c$ into the $y$-bracket: $(x-a)^2 + (mx + c - b)^2 = r^2$.
  3. Expand $(x-a)^2 = x^2 - 2ax + a^2$ and $(mx + (c-b))^2 = m^2x^2 + 2m(c-b)x + (c-b)^2$.
  4. Add the expansions, group $x^2$, $x$ and constant terms.
  5. Subtract $r^2$ to give $(1+m^2)x^2 + \big(2m(c-b) - 2a\big)x + \big(a^2 + (c-b)^2 - r^2\big) = 0$.
  6. Match coefficients to the required 'show that' form, or apply the discriminant for tangency/intersection-count parts.

Worked Micro-Example

Line $y = 2x + k$, circle $(x-3)^2 + (y-5)^2 = r^2$. Substitute: $(x-3)^2 + (2x + k - 5)^2 = r^2$. Expand: $x^2 - 6x + 9 + 4x^2 + 4(k-5)x + (k-5)^2 = r^2$. Collect: $5x^2 + (4k - 26)x + (k^2 - 10k + 34 - r^2) = 0$.

Variants â€” Same move, different surface

Line meets a parabola $y = x^2 + \dots$
Substitute the line into the parabola; the resulting quadratic's discriminant still controls intersection count.
Line given in form $x = my + c$ (vertical-leaning)
Substitute into the $x$-bracket of the circle to produce a quadratic in $y$ instead.
Line meets a rectangular hyperbola $xy = c^2$
Substitute $y = mx + c$ into $xy = c^2$ to get a quadratic in $x$ used for chord/tangent problems.
Circleâ€“circle intersection
Subtract the two circle equations first to obtain a linear 'radical line', then substitute that line back into one circle â€” reducing to the same lineâ€“circle move.
Tangency-condition question (find $k$ such that line is tangent)
Form the same quadratic, then set discriminant $= 0$ and solve for the parameter.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the perpendicular-distance-from-centre-to-line formula as a substitute for the substitution; it gives tangency but not the requested quadratic.
  • Trying to solve the circle for $y$ first (introducing a square root) instead of substituting the already-explicit line.
  • Reading 'show that' as licence to skip expansion and quote a memorised result.

Pitfalls

  • Expanding $(2x + k - 5)^2$ without the cross term $2 \cdot 2x \cdot (k-5) = 4(k-5)x$.
  • Sign errors when moving $r^2$ across the equality, leaving a stray $+r^2$ instead of $-r^2$.
  • Combining $x^2$ coefficients incorrectly: forgetting that $(mx)^2 = m^2 x^2$ adds to the $x^2$ from $(x-a)^2$.
  • Dropping the constant $(c-b)^2$ when grouping, especially when $c-b$ is itself a binomial in a parameter.
