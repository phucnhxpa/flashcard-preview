Binomial Expansion

Enumerate term-pairs whose exponents sum to the target power

Family: Product Term Matching Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question asks for a specific coefficient (often the constant term, x^k, or 'term independent of x') in a product of the form (short factor) × (a+bx)^n, where the short factor has only 2-3 terms and the binomial expansion is long. Visual cues: a small prefactor like (1 + ax), (2 + 1/x), or (1/81 + ax) multiplied by a binomial raised to a power. Equivalent phrasings include 'find the coefficient of x^k', 'find the term independent of x', or 'show that the coefficient of x^k is ...'. Fully expanding the product would be wasteful; only a few cross-terms actually contribute to the target power.

Motivation — Why this works

When two expressions are multiplied, the coefficient of x^k in the product is the sum over all pairs (a_i x^i)(b_j x^j) with i+j=k. Because the prefactor has only a handful of terms, this sum collapses to just one entry per term in the prefactor — at most two or three contributions. Listing these pairs systematically guarantees no contribution is missed and turns a long expansion into a short calculation. This is a direct application of the polynomial product rule (Cauchy product) and is the standard technique whenever a binomial expansion is wrapped in a small prefactor.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structure: a short factor (2-3 terms, possibly including 1/x) multiplying a binomial expansion (a+bx)^n. The target is the coefficient of a specific power x^k (or x^0).
  2. Rule out brute-force expansion: writing out all n+1 terms of the binomial and multiplying through term-by-term is unnecessary work.
  3. Commit to pair-matching: list every term in the short factor with its exponent e_i, then for each one, the partner needed from the binomial expansion has exponent k − e_i.
  4. Discard impossible pairings: if k − e_i is negative (and the binomial has only non-negative powers) or exceeds n, that pairing contributes 0. Watch carefully when the prefactor contains 1/x — partners then have higher-than-target exponents.
  5. For each surviving pairing, compute the binomial term using the general term C(n,r) a^{n-r} (bx)^r with r chosen so the exponent matches.
  6. Sum the contributions, taking care with signs (especially when the prefactor has a minus sign or when b is negative).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the short factor as ∑ a_i x^{e_i} with e_i listed explicitly (including negative e_i if 1/x appears).
  2. For each i, set r so that the binomial term C(n,r) a^{n-r} b^r x^r has exponent r = k − e_i.
  3. Reject any i where r < 0 or r > n (no such term exists).
  4. Compute each contribution: a_i × C(n,r) × a^{n-r} × b^r.
  5. Add all contributions to obtain the coefficient of x^k.
  6. If required, equate this coefficient to a given value and solve for the unknown parameter.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the coefficient of $x^2$ in $(2 + \tfrac{1}{x})(1+kx)^{16}$. Prefactor exponents: $0$ (from 2) and $-1$ (from $1/x$). Pair 1: need $r=2$, contribution $2 \cdot \binom{16}{2}k^2 = 240k^2$. Pair 2: need $r=3$, contribution $1 \cdot \binom{16}{3}k^3 = 560k^3$. Total: $240k^2 + 560k^3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Prefactor is quadratic, e.g. (1 + ax + bx^2)(...)^n
Three pairs contribute to x^k: exponents (0,k), (1,k−1), (2,k−2). Same enumeration logic, one extra row.
Prefactor contains negative powers, e.g. (2 + 1/x) or (a + b/x^2)
Partner exponents from the binomial must exceed k. E.g. pairing 1/x with x^{k+1} gives x^k. Don't forget to compute the higher-degree binomial term.
Asked for the term independent of x (constant term)
Set k = 0 in the same procedure. If the binomial has only non-negative powers, only the prefactor's constant × the binomial's constant survives.
Coefficient is given and a parameter must be found
Equate the resulting expression in the parameter to the given coefficient and solve (often a quadratic).

Common Misreadings

  • Considering only one pair (e.g. only the constant×constant pair) and missing the cross-term contribution.
  • Trying to expand the entire product term-by-term, which is far more laborious than necessary.
  • Forcing a parameter into the answer when the pair-matching shows the answer is independent of it.
  • Misreading a sign in the prefactor, e.g. treating (2 − 1/x) as (2 + 1/x).

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when the prefactor has a negative term — keep the minus attached to the coefficient throughout.
  • Dropping the parameter when squaring or cubing: e.g. (192p)x × (−3/2)x gives −288p x^2, not −288 x^2.
  • Forgetting that an odd power of a negative b gives a negative term (e.g. k^3 when k<0).
  • Failing to check whether a needed exponent r lies in [0, n]; pairings that demand r outside this range contribute 0.
  • Using only the binomial terms already computed in an earlier part of the question and forgetting to compute additional terms (e.g. the x^3 term) needed for the new pair-matching.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3/P4 binomial expansion scope. Uses the general term formula $\binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^r$ from the formula booklet (Binomial Series). For prefactors with 1/x the binomial expansion itself is still finite — do not confuse with the (1+x)^n series for non-integer n, which lives in a different part of the syllabus.
