Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a problem states one line is perpendicular to another and the gradient of one is known or computable. Cue phrases include 'perpendicular to the tangent/chord/line', 'normal to the curve', or 'at right angles to'. Often paired with a known point on the new line so that a full equation can be produced. Also fires when verifying that two given lines are perpendicular by checking $m_1 m_2 = -1$.

Motivation — Why this works

For non-vertical lines, perpendicularity is equivalent to the algebraic condition $m_1 m_2 = -1$, which follows from rotating a direction vector $(1, m)$ by $90^\circ$ to $(-m, 1)$, giving slope $-1/m$. This converts a geometric constraint into a one-line algebraic substitution. Once the gradient of the perpendicular is fixed, a single known point determines the line uniquely via point-slope form. The move is foundational for normals to curves, perpendicular bisectors, and altitude/right-angle problems.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the keyword 'perpendicular' linking the new line to a line whose gradient is already known or extractable.
  2. Rule out parallel-line reasoning ($m_1 = m_2$) and avoid trying to reconstruct the gradient from two points when one is already available.
  3. Commit to $m_\perp = -1/m$ because it is the unique algebraic encoding of the right-angle condition for non-vertical lines.
  4. Bridge to operations: compute $-1/m$ explicitly, then pair it with a known point $(x_0, y_0)$ in $y - y_0 = m_\perp(x - x_0)$.
  5. Tactically check sign and reciprocal carefully, and if $m=0$ or $m$ undefined, switch to the vertical/horizontal special case rather than applying the formula blindly.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the known gradient $m$ of the reference line (e.g. tangent gradient).
  2. Compute the perpendicular gradient $m_\perp = -1/m$.
  3. If an equation form is already given, match coefficients to confirm consistency.
  4. Substitute the known point $(x_0, y_0)$ on the perpendicular line into $y = m_\perp x + c$ to solve for $c$.
  5. State the final equation, simplifying fractions to a clean form.

Worked Micro-Example

Tangent gradient $m_T = 8$, so perpendicular gradient $m_L = -\tfrac{1}{8}$. Line $L$ passes through $(18, 4)$: $4 = -\tfrac{1}{8}(18) + k = -\tfrac{9}{4} + k$, hence $k = \tfrac{25}{4}$. Therefore $L: y = -\tfrac{1}{8}x + \tfrac{25}{4}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Normal to a curve at a point
Differentiate to get $m_T = f'(x_0)$, then use $m_N = -1/f'(x_0)$ with point $(x_0, f(x_0))$.
Tangent gradient is a fraction $m = a/b$
Perpendicular gradient becomes $-b/a$; invert and negate together rather than sequentially.
Horizontal reference line ($m = 0$)
Perpendicular is vertical, equation $x = x_0$; cannot use $y = mx + c$.
Vertical reference line (undefined gradient)
Perpendicular is horizontal, equation $y = y_0$.
Perpendicular bisector of a segment $AB$
First compute $m_{AB}$ from two points, take $-1/m_{AB}$, and pass through the midpoint of $AB$.
Verifying perpendicularity of two given lines
Check the product $m_1 m_2 = -1$ rather than constructing a new equation.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'perpendicular' as 'parallel' and equating gradients instead of using the negative reciprocal.
  • Reading 'normal' as a synonym for 'tangent' rather than the perpendicular to the tangent.
  • Assuming the perpendicular passes through the same point as the tangent when the question specifies a different point.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the negative sign: writing $m_\perp = 1/m$ instead of $-1/m$.
  • Inverting without negating, or negating without inverting.
  • Arithmetic slip when simplifying $-\tfrac{1}{8} \times 18$ as $-2.5$ instead of $-\tfrac{9}{4}$.
  • Applying $m_\perp = -1/m$ when $m = 0$, producing division by zero instead of recognising a vertical line.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure Maths content (P1 coordinate geometry, extended in P2/P3 for normals to curves via differentiation); no vector or 3D extensions required.
