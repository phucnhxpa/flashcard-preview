Proof

Factor out shared product to assemble target consecutive-integer form

Family: Strategic Factorisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in the inductive step of a summation proof once the inductive hypothesis has been substituted and the expression splits into two terms that visibly share a product of brackets (e.g. (k+1)(k+2)). It also fires when the target P(k+1) is itself a product of consecutive linear factors, signalling that the shared product is a substructure of the goal. Cue phrases: 'both terms contain', 'target is a product of three consecutive integers', 'one more linear factor needed'. Avoid this move's competitor — full expansion — whenever a clean common factor is visible.

Motivation — Why this works

Products of consecutive integers like (k+1)(k+2)(k+3) are preserved under factor extraction: if two summands share (k+1)(k+2), the residue bracket only needs to simplify to a single linear factor to complete the product. Extracting the common factor turns the algebra into a one-line bracket simplification rather than a cubic expansion-and-refactor. This is the same principle that makes telescoping and partial-fraction reassembly efficient: keep structure intact rather than destroy and rebuild it.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice both post-substitution terms contain the same pair of brackets (k+1)(k+2), and the target P(k+1) is a product of three consecutive integers.
  2. I rule out expanding everything to a cubic — that would force me to refactor a degree-3 polynomial, which is slow and error-prone.
  3. I commit to factoring (k+1)(k+2) out because the target only differs by one further linear factor, so the residue bracket should collapse to (k+3)/3.
  4. Concretely, I write the sum as (k+1)(k+2)[ k/3 + 1 ] and prepare to combine the bracket over a common denominator.
  5. Inside the bracket I keep the 1/3 coefficient attached to k carefully so I do not lose the factor of 1/3 when assembling the final triple product.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the common factor (k+1)(k+2) in both terms.
  2. Write (1/3)k(k+1)(k+2) + (k+1)(k+2) = (k+1)(k+2)[k/3 + 1].
  3. Combine the bracket over a common denominator: k/3 + 1 = (k+3)/3.
  4. Reassemble: (k+1)(k+2)·(k+3)/3 = (1/3)(k+1)(k+2)(k+3).
  5. Compare with P(k+1) target form to confirm the match.

Worked Micro-Example

Assume $\sum_{r=1}^{k} r(r+1) = \tfrac{1}{3}k(k+1)(k+2)$. Then $\sum_{r=1}^{k+1} r(r+1) = \tfrac{1}{3}k(k+1)(k+2) + (k+1)(k+2)$. Factoring: $(k+1)(k+2)\!\left[\tfrac{k}{3}+1\right] = (k+1)(k+2)\cdot\tfrac{k+3}{3} = \tfrac{1}{3}(k+1)(k+2)(k+3)$, which is $P(k+1)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Induction on $\sum r(r+1)(r+2) = \tfrac{1}{4}n(n+1)(n+2)(n+3)$
Common factor is $(k+1)(k+2)(k+3)$; the residue bracket simplifies to $(k+4)/4$.
Induction on $\sum r^2 = \tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)(2n+1)$
Common factor is $(k+1)$; the residue bracket simplifies to $\tfrac{1}{6}[k(2k+1)+6(k+1)] = \tfrac{1}{6}(2k+3)(k+2)$.
Induction on $\sum r^3 = \tfrac{1}{4}n^2(n+1)^2$
Common factor is $(k+1)^2$; the residue bracket simplifies to $\tfrac{1}{4}[k^2 + 4(k+1)] = \tfrac{1}{4}(k+2)^2$.
Divisibility induction (e.g. $f(k+1) - f(k)$ is a multiple of $m$)
Instead of factoring a shared product, add and subtract a term so the difference exposes a factor of $m$ explicitly.

Common Misreadings

  • Mistaking the target as requiring full expansion because of the $1/3$ coefficient, when in fact the coefficient travels cleanly through the factorisation.
  • Treating $(k+1)(k+2)$ as two separate factors and only pulling out $(k+1)$, leaving an awkward bracket that does not match the target.
  • Believing the move only works when coefficients are 1; it works equally well with rational coefficients provided they are tracked carefully.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the $1/3$ when extracting $(k+1)(k+2)$, e.g. writing the residue bracket as $k+1$ instead of $k/3 + 1$.
  • Combining $k/3 + 1$ incorrectly as $(k+1)/3$ instead of $(k+3)/3$.
  • Forgetting to compare the final expression to $P(k+1)$ explicitly, which is required for full marks in induction.
  • Expanding the cubic anyway and then making sign or coefficient errors during refactoring.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure proof-by-induction for summation identities; uses only GCSE-level factor extraction and fraction combination.
