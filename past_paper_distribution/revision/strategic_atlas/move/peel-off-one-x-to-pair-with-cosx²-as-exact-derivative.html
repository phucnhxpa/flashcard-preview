Integration

Peel off one x to pair with cos(x²) as exact derivative

Family: Integrand Restructuring Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integrand has the form x^n·cos(x²) (or x^n·sin(x²), x^n·e^{x²}) with n ≥ 2, where the inner function x² has derivative 2x and at least one spare x-factor is available. Typically arises when deriving a reduction formula for I_n = ∫x^n cos(x²) dx. Cue phrases: 'show that I_n = ... I_{n−2}', or any product of a power of x with a composite whose inner derivative is a single power of x.

Motivation — Why this works

Because d/dx[sin(x²)] = 2x·cos(x²), the combination x·cos(x²) dx is an exact differential ½ d[sin(x²)]. Splitting x^n = x^{n−1}·x lets dv = x·cos(x²) dx integrate exactly to v = ½ sin(x²), while u = x^{n−1} differentiates to a lower power. This is the only split that gives a closed-form v, and it engineers a clean drop of two in the exponent per IBP round — exactly what a reduction formula needs.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand is a product of a power x^n with cos(x²); the inner argument x² differentiates to 2x, so an x-factor is the 'currency' that unlocks the chain rule.
  2. Rule out the direct substitution u = x² for general n: it absorbs only one x and leaves x^{n−1} = u^{(n−1)/2}, producing fractional powers unless n is odd.
  3. Commit to integration by parts because a reduction formula is the goal, and IBP naturally lowers the polynomial power while leaving a similar integral.
  4. Bridge: split x^n = x^{n−1}·x and group the trailing x with cos(x²) so that dv = x cos(x²) dx is recognisably ½ d[sin(x²)].
  5. Tactically: take u = x^{n−1} (so du = (n−1)x^{n−2} dx) and v = ½ sin(x²); the resulting integral ∫x^{n−2} sin(x²) dx has the same x^{n−2}·(composite) shape, ready for a sister move that will close the recursion.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write I_n = ∫ x^n cos(x²) dx = ∫ x^{n−1} · [x cos(x²)] dx.
  2. Recognise x cos(x²) dx = ½ d[sin(x²)], so set dv = x cos(x²) dx and v = ½ sin(x²).
  3. Set u = x^{n−1}, du = (n−1) x^{n−2} dx.
  4. Apply IBP: I_n = ½ x^{n−1} sin(x²) − (n−1)/2 · ∫ x^{n−2} sin(x²) dx.
  5. If a reduction formula in I_n alone is required, repeat the same peeling trick on ∫ x^{n−2} sin(x²) dx (using d[cos(x²)] = −2x sin(x²) dx) to return to a multiple of I_{n−2}.

Worked Micro-Example

For I_n = ∫ x^n cos(x²) dx, write I_n = ∫ x^{n−1}·x cos(x²) dx. With u = x^{n−1}, dv = x cos(x²) dx, we get v = ½ sin(x²), du = (n−1)x^{n−2} dx. Hence I_n = ½ x^{n−1} sin(x²) − ((n−1)/2) ∫ x^{n−2} sin(x²) dx. A second IBP on the remaining integral (peeling off another x to pair with sin(x²)) closes a reduction in steps of 2.

Variants — Same move, different surface

x^n · sin(x²)
Pair one x with sin(x²): dv = x sin(x²) dx, v = −½ cos(x²); u = x^{n−1}.
x^n · e^{x²}
Pair one x with e^{x²}: dv = x e^{x²} dx, v = ½ e^{x²}; u = x^{n−1}.
x^n · sec²(x²) (Further Maths)
Pair one x with sec²(x²): dv = x sec²(x²) dx, v = ½ tan(x²).
x^{2k+1} · cos(x²) with odd power
Substitution u = x² works cleanly here, since x^{2k+1} dx = ½ u^k du; IBP is still needed but on a simpler integrand in u.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the whole integral with u = x² as the first move; for general n this leaves fractional powers of u and obstructs a clean reduction formula.
  • Choosing u = cos(x²) and dv = x^n dx — this differentiates the composite badly (introducing a new x factor) and inflates the polynomial.
  • Trying to split x^n as x^{n−2}·x², expecting x²·cos(x²) to be integrable — it has no elementary antiderivative.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the ½ factor in v = ½ sin(x²), which propagates through the reduction formula.
  • Sign error when the second peel uses d[cos(x²)] = −2x sin(x²) dx; the minus sign must be tracked.
  • Dropping the (n−1) factor that comes from differentiating x^{n−1}.
  • Failing to apply boundary substitution carefully when the question is a definite integral reduction.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Further Pure reduction-formula technique within Edexcel IAL FP2/FP3: integration by parts combined with recognising f(g(x))g'(x) as an exact derivative, no techniques beyond syllabus.
