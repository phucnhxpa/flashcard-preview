Vectors

Chain displacement vectors via triangle law to find a position vector

Family: Vector Path Chaining Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the question supplies a position vector (e.g. OA) and a displacement leaving its endpoint (e.g. AB), and asks for the position vector of the far endpoint (OB). Equivalent phrasings include 'find the coordinates of B given A and the vector AB' or 'point B is such that AB = ...'. The given data form a head-to-tail chain O → A → B with the intermediate point shared between the two vectors. Recognise it whenever you can trace an unbroken path of known vectors from the origin to the target point.

Motivation — Why this works

The triangle (or polygon) law of vector addition guarantees that OB = OA + AB whenever A lies on the path. Geometrically, travelling from O to A and then from A to B has the same net displacement as travelling directly from O to B. This converts a geometric routing problem into component-wise arithmetic and avoids needing the coordinates of B explicitly. The same principle extends to any number of intermediate points by simply adding more legs.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the unknown is a position vector OB but only OA and AB are given — two vectors sharing the intermediate point A.
  2. Rule out coordinate-geometry detours (no need to extract B's coordinates separately) and rule out subtraction (we are extending a path forward, not differencing two position vectors).
  3. Commit to the triangle law because the head of OA matches the tail of AB, forming a valid head-to-tail chain to the target.
  4. Bridge to operation: write OB = OA + AB and align the i, j, k components in a column for clean addition.
  5. Tactically, watch signs on each component and double-check by verifying that AB = OB − OA reproduces the given displacement.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the chain O → A → B and write OB = OA + AB.
  2. Stack the two vectors as columns aligning i, j, k components.
  3. Add corresponding components carefully, tracking signs.
  4. State OB in column or i,j,k form; if coordinates of B are requested, read them from OB.
  5. (Optional check) Compute OB − OA and confirm it equals the given AB.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\vec{OA} = \begin{pmatrix} 7 \\ 2 \\ -5 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\vec{AB} = \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ 4 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}$, find $\vec{OB}$.\\ By the triangle law, $\vec{OB} = \vec{OA} + \vec{AB} = \begin{pmatrix} 7 + (-2) \\ 2 + 4 \\ -5 + 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 6 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}$. Hence $B = (5, 6, -2)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Three-leg path O → A → B → C
Apply the polygon law: OC = OA + AB + BC, adding all legs componentwise.
Find a displacement AB given two position vectors OA and OB
Reverse the relation: AB = OB − OA (subtract tail position from head position).
Midpoint or ratio division of AB
Combine chaining with section formula, e.g. OM = OA + ½ AB for the midpoint.
2D column vectors or i, j form in M1/Mechanics
Same identity OB = OA + AB applied to two components only.
Closed polygon (e.g. parallelogram OABC)
Use the closure condition OA + AB + BC + CO = 0 to deduce a missing leg.

Common Misreadings

  • Subtracting instead of adding, computing OA − AB, which corresponds to no valid path.
  • Confusing AB with BA and so reversing the sign of the displacement.
  • Treating AB as the position vector of B (i.e. assuming OB = AB), ignoring OA entirely.
  • Misidentifying which point is the shared intermediate, e.g. trying OB = OA + BA.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when a component is negative, e.g. evaluating −5 + 3 as −8 rather than −2.
  • Misaligning i, j, k components when the vectors are written in different forms (column vs i,j,k notation).
  • Forgetting to state the final answer as a position vector or as coordinates when the question asks specifically for one form.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure/Core vectors (2D and 3D position and displacement vectors, triangle/polygon law). No use of dot/cross product or vector equations of lines is required for this move.
