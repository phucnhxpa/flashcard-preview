Trigger — When to use

Fires when a transformation of the form y = af(x + b) (or similar composition of a horizontal translation and vertical stretch) is applied to a function whose vertex/turning point/key feature is known, and the image of that point is also given. Cues include phrases like 'the curve y = af(x+b) has minimum point at ...' or 'the transformed graph passes through ... with vertex at ...'. The task is to determine the unknown constants a and b without expanding the algebraic form.

Motivation — Why this works

Graph transformations act predictably on individual points: f(x+b) sends (x₀, y₀) to (x₀ − b, y₀), and af(x) sends (x₀, y₀) to (x₀, ay₀). Composing these, the vertex (x₀, y₀) maps to (x₀ − b, ay₀). Equating this image to the given new vertex yields two independent equations — one purely in b (from x-coordinates) and one purely in a (from y-coordinates) — which decouple cleanly. This bypasses the need to substitute, expand, and re-derive the vertex from the transformed equation.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Student notices the transformation has the structure y = af(x + b): one parameter inside the bracket (horizontal) and one outside (vertical).
  2. They rule out expanding f(x + b) algebraically and re-finding the vertex by completing the square — too slow and error-prone.
  3. They commit to the point-tracking approach because the original vertex is explicitly known and transformations act point-wise in a decoupled way on x- and y-coordinates.
  4. They write down the image of the vertex under each transformation separately: x-coordinate shifts by −b, y-coordinate scales by a.
  5. They equate the image (x₀ − b, ay₀) to the given new vertex and solve the two resulting linear equations independently for a and b.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the original vertex (x₀, y₀) of y = f(x).
  2. Apply the inside transformation: under y = f(x + b), the vertex x-coordinate becomes x₀ − b (y unchanged).
  3. Apply the outside transformation: under y = af(x + b), the y-coordinate becomes ay₀.
  4. Set x₀ − b equal to the given new x-coordinate and solve for b.
  5. Set ay₀ equal to the given new y-coordinate and solve for a.
  6. Sanity-check the sign of b (left/right) and the sign of a (reflection if negative).

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose y = f(x) has vertex (13/3, 5), and y = af(x + b) has vertex (1/3, −10). Horizontal: 13/3 − b = 1/3 ⇒ b = 4. Vertical: 5a = −10 ⇒ a = −2. So the transformation is y = −2f(x + 4).

Variants — Same move, different surface

y = f(x) + c (vertical translation only)
Vertex y-coordinate becomes y₀ + c; x-coordinate unchanged.
y = af(x) + c (vertical stretch then shift)
Vertex y-coordinate becomes ay₀ + c; apply stretch before adding c.
y = f(ax + b)
Factor as f(a(x + b/a)); vertex x-coordinate becomes (x₀ − b)/a, i.e. horizontal stretch by 1/a then shift by −b/a.
y = f(x − b) (right shift)
Vertex x-coordinate becomes x₀ + b instead of x₀ − b.
y = −f(x) or y = f(−x) (reflections)
Reflection in x-axis negates y₀; reflection in y-axis negates x₀.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading f(x + b) as a shift to the right by b instead of to the left.
  • Treating a as acting on the x-coordinate (confusing horizontal and vertical stretches).
  • Assuming b shifts the y-coordinate or that a translates rather than scales.
  • Mistaking y = f(ax + b) for a simple shift, ignoring the embedded horizontal stretch.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors on b: f(x + b) shifts LEFT by b, so new x = x₀ − b, not x₀ + b.
  • Forgetting that a multiplies the entire y-coordinate, including any constant term in the original function value.
  • Misapplying to y = f(ax + b) without first factoring out a from the bracket.
  • Solving the two equations together as a system when they are independent — wasting time or introducing error.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P2/P3) graph transformations: translations, stretches, and reflections of y = f(x). Compositions are limited to one horizontal and one vertical transformation at a time, consistent with specification scope.
