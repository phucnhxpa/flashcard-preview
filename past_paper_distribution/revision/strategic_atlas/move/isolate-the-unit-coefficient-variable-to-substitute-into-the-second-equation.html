Algebra & Functions

Isolate the unit-coefficient variable to substitute into the second equation

Family: Substitution Solve Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires on a 2×2 linear system in two unknowns where at least one equation has a coefficient of 1 (or −1) on one of the variables, e.g. a + 4d = 26 paired with 2a + 9d = 56. The cue is visual: scan for a 'lonely' variable with no leading number. Common surfaces include arithmetic progression problems (solving for a and d from two term/sum conditions), simultaneous equations in coordinate geometry (line meets line), and pairs of constraints in mechanics or statistics worded problems.

Motivation — Why this works

Rearranging an equation with a unit coefficient produces an exact expression with no fractions, which keeps the substituted equation clean and integer-valued. Substituting collapses the 2×2 system into a single linear equation in one unknown, which is the minimal-work reduction. Elimination would also solve it but requires an extra scaling step; when a unit coefficient is already present, substitution is the path of least friction and least error.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the system is 2×2 linear and one equation, a + 4d = 26, has coefficient 1 on a.
  2. I rule out elimination as primary: it would need me to double equation (1) before subtracting, an avoidable extra step.
  3. I commit to substitution because isolating a from (1) introduces no fractions and no sign traps beyond a single distribution.
  4. Bridge: rearrange (1) to a = 26 − 4d, then plug this expression for a into (2) wherever a appears.
  5. Tactically, I expand carefully (watch the sign on −4d when multiplied by 2), solve the resulting linear equation in d, then back-substitute into a = 26 − 4d to recover a.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the variable with unit coefficient: a in (1) a + 4d = 26.
  2. Rearrange (1): a = 26 − 4d.
  3. Substitute into (2): 2(26 − 4d) + 9d = 56.
  4. Expand and collect: 52 − 8d + 9d = 56, so 52 + d = 56.
  5. Solve: d = 4.
  6. Back-substitute: a = 26 − 4(4) = 10.
  7. Check both equations: 10 + 16 = 26 ✓ and 20 + 36 = 56 ✓.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $a + 4d = 26$ and $2a + 9d = 56$. From (1): $a = 26 - 4d$. Sub into (2): $2(26 - 4d) + 9d = 56 \Rightarrow 52 + d = 56 \Rightarrow d = 4$. Then $a = 26 - 16 = 10$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Arithmetic progression: given $a + 4d = 26$ (the 5th term) and $S_{10} = 245$ giving $2a + 9d = 49$.
Same move: isolate $a$ from the term equation, substitute into the sum equation.
Line-meets-line in coordinate geometry, e.g. $y = 3x - 2$ and $2x + 5y = 11$.
The first equation already isolates $y$; substitute directly into the second to get a single equation in $x$.
Both equations have non-unit coefficients, e.g. $3a + 4d = 26$ and $2a + 9d = 56$.
Switch to elimination (scale and subtract) to avoid fractional substitutions.
Coefficient is $-1$, e.g. $-a + 4d = 26$.
Isolate via $a = 4d - 26$; the move is identical, just track the sign.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the system as needing elimination by default and doubling (1) unnecessarily, doing more algebra than required.
  • Choosing to isolate the wrong variable (e.g. $d$ from (1), giving $d = (26-a)/4$) and reintroducing fractions.
  • Mistaking a non-linear system (e.g. one equation quadratic) for this template — the move applies only to linear 2×2.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when distributing: writing $2(26 - 4d) = 52 + 8d$ instead of $52 - 8d$.
  • Forgetting to back-substitute, leaving the answer as just $d = 4$.
  • Substituting the rearranged expression back into the same equation it came from (yielding $0 = 0$).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1) simultaneous-equation technique; appears routinely in sequences/series problems and coordinate geometry, fully within syllabus.
