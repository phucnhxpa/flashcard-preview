Sequences & Series

Evaluate small finite sigma sums by direct term-by-term enumeration

Family: Direct Enumeration Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A sigma-notation sum with a small upper limit (typically n ≤ 5 or 6) where each term value is either given or already computed from earlier parts of the question. Common phrasings include 'Find ∑_{r=1}^{4}(2u_r + 1)' following a recurrence question, or 'Evaluate ∑_{r=1}^{n} f(r)' for small n with no clean closed form. Fires especially when the summand mixes a recurrence-defined u_r with a constant or linear adjustment, so no standard series formula applies cleanly.

Motivation — Why this works

For a small fixed number of terms, direct substitution into the summand and addition is the most reliable route: each step is concrete arithmetic with no risk of misapplying a formula. Linearity splits like ∑(2u_r + 1) = 2∑u_r + n are valid but introduce an extra layer of bookkeeping that is unnecessary when every u_r is already known. The principle is simply that ∑_{r=1}^{n} a_r = a_1 + a_2 + ... + a_n by definition.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the upper limit is small (here 4) — only a handful of terms to evaluate.
  2. I rule out splitting the sum via linearity or invoking ∑r, ∑r² formulae: the summand involves u_r from a recurrence, not a closed form.
  3. I commit to direct enumeration because every u_r value is already in hand from the earlier part.
  4. I list the r-values 1, 2, 3, 4 and compute 2u_r + 1 for each, keeping signs explicit.
  5. I add the four results carefully, grouping positives and negatives separately to control sign errors.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. List the r-values from the lower to upper limit.
  2. Substitute each r into the summand expression to get individual term values.
  3. Write the sum as an explicit chain a_1 + a_2 + ... + a_n with brackets around negatives.
  4. Add the terms (group positives and negatives if helpful) to obtain the final value.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $u_1=3,\ u_2=1,\ u_3=-3,\ u_4=-11$, evaluate $\sum_{r=1}^{4}(2u_r+1)$. Terms: $2(3)+1=7$, $2(1)+1=3$, $2(-3)+1=-5$, $2(-11)+1=-21$. Sum $= 7+3+(-5)+(-21) = 10 - 26 = -16$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Sum ∑_{r=1}^{5} u_r where u_r is given by a recurrence with no closed form
Compute u_1,...,u_5 from the recurrence first, then add directly.
Large upper limit with u_r arithmetic or geometric
Switch to closed-form ∑ formulae (AP/GP sum) instead of enumerating.
Sum like ∑_{r=1}^{n}(ar+b) with general n
Use ∑r = n(n+1)/2 and ∑1 = n via linearity rather than enumeration.
Sum ∑_{r=2}^{5} f(r) with a non-unit lower limit
Enumerate only r = 2, 3, 4, 5; do not include r = 1.

Common Misreadings

  • Misreading the lower limit as r = 0 instead of r = 1, adding an extra term.
  • Summing the raw u_r values and forgetting to apply the (2u_r + 1) transformation.
  • Treating the '+1' as added once at the end rather than once per term.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when adding mixed positive and negative terms.
  • Dropping a negative sign when substituting a negative u_r into 2u_r + 1.
  • Off-by-one error in the number of terms (n vs n+1 terms from r=1 to r=n).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure/Core scope: sigma notation and recurrence-defined sequences are standard P1/P2 content, and direct enumeration of small finite sums requires no techniques beyond GCSE arithmetic.
