Integration

Substitute u = √(linear) to convert surd integrand into polynomial

Family: Surd Substitution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integrand contains √(ax+b) — typically in the denominator or multiplied by a polynomial in x — and no standard antiderivative applies directly. The question either mandates the substitution u = √(ax+b) or leaves the choice open but the surd structure invites it. Cue phrases include 'using the substitution u = √(x+1)' or an integrand of the form P(x)/√(ax+b) where P is polynomial. The presence of a linear (not quadratic) expression under the root is the decisive feature.

Motivation — Why this works

Setting u equal to the surd itself (rather than to its radicand) makes u replace √(ax+b) directly, so any surd in the denominator becomes simply u. Squaring gives x as a polynomial in u, and differentiating gives dx = (2u/a) du, contributing a factor of u that typically cancels the surviving u in the denominator. The net effect is a fully rational, in fact polynomial, integrand in u that can be integrated term-by-term. This works precisely because the radicand is linear, so squaring inverts cleanly.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that √(x+1) sits in the denominator of x/√(x+1), so the integrand is irrational and no direct standard form applies.
  2. Rule out splitting or partial fractions (no rational structure) and rule out u = x+1, which would leave a √u in the denominator.
  3. Commit to u = √(x+1) because then the denominator becomes u itself, and dx will introduce another u, setting up a clean cancellation.
  4. Bridge: derive the three substitution pieces — x = u²−1, √(x+1) = u, and dx = 2u du.
  5. Tactically, substitute all three at once, cancel u/u immediately, then expand the bracket before integrating term-by-term.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Set u = √(x+1); square to get u² = x+1, so x = u²−1.
  2. Differentiate: dx/du = 2u, hence dx = 2u du.
  3. Rewrite the integral: ∫ x/√(x+1) dx = ∫ (u²−1)/u · 2u du.
  4. Cancel the u in the denominator with one factor of u from 2u du: ∫ 2(u²−1) du.
  5. Integrate term-by-term: (2/3)u³ − 2u + C.
  6. Back-substitute u = √(x+1): (2/3)(x+1)^{3/2} − 2(x+1)^{1/2} + C.
  7. For a definite integral, convert the x-limits to u-limits at the substitution stage rather than back-substituting.

Worked Micro-Example

Compute $\int \dfrac{x}{\sqrt{x+1}}\,dx$ with $u=\sqrt{x+1}$. Then $x=u^2-1$ and $dx=2u\,du$, giving $\int \dfrac{u^2-1}{u}\cdot 2u\,du = \int 2(u^2-1)\,du = \tfrac{2}{3}u^3 - 2u + C$. Back-substituting: $\tfrac{2}{3}(x+1)^{3/2} - 2(x+1)^{1/2} + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Integrand of the form P(x)·√(ax+b) (surd in numerator)
Same substitution u = √(ax+b); now u appears as a factor instead of cancelling, but the integrand still becomes polynomial in u.
General linear radicand √(ax+b)
Use u = √(ax+b), so x = (u²−b)/a and dx = (2u/a) du; the 1/a constant simply scales the result.
Definite integral ∫_p^q x/√(x+1) dx
Convert limits via u = √(x+1) (e.g. x=0 ↦ u=1, x=3 ↦ u=2) and evaluate in u directly without back-substituting.
Quadratic radicand √(x²+1) or √(1−x²)
Algebraic surd substitution fails; use a trigonometric/hyperbolic substitution instead (Further Maths).
Integrand x/(x+1)^{1/3} or similar fractional power
Use u = (x+1)^{1/3}; same philosophy — let u be the awkward radical so dx contributes a clearing power of u.

Common Misreadings

  • Choosing u = x+1 (the radicand) instead of u = √(x+1), which leaves a √u in the denominator and defeats the simplification.
  • Treating √(x+1) as if it could be simplified by partial fractions or by splitting x/√(x+1) as √(x+1) − 1/√(x+1) without noticing the substitution route is mandated.
  • Assuming the substitution generalises to √(x²+1) and trying to square it, which produces a non-polynomial integrand.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to replace every occurrence of x — the numerator x, the denominator √(x+1), and dx must all be converted to u.
  • Sign or coefficient slip when differentiating x = u²−1, giving dx = u du or 2u² du instead of 2u du.
  • Failing to back-substitute u = √(x+1) at the end (for indefinite integrals), or forgetting to change the limits (for definite integrals).
  • Cancelling u/u carelessly when u could be zero at a limit — though for u = √(x+1) this only matters at x = −1, usually outside the domain.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P3 integration by substitution) and revisited in Further Pure; only linear radicands are expected at A-level, with quadratic radicands reserved for Further Maths trig/hyperbolic substitutions.
