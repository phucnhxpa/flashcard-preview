Algebra & Functions

Exclude parameter value that collapses two solutions into one

Family: Distinctness Enforcement Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a problem demands two distinct solutions/intersections and a parameter k controls both candidates simultaneously. Typical cues: 'find values of k for which the equation has two distinct solutions', 'the line meets the curve at two distinct points', or modulus/absolute-value equations producing two case-solutions both depending on k. The danger is a boundary k at which the two cases coincide, yielding only one solution. Also signalled when squaring/cross-multiplying produces a discriminant or rational equation in k whose equality case must be tested.

Motivation — Why this works

Distinctness is a strict condition: two case-solutions x_1(k) and x_2(k) must differ. The collision value is found by solving x_1(k) = x_2(k); at that k the geometry degenerates (a tangency, a vertex-meeting, or a repeated root). Including this boundary would mis-count one intersection as two. Excluding it converts a non-strict inequality k \ge k_0 into the strict k > k_0 (or removes an isolated value from a range).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the working has produced two candidate x-values, both depending on k, and the question asks for two distinct solutions.
  2. Rule out the lazy answer 'k \ge k_0' that comes from existence conditions alone — existence is necessary but not sufficient for distinctness.
  3. Commit to checking when the two candidates coincide, because that is precisely the configuration distinctness forbids.
  4. Bridge: equate the two expressions for x (or set discriminant = 0) and solve for the offending k.
  5. Tactical: verify geometrically/algebraically what happens at the boundary (tangency, vertex hit, RHS = 0), then write the final answer with strict inequality, removing the boundary point.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two case-solutions x_1(k) and x_2(k) (e.g. from |...| = ... split into ± cases).
  2. Set x_1(k) = x_2(k) and solve for k to find the collision value k_0.
  3. Substitute k_0 back to confirm both cases yield the same x and check the geometric meaning (tangency / single intersection).
  4. Combine with the existence condition: if existence gave k \ge k_0, distinctness sharpens this to k > k_0.
  5. State the final parameter range with strict inequality, explicitly excluding k_0.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $|5-2x| = k$ intersected with a line giving cases $x = \tfrac{14}{k+2}$ and $x = \tfrac{4}{k-2}$. Existence needs $k \ge \tfrac{18}{5}$. Setting $\tfrac{14}{k+2} = \tfrac{4}{k-2}$ gives $14(k-2) = 4(k+2) \Rightarrow 10k = 36 \Rightarrow k = \tfrac{18}{5}$. At $k = \tfrac{18}{5}$ both yield $x = \tfrac{5}{2}$ with $5 - 2(\tfrac{5}{2}) = 0$ — a single tangential meeting. Hence two distinct intersections require $k > \tfrac{18}{5}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic with parameter: 'two distinct real roots'
Use discriminant > 0 (strict), not \ge 0; the equality \Delta = 0 is the collision case to exclude.
Line y = mx + c meeting a curve in two distinct points
Form the intersection quadratic in x and require its discriminant strictly positive; tangency is the excluded boundary.
Modulus equation |f(x)| = g(x;k) with two case-branches
Equate the two branch solutions to find the parameter at which they merge at f(x) = 0 (vertex of the V-shape).
Simultaneous equations with parameter giving two solution pairs
Equate the two x- (or y-) coordinates as functions of k and exclude the merging value.
Circle-line intersection, two distinct points
Distance from centre to line strictly less than radius; equality is tangency, excluded.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'two solutions' as merely 'solutions exist', so stopping at the existence inequality \ge.
  • Believing each algebraic case automatically delivers a distinct geometric point, without testing whether the two cases can coincide.
  • Confusing 'distinct real roots' with 'real roots', leading to \Delta \ge 0 instead of \Delta > 0.

Pitfalls

  • Writing k \ge \tfrac{18}{5} instead of k > \tfrac{18}{5} by not checking the boundary explicitly.
  • Cross-multiplying without noting sign of (k-2) or (k+2), causing spurious solutions.
  • Forgetting to combine the distinctness condition with the original existence/domain condition, producing an incomplete final range.
  • Failing to verify geometrically that the boundary k yields a tangency/single point rather than a crossing.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure scope: modulus equations (P2/P3), discriminant conditions for quadratics (P1), and line–curve intersection problems. No tools beyond standard algebraic manipulation are required.
