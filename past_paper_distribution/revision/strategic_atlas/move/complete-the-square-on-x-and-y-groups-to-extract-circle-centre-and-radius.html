Coordinate Geometry

Complete the square on x and y groups to extract circle centre and radius

Family: Completing The Square Frequency: 6 questions

Trigger — When to use

The circle is given in expanded general form x² + y² + Dx + Ey + F = 0 (or with an unknown constant k, or with equal non-unit leading coefficients on x² and y²), with no xy term and equal coefficients on x² and y². The question asks for the centre, the radius, the values of an unknown for tangency/intersection, or anything that requires the standard form (x−a)² + (y−b)² = r². Cues: 'find the centre and radius', 'show the circle has equation…', 'find the value of k such that…', or any later part needing distance-from-centre reasoning.

Motivation — Why this works

Completing the square rewrites x² + bx as (x + b/2)² − (b/2)², converting the general equation into the standard form (x−a)² + (y−b)² = r². This isolates the geometric data — centre (a, b) and radius r — which are not directly visible in the expanded equation. The underlying identity is the perfect-square identity (x + p)² = x² + 2px + p², applied independently to the x-group and y-group because there is no cross term. It is the canonical bridge between algebraic and geometric representations of a circle and is essential before applying any distance, tangency, or intersection condition.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation has x² and y² with equal (usually unit) coefficients and no xy term — this is a circle in general form.
  2. Notice the centre and radius are hidden inside the linear x and y terms; they cannot be read off until the equation is in standard form.
  3. Rule out the quadratic formula (gives x in terms of y, useless here) and rule out using the direct formula centre = (−D/2, −E/2), r² = D²/4 + E²/4 − F because (i) mark schemes typically expect to see the completed-square form and (ii) it is more transparent and reusable for later parts of the question.
  4. Commit to completing the square because it simultaneously delivers the centre, the radius², and a clean equation for any subsequent geometric work (tangents, intersections, distance from a point).
  5. Tactically: group x-terms together and y-terms together, move any constant to the RHS, complete each square independently, and remember to subtract the (b/2)² constants on the LHS or, equivalently, add them to the RHS.
  6. If the leading coefficients are not 1 (e.g. 2x² + 2y² + …), divide through first; if the equation contains an unknown k, carry it symbolically into r² = … − k for use in a later condition.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rearrange into the form (x² + Dx) + (y² + Ey) = −F (move the constant to the RHS).
  2. Complete the x-square: x² + Dx = (x + D/2)² − (D/2)².
  3. Complete the y-square: y² + Ey = (y + E/2)² − (E/2)².
  4. Substitute back and move the subtracted constants to the RHS: (x + D/2)² + (y + E/2)² = (D/2)² + (E/2)² − F.
  5. Read off centre (−D/2, −E/2) and radius r = √((D/2)² + (E/2)² − F), simplifying any surd to its exact form (e.g. √27 = 3√3).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $x^2 + y^2 + 6x - 4y - 14 = 0$: group as $(x^2+6x)+(y^2-4y)=14$. Complete the squares: $(x+3)^2 - 9 + (y-2)^2 - 4 = 14$, so $(x+3)^2 + (y-2)^2 = 27$. Hence centre $(-3, 2)$ and radius $\sqrt{27} = 3\sqrt{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation contains an unknown constant, e.g. x² − 6x + y² + 8y + k = 0
Carry k symbolically: standard form becomes (x−3)² + (y+4)² = 25 − k, giving r² = 25 − k for use in a tangency/intersection condition.
Non-unit leading coefficients, e.g. 2x² + 2y² + 8x − 12y + 5 = 0
Divide the whole equation through by 2 first to obtain unit coefficients on x² and y², then complete the square as usual.
Sphere in 3D: x² + y² + z² + ax + by + cz + d = 0
Complete the square in all three variables independently to obtain (x−p)² + (y−q)² + (z−s)² = r².
RHS already non-zero, e.g. x² + y² + 10x − 12y = k
Treat k as part of the RHS; after completing the squares, r² = 25 + 36 + k = 61 + k.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the equation as two separate quadratics to solve via the quadratic formula instead of recognising it as a single circle equation.
  • Assuming the centre is (D, E) read straight off the linear coefficients, ignoring the halving and sign flip.
  • Mistaking an equation with an xy term or unequal x² and y² coefficients for a circle (it is then an ellipse or rotated conic, outside this move's scope).

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when reading the centre: (x + 3)² gives centre x-coordinate −3, not +3; (y − 2)² gives +2.
  • Forgetting to subtract the (D/2)² and (E/2)² constants on the LHS (or equivalently add them to the RHS) after completing each square.
  • Leaving the radius as √27 instead of simplifying to 3√3 when 'exact' form is required.
  • Forgetting to divide through first when the leading coefficients on x² and y² are not 1.
  • Computing r when only r² was needed (or vice versa) — particularly when carrying an unknown k symbolically.

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 coordinate-geometry-of-the-circle scope. The standard form (x−a)² + (y−b)² = r² is given/expected; the general form x² + y² + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0 with centre (−g, −f) and radius √(g² + f² − c) is acceptable but completing the square is the expected working. Do not extend to rotated conics or general second-degree curves with xy terms — those are out of scope.
