Binomial Expansion

Substitute a small rational x-value to link expansion to target numerical surd

Family: Numerical Approximation Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question has two parts: part (a) produces a binomial expansion of some expression like $(a+bx)^n$ or $(1+kx)^n$ truncated to a polynomial, and part (b) asks for a numerical approximation of a specific surd or reciprocal root such as $1/\sqrt{4.1}$, $\sqrt[4]{5}$, or $\sqrt{1.01}$. The cue is the appearance of a numerical value 'close' to the constant inside the bracket of part (a). Re-phrasings: 'Hence, by choosing a suitable value of $x$, find an approximation to ...', 'Use your expansion in (a) to estimate ...', 'Find an approximation to ... giving your answer to N decimal places'.

Motivation — Why this works

A binomial expansion is only useful numerically if we feed it a small $x$ that makes the original expression evaluate to (something algebraically tied to) the target surd. Solving $a+bx = \text{target}$ (or $1+kx = \text{target}$) determines the substitution. Provided the chosen $x$ lies inside the validity interval $|kx|<1$ (or $|x|<a/|b|$), the truncated polynomial converges rapidly to the true value, and the smaller $x$ is, the fewer terms are needed for the required accuracy. After substituting, an algebraic rearrangement (factoring out constants, multiplying through by a power) often isolates the requested surd.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that part (b) names a specific numerical surd, and the only available tool is the polynomial from part (a) — so the move must be a substitution into that expansion.
  2. Rule out direct evaluation (calculator) — the question asks to USE the expansion — and rule out re-deriving a different expansion: the chosen $x$ must come from the form already produced in (a).
  3. Commit by setting the bracket from (a) equal to (or proportional to) the target inside the surd: e.g. $4+x = 4.1 \Rightarrow x=0.1$, or $1-\tfrac{x}{2} = \tfrac{199}{200} \Rightarrow x=\tfrac{1}{100}$.
  4. Bridge: verify $|x|$ lies strictly inside the validity range from part (a); if not, the expansion is invalid and a different scaling is needed.
  5. Tactical choice: prefer the smallest $x$ that hits the target (e.g. $x=0.01$ over $x=1$) so that $x^2, x^3$ corrections are tiny and the requested decimal-place accuracy is achievable without extra terms.
  6. After substituting, perform any algebraic tidying (factor out powers of constants, multiply both sides by $\sqrt[n]{c}$) needed to isolate the exact surd the question asks for.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Equate the bracket of the part-(a) expansion to the numerical value implied by the target: solve $a+bx = T$ for $x$.
  2. Check $|x|$ (or $|bx/a|$) lies in the validity interval established in part (a).
  3. Substitute that $x$ into the polynomial: $1 + c_1 x + c_2 x^2 + c_3 x^3$.
  4. Evaluate term-by-term using exact fractions or sufficiently precise decimals; keep one extra guard digit beyond the requested precision.
  5. Sum the terms to get an approximation to LHS $= (a+bx)^n$ at the chosen $x$.
  6. Algebraically rearrange to isolate the requested surd (e.g. multiply by $\sqrt[n]{a}$, take reciprocals, factor) and round to the required decimal places.

Worked Micro-Example

Given (from part a) $(4+x)^{-1/2} \approx \tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{x}{16} + \tfrac{3x^2}{256} - \tfrac{5x^3}{2048}$ for $|x|<4$. To approximate $1/\sqrt{4.1}$, set $x=0.1$ (valid since $0.1<4$): $\tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{0.1}{16} + \tfrac{3(0.01)}{256} - \tfrac{5(0.001)}{2048} \approx 0.5 - 0.00625 + 0.0001172 - 0.0000024 \approx 0.49386$ (to 5 d.p.).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Target surd not directly equal to LHS (e.g. approximate $\sqrt[4]{5}$ from $(1-\tfrac{x}{2})^{1/4}$)
Choose $x$ so the bracket becomes a fraction whose numerator/denominator factor through the target surd, then rearrange algebraically (e.g. $(\tfrac{199}{200})^{1/4}$ relates to $\sqrt[4]{5}$ via factoring 200).
Expansion is in form $(1+kx)^n$ with $k>1$
Use a smaller $x$ to keep $|kx|<1$; e.g. for $\sqrt{1.01}$ from $(1+x)^{1/2}$, take $x=0.01$.
Target lies outside obvious range (e.g. approximate $\sqrt{101}$)
Scale: write $\sqrt{101}=10\sqrt{1.01}$, then substitute the small $x=0.01$ into $(1+x)^{1/2}$ and multiply by 10.

Common Misreadings

  • Substituting $x$ equal to the target itself (e.g. $x=4.1$) instead of solving $a+bx=4.1$.
  • Choosing an $x$ outside the validity interval from part (a), invalidating the approximation.
  • Stopping at the decimal value of the LHS without performing the algebraic rearrangement to isolate the requested surd.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips on powers of small decimals: $(0.1)^3=0.001$, not $0.0001$; $(0.01)^2=10^{-4}$.
  • Premature rounding of intermediate terms causing loss of accuracy in the final decimal places.
  • Including too few terms when $x$ is not very small, so the $x^3$ contribution still affects the requested precision.
  • Mixing fractional and decimal forms inconsistently when summing the series.

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 binomial expansion for rational/negative indices (formula booklet: generalised binomial series). Stay with truncations to $x^3$ unless asked; do not invoke convergence tests or remainder estimates beyond informal smallness arguments — those are out of scope.
