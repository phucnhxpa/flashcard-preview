Differentiation

Equate f'(x) and f''(x) generally to form a quadratic in x

Family: Derivative Equation Formation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks for 'the other point' (or 'all points') where f'(x) = f''(x), or more generally where two derivative expressions coincide. The phrase 'other point' is the strongest cue: it presupposes a known solution (often found earlier in the question) and at least one more, signalling that a general equation in x must be solved. Also fires when one derivative is quadratic and the next is linear, so their difference is naturally a quadratic with up to two roots.

Motivation — Why this works

If f(x) is a cubic, then f'(x) is quadratic and f''(x) is linear, so f'(x) − f''(x) = 0 is a quadratic in x with (generically) two real roots. This matches the question's implication of exactly two qualifying x-values. Forming the general equation converts a calculus condition into a standard algebraic factorisation/quadratic-formula problem, and the previously found root acts as a built-in check.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the wording 'the other point' — this presupposes more than one solution, hinting at a polynomial equation with multiple roots.
  2. Rule out re-using the earlier method (e.g. substituting a specific x to find k); that work is done and k is now a known constant.
  3. Commit to forming the general equation f'(x) = f''(x) in x, since degree analysis (quadratic = linear) confirms a quadratic, hence two roots.
  4. Bridge: substitute the known value of k into both f'(x) and f''(x), then move every term to one side.
  5. Tactically decide whether to clear fractions early (multiply through by a common denominator) or factor out the known root (x − 5) directly to recover the other root quickly.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write down f'(x) and f''(x) with the known constants substituted (here k = 59/8).
  2. Set f'(x) = f''(x): 3x² − 2(59/8)x + 14 = 6x − 2(59/8).
  3. Move all terms to one side: 3x² − (59/4)x − 6x + 14 + 59/4 = 0.
  4. Combine like terms to get a clean quadratic: 3x² − (83/4)x + 115/4 = 0.
  5. Multiply through by 4 if helpful: 12x² − 83x + 115 = 0, then factorise or use the quadratic formula.
  6. Discard the already-known root (x = 5) to identify 'the other point'.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $f(x)=x^3-4x^2+3x+1$, so $f'(x)=3x^2-8x+3$ and $f''(x)=6x-8$. Setting $f'(x)=f''(x)$ gives $3x^2-8x+3=6x-8$, i.e. $3x^2-14x+11=0$. Factorising: $(3x-11)(x-1)=0$, so $x=1$ or $x=11/3$. If $x=1$ was found earlier, the 'other point' is $x=11/3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Finding where f(x) = f'(x) for a cubic f
Difference is still a cubic-degree-or-lower polynomial; rearrange to standard form and factor out any known root.
Quartic f with f'(x) = f''(x)
f'(x) is cubic, f''(x) quadratic; the equation becomes a cubic in x — use the known root to reduce via polynomial division.
Stationary points of g(x) = f'(x) − f''(x)
Same algebraic skeleton: form g(x) = 0 and treat as a polynomial equation.
Tangent meets curve again (parametric/coordinate geometry analogue)
Equate curve and tangent line equations; known contact point gives a repeated factor, leaving the 'other' intersection.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 'other point' as requiring a brand-new technique rather than just solving the general equation and discarding the known root.
  • Thinking k must be re-derived rather than carried in from the previous part.
  • Assuming the equation must be solved numerically when it factorises cleanly once fractions are cleared.

Pitfalls

  • Leaving the equation in the form 'quadratic = linear' and trying to factorise without first bringing all terms to one side.
  • Arithmetic slips when combining fractional coefficients (e.g. mishandling −59/4 + 14).
  • Forgetting to multiply through to clear denominators, leading to messy factorisation.
  • Reporting both roots when the question asks only for the 'other' point.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics: differentiation of polynomials and solving quadratics by factorisation or formula. No techniques beyond P1–P2 are required.
