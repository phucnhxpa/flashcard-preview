Algebra & Functions

Eliminate a parameter via substitution or division to collapse to a single-variable equation

Family: Parameter Elimination Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation contains two unknowns (e.g. a and r, or a and e) but the target answer — a 'show that' result, a quadratic/cubic in one variable, or a numerical value — involves only one of them. Visual cues: every term shares a common factor of the unwanted variable, OR an earlier part of the question has produced a relation expressing one unknown in terms of the other. Equivalent phrasings: 'reduce two unknowns to one', 'kill the spare variable', 'use part (a) as a bridge'. The structural mismatch between what you have (two-variable equation) and what you need (one-variable equation) is the signal.

Motivation — Why this works

A single equation in two unknowns is underdetermined, so the problem must be designed so one variable is removable — either because it factors out (homogeneity) or because a prior relation pins it down in terms of the other. Eliminating it converts the equation into a solvable single-variable polynomial or rational equation that matches the required target form. The underlying principle is dimensional/structural: if the target has no a (or no e), then a (or e) must cancel, and the algebra is engineered to allow exactly that. This is the critical bridge step that links the setup to the answer.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the mismatch: my current equation has two unknowns, but the target form (quadratic in r, cubic in a, numerical answer) involves only one.
  2. Rule out 'solve for one variable and back-substitute' as circular — there is only one equation, so isolating one unknown gives an expression, not a value.
  3. Scan for the elimination mechanism: (i) does the unwanted variable appear as a common factor of every term? If yes → divide through. (ii) Has an earlier part produced an equation of the form (unwanted) = f(wanted)? If yes → substitute.
  4. Commit to the move once the mechanism is identified, and confirm the variable being eliminated is non-zero (a ≠ 0 for a GP first term; e ≠ 0 for ellipse eccentricity) so the operation is valid.
  5. Execute the operation cleanly — divide every term, or substitute and simplify the resulting compound fraction — being careful that the structure collapses to the predicted single-variable form.
  6. Cross-multiply and rearrange to match the exact polynomial form demanded by the question (standard form, leading coefficient positive, etc.).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the variable to eliminate (the one absent from the target).
  2. Check the elimination route: common factor (divide) vs. prior relation (substitute).
  3. Justify non-zero status of the eliminated variable in one line.
  4. Apply the operation: divide each term by the common factor, OR substitute the expression from the prior part and simplify nested fractions.
  5. Cross-multiply to clear denominators.
  6. Expand and collect terms, rearranging into the standard polynomial form requested.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\frac{a}{1-r} = a + 8ar$ with $a \neq 0$, divide through by $a$: $\frac{1}{1-r} = 1 + 8r$. Multiply by $(1-r)$: $1 = (1+8r)(1-r) = 1 + 7r - 8r^2$, giving $8r^2 - 7r = 0$, so $r(8r-7)=0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

GP problem with two simultaneous conditions (e.g. $S_\infty = k$ and $u_3 = m$)
Divide one equation by the other to cancel $a$ — same homogeneity philosophy, different surface.
Conic problem where part (a) gave $e^2 = f(a)$ and part (b) needs an equation in $a$ alone
Substitute the expression for $e^2$ into the area/length formula, simplify the compound fraction by cancelling the $a^2$ denominators, then cross-multiply.
Trigonometric identity proofs where one side has a stray parameter
Use a Pythagorean or double-angle identity from the formula booklet to rewrite the stray term, eliminating it structurally.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to find a numerical value for the variable being eliminated before substituting — impossible without a second equation.
  • Treating the move as ordinary 'simplification' rather than a deliberate strategic step, then forgetting to justify non-zero division in a 'show that' proof.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to state $a \neq 0$ (or $e \neq 0$) before dividing — examiners may dock the mark in a 'show that' question.
  • Dividing by a variable that could be zero (e.g. $r$ in a GP can in principle be zero) without justification.
  • Arithmetic slips when expanding cross-multiplied products (e.g. $15a \cdot 2a^2 = 30a^3$, $164 \cdot 25 = 4100$) or losing a sign during rearrangement to standard form.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL Pure (P1–P4) and Further Pure scope. No formula booklet entry is invoked directly; the move relies on basic algebraic manipulation. Avoid generalising to systems requiring resultants or Gröbner bases — those are out of scope.
