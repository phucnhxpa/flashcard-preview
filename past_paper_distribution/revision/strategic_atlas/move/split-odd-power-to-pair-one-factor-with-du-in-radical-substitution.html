Integration

Split odd power to pair one factor with du in radical substitution

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an integrand has the form (odd power of x)·f(a − bx²) or x^(2k+1)/√(a−bx²), and the natural substitution u = a − bx² gives du = −2bx dx. The odd power guarantees one stray x can pair with dx to form x dx, while the remaining even power x^(2k) converts cleanly to a polynomial in u. Also applies whenever the derivative of the inner function appears 'almost' as a factor — one short by a single x.

Motivation — Why this works

Under u = a − bx², the differential du = −2bx dx already contains exactly one factor of x bundled with dx. Splitting x^(2k+1) = x^(2k)·x exposes that bundle for direct replacement, while x^(2k) = ((a−u)/b)^k converts the leftover algebra entirely into u. The whole integrand becomes a function of u alone, so the substitution closes without stray x's.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the radical √(a − bx²) (or similar composite) and an odd power of x in the numerator — classic u-sub signal.
  2. Rule out trig substitution or integration by parts: the derivative of the inside is essentially present, so polynomial u-sub is cheaper.
  3. Commit to u = a − bx² because du = −2bx dx absorbs both dx and one factor of x simultaneously.
  4. Bridge: rewrite x^(2k+1) dx = x^(2k) · (x dx), so x dx becomes −du/(2b) and the remaining x^(2k) is rewritten via x² = (a−u)/b.
  5. Tactically, change limits before integrating to avoid back-substitution, and watch the sign from du = −2bx dx (often flips limits).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Set u = a − bx²; compute du = −2bx dx, hence x dx = −du/(2b).
  2. Split the odd power: x^(2k+1) dx = x^(2k) · (x dx).
  3. Express x^(2k) in u via x² = (a−u)/b.
  4. Replace √(a−bx²) by √u (or the appropriate power).
  5. Convert limits: x = x₀ → u = a − bx₀².
  6. Simplify and integrate the resulting polynomial-in-u (possibly with √u in the denominator).

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_0^{9/4} \frac{t^3}{\sqrt{81-16t^2}}\,dt$. Let $u = 81-16t^2$, so $du = -32t\,dt$ and $t^2 = (81-u)/16$. Then $t^3\,dt = t^2(t\,dt) = \frac{81-u}{16}\cdot\left(-\frac{du}{32}\right)$. Limits: $t=0\Rightarrow u=81$, $t=9/4\Rightarrow u=0$. Integral $= \frac{1}{512}\int_0^{81}\frac{81-u}{\sqrt{u}}\,du$, which is elementary.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$\int x^5\sqrt{a-bx^2}\,dx$
Split $x^5 = x^4\cdot x$; pair $x\,dx$ with $du$, then $x^4 = ((a-u)/b)^2$.
$\int \sin^3 x\cos x\,dx$ with $u = \sin x$
Here $\cos x\,dx = du$ already; the odd power isn't needed for pairing — different mechanic.
$\int \sin^5 x\,dx$ with $u = \cos x$
Split $\sin^5 x = (\sin^2 x)^2\sin x = (1-u^2)^2$ times $-du$; same odd-power-splitting principle.
$\int \frac{x^3}{(1+x^2)^n}\,dx$ with $u = 1+x^2$
Split $x^3 = x^2\cdot x$; $x\,dx = du/2$ and $x^2 = u-1$.

Common Misreadings

  • Choosing u = √(a−bx²) directly, which complicates du and obscures the x dx pairing.
  • Attempting the substitution without splitting the odd power, then being stuck with a leftover x that won't convert.
  • Mistaking this for a trig-substitution surface and introducing x = (√a/√b)sin θ unnecessarily.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the factor 1/b when writing x² = (a−u)/b, losing a constant multiplier.
  • Sign errors from du = −2bx dx: the minus sign must be tracked, often via limit reversal.
  • Forgetting to update the limits when switching variable, then evaluating at the wrong endpoints.
  • Confusing $\sqrt{u}$ in the denominator with $u^{1/2}$ in the numerator when antidifferentiating.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL C34/Further Pure technique: u-substitution with limit change, polynomial manipulation, and integration of $u^{n}$ or $u^{n}/\sqrt{u}$. No hyperbolic or advanced trig substitution required.
