Form ratio of two computed areas to extract k in k:1

Family: Ratio Comparison Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question asks for a ratio R₁ : R₂ in the form k : 1 (or a : b with one part normalised) after two areas, volumes, or other quantities have been computed independently. Typical phrasings include 'express the ratio in the form k : 1', 'find the value of k', or 'show that the areas are in the ratio …'. The cue is that both quantities are already in hand as exact values and only a final division remains.

Motivation — Why this works

By definition, R₁ : R₂ = k : 1 means k = R₁/R₂, so once both pieces are computed exactly the answer is one division. The move matters because students often distrust 'clean' outcomes (especially k = 1 or other integers) and waste time re-deriving correct work. Recognising that geometric symmetry or algebraic identity can force a tidy ratio is what makes the final commitment quick and confident.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that both regions have been evaluated to exact fractions, so the ratio reduces to a single arithmetic step.
  2. Rule out the temptation to recompute either integral merely because the ratio is suspiciously clean (e.g. 1, 2, or a small integer).
  3. Commit by performing a symmetry or scale check: do the geometric features (width, height, axis of symmetry) make the clean ratio plausible?
  4. Bridge to the operation: write k = R₁/R₂ explicitly, substitute the exact fractions, and simplify.
  5. Tactical choice: keep values as fractions (not decimals) so cancellation is exact and k emerges in closed form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify R₁ and R₂ as previously computed exact values, taking care which is the antecedent of the ratio.
  2. Write k = R₁ / R₂.
  3. Substitute the exact fractional values and simplify by multiplying by the reciprocal.
  4. State the ratio in the required form k : 1 with k as an exact number.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose $R_1 = \tfrac{4}{3}$ and $R_2 = \tfrac{4}{3}$. Then $k = \dfrac{R_1}{R_2} = \dfrac{4/3}{4/3} = 1$, so $R_1 : R_2 = 1 : 1$. A symmetry check (parabola symmetric about $x=2$, chord $y=2$ cutting at $x=1,3$) confirms equal areas are geometrically reasonable.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Volumes of revolution in ratio k:1
Compute each volume by $\pi\int y^2\,dx$ first, then divide; the final ratio step is identical.
Ratio a:b with neither part normalised
Clear common factors from $R_1/R_2$ and present as integers $a:b$ in lowest terms instead of forcing a unit denominator.
Arc lengths or surface areas in Further Pure
Same final division; only the preceding integral set-up differs.
Ratio involving a parameter (e.g. find a so that R₁:R₂ = k:1)
Set $R_1(a)/R_2(a) = k$ and solve the resulting equation in $a$ rather than evaluating numerically.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 'k : 1' as 'k − 1' or as a difference rather than a quotient.
  • Assuming the larger region must be R₁, leading to inversion of the ratio.
  • Treating a clean answer like k = 1 as evidence of an arithmetic mistake.

Pitfalls

  • Inverting the ratio by dividing R₂ by R₁ instead of R₁ by R₂.
  • Converting to decimals prematurely and rounding, losing the exact value of k.
  • Recomputing one of the integrals 'just to check' and introducing a new sign or limit error.
  • Forgetting to simplify (e.g. leaving k as $\tfrac{8/3}{4/3}$ instead of $2$).

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure (areas by integration, ratio notation); no techniques beyond standard definite integration and fraction arithmetic are required.
