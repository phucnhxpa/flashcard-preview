Binomial Expansion

Expand (1+u)^n as generalized binomial series for fractional/negative n

Family: Series Generation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an expansion is requested for (1+u)^n with n not a non-negative integer (e.g. n = 1/3, -1/2, -2). Cue phrases include 'first three non-zero terms', 'expansion in ascending powers of x', or 'valid for |x| < ...'. Also fires after algebraic preprocessing has put an expression into the form (1+u)^n with |u|<1. Distinguished from the finite binomial theorem by the infinite-series character and the appearance of fractional/negative indices.

Motivation — Why this works

For any real n, (1+u)^n = Σ_{r≥0} [n(n−1)···(n−r+1)/r!] u^r, valid for |u|<1. The descending-factorial numerator no longer terminates when n is fractional or negative, producing an infinite series. Computing the first few generalized binomial coefficients and pairing each with u^r yields the required leading terms. Sign patterns emerge automatically from the (n−k) factors crossing zero.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice n is fractional (or negative), so the finite binomial theorem with C(n,r) integer coefficients does not apply.
  2. Rule out direct expansion via Pascal's triangle; commit to the generalized formula C(n,r) = n(n−1)···(n−r+1)/r!.
  3. Confirm the bracket is in the form (1+u)^n (factor out leading constant if not) so the series converges for |u|<1.
  4. Compute coefficients term by term, tracking sign from the descending factorial: a negative (n−k) flips sign.
  5. Substitute u^r carefully, raising the entire inner expression (coefficient and x) to power r, then collect.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify n and u so the expression is (1+u)^n; if needed, factor (a+bx)^n = a^n(1 + (b/a)x)^n.
  2. Write generalized coefficients: C(n,0)=1, C(n,1)=n, C(n,2)=n(n−1)/2!, C(n,3)=n(n−1)(n−2)/3!, ….
  3. Multiply each C(n,r) by u^r, squaring/cubing the full inner expression including its constant.
  4. Simplify each term to lowest terms, watching signs from negative (n−k) factors.
  5. Collect the requested number of non-zero terms; state validity range |u|<1 if asked.

Worked Micro-Example

Expand $(1+\tfrac{4}{3}x)^{1/3}$ to three terms. Term 0: $1$. Term 1: $\tfrac{1}{3}\cdot\tfrac{4}{3}x = \tfrac{4}{9}x$. Term 2: $\tfrac{(1/3)(-2/3)}{2}\cdot(\tfrac{4}{3})^2 x^2 = -\tfrac{1}{9}\cdot\tfrac{16}{9}x^2 = -\tfrac{16}{81}x^2$. So $(1+\tfrac{4}{3}x)^{1/3} \approx 1 + \tfrac{4}{9}x - \tfrac{16}{81}x^2$, valid for $|x|<\tfrac{3}{4}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Negative integer index, e.g. (1+x)^{-2}
Descending factorial gives (-2)(-3)(-4)…, producing pattern Σ(-1)^r (r+1)x^r; same formula, signs alternate cleanly.
Half-integer index, e.g. (1+x)^{-1/2}
Coefficients involve double factorials; commonly used to expand 1/√(1−kx) for approximations.
Non-unit leading constant, e.g. (4+x)^{1/2}
Factor out: 4^{1/2}(1+x/4)^{1/2} = 2(1+x/4)^{1/2}, then expand; validity becomes |x/4|<1.
Composite inner expression, e.g. (1−2x+x^2)^{-1}
Rewrite as ((1−x)^2)^{-1} = (1−x)^{-2} before applying the series.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating n=1/3 as if it terminates after a few terms like a positive integer expansion.
  • Reading 'first three non-zero terms' as 'terms up to x^3' without checking which terms vanish.
  • Misidentifying (a+bx)^n with a≠1 as already in standard form, skipping the factor-out step.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips in the descending factorial: e.g. (1/3 − 1) = −2/3, not +2/3.
  • Omitting the r! denominator, especially the 2! for the x^2 coefficient.
  • Failing to square/cube the constant inside u: ((4/3)x)^2 = (16/9)x^2, not (4/3)x^2.
  • Forgetting to state or check the validity range |u|<1 when the question requires it.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P2/P4): generalized binomial expansion for rational n with validity |u|<1, including factor-out preprocessing for non-unit leading constants.
