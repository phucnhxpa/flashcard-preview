Differentiation

Analyse sign of f'(x) to prove monotonicity or classify stationary points

Family: Sign Of Derivative Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question says 'show f is increasing/decreasing on …', 'prove f is monotonic', or asks you to classify a stationary point (max/min) without an explicit instruction to use f''(x). Visual cues: f(x) is a rational function (Möbius (ax+b)/(cx+d), or a quotient whose derivative will factor cleanly), or f'(x) has already been computed and presents as a product/quotient of linear factors over a positive denominator. Equivalent phrasings include 'deduce that f is one-to-one', 'hence find the nature of the turning point', or 'state, with reason, whether f is increasing'. The common structural tell is that the SIGN of f'(x) is decidable by inspection rather than its value being needed.

Motivation — Why this works

By the Monotonic Function Theorem, f is decreasing on an interval iff f'(x) < 0 throughout (similarly for increasing), and the First Derivative Test classifies a stationary point by the sign change of f' across it. Computing f'(x) and reducing the sign question to inspection of a constant numerator, a square denominator, or a small set of linear factors converts a qualitative demand into a one-line algebraic verification. This avoids the heavier machinery of f''(x) — particularly attractive for rational f, where f'' would require a second quotient rule. The underlying principle is that signs of products/quotients are determined factor-by-factor, with squares and positive constants contributing nothing to sign changes.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the verb 'show … decreasing/increasing' or 'classify the stationary point' — this immediately reframes the problem as 'determine the sign of f'(x)'.
  2. Rule out the definition-based approach (showing f(a) > f(b) for a < b) — too laborious for a 'show that' mark scheme — and rule out f''(x) when f is rational, because the second quotient rule is messy.
  3. Commit to differentiating once and reading off the sign: for a Möbius function the numerator of f' will collapse to the constant ad − bc; for a stationary-point classification, f' will already factor.
  4. Bridge to action: apply the quotient rule with u, v clearly labelled, simplify until the numerator is either a single constant or a product of linear factors.
  5. Tactical decision: isolate the parts of f'(x) whose sign is automatic — squared denominators are positive, named positive constants drop out — so that only one or two factors govern the sign.
  6. If classifying a stationary point, build a sign chart at the relevant critical x-values; if proving monotonicity, just state the sign on the whole stated domain, explicitly invoking the domain restriction (e.g. 'since x > 4, (x−4)² > 0').

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Differentiate: for f = u/v use f'(x) = (v·u' − u·v')/v² (mind the order to avoid a sign error).
  2. Simplify the numerator fully; for (ax+b)/(cx+d) it collapses to ad − bc.
  3. Identify automatically-signed pieces: v² > 0, positive constants, etc., and set them aside.
  4. Reduce the sign question to the remaining factor(s): a constant, or linear factors (px+q).
  5. For monotonicity: state the sign of f'(x) on the given interval, citing the domain (e.g. x > 4 ⇒ (x−4)² > 0), and conclude 'f is decreasing/increasing'.
  6. For stationary-point classification: tabulate signs of each linear factor on intervals separated by the critical points; multiply to get the sign of f'; a + → − change marks a local max, − → + a local min.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $f(x)=\dfrac{5x-3}{x-4}$, $x>4$. Quotient rule: $f'(x)=\dfrac{(x-4)(5)-(5x-3)(1)}{(x-4)^2}=\dfrac{-17}{(x-4)^2}$. Since $(x-4)^2>0$ for $x>4$ and $-17<0$, $f'(x)<0$ on the domain, so $f$ is decreasing.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Möbius function f(x) = (ax+b)/(cx+d)
Numerator of f' collapses to the constant ad − bc; sign of f' is the sign of ad − bc on the whole domain.
f'(x) is a product of linear factors over a positive denominator
Use a sign chart at each linear root; multiply factor signs interval-by-interval to classify each stationary point via First Derivative Test.
Derivative carries a negative leading constant k < 0
All factor-sign products flip; remember to include the constant's sign in the chart.
Denominator of f' can change sign on the domain
Track the denominator's sign in the chart as an additional factor; do not assume it is positive.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to prove monotonicity by evaluating f at two specific points — this checks two values, not all x in the interval.
  • Confusing 'f' changes from + to −' with 'f is decreasing' — the First Derivative Test concerns the sign of f', not of f.
  • Reaching for f''(x) to classify a stationary point when the first-derivative sign test is far cleaner.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error in the quotient rule: writing (u·v' − v·u')/v² instead of (v·u' − u·v')/v².
  • Forgetting to invoke the domain explicitly — e.g. saying '(x−4)² > 0' without noting x > 4 (or x ≠ 4) means the conclusion is technically unjustified.
  • Only checking one side of a critical point when applying the First Derivative Test, missing the sign-change requirement.
  • Ignoring the sign of a constant multiplier (a negative k flips the entire sign chart).

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL P2/P3/P4 scope: quotient rule and First Derivative Test are standard. The quotient rule is in the formula booklet; the monotonicity criterion (f'>0 ⇒ increasing) is stated in the specification but not the booklet. Avoid invoking strict-monotonicity-from-f'≥0 with isolated zeros — at IAL level stick to f'(x) > 0 (or < 0) throughout.
