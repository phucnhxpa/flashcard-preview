Trigonometry

Cross-multiply and reduce trig equation to quadratic in sinθ

Family: Algebraic Reduction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a trig equation has been rearranged into a single-variable form (after using identities like cos2θ or 1−2sin²θ) but still contains fractions on both sides with sinθ (or another single trig ratio) appearing in numerators/denominators. Typical cue: an equation of the form 1/f(sinθ) = k/sinθ, or any rational equation in one trig ratio. Recognise that clearing fractions will produce a polynomial whose highest power is sin²θ. The move is essentially 'treat sinθ as the unknown s and solve a quadratic'.

Motivation — Why this works

Cross-multiplication is valid whenever no denominator is zero, and it converts a rational equation into a polynomial equation with the same solution set (subject to checking excluded values). Because the highest power of sinθ produced is 2, the polynomial is a quadratic in sinθ, which the quadratic formula solves exactly. Solving for sinθ first and then inverting via arcsin separates the algebraic step from the trigonometric step, which is the standard A-level pattern for such problems.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices both sides are fractions in a single trig ratio (sinθ), so a polynomial equation is one cross-multiplication away.
  2. Rules out trying further identity manipulation — no obvious simplification remains, and the structure already screams 'quadratic in sinθ'.
  3. Commits to substituting s = sinθ mentally and treating the equation as a quadratic in s, since the highest power will be s².
  4. Bridges to action by cross-multiplying carefully, distributing the multiplier across every term on the opposite side.
  5. Plans to apply the quadratic formula (discriminant unlikely to factor neatly), then check each root lies in [−1,1] before taking arcsin and using symmetry to list all solutions in the given range.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Cross-multiply to clear both denominators: sinθ · 1 = 2 · (1 − 2sinθ − 4sin²θ).
  2. Expand the right-hand side: sinθ = 2 − 4sinθ − 8sin²θ.
  3. Collect all terms on one side: 8sin²θ + 5sinθ − 2 = 0.
  4. Let s = sinθ and apply the quadratic formula: s = (−5 ± √(25 + 64))/16 = (−5 ± √89)/16.
  5. Evaluate numerically: s ≈ 0.2771 or s ≈ −0.9021; verify each lies in [−1,1].
  6. Use θ = arcsin(s) and the symmetry sin(π − θ) = sinθ (and signs/quadrants) to list all θ in the required range.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\dfrac{1}{1-2\sin\theta-4\sin^2\theta}=\dfrac{2}{\sin\theta}$. Cross-multiplying: $\sin\theta = 2(1-2\sin\theta-4\sin^2\theta)$, giving $8\sin^2\theta+5\sin\theta-2=0$. Then $\sin\theta=\dfrac{-5\pm\sqrt{89}}{16}\approx 0.2771\text{ or }-0.9021$, and arcsin (with quadrant symmetry) yields the angles in the required range.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Rational equation in cosθ (e.g. 1/(1−2cos²θ) = k/cosθ)
Cross-multiply and substitute c = cosθ to get a quadratic 8c² + ... = 0; solve, check |c| ≤ 1, then use arccos with quadrant symmetry.
Rational equation in tanθ
Cross-multiply, substitute t = tanθ, solve the quadratic; no [−1,1] restriction, but watch for tanθ undefined points (θ = π/2 + kπ).
Equation with mixed sin and cos reducible via sin²θ = 1 − cos²θ (or vice versa)
First eliminate one ratio using the Pythagorean identity to obtain a single-variable rational equation, then cross-multiply and proceed as above.
Exponential analogue, e.g. 1/(1−2e^x−4e^{2x}) = 2/e^x
Substitute u = e^x, cross-multiply to get a quadratic in u, then solve and discard u ≤ 0 before taking ln.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the equation as linear in sinθ because only one fraction is visible, missing the sin²θ that appears after cross-multiplying.
  • Trying to apply a trig identity to the whole rational expression instead of clearing fractions first.
  • Reading 2/sinθ as (2/sin)·θ or otherwise mishandling the reciprocal trig structure.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips when distributing the factor 2 across all three terms on the right-hand side.
  • Sign errors when moving terms across the equals sign to form the standard quadratic.
  • Forgetting to check |sinθ| ≤ 1 for each root before taking arcsin (a root outside [−1,1] is rejected).
  • Reporting only the principal arcsin value and missing the second-quadrant (or third/fourth) solutions in the given range.
  • Failing to exclude θ-values that make an original denominator zero.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P2/P3 trig equations): uses standard identities, the quadratic formula, and arcsin with quadrant symmetry; no methods beyond A-level required.
