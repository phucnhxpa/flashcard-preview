Algebra & Functions

Substitute u for the squared term to expose a hidden quadratic

Family: Hidden Quadratic Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The equation contains only two non-constant powers of the variable, where one is the square of the other (e.g. t⁴ and t², or e^{2x} and e^x, or sin²x and sin⁴x, or cos²θ and cosθ). No odd-degree term in between is present. Equivalently: the equation 'looks like a quadratic' if you squint and replace the smaller-power term by a single letter. Common phrasings: 'quartic with only even powers', 'biquadratic', 'quadratic in disguise', 'quadratic in u where u = (something)²'.

Motivation — Why this works

A polynomial equation au² + bu + c = 0 is trivial to solve by factorisation or the quadratic formula, but a quartic au⁴ + bu² + c = 0 looks intimidating. The substitution u = (smaller power) collapses the degree from 4 to 2 — or more generally from 2n to n — without losing any solutions, because the map is a bijection on the relevant domain (after care with sign). This works because the equation is structurally a composition of a quadratic with a squaring map, so we are simply peeling off the outer layer first. It avoids needing factor theorem hunting, polynomial division, or Ferrari's method on the quartic.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the powers present: only the squared power and its square (e.g. a² and a⁴), with no a³ or a¹ term — a tell-tale 'gap' in the polynomial.
  2. Rule out direct quartic factorisation by trial roots (slow, error-prone) and rule out taking square roots of both sides (illegal: there is also a constant term that ruins that move).
  3. Commit to the substitution because the structural pattern (au⁴ + bu² + c) is exactly a quadratic composed with squaring — the cleanest reduction available.
  4. Introduce a fresh variable u = (squared term) explicitly, rewrite the whole equation in u, and confirm it is a genuine quadratic in u before solving.
  5. Decide between factorising (try product = ac, sum = b first; if integer factors exist, use them) and the quadratic formula (fall back if no clean factorisation).
  6. After solving for u, substitute back u = (squared term) and check each root for admissibility — e.g. reject negative values if u must be ≥ 0 (such as u = a² or u = e^x).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the squared relationship: write the equation as a(X²)² + b(X²) + c = 0 where X is the underlying variable.
  2. Let u = X² (or u = e^x, u = sinθ, etc., as appropriate).
  3. Rewrite: au² + bu + c = 0.
  4. Factorise (seek two numbers with product ac and sum b) or apply the quadratic formula.
  5. Obtain roots u = u₁, u = u₂.
  6. Substitute back: solve X² = u₁ and X² = u₂ (rejecting any u that is inadmissible, e.g. u < 0 when u = X² and X is real, or u ≤ 0 when u = e^x).
  7. Take the appropriate roots for X (remembering ± when un-squaring).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $5t^{4} - 24t^{2} - 5 = 0$. Let $u = t^{2}$, giving $5u^{2} - 24u - 5 = 0$. Factorising: $(5u + 1)(u - 5) = 0$, so $u = -\tfrac{1}{5}$ or $u = 5$. Reject $u = -\tfrac{1}{5}$ since $t^{2} \geq 0$; hence $t^{2} = 5$ and $t = \pm\sqrt{5}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Trig equation such as $2\sin^{4}x - 3\sin^{2}x + 1 = 0$
Let $u = \sin^{2}x$; solve the quadratic in u, then solve $\sin^{2}x = u_i$ within the given range.
Exponential equation such as $e^{2x} - 5e^{x} + 6 = 0$
Let $u = e^{x}$ (note $u > 0$); solve $u^{2} - 5u + 6 = 0$, then take $x = \ln u$ for each positive root.
Quadratic in $\cos\theta$ such as $2\cos^{2}\theta + 3\cos\theta - 2 = 0$
Let $u = \cos\theta$ (note $-1 \leq u \leq 1$); solve and reject any root outside [−1, 1].
Equation involving $\sqrt{x}$ and $x$, e.g. $x - 5\sqrt{x} + 6 = 0$
Let $u = \sqrt{x}$ (so $u \geq 0$); solve $u^{2} - 5u + 6 = 0$, then square to recover x.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to factorise the quartic directly in the original variable without spotting the even-powers-only pattern.
  • Attempting to take a square root of both sides of $aX^{4} + bX^{2} + c = 0$, which is invalid because of the constant c.
  • Treating an equation with an odd-power term (e.g. $X^{4} + X^{3} + X^{2} = 0$) as a hidden quadratic — the technique only applies when the powers are exactly 2k and k.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to substitute back from u to the original variable after solving the quadratic.
  • Forgetting the ± when un-squaring: $X^{2} = u$ gives $X = \pm\sqrt{u}$, not just the positive root.
  • Failing to reject inadmissible roots (e.g. negative u when u = X² with X real, or non-positive u when u = e^x).
  • Arithmetic slips in the product-sum factorisation when the leading coefficient a ≠ 1 — splitting the middle term wrongly.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1–P4); no formula booklet entry is needed beyond the standard quadratic formula. Avoid generalising to quartics with all four powers present (those need factor theorem or Ferrari's method, which is out of scope).
