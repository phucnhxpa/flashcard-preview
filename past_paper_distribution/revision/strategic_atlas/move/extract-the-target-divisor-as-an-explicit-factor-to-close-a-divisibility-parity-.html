Trigger — When to use

Fires at the FINAL stage of a divisibility or parity proof, after substitution and simplification, when the expression is a sum/product whose terms all share the target divisor d as a common factor (or when one factor visibly contains d). Visual cues: every numerical coefficient is a multiple of d (e.g. 8, 2b all even when proving 'even'; 576, 64, 64 all divisible by 64); or a bracket like (8−2b), (2n+4), (3k+9) literally contains d. Equivalent phrasings: 'pull out a 2', 'take 64 outside', 'write as d × (integer)'. The move also fires preparatorily in induction: when f(k+1) has been rearranged to a sum of d-multiples plus a multiple of f(k), factoring d out of the residual completes the inductive step.

Motivation — Why this works

By definition, an integer N is divisible by d iff N = d·k for some integer k (with d=2 giving evenness). The most direct way to discharge this definition is to algebraically exhibit the factor of d. Factor-extraction works because integer coefficients distribute: if every term has d as a factor, the distributive law gives d·(integer combination), and the bracketed expression is automatically an integer because integers are closed under +, −, ×. This bypasses case-splits on parity, modular arithmetic, or full polynomial expansion — all of which are heavier tools that obscure the structure already visible.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Recall the goal: I need to write the expression in the form d × (integer) — that IS the definition of divisibility by d, so any other approach is indirect.
  2. Scan the simplified expression for the target divisor: are all coefficients multiples of d? Does any bracketed factor already contain d as a sub-factor (e.g. 8−2b = 2(4−b))?
  3. Rule out alternatives: I do NOT want to expand a product into a polynomial and argue term-by-term, nor split into parity cases on the variable, nor invoke modular arithmetic — these add work without aiding the proof.
  4. Commit: apply the distributive law in reverse, pulling d outside. This is a one-line operation.
  5. Tactical check: confirm the residual bracket is an integer by citing the integrality of the variables (e.g. 'b ∈ ℤ', 'k ∈ ℤ', 'M ∈ ℤ from the inductive hypothesis'). This sentence is mark-bearing.
  6. Self-audit: if any coefficient FAILS to be divisible by d, that signals an algebraic slip earlier — go back and recheck rather than fudge the factoring.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the target divisor d (from the question statement: 'prove … is even' → d=2; 'divisible by 64' → d=64).
  2. Verify each term's coefficient is divisible by d; compute the quotients (e.g. 576/64 = 9, 64/64 = 1).
  3. Apply distributivity in reverse: write the expression as d·(sum of quotient terms). E.g. 576M + 64k + 64 = 64(9M + k + 1).
  4. Name the bracketed quantity as an integer k (or any letter), explicitly stating why it is an integer (closure of ℤ under +, −, ×, given the variables are integers).
  5. Conclude with the definitional sentence: 'Therefore N = d·k where k ∈ ℤ, so d | N' (or '… so N is even').

Worked Micro-Example

Goal: prove $abc$ is even, where $a = 8 - 2b$, $c = b+2$, and $b \in \mathbb{Z}^+$. $abc = (8-2b)\,b\,(b+2) = 2(4-b)\cdot b\cdot(b+2) = 2k$, where $k = (4-b)\,b\,(b+2) \in \mathbb{Z}$ (integers closed under multiplication). Hence $abc$ is even. $\blacksquare$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Induction on f(n) = a^{pn+r} + b^{qn+s} (different growth factors)
First rewrite f(k+1) using exponent laws: a^{p(k+1)+r} = a^p · a^{pk+r}, similarly for b. Then split f(k+1) as a^p·f(k) + (b^q − a^p)·b^{qk+s}, and factor the target divisor d out of the residual coefficient (b^q − a^p) — this is where the divisibility lives.
Proving divisibility by 6 (or any composite)
Either factor 6 out directly if all coefficients are multiples of 6, or argue separately that the expression is divisible by 2 AND by 3 (using gcd(2,3)=1). Same factor-extraction logic, applied twice.
Product of n consecutive integers, divisible by n!
Factor-extraction extends: among any n consecutive integers, one is divisible by each prime up to n with the right multiplicity. Cite this rather than trying to factor by hand.
Surd / algebraic-fraction proofs requiring a specific form
The same 'pull out the target factor' logic applies: if asked to show an expression equals k√2 for integer k, factor √2 out of every term.

Common Misreadings

  • Expanding a product fully into polynomial form and then trying to argue term-by-term — needlessly creates a cubic/quartic to wrangle.
  • Splitting on parity of the variable when the factor of 2 is already present regardless of parity (e.g. (8−2b) is even for ALL integer b).
  • Writing 'clearly divisible by 64' without showing the factorisation — Edexcel mark schemes require the explicit d × (integer) form.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to state that the bracketed expression is an integer — this is a separate mark on Edexcel mark schemes for both induction and direct proof questions.
  • Arithmetic slip in the quotient (e.g. writing 576/64 = 8 instead of 9) — always double-check by multiplying back.
  • In induction, confusing the growth factor with the base (writing 3 instead of 3³ = 27 when the exponent jumps by 3).
  • Leaving the factored form as d·(expression involving a non-integer or undefined symbol) — every variable inside must be referenced as ∈ ℤ, ideally citing the inductive hypothesis.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (proof by deduction, P1) and Further Pure (proof by induction, FP1). No formula booklet entry; the move uses only the definition of divisibility and the distributive law. Avoid invoking modular arithmetic / congruences in the write-up — though valid, they sit outside the IAL syllabus and may not earn marks under the 'method shown' criterion.
