Complex Numbers

Translate Möbius locus conditions into modulus/argument equations before Cartesian substitution

Family: Locus Transformation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A Möbius-type transformation w = (z − a)/(z − b) (or similar rational function) is given, and the question asks for the locus in the z-plane corresponding to a condition on w such as |w| = 1, |w| = k, arg(w) = θ, Re(w) = 0, or Im(w) = 0. Phrasings include 'find the locus of z when w lies on...' or 'show that the image of the line/circle under this map is...'. The cue is that w is built as a quotient of linear expressions in z, so modulus/argument rules apply cleanly.

Motivation — Why this works

Because |w| = |z − a|/|z − b| and arg(w) = arg(z − a) − arg(z − b), conditions on |w| or arg(w) translate directly into standard z-plane loci: perpendicular bisectors, Apollonius circles, or arcs through two fixed points. These are loci students already know how to write down, so recognising the structure avoids the heavy algebra of substituting z = x + iy and simplifying real/imaginary parts. The substitution route is reserved for conditions like Re(w) = 0 or Im(w) = 0, where modulus/argument rules give no shortcut.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice w is a quotient of two linear expressions in z, and the condition is on |w| or arg(w) — so quotient rules for modulus/argument should apply.
  2. I rule out brute-force z = x + iy substitution because it would generate a quartic-looking mess that ultimately reduces to a standard locus anyway.
  3. I commit to the modulus/argument route because |w| = 1 immediately gives |z − a| = |z − b|, a perpendicular bisector I can state directly.
  4. I rewrite the condition: |w| = k becomes |z − a| = k|z − b|; arg(w) = θ becomes arg(z − a) − arg(z − b) = θ.
  5. I then identify the standard locus (line, circle, or arc), find its key parameters (midpoint, centre, radius), and only fall back to Cartesian substitution if the condition is on Re(w) or Im(w).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the form of the condition: |w| = k, arg(w) = θ, or Re/Im(w) = 0.
  2. Apply quotient rules: |w| = |z − a|/|z − b|; arg(w) = arg(z − a) − arg(z − b).
  3. Rewrite the condition purely in z (e.g., |z − a| = k|z − b|).
  4. Recognise the standard locus: perpendicular bisector if k = 1; Apollonius circle if k ≠ 1; arc of a circle through a and b if arg-based.
  5. Compute parameters: for the bisector, find midpoint and gradient; for the Apollonius circle, square both sides, expand, and complete the square to extract centre and radius.
  6. State the locus in clean Cartesian or modulus form, noting any half-line/arc restrictions.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $w = \dfrac{z-1}{z+1}$ and find the locus of $z$ when $|w| = 1$. Then $|z-1| = |z+1|$, the perpendicular bisector of the segment from $1$ to $-1$, which is the imaginary axis $\operatorname{Re}(z) = 0$. If instead $|w| = 2$, then $|z-1| = 2|z+1|$; squaring gives $(x-1)^2 + y^2 = 4[(x+1)^2 + y^2]$, simplifying to $\left(x + \tfrac{5}{3}\right)^2 + y^2 = \tfrac{16}{9}$, an Apollonius circle.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Condition |w| = k with k ≠ 1
Write |z − a| = k|z − b|, square both sides, expand, and complete the square to obtain an Apollonius circle with explicit centre and radius.
Condition arg(w) = θ (constant)
Use arg(z − a) − arg(z − b) = θ; the locus is an arc of a circle through a and b (a half-line if θ = 0 or π), with the inscribed-angle direction determined by the sign of θ.
Condition Re(w) = 0 or Im(w) = 0
Substitute z = x + iy, multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate of the denominator, separate real and imaginary parts, and set the required part to zero.
Inverse direction: z lies on a given line/circle, find locus of w
Solve for z in terms of w (z = (bw − a)/(w − 1) for w = (z−a)/(z−b)) and substitute into the z-condition; the same modulus/argument tricks apply on the w-side.

Common Misreadings

  • Jumping straight to z = x + iy substitution without checking whether a modulus/argument shortcut delivers the locus immediately.
  • Treating |w| = 1 as a circle condition on w and forgetting that the question asks for the corresponding locus in the z-plane.
  • Reading arg(w) = θ as a full line rather than a half-line/arc.

Pitfalls

  • For arg-based loci, forgetting the half-line/arc restriction: arg(z − a) = θ is a ray from a, not a full line through a.
  • Mixing up which constant is a (numerator zero) and which is b (denominator pole) when writing |z − a| = |z − b|.
  • When squaring |z − a| = k|z − b|, dropping a factor or mishandling the (k² − 1) coefficient that appears on the x² and y² terms.
  • Failing to exclude z = b (the pole) from the stated locus when relevant.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope: standard loci (perpendicular bisectors, circles, half-lines, Apollonius circles) and Möbius-style transformations between z- and w-planes are core FP content.
