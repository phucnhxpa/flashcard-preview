Algebra & Functions

Square both sides to eliminate paired moduli and reduce to a quadratic

Family: Squaring To Remove Moduli Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an equation has the form |f(x)| = |g(x)| (or a|f(x)| = b|g(x)| with a,b ≥ 0), so both sides are guaranteed non-negative. Typical surfaces: equidistance conditions, perpendicular-bisector setups |z-a|=|z-b|, modulus equations from coordinate geometry, and intersection problems involving |…| = |…|. The cue is 'modulus on both sides, no loose linear term outside'. If a sign-splitting case analysis would produce more than two cases, squaring is almost always faster.

Motivation — Why this works

Since |A| = |B| iff A² = B² (both sides non-negative, squaring is monotonic on [0,∞)), squaring is an equivalence here — no extraneous solutions are introduced. This collapses up to four sign combinations into a single polynomial equation. The resulting quadratic (or factorable difference of squares (A-B)(A+B)=0) captures both geometric branches simultaneously. It trades a combinatorial case-split for routine algebraic expansion.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices both sides are wrapped in modulus signs, so both are non-negative regardless of x.
  2. Rules out case analysis on sign intervals — that would mean 3+ cases and many sign-error opportunities.
  3. Commits to squaring because |A|=|B| ⇔ A²=B² is a clean equivalence with no extraneous-root check needed.
  4. Bridges to the operation by writing (coeff)²·(inner)² on each side, ready to expand or factor as a difference of squares.
  5. Tactically decides whether to expand fully into a quadratic or to factor as (aA-bB)(aA+bB)=0 — the latter is faster when each bracket is linear.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Confirm both sides are non-negative (each is a modulus, possibly times a non-negative constant).
  2. Square both sides: a²f(x)² = b²g(x)².
  3. Either (i) move to one side and factor as a difference of squares: (af(x)-bg(x))(af(x)+bg(x)) = 0, or (ii) expand and collect into a quadratic Ax² + Bx + C = 0.
  4. Solve each linear factor (route i) or apply the quadratic formula / factorisation (route ii).
  5. State both roots; no extraneous-root check is required because squaring was an equivalence.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $9|k+7| = 14|k-3|$. Square: $81(k+7)^2 = 196(k-3)^2$. Factor as a difference of squares: $[9(k+7)-14(k-3)][9(k+7)+14(k-3)] = 0$, i.e. $(-5k+105)(23k-21)=0$. Hence $k = 21$ or $k = \tfrac{21}{23}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Complex modulus equation $|z-a| = |z-b|$ with $z=x+iy$
Square both sides and expand $|z-a|^2 = (z-a)\overline{(z-a)}$ to obtain the perpendicular-bisector line in $x,y$.
$|f(x)| = c$ with $c \ge 0$
Square to $f(x)^2 = c^2$, then factor as $(f(x)-c)(f(x)+c)=0$; equivalent to the standard two-case split.
$|f(x)| = |g(x)|$ where $f,g$ are linear
Prefer the difference-of-squares factorisation $(f-g)(f+g)=0$ for two quick linear equations rather than expanding a quadratic.
$a|f(x)| = b|g(x)|$ with $a,b>0$ from equidistance/ratio problems
Square to $a^2 f^2 = b^2 g^2$; method is unchanged, the constants just scale the resulting quadratic.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $|A|=|B|$ as if it forces $A=B$, silently dropping the $A=-B$ branch.
  • Believing squaring always introduces extraneous roots and adding an unnecessary verification step.
  • Confusing this surface with $|f(x)|=g(x)$, where squaring really can introduce extraneous roots when $g<0$.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips when expanding $(ak+b)^2$ with large coefficients (e.g. $462^2$); using the difference-of-squares factorisation avoids this.
  • Forgetting to divide the resulting quadratic by a common factor before applying the quadratic formula, leading to clumsy discriminants.
  • Dropping a coefficient when squaring $a|f(x)|$ — must square the constant as well: $(a|f|)^2 = a^2 f^2$.
  • In complex-number versions, treating $|z|^2$ as $z^2$ instead of $z\bar z$.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL technique for modulus equations in Pure (P3 modulus functions) and for perpendicular-bisector loci in Further Pure complex numbers; uses only quadratic-formula and difference-of-squares tools.
