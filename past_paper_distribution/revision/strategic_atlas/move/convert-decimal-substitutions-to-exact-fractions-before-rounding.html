Algebra & Functions

Convert decimal substitutions to exact fractions before rounding

Family: Precision Management Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Substituting a decimal value of $x$ into an algebraic expression produces an awkward decimal denominator (e.g. $5 + 3(0.5) = 6.5$) that will be squared, cubed, or further combined. The question demands a specific decimal precision (3 d.p., 4 s.f., etc.) and any intermediate rounding could corrupt the final digit. Cues include 'point-five' denominators, fractions with non-integer denominators feeding into powers, and instructions like 'give your answer correct to ...'.

Motivation — Why this works

Rational arithmetic is exact: rewriting $6.5$ as $\tfrac{13}{2}$ converts $\tfrac{2}{6.5}$ into $\tfrac{4}{13}$, and squaring gives $\tfrac{16}{169}$ — a single exact rational. Only the final division to a decimal introduces any rounding, so the stated precision is fully preserved. This exploits the fact that any terminating decimal is exactly an integer-over-power-of-10, so it can always be replaced by an exact fraction.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the substituted value yields a non-integer denominator that will be raised to a power or combined further — a classic premature-rounding hazard.
  2. Rules out evaluating $2 \div 6.5$ as a decimal first, because squaring a truncated value compounds the error.
  3. Commits to working in exact fractions until the very last step, since the question fixes a decimal precision target.
  4. Rewrites the decimal as an integer fraction (here $6.5 = \tfrac{13}{2}$) so dividing-by-a-fraction becomes integer multiplication.
  5. Keeps numerator and denominator separate through any squaring/cubing, and only converts to a decimal at the final step before rounding.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the awkward decimal produced by substitution.
  2. Rewrite it as an exact fraction $\tfrac{p}{q}$ with integer $p, q$.
  3. Carry out all algebraic operations (reciprocate, square, multiply) using fraction arithmetic.
  4. Reduce to a single rational $\tfrac{a}{b}$ in lowest terms (optional but tidy).
  5. Perform $a \div b$ once and round to the stated precision.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $y = \left(\dfrac{2}{5+3x}\right)^2$ at $x = 0.5$ to 3 d.p.\\ Denominator: $5 + 1.5 = 6.5 = \tfrac{13}{2}$.\\ So $\dfrac{2}{6.5} = \dfrac{4}{13}$, and $y = \left(\dfrac{4}{13}\right)^2 = \dfrac{16}{169}$.\\ Final division: $16 \div 169 = 0.09467\ldots \Rightarrow y \approx 0.095$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Simpson's Rule / trapezium rule table entries with decimal $x$-values producing 'point-five' denominators
Keep each $f(x_i)$ as an exact fraction or in surd form during the weighted sum, decimalising only at the final $\tfrac{h}{3}(\ldots)$ step.
Iteration formulae $x_{n+1} = g(x_n)$ where the question asks for $x_3$ to a stated d.p.
Carry full calculator precision (use ANS) rather than fractions, but the same principle: never round intermediate iterates before the final one.
Binomial expansion evaluated at $x = \tfrac{1}{2}$ or similar to approximate $\sqrt{\,}$ values
Keep each term as an exact fraction, sum exactly, then convert to a decimal once at the end.
Differentiation/integration follow-ups: substituting a fractional $x$ into $f'(x)$ to find a gradient
Substitute the fraction form (e.g. $\tfrac{1}{2}$ rather than $0.5$) directly so all arithmetic stays rational.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'give your answer to 3 d.p.' as permission to round intermediate values to 3 d.p. as well.
  • Believing a calculator's stored 0.30769… is exact and so squaring it is safe.
  • Mistaking the move as merely cosmetic (fractions vs decimals) rather than a precision safeguard.

Pitfalls

  • Rounding $2/6.5 \approx 0.308$ first and then squaring; the error sometimes flips the final rounded digit.
  • Squaring only the numerator or only the denominator instead of the whole fraction.
  • Forgetting to invert when dividing by a fraction: $2 \div \tfrac{13}{2} = \tfrac{4}{13}$, not $\tfrac{13}{4}$.
  • Cancelling incorrectly when reducing $\tfrac{16}{169}$ (note $169 = 13^2$, not divisible by 2 or 4).

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure scope: arithmetic of rationals, substitution into algebraic expressions, and rounding conventions on P1–P4 papers. The same precision discipline is rewarded in numerical-methods and statistics questions where 'show that' or specified-d.p. marks are at stake.
