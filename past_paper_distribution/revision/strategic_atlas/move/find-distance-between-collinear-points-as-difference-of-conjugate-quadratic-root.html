Coordinate Geometry

Find distance between collinear points as difference of conjugate quadratic roots

Family: Distance Via Roots Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Two points $P$ and $Q$ lie on the same horizontal (or vertical) line, and their varying coordinates are the two roots of a quadratic, typically written $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{\Delta}}{2a}$. The distance $PQ$ is requested. Cue phrases include 'find $PQ$' after solving a quadratic for intersection points, or any setup where one coordinate is shared and the other comes from a $\pm\sqrt{\,}$ expression.

Motivation — Why this works

When $y_P = y_Q$, $PQ = |x_P - x_Q|$, and for conjugate roots of a quadratic the symmetric part $-b/(2a)$ cancels, leaving $|x_1 - x_2| = \sqrt{\Delta}/|a|$. This is exactly the well-known identity $(x_1-x_2)^2 = (x_1+x_2)^2 - 4x_1 x_2 = \Delta/a^2$. Exploiting the $\pm$ symmetry avoids simplifying each root individually and then subtracting, cutting arithmetic and error risk.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that $P$ and $Q$ share a coordinate (e.g. both on $y = -16$), so the distance reduces to a 1D difference.
  2. Rule out the full distance formula $\sqrt{(\Delta x)^2 + (\Delta y)^2}$ since one component is zero.
  3. Commit to subtracting the two roots; recognise the $\pm\sqrt{\Delta}$ symmetry will cause the $-b/(2a)$ part to cancel.
  4. Write $x_P - x_Q = \frac{2\sqrt{\Delta}}{2a} = \frac{\sqrt{\Delta}}{a}$ directly, skipping individual root simplification.
  5. Take absolute value, then simplify the surd fraction (rationalise/reduce) to a tidy form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Confirm shared coordinate so $PQ = |x_P - x_Q|$ (or $|y_P - y_Q|$).
  2. Write the two roots as $\frac{-b + \sqrt{\Delta}}{2a}$ and $\frac{-b - \sqrt{\Delta}}{2a}$.
  3. Subtract: $|x_P - x_Q| = \frac{2\sqrt{\Delta}}{|2a|} = \frac{\sqrt{\Delta}}{|a|}$.
  4. Substitute the numerical $\Delta$ and $a$, then simplify the surd.

Worked Micro-Example

Roots of $9x^2 - 48x + 16 = 0$ give $x = \frac{48 \pm 12\sqrt{3}}{18}$. With $P, Q$ both on $y = -16$, $PQ = \left|\frac{48 + 12\sqrt{3}}{18} - \frac{48 - 12\sqrt{3}}{18}\right| = \frac{24\sqrt{3}}{18} = \frac{4\sqrt{3}}{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two points on a vertical line $x = k$ with $y$-coordinates from a quadratic in $y$.
Apply the same root-difference shortcut to $y$ instead of $x$: $PQ = \sqrt{\Delta}/|a|$.
Chord length of a parabola/circle cut by a horizontal line, intersection $x$-values from a quadratic.
Use $\sqrt{\Delta}/|a|$ directly as the chord length without solving for each root.
Only $(x_1+x_2)$ and $x_1 x_2$ are known (Vieta), no explicit roots.
Use $|x_1 - x_2| = \sqrt{(x_1+x_2)^2 - 4x_1 x_2}$ instead of the $\pm$ shortcut.
Points are not on an axis-parallel line.
Shortcut does not apply; use full distance formula $\sqrt{(\Delta x)^2 + (\Delta y)^2}$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the situation as a general 2D distance and invoking $\sqrt{(\Delta x)^2 + (\Delta y)^2}$ even though $\Delta y = 0$.
  • Thinking each root must be simplified to lowest terms first before subtracting, missing the $\pm$ cancellation.
  • Confusing the root-difference $\sqrt{\Delta}/|a|$ with the sum of roots $-b/a$.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the absolute value when the order of $P, Q$ is not specified, producing a negative length.
  • Arithmetic slips reducing $24/18$: it equals $4/3$, not $3/4$ or $12/9$.
  • Forgetting the factor of 2 when subtracting conjugate roots, getting $\sqrt{\Delta}/(2a)$ instead of $\sqrt{\Delta}/a$.
  • Failing to rationalise or simplify the final surd (e.g. leaving $4\sqrt{3}/3$ as $4/\sqrt{3}$ unsimplified, or vice versa, depending on the mark scheme's preferred form).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (P1–P2) coordinate geometry and quadratic theory; uses only the quadratic formula, surd manipulation, and the distance between two points on an axis-parallel line.
