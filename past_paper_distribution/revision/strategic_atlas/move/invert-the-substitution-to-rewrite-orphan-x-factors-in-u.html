Integration

Invert the substitution to rewrite orphan x-factors in u

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a substitution u = g(x) is suggested (often u = ax+b inside a surd or power) but the integrand also contains a standalone factor of x (or another power of x) that is NOT proportional to g'(x). Typical surface: ∫ x√(ax+b) dx, ∫ x(2x−1)^n dx, ∫ x/(x+1)^k dx. The differential du = g'(x) dx absorbs only one piece, leaving an 'orphan' x. Cue phrase: after writing dx = du/g'(x), an x is still visible in the integrand.

Motivation — Why this works

A substitution succeeds only when every occurrence of x and dx is rewritten in u and du; otherwise the integral cannot be evaluated as a function of u. When g'(x) is constant (or the orphan factor isn't a multiple of g'(x)), the differential relationship alone cannot eliminate x. Rearranging u = g(x) to x = g^{-1}(u) provides the missing bridge and turns the integrand into a pure polynomial/power expression in u, which is then integrable term-by-term.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the integrand splits into a 'composite' part (e.g. √(4x+1)) that the substitution targets, and a 'loose' x factor sitting outside it.
  2. Rule out the shortcut where x dx folds into du: check du/dx = 4 (a constant), so the loose x is not absorbed by du; integration by parts is possible but heavier and the substitution hint is given.
  3. Commit to using the substitution fully: every x must become a function of u, including the orphan factor, by inverting u = g(x).
  4. Bridge: solve u = 4x+1 algebraically for x to get x = (u−1)/4, and write dx = (1/4) du and √(4x+1) = u^{1/2}.
  5. Substitute all three pieces simultaneously, pull constants outside, and expand to a sum of powers of u ready to integrate.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write down the substitution u = g(x); here u = 4x+1.
  2. Rearrange for x: x = (u−1)/4.
  3. Differentiate to get du/dx = 4, hence dx = (1/4) du.
  4. Rewrite the composite: √(4x+1) = u^{1/2}.
  5. Replace x, the composite, and dx in the integrand simultaneously.
  6. Collect constants and expand into a sum of powers of u ready for term-by-term integration.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int x\sqrt{4x+1}\,dx$ with $u=4x+1$. Then $x=\tfrac{u-1}{4}$, $dx=\tfrac{1}{4}du$, $\sqrt{4x+1}=u^{1/2}$. The integral becomes $\int \tfrac{u-1}{4}\cdot u^{1/2}\cdot\tfrac{1}{4}du = \tfrac{1}{16}\int (u^{3/2}-u^{1/2})\,du = \tfrac{1}{16}\left(\tfrac{2}{5}u^{5/2}-\tfrac{2}{3}u^{3/2}\right)+C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

∫ x(2x−1)^7 dx with u = 2x−1
Rearrange to x = (u+1)/2 and dx = du/2; expand (u+1)u^7/4 before integrating.
∫ x/√(x+3) dx with u = x+3
Use x = u−3 and dx = du; integrand becomes (u−3)u^{-1/2} = u^{1/2} − 3u^{-1/2}.
Definite integral ∫_a^b x√(4x+1) dx
Convert limits as well: x=a → u=4a+1, x=b → u=4b+1, then evaluate in u without resubstituting.
Higher power orphan, e.g. ∫ x²√(2x+1) dx
Invert to x=(u−1)/2 and square it: x² = (u−1)²/4, leading to a polynomial in u times u^{1/2}.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating du = 4 dx as sufficient and leaving the standalone x in the integrand, producing an unworkable mix of x and u.
  • Assuming the substitution only works when the integrand is exactly f(g(x))·g'(x), and abandoning the hint in favour of integration by parts.
  • Confusing this with the 'reverse chain rule' shortcut, where no inversion is needed.

Pitfalls

  • Algebraic slip in inverting: writing x = (u+1)/4 instead of (u−1)/4.
  • Forgetting the 1/4 from dx = (1/4) du, losing a constant factor.
  • Not expanding (u−1)·u^{1/2} into u^{3/2} − u^{1/2} before integrating, leading to errors in applying the power rule.
  • For definite integrals, converting the integrand to u but forgetting to convert the limits (or vice versa).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P3/P4) integration by substitution; only requires inverting a linear substitution u = ax+b and standard power-rule integration.
