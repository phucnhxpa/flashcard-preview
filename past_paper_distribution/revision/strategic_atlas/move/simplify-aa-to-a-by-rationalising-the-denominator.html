Algebra & Functions

Simplify a/√a to √a by rationalising the denominator

Family: Rationalisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an evaluated expression takes the form $a/\sqrt{a}$ (a rational number divided by the square root of the same number), or more generally $k/\sqrt{k}$ where the numerator equals the radicand. Common surfaces: computing $f(c)$ at a specific input, stating a minimum/range value, or simplifying a final answer. Cue phrases include 'give your answer in simplest surd form', 'state the range', or any final answer where a single surd appears in the denominator.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity $a/\sqrt{a} = \sqrt{a}$ holds because $a = (\sqrt{a})^2$, so $a/\sqrt{a} = (\sqrt{a})^2/\sqrt{a} = \sqrt{a}$. Equivalently, multiplying numerator and denominator by $\sqrt{a}$ rationalises the denominator: $a\sqrt{a}/a = \sqrt{a}$. Edexcel mark schemes require surds in simplest form with rational denominators, so this simplification is mandatory for the final accuracy mark.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the answer has a surd in the denominator and the numerator is the same number as the radicand — a tell-tale a/√a pattern.
  2. Rule out leaving it as-is: a surd denominator costs the simplification mark; rule out decimal approximation since 'exact / surd form' is demanded.
  3. Commit to rationalising because the identity $a/\sqrt{a}=\sqrt{a}$ collapses everything to a single surd.
  4. Bridge: either multiply top and bottom by $\sqrt{a}$, or directly rewrite $a$ as $(\sqrt{a})^2$ and cancel.
  5. Check whether the resulting $\sqrt{a}$ itself simplifies (e.g. $\sqrt{12}=2\sqrt{3}$) before declaring the answer final.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the form $a/\sqrt{a}$ in the evaluated expression.
  2. Multiply numerator and denominator by $\sqrt{a}$: $a/\sqrt{a} = a\sqrt{a}/a$.
  3. Cancel the factor of $a$ to obtain $\sqrt{a}$.
  4. If $a$ has a square factor, simplify the surd further (e.g. $\sqrt{12}=2\sqrt{3}$).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $f(x)=\dfrac{x^2+3}{\sqrt{2x^2-1}}$, evaluate $f(2)$.\ $f(2)=\dfrac{4+3}{\sqrt{8-1}}=\dfrac{7}{\sqrt{7}}=\dfrac{7\sqrt{7}}{7}=\sqrt{7}.$\ Hence $f(2)=\sqrt{7}$ in simplest surd form.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Numerator is a multiple of the radicand, e.g. $12/\sqrt{3}$
Rationalise: $12/\sqrt{3}=12\sqrt{3}/3=4\sqrt{3}$.
Form $a/\sqrt{a}$ with $a$ non-square, e.g. $12/\sqrt{12}$
Apply the identity to get $\sqrt{12}$, then simplify to $2\sqrt{3}$.
Denominator $a+b\sqrt{c}$ (binomial surd)
Use the conjugate $a-b\sqrt{c}$ instead of a single $\sqrt{a}$ multiplier.
Stating the range/minimum of a function
After finding the minimum value as $a/\sqrt{a}$, write the range as $f(x)\ge\sqrt{a}$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $7/\sqrt{7}$ as already simplified because there is only one surd.
  • Thinking the simplification requires a conjugate (as for $a+b\sqrt{c}$) rather than just $\sqrt{a}$.
  • Confusing $a/\sqrt{a}=\sqrt{a}$ with $\sqrt{a}/a=1/\sqrt{a}$ and applying the wrong direction.

Pitfalls

  • Leaving the answer as $7/\sqrt{7}$ and losing the simplification/accuracy mark.
  • Arithmetic slip evaluating the radicand, e.g. $\sqrt{2\cdot 4 -1}=\sqrt{9}$ instead of $\sqrt{7}$.
  • Forgetting to simplify the resulting surd further when the radicand has a square factor.
  • Multiplying only the denominator by $\sqrt{a}$ (changing the value) instead of both numerator and denominator.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure 1 surd manipulation; the identity $a/\sqrt{a}=\sqrt{a}$ and rationalising single-surd denominators are core C12/P1 skills used throughout Pure and Further Pure.
