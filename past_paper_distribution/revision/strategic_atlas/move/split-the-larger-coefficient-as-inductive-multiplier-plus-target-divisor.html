Proof

Split the larger coefficient as inductive multiplier plus target divisor

Family: Divisibility Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in induction-divisibility problems of the form $f(n)=A^n+B^n$ (or similar two-term sums) once the exponent step has been peeled, leaving $f(k+1)=a\cdot A^k + b\cdot B^k$ with unequal coefficients $a\neq b$. Cue: the two coefficients differ by exactly the target divisor $d$ (e.g. $9-2=7$). The aim is to rewrite $f(k+1)$ as $\lambda f(k)+d\cdot(\text{term})$ so both pieces are visibly divisible by $d$.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity $a\cdot A^k+b\cdot B^k = a(A^k+B^k)+(b-a)B^k = aF(k)+(b-a)B^k$ converts an awkward two-term expression into the inductive hypothesis plus a clean residue. When $b-a$ equals the target divisor, the residue carries an explicit factor of $d$, so divisibility follows immediately. This is exactly why such induction problems are engineered with growth-factor differences matching the divisor.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that after peeling, $f(k+1)$ has two terms with different coefficients, but $f(k)$ has both terms with coefficient $1$.
  2. Rule out subtracting $f(k)$ from $f(k+1)$ directly — it leaves a messy expression whose divisibility is not transparent.
  3. Commit to using the smaller coefficient as a bridge multiplier $\lambda$, since $\lambda f(k)$ then absorbs both terms cleanly.
  4. First concrete operation: write $b=\lambda+(b-\lambda)$ and split the second term so a copy of $f(k)$ emerges.
  5. Verify $b-\lambda$ equals (a multiple of) the target divisor; this is the algebraic check that the move will work.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two coefficients $a,b$ in $f(k+1)=a\cdot A^k+b\cdot B^k$ (WLOG $a\le b$).
  2. Choose bridge multiplier $\lambda=a$.
  3. Rewrite: $f(k+1)=a(A^k+B^k)+(b-a)B^k = a\,f(k)+(b-a)B^k$.
  4. Confirm $(b-a)$ is divisible by the target divisor $d$.
  5. State: $a\,f(k)$ is divisible by $d$ by the inductive hypothesis; $(b-a)B^k$ is divisible by $d$ by inspection.
  6. Conclude $d\mid f(k+1)$.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $f(n)=2^{n+2}+3^{2n+1}$; assume $7\mid f(k)$. Then $f(k+1)=2\cdot 2^{k+2}+9\cdot 3^{2k+1}=2(2^{k+2}+3^{2k+1})+7\cdot 3^{2k+1}=2f(k)+7\cdot 3^{2k+1}$. Both summands are divisible by $7$, so $7\mid f(k+1)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Bridge with the larger coefficient instead
Use $\lambda=b$: $f(k+1)=b\,f(k)-(b-a)A^k$. Equally valid; sign is negative but the factor $(b-a)$ still supplies the divisor.
$f(n)=8^n-3^n$, divisible by $5$
After peeling, $f(k+1)=8\cdot 8^k-3\cdot 3^k=3(8^k-3^k)+5\cdot 8^k=3f(k)+5\cdot 8^k$; the difference $8-3=5$ matches the target.
$f(n)=5^n+2\cdot 11^n$ divisible by $3$
Coefficients after peeling are $5$ and $22$; split $22=5+17$? No — instead use difference $11-5=6$ which is a multiple of $3$, so the same template applies with $\lambda=5$.
Three-term sums, e.g. $f(n)=a^n+b^n+c^n$
Apply the split twice, peeling one growth factor at a time, or use $f(k+1)=a\,f(k)+(b-a)b^k+(c-a)c^k$ and check both residues are divisible by $d$.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to subtract $f(k)$ from $f(k+1)$ directly without a coefficient split, producing an expression whose divisibility by $d$ is not manifest.
  • Believing the multiplier must be $1$ (i.e. trying to express $f(k+1)=f(k)+\text{something}$), which only works when the coefficients are $1$ and $1+d$.
  • Confusing this with the 'add and subtract' technique where one inserts $\pm A^k\cdot B$ — here the rewrite is purely a coefficient decomposition.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slip in the difference $b-a$ (e.g. writing $9-2=5$), which destroys the divisibility argument.
  • Failing to justify both pieces explicitly: the mark scheme demands citing the inductive hypothesis for $\lambda f(k)$ and identifying the explicit factor of $d$ in the residue.
  • Choosing $\lambda$ so that $b-\lambda$ is not a multiple of $d$ — the move only works when the coefficient difference aligns with the target divisor.
  • Forgetting to keep $B^k$ (not $B^{k+1}$) in the residue after the split — index bookkeeping must be consistent with the peeling step.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P4/FP1 induction-divisibility material; uses only integer arithmetic, index laws, and the inductive hypothesis. No modular arithmetic notation required.
