Choose the quadratic formula when factor pairs are non-obvious

Family: Quadratic Factorisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a quadratic ax² + bx + c = 0 has large or awkward coefficients so that |ac| has many factor pairs, and no splitting of b is visible by inspection within a few seconds. Typical cues: a > 10, |ac| > 100, mixed signs on a and c, or coefficients arising from clearing fractions/substitution. Also fires when the student has limited time and wants a guaranteed deterministic route. Phrasings include 'solve exactly', 'find the values of x', or a follow-on quadratic produced after substitution.

Motivation — Why this works

The quadratic formula x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac))/(2a) always solves a quadratic, while factor-by-inspection depends on quickly spotting integers p, q with p + q = b and pq = ac. When ac is large, the search space of factor pairs grows and the expected time cost exceeds the formula's fixed cost. If the discriminant turns out to be a perfect square, the formula recovers the same rational roots factoring would have found, with no risk of mis-splitting.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices a, b, c are not small (here a = 27, c = −5, so ac = −135 with many factor pairs).
  2. Rules out trial factoring: finding p + q = −42, pq = −135 is not immediate, and completing the square on a = 27 is also messy.
  3. Commits to the quadratic formula because it is guaranteed and the arithmetic is bounded.
  4. Bridges to first operation by writing down a, b, c carefully with signs and computing b² − 4ac.
  5. Tactically checks whether the discriminant is a perfect square; if so, simplifies the surd to an integer and reduces the resulting fractions to lowest terms.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify a, b, c with correct signs (e.g. a = 27, b = −42, c = −5).
  2. Compute the discriminant Δ = b² − 4ac, being careful with the sign of −4ac when c < 0.
  3. Test whether Δ is a perfect square; if so, take √Δ exactly, otherwise leave as a surd.
  4. Apply x = (−b ± √Δ)/(2a) and substitute the numerical values.
  5. Simplify each root as a fraction in lowest terms (or as an exact surd).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $27x^2 - 42x - 5 = 0$. Here $a=27,\ b=-42,\ c=-5$, so $\Delta = (-42)^2 - 4(27)(-5) = 1764 + 540 = 2304 = 48^2$. Then $x = \dfrac{42 \pm 48}{54}$, giving $x = \dfrac{90}{54} = \dfrac{5}{3}$ or $x = \dfrac{-6}{54} = -\dfrac{1}{9}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Discriminant is not a perfect square (e.g. 3x² − 5x + 1 = 0)
Leave the answer in exact surd form x = (5 ± √13)/6; do not attempt to factor.
Quadratic arising after a substitution u = xⁿ or u = eˣ
Solve for u via the formula, then back-substitute and solve the resulting equation(s) for x.
Hidden quadratic in a trig equation, e.g. 6sin²θ − 5sinθ + 1 = 0
Apply the formula in sinθ, then solve sinθ = root within the stated interval.
Small, friendly coefficients (e.g. x² − 5x + 6 = 0)
Prefer factoring by inspection; the formula is overkill and slower.
Coefficient a = 1 with even b
Completing the square is often quickest and also yields the vertex form.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the absence of an obvious factorisation as evidence the equation has no rational roots.
  • Assuming a 'large coefficient' quadratic must have irrational roots and skipping the perfect-square check on Δ.
  • Confusing the formula's −b with b when b is already negative, leading to sign errors.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error in −4ac when c is negative (writing −540 instead of +540).
  • Arithmetic slip in squaring b, especially with two-digit negatives.
  • Failing to simplify fractional roots (e.g. leaving 90/54 instead of 5/3).
  • Mis-identifying √2304 by guessing rather than factoring (2304 = 2⁸ · 3² ⇒ √2304 = 16·3 = 48).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1) quadratic equations and reused throughout P2–P4 wherever a hidden quadratic appears (logs, trig, exponentials); no content beyond the standard formula and discriminant is required.
