Factorise integer-coefficient quadratics by inspecting factor pairs of ac

Family: Factorisation By Inspection Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The quadratic ax² + bx + c = 0 has small integer coefficients, with a and c admitting only a handful of factor pairs. The product ac decomposes neatly into a pair (p, q) summing to b, signalling rational roots. Equivalently: the leading coefficient is prime or has few divisors, the constant term is small, and there is no explicit instruction to use the quadratic formula or completing the square. A quick mental check suggests the discriminant b² − 4ac is a perfect square.

Motivation — Why this works

Factorisation by inspection exploits the identity ax² + bx + c = (mx + p)(nx + q) where mn = a, pq = c, and mq + np = b. By enumerating the limited factor pairs of ac and finding the unique pair summing to b (the 'ac-method'), we convert solving into a finite search rather than a surd computation. This is faster, less error-prone, and yields exact rational roots immediately — avoiding the arithmetic burden of the quadratic formula when it is unnecessary. It also reveals the algebraic structure of the polynomial, which is useful for subsequent manipulation (e.g. partial fractions, sketching).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the quadratic has small integer coefficients — a is prime or has 1–2 factor pairs, and |c| is small — so inspection is feasible.
  2. I consider the alternatives: quadratic formula (works always but introduces surds and arithmetic risk) and completing the square (forced if the question demands surd or vertex form). Neither is needed here if the discriminant is a perfect square.
  3. I commit to inspection because the factor-pair search space is tiny: I compute ac and ask which pair (p, q) with pq = ac sums to b. Sign logic narrows it: if c < 0 the pair has opposite signs; if c > 0 both share the sign of b.
  4. I write down the candidate pair, split the middle term bx as px + qx, and factor by grouping. I extract the common bracket to read off (mx + p)(nx + q).
  5. I read the roots directly from the factored form by setting each bracket to zero, and verify by re-expanding to confirm the middle term.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute the product ac and identify b.
  2. List integer factor pairs (p, q) of ac, applying sign logic: same sign if ac > 0, opposite signs if ac < 0.
  3. Select the pair satisfying p + q = b.
  4. Split the middle term: ax² + bx + c = ax² + px + qx + c.
  5. Factor by grouping: extract common factors from the first two and last two terms, then extract the common bracket to obtain (mx + p′)(nx + q′).
  6. Set each factor to zero and solve: x = −p′/m, x = −q′/n.
  7. Verify by expanding the factored form back to the original quadratic.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $5x^2 - 2x - 3 = 0$. Compute $ac = -15$; seek pair with product $-15$ and sum $-2$: $(3, -5)$. Split: $5x^2 + 3x - 5x - 3 = 0$. Group: $x(5x + 3) - 1(5x + 3) = (5x + 3)(x - 1) = 0$. Hence $x = -\tfrac{3}{5}$ or $x = 1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Monic quadratic x² + bx + c
Skip the ac step; directly find p, q with pq = c and p + q = b, then write (x + p)(x + q).
Non-monic with multiple factor pairs of a
Enumerate factor pairs (m, n) of a as well, and test mq + np = b for each combination — or stick with the ac-method which avoids this by splitting the middle term.
Disguised quadratic, e.g. in u = x² or u = e^x
Substitute u, factorise the quadratic in u by inspection, then back-substitute and solve for x.
Discriminant is not a perfect square
Inspection fails; fall back to the quadratic formula x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac))/(2a).

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming a quadratic does not factorise just because the leading coefficient is not 1, and reaching for the formula prematurely.
  • Forgetting to consider negative factor pairs when c > 0 but b < 0 (both factors should be negative).
  • Treating the ac-method pair (p, q) as the constants in the brackets directly, instead of using them to split the middle term.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when c < 0: assigning the wrong sign to p or q produces an incorrect middle term.
  • Mis-ordering the split (e.g. writing +5x − 3x instead of +3x − 5x) so that grouping fails to yield a common bracket.
  • In non-monic cases, finding p + q = b with pq = c (instead of pq = ac), which is invalid when a ≠ 1.
  • Forgetting to verify by re-expansion, allowing a sign slip to propagate into wrong roots.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1 (quadratics) and reused throughout P2–P4 and FP1. The quadratic formula in the formula booklet is the fallback; factorisation by inspection is expected fluency and is not itself a booklet entry. Avoid extending this to non-integer or irrational coefficients where inspection is not reliable.
