Sequences & Series

Use b² = ac to encode three terms in geometric progression

Family: Gp Characterisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a problem states that three specified expressions (or three specific terms of an expansion/sequence) are consecutive terms of a geometric progression. Cue phrases include 'are in geometric progression', 'form a GP', 'are three consecutive terms of a geometric series', or 'the common ratio r exists such that ...'. The three quantities are usually given as algebraic expressions in a single unknown, and you are asked to find that unknown. No common ratio is named in the question.

Motivation — Why this works

For three consecutive GP terms a, b, c the defining property b/a = c/b rearranges to b² = ac, i.e. b is the geometric mean of a and c. This single equation encodes the constant-ratio property while eliminating the common ratio r entirely, collapsing what would be two equations (b = ar, c = ar²) into one. The result is a single polynomial equation in the unknown, which is the most efficient route to a solution.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that three expressions are flagged as consecutive GP terms, so a constant ratio r links them.
  2. Rule out introducing r explicitly: that would create two equations (b = ar and c = ar²) and an extra variable to eliminate.
  3. Commit to the geometric mean identity b² = ac because it captures both ratio equations in one shot and keeps the unknown (x, n, k, ...) as the only variable.
  4. Bridge to operation: square the middle expression in full (including any powers of x) and set it equal to the product of the outer two expressions.
  5. Tactically simplify by dividing through by common numerical factors and matching powers of the unknown before solving.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Label the three given expressions a, b, c in the order they appear in the GP.
  2. Write the equation b² = ac.
  3. Substitute the algebraic expressions and expand both sides carefully, including powers of the unknown.
  4. Cancel common numerical factors and equate powers/coefficients to obtain a single equation in the unknown.
  5. Solve and discard any extraneous roots (e.g. x = 0 if it makes terms vanish, or negative values if the context forbids them).

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose $T_2 = 2916x$, $T_3 = 4860x^2$, $T_5 = 2160x^4$ are consecutive GP terms. Then $(4860x^2)^2 = (2916x)(2160x^4)$, giving $23\,619\,600\,x^4 = 6\,298\,560\,x^5$. Dividing by $x^4$ (with $x \neq 0$): $x = \dfrac{23\,619\,600}{6\,298\,560} = \dfrac{15}{4}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Three terms of an arithmetic progression
Use the arithmetic mean property $2b = a + c$ instead of $b^2 = ac$.
Four terms of a GP given
Form two overlapping triples and apply $b^2 = ac$ to each, giving two equations in the unknowns.
Three terms of a binomial expansion in GP
Compute the relevant $\binom{n}{r}$ coefficients first, then apply $b^2 = ac$ to solve for $n$ or $x$.
Three logarithms in AP equivalent to three values in GP
If $\log a, \log b, \log c$ are in AP then $a, b, c$ are in GP, so $b^2 = ac$ still applies.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing 'consecutive terms of a GP' with 'consecutive terms of the expansion/sequence' — the listed terms are consecutive within the GP, not necessarily adjacent in the original sequence.
  • Treating the three expressions as in AP and using $2b = a + c$ by mistake.
  • Assuming the common ratio must be positive or rational and discarding valid solutions on that basis.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to square the entire middle term including its variable factor, leading to wrong powers of the unknown.
  • Cancelling a factor of $x$ (or $x^k$) without recording $x \neq 0$, then missing or wrongly including the trivial root.
  • Arithmetic slips when squaring large coefficients — keep factors in factorised form for as long as possible.
  • Failing to check the obtained value actually produces a non-degenerate GP (no zero terms).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 (sequences and series) and P3/P4 (binomial expansion); the geometric mean identity $b^2 = ac$ is the standard examiner-expected route for 'three terms in GP' problems.
