Algebra & Functions

Consolidate logarithmic terms via log laws to match a required p + ln q form

Family: Log Law Simplification Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

The expression you have just evaluated (typically the result of a definite integral, the solution of a differential equation, or a 'show that' final line) contains two or more separate logarithmic terms — e.g. ln A − ln B, ln A + ln B, or −ln(1/k) — together with loose rational constants. The question explicitly demands the answer in a structured exact form such as 'p + ln q', 'a + ln b', or 'a ln(b/c)'. Equivalent cues: the mark scheme phrasing 'in the form …', a 'show that' instruction, or the presence of awkward constants like −ln(1/4) that beg to be flipped. This is the signal to consolidate: one rational constant + exactly one logarithm.

Motivation — Why this works

The log laws ln A − ln B = ln(A/B), ln A + ln B = ln(AB), and −ln(1/k) = ln k let you collapse multiple log terms into a single ln. This is essential because the demanded form (p + ln q) literally has only one ln slot — leaving the answer as a sum/difference of logs forfeits 'show that' marks even though it is numerically equivalent. Consolidating before any numerical substitution also keeps the answer exact (e.g. ln(3.5/0.5) = ln 7 rather than a decimal), which is what the mark scheme rewards.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the current expression has the right numerical value but the wrong shape: there are two or more ln terms, or a −ln(1/k), plus stray fractions.
  2. Rule out further integration/substitution tricks — the calculus is done; this is purely an algebraic tidy-up using log laws.
  3. Rule out evaluating the logs numerically (e.g. ln 5 ≈ 1.609); the question wants an exact form, so decimals are the wrong route.
  4. Commit to consolidation: separate the rational constants from the log terms, combine each group independently, then write a + ln b.
  5. Tactical choice on the log side: if the surface is ln A − ln B, use the quotient rule to get ln(A/B); if it is ln A + ln B, use the product rule to get ln(AB); if a term has the form −ln(1/k), flip it to +ln k first to expose the cleanest combination.
  6. Tactical choice on the constant side: take a common denominator and add carefully (sign errors here are the most common loss of marks).
  7. Finally, read off p (or a) and q (or b) directly from the consolidated expression and check the form matches what was asked.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Group the expression as (rational constants) + (logarithmic terms).
  2. Rewrite any −ln(1/k) as +ln k to remove negative-fraction logs.
  3. Apply ln A − ln B = ln(A/B) or ln A + ln B = ln(AB) until exactly one ln remains.
  4. Combine the rational constants over a common denominator into a single fraction.
  5. Write the result as p + ln q (or a + ln b) and identify p, q explicitly.
  6. If a substitution (e.g. h = 3.5, evaluation at bounds) is still pending, perform it AFTER consolidation so the argument of ln simplifies to an integer or simple fraction.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose evaluating an integral gives $\left[-x - \ln(1-x) + \ln(1+x)\right]_{1/2}^{3/4}$. At $x=3/4$: $-\tfrac34 - \ln\tfrac14 + \ln\tfrac74 = -\tfrac34 + \ln 4 + \ln\tfrac74 = -\tfrac34 + \ln 7$. At $x=1/2$: $-\tfrac12 + \ln 2 + \ln\tfrac32 = -\tfrac12 + \ln 3$. Subtracting: $-\tfrac14 + \ln\tfrac73$, so $p=-\tfrac14,\; q=\tfrac73$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Solving a differential equation where the particular solution contains ln A − ln|f(x)| before substituting a target x-value.
Combine to ln(A/f(x)) FIRST, then substitute, so the argument simplifies to a clean integer (e.g. ln(3.5/0.5) = ln 7) and the final answer stays exact.
The two log terms are added rather than subtracted.
Use the product rule ln A + ln B = ln(AB) — same family, different log law.
A coefficient sits in front, e.g. 2 ln 3 + ln 5.
First push the coefficient inside as a power: 2 ln 3 = ln 9, then combine with the product rule to ln 45.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the question as asking for a decimal approximation and evaluating the logs numerically.
  • Thinking the consolidation is cosmetic and leaving ln A − ln B in the final answer when 'in the form a + ln b' was demanded.
  • Confusing the quotient rule with a non-existent 'subtraction inside the log' rule.

Pitfalls

  • Writing ln A − ln B = ln(A − B) — a fatal log-law error.
  • Applying the quotient rule the wrong way round: ln 3.5 − ln(4−h) becoming ln(3.5(4−h)) instead of ln(3.5/(4−h)).
  • Forgetting to flip −ln(1/k) into +ln k, leaving negative logs of fractions that obscure the simplification.
  • Sign/arithmetic slip when combining rational constants, e.g. −3/4 − (−1/2) miscomputed as −5/4 instead of −1/4.
  • Rounding ln 7 (or similar) prematurely and losing the exact 'show that' form.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL P2/P3/P4 scope: uses only the standard log laws (product, quotient, power) listed in the formula booklet's logarithm section, plus basic fraction arithmetic. No need for change-of-base or complex-log generalisations.
