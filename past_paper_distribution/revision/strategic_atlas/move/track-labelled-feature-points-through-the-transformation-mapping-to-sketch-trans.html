Algebra & Functions

Track labelled feature points through the transformation mapping to sketch transformed curves

Family: Point Tracking Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question shows a sketch of y = f(x) with specific labelled points (x-intercepts, y-intercept, turning points, or 'touch' points) but gives NO algebraic formula for f(x). It then asks you to sketch a transformed curve such as y = f(x) + a, y = f(x − a), y = af(x), y = f(ax), y = −f(x), y = f(−x), or y = |f(x)|. Equivalent phrasings: 'On a separate diagram, sketch the curve with equation ...', 'showing the coordinates of the points where the curve meets the axes', 'state the coordinates of the image of the minimum/maximum/touch point'. The dead giveaway is the presence of labelled coordinates plus the absence of an explicit f(x).

Motivation — Why this works

Without an explicit formula for f(x), no algebraic substitution, expansion, or differentiation is possible — the only data available are the coordinates of the labelled features. Each transformation of f corresponds to a fixed coordinate mapping (x, y) → (x', y'), so applying that mapping to every labelled point is both necessary and sufficient to determine the new sketch. This works because translations, stretches, and reflections preserve the qualitative shape of the curve (continuity, smoothness, whether an axis is crossed or touched), so once the mark-bearing points are correctly relocated the rest of the sketch follows. The marks in the markscheme are tied directly to these transformed coordinates.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the question provides a graphical definition of f with several labelled coordinate points, and explicitly asks to 'state the coordinates' of features on the new curve — this signals that coordinates are the mark-bearing objects.
  2. Rule out trying to reconstruct f(x) algebraically (e.g. fitting a cubic through the three given points): no formula is given, the curve may not be polynomial, and even if a fit existed it would waste time and risk algebraic errors.
  3. Rule out plotting many sample points: the curve is only known at the labelled features, so dense plotting is impossible.
  4. Commit to point-tracking: read off the coordinate mapping rule that the transformation induces, e.g. y = f(x − a) ⇒ (x, y) → (x + a, y); y = af(x) ⇒ (x, y) → (x, ay); y = f(bx) ⇒ (x, y) → (x/b, y); y = |f(x)| ⇒ points with y < 0 reflect to (x, −y).
  5. List EVERY labelled point on the original sketch (including the y-intercept and any point that 'looks unchanged' such as the origin), then apply the mapping to each in turn.
  6. Preserve qualitative information: a 'touch' point stays a touch point, a minimum stays a minimum (unless reflected, in which case it becomes a maximum), and the overall shape (cubic-like, parabola-like, etc.) is retained.
  7. Draw the new curve through the transformed points and label each new coordinate explicitly on the diagram.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. List all labelled feature points from the original curve, e.g. {(−1, 0), (0, 2), (3, 0) touch} or {(0, 0), (6, 0), (3, −1) min}.
  2. Identify the transformation and write its coordinate mapping in the form (x, y) → (x', y'). For example: y = f(x) + 3 gives (x, y) → (x, y + 3); y = f(2x) gives (x, y) → (x/2, y); y = −f(x) gives (x, y) → (x, −y).
  3. Apply the mapping to each listed point individually, recording the new coordinates.
  4. For composite transformations such as y = 2f(3x − 1) + 4, decompose into a chain (inner shift, inner stretch, outer stretch, outer shift) and apply mappings sequentially to each point.
  5. Sketch the new curve preserving the original qualitative shape, axes orientation, and 'cross vs touch' behaviour at each point.
  6. Label every transformed point with its exact coordinates on the new diagram.

Worked Micro-Example

Given y = f(x) has labelled points (−1, 0), (0, 2), (3, 0) (touch). Sketch y = f(x − 2). Mapping: (x, y) → (x + 2, y). Apply: (−1, 0) → (1, 0); (0, 2) → (2, 2); (3, 0) touch → (5, 0) touch. The new sketch crosses the x-axis at (1, 0), passes through (2, 2), and touches the x-axis at (5, 0).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Vertical translation/stretch y = af(x) + b
Mapping (x, y) → (x, ay + b); x-coordinates unchanged, y-intercepts shift, x-intercepts generally do NOT remain on the x-axis unless b = 0.
Horizontal stretch y = f(bx)
Mapping (x, y) → (x/b, y); intercepts on the y-axis are fixed (since x = 0 maps to 0), other x-coordinates scale by 1/b.
Reflection y = −f(x) or y = f(−x)
(x, y) → (x, −y) or (x, y) → (−x, y) respectively; minima become maxima under y = −f(x).
Modulus y = |f(x)|
Conditional mapping: points with y ≥ 0 stay; points with y < 0 reflect to (x, −y). A minimum at (3, −1) becomes a maximum at (3, 1).
Modulus y = f(|x|)
Keep the right-hand portion (x ≥ 0) and reflect it in the y-axis to form the left-hand portion; original left-hand points are discarded.
f(x) IS given explicitly (e.g. a cubic)
Algebraic substitution is now possible, but point-tracking on the key features is still the fastest route for a sketch question.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to fit/guess a formula for f(x) (e.g. assuming it's a cubic through the three labelled points) and then transforming the formula — wastes time and risks error.
  • Treating y = f(x − 2) as a leftward shift instead of rightward (sign confusion in the inner mapping).
  • Treating y = f(2x) as a horizontal stretch by factor 2 instead of factor 1/2.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to transform ALL labelled points — particularly the y-intercept or a point at the origin that 'looks unchanged' but may move under a translation.
  • Failing to label the new coordinates on the sketch — markschemes award marks for the labelled coordinates, not just the shape.
  • Losing the qualitative feature: a 'touch' point must remain a touch point; a reflected minimum must be relabelled as a maximum.
  • For composite transformations, applying the mappings in the wrong order (e.g. shifting before stretching when the algebra requires the reverse).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P2/P3 transformations of graphs: y = f(x) + a, y = f(x + a), y = af(x), y = f(ax), y = −f(x), y = f(−x), y = |f(x)|, y = f(|x|), and simple compositions. No formula booklet entry is needed — the mappings are expected to be memorised. Avoid invoking matrix transformations (FP1) or general affine transformations beyond the syllabus.
