Integration

Integrate tan(ax) as −(1/a)ln|cos(ax)| via f'/f recognition

Family: Trig Standard Integral Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the integrand is tan(x), tan(ax), or tan(ax+b), often appearing as a residual after integration by parts or a substitution. The tangent is not on the immediate standard-integral list (unlike sin, cos, sec²), so direct antidifferentiation stalls. The cue is recognising tan = sin/cos, where the numerator is (up to sign) the derivative of the denominator. Equivalent surfaces include cot, where cos/sin gives the analogous f'/f pattern.

Motivation — Why this works

Writing tan(ax) = sin(ax)/cos(ax) exposes the structure −(1/a)·f'(x)/f(x) with f(x) = cos(ax) and f'(x) = −a sin(ax). Since ∫f'/f dx = ln|f| + C is a standard logarithmic integral, this reveals tan as a logarithm in disguise. The chain-rule factor a is absorbed by dividing by a, yielding ∫tan(ax) dx = −(1/a)ln|cos(ax)| + C. This is bookwork at A-level and converts a stuck trigonometric integral into a clean log expression.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the integrand is tan(2x) — not on my immediate standard list of sin, cos, sec², cosec², sec·tan, cosec·cot.
  2. I rule out a u-substitution as 'creative work' — tan integrals are standard, so there should be a memorised form.
  3. I commit to rewriting tan = sin/cos because the derivative of cos is −sin, signalling an f'/f logarithmic integral.
  4. I bridge by writing ∫tan(2x) dx = ∫sin(2x)/cos(2x) dx and forcing the numerator to be exactly the derivative of cos(2x), which is −2sin(2x).
  5. Tactically, I balance the introduced −2 by a compensating −1/2 outside, giving −(1/2)ln|cos(2x)| + C, then track this minus sign carefully through any outer multiplier.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the standard form: ∫tan(ax) dx = −(1/a)ln|cos(ax)| + C.
  2. If derived rather than quoted: rewrite tan(ax) = sin(ax)/cos(ax).
  3. Force f'/f: multiply and divide by −a so the numerator becomes −a sin(ax) = (d/dx)cos(ax).
  4. Apply ∫f'/f dx = ln|f|, giving −(1/a)ln|cos(ax)| + C.
  5. Substitute back into the surrounding expression, carrying any external constants and minus signs.
  6. Evaluate at the given limits, simplifying ln values such as ln(1) = 0 and ln(1/2) = −ln 2.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_{0}^{\pi/6} \tfrac{1}{2}\tan(2x)\,dx$. Using $\int\tan(2x)\,dx = -\tfrac{1}{2}\ln|\cos(2x)| + C$, the integral equals $\tfrac{1}{2}\cdot\left[-\tfrac{1}{2}\ln|\cos(2x)|\right]_{0}^{\pi/6} = -\tfrac{1}{4}\bigl(\ln\tfrac{1}{2} - \ln 1\bigr) = \tfrac{1}{4}\ln 2.$

Variants — Same move, different surface

∫tan(x) dx
No chain-rule factor; result is −ln|cos(x)| + C, equivalently ln|sec(x)| + C.
∫cot(ax) dx
Use cot = cos/sin; numerator is the derivative of sin(ax), giving (1/a)ln|sin(ax)| + C with a positive sign.
∫tan(ax+b) dx
Linear inner function still contributes only the factor 1/a; result is −(1/a)ln|cos(ax+b)| + C.
∫f'(x)tan(f(x)) dx
After substitution u = f(x), reduces to ∫tan(u) du = −ln|cos(u)| + C.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating tan as a 'standard derivative' and writing ∫tan(x) dx = sec²(x), confusing it with the derivative of tan rather than its integral.
  • Reading tan(2x) as 2tan(x) and integrating term-by-term.
  • Mistaking the integrand for sec²(2x) and producing (1/2)tan(2x) instead of a logarithm.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the 1/a factor from the chain rule on the inner function ax.
  • Sign error: writing +ln|cos(ax)| instead of −ln|cos(ax)|, losing the minus from d/dx[cos] = −sin.
  • Dropping the absolute value bars inside the logarithm, which matters when cos(ax) is negative on the interval.
  • Failing to check that cos(ax) is non-zero on the integration interval (the integrand is undefined where cos vanishes).

A-level Scope Notes

This is a standard result in Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P3/P4) under integration of trigonometric functions and the recognition of ∫f'(x)/f(x) dx = ln|f(x)| + C. No use of complex logarithms or hyperbolic identities is required.
