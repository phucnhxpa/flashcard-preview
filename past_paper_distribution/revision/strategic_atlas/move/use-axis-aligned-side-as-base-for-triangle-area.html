Coordinate Geometry

Use axis-aligned side as base for triangle area

Family: Base Height Area Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Two of the three triangle vertices share a coordinate (both have y = 0, both have x = 0, or more generally lie on a common horizontal or vertical line). The connecting segment is then axis-parallel and serves as a ready-made base. Cues: vertex list contains two zero y-values, two zero x-values, or two equal y- or x-coordinates. This setup makes ½ × base × height immediate, sidestepping the shoelace formula.

Motivation — Why this works

When two vertices lie on a common horizontal (or vertical) line, the segment between them is parallel to an axis, so its length is just the absolute difference of the differing coordinate. The triangle's height is then the perpendicular distance from the third vertex to that line, which is again a single absolute coordinate difference. This reduces the area to one multiplication, avoiding the shoelace expansion or Heron's formula and the arithmetic errors they invite.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I scan the three vertices and notice two of them share the same y-coordinate (or x-coordinate), so the side joining them is axis-parallel.
  2. I rule out the shoelace formula and Heron's formula — they would work but are heavier than necessary given the alignment.
  3. I commit to ½ × base × height because the base length and the perpendicular height each reduce to a single |coordinate difference|.
  4. I take the axis-parallel segment as the base and read off the perpendicular height as the absolute value of the third vertex's offset from that line.
  5. I compute |Δ| for the base, |Δ| for the height, multiply, and halve — keeping signs absorbed in the absolute values throughout.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Spot the pair of vertices sharing a y-coordinate (or x-coordinate); declare that segment the base.
  2. Base length = |difference of the non-shared coordinates of those two vertices|.
  3. Height = |perpendicular distance from the third vertex to the line containing the base| = |its differing coordinate − shared coordinate|.
  4. Area = ½ × base × height.

Worked Micro-Example

Vertices $A(-4,0)$, $B(0,6)$, $C(7,0)$. Since $A$ and $C$ share $y=0$, take $AC$ as base: $|7-(-4)|=11$. Height from $B$ to the x-axis is $|6|=6$. Area $= \tfrac{1}{2}\times 11 \times 6 = 33$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two vertices share an x-coordinate (segment vertical)
Use the vertical segment as base (|Δy|), and height = |Δx| from the third vertex to that vertical line.
Two vertices share a non-zero y-value, e.g. both on y = k
Base = |Δx|; height = |y₃ − k| where y₃ is the third vertex's y-coordinate.
No two vertices share a coordinate
Fall back to shoelace: Area $= \tfrac{1}{2}|x_1(y_2-y_3)+x_2(y_3-y_1)+x_3(y_1-y_2)|$.
One side lies on the y-axis
Base measured along the y-axis (|Δy|); height = |x| of the off-axis vertex.

Common Misreadings

  • Defaulting to the shoelace formula without first checking whether two vertices share a coordinate.
  • Treating the longest side as the base by reflex, ignoring the simpler axis-aligned side.
  • Confusing 'height' with the length of another side of the triangle rather than a perpendicular distance.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error in the base length when one coordinate is negative, e.g. computing $7-(-4)=3$ instead of $11$ — always take absolute value.
  • Using $|AB|$ or $|BC|$ as the height instead of the perpendicular distance from $B$ to line $AC$.
  • Forgetting the factor of ½ in the area formula.
  • When the shared line is $y=k$ with $k\neq 0$, using $|y_3|$ instead of $|y_3-k|$ for the height.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure coordinate geometry; uses only distance along axes and the elementary triangle-area formula, no vectors or determinants required.
