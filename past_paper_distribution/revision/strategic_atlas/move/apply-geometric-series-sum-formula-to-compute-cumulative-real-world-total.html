Sequences & Series

Apply geometric series sum formula to compute cumulative real-world total

Family: Series Sum Application Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question asks for a CUMULATIVE total across a span of years/periods (e.g. 'total amount of money given from 2018 to 2022 inclusive', 'total production over the first 10 years', 'total distance travelled in 6 swings'), where each period's contribution is one term of a geometric sequence already established in an earlier part. Look for the words 'total', 'sum', 'altogether', or 'in the n years' combined with a multiplicative growth/decay context (population growing 2% per year, depreciation, salary increases). The presence of an inclusive year range is the strongest cue — it tells you to count terms carefully.

Motivation — Why this works

Each period's value forms a geometric progression with first term a and common ratio r, so the cumulative total is exactly Sₙ = a(rⁿ − 1)/(r − 1). Adding the terms one by one would work but wastes time and risks arithmetic slips, especially with non-integer ratios like 1.02. The closed-form sum is the canonical Edexcel method and earns the method mark in one line. Underlying identity: 1 + r + r² + … + rⁿ⁻¹ = (rⁿ − 1)/(r − 1), which comes from telescoping (1 − r)(1 + r + … + rⁿ⁻¹) = 1 − rⁿ.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that each period's contribution is a term of a GP already set up — so the running total is a sum of consecutive GP terms, not a single term.
  2. Rule out: computing each year individually and adding (slow, error-prone), or using the nth term formula uₙ = arⁿ⁻¹ (that gives only the LAST year, not the total).
  3. Commit to Sₙ = a(rⁿ − 1)/(r − 1) — it is in the IAL formula booklet and produces the answer in one substitution.
  4. Count the number of terms carefully from the inclusive range: 'from year Y₁ to year Y₂ inclusive' gives n = Y₂ − Y₁ + 1, NOT Y₂ − Y₁.
  5. Choose the orientation of the formula tactically: with r > 1 use (rⁿ − 1)/(r − 1) so both numerator and denominator are positive; with r < 1 use (1 − rⁿ)/(1 − r) for the same reason.
  6. Read the rounding instruction ('to the nearest £', '3 s.f.') and keep full precision until the final step to avoid premature-rounding error.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify a (the first term, i.e. value in the first counted period) and r (the common ratio).
  2. Determine n by inclusive count: n = (last index) − (first index) + 1.
  3. Write Sₙ = a(rⁿ − 1)/(r − 1), substituting the values.
  4. Compute rⁿ to full calculator precision (do NOT round here).
  5. Form the numerator a(rⁿ − 1), then divide by (r − 1).
  6. Round only at the final step, to the accuracy specified in the question.

Worked Micro-Example

Population in 2018 is 50000, growing at 2% per year. Each adult gives £1; find total given from 2018 to 2022 inclusive. Here a = 50000, r = 1.02, n = 5. Then S₅ = 50000(1.02⁵ − 1)/(1.02 − 1) = 50000(0.1040808…)/0.02 = 260202.008… ≈ £260202.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Decay/depreciation context with r < 1 (e.g. machine value falling 5% per year)
Use Sₙ = a(1 − rⁿ)/(1 − r) so both top and bottom are positive — algebraically identical, just tidier.
Total over a sub-range not starting at year 1 (e.g. 'from 2019 to 2022')
Either compute S(end) − S(start−1), or re-index with new first term ar^(k) and recount n.
Cumulative total exceeds a threshold ('in which year does the total first exceed …')
Set Sₙ > threshold, solve a(rⁿ − 1)/(r − 1) > T for n using logarithms, then take ⌈n⌉.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 'between 2018 and 2022' or 'from 2018 to 2022' as n = 4 instead of n = 5 — inclusive ranges add one.
  • Forgetting that each person gives £1 (or whatever per-unit amount) so the monetary total equals the population sum — units must be tracked.
  • Confusing 'total' (sum) with 'amount in the final year' (single nth term) and applying uₙ = arⁿ⁻¹ instead of Sₙ.

Pitfalls

  • Using rⁿ⁻¹ in the sum formula instead of rⁿ — the sum uses rⁿ; only the nth-term formula uses rⁿ⁻¹.
  • Rounding rⁿ (e.g. 1.02⁵) to 3 or 4 d.p. before dividing by (r − 1); since r − 1 = 0.02 is small, this magnifies the error massively.
  • Mis-typing the formula by dropping the '−1' in the numerator, giving arⁿ/(r−1) instead of a(rⁿ−1)/(r−1).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 scope. The formula Sₙ = a(rⁿ − 1)/(r − 1) for a geometric series is in the IAL formula booklet under 'Sequences and series'. Do not invoke the infinite sum a/(1 − r) unless |r| < 1 AND the question explicitly asks for an infinite/long-term total.
