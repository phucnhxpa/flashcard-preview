Trigger — When to use

Fires when a triangle has two of its vertices on a known line $\ell$ and the third vertex $A$ off the line, and you already know $|AP|$ and $\cos\theta$ (the angle between $AP$ and $\ell$) from earlier parts. Cues: 'find the area of triangle ...', isosceles condition fixing the base length, and an angle between a vector and a line computed in a previous part. Especially useful when the third vertex on the line (e.g. $E$) is not yet determined, so a cross-product approach is premature.

Motivation — Why this works

Dropping a perpendicular from $A$ to $\ell$ at foot $F$ splits $AP$ into a parallel piece $PF = |AP|\cos\theta$ and a perpendicular piece $h = |AP|\sin\theta$, where $h$ is exactly the triangle's height above base $PE \subset \ell$. Pythagoras gives $h^2 = |AP|^2 - PF^2$ directly, bypassing a separate $\sin\theta$ computation. Then Area $= \tfrac12 \cdot PE \cdot h$ closes the problem with quantities already in hand. This is cleaner than $\tfrac12|\vec{AP}\times\vec{AE}|$ when $E$ has not yet been located.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the triangle has a side $PE$ lying along the known line $\ell$, so the natural height is the perpendicular distance from $A$ to $\ell$.
  2. Rule out the cross-product area formula because the third vertex $E$ on $\ell$ hasn't been pinned down yet — using it would force a detour.
  3. Commit to the projection picture: $|AP|$ and $\cos\theta$ are already known, so the parallel projection $PF$ drops out instantly.
  4. Bridge: compute $PF = |AP|\cos\theta$, then get $h$ from $h^2 = |AP|^2 - PF^2$ rather than re-deriving $\sin\theta$.
  5. Tactical: identify the base length from the problem's geometric condition (here isosceles gives $PE = |AP|$), then assemble Area $= \tfrac12 \cdot PE \cdot h$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify base $PE$ along $\ell$ and height $h = $ perpendicular distance from $A$ to $\ell$.
  2. Compute the parallel projection $PF = |AP|\cos\theta$ using the angle from the previous part.
  3. Apply Pythagoras: $h^2 = |AP|^2 - PF^2$, then take the positive root.
  4. Determine $PE$ from the geometric condition stated (e.g. isosceles, midpoint, given length).
  5. Assemble Area $= \tfrac12 \cdot PE \cdot h$ and simplify the surd.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $|AP| = 2\sqrt{5}$, $\cos\theta = \dfrac{\sqrt{10}}{50}$, and $PE = 2\sqrt{5}$ (isosceles). Then $PF = 2\sqrt{5}\cdot\dfrac{\sqrt{10}}{50} = \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{5}$, so $h^2 = 20 - \dfrac{2}{25} = \dfrac{498}{25}$ and $h = \dfrac{\sqrt{498}}{5}$. Area $= \tfrac12\cdot 2\sqrt{5}\cdot\dfrac{\sqrt{498}}{5} = \dfrac{\sqrt{2490}}{5}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Distance from a point to a line in 3D as a standalone task
Use $h = |\vec{AP}|\sin\theta$ with $\sin\theta$ from $\sin^2\theta = 1-\cos^2\theta$, or equivalently $h = \dfrac{|\vec{AP}\times\hat{d}|}{1}$ where $\hat{d}$ is the unit direction of $\ell$.
Triangle area with all three vertices known as position vectors
Switch to Area $= \tfrac12|\vec{AP}\times\vec{AE}|$ — no projection needed since both edge vectors from a common vertex are available.
Foot of perpendicular $F$ explicitly required
Parameterise $\ell$ as $\vec{r}=\vec{p}+t\vec{d}$, set $(\vec{r}-\vec{a})\cdot\vec{d}=0$ to find $t$, then recover $F$ and $h=|\vec{AF}|$.
Parallelogram area on the same configuration
Same $h$, but Area $= PE\cdot h$ (no factor of $\tfrac12$).

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the angle $\theta$ between $AP$ and the line $\ell$ as the interior angle $\angle APE$ of the triangle — they generally differ.
  • Applying $\tfrac12\cdot AP\cdot PE\cdot\sin(\angle APE)$ with $\theta$ substituted for $\angle APE$.
  • Reading $PE$ as the projection $PF$ rather than the full base length given by the isosceles/length condition.

Pitfalls

  • Writing $PF^2 = |AP|\cos^2\theta$ instead of $(|AP|\cos\theta)^2 = |AP|^2\cos^2\theta$.
  • Forgetting to take the positive square root for $h$, or losing a factor of $25$ when combining $|AP|^2 - PF^2$ over a common denominator.
  • Using $PE = PF$ (the projection) as the base instead of the actual side length of the triangle.
  • Arithmetic slip when simplifying surds like $\sqrt{5}\cdot\sqrt{498} = \sqrt{2490}$.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only Edexcel IAL Pure/Further Pure vector tools: dot product, angle between a vector and a line, Pythagoras, and $\tfrac12\cdot\text{base}\cdot\text{height}$. Cross product is mentioned only as an FP3-level alternative; the main method stays within standard IAL vectors.
