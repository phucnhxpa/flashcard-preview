Proof

Substitute derived constraint into curve equation to force a contradiction

Family: Proof By Contradiction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a problem asks you to prove that no point on a curve satisfies some auxiliary condition (e.g. vertical tangent, horizontal tangent, lies on a given line, intersects another curve). The auxiliary condition reduces to a simple relation between the coordinates such as $y=x$, $y=0$, or $y=mx+c$. You must show the system of {curve equation, auxiliary relation} has no real solution. Phrasings include 'show that C has no vertical tangents', 'prove the line does not meet C', or 'show no such point exists'.

Motivation — Why this works

Substituting the auxiliary relation into the curve equation eliminates one variable, collapsing a 2D problem into a single-variable equation in $x$ (or $y$). If that equation forces $x^2$ (or a sum of squares) to equal a negative number, real solutions are impossible and the original assumption fails. The contradiction is clean because non-negativity of squares over $\mathbb{R}$ is an unassailable fact, so the proof needs no further machinery.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the question asks to prove non-existence, so a direct construction won't work — contradiction or exhaustion is needed.
  2. Rule out graphical or hand-wave arguments: the mark scheme will demand algebra, and the two conditions (curve + auxiliary) form a solvable system.
  3. Commit to substitution because it eliminates one variable cheaply and turns 'no point exists' into 'this equation has no real root', a standard A-level check.
  4. Bridge: take the auxiliary relation (here $y=x$) and substitute into the curve equation to get a polynomial in $x$ alone.
  5. Inside the move, simplify carefully, isolate $x^2$ (or apply discriminant if it's a quadratic in $x$ with mixed terms), and explicitly cite $x^2 \ge 0$ or $\Delta < 0$ as the contradiction.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the auxiliary relation forced by the side-condition (e.g. denominator $=0$ gives $y=x$).
  2. Substitute this relation into the curve equation to eliminate one variable.
  3. Simplify to a single-variable equation, typically of the form $ax^2 + b = 0$ or a quadratic.
  4. Rearrange to isolate $x^2$ or compute the discriminant.
  5. Show no real solution exists (either $x^2 = $ negative, or $\Delta < 0$).
  6. State the conclusion: no point on the curve meets the side-condition, hence the required property holds.

Worked Micro-Example

Show $C: x^2 + 4xy - 2y^2 + 18 = 0$ has no vertical tangent. The vertical-tangent condition gives $y=x$. Substituting: $x^2 + 4x^2 - 2x^2 + 18 = 3x^2 + 18 = 0$, so $x^2 = -6$. Since $x^2 \ge 0$ for all real $x$, no real $x$ satisfies this, so no such point on $C$ exists. $\blacksquare$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Proving no horizontal tangent on an implicit curve
Set the numerator of $\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x = 0$, derive a relation between $x$ and $y$, substitute into the curve, and show no real solution.
Proving a line $y = mx + c$ does not meet a curve
Substitute the line into the curve to get a quadratic in $x$, then show its discriminant is negative.
Proving two curves do not intersect
Equate or substitute one equation into the other and show the resulting equation has no real roots (often via discriminant or sum-of-squares argument).
Proving an equation has no real solution in a Further Pure context
Reduce to $f(x)^2 + k = 0$ with $k>0$ and cite non-negativity of squares.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the problem as requiring a graphical sketch or appeal to 'shape of curve' rather than algebraic proof.
  • Believing that finding $\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x$ is undefined somewhere automatically proves a vertical tangent exists, without checking that such a point lies on the curve.
  • Confusing 'no real solution' with 'no solution', and writing about complex roots as if they were admissible points on $C$.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips in substitution, e.g. writing $4x \cdot x = 4x$ instead of $4x^2$, or mishandling the sign of $-2y^2$.
  • Failing to state the reason explicitly ($x^2 \ge 0$ for real $x$, or $\Delta < 0$) — examiners require this justification.
  • Stopping at $x^2 = -6$ without drawing the conclusion about non-existence on $C$.
  • Forgetting to also recover $y$ (when relevant) or to check that the auxiliary condition was correctly derived before substituting.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure (implicit differentiation, tangents, simultaneous equations, discriminant) and standard proof techniques; no tools beyond non-negativity of real squares and quadratic theory are required.
