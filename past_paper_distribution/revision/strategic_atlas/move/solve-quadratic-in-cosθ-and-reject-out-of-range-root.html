Trigonometry

Solve quadratic in cosθ and reject out-of-range root

Family: Validity Filtering Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation has been reduced (via identity substitution such as sin²θ = 1 − cos²θ) to a quadratic in cosθ, and the quadratic formula or factorisation yields two candidate values. Cues: presence of cos²θ and cosθ terms, surd or decimal roots emerging from the discriminant, or one root visibly exceeding 1 in magnitude. Whenever u = cosθ (or sinθ) is used as a substitution, the range constraint |u| ≤ 1 must be checked before solving for θ.

Motivation — Why this works

A quadratic in u treats u as a free real variable, but cosθ is bounded in [−1, 1]. Any root with |u| > 1 corresponds to no real angle, since arccos is undefined outside this interval. Filtering preserves the equivalence between the original trig equation and the algebraic surrogate. Skipping this check produces phantom solutions or calculator errors when arccos is applied.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the equation has reduced to a quadratic in cosθ; I'll let u = cosθ and apply the quadratic formula.
  2. Once I have two candidate u-values, I rule out any with |u| > 1 because cosθ cannot exceed 1 in magnitude — no real θ exists.
  3. I commit to the surviving root(s), noting that negative values of cosθ are perfectly valid since cosθ ∈ [−1, 1].
  4. The sign of the accepted root tells me which quadrants contain the solutions, guiding the next step (principal value plus symmetry).
  5. I keep the surd form for exactness but compute a decimal to check the range condition reliably.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Let u = cosθ and write the quadratic au² + bu + c = 0.
  2. Apply the quadratic formula to get u = (−b ± √(b²−4ac))/(2a).
  3. Compute decimal approximations of both roots.
  4. Reject any root with |u| > 1; record reasoning briefly.
  5. Retain surviving root(s) and note the sign to anticipate the relevant quadrants for θ.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $4\cos^2\theta - 8\cos\theta + 1 = 0$. Quadratic formula: $\cos\theta = \dfrac{8 \pm \sqrt{80}}{8} = 1 \pm \dfrac{\sqrt{5}}{2}$. Then $1 + \tfrac{\sqrt5}{2} \approx 2.118$ — reject (out of $[-1,1]$); $1 - \tfrac{\sqrt5}{2} \approx -0.118$ — accept. Since $\cos\theta < 0$, $\theta$ lies in Q2 and Q3.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic in sinθ after using cos²θ = 1 − sin²θ
Same filter |u| ≤ 1; reject roots outside [−1, 1] before applying arcsin.
Quadratic in tanθ after dividing by cos²θ or using sec²θ = 1 + tan²θ
No rejection needed: tanθ has range ℝ, so both algebraic roots correspond to real angles.
Quadratic in secθ or cosecθ
Reject roots with |u| < 1, since |secθ|, |cosecθ| ≥ 1.
Disguised quadratic e.g. in cos2θ or cos(θ/2)
Apply the same |u| ≤ 1 filter to the compound argument before unwinding to θ over the required interval.

Common Misreadings

  • Rejecting the negative root because of an assumption that cosθ must be positive — negative values in [−1, 0] are perfectly valid.
  • Treating the quadratic as if u were unrestricted and proceeding to arccos a value > 1.
  • Confusing the cosθ filter with the secθ filter (opposite inequality).

Pitfalls

  • Applying arccos to ≈2.118 and obtaining a calculator 'Math Error' or complex value, then forcing an answer anyway.
  • Rejecting both roots through a flipped inequality and concluding 'no solutions'.
  • Dropping the surd form too early and losing exactness in a 'give exact answers' question.
  • Forgetting to retain the sign information needed to locate quadrants in the next step.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P2/P3 trig-equation technique: quadratic substitution combined with the range of cosθ/sinθ, well within the Pure Mathematics syllabus.
