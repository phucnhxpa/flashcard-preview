Algebra & Functions

Form new quadratic from sum and product of transformed roots

Family: New Equation Construction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question asks to 'form/find a quadratic equation whose roots are ...' where the new roots are expressions in the original roots α, β (e.g. 2α/β and 2β/α, α², β², α+1/β, etc.). Cue phrases include 'find an equation with integer coefficients whose roots are', 'show that the roots of ... satisfy', or any Further Pure 1 part (b) following a part (a) that established α+β and αβ. The demand is the equation itself, not the numerical roots.

Motivation — Why this works

Any monic quadratic with roots R₁, R₂ can be written x²−(R₁+R₂)x+R₁R₂ = 0, so only two scalars — the new sum S and new product P — are needed. Because R₁ and R₂ are symmetric functions of α and β, S and P collapse to expressions in α+β and αβ via Newton-style identities, which were already computed from the original equation by Vieta. This avoids solving for α, β individually (often complex) and keeps everything inside the symmetric-polynomial framework that FP1 is built on.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Student notices the new roots R₁, R₂ are symmetric in α and β, so the new equation's coefficients must be expressible via α+β and αβ.
  2. They rule out finding α, β explicitly from the original quadratic — too messy, and pointless when symmetric combinations suffice.
  3. They commit to the template x²−Sx+P = 0 and resolve to compute S = R₁+R₂ and P = R₁R₂ separately.
  4. First concrete operation: write down R₁ and R₂, then evaluate the easier of {S, P} (often the product, where factors cancel) to build momentum.
  5. Tactical decisions: choose whether to clear denominators inside S and P first (cleaner) or at the end (when assembling the final equation with integer coefficients).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Read off α+β and αβ from the original quadratic via Vieta.
  2. Define the new roots R₁, R₂ in terms of α, β.
  3. Compute P = R₁R₂, simplifying any cancellations of α, β.
  4. Compute S = R₁+R₂, combining over a common denominator and rewriting in terms of α+β and αβ (using identities such as α²+β² = (α+β)²−2αβ).
  5. Substitute the known values of α+β and αβ to get numerical S and P.
  6. Write x²−Sx+P = 0; if S or P is fractional, multiply through by the common denominator to obtain integer coefficients.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $x^2-5x+3=0$ with roots $\alpha,\beta$, so $\alpha+\beta=5$, $\alpha\beta=3$. Form the equation with roots $2\alpha/\beta$ and $2\beta/\alpha$. Product $=\frac{2\alpha}{\beta}\cdot\frac{2\beta}{\alpha}=4$. Sum $=\frac{2\alpha^2+2\beta^2}{\alpha\beta}=\frac{2((\alpha+\beta)^2-2\alpha\beta)}{\alpha\beta}=\frac{2(25-6)}{3}=\tfrac{38}{3}$. So $x^2-\tfrac{38}{3}x+4=0$, i.e. $3x^2-38x+12=0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

New roots α², β²
S = (α+β)²−2αβ, P = (αβ)²; no fractions, so no clearing needed.
New roots α+1/β, β+1/α
Combine over common denominator αβ; S and P both reduce to (α+β) and αβ once simplified.
New roots 1/α, 1/β (reciprocal roots)
Equivalent to reversing the coefficient order of the original quadratic; S = (α+β)/(αβ), P = 1/(αβ).
New roots α+k, β+k (shifted roots)
S = (α+β)+2k, P = αβ+k(α+β)+k²; alternatively substitute y = x−k into the original.
New roots kα, kβ (scaled roots)
S = k(α+β), P = k²αβ; or substitute x = y/k into the original.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to solve the original quadratic for α and β explicitly, then substituting — error-prone, especially with surds or complex roots.
  • Treating the new roots as if they were already coefficients, e.g. writing the equation as x²+(2α/β)x+(2β/α)=0.
  • Stopping after computing S and P without actually writing the equation, losing the final marks.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error in the template: writing x²+Sx+P = 0 instead of x²−Sx+P = 0, which double-negates when S itself is negative.
  • Failing to clear fractions to obtain integer coefficients when the question explicitly demands them.
  • Algebra slip in expanding α²+β² as (α+β)²−2αβ — dropping the factor of 2 or the minus sign.
  • Mixing up which is sum and which is product when both involve fractions with αβ in the denominator.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure 1 'roots of a quadratic' material; relies only on Vieta's formulas for quadratics and elementary symmetric-function identities — no use of resultants or higher-degree theory.
