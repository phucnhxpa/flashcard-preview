Algebra & Functions

Equate curve and line then clear denominators to form a polynomial

Family: Intersection To Polynomial Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever you must find (or count) intersection points between a rational curve such as y = a/(x−b) or y = a/x + k and a polynomial (typically a line y = mx + c). Visual cues: one equation has a variable in the denominator while the other is polynomial, so terms cannot be compared directly. Equivalent phrasings of the trigger include 'find where the line meets the curve', 'show the line is a tangent to the curve', or 'find the value of k for which the line and curve meet exactly once' — all of these need a polynomial equation in x before any further analysis (factorising, quadratic formula, discriminant) is possible.

Motivation — Why this works

At an intersection, both equations share the same (x, y), so equating the two y-expressions gives a single equation in x. Multiplying through by the denominator clears the fraction and converts the rational equation into a polynomial — almost always a quadratic when a linear curve meets a simple rational curve — which is solvable by factorising, the quadratic formula, or analysable via the discriminant b²−4ac. This is the canonical bridge from 'rational/transcendental form' to 'polynomial form', and it is essential because tools like the discriminant test, Vieta's relations, and standard factorisation all require a polynomial in standard form.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that intersection means equal y-values, so write y_curve = y_line as a single equation in x.
  2. Observe that the resulting equation is not a polynomial — there is a 1/x or 1/(x−b) term — so direct factorisation, the quadratic formula, or discriminant analysis cannot yet be applied.
  3. Rule out alternatives: substituting backwards, graphing, or differentiating-and-equating-gradients alone won't deliver the algebraic root structure the question demands.
  4. Commit to clearing the denominator by multiplying every term on both sides by that denominator; verify the value that would make the denominator zero is excluded from the domain, so no information is lost.
  5. Expand the resulting product of linears (or polynomial × linear) carefully and collect all terms on one side to reach standard form Ax² + Bx + C = 0.
  6. Identify coefficients A, B, C explicitly (especially when a parameter like k is involved) so they are ready for the next step — solving, applying the discriminant, or invoking sum/product of roots.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Set the two y-expressions equal: e.g. a/(x−b) = mx + c (or a/x + k = mx + c).
  2. Multiply EVERY term on both sides by the denominator (x−b) or x — including any constant term such as k.
  3. Expand all brackets fully, watching signs carefully.
  4. Move all terms to one side to obtain Ax² + Bx + C = 0 in standard descending-power form.
  5. Read off A, B, C (which may involve a parameter), ready for factorising, quadratic formula, or discriminant analysis.
  6. Check that any value excluded by the original domain (e.g. x = b or x = 0) is not a spurious root.

Worked Micro-Example

Find intersections of $y = \tfrac{6}{x-2}$ and $y = 2x+7$. Equate: $\tfrac{6}{x-2} = 2x+7$. Multiply by $(x-2)$: $6 = (2x+7)(x-2) = 2x^2+3x-14$. Rearrange: $2x^2+3x-20 = 0$, which factorises as $(2x-5)(x+4)=0$, giving $x = \tfrac{5}{2}$ or $x=-4$ (both $\ne 2$, so both valid).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Tangency / number-of-intersections problems with a parameter k
After clearing to Ax² + Bx + C = 0, apply the discriminant: b²−4ac = 0 for tangent, > 0 for two intersections, < 0 for none. Coefficients will involve k.
Curve of the form y = a/(x−b)² meeting a line
Multiplying by (x−b)² produces a cubic rather than a quadratic; solve by factor theorem or inspection.
Two rational curves intersecting
Multiply through by the product of both denominators; the resulting polynomial may be higher-degree but the strategy is identical.
Line meeting a parabola y² = 4ax
Substitute the line into the curve equation rather than equate y's; same end-goal of reducing to a polynomial in one variable.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to manipulate the rational equation in place (cross-multiplying only one side, or moving the 1/x to the other side) instead of clearing the denominator outright — leads to circular algebra.
  • On tangency problems, differentiating the curve and equating gradients to the line's slope without also using the 'lies on both curves' condition — gives one equation in two unknowns.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to multiply EVERY term by the denominator — particularly a constant like k or c sitting alone on one side.
  • Sign errors when moving a −2x² (or similar) across the equals sign to reach standard form.
  • Leaving the equation as 'number = quadratic' and trying to factorise without first moving everything to one side.
  • Failing to check whether the cleared denominator's forbidden value (x = b or x = 0) appears as a spurious root after multiplication.
  • When a parameter is present, mis-collecting the x-coefficient (e.g. writing B = k − 10 as 10 − k) and then mis-signing the discriminant.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1–P4): equating two curves, solving quadratics, and applying the discriminant b²−4ac are core skills. The discriminant condition is in the formula booklet implicitly via the quadratic formula. Avoid generalising to higher-degree resultants or systems requiring elimination theory — those are out of scope.
