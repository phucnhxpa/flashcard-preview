Strategic Atlas / Integration / Exponential Simplification
Integration

Square e^{x/2} via index law to collapse to e^x

Family: Exponential Simplification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a volume-of-revolution integrand of the form k·g(x)·e^{x/2} (or any e^{ax/2}-style fractional exponent) must be squared because V = π∫y² dx. Also fires when [f(x)]² appears in mean-square value or arc-length-style setups containing e^{x/n}. The cue is a fractional exponent inside an expression that the formula forces you to square. Recognise it before expanding brackets.

Motivation — Why this works

By the index law e^{x/2}·e^{x/2} = e^{x/2 + x/2} = e^x, squaring eliminates the awkward fractional exponent and produces e^x, whose derivative and antiderivative are itself. This makes the resulting polynomial × e^x integral solvable by repeated integration by parts without carrying chain-rule scale factors. Keeping the fractional exponent forces every IBP step to multiply by 2, inflating arithmetic and inviting sign errors. The simplification also typically matches the cleaner form the question is steering towards.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the volume formula demands [f(x)]², and f contains e^{x/2}, a fractional exponent.
  2. Rule out expanding (4−x)e^{x/2} first then squaring — that doubles the work and obscures the e^{x/2}·e^{x/2} pairing.
  3. Commit to squaring factor-by-factor using (abc)² = a²b²c², because each factor simplifies independently.
  4. First concrete operation: square the constant (1/4)² = 1/16, square the polynomial (4−x)², and apply e^{x/2}·e^{x/2} = e^x.
  5. Pull the constant 1/16 outside the integral so the integrand is the clean form (4−x)²e^x, ready for IBP.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write V = π∫₀⁴ [(1/4)(4−x)e^{x/2}]² dx.
  2. Apply (abc)² = a²b²c² to split: (1/4)² · (4−x)² · (e^{x/2})².
  3. Compute (1/4)² = 1/16, leave (4−x)² as is, and use the index law (e^{x/2})² = e^{x/2+x/2} = e^x.
  4. Take the constant outside: V = (π/16)∫₀⁴ (4−x)² e^x dx.
  5. Identify K = π/16 and proceed to integrate (4−x)²e^x by parts.

Worked Micro-Example

Find V when y = \tfrac{1}{4}(4-x)e^{x/2} is rotated about the x-axis from 0 to 4.\\ V = \pi\int_0^4 \tfrac{1}{16}(4-x)^2 (e^{x/2})^2\,dx = \tfrac{\pi}{16}\int_0^4 (4-x)^2 e^{x}\,dx, \\ since (e^{x/2})^2 = e^{x/2+x/2} = e^{x}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Integrand contains e^{x/3} and must be squared
Index law gives (e^{x/3})² = e^{2x/3}; no full collapse, but the exponent is unified to a single term.
Integrand contains √x = x^{1/2}
Squaring yields x, turning a surd integral into a polynomial integral.
Mean-square value of f(x) = e^{-x/2}g(x) over [a,b]
Squaring gives e^{-x}g(x)², again collapsing to integer-exponent exponential.
f(x) = e^{x/2} + e^{-x/2} (cosh-like)
Squaring gives e^x + 2 + e^{-x}; cross term simplifies to a constant via e^{x/2}·e^{-x/2}=1.

Common Misreadings

  • Forgetting to square the constant factor 1/4, leading to K = π instead of π/16.
  • Writing (e^{x/2})² = e^{x²/4}, confusing squaring the function with squaring the exponent.
  • Squaring (4−x)e^{x/2} as (4−x)²e^{x/2} (forgetting to square the exponential at all).
  • Treating e^{x/2}·e^{x/2} as e^{x/4} by multiplying exponents instead of adding.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to square every factor — constant, polynomial, and exponential — independently.
  • Expanding (4−x)² prematurely before pulling out 1/16, cluttering the integrand for IBP.
  • Losing the π from the volume formula when factoring out 1/16.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the index law a^m·a^n = a^{m+n} and the standard volume-of-revolution formula V = π∫y²dx, both core Edexcel IAL Pure content; the subsequent integration by parts on (4−x)²e^x is also within syllabus.
