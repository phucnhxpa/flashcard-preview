Substitute linear equation into quadratic to reduce to one-variable quadratic

Family: Simultaneous Substitution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A simultaneous system contains one linear equation and one quadratic (or higher-degree) equation in two unknowns. Typical phrasings: 'solve simultaneously', 'find the exact values of x and y', or a follow-up part that introduces a linear constraint like y = x + k or x + y = c alongside an earlier quadratic relation. The linear equation is often already solved for one variable, or trivially rearrangeable.

Motivation — Why this works

Substituting the linear expression eliminates one variable, collapsing the system into a single-variable quadratic that can be solved by factorising, completing the square, or the quadratic formula. This works because the linear equation defines an exact dependence between the variables, so any solution of the system must lie on that line — restricting the quadratic to that line gives a 1D equation. Substitution is preferred over elimination by subtraction (which preserves two unknowns) and over substituting the quadratic into the linear (which produces square roots and case-splits).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the system has two equations in two unknowns, with one linear and one of degree two.
  2. Rule out elimination by subtraction (won't reduce variable count when degrees differ) and rule out solving the quadratic for one variable first (introduces ± and is messier).
  3. Commit to substitution because the linear equation is already (or easily) solved for one variable — this guarantees a clean single-variable quadratic.
  4. Pick the variable that the linear equation gives explicitly, and prepare to replace every occurrence of it in the quadratic with the linear expression in brackets.
  5. After substituting, expand carefully (especially squared brackets and negative coefficients), collect like terms to standard form ax² + bx + c = 0, then solve and back-substitute into the linear equation to recover the partner values.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rearrange the linear equation so one variable is the subject, e.g. q = p + 2.
  2. Substitute this expression for that variable into the quadratic equation, using brackets around the substituted expression.
  3. Expand all brackets carefully — in particular (p + 2)² = p² + 4p + 4 and −p(p + 2) = −p² − 2p.
  4. Collect like terms and rearrange to the standard form ax² + bx + c = 0.
  5. Solve the quadratic by factorising, completing the square, or the quadratic formula (depending on whether 'exact' answers are demanded).
  6. Back-substitute each root into the linear equation to obtain the corresponding second-variable value, and present solutions as ordered pairs.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $p^2 + q^2 - pq = 8$ together with $q = p + 2$. Substitute: $p^2 + (p+2)^2 - p(p+2) = 8$. Expand: $p^2 + p^2 + 4p + 4 - p^2 - 2p = 8$, giving $p^2 + 2p - 4 = 0$. Quadratic formula: $p = -1 \pm \sqrt{5}$, hence $q = 1 \pm \sqrt{5}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Linear equation given as ax + by = c (not solved for a variable)
First rearrange to make the variable with coefficient 1 (or smallest coefficient) the subject to minimise fractions before substituting.
Quadratic curve meets a straight line in coordinate geometry (find intersection points)
Substitute y = mx + c into the curve equation; the discriminant of the resulting quadratic also classifies tangency (Δ = 0), secant (Δ > 0), or no intersection (Δ < 0).
Circle equation with a linear chord, e.g. (x−a)² + (y−b)² = r² and y = mx + c
Substitute y from the line into the circle, expand, and solve the resulting quadratic in x.
Both equations are quadratic
Substitution still works but may yield a quartic; look for symmetry (use s = p + q, d = p − q) or subtract the two quadratics first to obtain a linear or factorable relation, then substitute.
Three unknowns with one linear and two further equations (FP-style)
Use the linear equation to eliminate one variable, reducing to a 2-variable system, then iterate the substitution strategy.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the system as requiring elimination by adding/subtracting equations, which fails to reduce variable count when one equation is quadratic.
  • Substituting the linear expression into itself or only into part of the quadratic (e.g. forgetting the cross term −pq).
  • Reading 'exact value' as a hint to factorise, then giving up when factorisation fails — instead it signals the quadratic formula or completing the square.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when expanding −p(p + 2) = −p² − 2p; students sometimes write −p² + 2p.
  • Failing to expand (p + 2)² fully — dropping the +4 constant or the cross term 4p.
  • Forgetting to substitute into every occurrence of the variable, especially in the cross term.
  • Stopping after finding p and not back-substituting to find q (or vice versa) — the question asks for the full solution pair.
  • Pairing the roots incorrectly when back-substituting (e.g. matching the larger p with the smaller q).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure Maths content (P1/P2): solving simultaneous equations where one is linear and one is quadratic, including line-curve and line-circle intersections. The resulting quadratic is solved by methods within the IAL Pure syllabus (factorising, completing the square, quadratic formula).
