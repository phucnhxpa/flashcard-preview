Expand squared surd binomial via (a−b)² identity

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The expression is a squared binomial whose terms involve surds or reciprocal surds, e.g. (√r − 1/√r)², (√a + √b)², or (x^{1/2} − x^{−1/2})². The outer square together with surd/reciprocal-surd inner terms signals that the perfect-square identity will collapse the cross-term cleanly. Often appears inside integration setup, differentiation prep, or surd-simplification questions where a 'rational' form is needed before the next step.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity (a − b)² = a² − 2ab + b² is preferable to FOIL because it makes the cross-term −2ab visible as a single object. When a and b are reciprocal surds, ab = 1, so −2ab = −2 is a clean integer and all surds disappear. The squared terms a² = r and b² = 1/r are also rational, leaving a tidy expression r − 2 + 1/r ready for integration, differentiation, or further manipulation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the whole expression is (·)², so a perfect-square expansion is the natural tool.
  2. Rule out converting to a single fraction first or distributing term-by-term — both add algebra without simplifying surds.
  3. Commit because the inner terms √r and 1/√r are mutual reciprocals, guaranteeing the cross-term 2ab collapses to a constant.
  4. Bridge: label a = √r, b = 1/√r and write down the identity template a² − 2ab + b² before substituting.
  5. Tactically track the sign: the binomial has a minus, so the middle term is −2ab, not +2ab.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify a and b in the binomial; here a = √r, b = 1/√r.
  2. Apply (a − b)² = a² − 2ab + b².
  3. Compute a² = (√r)² = r.
  4. Compute 2ab = 2 · √r · (1/√r) = 2.
  5. Compute b² = (1/√r)² = 1/r.
  6. Assemble with correct sign: r − 2 + 1/r.

Worked Micro-Example

Simplify $\left(\sqrt{r} - \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{r}}\right)^2$. Let $a = \sqrt{r},\ b = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{r}}$. Then $a^2 = r$, $b^2 = \tfrac{1}{r}$, and $2ab = 2\sqrt{r}\cdot\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{r}} = 2$. Hence $\left(\sqrt{r} - \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{r}}\right)^2 = r - 2 + \tfrac{1}{r}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

(√a + √b)² in surd simplification
Cross-term becomes +2√(ab); it stays a surd unless ab is a perfect square.
(x^{1/2} + x^{−1/2})² before integration
Expands to x + 2 + 1/x; sign of the middle term flips to +2 because the binomial has a plus.
(√r − 1/√r)³ via binomial expansion
Use binomial theorem with reciprocal-surd cancellation; multiple cross-terms become integers or 1/r-type terms.
(e^{x/2} − e^{−x/2})² in hyperbolic-style problems
Same identity; cross-term −2·e^{x/2}·e^{−x/2} = −2, giving e^x − 2 + e^{−x}.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating (√r − 1/√r)² as (√r)² − (1/√r)², confusing a squared binomial with a difference of squares and dropping the cross-term.
  • Reading the expression as √(r − 1/√r)·² and trying to simplify under a single root.
  • Assuming the surds 'cancel' entirely and writing the answer as just r − 1/r.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the factor of 2 on the cross-term (writing ab instead of 2ab).
  • Sign error: using +2ab when the binomial has a minus sign.
  • Mishandling the reciprocal: writing (1/√r)² as 1/√r or as √(1/r²) instead of 1/r.
  • Leaving the answer as r − 2 + r^{−1} when the next step (e.g. integration) wants it in 1/r form, or vice versa.

A-level Scope Notes

Pure algebra of surds and indices; sits within Edexcel IAL P1 (surds, indices) and is a standard preparatory step for P2/P3 integration of expressions like ∫(√r − 1/√r)² dr.
