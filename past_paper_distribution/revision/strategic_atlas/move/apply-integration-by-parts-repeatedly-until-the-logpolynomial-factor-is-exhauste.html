Integration

Apply integration by parts repeatedly until the log/polynomial factor is exhausted

Family: Integration By Parts Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

After a first IBP on integrals like ∫(ln x)² dx or ∫x²eˣ dx, the residual integral still contains a non-elementary factor (e.g. ∫2 ln x dx) that cannot be antidifferentiated directly. The cue is that the residual has the same structural form as the original but with the troublesome factor reduced by one degree. This signals that a second (or further) round of IBP is required, not a change of method. Equivalently: 'first IBP didn't finish the job, but it made progress'.

Motivation — Why this works

Each IBP round differentiates the awkward factor — (ln x)ⁿ becomes n(ln x)ⁿ⁻¹/x, or xⁿ becomes nxⁿ⁻¹ — strictly reducing its degree while keeping the rest of the integrand manageable. Because the degree is a non-negative integer, the process must terminate after finitely many rounds with a residual that is elementary (constant or pure exponential). Recognising this in advance reassures the student that the method is correct even when the first round yields another awkward-looking integral.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the residual integral after the first IBP still has a logarithmic (or polynomial) factor of one lower degree, not a standard antiderivative.
  2. I rule out switching method (substitution, partial fractions) — the structure is the same, so a different technique would not help.
  3. I commit to a second IBP because each round provably reduces the degree of the awkward factor by one, guaranteeing termination.
  4. I set up the second IBP on the residual using the same 'differentiate the log/polynomial, integrate the rest' choice as before.
  5. After completing the inner IBP I substitute the result back into the outer expression, watching the sign of the bracketed term carefully and finally adding +C.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the residual integral that still requires IBP (e.g. ∫2 ln x dx after the first round on ∫(ln x)² dx).
  2. Choose u and dv/dx for the second IBP using the same priority (LIATE): u = ln x, dv/dx = 2, so du/dx = 1/x, v = 2x.
  3. Apply the IBP formula: ∫2 ln x dx = 2x ln x − ∫2x·(1/x) dx = 2x ln x − 2x.
  4. Substitute this expression back into the outer result, keeping the leading minus sign in front of the bracket.
  5. Expand the bracket carefully and add +C: x(ln x)² − 2x ln x + 2x + C.

Worked Micro-Example

Compute $\int (\ln x)^2\,dx$. First IBP ($u=(\ln x)^2$, $dv=dx$): $x(\ln x)^2 - \int 2\ln x\,dx$. Second IBP on $\int 2\ln x\,dx$ ($u=\ln x$, $dv=2\,dx$): $2x\ln x - \int 2\,dx = 2x\ln x - 2x$. Substituting back: $\int(\ln x)^2\,dx = x(\ln x)^2 - 2x\ln x + 2x + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

∫xⁿeˣ dx for n = 2, 3, ...
Each IBP differentiates the polynomial factor, reducing its degree by one; n rounds are needed before the residual becomes ∫(constant)·eˣ dx.
∫xⁿ sin x dx or ∫xⁿ cos x dx
Same iterated structure, but the trig factor cycles sin → −cos → −sin; track signs carefully across rounds.
∫eˣ sin x dx (cyclic IBP)
Two rounds of IBP reproduce the original integral; rearrange algebraically to solve for it instead of expecting termination by degree reduction.
∫x(ln x)² dx
First IBP with dv = x dx leaves a residual ∫x ln x dx, which itself needs another IBP — same iterated pattern with an extra polynomial factor.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the residual integral as 'unsolvable' after the first IBP and abandoning the method.
  • Switching to substitution or another technique when the structure clearly indicates a second IBP.
  • Trying to differentiate the wrong factor in the second round (e.g. choosing u = 2 instead of u = ln x), which loops back to the original difficulty.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when substituting the inner result back: the bracket (2x ln x − 2x) is subtracted, so it becomes −2x ln x + 2x.
  • Forgetting the factor of 2 (or other constants) carried through from the first IBP into the second.
  • Omitting the constant of integration +C at the final step.
  • Miscomputing v in the second IBP (e.g. taking v = x instead of v = 2x when dv/dx = 2).

A-level Scope Notes

Iterated integration by parts on ∫(ln x)ⁿ dx, ∫xⁿeˣ dx, and ∫xⁿ sin x / cos x dx is standard Edexcel IAL Pure (P3/P4) material; cyclic IBP for ∫eˣ sin x dx also appears. Tabular/DI shortcuts are not part of the IAL syllabus and should not be used in working.
