Differentiation

Apply chain rule to differentiate composite function as outer×inner derivative

Family: Chain Rule Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever you see a function-of-a-function: a power, root, exponential, logarithm, or trig function applied to a non-trivial inner expression. Visual cues include brackets raised to a power like (3x−2)^6 or (1+2ln y)^{1/2}, roots of expressions √(f(x)), powers of trig functions like sec^4 x or sin^n x, and compositions like e^{f(x)} or ln(f(x)). Equivalently phrased: 'the variable is buried inside another function', or 'there is an inner function whose derivative is not 1'. Distinguish from product/quotient rule cases — here there is only ONE function nested inside another, not two functions multiplied or divided.

Motivation — Why this works

The chain rule expresses dy/dx for y = f(g(x)) as f'(g(x))·g'(x), factorising the derivative of a composite into (derivative of outer)·(derivative of inner). This avoids catastrophic alternatives like binomial expansion of (3x−2)^6 followed by term-by-term differentiation, and it preserves the compact factored form that downstream parts (integration, evaluation, factorisation) almost always require. The principle is the formula booklet entry dy/dx = (dy/du)(du/dx). Choosing u to be the inner expression makes both dy/du and du/dx trivial to compute separately.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structural composition: identify an outer 'wrapper' function (power, root, exp, ln, trig) applied to an inner expression that is not just x.
  2. Rule out product rule (no two distinct functions multiplied) and quotient rule (no genuine ratio); rule out expanding brackets as tedious and error-prone for high powers like ^6.
  3. Commit to chain rule because the composition is clean and both pieces — outer u^n (or √u, e^u, ln u) and inner u = g(x) — differentiate in one line each.
  4. Bridge to operation: explicitly write u = (inner expression), so y becomes a simple function of u; this externalises the chain and prevents forgetting the inner derivative.
  5. Tactical decision: compute du/dx separately and BEFORE multiplying — this is where the most common error (dropping the inner derivative factor) occurs.
  6. Multiply dy/du · du/dx, then back-substitute u = g(x), and finally simplify, keeping factored form when downstream work (integration, factorisation) will exploit it.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify outer function F and inner function u = g(x) so that y = F(u).
  2. Compute dy/du by differentiating F treating u as the variable (e.g. F = u^n ⟹ dy/du = n u^{n−1}; F = √u ⟹ (1/2)u^{−1/2}; F = sec u ⟹ sec u tan u).
  3. Compute du/dx by differentiating the inner expression g(x).
  4. Multiply: dy/dx = (dy/du)·(du/dx).
  5. Substitute u = g(x) back in and simplify, retaining factored form if a 'hence' part follows.

Worked Micro-Example

Differentiate $y=(3x-2)^6$. Let $u=3x-2$, so $y=u^6$. Then $\tfrac{dy}{du}=6u^5$ and $\tfrac{du}{dx}=3$. Hence $\tfrac{dy}{dx}=6u^5\cdot 3=18(3x-2)^5$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Root/fractional power outer, e.g. $(1+2\ln y)^{1/2}$
Outer derivative is $\tfrac{1}{2}u^{-1/2}$; inner derivative of $\ln y$ contributes $1/y$, giving $\tfrac{1}{y\sqrt{1+2\ln y}}$.
Power of a single trig function, e.g. $\sec^n x$ or $\sin^n x$
Treat as $u^n$ with $u=\sec x$ (or $\sin x$); inner derivative is $\sec x \tan x$ (or $\cos x$), so $\tfrac{d}{dx}\sec^n x = n\sec^n x\tan x$.
Exponential of a function, $y=e^{f(x)}$
dy/dx = f'(x)·e^{f(x)} — same logic, exponential outer.
Logarithm of a function, $y=\ln(f(x))$
dy/dx = f'(x)/f(x).
Doubly nested composition, e.g. $(\ln(x^2+1))^3$ or $\sin(e^x)$
Apply chain rule twice: differentiate outermost, then inner composite, multiplying all three derivative factors.

Common Misreadings

  • Mistaking a composite for a product (e.g. treating (3x−2)^6 as needing the product rule because 'there are two things').
  • Trying to expand brackets via the binomial theorem instead of recognising the composition.
  • Confusing $\sec^4 x$ (power of sec) with sec(4x) (sec of a multiple) — different inner functions.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the inner derivative ×g'(x) — the single most common chain rule error (e.g. writing 6(3x−2)^5 without the ×3).
  • Reducing the power incorrectly: writing (3x−2)^4 instead of (3x−2)^5 after differentiating ^6.
  • Writing d/dy[ln y] = 1 instead of 1/y, or forgetting the factor 2 from d/dy[2 ln y].
  • On $\sec^n x$, forgetting that the inner derivative of sec x is sec x tan x (not just tan x), which produces an extra sec x factor.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 scope; uses the formula-booklet identity dy/dx = (dy/du)(du/dx). Standard derivatives (e^x, ln x, sin x, cos x, sec x, tan x) are also booklet entries. Avoid invoking implicit-function or multivariable chain rule generalisations — keep to single-variable composition.
