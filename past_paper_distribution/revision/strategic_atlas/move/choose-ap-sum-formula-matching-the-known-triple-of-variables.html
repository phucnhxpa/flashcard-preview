Sequences & Series

Choose AP sum formula matching the known triple of variables

Family: Formula Selection Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question asks for an arithmetic series sum S_n (or asks you to find one of a, d, n, l, S_n given the others) and you must decide between the two booklet forms: S_n = n/2(2a + (n-1)d) and S_n = n/2(a + l). The cue is to inventory which three of {a, d, n, l, S_n} are known. If a and l are both in hand (endpoints given or already computed), the (a + l) form is signalled; if d is known but l is not, the (2a + (n-1)d) form is signalled. Phrasings include 'first and last term given', 'sum of first n terms', or 'd and S_n given, find a'.

Motivation — Why this works

Both AP sum formulas are equivalent (since l = a + (n-1)d), but each foregrounds a different subset of variables. Selecting the form whose variables are already known turns the problem into a single substitution or a single linear equation in one unknown — no auxiliary computation of l or d is needed. This minimises arithmetic steps, reduces error risk, and avoids circular reasoning (e.g. needing a to find l, then using l to find a). The underlying identity is simply that the average of an AP is (a + l)/2, multiplied by n.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question concerns an AP sum: list everything known — typically three of {a, d, n, l, S_n}.
  2. Recall both formula options from the booklet: S_n = n/2(2a + (n-1)d) and S_n = n/2(a + l).
  3. Rule out the form that needs a quantity you don't have: e.g. if l is unknown and would itself require a to compute, the (a + l) form is circular.
  4. Commit to the form whose three required inputs are exactly the three knowns, leaving at most one unknown to solve for.
  5. Substitute the three known values directly; if an unknown remains (a, d, or n), set up and solve the resulting linear (or, for n, quadratic) equation.
  6. Sanity-check by ensuring the term you substituted as 'last term' really is the nth term of the AP in question (not a related GP or a shifted index).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Inventory knowns: write down values of a, d, n, l, S_n that are given or already derived.
  2. If {a, l, n} known → use S_n = n/2(a + l); compute directly.
  3. If {a, d, n} known and S_n sought → use S_n = n/2(2a + (n-1)d); compute directly.
  4. If S_n, n, d known and a sought → use S_n = n/2(2a + (n-1)d), substitute and solve linearly for a.
  5. If S_n, n, a known and d sought → same formula, solve linearly for d.
  6. Verify the answer by substituting back or by checking units/magnitude.

Worked Micro-Example

Given a = 100, A_{20} = 62 (so l = 62), find S_{20}. Both endpoints known ⇒ use S_n = n/2(a + l). Then S_{20} = 20/2 · (100 + 62) = 10 × 162 = 1620.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Only a, d, n known (no last term)
Use S_n = n/2(2a + (n-1)d) — forced choice.
S_n, n, d known; a is the unknown
Use S_n = n/2(2a + (n-1)d) and solve the resulting linear equation in a.
Geometric series with analogous choice
Pick between S_n = a(r^n − 1)/(r − 1) and S_n = (a − lr)/(1 − r) depending on whether r^n or the last term l is more accessible.
Last term not given but easily computed from a and d
Either compute l = a + (n-1)d first and use the (a + l) form, or skip straight to the (2a + (n-1)d) form — choose whichever has cleaner arithmetic.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating a 'sum' question as a 'term' question — using u_n = a + (n-1)d when S_n is what's given.
  • Assuming the last term equals a value from a related sequence (e.g. a paired GP term) rather than the AP's own nth term.
  • Reading 'sum of first 10 terms equals 360' as 'the 10th term is 360'.

Pitfalls

  • Off-by-one error: writing nd instead of (n-1)d in the bracket.
  • Using n/2 with the wrong n (e.g. n = 11 for a 12-week sum).
  • Confusing l with the (n+1)th term or with a value from a different sequence in the same question.
  • Forgetting that S_n is fixed for a specific n — don't treat it as a general expression.

A-level Scope Notes

Both formulas appear in the Edexcel IAL formula booklet (Pure Mathematics, Arithmetic Series). This move is core P1/P2 content and recurs in modelling questions. Stay within finite arithmetic series; do not invoke continuous analogues or generating-function arguments.
