Sequences & Series

Fencepost-count calendar years to fix the term index

Family: Index Alignment Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a worded sequence problem anchors the initial value to a calendar event ('start of 2018', 'in the first year', 'on 1 January 2020') and asks for the value at a later calendar event. Cue phrases include 'at the start of', 'at the beginning of year N', 'after k years', or 'in the year 20XX'. The risk is that the year gap (e.g. 2022 − 2018 = 4) can be confused with either the term index n or the exponent n − 1. Applies to geometric growth/decay (interest, depreciation, population) and arithmetic models alike.

Motivation — Why this works

In u_n = ar^{n-1}, the exponent counts the number of growth periods that have elapsed, while the index n counts the term itself, including the initial one. The starting value experiences zero growth periods, so it sits at n = 1 with exponent 0. Mapping each calendar marker to its term index — or equivalently counting 'gaps' between markers — converts ambiguous English into an unambiguous exponent. Both interpretations give the same exponent, which is why fence-posting (posts vs gaps) is the cleanest mental model.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question pins the initial value to a calendar moment and asks for the value at another calendar moment — this is an index-alignment problem, not a pure computation.
  2. Rule out the temptation to subtract years and plug straight into the exponent without checking whether n or n − 1 is wanted.
  3. Commit to listing the calendar-to-index correspondence explicitly, because the cost (one line of writing) is tiny compared to the cost of an off-by-one error.
  4. Bridge: write 'start of 2018 → u₁' and increment by one for each subsequent January until the target year is reached.
  5. Tactical check: cross-validate by counting growth periods (target year − start year) and confirm this equals n − 1 before substituting into ar^{n-1}.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the calendar marker attached to the initial value and assign it to u₁ (or u₀ if the question defines it that way).
  2. List the correspondence year-by-year: 2018 → u₁, 2019 → u₂, 2020 → u₃, 2021 → u₄, 2022 → u₅.
  3. Cross-check by computing the number of growth periods: target year − start year = 2022 − 2018 = 4.
  4. Set the exponent in ar^{n-1} equal to the number of growth periods, so exponent = 4 and n = 5.
  5. Substitute: u₅ = 50000 × (1.02)⁴.

Worked Micro-Example

A sum of £50000 is invested at the start of 2018, growing by 2% each year. Find its value at the start of 2022.\\ Map: 2018 → u_1, 2019 → u_2, 2020 → u_3, 2021 → u_4, 2022 → u_5.\\ Growth periods: 2022 - 2018 = 4, so use u_n = ar^{n-1} with n = 5.\\ u_5 = 50000 \times (1.02)^4 = £54121.61 \text{ (2 d.p.)}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Question indexes from u₀ instead of u₁ (e.g. 'let u₀ be the initial deposit')
Then 2022 corresponds to u₄, but the exponent is still 4 — exponent always equals the number of growth periods, independent of labelling.
Arithmetic sequence with annual fixed addition (e.g. salary rises by £1500 per year)
Use u_n = a + (n − 1)d with the same fence-post mapping; the coefficient of d equals the number of years elapsed.
Question asks for the total accumulated over the years (sum, not term)
Use S_n with n = number of terms in the listed correspondence; the index of the last term still comes from fence-posting.
Phrasing 'after k years' rather than 'at the start of year Y'
'After k years' means k growth periods have elapsed, so the value is ar^k = u_{k+1}; no calendar mapping needed but the same gap-vs-post logic applies.
Depreciation problem (r < 1) such as a car losing 15% of its value each year
Identical index logic; just use r = 0.85 in ar^{n-1}.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the year difference 2022 − 2018 = 4 as the term index n and then using exponent n − 1 = 3, undercounting one growth period.
  • Computing (1.02)⁵ by reasoning 'there are 5 years from 2018 to 2022 inclusive', overcounting one growth period.
  • Assuming the initial value has already undergone one year of growth, placing it at u₂ rather than u₁.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to write down the correspondence and relying on mental arithmetic, where 'gaps' and 'posts' get conflated.
  • Switching between u₀ and u₁ conventions mid-solution.
  • Forgetting that 'at the start of year Y' means before that year's growth, while 'at the end of year Y' means after — a one-period shift.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard for Edexcel IAL P1/P2 geometric and arithmetic sequence applications (compound interest, depreciation, savings models); no content beyond ar^{n-1} and a + (n−1)d is required.
