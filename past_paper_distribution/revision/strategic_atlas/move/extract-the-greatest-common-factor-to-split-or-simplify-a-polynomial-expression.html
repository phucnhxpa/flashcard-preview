Algebra & Functions

Extract the greatest common factor to split or simplify a polynomial expression

Family: Common Factor Extraction Frequency: 5 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever every term of an expression visibly shares a factor — typically a power of x, a numerical GCF, an exponential like e^{kx}, or a recurring product like n(n+1). Key visual cues: (i) a polynomial equation with no constant term (so x = 0 is automatically a root), (ii) several terms with the same transcendental factor after integration by parts, (iii) coefficients with an obvious numerical GCF, or (iv) an evaluated 'exact value' answer that is a difference of two terms sharing a fraction. Equivalently phrased: 'every term divisible by …', 'common factor staring at me', or 'mark scheme expects single factored form'.

Motivation — Why this works

Pulling out a common factor converts a sum into a product, which (a) immediately reveals roots via the zero-product property, (b) reduces a high-degree polynomial to lower-degree pieces solvable by standard methods, and (c) presents an 'exact' answer in the compact form Edexcel mark schemes reward. The underlying principle is the distributive law in reverse: AB + AC = A(B+C). For polynomial equations it preserves all roots (whereas dividing by the factor would discard the root that makes it zero).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Scan all terms and notice a literal factor common to every one — e.g. x in 3x³+26x²−9x, or e^{3x} in the post-IBP expression, or ¼ in ¼e²−¼.
  2. Rule out heavier machinery: the Factor Theorem with trial roots, synthetic division, or expanding a target product and comparing coefficients — these are all slower when a common factor is visible.
  3. Commit to extraction because it (a) cannot lose information if I FACTOR (not divide), and (b) splits the problem into a trivial piece plus a smaller residual problem.
  4. Decide how 'greedy' to be: extract the FULL GCF (numerical GCF × highest common power of x) so the residual bracket has the simplest possible coefficients — e.g. take 10x out of 20x³−50x²−30x, not just x.
  5. If fractional coefficients are present, also unify denominators inside the bracket (e.g. write 1/3, 2/9, 2/27 over LCD 27) so the factored form is (1/27)e^{3x}(9x²−6x+2).
  6. Apply the zero-product property (for equations) or simply present the factored expression (for 'exact value' / simplification questions), then attack the residual quadratic/cubic with standard tools.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the GCF of all terms: numerical GCF × lowest power of each shared variable/transcendental factor.
  2. Divide each term by the GCF to obtain the residual bracket; double-check signs term-by-term.
  3. Write the expression as GCF × (residual), e.g. 10x(2x²−5x−3) or (e^{3x}/27)(9x²−6x+2)+C.
  4. If solving an equation, set each factor equal to zero: GCF = 0 gives one set of roots, residual = 0 gives the rest.
  5. Solve the residual (usually a quadratic) by factoring, completing the square, or the quadratic formula.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $20x^3 - 50x^2 - 30x = 0$. Every term shares $10x$, so factor: $10x(2x^2 - 5x - 3) = 0$. The bracket factors as $(2x+1)(x-3)$, giving $x = 0,\ -\tfrac{1}{2},\ 3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Polynomial equation with no constant term, e.g. 6x⁴+3x³−9x² = 0
Factor out the highest common power of x along with the numerical GCF: 3x²(2x²+x−3) = 0, yielding a repeated root x=0.
Post-IBP integral with repeated e^{kx}, e.g. ∫x²e^{3x} dx
Factor e^{3x} from every term and unify rational coefficients over the LCD to produce the compact mark-scheme form (e^{3x}/27)(9x²−6x+2)+C.
'Exact value' definite-integral answer like ¼e²−¼
Factor the rational constant: ¼(e²−1). No algebra changes — purely a presentation step.
Sum-of-series proofs producing ¼n²(n+1)²−½n(n+1)
Factor n(n+1)/4 to reduce the residual bracket to a quadratic n²+n−2 = (n+2)(n−1), giving the four-linear-factor target form.
Repeated-IBP for ∫x²sin x dx
Group sin x and cos x terms separately, then factor each — same logic, different transcendental factors.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating a polynomial with no constant term as a 'general cubic' and reaching for the Factor Theorem — wasting time on trial roots when x is an obvious factor.
  • Believing that leaving an answer as a sum of separate terms is equally acceptable as the factored form — Edexcel often awards the final A-mark only for the simplified product.

Pitfalls

  • Dividing the equation through by the common factor instead of factoring it out — this silently discards the root x = 0 (or whichever value makes the factor zero).
  • Extracting only part of the GCF (e.g. just x rather than 10x) so the residual quadratic carries needlessly large coefficients.
  • Sign errors when dividing each term by the GCF, particularly with negative middle terms.
  • Arithmetic slips when re-expressing fractional coefficients over a common denominator (the −6x/27 term is a frequent culprit).
  • Forgetting +C after producing a tidy factored indefinite-integral answer.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1–P4) and Further Pure scope: distributive law, zero-product property, factoring polynomials/quadratics, and standard sums-of-series identities (FP1 formula booklet entries for Σr, Σr²). No Galois-theoretic or general factorisation theory is invoked — students should not over-generalise to non-commutative or modular settings.
