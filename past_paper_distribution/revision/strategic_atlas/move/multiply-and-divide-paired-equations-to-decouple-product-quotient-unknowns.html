Algebra & Functions

Multiply and divide paired equations to decouple product-quotient unknowns

Family: Product Quotient Elimination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Two simultaneous equations present the same two unknowns once as a product $xy = p$ and once as a quotient $x/y = q$ (or equivalently $ae$ and $a/e$). The system is multiplicative, not linear, so standard elimination by addition/subtraction does not apply directly. Cue phrases include 'given the product and the ratio', or geometric sequence problems where $ar$ and $a/r$ both appear, or ellipse problems linking $a$ and eccentricity $e$ via $ae$ and $a/e$.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying the equations gives $(xy)(x/y) = x^2$, eliminating $y$ in one stroke; dividing gives $(xy)/(x/y) = y^2$, eliminating $x$. This exploits the reciprocal structure: the offending variable cancels because it appears with opposite exponents. The method bypasses substitution, avoids nested fractions, and produces perfect squares whose positive roots are usually the required values when the unknowns are known to be positive (lengths, eccentricities, common ratios).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the two given expressions are a product and a quotient of the same pair of unknowns — this is a multiplicative system, not a linear one.
  2. I rule out solving one equation for one variable and substituting: it works but creates compound fractions and invites arithmetic slips.
  3. I commit to multiply-and-divide because the reciprocal structure means one variable will cancel cleanly each time, leaving a single squared unknown.
  4. First concrete operation: multiply the two equations to isolate $x^2$, then divide them to isolate $y^2$.
  5. Tactical choice: take the positive square root only if the context (lengths, eccentricity, positive ratio) guarantees positivity; otherwise keep $\pm$ and use a sign argument.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the pairing: equation 1 of the form $xy = p$, equation 2 of the form $x/y = q$ (or vice versa).
  2. Multiply the two equations: $(xy)(x/y) = pq \Rightarrow x^2 = pq$.
  3. Divide the equations: $(xy)/(x/y) = p/q \Rightarrow y^2 = p/q$.
  4. Take square roots, choosing signs from the problem context (positivity, range constraints).
  5. Substitute back into either original equation to verify consistency.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $a/e = 13/2$ and $ae = 72/13$. Multiply: $a^2 = (13/2)(72/13) = 36$, so $a = 6$. Divide: $e^2 = (72/13) \div (13/2) = 144/169$, so $e = 12/13$. Check: $ae = 6 \cdot 12/13 = 72/13$. ✓

Variants — Same move, different surface

Geometric progression with given $u_2 u_4 = p$ and $u_4/u_2 = q$ (i.e. $a^2 r^4 = p$ and $r^2 = q$)
Same trick on the pair $(ar, ar^3)$ or $(a/r, ar)$: multiply to get $a^2$, divide to get $r^2$.
Ellipse parameters where $ae$ and $b^2 = a^2(1-e^2)$ are given
Solve for $a$ and $e$ from $ae = c$ together with another product/ratio relation; multiply/divide to decouple.
Two positive numbers with given product and ratio
$xy = p$, $x/y = q$ directly yields $x = \sqrt{pq}$, $y = \sqrt{p/q}$.
Logarithmic system $\log x + \log y = s$, $\log x - \log y = d$
This is the additive form of the same idea: add/subtract to decouple, then exponentiate.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the system as linear and trying to add/subtract the equations directly.
  • Taking logs of both equations to linearise — works but is unnecessary overhead at A-level.
  • Assuming substitution is the only systematic method, missing the symmetry.

Pitfalls

  • Dividing the wrong way round and obtaining $1/y^2$ instead of $y^2$.
  • Arithmetic slips when multiplying/dividing fractions (e.g. $72/13 \times 2/13$).
  • Forgetting the $\pm$ when the context does not guarantee positivity.
  • Failing to verify the final pair satisfies both original equations.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths simultaneous-equation technique and appears in geometric series (P2), conic sections (FP1/FP3 ellipse eccentricity), and product/ratio word problems.
