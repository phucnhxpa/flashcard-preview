Complex Numbers

Substitute w=u+iv and square moduli to extract Cartesian locus

Family: Cartesian Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a single complex variable satisfies a modulus equation of the form |f(w)| = k|g(w)| or |f(w)| = c, and the question asks for the locus, Cartesian equation, or to identify/sketch the curve. Cue phrases include 'find the Cartesian equation', 'show that the locus is a circle/line', or 'sketch the set of points w such that...'. Also fires when a geometric description (centre, radius, perpendicular bisector) is requested but no purely geometric shortcut is obvious.

Motivation — Why this works

Writing w = u+iv separates real and imaginary parts inside each modulus, so |a+bi|² = a²+b² turns each side into a real polynomial in u and v. Squaring is reversible because both sides of a modulus equation are non-negative, so no spurious solutions are introduced. The resulting polynomial is at most quadratic in u, v, which by Apollonius' theorem must be a circle or a line — both standard A-level loci.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation involves moduli of linear expressions in a single w, and the question wants a locus or Cartesian form.
  2. Rule out a purely geometric reading (e.g. perpendicular bisector) because the coefficients on the two moduli are unequal, so it is an Apollonius circle rather than a line.
  3. Commit to the algebraic route: set w = u+iv to expose real and imaginary parts.
  4. Bridge to action by simplifying each bracket to the form (real)+i(imag) before applying |·|² = Re²+Im².
  5. Inside the move, square both sides simultaneously, expand carefully, collect u²+v² terms, and complete the square at the end to read off centre and radius.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Let w = u+iv with u,v ∈ ℝ.
  2. Rewrite each modulus argument as (real part) + i(imaginary part), being careful with factors of i (e.g. wi = -v+ui).
  3. Apply |a+bi|² = a²+b² to each side to remove the modulus signs.
  4. Square both sides of the original equation to clear square roots, keeping any constant multiplier squared (e.g. 2 becomes 4).
  5. Expand both sides fully and collect like terms.
  6. Rearrange into the form A(u²+v²)+Bu+Cv+D = 0, then divide through by A.
  7. Complete the square in u and in v to read off the centre and radius (or recognise a line if the quadratic terms cancel).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve |2+wi| = 2|w-1|. With w=u+iv: 2+wi = (2-v)+ui and w-1 = (u-1)+iv. Squaring: (2-v)² + u² = 4[(u-1)² + v²]. Expanding gives u²+v²-4v+4 = 4u²-8u+4+4v², so 3u²+3v²-8u+4v = 0, i.e. (u-\tfrac{4}{3})² + (v+\tfrac{2}{3})² = \tfrac{20}{9}, a circle centre (\tfrac{4}{3},-\tfrac{2}{3}), radius \tfrac{2\sqrt5}{3}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Apollonius locus |z-a| = k|z-b| with k ≠ 1
Same substitute-and-square; the (k²-1) coefficient on u²+v² produces a circle. If k=1 the quadratic terms cancel and the locus is the perpendicular bisector of ab.
Single modulus equation |z-a| = r
Squaring gives (u-Re a)² + (v-Im a)² = r² immediately — no need for full expansion.
Modulus involving conjugate or scaled w, e.g. |w+i\bar w|
Substitute w=u+iv and \bar w=u-iv first, simplify to (real)+i(imag), then proceed as usual.
Modulus inequality |z-a| < k|z-b|
Same algebra, but track the inequality sign through squaring (valid since both sides are non-negative) to obtain an interior/exterior region.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 2+wi as having real part 2 and imaginary part w, ignoring that w itself has an imaginary component (wi = -v+ui).
  • Reading |z-a|=k|z-b| with k≠1 as a perpendicular bisector instead of an Apollonius circle.
  • Confusing |w-1| with |w|-1 or with Re(w)-1 when separating parts.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to square the constant multiplier (e.g. RHS coefficient 2 must become 4 after squaring).
  • Sign errors when expanding (2-v)² or (u-1)².
  • Failing to divide through by the leading coefficient before completing the square, giving a wrong radius.
  • Dropping the +v² or +u² term when collecting, so the locus appears to be a line when it is actually a circle.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope: loci of complex numbers, Cartesian equations of circles and lines, and standard expansion/completion of the square. No transformations beyond w=u+iv are required.
