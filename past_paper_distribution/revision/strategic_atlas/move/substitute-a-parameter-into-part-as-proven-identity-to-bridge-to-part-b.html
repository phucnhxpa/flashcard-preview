Trigonometry

Substitute a parameter into part (a)'s proven identity to bridge to part (b)

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Part (b) presents an equation whose LHS (or full structure) is visibly identical to part (a)'s 'show that' identity, except the dummy variable θ has been replaced by a new expression such as 2x, 3x, x/2, or x − π/6. Strong verbal cues are 'hence', 'hence solve', or 'using your answer to part (a)' — these explicitly forbid re-deriving from scratch. Equivalent triggers: the same composite trig fraction reappears in part (b); part (b)'s equation contains the exact LHS of part (a) as a sub-expression; the question splits 'prove' (part a) and 'solve' (part b) across the same algebraic skeleton.

Motivation — Why this works

The proven identity from part (a) is a pre-built tool that collapses a tangle of mixed trig functions (tan, cos, sin 2θ, sin 3θ, csc θ, …) into a single clean polynomial equation in one trig ratio (typically sin or cos of the new argument). Substituting θ → (new argument) is valid because the identity holds for all θ in its domain, so it holds in particular when θ takes the new expression's value. This is the standard 'hence' scaffolding pattern in Edexcel IAL: part (a) does the algebraic heavy lifting, part (b) reuses it and only requires solving plus domain bookkeeping. Skipping the substitution forces a from-scratch derivation that wastes time and risks fresh errors.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that part (b)'s equation has the same algebraic skeleton as part (a)'s LHS, with θ replaced by a new expression (e.g. 2x).
  2. Rule out trying to expand or rederive: the word 'hence' (or 'using your answer to part (a)') is an examiner instruction to reuse, not redo.
  3. Rule out alternative trig manipulations (R-form, double-angle expansion, Weierstrass) — they would duplicate work already done in part (a).
  4. Commit to the substitution θ = (new argument): the proven identity transports directly under this renaming.
  5. Write down the transported equation explicitly, e.g. 3 sin²(2x) + 8 sin(2x) − 3 = 0, treating sin(2x) as the new unknown.
  6. Update the domain: if x ∈ [a, b] and the new argument is 2x, then 2x ∈ [2a, 2b] — track this carefully because the solving phase depends on it.
  7. Plan the next step: solve the resulting quadratic (or polynomial) in the single trig ratio, then convert back to x at the end.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the substitution: read off what θ in part (a) corresponds to in part (b) (e.g. θ = 2x, θ = 3x, θ = x − π/6).
  2. Quote part (a): 'By part (a) with θ = 2x, the equation becomes …' and write the transported identity verbatim.
  3. Replace any remaining trig terms on the RHS using basic definitions (e.g. csc θ = 1/sin θ) so both sides share the same trig ratio.
  4. Adjust the domain: transform x ∈ [a, b] into (new argument) ∈ [new bounds].
  5. Solve the resulting polynomial equation in the single trig ratio (factor or quadratic formula).
  6. Back-substitute to recover x, keeping only solutions inside the original x-domain, and check no original denominator vanishes.

Worked Micro-Example

Part (a) proved $8\tan\theta = 3\cos\theta \Rightarrow 3\sin^2\theta + 8\sin\theta - 3 = 0$. Part (b): solve $8\tan 2x = 3\cos 2x$ for $x\in[0°,90°]$. Set $\theta = 2x$, so by (a): $3\sin^2 2x + 8\sin 2x - 3 = 0$, giving $(3\sin 2x - 1)(\sin 2x + 3) = 0$, hence $\sin 2x = \tfrac{1}{3}$ (rejecting $-3$). With $2x\in[0°,180°]$: $2x = 19.47°$ or $160.53°$, so $x \approx 9.74°$ or $80.26°$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Part (a) proves identity in θ; part (b) uses θ = 3x or θ = x/2
Same substitution logic; multiply or halve the domain bounds accordingly when tracking the new argument's range.
Part (a) gives an R-form result R sin(θ + α); part (b) sets θ = 2x − 30°
Substitute the linear expression for θ; the domain transforms by both scaling and shifting — handle each carefully.
Part (a) proves a fractional identity LHS = simple form; part (b) sets that LHS equal to another trig expression
Replace the LHS with its simple form, then convert the RHS (e.g. 2 csc θ = 2/sin θ) so both sides share one trig ratio; cross-multiply to a polynomial.

Common Misreadings

  • Ignoring 'hence' and re-deriving part (a) inside part (b), wasting time and re-introducing algebra errors.
  • Treating the substitution as merely cosmetic and forgetting it changes the variable's domain.
  • Mistaking the new argument: e.g. reading 8 tan 2x as if θ = x rather than θ = 2x.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to transform the domain: x ∈ [0°, 90°] becomes 2x ∈ [0°, 180°], not [0°, 90°] — failing to double the range loses solutions.
  • Not checking that final x-values keep original denominators nonzero (e.g. cos 2x ≠ 0 if it appeared in the starting equation).
  • Dividing back by the substitution factor incorrectly at the end (e.g. forgetting x = (2x-value)/2).

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL P2/P3/C3 trigonometry: standard identities (Pythagorean, double-angle, reciprocal) from the formula booklet are the only tools needed. Do not generalise to complex-variable substitutions or to identities outside the booklet — the move is purely about reusing a part-(a) result by renaming its dummy variable.
