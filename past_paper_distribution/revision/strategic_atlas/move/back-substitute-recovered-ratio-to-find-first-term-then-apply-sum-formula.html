Sequences & Series

Back-substitute recovered ratio to find first term, then apply sum formula

Family: Parameter Recovery Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in geometric series problems where the common ratio $r$ has just been determined (typically by dividing two given term equations) and a single-term equation such as $u_2 = ar = k$ remains. The question then asks for a specific partial sum $S_n$ or $S_\infty$, which requires both $a$ and $r$. Cue phrases: 'hence find $S_n$', 'find the sum of the first $n$ terms', or 'find $a$ and hence...'.

Motivation — Why this works

The geometric sum formula $S_n = a(1 - r^n)/(1 - r)$ is fully determined by the parameter pair $(a, r)$. Once $r$ is known, any single equation linking $a$ and $r$ collapses to a one-step linear equation in $a$. Choosing the lowest-index term equation (e.g. $u_2 = ar$ rather than $u_5 = ar^4$) minimises arithmetic and avoids re-using fractional powers.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice I already have $r$ from the previous part and still need $a$ before any sum can be computed.
  2. I rule out solving the higher-index equation $ar^4 = \dots$ because it forces me to handle $r^4$ unnecessarily when a simpler $ar = \dots$ equation is available.
  3. I commit to back-substituting $r$ into $u_2 = ar$ to recover $a$ in one step.
  4. With $a$ and $r$ in hand, I move directly to $S_n = a(1-r^n)/(1-r)$ and substitute the required $n$.
  5. Tactically I track the sign of $r^n$ (odd vs even $n$) and keep $1-r$ as an exact fraction to avoid rounding drift.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Pick the simplest term equation containing $a$, e.g. $ar = u_2$.
  2. Substitute the known $r$ and solve $a = u_2 / r$ exactly.
  3. Write down $S_n = a(1 - r^n)/(1 - r)$ with the required $n$.
  4. Compute $1 - r$ as an exact fraction; compute $r^n$ on a calculator, watching the sign.
  5. Combine and evaluate, rounding only at the final step to the requested accuracy.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $r = -2/5$ and $u_2 = ar = -320$: $a = -320 \div (-2/5) = 800$. Then $S_{13} = \dfrac{800\bigl(1 - (-2/5)^{13}\bigr)}{1 - (-2/5)} = \dfrac{800\bigl(1 - (-2/5)^{13}\bigr)}{7/5} \approx 571.43$ (2 d.p.).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Sum to infinity requested with $|r| < 1$
Use $S_\infty = a/(1-r)$ instead; no power computation needed.
Given $u_3$ or $u_k$ rather than $u_2$
Solve $a = u_k / r^{k-1}$; arithmetic is heavier but the structure is identical.
Arithmetic series analogue
After recovering $d$, back-substitute to find $a$ from a given term, then apply $S_n = \tfrac{n}{2}(2a + (n-1)d)$.
Recurrence-defined geometric sequence
Identify $r$ from the recurrence, read $a = u_1$ directly, then apply the sum formula.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing $u_2 = ar$ with $u_2 = ar^2$ (off-by-one in the exponent convention $u_n = ar^{n-1}$).
  • Treating the recovered $a$ as if it were $u_2$ itself and substituting $-320$ for $a$ in the sum formula.
  • Assuming $S_n \approx S_\infty$ is automatic without checking $|r|<1$ or that $n$ is large enough.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when raising a negative $r$ to an odd power; remember odd power $\Rightarrow$ negative.
  • Mis-evaluating $1 - r$ for negative $r$: $1 - (-2/5) = 7/5$, not $3/5$.
  • Premature rounding of $r^n$ or of $1-r$ which corrupts the final 2 d.p. value.
  • Forgetting to divide by $1-r$ (i.e. multiplying by $1-r$ instead) when manipulating the compound fraction.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P2) geometric series content: term formula $u_n = ar^{n-1}$, partial sum $S_n = a(1-r^n)/(1-r)$, and (where $|r|<1$) sum to infinity.
