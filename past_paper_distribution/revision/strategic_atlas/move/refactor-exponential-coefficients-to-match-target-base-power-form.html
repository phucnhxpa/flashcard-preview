Algebra & Functions

Refactor exponential coefficients to match target base-power form

Family: Exponential Regrouping Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in the inductive step of a closed-form proof (e.g. P(n): u_n = A·a^n + B·b^n) once the recurrence has been substituted and like-base terms collected. You are left with coefficients such as 12·2^k or 18·3^k that do not yet visibly match the target P(k+1) or P(k+2). Cue: the bases agree but the leading constants are 'too big' by a factor that is itself a power of the base.

Motivation — Why this works

Any integer coefficient that contains a factor of the base can be absorbed into the exponent: c·a^m·a^k = c·a^{k+m}. Here 12 = 3·2² and 18 = 2·3² expose hidden powers of 2 and 3, letting the constant be split into the formula coefficient (3 or 2) times an exponent shift. This is the algebraic bridge that converts the raw simplified expression into the literal P(k+2) statement the examiner wants to see.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice my simplified expression 12·2^k − 18·3^k has the right bases but the wrong-looking constants compared with the target 3·2^{k+2} − 2·3^{k+2}.
  2. I rule out re-deriving from scratch or trying to factor common terms across bases — the bases are different, so each term must be handled separately.
  3. I commit to absorbing the numerical coefficients into the exponential by writing each constant as (target coefficient) × (base power).
  4. Concretely: 12 = 3·4 = 3·2² and 18 = 2·9 = 2·3², so each piece carries a factor a² that merges with a^k.
  5. I then write the exponent step explicitly (a²·a^k = a^{k+2}) so the final line literally reads as P(k+2).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the target closed form at the new index, e.g. 3·2^{k+2} − 2·3^{k+2}.
  2. For each term, factor the numerical coefficient as (formula constant) × (base)^(index shift): 12 = 3·2², 18 = 2·3².
  3. Combine with the existing a^k: 12·2^k = 3·2²·2^k = 3·2^{k+2}.
  4. Repeat for the other base: 18·3^k = 2·3²·3^k = 2·3^{k+2}.
  5. Write the conclusion line: u_{k+2} = 3·2^{k+2} − 2·3^{k+2}, i.e. P(k+2) holds.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose the inductive simplification gives $u_{k+2} = 12\cdot 2^k - 18\cdot 3^k$. Then $12\cdot 2^k = 3\cdot 2^{2}\cdot 2^k = 3\cdot 2^{k+2}$ and $18\cdot 3^k = 2\cdot 3^{2}\cdot 3^k = 2\cdot 3^{k+2}$. Hence $u_{k+2} = 3\cdot 2^{k+2} - 2\cdot 3^{k+2}$, which is $P(k+2)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Single-base induction, e.g. proving u_n = 5·3^n from a recurrence yielding 45·3^k.
Write 45 = 5·3² to obtain 5·3^{k+2}; only one base to refactor.
Step from k to k+1 rather than k+2.
Factor a single power of the base: e.g. 6·2^k = 3·2·2^k = 3·2^{k+1}.
Target form has negative or fractional coefficient, e.g. -2·3^{k+1}.
Carry the sign through the refactor: -6·3^k = -2·3·3^k = -2·3^{k+1}.
Closed form involves base 4 = 2² mixed with base 2.
Rewrite 4^k = 2^{2k} (or vice versa) before refactoring coefficients to align bases.
Geometric series sum proofs where coefficients like a(r²−1)/(r−1) appear.
Same idea: split the constant into (formula constant)·(power of r) to land on r^{k+1}.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 12·2^k − 18·3^k as already final and asserting it equals P(k+2) without showing the refactor.
  • Trying to factor a common constant across the two terms (e.g. pulling out 6) — this destroys the base-power structure.
  • Confusing 'coefficient × base^k' with 'base^{k+constant}' and writing 12·2^k = 2^{k+12}.

Pitfalls

  • Leaving the answer as 12·2^k − 18·3^k without explicitly showing it equals the target form — examiners require this final identification line.
  • Incorrect index arithmetic: writing 2²·2^k as 2^{k+1} instead of 2^{k+2}.
  • Sign errors when the recurrence introduces minus signs into the coefficient (e.g. forgetting that −18 = −2·3²).
  • Failing to state the conclusion 'therefore P(k+2) is true', which is a separate mark in the induction scheme.

A-level Scope Notes

This is the standard final algebraic step in proof-by-induction questions on linear recurrences in Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP1), where closed forms u_n = A·a^n + B·b^n are routine; no content beyond index laws and integer factorisation is required.
