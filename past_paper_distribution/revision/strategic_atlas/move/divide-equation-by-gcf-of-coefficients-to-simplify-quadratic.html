Divide equation by GCF of coefficients to simplify quadratic

Family: Equation Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a polynomial equation set equal to zero has all coefficients sharing a common integer factor greater than 1, e.g. $3x^2 - 12x + 9 = 0$ where $\gcd(3,12,9)=3$. Also triggers when coefficients are all even, all multiples of 5, or all share a surd/algebraic factor. The cue is visual: every term — including the constant — is divisible by the same number, and the RHS is 0 (or also divisible by that factor).

Motivation — Why this works

Dividing both sides of an equation by a non-zero constant preserves the solution set, since $A=B \iff A/k = B/k$ for $k\neq 0$. Reducing the leading coefficient to 1 (or smaller) makes factorisation by inspection feasible — for $x^2+bx+c=0$ one only searches integer pairs multiplying to $c$ and summing to $b$. It also reduces arithmetic load if the quadratic formula or completing the square is later needed, lowering error risk.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that every coefficient (including the constant term) shares a common factor, and the equation is set to zero.
  2. Rules out factorising the GCF out as a bracket — that is valid for an expression but unnecessarily clutters an equation; rules out jumping to the quadratic formula with bulky numbers.
  3. Commits to dividing both sides by the GCF because it is reversible, preserves roots, and produces a monic (or near-monic) quadratic.
  4. Bridges to action by computing the GCF of the coefficients and dividing each term — including the RHS — by that value.
  5. Tactically checks: is the RHS truly zero (or also divisible)? Is the GCF the largest possible? Should the leading coefficient be made positive by dividing by a negative GCF?

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the GCF of all coefficients on both sides of the equation.
  2. Divide every term on the LHS and RHS by that GCF.
  3. Write the simplified equation, confirming the leading coefficient is now 1 (or as small as possible).
  4. Proceed with factorisation, completing the square, or the quadratic formula on the simpler form.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $3x^2 - 12x + 9 = 0$. All coefficients share GCF $3$, so divide through: $x^2 - 4x + 3 = 0$. Factorise: $(x-1)(x-3)=0$, giving $x=1$ or $x=3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

All coefficients even, e.g. $2x^2 - 10x + 8 = 0$
Divide by 2 to get $x^2 - 5x + 4 = 0$, then factorise as $(x-1)(x-4)=0$.
Negative leading coefficient, e.g. $-2x^2 + 6x - 4 = 0$
Divide by $-2$ to obtain $x^2 - 3x + 2 = 0$, simultaneously simplifying and making the leading coefficient positive.
RHS non-zero, e.g. $3x^2 - 12x + 9 = 6$
Divide every term including the RHS: $x^2 - 4x + 3 = 2$, then rearrange to $x^2 - 4x + 1 = 0$.
Cubic with common factor, e.g. $4x^3 - 8x^2 + 12x = 0$
Divide by 4 to get $x^3 - 2x^2 + 3x = 0$, then factor out $x$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the GCF as something that must be 'factored out into a bracket' rather than divided away on an equation.
  • Believing that dividing by a constant changes the roots (confusing with dividing by a variable expression, which can lose solutions).
  • Overlooking the constant term and only checking the GCF of the $x^2$ and $x$ coefficients.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to divide the RHS when it is non-zero.
  • Dividing by a value that is not actually a factor of every term (e.g. dividing $3x^2 - 12x + 10 = 0$ by 3).
  • Dropping a sign when dividing by a negative GCF.
  • Failing to spot the GCF and applying the quadratic formula to large numbers, increasing arithmetic errors.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard simplification within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1 quadratics) and reused throughout Further Maths whenever polynomial equations arise; the validity rests only on the elementary equivalence $A=B \iff A/k = B/k$ for $k\neq 0$.
