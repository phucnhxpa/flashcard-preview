Negate both sides to make leading coefficient positive before factorising

Family: Sign Normalisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a quadratic (or polynomial) equation appears with a negative leading coefficient, e.g. $-2y^2 + y + 1 = 0$ or $-x^2 + 5x - 6 = 0$. Also triggers when a substitution (such as $y = \sin x$ or $u = e^x$) produces a quadratic in standard form but with the $y^2$ term negative. The cue is: 'I want to factorise, but the leading sign is wrong for my usual heuristics.' Equally applies to discriminant or completing-the-square work where a positive leading coefficient streamlines arithmetic.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying every term of an equation by $-1$ preserves the solution set because $0 \cdot (-1) = 0$. Standard factor-pair and product-sum methods are practised on quadratics $ax^2 + bx + c$ with $a > 0$, so normalising removes a layer of sign tracking. It also makes the discriminant $b^2 - 4ac$ and the quadratic formula visually cleaner. The cost is one line of working; the benefit is a sharp drop in sign errors during factorisation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the leading coefficient is negative, e.g. $-2y^2 + y + 1 = 0$, which will make factor-pair search clumsy.
  2. I rule out forcing the factorisation as-is or extracting a $-1$ as a common factor on one side only — both leave me prone to sign slips.
  3. I commit to multiplying through by $-1$ because the right-hand side is $0$, so the equation is unchanged in solutions.
  4. Bridge: rewrite every term with its sign flipped to land on a standard $ay^2 + by + c = 0$ with $a > 0$.
  5. Tactically, I double-check that the constant term and middle term have both flipped, then apply product-sum factorisation to the cleaned-up quadratic.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify that the leading coefficient is negative.
  2. Multiply every term on both sides by $-1$ (RHS is $0$, so it stays $0$).
  3. Rewrite the equation in standard form $ay^2 + by + c = 0$ with $a > 0$.
  4. Factorise (or apply the formula / complete the square) on the normalised quadratic.
  5. Solve and, if a substitution was used, back-substitute to recover the original variable.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $-2\sin^2 x + \sin x + 1 = 0$ for $\sin x$. Let $y = \sin x$, giving $-2y^2 + y + 1 = 0$. Multiply by $-1$: $2y^2 - y - 1 = 0$. Factor pair for product $-2$, sum $-1$ gives $(2y+1)(y-1) = 0$, so $y = -\tfrac{1}{2}$ or $y = 1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Completing the square on $-x^2 + 6x - 5$
Negate to $x^2 - 6x + 5$ (or factor out $-1$) before completing the square, then reinstate the overall sign at the end.
Quadratic inequality $-x^2 + 3x + 4 \geq 0$
Multiplying by $-1$ flips the inequality to $x^2 - 3x - 4 \leq 0$; the equation case is sign-flip-only, but inequalities require reversing the relation.
Cubic or higher with negative leading term, e.g. $-x^3 + 2x^2 + x - 2 = 0$
Negate to $x^3 - 2x^2 - x + 2 = 0$ before applying the factor theorem or grouping.
Discriminant check on $-3x^2 + 4x + 1 = 0$
Optional negation to $3x^2 - 4x - 1 = 0$ keeps $a$, $b$, $c$ positive-leading for cleaner $b^2 - 4ac$ arithmetic.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the negation as 'factor out $-1$' on only one side, producing $-1(2y^2 - y - 1) = 0$ and then forgetting the $-1$ when factorising.
  • Believing the sign flip changes the roots — students sometimes negate their final answers unnecessarily.
  • Confusing the equation case with the inequality case and flipping a non-existent inequality sign.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to negate every term — e.g. leaving the constant as $+1$ instead of changing it to $-1$.
  • Negating only the quadratic term and not the linear term.
  • Applying the move to one side only (e.g. when RHS is not $0$) without negating the other side.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1–P4) — used routinely in quadratic factorisation, trigonometric equations reducible to quadratics, and quadratic inequalities. No content beyond standard A-level algebra is required.
