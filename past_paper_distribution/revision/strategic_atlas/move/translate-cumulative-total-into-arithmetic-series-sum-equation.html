Sequences & Series

Translate cumulative total into arithmetic series sum equation

Family: Formula Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a context problem describes a cumulative quantity built up across discrete periods — phrases like 'a total of', 'altogether', 'in the first N years', 'combined output', or 'sum extracted'. The presence of a fixed first value and a constant increment/decrement (production falls by 15 tonnes per year, savings rise by £20 per month) confirms an arithmetic progression. The total figure is the sum S_N, not the Nth term u_N. Often a follow-up part asks to 'show that N satisfies' a given quadratic.

Motivation — Why this works

The word 'total' identifies the quantity as a partial sum of an AP, so S_N = N/2·(2a + (N−1)d) directly encodes the situation. Equating this closed form to the given total produces a single equation in N. Because S_N is quadratic in N, the result is naturally a quadratic equation — exactly the form the next part typically asks the student to derive. Choosing this formula (rather than S_N = N/2·(a + l)) is correct whenever d is known but the final term l is not.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the wording 'a total of … extracted/produced/saved' — this is a cumulative quantity, not a single-year value.
  2. Rule out the nth-term formula u_n = a + (n−1)d, which would only give one year's output, and rule out S_N = N/2·(a+l) since l is not supplied.
  3. Commit to S_N = N/2·(2a + (N−1)d) because a and d are both known and the unknown is N.
  4. Substitute the numerical a, d, and the given total to obtain a single equation in N.
  5. Anticipate that clearing the 1/2 and expanding will produce a quadratic in N matching the form requested in the next part.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify a, d from the context and the given cumulative total T.
  2. Write S_N = N/2·(2a + (N−1)d) = T.
  3. Simplify the bracket: 2a + (N−1)d → linear expression in N.
  4. Multiply both sides by 2 to clear the fraction: 2T = N·(linear in N).
  5. Expand and rearrange into standard quadratic form aN² + bN + c = 0.

Worked Micro-Example

Production starts at $a=480$ tonnes and falls by $d=-15$ tonnes per year; total extracted in $N$ years is $7770$. Then $S_N = \tfrac{N}{2}(2(480) + (N-1)(-15)) = 7770$, so $\tfrac{N}{2}(975 - 15N) = 7770$. Multiplying by 2: $975N - 15N^2 = 15540$, giving $15N^2 - 975N + 15540 = 0$, i.e. $N^2 - 65N + 1036 = 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Last term l is stated instead of d (e.g. 'on the final day she ran 30 km')
Use S_N = N/2·(a + l) = T directly; this gives a linear equation in N.
Geometric context ('each year produces 95% of the previous')
Switch to S_N = a(1−r^N)/(1−r) = T; the unknown N appears in an exponent, requiring logarithms rather than a quadratic.
Total exceeds a threshold ('find the least N such that total > 10000')
Set S_N > T and solve the resulting quadratic inequality, taking the smallest integer N.
Sum to infinity of a convergent GP ('eventually extracts a total of …')
Use S_∞ = a/(1−r) instead of a finite sum formula.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 'total' as the Nth-year output and applying u_N = a + (N−1)d.
  • Treating the situation as geometric because the quantity decreases each year, when in fact the decrease is by a constant amount.
  • Using S_N = N/2·(a+l) and inventing l from the context, leading to a wrong equation.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when expanding (N−1)(−15); the −15 must distribute over both N and −1, giving −15N + 15.
  • Forgetting to multiply through by 2 before equating, leaving a fraction that obscures the quadratic.
  • Dropping the negative sign on the N² term when rearranging to standard form.
  • Failing to simplify by a common factor, which can make matching the printed 'show that' form harder.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 arithmetic series content: standard AP sum formula and quadratic manipulation, no techniques beyond the specification.
