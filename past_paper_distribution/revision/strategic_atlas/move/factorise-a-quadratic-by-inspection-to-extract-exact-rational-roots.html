Factorise a quadratic by inspection to extract exact rational roots

Family: Quadratic Factorisation Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0 has small integer coefficients and you suspect the discriminant is a perfect square (so roots are rational). Visual cues: leading coefficient is 1 (monic) or a small prime; constant term has few factor pairs; middle coefficient is small enough to inspect candidate sums quickly. Equivalently: 'the numbers look friendly' — typical in mechanics/geometry problems where exact answers like θ = 3 or x = 1 are expected. If b² − 4ac is a perfect square (e.g. 25, 49), factorisation is the cleanest route; reach for the quadratic formula only if inspection fails.

Motivation — Why this works

Factorisation rewrites ax² + bx + c as (px − r)(qx − s) and then applies the null factor law: a product is zero iff one factor is zero, yielding the roots immediately as exact rationals. Underlying principle: for monic quadratics, x² + bx + c = (x − p)(x − q) where p + q = −b and pq = c (Vieta's relations); for non-monic, the AC-method splits the middle term using a factor pair of ac that sums to b. This avoids surd manipulation that the quadratic formula would otherwise introduce, even when the discriminant is a perfect square. In context (sectors, geometry, kinematics), exact rational roots are usually required.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation is a quadratic with small integer coefficients — flag for factorisation rather than the formula.
  2. Quickly estimate the discriminant b² − 4ac; if it is (or looks like) a perfect square, commit to factorisation. Rule out completing the square as overkill and the quadratic formula as slower for this case.
  3. Read off the SIGN structure: from the signs of b and c deduce whether the two bracket constants are both positive, both negative, or of opposite sign (c > 0 ⇒ same sign as each other, opposite to b; c < 0 ⇒ opposite signs).
  4. If monic, list factor pairs of c and pick the pair summing to −b. If non-monic, compute ac, list its factor pairs, and pick the pair summing to b — then split the middle term.
  5. Group and factor out the common bracket; verify by expansion mentally before applying the null factor law.
  6. State BOTH roots explicitly — don't stop at the factored form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify a, b, c. If a = 1 (monic): seek integers p, q with p + q = −b and pq = c, then write (x − p)(x − q) = 0.
  2. If a ≠ 1: compute the product ac, find a factor pair (m, n) with m + n = b and mn = ac.
  3. Split the middle term: ax² + mx + nx + c, then group: (ax² + mx) + (nx + c).
  4. Factor each group to expose a common bracket, then factor it out to get (px − r)(qx − s) = 0.
  5. Apply the null factor law: set each bracket to zero and solve for x to obtain the two exact roots.
  6. Sanity-check by substituting one root back, or by expanding the factored form.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $3\theta^2 - 13\theta + 12 = 0$. Compute $ac = 36$; need pair summing to $13$: take $9, 4$. Split: $3\theta^2 - 9\theta - 4\theta + 12 = 3\theta(\theta - 3) - 4(\theta - 3) = (3\theta - 4)(\theta - 3) = 0$. Hence $\theta = \tfrac{4}{3}$ or $\theta = 3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Monic quadratic x² + bx + c = 0 (e.g. x² − 4x + 3 = 0)
Skip the AC-step; directly find p, q with p + q = −b, pq = c, then write (x − p)(x − q) = 0.
Non-monic with prime leading coefficient (e.g. 3θ² − 13θ + 12)
One bracket must be (3θ ± ?), the other (θ ± ?); use AC-method or directly trial constants of the brackets.
Discriminant is not a perfect square
Factorisation by inspection fails — switch to the quadratic formula x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac))/(2a) or complete the square.
Disguised quadratic (e.g. in sin x, e^x, or √x)
Substitute u = sin x (etc.), factorise in u, then back-substitute and solve.

Common Misreadings

  • Reaching for the quadratic formula by reflex without checking whether a quick factor pair exists.
  • Misidentifying the equation as un-factorable because the leading coefficient is not 1, when AC-splitting would resolve it.
  • Forgetting Vieta's sign logic and writing (x + 1)(x + 3) when the sum is negative.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when both bracket constants should be negative (c > 0, b < 0 case).
  • Choosing a factor pair with the right product but wrong sum (e.g. 12 and 3 instead of 9 and 4 for ac = 36, b = 13).
  • Stopping at (x − p)(x − q) = 0 without writing the two solutions explicitly.
  • Dividing through by a variable factor (losing a root) instead of using the null factor law.
