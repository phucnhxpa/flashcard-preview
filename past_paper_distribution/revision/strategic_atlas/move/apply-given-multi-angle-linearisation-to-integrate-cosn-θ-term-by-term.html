Integration

Apply given multi-angle linearisation to integrate cos^n θ term-by-term

Family: Partial Fractions Then Integrate Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integrand contains a high power of cos θ or sin θ (typically cos^4, cos^5, cos^6, sin^4, etc.) that is not directly integrable in closed form. A previous part of the question (often via De Moivre / (z + z^{-1})^n expansion) has supplied a linearisation expressing 2^{n-1}cos^n θ as a sum of cos(kθ) terms. The current part asks for a definite or indefinite integral involving that power, possibly combined with lower-order cos(kθ) terms designed to cancel.

Motivation — Why this works

Each cos(kθ) integrates immediately to sin(kθ)/k, whereas integrating cos^n θ directly requires repeated reduction formulas. The linearisation derived from (z + z^{-1})^n with z = e^{iθ} converts a product into a sum, transferring all the difficulty into the (already completed) algebraic expansion. Examiners frequently insert extra lower-frequency cosine terms in the integrand so they collapse with terms in the linearisation, rewarding students who simplify before integrating.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices a high power cos^n θ inside the integral and recalls that part (b) just produced an identity 2^{n-1}cos^n θ = Σ a_k cos(kθ) + c.
  2. Rules out using a reduction formula or repeated double-angle substitution as too slow and error-prone for the time budget.
  3. Commits to substituting the linearisation because every resulting term is now of the form cos(kθ) or a constant, each trivially integrable.
  4. Plans to combine like cos(kθ) terms across the substituted expression and any other cos(mθ) pieces in the original integrand before integrating.
  5. Decides to integrate term-by-term using ∫cos(kθ)dθ = sin(kθ)/k, then evaluate at the limits using exact values from the unit circle / CAST diagram.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Quote the linearisation from the earlier part, e.g. 32cos^6 θ = cos 6θ + 6cos 4θ + 15cos 2θ + 10.
  2. Substitute into the integrand and combine like cos(kθ) terms with any other cosine pieces.
  3. Integrate term-by-term: ∫cos(kθ)dθ = sin(kθ)/k; ∫c dθ = cθ.
  4. Substitute the upper and lower limits, using exact unit-circle values for sin at multiples of π/3, π/4, π/6.
  5. Simplify surd fractions and π terms to a single closed-form answer.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_0^{\pi/3}(32\cos^6\theta - 4\cos 2\theta)\,d\theta$. Using $32\cos^6\theta = \cos 6\theta + 6\cos 4\theta + 15\cos 2\theta + 10$, the integrand becomes $\cos 6\theta + 6\cos 4\theta + 11\cos 2\theta + 10$. Integrating gives $\left[\tfrac{\sin 6\theta}{6} + \tfrac{3\sin 4\theta}{2} + \tfrac{11\sin 2\theta}{2} + 10\theta\right]_0^{\pi/3} = 0 + \tfrac{3}{2}(-\tfrac{\sqrt 3}{2}) + \tfrac{11}{2}(\tfrac{\sqrt 3}{2}) + \tfrac{10\pi}{3} = 2\sqrt 3 + \tfrac{10\pi}{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Integrand contains sin^n θ with a supplied linearisation
Use the (z − z^{-1})^n expansion; odd n gives sin(kθ) terms, even n gives cos(kθ) terms with alternating signs.
Mixed product such as cos^4 θ · sin^2 θ
Expand both (z + z^{-1})^4 and (z − z^{-1})^2, multiply, then collect into a single linear combination of cos(kθ).
Indefinite integral rather than definite
Same substitution and term-by-term integration; add +C and skip limit evaluation.
Linearisation not given — must be derived first
Apply De Moivre to (2cos θ)^n = (z + z^{-1})^n with z = e^{iθ}, expand binomially, pair conjugate terms into 2cos(kθ).

Common Misreadings

  • Forgetting to subtract the additional lower-order cosine term in the original integrand, leaving the wrong coefficient (e.g. 15cos 2θ instead of 11cos 2θ).
  • Treating 32cos^6 θ as if the linearisation equals cos^6 θ itself, dropping the factor of 32.
  • Reading the question as requiring a reduction formula rather than reusing the previous part's identity.

Pitfalls

  • Omitting the 1/k factor when integrating cos(kθ) → sin(kθ)/k.
  • Mis-evaluating sines at multiples of π/3 or 2π/3 (e.g. sin(4π/3) = −√3/2, not +√3/2); a quick CAST diagram avoids this.
  • Arithmetic slips when combining multiple surd fractions with different denominators in the final step.
  • Forgetting that sin(2π) = 0 and sin(0) = 0, leading to spurious nonzero contributions.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL Further Pure (De Moivre's theorem applications and trigonometric integration); the linearisation is normally supplied or derived in an earlier part of the same question.
