Integration

Choose unfactored power form when bounds give perfect-square radicands

Family: Form Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

After integrating (e.g. via substitution u = x+1), two equivalent antiderivative forms are available: a sum of fractional powers like $(2/3)(x+1)^{3/2} - 2(x+1)^{1/2}$ or a factored form like $(2/3)(x-2)\sqrt{x+1}$. The bounds make $(x+1)$ a perfect square (or 1), so fractional powers evaluate to clean integers. This fires whenever you must choose a form before substituting integer limits into an expression involving $(\text{linear})^{p/q}$.

Motivation — Why this works

Each term of the unfactored form depends only on $(x+1)^{k/2}$, so when $(x+1)$ is a perfect square the powers reduce to integers independently. The factored form bundles a separate linear factor $(x-2)$ with $\sqrt{x+1}$, forcing an extra multiplication and an extra sign to track at each bound. Independent integer evaluations are mechanically safer and align with the structure naturally produced by reverse-chain or substitution methods, so no algebraic refactoring is needed.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice my antiderivative can be written as a sum of half-integer powers of $(x+1)$ or as a single factored product.
  2. I rule out the factored form because $(x-2)\sqrt{x+1}$ requires multiplying a signed integer by a surd at each bound, which adds an arithmetic step with no payoff.
  3. I commit to the sum-of-powers form because at $x=3$ and $x=0$ the radicand $(x+1)$ is $4$ and $1$ — both perfect squares — making each fractional power a clean integer.
  4. First concrete operation: write $F(x) = \tfrac{2}{3}(x+1)^{3/2} - 2(x+1)^{1/2}$ and prepare to evaluate $F(3) - F(0)$.
  5. Tactically, I compute $4^{3/2}$ as $(\sqrt{4})^3 = 8$, not $4^3$, and keep the two terms separate until the final subtraction to avoid sign slips.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the antiderivative as $F(x) = \tfrac{2}{3}(x+1)^{3/2} - 2(x+1)^{1/2}$.
  2. Evaluate at upper bound: $F(3) = \tfrac{2}{3}(4)^{3/2} - 2(4)^{1/2} = \tfrac{2}{3}(8) - 2(2) = \tfrac{16}{3} - 4 = \tfrac{4}{3}$.
  3. Evaluate at lower bound: $F(0) = \tfrac{2}{3}(1)^{3/2} - 2(1)^{1/2} = \tfrac{2}{3} - 2 = -\tfrac{4}{3}$.
  4. Subtract: $F(3) - F(0) = \tfrac{4}{3} - (-\tfrac{4}{3}) = \tfrac{8}{3}$.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_0^3 \dfrac{x}{\sqrt{x+1}}\,dx$. With $u = x+1$ the antiderivative is $\tfrac{2}{3}(x+1)^{3/2} - 2(x+1)^{1/2}$. At $x=3$: $\tfrac{2}{3}(8) - 2(2) = \tfrac{4}{3}$. At $x=0$: $\tfrac{2}{3} - 2 = -\tfrac{4}{3}$. Result: $\tfrac{8}{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Bounds make $(x+1)$ equal to $0$ or $1$
Either form works; the unfactored form is still cleaner because every term vanishes or equals 1 independently.
Bounds give a non-square radicand, e.g. $x+1 = 3$
Prefer the factored form $(2/3)(x-2)\sqrt{x+1}$ so the surd $\sqrt{3}$ appears once instead of needing $3^{3/2} = 3\sqrt{3}$ separately.
Trig integrals with $\sin^{n}\theta$ at bounds $0, \pi/2$
Keep terms split as separate powers rather than factoring, since each power evaluates cleanly at $0$ or $1$.
Logarithmic antiderivative like $x\ln x - x$ on integer bounds
Keep the two-term form rather than factoring as $x(\ln x - 1)$, so $\ln$ values combine additively.

Common Misreadings

  • Believing the factored form is 'simpler' because it has fewer terms, when in fact term count is less important than ease of evaluation at the given bounds.
  • Thinking the two forms are not equivalent and re-deriving the antiderivative instead of recognising they differ only by algebraic factoring.
  • Assuming you must always factor a surd expression to its simplest form before substituting.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the minus sign on the lower-limit value: computing $\tfrac{4}{3} - \tfrac{4}{3} = 0$ instead of $\tfrac{4}{3} - (-\tfrac{4}{3}) = \tfrac{8}{3}$.
  • Misreading $4^{3/2}$ as $4^3 = 64$ rather than $(\sqrt{4})^3 = 8$.
  • Dropping the $\tfrac{2}{3}$ coefficient when substituting, especially after a substitution step.
  • Combining the two terms over a common denominator before substitution and then losing track of signs.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits within Edexcel IAL Pure (C34/P3) integration by substitution and definite integral evaluation; uses only standard exponent laws and surd arithmetic, no techniques beyond the syllabus.
