Proof

Apply strong induction with two base cases for a second-order linear recurrence

Family: Strong Induction Framing Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the recurrence relating terms of a sequence has the form u_{n+2} = a·u_{n+1} + b·u_n (or any rule depending on two previous terms), and you are asked to prove a closed form by induction. Cue phrases include 'prove by induction that u_n = …' alongside a recurrence that names two predecessors. Standard one-step induction will visibly fail to provide enough substitutions. Also fires for Fibonacci-style sequences and any second-order linear recurrence with given u_1, u_2.

Motivation — Why this works

Computing u_{k+2} from the recurrence requires closed forms for both u_{k+1} and u_k, so the inductive hypothesis must supply two consecutive truths simultaneously. Strong induction — assuming P(k) ∧ P(k+1) — delivers exactly the two substitutions the recurrence demands. Because the inductive step now starts a chain anchored by two terms, two base cases (n=1 and n=2) are required; otherwise the domino chain cannot begin. The logical structure mirrors the order of the recurrence: order-2 recurrence ↔ 2 hypotheses ↔ 2 base cases.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the recurrence references u_n and u_{n+1} together — it is second order, not first order.
  2. Rule out the textbook P(k) ⇒ P(k+1) template: it provides only one closed form, leaving the other term in the recurrence unjustified.
  3. Commit to strong induction with hypothesis P(k) ∧ P(k+1), recognising this matches the two inputs the recurrence consumes.
  4. Bridge to execution: state the proposition P(n), verify P(1) and P(2) separately as the two anchor cases.
  5. Inside the step, substitute both closed forms into u_{k+2} = a·u_{k+1} + b·u_k, then algebraically reorganise to match the closed form evaluated at k+2.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. State proposition P(n): u_n = 3·2^n − 2·3^n (or whatever closed form is given).
  2. Verify base case n=1: compute 3·2^1 − 2·3^1 = 0 and check this equals the given u_1.
  3. Verify base case n=2: compute 3·2^2 − 2·3^2 = −6 and check this equals the given u_2.
  4. Assume P(k) and P(k+1) both hold for some k ≥ 1 (strong inductive hypothesis).
  5. Use the recurrence: u_{k+2} = 5u_{k+1} − 6u_k = 5(3·2^{k+1} − 2·3^{k+1}) − 6(3·2^k − 2·3^k).
  6. Factor powers of 2 and 3 separately to obtain 3·2^{k+2} − 2·3^{k+2}, which is P(k+2).
  7. Conclude by induction P(n) holds for all n ≥ 1.

Worked Micro-Example

Let u_1=0, u_2=−6, u_{n+2}=5u_{n+1}−6u_n. Claim: u_n=3·2^n−2·3^n. Bases: 3·2−2·3=0=u_1; 3·4−2·9=−6=u_2. Assume true at k and k+1. Then u_{k+2}=5(3·2^{k+1}−2·3^{k+1})−6(3·2^k−2·3^k)=2^k(30−18)−3^k(30−18)·… regrouping gives 3·2^{k+2}−2·3^{k+2}, so P(k+2) holds.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Third-order recurrence u_{n+3}=f(u_{n+2},u_{n+1},u_n)
Assume P(k), P(k+1), P(k+2) and verify three base cases n=1,2,3.
Fibonacci-type identity, e.g. proving F_{n+2}=F_{n+1}+F_n satisfies a closed form
Same two-hypothesis, two-base-case template; substitute both closed forms into the recurrence.
Second-order recurrence with shifted indexing u_n=a·u_{n−1}+b·u_{n−2}
Bases at the two smallest indices in scope; hypothesis assumes P(k−1) and P(k), conclude P(k+1).
Matrix-power proofs where M^n has a claimed form satisfying a 2-term relation
Use strong induction on n with two base cases M^1, M^2.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the recurrence as first-order and attempting standard P(k) ⇒ P(k+1), then finding u_{k−1} unaccounted for.
  • Verifying only n=1 and assuming the chain starts there, ignoring that the step needs two consecutive truths.
  • Confusing 'strong induction' with simply assuming P(j) for all j ≤ k without recognising that for this proof only the two latest are used.

Pitfalls

  • Omitting the second base case n=2 — the proof is logically incomplete.
  • Substituting only one closed form into the recurrence and leaving the other term as u_k unsimplified.
  • Algebra slips when factoring 2^k and 3^k — failing to pull out 2^{k+2} and 3^{k+2} cleanly.
  • Stating 'assume true for all k' without making explicit that both P(k) and P(k+1) are being used.

A-level Scope Notes

Strong induction with multiple base cases is within Edexcel IAL Further Pure proof-by-induction scope, typically applied to second-order linear recurrences and sequence identities.
