Trigger — When to use

The curve is given by an equation F(x,y)=0 where x and y are intertwined and cannot be cleanly rearranged to y=f(x). Tell-tale structural cues are: a y² (or higher y-power) term, a cross-term such as xy, x²y, or xy², and/or transcendental y-terms like sin(y), e^y, ln(y). Equivalent phrasings: 'find dy/dx for the curve …', 'find the gradient at the point (a,b) on the curve …', or any question giving a polynomial/transcendental relation in x and y rather than y explicitly. Trying to solve for y first would require the quadratic formula or branch choices and make differentiation messy.

Motivation — Why this works

Implicit differentiation treats y as an unknown differentiable function y(x) and differentiates every term of F(x,y)=0 with respect to x. Pure x-terms differentiate by the power/chain rule normally, pure y-terms pick up a factor of dy/dx via the chain rule (d/dx[g(y)] = g'(y)·dy/dx), and mixed terms like x·y require the product rule combined with the chain rule on the y-factor. The result is a single linear equation in dy/dx, which can be solved algebraically — bypassing any need to isolate y or handle ± branches.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation mixes x and y nontrivially: there is a y² (or higher power) and/or a cross-term such as xy, so y is not a clean function of x.
  2. Rule out explicit rearrangement: solving for y would invoke the quadratic formula, introducing ± and surds whose derivative would be unpleasant; rule out parametric methods since no parameter t is given.
  3. Commit to implicit differentiation: keep the equation as-is and apply d/dx to every term, treating y as y(x).
  4. Plan term-by-term: x^n → n x^(n−1); y^n → n y^(n−1)·(dy/dx) (chain rule); x·y → 1·y + x·(dy/dx) (product rule); constants → 0; transcendental in y → derivative·(dy/dx).
  5. After differentiating, gather all terms containing dy/dx onto one side, factor dy/dx out, and divide to obtain dy/dx as a rational expression in x and y.
  6. If a gradient at a specific point is asked, substitute (x,y) coordinates only at the very end — never before differentiating.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Apply d/dx to every term of F(x,y)=0.
  2. For each pure x-term x^n: write n x^(n−1).
  3. For each pure y-term y^n: write n y^(n−1)·(dy/dx) — never forget the dy/dx factor.
  4. For each mixed term of the form x^a · y^b: apply product rule, giving a x^(a−1) y^b + b x^a y^(b−1)·(dy/dx).
  5. For transcendental y-terms (sin y, e^y, ln y): write cos(y)·(dy/dx), e^y·(dy/dx), (1/y)·(dy/dx) respectively.
  6. Constants differentiate to 0.
  7. Collect all dy/dx terms on the left, all non-dy/dx terms on the right.
  8. Factor dy/dx and divide: dy/dx = (non-dy/dx side) / (coefficient of dy/dx).
  9. Simplify by factoring common constants from numerator and denominator.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $x^2 + 2xy - 2y^2 + 18 = 0$. Differentiate term-by-term: $2x + 2\!\left(y + x\tfrac{dy}{dx}\right) - 4y\tfrac{dy}{dx} = 0$. Collect: $(2x - 4y)\tfrac{dy}{dx} = -2x - 2y$. Therefore $\tfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{-2(x+y)}{2(x-2y)} = \dfrac{x+y}{2y-x}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Higher-power mixed terms such as x³y² in the equation
Apply product rule then chain rule: d/dx(x³y²) = 3x²y² + x³·2y·(dy/dx); same logic, just more algebra to collect dy/dx.
Transcendental y-terms like sin(y), e^y, ln(y)
Use chain rule with the appropriate outer derivative: d/dx[sin y] = cos(y)·(dy/dx), d/dx[e^y] = e^y·(dy/dx), d/dx[ln y] = (1/y)·(dy/dx).
Curve given parametrically as x=f(t), y=g(t)
Do NOT use implicit differentiation; instead use dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt).
Gradient required at a specific point (a,b)
Differentiate implicitly first to obtain dy/dx in terms of x and y, then substitute x=a, y=b at the end.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to rearrange to y=f(x) first, which forces the quadratic formula and ± branches and wastes time.
  • Mistaking the equation for a parametric form and applying (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) when no parameter is present.
  • Treating y as a constant and differentiating y² to 2y (no dy/dx factor).

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the dy/dx factor on every differentiated y-term — the single most common error.
  • Forgetting the product rule on cross-terms like xy: must give y + x(dy/dx), not just x(dy/dx) or just y.
  • Sign errors when collecting dy/dx terms across the equals sign.
  • Substituting the point's coordinates before differentiating, which destroys the y-dependence and yields a wrong derivative.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 (Pure Maths) implicit differentiation. Uses the chain rule and product rule (no formula-booklet entry needed — these are assumed). Avoid invoking partial derivatives or the formula dy/dx = −F_x/F_y, which is out of scope at IAL.
