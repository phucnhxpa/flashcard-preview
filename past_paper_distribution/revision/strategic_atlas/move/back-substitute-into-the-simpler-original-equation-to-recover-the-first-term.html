Trigger — When to use

After solving a pair of geometric-sequence simultaneous equations for the common ratio r, you still need to find the first term a (or u₁). Two original equations of the form a·r^m = c₁ and a·r^n = c₂ both contain a, so a choice must be made. The cue is the asymmetry between the two equations: one has a lower power of r, an integer (or simpler) RHS, or avoids a sum like (r + r²). Recognise also that the derived quadratic in r no longer contains a, so you must return to an original equation, not the eliminated one.

Motivation — Why this works

Both original equations are mathematically valid for recovering a, but exam-time arithmetic risk is minimised by choosing the equation with the lower power of r and the cleaner RHS. Computing r² is easier than r⁵; dividing by 1/4 is cleaner than dividing by 1/32; a single product a·r^k = c is simpler than a·(r + r²) = c which requires evaluating a sum first. The underlying principle is just substitution into a one-variable linear equation in a — picking the version with the fewest fractional operations preserves accuracy and gives a quick self-check by substituting into the other equation.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice that I now have a numerical value for r, and I need to find a (or u₁). Both original equations still contain a, so I have a choice.
  2. I rule out substituting into the derived quadratic — that equation was constructed by eliminating a and so cannot recover it. I must return to an original equation.
  3. I compare the two original equations: which has the smaller power of r, the simpler RHS, and the fewest combined operations? I commit to that one.
  4. I evaluate r^k for the chosen equation, being careful with the sign if r is negative and with fraction arithmetic if r is fractional.
  5. I solve the resulting one-step linear equation a·(r^k) = c for a by multiplying both sides by the reciprocal of r^k.
  6. If time permits, I substitute (a, r) into the other original equation as a verification cross-check.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two original equations a·r^m = c₁ and a·r^n = c₂ (with m < n).
  2. Select the equation with the lower power and/or simpler RHS — typically a·r^m = c₁.
  3. Compute r^m using the known value of r, taking care with signs and fraction cubes/powers.
  4. Substitute to obtain a·(r^m) = c₁.
  5. Solve a = c₁ / r^m = c₁ × (1/r^m) by multiplying by the reciprocal.
  6. (Optional) Verify by substituting a and r into the unused original equation.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $ar^2 = 12$ and $ar^5 = 1.5$, dividing gives $r^3 = 1/8$ so $r = 1/2$. Choose the simpler equation $ar^2 = 12$: then $a \cdot (1/2)^2 = 12 \Rightarrow a \cdot 1/4 = 12 \Rightarrow a = 48$. Check: $48 \cdot (1/2)^5 = 48/32 = 1.5$ ✓.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Negative fractional ratio, e.g. r = −2/3 with equations u₁(r + r²) = 6 and u₁r³ = 8
Prefer u₁r³ = 8 (single product) over u₁(r + r²) = 6 (requires summing r and r²). Compute (−2/3)³ = −8/27, then u₁ = 8 × (−27/8) = −27.
Arithmetic sequence simultaneous equations in a and d
Same principle: after finding d, substitute into whichever equation a + (n−1)d = c has the cleaner constants.
Sum-formula equations, e.g. S_n given alongside a term formula
Back-substitute into the term formula a·r^(k−1) = u_k rather than into S_n, since the sum formula involves more arithmetic.

Common Misreadings

  • Substituting r into the derived quadratic (which no longer contains a) and concluding a cannot be found.
  • Assuming both equations are equally easy and picking the higher-power one, then making a fraction error.
  • Treating u₁(r + r²) = 6 as u₁r + r² = 6 by dropping the bracket.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when raising a negative fraction to an odd power: (−2/3)³ is negative, not positive.
  • Flipping the reciprocal incorrectly: dividing by −8/27 means multiplying by −27/8, not 27/8.
  • Off-by-one in the exponent of u_n = ar^(n−1): the third term is ar², not ar³.
  • Forgetting that r² is positive even when r is negative, which can mask a sign error elsewhere.

A-level Scope Notes

This sits squarely within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 sequences and series (geometric sequences, formula u_n = ar^(n−1) from the formula booklet). No out-of-scope techniques are needed; avoid invoking logarithms to solve for r unless the question explicitly requires it.
