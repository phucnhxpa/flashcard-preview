Proof

Select proof by induction for a universal summation identity

Family: Induction Framework Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks to prove a statement of the form $\sum_{r=1}^{n} f(r) = g(n)$ for all positive integers $n$. Cues include the phrases 'prove by induction', 'for all $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$', or appearance in an FP1 induction chapter. Also triggers when no closed-form summation toolkit is permitted and the statement is parameterised by $n$.

Motivation — Why this works

Induction converts an infinite family of propositions $P(n)$ into two manageable checks: a base case $P(1)$ and the implication $P(k) \Rightarrow P(k+1)$. Summation identities are particularly amenable because $\sum_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r) = \sum_{r=1}^{k} f(r) + f(k+1)$, so the inductive hypothesis substitutes directly to create an algebraic bridge to the target $g(k+1)$. The Principle of Mathematical Induction then guarantees $P(n)$ for every positive integer.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the universal quantifier 'for all positive integers $n$' and that the LHS is a finite sum depending on $n$.
  2. Rule out direct proof via standard formulae ($\sum r$, $\sum r^2$, $\sum r^3$) since the question explicitly demands induction, and rule out telescoping unless a transparent partial-fraction decomposition is visible.
  3. Commit to induction because the recursive structure $S_{k+1} = S_k + f(k+1)$ makes the inductive step purely algebraic.
  4. Bridge to the first operation by defining the proposition $P(n)$ precisely and stating the four-part template.
  5. Tactically, plan to factor $g(k+1)$ in advance so the algebraic target after substituting the hypothesis is clear.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. State $P(n)$: the proposition $\sum_{r=1}^{n} f(r) = g(n)$.
  2. Base case: verify $P(1)$ by computing both sides at $n=1$.
  3. Inductive hypothesis: assume $P(k)$ holds for some positive integer $k$.
  4. Inductive step: write $\sum_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r) = \sum_{r=1}^{k} f(r) + f(k+1)$, substitute the hypothesis, and manipulate algebraically to obtain $g(k+1)$.
  5. Conclusion: state that since $P(1)$ is true and $P(k) \Rightarrow P(k+1)$, by the Principle of Mathematical Induction $P(n)$ holds for all positive integers $n$.

Worked Micro-Example

Prove $\sum_{r=1}^{n} r(r+1) = \tfrac{1}{3}n(n+1)(n+2)$. Base: $n=1$ gives LHS $=2$, RHS $=\tfrac{1}{3}(1)(2)(3)=2$. Assume $\sum_{r=1}^{k} r(r+1) = \tfrac{1}{3}k(k+1)(k+2)$. Then $\sum_{r=1}^{k+1} r(r+1) = \tfrac{1}{3}k(k+1)(k+2) + (k+1)(k+2) = \tfrac{1}{3}(k+1)(k+2)(k+3)$, which is $g(k+1)$. Hence by induction the result holds for all $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Divisibility induction (e.g. $f(n) = 3^{2n}-1$ divisible by 8)
Replace sum decomposition with examining $f(k+1) - \lambda f(k)$ or $f(k+1)$ rewritten using the hypothesis to factor out the divisor.
Matrix induction (e.g. $\mathbf{M}^n$ has a closed form)
Inductive step multiplies the assumed $\mathbf{M}^k$ by $\mathbf{M}$ on the right (or left) and simplifies entry-wise to match the closed form at $k+1$.
Recurrence-defined sequence ($u_{n+1}=g(u_n)$, prove closed form)
Inductive step uses the recurrence relation to express $u_{k+1}$ in terms of $u_k$, then substitutes the hypothesis.
Inequality induction ($\text{e.g. } 2^n > n^2$ for $n \geq 5$)
Base case starts at the smallest valid $n$, and the inductive step combines the hypothesis with an auxiliary inequality rather than an exact equation.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the question as if standard summation results ($\sum r$, $\sum r^2$) may be invoked, bypassing induction entirely.
  • Attempting to prove both $P(k) \Rightarrow P(k+1)$ and $P(k+1) \Rightarrow P(k)$, mistaking induction for an iff statement.
  • Confusing 'prove for all $n$' with verifying several numerical cases instead of a general inductive step.

Pitfalls

  • Omitting an explicit base case or final conclusion sentence — Edexcel mark schemes award specific marks for each.
  • Writing 'assume $P(n)$ is true' rather than 'assume $P(k)$ is true for some positive integer $k$', conflating the bound variable.
  • Failing to factor $g(k+1)$ cleanly, leaving the algebra in an unmatched form and losing the final 'shown' mark.
  • Stopping after algebraic manipulation without the closing statement invoking the Principle of Mathematical Induction.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP1) induction; the four-part template (base, assume, step, conclude) is the expected mark-scheme structure for summation, divisibility, matrix, and recurrence proofs.
