Proof

Substitute rearranged inductive hypothesis to expose the divisor as a common factor

Family: Hypothesis Substitution Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires inside the inductive step of a divisibility proof, after you have written f(k+1) and decomposed any exponentials by one power (e.g. 4^{k+1} = 4·4^k, 5^{2k+3} = 25·5^{2k+1}). The trigger is recognising that one of the terms appearing in f(k+1) — typically a single exponential like 4^k or 4^{k+2} — also appears (or can be isolated) from the assumed hypothesis f(k) = dM. Equivalent cues: 'I see the same exponential block in both f(k) and f(k+1)', or 'My hypothesis can be rearranged to express that block as dM minus the rest'. This is the standard Edexcel IAL induction-on-divisibility setup.

Motivation — Why this works

Rearranging the hypothesis to isolate one term (e.g. 4^k = 9m − 6k + 1, or 4^{k+2} = 21m − 5^{2k+1}) and substituting it into f(k+1) injects an explicit multiple of the target divisor d into the expression. The remaining (non-dM) terms then collapse — by design of the original problem — into another multiple of d, because the coefficients have been engineered so their combination is divisible by d. The underlying principle is simply: f(k+1) = (something)·f(k) + (clean remainder), where the remainder is forced to be a multiple of d by the algebraic structure. Substitution makes this decomposition mechanical and visible.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice f(k+1), after splitting one power off, contains an exponential block (e.g. 4^k or 4^{k+2}) that also appears in my inductive hypothesis f(k) = dM.
  2. I weigh two strategies: (A) isolate that block from the hypothesis and substitute directly into f(k+1); (B) compute f(k+1) − c·f(k) for a suitable constant c so the exponential cancels. Both are valid, but (A) is more mechanical and less prone to sign slips, and matches the Edexcel textbook style.
  3. I commit to (A) because the substitution will guarantee a dM term appears, and I expect the residual non-dM terms to combine with a coefficient that is itself a multiple of d (this is not luck — it is forced by f(k) being divisible by d in the first place).
  4. I rearrange the hypothesis to isolate the chosen block, e.g. 4^k = 9m − 6k + 1, being careful with signs.
  5. I substitute into f(k+1), expand fully, and collect like terms. I then check that EVERY remaining coefficient is a multiple of d — if even one isn't, I've made an arithmetic slip.
  6. I factor d out explicitly, write the bracket as an integer expression in m and k, and state that this integer is an integer (an Edexcel mark-scheme requirement). I also use a fresh letter if needed to avoid reusing m in a circular way.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Assume f(k) = dM (hypothesis); rearrange to isolate one exponential block, e.g. a^k = dM − (other terms).
  2. Write f(k+1) and split one power off the exponential(s), e.g. a^{k+1} = a·a^k.
  3. Substitute the isolated expression for a^k into f(k+1).
  4. Expand the bracket carefully, watching signs (a·(dM − rest) = adM − a·rest).
  5. Collect like terms and verify every coefficient is a multiple of d.
  6. Factor d out: f(k+1) = d·(integer expression in M and k).
  7. State explicitly that the bracket is an integer, hence f(k+1) is divisible by d.

Worked Micro-Example

Prove $f(n)=4^n+6n-1$ is divisible by 9. Hypothesis: $4^k = 9m - 6k + 1$. Then $f(k+1) = 4\cdot 4^k + 6k + 5 = 4(9m - 6k + 1) + 6k + 5 = 36m - 24k + 4 + 6k + 5 = 36m - 18k + 9 = 9(4m - 2k + 1)$. Since $4m - 2k + 1 \in \mathbb{Z}$, $f(k+1)$ is divisible by 9. ∎

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two distinct exponentials, e.g. f(n) = 4^{n+1} + 5^{2n−1} divisible by 21
Isolate one exponential block (either choice works) from the hypothesis, substitute, and verify the residual exponential picks up coefficient ±21 (or a multiple). The difference of the two multipliers is forced to be a multiple of d.
Multiply-and-subtract alternative
Compute f(k+1) − c·f(k) directly, where c is the multiplier from the power-split. The exponential cancels, leaving a remainder that must be checked to be a multiple of d. Equivalent in content but bypasses explicit substitution.
Three-term or polynomial-residual expressions
After substitution, the residual may need careful regrouping before the common factor emerges; check each coefficient individually.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to factor d out of the original f(k+1) directly without invoking the hypothesis — this is non-inductive and almost always fails.
  • Subtracting f(k) from f(k+1) without the correct multiplier (e.g. f(k+1) − f(k) instead of f(k+1) − 4f(k)), leaving an exponential that doesn't cancel.
  • Substituting back into f(k) instead of into f(k+1).

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when expanding the substituted bracket, e.g. writing 4(9m + 6k − 1) instead of 4(9m − 6k + 1).
  • Failing to verify that EVERY residual coefficient is a multiple of d — the crux of the proof and explicitly required for full marks.
  • Omitting the closing statement that the bracketed expression is an integer — Edexcel mark schemes deduct for this.
  • Reusing the letter m for the new integer factor, which can look like circular reasoning; introduce a fresh letter (J, p, etc.) where appropriate.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL Further Pure 1 (FP1) 'Proof by induction — divisibility' (and equivalently CP1/FP1 in different specifications). No formula booklet entry is needed; the technique is self-contained. Avoid invoking modular arithmetic / congruence notation (≡ mod d) in the written solution — it is out of scope and examiners expect the substitution-and-factor presentation.
