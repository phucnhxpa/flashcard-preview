Integration

Multiply integrand by 1 to manufacture IBP product structure

Family: Integration By Parts Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integrand is a single function (typically $\ln x$, $(\ln x)^n$, $\arcsin x$, or $\arctan x$) with no elementary antiderivative on the standard list, and no obvious substitution simplifies it. There is no visible product, so integration by parts seems inapplicable at first glance. The cue is: 'I recognise this function, I can differentiate it cleanly, but I cannot integrate it directly.' That asymmetry — easy to differentiate, hard to integrate — is the signal to pair it with $1$.

Motivation — Why this works

Integration by parts requires a product $u\,\dfrac{dv}{dx}$, but the identity $f(x) = f(x)\cdot 1$ is always available. Choosing $\dfrac{dv}{dx}=1$ gives $v=x$ at zero cost, while $u=f(x)$ converts the problem into $xf(x) - \int x f'(x)\,dx$. The trick succeeds precisely when $xf'(x)$ is simpler than $f(x)$ — true for logarithms (the $1/x$ from differentiation cancels the $x$ from $v$) and for inverse trig functions (whose derivatives are algebraic).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand is a lone non-elementary-to-integrate function such as $(\ln x)^2$, with no product visible.
  2. Rule out direct integration (not on the standard list) and rule out substitution $t=\ln x$ since it just relocates the difficulty into $\int t^2 e^t\,dt$.
  3. Commit to IBP after recalling that $\int \ln x\,dx$ is solved by writing $\ln x = \ln x \cdot 1$ — the same device generalises.
  4. Bridge: rewrite the integrand as $f(x)\cdot 1$, take $u=f(x)$ and $\dfrac{dv}{dx}=1$, so $v=x$.
  5. Tactically check that $xf'(x)$ is genuinely simpler; if it still contains a logarithm to a lower power, plan a second IBP round.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rewrite $\int f(x)\,dx$ as $\int f(x)\cdot 1\,dx$.
  2. Set $u=f(x)$ and $\dfrac{dv}{dx}=1$, giving $\dfrac{du}{dx}=f'(x)$ and $v=x$.
  3. Apply IBP: $\int f(x)\,dx = x f(x) - \int x f'(x)\,dx$.
  4. Simplify $xf'(x)$ — typically $x$ cancels a $1/x$ or leaves an algebraic expression — and integrate the residual, repeating IBP if needed.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int (\ln x)^2\,dx$. Write as $\int (\ln x)^2 \cdot 1\,dx$ with $u=(\ln x)^2$, $\dfrac{dv}{dx}=1$, so $\dfrac{du}{dx}=\dfrac{2\ln x}{x}$, $v=x$. Then $\int(\ln x)^2 dx = x(\ln x)^2 - \int 2\ln x\,dx$. A second IBP round on $\int \ln x\,dx = x\ln x - x$ gives the final answer $x(\ln x)^2 - 2x\ln x + 2x + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$\int \ln x\,dx$
Base case: $u=\ln x$, $\dfrac{dv}{dx}=1$, leading to $x\ln x - x + C$.
$\int \arctan x\,dx$
Take $u=\arctan x$, $\dfrac{dv}{dx}=1$; residual $\int \dfrac{x}{1+x^2}\,dx$ is a standard $\ln$ integral.
$\int \arcsin x\,dx$
Take $u=\arcsin x$, $\dfrac{dv}{dx}=1$; residual $\int \dfrac{x}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\,dx$ solved by inspection or substitution.
$\int (\ln x)^n\,dx$ for integer $n\ge 1$
Same setup; each IBP round reduces the power of $\ln x$ by one until reaching the base case.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the integrand as 'not a product' and concluding IBP is inapplicable, then giving up or guessing an antiderivative.
  • Reaching for substitution $t=\ln x$ first, which transforms $\int (\ln x)^2 dx$ into $\int t^2 e^t\,dt$ — solvable but no simpler than the original.
  • Trying to use $u=1$, $\dfrac{dv}{dx}=f(x)$, which defeats the purpose since $v=\int f(x)\,dx$ is the unknown.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the chain rule when differentiating $(\ln x)^2$: writing $\dfrac{du}{dx}=2\ln x$ instead of $\dfrac{2\ln x}{x}$.
  • Dropping the constant of integration on the final answer.
  • Sign errors in the IBP formula, especially when a second round of IBP is required.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics integration by parts; the surfaces $\ln x$, $(\ln x)^2$, $\arctan x$, $\arcsin x$ are all standard examination targets in P3/P4 and Further Pure.
