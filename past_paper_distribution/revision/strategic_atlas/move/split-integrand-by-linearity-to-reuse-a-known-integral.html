Integration

Split integrand by linearity to reuse a known integral

Family: Linearity Decomposition Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a new integral's integrand is structurally close to one already evaluated (numerically by trapezium rule, or analytically) earlier in the question, or is a sum/difference of standard pieces. Cues include: 'hence' instructions linking parts; integrands of the form (old integrand) + constant, (old integrand) + 1/x, k·(old integrand), or c − (old integrand); and polynomial-times-exponential integrands like (ax²+bx+c)eˣ where each monomial-times-eˣ has a standard antiderivative. In short, whenever you can read the integrand as f + g where ∫f is already in hand or trivial, this move fires.

Motivation — Why this works

Linearity of integration, ∫(αf + βg) dx = α∫f dx + β∫g dx, lets a complicated integral be reassembled from cheap pieces. This is exactly what 'hence' parts in Edexcel papers reward: rather than re-tabulating values for the trapezium rule or pushing one big integration by parts, you reuse a previously computed estimate or apply a standard antiderivative to each piece. The principle solves two problems at once — it eliminates redundant computation and it shrinks each sub-problem to a well-rehearsed pattern with fewer error opportunities.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the new integrand looks suspiciously like the previous part's integrand plus or minus something simple (a constant, 1/x, a scalar multiple), or that it is a sum of monomials times a common factor like eˣ.
  2. Rule out brute force: re-applying the trapezium rule would need y-values not given in the table; doing one giant IBP on (x²−8x+16)eˣ would track a long expression through multiple stages where signs compound errors.
  3. Commit to linearity because every sub-integral is then either (a) already estimated in an earlier part, (b) a constant times the interval length, or (c) a textbook antiderivative.
  4. Bridge to operation: write the integrand explicitly as a sum, e.g. 26/x − x = (25/x − x) + 1/x, or (x²−8x+16)eˣ = x²eˣ − 8xeˣ + 16eˣ, then split the integral term-by-term using linearity.
  5. Tactical decision: when substituting a numerical estimate from an earlier part, use the unrounded value (e.g. 2.96225, not 2.96) and round only at the final step to avoid premature rounding error.
  6. Final check: reassemble carefully, watching signs and coefficients (a −8 in front of an integral must survive to the end).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rewrite the integrand as an explicit sum/difference: e.g. ∫(26/x − x) dx = ∫(25/x − x) dx + ∫(1/x) dx; or ∫(4 − y) dx = ∫4 dx − ∫y dx; or ∫(x²−8x+16)eˣ dx = ∫x²eˣ dx − 8∫xeˣ dx + 16∫eˣ dx.
  2. For each sub-integral, identify its source: previously estimated value, constant × interval length, or standard antiderivative.
  3. Evaluate the trivial pieces exactly (e.g. ∫_{0.5}^{3} 4 dx = 4 × 2.5 = 10; ∫_{5}^{7} (1/x) dx = ln(7/5)).
  4. Substitute the carried-over estimate (full precision) for the recurring piece.
  5. Combine all sub-results, paying attention to signs and coefficients, and round only at the end.

Worked Micro-Example

Given ∫_{5}^{7}(25/x − x)dx ≈ 5.39 from part (a), evaluate ∫_{5}^{7}(26/x − x)dx. Write 26/x − x = (25/x − x) + 1/x, so ∫_{5}^{7}(26/x − x)dx = 5.39 + ∫_{5}^{7}(1/x)dx = 5.39 + ln(7/5) ≈ 5.39 + 0.3365 ≈ 5.73.

Variants — Same move, different surface

New integrand is a scalar multiple of the old: e.g. ∫50/x − 2x dx after ∫25/x − x dx is known
Pull the constant out: ∫k·f dx = k·∫f dx, then substitute the known estimate.
New integrand is c − (old integrand), e.g. ∫(4 − y) dx after ∫y dx
Split as ∫c dx − ∫y dx; the constant integrates to c·(b−a). Watch the subtraction order.
Polynomial × eˣ (or × sin x, cos x) where each monomial term needs IBP of different depth
Split into ∫xⁿeˣ dx pieces; do IBP separately on each (0, 1, or 2 rounds depending on n), then recombine with original coefficients.
Series questions linking a new sum to a previously computed one
Same idea: express the new sum as (known sum) ± a few extra terms and evaluate only the extras.

Common Misreadings

  • Misreading an additive constant inside the integrand as a multiplicative factor outside, e.g. treating ∫(3 + 25/x − x) dx as 3·∫(25/x − x) dx.
  • Failing to spot the algebraic split (e.g. not seeing 26/x = 25/x + 1/x) and instead trying to build a fresh table of values for the trapezium rule.
  • Treating the entire polynomial as u in a single IBP for ∫(x²−8x+16)eˣ dx, producing a long expression where sign errors compound.

Pitfalls

  • Using a rounded estimate from the earlier part too early (e.g. 2.96 instead of 2.96225), causing the final answer to drift in the last decimal.
  • Forgetting that the lower limit is not 0: e.g. computing ∫_{0.5}^{3} 4 dx as 4×3 = 12 instead of 4×2.5 = 10.
  • Subtracting in the wrong order, e.g. writing (estimate) − (constant integral) instead of the reverse.
  • Losing a coefficient (the −8 or +16) when recombining sub-integrals after IBP.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL P2/P3/P4 integration: linearity of the integral, trapezium rule from P2, standard antiderivatives (∫1/x dx = ln|x|, ∫eˣ dx = eˣ) and integration by parts from P3. No use of Lebesgue, improper-integral convergence theorems, or multivariable extensions of linearity is needed.
