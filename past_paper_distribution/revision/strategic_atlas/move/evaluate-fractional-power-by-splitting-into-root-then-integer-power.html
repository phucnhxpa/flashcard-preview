Evaluate fractional power by splitting into root then integer power

Family: Fractional Exponent Evaluation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A non-calculator evaluation produces an expression of the form a^{p/q} or a^{-p/q} where a is a perfect q-th power (e.g. 4^{-5/2}, 8^{2/3}, 27^{-4/3}, 16^{3/4}). Typical cues: substituting an integer into a term with fractional exponent during definite integration, evaluating f(a) for power functions, or simplifying surd-style expressions. Whenever the base is a recognisable perfect square, cube, or fourth power, this move fires.

Motivation — Why this works

The index law a^{m/n} = (\sqrt[n]{a})^m lets us replace a fractional exponent with a root followed by an integer power, and a^{-k} = 1/a^k handles the sign. Taking the root first keeps numbers small and exact, avoiding large intermediate integers. This produces a clean rational answer suitable for combining with other exact terms (fractions, surds) in the rest of the calculation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices a fractional (possibly negative) exponent on an integer base that must be evaluated exactly.
  2. Rules out raising to the numerator power first, since that produces a large integer whose root is harder to extract by hand.
  3. Commits to the root-first route after checking the base is a perfect q-th power for the denominator q.
  4. Rewrites a^{-p/q} as 1/(\sqrt[q]{a})^p, separating sign, root, and integer power into three controlled steps.
  5. Computes the small root, raises to the small integer power, applies the reciprocal if needed, then combines with surrounding coefficients as a fraction.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the base a and the exponent m/n (track the sign separately).
  2. If the exponent is negative, rewrite as a reciprocal: a^{-m/n} = 1/a^{m/n}.
  3. Take the n-th root of a: compute \sqrt[n]{a} as an integer (require a to be a perfect n-th power).
  4. Raise that integer to the m-th power.
  5. Multiply by any coefficient and combine with other terms over a common denominator.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate -12 \cdot 4^{-5/2}. Write 4^{-5/2} = 1/(\sqrt{4})^5 = 1/2^5 = 1/32. Then -12 \cdot \tfrac{1}{32} = -\tfrac{12}{32} = -\tfrac{3}{8}. Combining with, say, 96 + 4 gives 100 - \tfrac{3}{8} = \tfrac{797}{8}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Positive fractional exponent like 27^{4/3}
Skip the reciprocal step: \sqrt[3]{27} = 3, then 3^4 = 81.
Negative fractional exponent like 8^{-2/3}
Reciprocate after evaluating: 8^{2/3} = 2^2 = 4, so 8^{-2/3} = 1/4.
Fourth-root cases like 16^{3/4}
Use \sqrt[4]{16} = 2, then 2^3 = 8.
Evaluating definite integrals where F(a) - F(b) contains x^{-n/2} terms
Apply the move at each limit and combine the resulting fractions over a common denominator.
Non-perfect-power base like 2^{5/2}
Leave in surd form: 2^{5/2} = (\sqrt{2})^5 = 4\sqrt{2}, no integer simplification.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading a^{-5/2} as -(a^{5/2}) instead of 1/a^{5/2}.
  • Treating the denominator of the exponent as the power and the numerator as the root (i.e. swapping m and n).
  • Assuming the base must always simplify to an integer when it does not (e.g. 5^{3/2}, which stays as 5\sqrt{5}).

Pitfalls

  • Computing 4^5 = 1024 first and then trying to take \sqrt{1024} — slow and error-prone.
  • Dropping the negative sign on the exponent and getting 32 instead of 1/32.
  • Forgetting to apply the coefficient sign, e.g. writing +3/8 instead of -3/8.
  • Arithmetic slips when combining the fraction with integer terms (failing to use a common denominator).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1/P2 index laws and surds, P2/P3 integration evaluations). Bases used in exam contexts are almost always small perfect powers (4, 8, 9, 16, 25, 27, 32, 64), so the n-th root is always an integer or a simple surd.
