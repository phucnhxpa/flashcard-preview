Complex Numbers

Evaluate discriminant sign to classify root type at specific parameter value

Family: Discriminant Classification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A parametric quadratic (or pair of roots in the form $p \pm \sqrt{q(d)}$) is given, and a specific value of the parameter is substituted. Before declaring roots real or complex, the solver must check the sign of the quantity under the square root. Cues include: 'find the roots when $d = \ldots$', or 'express in the form $a + ib$' where $b$ may turn out to be zero. Fires whenever a discriminant-like expression depends on a parameter that has just been pinned down.

Motivation — Why this works

The expression $\sqrt{D}$ behaves fundamentally differently by sign of $D$: $D>0$ gives two distinct real roots, $D=0$ a repeated real root, and $D<0$ a complex conjugate pair $p \pm i\sqrt{|D|}$. Without classifying first, a student may write $i\sqrt{D}$ when $D$ is in fact positive, or miss that the answer collapses to reals. Sign-checking the discriminant is the gatekeeper that determines which algebraic form to present.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the roots are written as $p \pm \sqrt{D(d)}$ and the question fixes a specific $d$.
  2. Rule out blindly inserting $i$ just because the question is in a 'complex numbers' chapter — the discriminant might still be positive.
  3. Commit to evaluating $D(d)$ numerically first and reading off its sign before any square-rooting.
  4. Bridge: substitute the given $d$, simplify $D$, then choose the real-root branch ($\sqrt{D}$) or complex-root branch ($i\sqrt{-D}$) accordingly.
  5. Tactical: if the question demands the form $a+ib$, present real roots as $a + 0i$ to satisfy the prescribed format.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the parameter-dependent discriminant $D(d)$ under the square root.
  2. Substitute the given value of $d$ to compute $D$.
  3. Classify: $D>0$ (two reals), $D=0$ (repeated real), $D<0$ (complex conjugate pair).
  4. Take $\sqrt{D}$ if $D \ge 0$, or write $i\sqrt{-D}$ if $D<0$.
  5. Write final roots in the form requested (e.g. $a+ib$, with $b=0$ allowed).

Worked Micro-Example

Roots are $x = 5 \pm \sqrt{25 - d^2}$. For $d=4$: $25 - 16 = 9 > 0$, so roots are real. Then $x = 5 \pm 3$, giving $x = 8$ and $x = 2$, written as $8 + 0i$ and $2 + 0i$ in the required form.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Same setup with $d=6$
$25-36=-11<0$, so roots are complex: $x = 5 \pm i\sqrt{11}$.
Boundary value $d=5$
$25-25=0$, giving a repeated real root $x=5$.
Standard quadratic $ax^2+bx+c=0$ with parameter in $b$ or $c$
Compute $\Delta = b^2-4ac$ at the chosen parameter value and classify roots identically.
Quadratic in $z$ with complex coefficients reduced to real discriminant
Same sign analysis applies once $D$ is shown to be real.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming any 'complex numbers' question must yield non-real roots and inserting $i$ unnecessarily.
  • Treating $\sqrt{25-d^2}$ as automatically imaginary because $d^2$ looks large, without computing.
  • Forgetting that real roots still need to be expressed as $a+0i$ when the form $a+ib$ is demanded.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error in computing $25 - d^2$ (e.g. writing $d^2-25$).
  • Writing $\sqrt{-D} = -\sqrt{D}$ instead of $i\sqrt{D}$ when $D<0$.
  • Dropping the $\pm$ and reporting only one root.

A-level Scope Notes
