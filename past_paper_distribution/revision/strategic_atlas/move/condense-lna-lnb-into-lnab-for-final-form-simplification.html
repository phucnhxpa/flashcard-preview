Algebra & Functions

Condense ln(a) − ln(b) into ln(a/b) for final-form simplification

Family: Logarithm Condensation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a final expression — typically the output of an integration, a rearranged ODE solution, or an algebraic simplification — contains two separate logarithmic terms joined by subtraction, e.g. ln(f(x)) − ln(c) or ln(f(x)) − ln(g(x)). Common cues are a stray ln(constant) sitting next to a ln(linear) term, or a mark scheme demanding 'a single logarithm' / 'simplified form'. Also fires when the constant of integration absorbs a logarithmic term that can be merged. Subtraction of two logs with the same base is the strongest cue.

Motivation — Why this works

The subtraction law ln(a) − ln(b) = ln(a/b) is a direct consequence of the quotient property of exponentials and is valid for all a, b > 0. Condensing produces a single logarithm, which is the canonical 'simplified' form expected by Edexcel mark schemes and makes downstream operations (exponentiating, applying boundary conditions, sketching) cleaner. It also avoids carrying redundant constant log terms that obscure the structure of the answer.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Student notices two ln terms in the expression with a minus sign between them, e.g. ln(4x+5) − ln(4), and recognises this as a condensation opportunity.
  2. They rule out exponentiating prematurely or distributing the minus inside the argument (which would be a rule violation), and also rule out absorbing the ln(4) into the constant of integration if the question demands an explicit single-log form.
  3. They commit to the subtraction law because it is a single-step identity that immediately yields the requested simplified form without altering the solution's validity domain.
  4. The bridge to action is to bracket the two log terms together, leaving any non-log constants (e.g. +1/81) outside, then apply ln(a) − ln(b) = ln(a/b).
  5. Tactically, they decide whether to leave the quotient as (4x+5)/4 (preserving integer coefficients matching the original problem) or split it into 1 + 5/(4x) — usually the former is preferred for matching mark scheme presentation.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Scan the expression and isolate the two log terms joined by subtraction, ignoring any non-logarithmic constants.
  2. Verify both arguments are positive on the relevant domain so the law applies.
  3. Apply ln(a) − ln(b) = ln(a/b) to merge: ln(4x+5) − ln(4) → ln((4x+5)/4).
  4. Reattach any non-log constants (e.g. +1/81) outside the new single logarithm.
  5. If required by the question, continue simplification (e.g. multiply through, take cube root) using the condensed form.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\tfrac{y^3}{3} = \ln(4x+5) + \tfrac{1}{81} - \ln 4$, condense the logs: $\tfrac{y^3}{3} = \ln\!\left(\tfrac{4x+5}{4}\right) + \tfrac{1}{81}$. Multiply by 3 and take the cube root: $y = \sqrt[3]{\,3\ln\!\left(\tfrac{4x+5}{4}\right) + \tfrac{1}{27}\,}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two log terms joined by addition, e.g. ln(f(x)) + ln(g(x))
Use the product law ln(a) + ln(b) = ln(ab) instead of the quotient law.
A coefficient sits in front of one log, e.g. 2ln(x) − ln(y)
First apply the power law k·ln(a) = ln(a^k) to absorb the coefficient, then subtract: ln(x^2/y).
Solution of a separable ODE producing ln|y| − ln|C| + …
Combine as ln|y/C| and exponentiate to obtain y = C·(…) form.
Definite-integral evaluation giving ln(b) − ln(a)
Condense to ln(b/a) before evaluating numerically, especially if the question asks for an exact form.
Mixed bases, e.g. log_2(a) − log_2(b)
Same law applies in any base: log_2(a) − log_2(b) = log_2(a/b).

Common Misreadings

  • Treating non-log constants as if they were inside the logarithm and trying to absorb them into the argument.
  • Reading ln(4x+5) − ln(4) as a single logarithm of the difference, i.e. ln((4x+5) − 4).
  • Assuming the rule requires equal coefficients and refusing to apply the power law first when one term has a coefficient.
  • Mistaking ln(a) − ln(b) for ln(a)/ln(b).

Pitfalls

  • Writing ln(4x+5) − ln(4) = ln(4x+1) by subtracting arguments instead of dividing them.
  • Pulling the +1/81 inside the logarithm as ln((4x+5)/4) + 1/81 → ln((4x+5)/4 + 1/81), conflating addition outside with multiplication inside.
  • Forgetting to retain the modulus signs (ln|·|) when arguments could be negative on the domain.
  • Cancelling the 4 prematurely as ln((4x+5)/4) = ln(x + 5/4), which is fine algebraically but may not match the mark scheme's preferred form.

A-level Scope Notes

All identities used (quotient, product, power laws of logarithms) are core Edexcel IAL Pure content (P2/P3) and are routinely required in C3/C4-style integration, differential equation, and exponential-model questions.
