Algebra & Functions

Rewrite e^{a ln b} as b^a to collapse log-exp composites

Family: Index Law Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an exponential e^{...} contains a logarithm multiplied by another factor in its exponent, e.g. e^{c·ln d}, e^{-kt} after substituting k = (1/T)ln(R), or e^{(ln a)x}. Typical cues: a substitution into an exponential decay/growth model produces a nested ln inside e^{...}; a calculator entry would require many brackets; the question asks for an exact form. Also fires when converting between bases, since a^x = e^{x ln a}.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity e^{a ln b} = b^a is a direct consequence of e^{ln b} = b combined with the index law (e^{ln b})^a = e^{a ln b}. Applying it strips the exponential–logarithm pair, leaving a clean power of a rational or simple base. This is cleaner for exact answers, more reliable on a calculator (fewer nested brackets), and exposes structure for later parts of a question (e.g. reusing the same numerical value).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the exponent has the shape (constant)·ln(something), sitting inside an e^{...}.
  2. Rule out direct calculator entry: nested ln inside exp invites bracket/sign errors and won't give an exact form.
  3. Commit because the identity e^{a ln b} = b^a is exact, reversible, and converts to a single power.
  4. Identify a (the multiplier of ln) and b (the argument of ln) cleanly, keeping any negative sign with a.
  5. Decide whether to leave the result as b^a or flip to (1/b)^{-a} so the exponent is positive and the base ≤ 1.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Spot the pattern e^{a·ln b} inside the expression; isolate the multiplier a and the argument b.
  2. Apply the identity: e^{a·ln b} = b^a, carrying signs carefully (a can be negative or fractional).
  3. Optionally use b^{-a} = (1/b)^a to make the exponent positive or the base simpler.
  4. Substitute back into the surrounding expression and continue (evaluate numerically or keep exact).

Worked Micro-Example

Simplify $e^{-(9/5)\ln(5/3)}$.\\ Identify $a = -9/5$, $b = 5/3$, so $e^{a\ln b} = b^a = (5/3)^{-9/5}$.\\ Flip the base: $(5/3)^{-9/5} = (3/5)^{9/5} = 0.6^{1.8} \approx 0.3988$.\\ Hence $\theta = 198 - 180\,(3/5)^{9/5} \approx 126.2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Converting bases for differentiation/integration, e.g. $2^x = e^{x\ln 2}$
Use the identity in reverse: rewrite $a^x$ as $e^{x\ln a}$ before differentiating or integrating.
Solving $a^x = b$ in exponential models
Take logs to get $x = \ln b / \ln a$; the same identity underpins why $e^{(\ln b/\ln a)\ln a} = b$.
Simplifying $e^{k\ln f(x)}$ inside an integrand
Replace by $[f(x)]^k$ before integrating — often turns the integral into a standard power rule.
Compound interest / decay with $e^{-kt}$ where $k = \tfrac{1}{T}\ln R$
After substituting $t$, the exponent becomes $(t/T)\ln R$, collapsing to $R^{t/T}$.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading $e^{a\ln b}$ as $a\cdot e^{\ln b} = ab$, treating the exponent multiplication as outside the exponential.
  • Confusing $e^{a\ln b}$ with $e^{\ln(ab)} = ab$ by mis-applying the log power rule.
  • Thinking the identity needs $a$ to be a positive integer; it holds for any real $a$.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors: dropping the minus in the exponent and writing $(5/3)^{9/5}$ instead of $(5/3)^{-9/5}$.
  • Forgetting $b^{-a} = (1/b)^a$, leading to awkward negative-exponent entries on a calculator.
  • Mis-pairing the multiplier with the wrong logarithm when several ln terms appear in the exponent.
  • Applying the identity when the exponent is $a + \ln b$ rather than $a\cdot\ln b$ — additive vs multiplicative structure must be checked.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the basic identity $e^{\ln x} = x$ and index laws from Edexcel IAL P2/P3, routinely needed in exponential growth/decay (Newton's law of cooling, population models) and in change-of-base manipulations in C34/Further Pure.
