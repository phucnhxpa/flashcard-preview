Sequences & Series

Set differentiation depth from the highest power demanded

Family: Order Planning Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a Taylor/Maclaurin question states a stopping condition before any calculus begins: 'up to and including the term in x⁴', 'as far as the term in x³', 'first four non-zero terms', or 'find the series up to x^n'. Also fires when a later part says 'hence find an approximation correct to n decimal places', which implicitly bounds how many terms you need. The cue is any phrase that fixes how high a power you must reach.

Motivation — Why this works

In a Taylor series about x = a, the coefficient of (x − a)^n is f^(n)(a)/n!, so producing the x^n term requires exactly the nth derivative evaluated at a. Counting derivatives in advance lets you lay out a clean table f, f', f'', … f^(n) without redoing work or running short. It also flags whether 'non-zero' wording forces extra derivatives because some vanish at the expansion point.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the stopping phrase in the question and identify the highest power n that must appear.
  2. Rule out stopping at the (n−1)th derivative — the x^n coefficient genuinely needs f^(n)(a).
  3. Commit to producing a derivative table of length n+1: f(a), f'(a), …, f^(n)(a).
  4. Bridge to action: write a vertical list f, f', f'', … with space for evaluation at x = a before any algebra.
  5. If the wording is 'first k non-zero terms', mentally check whether the function has parity (sin, cos) or another reason for some derivatives to vanish at a, and increase the depth accordingly.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Parse the question to extract the highest power n required, or the number k of non-zero terms.
  2. Plan a derivative list f(a), f'(a), f''(a), …, f^(n)(a) — that is n+1 evaluations.
  3. If 'non-zero terms' is specified, anticipate that some f^(r)(a) may be zero and budget extra derivatives.
  4. Lay out the table before differentiating so each row is filled in order and none is skipped.
  5. After computing, assemble the series ∑ f^(r)(a)(x−a)^r / r! up to r = n (or until k non-zero terms are reached).

Worked Micro-Example

Find the Maclaurin expansion of $\cos x$ up to and including the term in $x^4$. Highest power $n=4$, so plan five evaluations $f(0),f'(0),f''(0),f'''(0),f^{(4)}(0) = 1,0,-1,0,1$. Series: $1 - \tfrac{x^2}{2!} + \tfrac{x^4}{4!}$. Note that 'first three non-zero terms' would have demanded the same depth here, but for $\sin x$ it would force going up to $f^{(5)}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Even/odd functions like $\cos x$ or $\sin x$ at $a=0$
Every other derivative vanishes at 0; double the planned depth when 'first k non-zero terms' is asked.
Taylor series about $x = a \ne 0$ (e.g. expand $\ln x$ about $x=1$)
Same depth count, but each derivative is evaluated at $a$, not 0.
'Approximate to n decimal places' instead of an explicit power
Estimate how many terms keep the truncation error below $0.5\times 10^{-n}$, then plan derivatives to that depth.
Series obtained by multiplying or composing two known expansions (e.g. $e^x \cos x$ up to $x^4$)
Truncate each factor at $x^n$ before multiplying instead of differentiating $n$ times directly.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 'up to $x^3$' as 'up to the second derivative', miscounting by one.
  • Treating 'first four non-zero terms' as 'first four derivatives' regardless of which evaluate to zero.
  • Confusing 'up to and including $x^n$' (inclusive) with 'up to $x^n$' meaning strictly less than n.

Pitfalls

  • Stopping one derivative early and losing the leading required coefficient.
  • For $\sin x$, $\cos x$ or similar, computing only n+1 derivatives when 'non-zero terms' demanded more.
  • Forgetting the $1/r!$ factor when assembling the series after differentiation.
  • Mixing up the expansion point: differentiating but evaluating at the wrong value of $x$.
