Trigonometry

Select a trig identity that bridges current form to target form

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

The expression on hand and the target form use trig functions of DIFFERENT arguments or DIFFERENT structural shapes — e.g. you have sin x cos x but need sin 2x, you have sin 3θ but need sin 2θ, or you have sin(θ − 30°) but need tan θ. The mismatch is the cue: products of same-angle sin·cos point to double-angle; triple/quadruple angles point to a compound-angle split (3θ = 2θ + θ); compound angles like (θ ± α) with α a standard angle (30°, 45°, 60°) point to sin(A ± B) / cos(A ± B) expansion. In short, fire this move whenever the question's required form names a specific trig structure that your current expression doesn't yet display.

Motivation — Why this works

Trig identities act as translators between equivalent forms. Choosing the RIGHT identity is the heart of the problem: the goal is to convert what you have into a shape that either matches the target directly or factors against another part of the expression (e.g. a denominator). Double-angle sin 2x = 2 sin x cos x collapses a product into a single function; compound-angle sin(A ± B), cos(A ± B) decomposes a single function into a linear combination of sin and cos of the base angle; the split kθ = (k−1)θ + θ exposes lower multiples that may already appear elsewhere. The principle: pick the identity whose output structurally aligns with the target's named functions and arguments.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structural mismatch: list the trig functions and arguments present in your current expression, and list those required by the target form.
  2. Rule out brute substitution (e.g. t = tan x/2) and rule out 'wrong-direction' identities like triple-angle formulae that introduce cubes when the target is linear in sin 2θ.
  3. Commit to the identity whose RHS contains the target's arguments: products of sin·cos at the same angle ⇒ double-angle; sin/cos of multiple angle kθ when target features (k−1)θ ⇒ compound-angle split kθ = (k−1)θ + θ; sin/cos of (θ ± α) with α standard ⇒ sin(A ± B) expansion to surface sin θ and cos θ separately.
  4. Bridge to operation: write the identity explicitly with the actual angles substituted (e.g. sin 3θ = sin 2θ cos θ + cos 2θ sin θ), then plug in any exact values (sin 30° = 1/2, cos 30° = √3/2).
  5. Tactical check: confirm coefficients carry through correctly (halving when sin x cos x = ½ sin 2x; signs in cos(A − B) = cos A cos B + sin A sin B). Group terms so they align with what the next step needs (e.g. factor out (sin θ − cos θ) to cancel a denominator).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the current expression and the target side-by-side; identify the trig functions/arguments that don't yet match.
  2. Choose the identity: double-angle (sin 2x = 2 sin x cos x, cos 2x = 1 − 2 sin²x = 2 cos²x − 1), compound-angle split (sin(kθ) = sin((k−1)θ + θ)), or compound-angle expansion (sin(A ± B), cos(A ± B)).
  3. Apply the identity literally, keeping all coefficients; substitute exact values for any standard angles.
  4. Simplify and group terms so the result either matches the target form or factors against another part of the expression.
  5. Read off requested constants (A, B, etc.) or proceed to cancellation/rearrangement.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\frac{dy}{dx} = 4 - 24\sin x\cos x$, target form $A + B\sin 2x$. Identify mismatch: have $\sin x\cos x$, need $\sin 2x$. Apply $\sin 2x = 2\sin x\cos x$, so $\sin x\cos x = \tfrac{1}{2}\sin 2x$. Substitute: $-24\sin x\cos x = -12\sin 2x$, giving $\frac{dy}{dx} = 4 - 12\sin 2x$, so $A = 4$, $B = -12$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Product sin x cos x appearing after differentiation, target involves sin 2x
Use sin 2x = 2 sin x cos x, remembering the factor of ½ when converting.
sin²x or cos²x in an expression to be integrated or matched to sin 2x / cos 2x form
Use cos 2x = 1 − 2sin²x or cos 2x = 2cos²x − 1 BEFORE differentiating/integrating to avoid the chain-rule-then-identity route.
Triple-angle terms (sin 3θ, cos 3θ) with denominator involving (sin θ ± cos θ)
Split 3θ = 2θ + θ and apply sin(A+B), cos(A+B); avoid the cubic triple-angle formulae which force sum-of-cubes factorisation.
Equation with sin(θ ± α) where α is a standard angle (30°, 45°, 60°), target is tan θ
Expand via sin(A ± B), substitute exact values for sin α, cos α, then divide through by cos θ to reach tan θ.
sin 4θ, cos 4θ in identities
Choose split 4θ = 2θ + 2θ or 4θ = 3θ + θ depending on which lower multiple appears in the target.

Common Misreadings

  • Reaching for triple-angle formulae (sin 3θ = 3 sin θ − 4 sin³θ) when a 3θ = 2θ + θ split would align better with the rest of the expression.
  • Differentiating sin²x by chain rule and then converting, when rewriting sin²x = ½(1 − cos 2x) first would be cleaner.
  • Treating sin(θ − 30°) as sin θ − sin 30° (dropping the compound-angle structure entirely).

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the ½ factor: writing 2 sin x cos x = 2 sin 2x instead of sin 2x.
  • Sign error in cos(A − B) = cos A cos B + sin A sin B (the cross-term is +, not −).
  • Sign error in cos(A + B): the cross-term is −sin A sin B, not +.
  • Forgetting to distribute an external coefficient (e.g. the factor of 2) over the substituted exact values.
  • Subtracting only part of a multi-term expansion (e.g. dropping the −sin 2θ sin θ term when computing sin 3θ − cos 3θ).

A-level Scope Notes

All identities used are in the Edexcel IAL formula booklet (P3 Trigonometry section): double-angle, compound-angle (sum/difference), and the standard exact values for 30°, 45°, 60°. Triple-angle formulae are derivable but not booklet-listed; prefer the compound-angle split. Avoid the Weierstrass t = tan(x/2) substitution (out of scope for IAL P3).
