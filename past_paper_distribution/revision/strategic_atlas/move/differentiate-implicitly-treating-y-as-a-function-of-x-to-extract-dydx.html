Differentiation

Differentiate implicitly treating y as a function of x to extract dy/dx

Family: Implicit Differentiation Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever the curve equation mixes x and y so that solving for y explicitly is awkward or wasteful. Concrete cues: a product like xy = c (rectangular hyperbola), a sum of mixed powers like x² + xy + y² = k, or transcendental mixtures such as 4y² + 3x = 6ye^{-2x} where y cannot be isolated cleanly. Also fires when y IS isolatable (e.g. y = 36/x) but implicit differentiation produces a tidier gradient formula (dy/dx = −y/x) that is faster to evaluate at a given point. Equivalent phrasings: 'find the gradient of the curve at P', 'show that dy/dx = …', 'find the equation of the tangent/normal at point P on the curve f(x,y)=0'.

Motivation — Why this works

Implicit differentiation works because if y is a (locally) differentiable function of x, then any expression involving y is a composite function of x, so d/dx[f(y)] = f'(y)·dy/dx by the chain rule, and products xy require the product rule. This produces a single linear equation in dy/dx that can be solved without ever needing y as the explicit subject. The technique converts a structural relationship between x and y into a gradient formula valid at every point on the curve, which is exactly what tangent/normal questions need.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structure of the equation: are x and y multiplied (xy), is y raised to a power (y²), or does y appear inside a transcendental function (e^y, ln y, sin y)? Any of these signal that y is entangled with x.
  2. Rule out making y the subject: either it is impossible (e.g. 4y² + 3x = 6ye^{-2x}), or it is possible but produces a messier derivative requiring re-substitution (e.g. y = 36/x → dy/dx = −36/x² still needs y back to simplify).
  3. Commit to implicit differentiation because it treats every y-term uniformly via the chain rule and yields dy/dx in terms of both x and y — the cleanest form for substituting a known point P(x₀, y₀).
  4. Bridge to operation: differentiate both sides of the equation with respect to x term-by-term, attaching a dy/dx factor whenever a y is differentiated, and using the product rule wherever x and y multiply.
  5. Tactical decision: collect all dy/dx terms on one side, factorise, then divide to isolate dy/dx. Only then substitute the coordinates of the point of interest.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Apply d/dx to both sides of the curve equation.
  2. For each x-only term: differentiate normally (e.g. d/dx(3x) = 3).
  3. For each y-only term: differentiate w.r.t. y and multiply by dy/dx (e.g. d/dx(4y²) = 8y·dy/dx).
  4. For products of x and y: apply product rule (e.g. d/dx(xy) = y + x·dy/dx).
  5. For products of f(x) and g(y): combine product and chain rules (e.g. d/dx(6y·e^{-2x}) = 6e^{-2x}·dy/dx − 12y·e^{-2x}).
  6. Collect all terms containing dy/dx on one side, factorise out dy/dx, and divide to obtain dy/dx = (expression in x and y).
  7. Substitute the given point P(x₀, y₀) to evaluate the gradient numerically.

Worked Micro-Example

Curve: $xy = 36$ at $P(4,9)$. Differentiate: $y + x\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$ ⇒ $\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{y}{x}$. At $P$: $m_T = -\frac{9}{4}$, so the tangent has gradient $-\tfrac{9}{4}$ and the normal has gradient $\tfrac{4}{9}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Rectangular hyperbola given parametrically as x = ct, y = c/t
Use dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) = (−c/t²)/c = −1/t². Same gradient as implicit route, just routed through the parameter.
Standard conic such as x² + y² = r² or x² + xy + y² = k
Same procedure: 2x + 2y·dy/dx = 0, etc. Just more terms; the product rule appears wherever xy occurs.
Logarithmic/exponential mix like ln y + xy = c
d/dx(ln y) = (1/y)·dy/dx; the rest follows as usual. Chain rule on every y-function.
Trigonometric in y, e.g. sin(y) + x² = 1
d/dx(sin y) = cos(y)·dy/dx; isolate dy/dx the same way.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating y as a constant when differentiating, so 4y² differentiates to 0 instead of 8y·dy/dx.
  • Seeing xy = 36 and rewriting as y = 36/x first — works, but slower and obscures the symmetric form dy/dx = −y/x.
  • Mistaking the equation for one solvable explicitly when a transcendental coupling (like ye^{-2x}) actually blocks isolation.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the dy/dx factor when differentiating any function of y (the most common error).
  • Forgetting the product rule on xy and writing dy/dx = 0 instead of y + x·dy/dx = 0.
  • Sign error when rearranging — e.g. writing dy/dx = y/x instead of −y/x for xy = c.
  • Failing to collect all dy/dx terms before dividing, leaving dy/dx on both sides.
  • Substituting the point's coordinates before isolating dy/dx, which can hide algebraic errors.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 (implicit differentiation) and reused in Further Pure for conics. No formula-booklet entry is needed — chain rule and product rule are assumed. Avoid invoking partial derivatives or the multivariable implicit function theorem; at IAL level, y is simply a differentiable function of x and we apply the single-variable chain rule.
