Algebra & Functions

Multiply through by the LCD to clear denominators and obtain a polynomial equation

Family: Clearing Denominators Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever the working equation contains one or more fractions with variable or numeric denominators that block direct algebraic manipulation. Visual cues include: a 1/p (or k/p) term sitting alongside polynomial terms after a substitution; two rational expressions set equal across an '=' sign (cross-multiplication setup); or fractional coefficients in an equation when integer coefficients are required. Equivalent phrasings: 'clear the fraction', 'cross-multiply', 'multiply both sides by the common denominator', 'scale to integer coefficients'. The signal is essentially: a polynomial is hiding behind one or more denominators, and you want it exposed.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying every term by the least common denominator (LCD) of all fractions present eliminates the denominators in a single step, converting a rational equation into a polynomial equation that can then be factorised, rearranged into standard quadratic form, or solved via the quadratic formula. The underlying principle is that multiplying both sides of an equation by a nonzero quantity preserves the solution set. When the LCD involves a variable factor (like p or x−a), the student must verify that factor is nonzero on the relevant domain so no spurious roots are introduced and no genuine roots are lost.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structural obstruction: there is at least one fraction in the equation (variable in a denominator, or a fractional coefficient) preventing standard polynomial techniques.
  2. Rule out alternatives: combining fractions over a common denominator and then setting numerators equal works but is slower and more error-prone; isolating the fraction and squaring is wrong here because the issue is division, not a surd.
  3. Commit to multiplying through by the LCD because it is the most direct path to a polynomial in standard form, and a single multiplicative step touches every term uniformly.
  4. Identify the LCD: for a single fractional term like 3/p, the LCD is p; for two fractions a/(x−1) = b/(x−2), it is (x−1)(x−2); for fractional coefficients, it is the LCM of the numeric denominators.
  5. Check the LCD is nonzero on the domain (e.g. p = 3^x > 0 always; or note x ≠ 1, 2 as excluded values to revisit at the end), so the multiplication is valid and reversible.
  6. Multiply EVERY term — including constants and any term that didn't originally contain a fraction — then collect to standard form ax² + bx + c = 0 (or higher-degree analogue).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify all denominators present and form the LCD.
  2. State (briefly) the domain restriction that makes the LCD nonzero.
  3. Multiply each term of the equation by the LCD, distributing carefully.
  4. Cancel matching factors in each term so all denominators disappear.
  5. Expand any resulting products and collect like terms.
  6. Rearrange to standard polynomial form = 0, ready for factorisation or the quadratic formula.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $9p + 26 = \dfrac{3}{p}$ with $p = 3^x > 0$. Multiply every term by $p$: $9p^2 + 26p = 3$. Rearrange: $9p^2 + 26p - 3 = 0$, which factorises as $(9p - 1)(p + 3) = 0$, giving $p = \tfrac{1}{9}$ (rejecting $p = -3$ since $p > 0$).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Single variable denominator after a substitution, e.g. q + 5/q = 6 from 2^x + 5·2^(−x) = 6
Multiply through by q to obtain q² − 6q + 5 = 0; note q > 0 to discard non-positive roots.
Two rational expressions equated, e.g. (2x+3)/(x−1) = (x+3)/(x−2)
Cross-multiply by the product (x−1)(x−2): (2x+3)(x−2) = (x+3)(x−1); record x ≠ 1, 2 as excluded values.
Fractional numeric coefficients, e.g. x² + (3/7)x + 4 = 0 with 'integer coefficients' required
Multiply through by the LCM of denominators (here 7) to give 7x² + 3x + 28 = 0; check gcd of coefficients is 1.
Surd denominator like 1/(√a − √b)
Different family (rationalising), but same spirit: multiply by the conjugate (√a + √b) to clear the surd via difference of squares.

Common Misreadings

  • Pairing each numerator with its own denominator when cross-multiplying, e.g. writing (2x+3)(x−1) = (x+3)(x−2) instead of crossing the products correctly.
  • Treating a fractional coefficient as already acceptable when the question explicitly demands integer coefficients.
  • Mistaking 1/p for a surd and trying to square the equation instead of multiplying by p.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to multiply EVERY term by the LCD — particularly constants or terms that contained no fraction originally.
  • Failing to record the domain restriction (p > 0, or x ≠ excluded values), which is needed to reject extraneous roots later or to justify the multiplication.
  • Sign errors when expanding products like (x−1)(x−2) after cross-multiplying.
  • Not checking gcd of final coefficients when the question asks for the 'simplest' integer-coefficient form.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1–P4) and Further Pure (FP1). No formula booklet entry is invoked; this is a foundational algebraic manipulation. Avoid generalising to multiplying by expressions that may be zero on the domain without explicit justification, as this can introduce extraneous solutions.
