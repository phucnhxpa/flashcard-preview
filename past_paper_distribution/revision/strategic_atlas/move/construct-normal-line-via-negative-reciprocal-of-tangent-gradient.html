Coordinate Geometry

Construct normal line via negative reciprocal of tangent gradient

Family: Line Equation Construction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question asks for the normal to a curve at a given point P, or asks for where the normal meets an axis or another line. Cues include phrases like "the normal at P", "the normal meets the x-axis at N", or "find the equation of the normal". Often appears after an earlier part has already established dy/dx or the tangent gradient at P. Common in triangle-area sub-parts where O, P, and N are vertices.

Motivation — Why this works

The normal at P is by definition perpendicular to the tangent there. For two non-vertical perpendicular lines, m₁·m₂ = −1, so m_normal = −1/m_tangent. Once the gradient and a point are known, the point-gradient form y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) determines the line uniquely. Setting y = 0 (or x = 0) then yields the axis intercept needed for the geometric construction.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question says "normal", not "tangent" — flag that a perpendicular gradient is required.
  2. Rule out reusing m_tangent directly; also rule out re-differentiating, since dy/dx at P is already (or easily) available.
  3. Commit to the recipe: compute m_tangent at P, take −1/m_tangent, then apply point-gradient form at P.
  4. Bridge: evaluate dy/dx at the given x-coordinate of P to get a clean numeric gradient before inverting.
  5. Tactical: keep surds rational (multiply through by √3 etc.), and if an axis intercept is needed, substitute y = 0 immediately rather than rearranging into y = mx + c first.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute dy/dx and evaluate at P to get m_tangent.
  2. Form m_normal = −1/m_tangent and simplify (rationalise surds).
  3. Write normal: y − y₁ = m_normal (x − x₁) with P = (x₁, y₁).
  4. If an intercept is required, set y = 0 (x-intercept) or x = 0 (y-intercept) and solve.
  5. State the resulting point or equation in the form requested.

Worked Micro-Example

At P(2, π/3) on a curve with dy/dx = 1/(2√3) at x = 2: m_normal = −2√3. Normal: $y - \tfrac{\pi}{3} = -2\sqrt{3}(x-2)$. Setting y = 0: $x - 2 = \tfrac{\pi}{6\sqrt{3}} = \tfrac{\pi\sqrt{3}}{18}$, so $N = \left(2 + \tfrac{\pi\sqrt{3}}{18},\, 0\right)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Tangent line at P instead of normal
Use m_tangent directly in y − y₁ = m(x − x₁); skip the negative-reciprocal step.
Curve given implicitly, e.g. x² + xy + y² = 7
Obtain dy/dx by implicit differentiation, evaluate at P, then proceed with −1/m_tangent.
Curve given parametrically
Use dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) at the parameter value of P; remainder unchanged.
Horizontal tangent (m_tangent = 0)
Normal is the vertical line x = x₁; the negative reciprocal formula does not apply directly.
Vertical tangent (dx/dy = 0)
Normal is horizontal: y = y₁.
Normal meets the curve again or another line
Substitute the normal equation into the curve/line equation and solve simultaneously instead of just setting y = 0.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading "normal" as "tangent" and using m_tangent for the line.
  • Treating "normal" as the line from the origin to P.
  • Assuming the normal passes through the origin, so omitting the −y₁ term in point-gradient form.
  • Confusing "normal at P" with "perpendicular bisector of OP".

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the minus sign when forming −1/m_tangent.
  • Rationalising π/(6√3) incorrectly instead of obtaining π√3/18.
  • Sign slip when solving −y₁ = m_normal(x − x₁): the two negatives should cancel.
  • Leaving the answer as y = mx + c when the question asks for an exact intercept or the form ax + by + c = 0.
  • Failing to handle the special case m_tangent = 0 (vertical normal) or undefined m_tangent (horizontal normal).

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure (P1–P4): differentiation of standard functions, implicit/parametric differentiation, and straight-line geometry using m₁m₂ = −1. No methods beyond syllabus are required.
