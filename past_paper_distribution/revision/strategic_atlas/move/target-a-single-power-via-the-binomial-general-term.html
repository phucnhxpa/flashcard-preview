Binomial Expansion

Target a single power via the binomial general term

Family: General Term Targeting Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question references one specific term or coefficient in an expansion of $(p+qx)^n$ — phrases like 'the coefficient of $x^k$', 'the term in $x^k$ is ...', or 'find the value of $a$ such that ...$x^k$ has coefficient ...'. Also triggers when only a numeric constant or a single power is needed and a full expansion would be wasteful. Common surface: $(3+ax)^6$ has an $x^4$ term equal to $540x^4$.

Motivation — Why this works

The general term $T_{r+1} = \binom{n}{r} X^{n-r} Y^r$ parameterises every term in the expansion by one index $r$. Because the power of $x$ in each term is a known linear function of $r$, fixing the desired power determines $r$ uniquely, isolating exactly one term. This turns 'expand and inspect' into 'solve for $r$, then evaluate one coefficient', which is faster, less error-prone, and scales to large $n$.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question asks about a single power of $x$, not the whole expansion.
  2. Rule out full expansion (7 terms here) as unnecessary work and a source of arithmetic slips.
  3. Commit to the general-term formula because the power of $x$ in $T_{r+1}$ is determined by $r$ alone.
  4. Bridge: write $T_{r+1} = \binom{6}{r} 3^{6-r} (ax)^r$ and set the exponent of $x$ equal to the target $4$, giving $r=4$.
  5. Tactically separate the numerical part $\binom{6}{4}\cdot 3^{2}$ from the unknown part $a^4$, then equate the coefficient to $540$ and solve.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify $X$, $Y$, $n$ in $(X+Y)^n$: here $X=3$, $Y=ax$, $n=6$.
  2. Write the general term $T_{r+1} = \binom{n}{r} X^{n-r} Y^r = \binom{6}{r} 3^{6-r} a^r x^r$.
  3. Match the power of $x$: set $r$ equal to the target exponent (here $r=4$).
  4. Evaluate the numerical pieces: $\binom{6}{4}=15$, $3^{2}=9$.
  5. Form the targeted term: $15 \cdot 9 \cdot a^4 x^4 = 135 a^4 x^4$.
  6. Equate the coefficient to the given value: $135 a^4 = 540$, so $a^4 = 4$ and $a = \pm\sqrt{2}$ (take the value consistent with any stated sign condition).

Worked Micro-Example

Find $a$ such that the term in $x^4$ in $(3+ax)^6$ equals $540 x^4$. General term: $T_{r+1}=\binom{6}{r}3^{6-r}(ax)^r$. Set $r=4$: $T_5=\binom{6}{4}3^{2}a^4 x^4 = 15\cdot 9\cdot a^4 x^4 = 135 a^4 x^4$. Equate: $135 a^4 = 540 \Rightarrow a^4 = 4 \Rightarrow a = \pm\sqrt{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Coefficient of $x^k$ in $(p+qx)^n$ with positive integer $n$
Set $r=k$ in $T_{r+1}=\binom{n}{r}p^{n-r}q^r x^r$ and read off the coefficient.
Coefficient of $x^k$ in $(a+bx^m)^n$ where the variable carries a power
Power of $x$ is $mr$; solve $mr=k$ for $r$ (must be a non-negative integer, else coefficient is $0$).
Coefficient of $x^k$ in a product like $(1+cx)(p+qx)^n$
Use the general term to pick out the $x^k$ and $x^{k-1}$ terms of $(p+qx)^n$, then combine after multiplying by $(1+cx)$.
Infinite binomial series $(1+x)^n$ for $n$ negative or fractional (FP / Pure)
Same formula $T_{r+1}=\binom{n}{r}x^r$ with generalised $\binom{n}{r}=\dfrac{n(n-1)\cdots(n-r+1)}{r!}$; valid only for $|x|<1$.
Independent (constant) term in $(ax^p + bx^{-q})^n$
Power of $x$ is $pr - q(n-r)$; set this to $0$ and solve for $r$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $Y$ as $a$ rather than $ax$, so the $a^r$ factor is dropped.
  • Reading 'the term in $x^4$' as the 4th term and using $r=3$ instead of matching the power of $x$.
  • Assuming the coefficient is just $\binom{n}{r}$ and forgetting the $p^{n-r} q^r$ factor.
  • When the term involves $x^m$ with $m\neq 1$, equating $r$ (not $mr$) to the target power.

Pitfalls

  • Index slip: confusing $T_{r+1}$ with 'the $(r+1)$th term' and setting $r+1=k$ instead of $r=k$.
  • Sign loss when $Y=-bx$: forgetting that $(-b)^r$ alternates in sign.
  • Arithmetic slip in $\binom{n}{r}$ or in $p^{n-r}$ when $p\neq 1$.
  • Forgetting to consider $a$ negative: $a^4=4$ gives $a=\pm\sqrt{2}$ unless the question fixes the sign.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (binomial expansion for positive integer $n$); the negative/fractional $n$ variant belongs to the binomial series topic and requires $|x|<1$ for validity.
