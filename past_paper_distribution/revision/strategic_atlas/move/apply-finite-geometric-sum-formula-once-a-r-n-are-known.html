Sequences & Series

Apply finite geometric sum formula once a, r, n are known

Family: Direct Formula Application Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a geometric sequence has been fully identified — first term a, common ratio r, and a finite term count n are all known — and the question asks for the sum of the first n terms (S_n). Typical phrasings: 'find the sum of the first 4 terms', 'evaluate S_n', or 'total after n terms'. The move is a direct substitution into S_n = a(1 − r^n)/(1 − r), with no need for further setup beyond careful arithmetic.

Motivation — Why this works

The closed form S_n = a(1 − r^n)/(1 − r) is derived from the telescoping identity (1 − r)S_n = a(1 − r^n), valid for any r ≠ 1. Once a, r, n are pinned down, the sum is a single substitution; the only real work is arithmetic on r^n and the denominator. Choosing the (1 − r^n)/(1 − r) form when |r| < 1 keeps both numerator and denominator positive-leaning and reduces sign-handling errors.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question asks for a finite sum S_n and I already have a, r, and n in hand — so the closed form is the direct route.
  2. I rule out term-by-term addition (slow and error-prone) and rule out the infinite sum formula (n is finite here).
  3. I commit to S_n = a(1 − r^n)/(1 − r) because |r| < 1 makes this form the cleanest; the alternative a(r^n − 1)/(r − 1) is algebraically equivalent but invites sign slips.
  4. First concrete step: compute r^n carefully, watching the parity of n when r is negative.
  5. Tactically, I simplify 1 − r^n and 1 − r as fractions before combining, and convert division by a fraction into multiplication by its reciprocal.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify a, r, n from the problem (often via ar = …, ar^2 = …, etc.).
  2. Compute r^n, paying attention to sign when r < 0 and n is even/odd.
  3. Form the numerator a(1 − r^n) and denominator (1 − r) as exact fractions.
  4. Substitute into S_n = a(1 − r^n)/(1 − r).
  5. Simplify by inverting the denominator fraction and multiplying; cancel common factors before multiplying out.

Worked Micro-Example

Given a = 30 and r = −1/5, find S_4. Compute r^4 = (−1/5)^4 = 1/625. Then S_4 = 30(1 − 1/625)/(1 − (−1/5)) = 30 · (624/625) / (6/5) = 30 · (624/625) · (5/6) = 624/25 = 24.96.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Remainder after n terms when |r| < 1
Compute S_∞ − S_n = a r^n /(1 − r); equivalently the tail of the geometric series.
r > 1 (growing sequence)
Prefer the form S_n = a(r^n − 1)/(r − 1) so both top and bottom are positive, avoiding double negatives.
n unknown, S_n given
Substitute as before, then solve a(1 − r^n)/(1 − r) = S_n for r^n and take logs to extract n.
Sum starting from term k, not term 1
Use S_n − S_{k−1}, or factor: sum from term k of length m equals a r^{k−1}(1 − r^m)/(1 − r).

Common Misreadings

  • Treating a finite sum question as an infinite sum and using a/(1 − r) instead of a(1 − r^n)/(1 − r).
  • Mistaking the term count: using n = number-of-the-last-term vs. number-of-terms inconsistently.
  • Reading 'sum to the nth term' as 'value of the nth term' (confusing S_n with u_n).

Pitfalls

  • Computing (−1/5)^4 as −1/625 (forgetting an even power kills the sign).
  • Errors when dividing by 6/5 — forgetting to invert and multiply.
  • Mixing the two formula variants and ending with an overall sign error.
  • Arithmetic slips when combining 1 − r^n over a common denominator (e.g., 1 − 1/625 → 624/625).

A-level Scope Notes

This is core Edexcel IAL Pure (P2) material on geometric series; the formula S_n = a(1 − r^n)/(1 − r) is given in the formula booklet and applies whenever r ≠ 1.
