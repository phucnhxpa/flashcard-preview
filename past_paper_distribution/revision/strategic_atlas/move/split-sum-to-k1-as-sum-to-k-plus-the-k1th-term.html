Strategic Atlas / Proof / Induction Summation Split
Proof

Split sum to k+1 as sum to k plus the (k+1)th term

Family: Induction Summation Split Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in the inductive step of a proof by induction on a summation identity ∑_{r=1}^{n} f(r) = g(n). After assuming P(k), you must show P(k+1), i.e. that ∑_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r) equals g(k+1). Cue phrases: 'assume true for n = k', 'prove true for n = k+1', upper limit increasing by one. Whenever the LHS of the target involves a sum whose upper limit shifts from k to k+1, this split is the standard first move.

Motivation — Why this works

The inductive hypothesis only gives a closed form for ∑_{r=1}^{k} f(r); it says nothing directly about ∑_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r). Peeling off the last term — ∑_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r) = ∑_{r=1}^{k} f(r) + f(k+1) — creates a slot where the hypothesis can be substituted, converting the problem into routine algebra. This works because finite sums are associative: appending one term to a sum of k terms gives a sum of k+1 terms. Once the substitution is made, the goal reduces to showing g(k) + f(k+1) = g(k+1).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the inductive step asks about a sum whose upper limit is k+1, while my hypothesis is phrased in terms of a sum to k.
  2. I rule out re-deriving the formula from scratch or trying to manipulate the closed form g(k+1) directly — induction requires me to use P(k).
  3. I commit to splitting the sum because it is the unique manoeuvre that creates an occurrence of ∑_{r=1}^{k} f(r), which the hypothesis can replace.
  4. Concretely, I write ∑_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r) = ∑_{r=1}^{k} f(r) + f(k+1), being careful to evaluate f at r = k+1.
  5. After substituting g(k) for the first piece, my remaining task is purely algebraic: factor and rearrange to match g(k+1).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. State that you are considering ∑_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r) (the LHS of P(k+1)).
  2. Split: ∑_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r) = ∑_{r=1}^{k} f(r) + f(k+1).
  3. Substitute the inductive hypothesis ∑_{r=1}^{k} f(r) = g(k) into the first term.
  4. Simplify g(k) + f(k+1), typically by taking out a common factor, to obtain g(k+1).
  5. Conclude that P(k+1) holds whenever P(k) does.

Worked Micro-Example

Prove ∑_{r=1}^{n} r(r+1) = n(n+1)(n+2)/3. Assume ∑_{r=1}^{k} r(r+1) = k(k+1)(k+2)/3. Then ∑_{r=1}^{k+1} r(r+1) = ∑_{r=1}^{k} r(r+1) + (k+1)(k+2) = k(k+1)(k+2)/3 + (k+1)(k+2) = (k+1)(k+2)[k/3 + 1] = (k+1)(k+2)(k+3)/3, which is the required form for n = k+1.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Induction on a product identity, e.g. ∏_{r=1}^{n} (1 + 1/r)
Split multiplicatively: ∏_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r) = (∏_{r=1}^{k} f(r)) · f(k+1), then substitute the hypothesis.
Induction on a sum starting at r = 0 or r = 2 instead of r = 1
The split is identical; just preserve the lower limit when separating off the (k+1)th term.
Matrix induction, e.g. proving M^n has a given form
Analogue is M^{k+1} = M^k · M; use the hypothesis for M^k and multiply by one more copy of M.
Divisibility induction with a recurrence-defined sequence
Replace 'split the sum' by 'use the recurrence to express the (k+1)th term via earlier terms', then apply the hypothesis.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating P(k+1) as something to prove from scratch and never invoking P(k).
  • Confusing the split with re-indexing — students sometimes shift the dummy variable instead of peeling off the last term.
  • Believing the split itself completes the inductive step, without performing the algebraic simplification to reach g(k+1).

Pitfalls

  • Evaluating f(k+1) incorrectly, e.g. writing (k+1)(k+1) instead of (k+1)(k+2) for f(r) = r(r+1).
  • Dropping the +f(k+1) term while substituting the hypothesis.
  • Failing to factor (k+1) (or (k+2)) cleanly, leaving an expanded polynomial that does not visibly match g(k+1).
  • Forgetting to write the final concluding sentence linking P(k) ⇒ P(k+1).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard technique in Edexcel IAL Further Pure proof-by-induction questions on summation formulae (FP1); the multiplicative and matrix variants also appear within FP scope.
