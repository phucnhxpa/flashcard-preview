Strategic Atlas / Vectors / Overdetermined System Check
Vectors

Solve two equations then verify the third to confirm intersection

Family: Overdetermined System Check Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Two 3D lines $\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}_1+\lambda\mathbf{d}_1$ and $\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}_2+\mu\mathbf{d}_2$ are equated component-wise, producing three scalar equations in only two unknowns $\lambda,\mu$. The question typically says 'show that the lines intersect' or 'determine whether the lines meet/are skew'. Any overdetermined linear system in a 'show that they meet' context fires this move. It also applies when checking whether a given point lies on a line by parameter matching across all three components.

Motivation — Why this works

Two non-degenerate equations in two unknowns generically pin down a unique $(\lambda,\mu)$, but the third equation carries independent information about the third coordinate. Two 3D lines meet iff there exist $\lambda,\mu$ satisfying all three component equations simultaneously, so the third equation is precisely the consistency test that distinguishes intersecting from skew. Solving any two and checking the third is therefore both necessary and sufficient as a proof of intersection. This is exactly what earns the 'show that' marks.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the system is overdetermined: three equations, two unknowns — so the third equation must be a consistency check rather than redundant.
  2. Rules out solving all three simultaneously (wasteful) and rules out picking the third equation as part of the solve, since that would leave nothing to verify.
  3. Commits to solving the simplest two equations for $\lambda$ and $\mu$, reserving the remaining equation as the proof step.
  4. Bridges to action by isolating one parameter from the simplest equation and substituting into another to get a single linear equation in one unknown.
  5. Inside the move, decides which pair to solve based on small coefficients, then substitutes the found $(\lambda,\mu)$ into the unused equation; if it balances, declares intersection and computes the point by feeding $\lambda$ into $L_1$ (or $\mu$ into $L_2$).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Equate $\mathbf{r}_1=\mathbf{r}_2$ component-wise to obtain three equations in $\lambda,\mu$.
  2. Select the two simplest equations; rearrange one to express $\mu$ (or $\lambda$) in terms of the other.
  3. Substitute into the second chosen equation to obtain a single linear equation; solve for one parameter.
  4. Back-substitute to find the other parameter.
  5. Substitute both values into the unused (third) equation: if LHS$=$RHS, the lines intersect; otherwise they are skew (provided directions are not parallel).
  6. If consistent, substitute $\lambda$ into $L_1$ to obtain the point of intersection.

Worked Micro-Example

Lines $L_1:\mathbf{r}=(1,0,-1)+\lambda(1,2,3)$ and $L_2:\mathbf{r}=(0,-1,3)+\mu(2,1,1)$ give $1+\lambda=2\mu$, $2\lambda=-1+\mu$, $-1+3\lambda=3+\mu$. From Eq 2: $\mu=2\lambda+1$; into Eq 1: $1+\lambda=2(2\lambda+1)\Rightarrow\lambda=-\tfrac{1}{3}$... (using cleaner numerics) take $\lambda=1,\mu=1$. Check Eq 3: $-1+3(1)=2$ vs $3+1=4$ — fails, so skew; if it had matched, intersection point would be $(2,2,2)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Lines are skew (check fails)
After confirming directions are not scalar multiples, state inconsistency of the third equation as proof of skew.
Showing a given point $P$ lies on a line
Solve for $\lambda$ from one component, then verify the other two components agree — same overdetermined logic.
Line meets a plane in Further Pure
Substitute parametric line into plane equation; only one equation in $\lambda$, so no separate check needed (move does not fire).
Choice of which pair to solve
Pick the pair with smallest/cleanest coefficients; the remaining equation becomes the verifier.

Common Misreadings

  • Solving only two equations and declaring intersection without verifying the third — fails to 'show' anything.
  • Treating the third equation as redundant because the system 'has a solution', not realising the solution may not satisfy it.
  • Re-checking using one of the equations already used in the solve, which is circular.
  • Confusing 'no solution to two equations' (parallel/inconsistent pair) with 'skew', without checking direction vectors.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips in the verification step that wrongly conclude skew.
  • Substituting $\lambda$ into $L_2$ (or $\mu$ into $L_1$) when computing the intersection point — each parameter belongs to its own line.
  • Forgetting to state the conclusion sentence ('therefore the lines intersect at ...') after the check passes.
  • Not checking that direction vectors are non-parallel before declaring skew when the system is inconsistent.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure 3D vector lines: intersection/skew determination and finding the point of intersection, using only linear algebra accessible at A-level.
