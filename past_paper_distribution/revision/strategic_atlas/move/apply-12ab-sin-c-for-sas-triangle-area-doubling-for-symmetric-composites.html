Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a triangle (or a sub-triangle of a composite figure such as a kite, rhombus, sector–triangle hybrid, or segment problem) presents two known side lengths together with the angle sandwiched between them — the classic SAS configuration. Visual cues: a labelled angle at a vertex with two adjacent sides given; a kite/rhombus split by a diagonal into two congruent SAS triangles; a sector minus an isoceles triangle for a segment area. Equivalent phrasings of the trigger: 'two sides and included angle known', 'no perpendicular height available', 'third side missing or only computable via cosine rule'. If you instead see all three sides (use Heron's) or a base with a perpendicular height (use ½bh), this move is NOT the right tool.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity Area = ½ab sin C is the direct SAS area formula and exists precisely to bypass the need for either a perpendicular height or a third side. It works because the height from the apex onto side a equals b sin C, so ½ × base × height collapses to ½ab sin C. Choosing it preserves the original exact data — no rounding propagates from intermediate cosine-rule or Pythagoras steps — and for symmetric composite shapes (kite, rhombus) doubling one SAS triangle is faster and more accurate than reconstructing diagonals for ½d₁d₂.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the data given at the triangle: two sides and the angle wedged between them — this is SAS, not SSS or RHS.
  2. Rule out ½ × base × height: no perpendicular height is given, and dropping one would require extra trig.
  3. Rule out Heron's formula: the third side is unknown, and computing it via the cosine rule first would be wasted work for an area question.
  4. Rule out ½d₁d₂ for a kite/rhombus when only one diagonal (or none) is naturally available, or when the other diagonal would only come from a rounded intermediate value.
  5. Commit to A = ½ab sin C — it maps one-to-one onto the data already in hand.
  6. If the figure is a kite/rhombus split by a diagonal of symmetry, compute the area of one SAS triangle and double it; if it is a segment, subtract this triangle area from the corresponding sector area.
  7. Tactical check: confirm the angle used is the one BETWEEN the two chosen sides, and set the calculator to the correct angle mode (radians vs degrees) matching the question.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Label the two known sides as a and b, and the included angle as C.
  2. Set calculator to the correct mode: radians if C is given as a pure number (e.g. 0.37, 2), degrees if marked with °.
  3. Evaluate sin C.
  4. Compute A = ½ × a × b × sin C.
  5. If the figure is a symmetric composite (kite, rhombus, two congruent triangles), multiply by 2; if it is a segment, subtract from the sector area ½r²θ.
  6. Round only at the final stage to the requested significant figures.

Worked Micro-Example

Triangle with $a = 0.6$, $b = 1.4$, included angle $C = 2$ rad. Then $A = \tfrac12 (0.6)(1.4)\sin 2 = 0.42 \sin 2 \approx 0.3819\ \text{m}^2$. If this triangle is half of a kite split by its line of symmetry, the kite area is $2 \times 0.3819 \approx 0.7638\ \text{m}^2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Segment of a circle (sector minus triangle)
Use ½r²θ − ½r²sin θ = ½r²(θ − sin θ); the triangle term is ½ab sin C with a = b = r and C = θ.
Kite or rhombus with a known line of symmetry
Split along the symmetry diagonal into two congruent SAS triangles; area = 2 × ½ab sin C, avoiding the need for both diagonals.
General polygon area
Triangulate from a single vertex and sum ½ab sin C over the resulting triangles.
Obtuse included angle
No change — sin C remains positive for 0 < C < π, so the formula is applied identically.

Common Misreadings

  • Mistaking a non-included angle (one of the other two angles of the triangle) for the included angle and applying ½ab sin C anyway.
  • Treating a kite area question as automatically requiring ½d₁d₂ when in fact only one diagonal is naturally accessible.
  • Reading an angle given as a pure number (e.g. 0.37) as degrees rather than radians.

Pitfalls

  • Calculator left in degree mode for a radian-angle problem: sin(0.37°) ≈ 0.00646 instead of sin(0.37 rad) ≈ 0.3616.
  • Forgetting to double the single-triangle area when the full figure is a kite, rhombus, or two-triangle composite.
  • Using a rounded intermediate length (e.g. a diagonal computed earlier) in place of the original exact side, introducing avoidable rounding error.
  • Pairing the wrong vertex angle with the chosen sides — always verify the angle sits between the two sides being multiplied.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics; the formula Area = ½ab sin C appears in the P2 formula booklet under 'Trigonometry'. Stay with planar Euclidean triangles — do not extend to spherical or 3D oblique triangles, which are out of scope.
