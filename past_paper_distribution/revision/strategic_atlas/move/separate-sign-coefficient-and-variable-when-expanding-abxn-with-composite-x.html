Binomial Expansion

Separate sign, coefficient, and variable when expanding (a+bx)^n with composite X

Family: Composite Substitution Bookkeeping Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when applying the generalised binomial expansion (1+X)^n with negative or fractional n, and X = cx/d is itself a signed fraction in x, e.g. X = -5x/2, X = -3x/4, or X = 2x/5. The cue is that each power X^k = (c/d)^k x^k carries its own sign from (-1)^k, while the falling factorial n(n-1)(n-2)... already produces signs of its own. Whenever both n<0 and the coefficient of x in X is negative, sign errors are highly likely without disciplined bookkeeping.

Motivation — Why this works

The generalised binomial coefficient n(n-1)...(n-k+1)/k! and the substitution X^k are independent pieces of the term, so evaluating them separately and only multiplying at the end isolates each potential sign error. For negative n the falling factorial alternates predictably, and for X = -5x/2 the factor (-1)^k from X^k can either reinforce or cancel that alternation. Tracking the two streams in parallel makes it transparent why, e.g., the x^3 term ends up positive: two negatives cancel. This discipline mirrors the structure of the formula itself and keeps work auditable.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Student notices that X is not just x but a signed fraction -5x/2, so every X^k will produce a sign and a power of 5/2.
  2. They rule out substituting X directly into a single combined coefficient, since this risks losing or duplicating signs.
  3. They commit to a three-column scheme: falling factorial / k! on one side, (-5/2)^k x^k on the other, then multiply.
  4. First concrete operation: list n, n-1, n-2 = -2, -3, -4 and powers (-5/2)^1 = -5/2, (-5/2)^2 = 25/4, (-5/2)^3 = -125/8.
  5. Tactically, they keep fractions unsimplified until the final multiply, so common factors with k! are visible and sign cancellations are explicit.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify n and X; here n = -2, X = -5x/2.
  2. For each k, compute the falling-factorial coefficient n(n-1)...(n-k+1)/k! as a signed rational.
  3. Separately compute X^k = (-5/2)^k x^k, recording the sign from (-1)^k explicitly.
  4. Multiply the two pieces to get the term, simplifying signs last.
  5. k=1: (-2)(-5x/2) = 5x.
  6. k=2: [(-2)(-3)/2]·(25x^2/4) = 3·25x^2/4 = 75x^2/4.
  7. k=3: [(-2)(-3)(-4)/6]·(-125x^3/8) = (-4)·(-125x^3/8) = 125x^3/2.

Worked Micro-Example

Expand $(1-5x/2)^{-2}$ up to $x^3$. Falling factorials: $-2,\ (-2)(-3)/2!=3,\ (-2)(-3)(-4)/3!=-4$. Powers of $X$: $-5x/2,\ 25x^2/4,\ -125x^3/8$. Multiplying: $1+5x+\tfrac{75}{4}x^2+\tfrac{125}{2}x^3+\cdots$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Expansion of $(1+3x/4)^{1/2}$
X is positive so no sign alternation from X^k; only the falling factorial 1/2, -1/2, -3/2,... produces signs.
Expansion of $(1-2x/3)^{-1/2}$
Both n<0 fractional and X negative; signs from (-1)^k and from the falling factorial both alternate, often reinforcing to give all-positive terms.
Expansion of $(2-5x)^{-2}$ requiring factorisation first
Factor out 2^{-2} and rewrite as (1-5x/2)^{-2}; then apply the same separated bookkeeping with X=-5x/2.
Finding a specific coefficient (e.g. coefficient of $x^4$)
Only one (k=4) term needs the two-column computation, but the same separation principle prevents sign slips.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating X as simply x and forgetting to raise -5/2 to the k-th power.
  • Believing the sign of each term must alternate because n is negative, ignoring the second sign source from X.
  • Thinking the formula requires (1+X)^n with X already positive, and so dropping the minus from -5x/2.

Pitfalls

  • Losing a minus sign when cubing -5/2, writing +125/8 instead of -125/8.
  • Miscounting the falling factorial: using (n-1) instead of (n-2) for the third factor.
  • Forgetting that (-5x/2)^2 = +25x^2/4, not -25x^2/4.
  • Combining sign and magnitude too early and losing track of which negatives have already been absorbed.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 generalised binomial expansion for rational n with |X|<1; no content beyond standard A-level scope.
