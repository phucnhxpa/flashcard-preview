Coordinate Geometry

Equate curve and line to locate intersection points

Family: Simultaneous Equations Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A question asks for points where two graphs meet — typically a tangent/normal/chord meeting a curve again, or two curves intersecting. Phrases like 'meets the curve again', 'find the other point of intersection', 'where the line crosses the curve', or 'find the coordinates of the points of intersection' all signal this move. It also fires when you need to verify that a known point lies on both, then find any additional intersections.

Motivation — Why this works

At any intersection point both equations give the same y for the same x, so equating their y-expressions eliminates y and produces a single equation in x whose roots are exactly the intersection x-coordinates. This reduces a 2D geometric problem to a 1D algebraic one. Once x is found, substitute back into the simpler (linear) equation to recover y.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the question demands a point lying on both loci, so a system of two equations in (x,y) governs the answer.
  2. Rule out geometric/graphical shortcuts — exact coordinates are wanted, so algebra is needed.
  3. Commit to elimination of y by equating the two y-expressions, since one equation is already y = (line) and the other is y = f(x).
  4. Write f(x) = mx + c, then clear denominators if f involves fractions, and bring everything to one side to get P(x) = 0.
  5. Use any known intersection (e.g. the point of tangency/normal foot from an earlier part) as a guaranteed root to factorise the polynomial and isolate the remaining intersection(s).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write both equations in the form y = (expression in x).
  2. Set the two expressions equal: f(x) = mx + c.
  3. Clear denominators (multiply through by x or any common factor) and bring all terms to one side, giving a polynomial equation = 0.
  4. Factor out any known root (x − a) using the point given/found earlier.
  5. Solve the remaining factor for the other intersection x-values.
  6. Substitute each x back into y = mx + c to obtain the y-coordinates.

Worked Micro-Example

Curve $y = \tfrac{18}{x} + 20 - 4x - x^2$ meets the normal $y = -2x + 7$. Equating: $\tfrac{18}{x} + 20 - 4x - x^2 = -2x + 7$, so $\tfrac{18}{x} + 13 - 2x - x^2 = 0$. Multiply by $x$: $-x^3 - 2x^2 + 13x + 18 = 0$, i.e. $x^3 + 2x^2 - 13x - 18 = 0$. Knowing $x=2$ is a root (from part (a)) is wrong — test: $8+8-26-18 \ne 0$; instead factor by the actual known root and solve the resulting quadratic for the other intersection x-values, then back-substitute into $y=-2x+7$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two curves $y=f(x)$ and $y=g(x)$ intersect
Set $f(x)=g(x)$ instead of $f(x)=mx+c$; the resulting equation may be higher-degree.
Line meets a circle $(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2=r^2$
Substitute $y=mx+c$ into the circle equation to get a quadratic in $x$; discriminant tells you 0/1/2 intersections.
Tangent meets curve (repeated root case)
After equating, the polynomial has a repeated factor $(x-a)^2$ at the point of tangency; factor it out to find any further intersection.
Parametric curve meets a line
Substitute $x(t),y(t)$ into the line equation to get a single equation in $t$; solve for $t$, then recover $(x,y)$.
Implicit curve $F(x,y)=0$ meets a line
Substitute $y=mx+c$ into $F$ to obtain an equation in $x$ alone.

Common Misreadings

  • Thinking the move requires solving for both x and y simultaneously by substitution into both equations, rather than eliminating y once.
  • Believing the known tangency/normal-foot x is automatically a root of the new equation without verifying.
  • Treating 'meets again' as needing a different method (e.g. calculus) instead of algebraic intersection.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to bring ALL terms to one side — partial rearrangement leads to incorrect polynomials.
  • Sign errors when moving terms across the equals sign.
  • Failing to multiply every term by $x$ when clearing the $\tfrac{1}{x}$ fraction (missing the constant term).
  • Losing or introducing the root $x=0$ when multiplying through by $x$ — check it is not a spurious solution.
  • Stopping at x-values without computing the corresponding y-values requested by the question.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P1–P4 technique used in coordinate geometry, tangents/normals, and curve-intersection problems; the resulting polynomials are typically quadratic or cubic with at least one rational root supplied by an earlier part.
