Binomial Expansion

Treat composite second term as single unit in binomial expansion

Family: Composite Term Identification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The expression has the form $(a+bx)^n$ or more generally $(a+B)^n$ where the second term $B$ is a product (e.g. $px$, $2x$, $-\tfrac{x}{3}$, $kx^2$) combining a coefficient/parameter with a power of $x$. The cue is a non-trivial second slot that must be raised as a whole to power $r$. Common phrasings: 'find the first three terms in ascending powers of $x$', 'expand in ascending powers of $x$ up to and including $x^k$'. Fires whenever $b$ is not a bare $x$.

Motivation — Why this works

The binomial theorem $(a+b)^n=\sum\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r$ requires exactly two identified terms; whatever sits in the second slot is raised to the power $r$ as a unit. Bundling the coefficient with $x$ ensures $(bx)^r=b^r x^r$, so the coefficient (or parameter) is correctly raised alongside $x$. This preserves parametric structure for any follow-up part that links coefficients of $x$ and $x^2$. Splitting them loses powers of the coefficient and corrupts every subsequent term.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the expression matches $(a+b)^n$ with $a$ a constant and $b$ a product like $px$, not just $x$.
  2. Rule out expanding only powers of $x$ while leaving the coefficient $p$ unraised — that would break the binomial identity.
  3. Commit to setting $b=px$ as one indivisible unit so that $(px)^r=p^r x^r$ is forced by the formula.
  4. Bridge to writing the general term $\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}(px)^r$ and evaluating for the required values of $r$ (typically $r=0,1,2$).
  5. Inside each term, evaluate $a^{n-r}$, $\binom{n}{r}$, and $p^r$ as separate numerical/parametric factors before combining.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify $a$, $b$, and $n$; explicitly write $b=px$ (or whatever the composite term is).
  2. Write the general term $T_{r+1}=\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}(px)^r=\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}p^r x^r$.
  3. Substitute $r=0,1,2,\ldots$ up to the required power of $x$.
  4. Evaluate $\binom{n}{r}$, $a^{n-r}$, and $p^r$ separately, then multiply.
  5. Collect the result as a polynomial in $x$ with coefficients expressed in $p$.

Worked Micro-Example

Expand $(2+px)^6$ in ascending powers of $x$ up to $x^2$. With $a=2$, $b=px$, $n=6$: $r=0$ gives $\binom{6}{0}2^6=64$; $r=1$ gives $\binom{6}{1}2^5(px)=6\cdot32\cdot px=192px$; $r=2$ gives $\binom{6}{2}2^4(px)^2=15\cdot16\cdot p^2 x^2=240p^2x^2$. So $(2+px)^6\approx 64+192px+240p^2x^2+\cdots$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$(3+2x)^5$ with numeric composite second term
Set $b=2x$; then $(2x)^r=2^r x^r$, so the $x^r$ coefficient carries an extra factor $2^r$.
$(1-\tfrac{x}{3})^8$ with negative fractional composite
Set $b=-\tfrac{x}{3}$; signs alternate via $(-1)^r$ and denominators $3^r$ accumulate.
$(2+kx^2)^n$ where $b$ contains a higher power of $x$
Set $b=kx^2$; then $(kx^2)^r=k^r x^{2r}$, so only even powers of $x$ appear and indexing of 'first three terms' shifts.
$(1+ax)^n(1+bx)^m$ product expansions
Apply the move to each bracket separately, keeping $ax$ and $bx$ bundled, then multiply and collect like powers of $x$.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading the second term as just $x$ and treating $p$ as an external multiplier, giving $240px^2$ instead of $240p^2x^2$.
  • Thinking 'ascending powers of $x$' means ignore $p$ when collecting coefficients, leading to dropped powers of $p$.
  • Confusing $(px)^r$ with $p(x)^r$ — i.e. distributing the exponent only to $x$.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to raise the parameter/coefficient to the power $r$: writing $240px^2$ instead of $240p^2x^2$.
  • Miscomputing $\binom{6}{2}=15$ (slips like 12, 20, or 30).
  • Sign errors when $b$ is negative — failing to track $(-1)^r$.
  • Mixing up $a^{n-r}$ with $a^r$ when reading the formula in haste.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P2/C2 binomial expansion of $(a+b)^n$ with positive integer $n$; identification of composite second terms is core technique and links directly to subsequent 'find $p$ given coefficient relation' subparts.
