Sequences & Series

Expand cubic product into r³ − r and apply standard sum formulae

Family: Summand Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The summand is a product of three linear factors in r whose expansion produces only powers of r that appear in the formula book (Σr, Σr², Σr³). Typical surfaces include r(r+1)(r−1), r(r−1)(r+1), or any product reducible via difference of squares to r³ − r. The question often has an earlier part establishing Σr³ or Σr², signalling that a decomposition route is expected.

Motivation — Why this works

Because (r+1)(r−1) = r² − 1, the product collapses to r³ − r, which is a linear combination of standard power sums. Σr and Σr³ both have closed forms in the Edexcel formula book, so splitting the sum reduces the problem to substitution and algebraic tidying. This avoids more elaborate techniques (telescoping, induction) when the polynomial structure already lines up with bookwork results.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the summand is a polynomial product in r — three linear factors, no fractions, no telescoping shape.
  2. I rule out method of differences and partial fractions: there's nothing to cancel and no denominator to split.
  3. I commit to expansion because (r+1)(r−1) is a difference of squares, so the product simplifies cleanly to r³ − r.
  4. First concrete operation: rewrite r(r+1)(r−1) = r(r²−1) = r³ − r, then split the sum termwise.
  5. Tactically, I'll quote Σr³ = ¼n²(n+1)² and Σr = ½n(n+1), then factor out ½n(n+1) from the difference to reach the target factorised form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Recognise (r+1)(r−1) = r² − 1 via difference of squares.
  2. Expand: r(r+1)(r−1) = r³ − r.
  3. Split the sum: Σ_{r=1}^{n}(r³ − r) = Σr³ − Σr.
  4. Substitute formulae: ¼n²(n+1)² − ½n(n+1).
  5. Factor out ½n(n+1) and simplify to the requested closed form.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\sum_{r=1}^{n} r(r+1)(r-1)$. Expand: $r(r+1)(r-1) = r(r^2-1) = r^3 - r$. So $\sum_{r=1}^{n}(r^3-r) = \tfrac{1}{4}n^2(n+1)^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}n(n+1) = \tfrac{1}{4}n(n+1)\big[n(n+1) - 2\big] = \tfrac{1}{4}n(n+1)(n^2+n-2) = \tfrac{1}{4}n(n+1)(n+2)(n-1).$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Summand r(r+1)(r+2)
Expand to r³ + 3r² + 2r and use Σr³, Σr², Σr; or telescope using ¼[r(r+1)(r+2)(r+3) − (r−1)r(r+1)(r+2)].
Summand r(r−1)(r−2)
Expand to r³ − 3r² + 2r and combine three standard sums; alternatively recognise as 6·C(r,3) for a combinatorial shortcut.
Summand r²(r+1) or r(r+1)²
Expand to r³ + r² and split into Σr³ + Σr² — same decomposition philosophy, two terms instead of two-with-subtraction.
Summand (2r−1)(2r+1) = 4r² − 1
Difference-of-squares trick again, but now produces Σr² and a constant sum n.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying partial fractions on r(r+1)(r−1) — it's a polynomial, not a rational function.
  • Treating it as a telescoping problem and inventing artificial differences when straightforward expansion suffices.
  • Missing the difference-of-squares structure and laboriously expanding (r+1)(r−1) as r² + r − r − 1 with arithmetic risk.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error: writing (r+1)(r−1) = r² + 1 instead of r² − 1.
  • Forgetting the minus sign when splitting Σ(r³ − r) into Σr³ − Σr.
  • Failing to factor ½n(n+1) at the end, leaving the answer in unfactorised form when the question demands a product.
  • Mis-quoting Σr³ as ¼n(n+1)² or similar typo from the formula book.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP1) — uses only Σr and Σr³ from the formula book and standard polynomial expansion. No generating functions or advanced techniques required.
