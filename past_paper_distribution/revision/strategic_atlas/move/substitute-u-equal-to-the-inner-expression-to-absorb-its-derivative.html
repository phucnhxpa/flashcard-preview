Integration

Substitute u equal to the inner expression to absorb its derivative

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the integrand contains a composite block raised to a power (e.g. (x+1)³, (1+ln x)², (1+sin²x)³, (1+eˣ)²) AND the rest of the integrand either (a) contributes the exact derivative of that inner block as a factor, or (b) is a trivial linear expression whose derivative is constant. Visual cues: a 'lump' f(x) appearing inside a power in the denominator or under a root, alongside f'(x) elsewhere in the integrand, or simply a linear lump (ax+b)ⁿ. Equivalent phrasings: 'reverse chain rule', 'spot du/dx already there', 'collapse the inner bracket'.

Motivation — Why this works

Setting u equal to the inner expression turns the composite (f(x))ⁿ into uⁿ, while du = f'(x)dx absorbs the matching factor in the integrand (or is just dx scaled by a constant when f is linear). This is the reverse chain rule: it dismantles the nested structure and produces an integral in u that is purely algebraic — a power, polynomial, or simple rational function — which can then be integrated termwise. The substitution succeeds precisely when every remaining x can be expressed in u (e.g. x = u−1) with no leftover transcendental residue.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the dominant structural complexity: an inner expression f(x) appearing inside a power, root, or exponential — this is the 'lump' that obstructs direct integration.
  2. Check whether f'(x) appears (up to constant) elsewhere in the integrand. If yes, the substitution is forced; if f is linear, f'(x) is just a constant and dx → du/a trivially.
  3. Rule out alternatives: targeting the numerator (e.g. u = x² or u = ln x alone) typically makes the inner power messier; partial fractions on a repeated linear denominator works but is more laborious; integration by parts adds layers rather than removing them.
  4. Commit to u = f(x) (or u = 1 + f(x) if a constant shift makes the denominator/bracket exactly uⁿ). Verify that every remaining x in the integrand can be rewritten in u — this is the cleanliness check.
  5. Convert dx via du = f'(x)dx and rewrite any leftover x using the inverse relation x = g(u). Convert limits if the integral is definite, mapping x-endpoints directly to u-endpoints.
  6. Once the integral is a rational/polynomial function of u, split into separate u-powers term by term for integration.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the inner block f(x) and set u = f(x) (or u = 1 + f(x) to match the bracket exactly).
  2. Differentiate: compute du/dx = f'(x), so du = f'(x)dx (rearrange dx if needed).
  3. Rewrite every x-dependent piece in terms of u: powers of f(x) become powers of u; isolated x's become g(u) via the inverse relation.
  4. For definite integrals, convert the limits: x = a → u = f(a), x = b → u = f(b).
  5. Assemble the new integrand entirely in u and du, confirming no x remains.
  6. Split the resulting expression (e.g. (u−1)²/u³ = 1/u − 2/u² + 1/u³) and integrate termwise.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_1^3 \frac{x^2}{(x+1)^3}\,dx$. Let $u = x+1$, so $x = u-1$, $dx = du$, limits $2 \to 4$. Then $\int_2^4 \frac{(u-1)^2}{u^3}\,du = \int_2^4 \left(\frac{1}{u} - \frac{2}{u^2} + \frac{1}{u^3}\right)du$, which integrates directly to $\left[\ln u + \tfrac{2}{u} - \tfrac{1}{2u^2}\right]_2^4$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Linear denominator power, e.g. $\int \frac{2x+3}{(x-1)^4}dx$
Let $u = x-1$, rewrite numerator as $2(u+1)+3 = 2u+5$, then split into $u$-powers.
Linear inner with non-unit coefficient, e.g. $\int (2x+5)^7 dx$
Let $u = 2x+5$; since $du = 2\,dx$, an extra factor of $1/2$ appears — same logic, slight bookkeeping.
Logarithmic composite, e.g. $\int \frac{\ln x}{x(1+\ln x)^2}dx$
Let $u = 1+\ln x$; then $du = (1/x)dx$ absorbs the $1/x$ factor and $\ln x = u-1$.
Trigonometric composite, e.g. $\int \sin x \cos x (1+\sin^2 x)^3 dx$
Let $u = 1+\sin^2 x$; then $du = 2\sin x\cos x\, dx$ absorbs the product.
Exponential composite, e.g. $\int \frac{e^x}{(1+e^x)^2}dx$
Let $u = 1+e^x$; then $du = e^x dx$ absorbs the exponential factor.

Common Misreadings

  • Targeting the numerator (e.g. u = x² or u = ln x) instead of the inner expression of the denominator's power — this leaves the hard structure intact.
  • Choosing u = f(x) when u = 1 + f(x) would match the bracket exactly, producing a messier integrand like $u/(1+u)^2$ instead of $(u-1)/u^2$.
  • Attempting partial fractions on a repeated linear denominator without substitution — valid but unnecessarily laborious.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to convert leftover x's in the numerator (e.g. leaving x² instead of (u−1)²).
  • Forgetting to convert the limits in a definite integral, or converting them in the wrong direction.
  • Failing to verify that no x-dependent residue remains — if f'(x) is absent and f is non-linear, the substitution will leave an unresolved $x = g(u)$ that defeats the purpose.
  • Dropping the $1/a$ factor when u = ax+b with a ≠ 1.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 'Integration by substitution'. Uses the reverse chain rule (formula booklet lists standard integrals; substitution itself is not tabulated). Avoid generalising to trigonometric substitutions involving inverse trig beyond the IAL syllabus, and do not invoke complex-valued substitutions.
