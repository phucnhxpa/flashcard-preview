Algebra & Functions

Substitute u = base^d to bridge fractional-power equation onto an already-solved polynomial

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a 'Hence' part follows a polynomial you have just solved (typically a cubic or quadratic) and presents a new equation whose terms are powers of a single expression in arithmetic progression — e.g. exponents 3/2, 1, 1/2, or 1, 2/3, 1/3, or 3z, 2z, z. Visual cues: identical numerical coefficients to the earlier polynomial, three terms whose exponents are in ratio 3:2:1 (or 2:1 for quadratic), and a common base such as (y+3), y, e^t, sin θ, or 9^z. The 'Hence' wording is the strongest signal that the examiner has engineered the new equation to be the old one in disguise.

Motivation — Why this works

If the exponents of a common base form an arithmetic progression with common difference d, then the substitution u = base^d converts every term into an integer power of u, collapsing the equation into a polynomial. This works because (base^d)^k = base^(kd), so successive AP exponents become u, u^2, u^3, …. The move recycles the prior part's roots wholesale, eliminating fractional/transcendental exponents and avoiding any fresh factorisation. The underlying principle is the index law (a^m)^n = a^{mn}.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the keyword 'Hence' — this mandates reusing part (a)'s polynomial and its roots, so the new equation must be structurally identical.
  2. Read off the exponents of the common base in the new equation and check whether they sit in an arithmetic progression (e.g. 3/2, 1, 1/2 or 1, 2/3, 1/3 or 3z, 2z, z). Compare the numerical coefficients with those of part (a) — they should match exactly.
  3. Rule out direct expansion (e.g. expanding (y+3)^{3/2}) because fractional powers don't expand polynomially, and rule out solving from scratch because that ignores the 'Hence' instruction.
  4. Commit to the substitution u = base^d, where d is the smallest exponent (the common difference of the AP). Then the largest term becomes u^3, the middle u^2, the smallest u — exactly the cubic from part (a).
  5. Import the roots of u from part (a) directly. Then back-substitute carefully, remembering that u may be constrained (e.g. u = (y+3)^{1/2} ≥ 0, or u = e^t > 0), so reject inadmissible roots before solving for the original variable.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the common base B and the smallest exponent d in the new equation.
  2. Set u = B^d. Then the terms B^{3d}, B^{2d}, B^{d} become u^3, u^2, u respectively.
  3. Rewrite the equation entirely in u — it should now be identical to the polynomial from the previous part.
  4. Quote (do not re-derive) the roots u = u_1, u_2, u_3 from part (a).
  5. Discard any roots violating the implicit domain of u (e.g. u ≥ 0 if u is a principal square root; u > 0 if u = e^t or u = 9^z).
  6. Back-substitute: solve B^d = u_i for the original variable (raise to power 1/d, take a logarithm, or invert the trig function as appropriate).

Worked Micro-Example

Given (from part a) that $20x^3 - 50x^2 - 30x = 0$ has roots $x = 0, -\tfrac12, 3$. Solve $20(y+3)^{3/2} - 50(y+3) - 30(y+3)^{1/2} = 0$. Let $u = (y+3)^{1/2}$, so $u \ge 0$ and the equation becomes $20u^3 - 50u^2 - 30u = 0$, giving $u \in \{0, -\tfrac12, 3\}$. Reject $u = -\tfrac12$. Then $y+3 = u^2$ gives $y = -3$ or $y = 6$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Fractional-power base such as (y+3)^{1/2}
Set u = (y+3)^{1/2}; constrain u ≥ 0 (principal square root).
Cube-root powers, e.g. exponents 1, 2/3, 1/3 in y
Set u = y^{1/3}; back-substitute via y = u^3 (no sign restriction since real cube roots are bijective).
Exponential disguise, e.g. e^{3t} - 5e^{2t} + 6e^t = 0
Set u = e^t with u > 0; back-substitute via t = ln u and reject non-positive roots.
Power-of-constant disguise, e.g. terms in 9^{3z}, 9^{2z}, 9^z
Set u = 9^z with u > 0; back-substitute z = log_9 u.
Trigonometric disguise, e.g. 3\sin^3\theta + 26\sin^2\theta - 9\sin\theta = 0
Set u = \sin\theta with -1 ≤ u ≤ 1; reject roots outside this range, then solve \sin\theta = u.
Quadratic-in-disguise, e.g. y^4 + 5y^2 - 6 = 0
Set u = y^2 with u ≥ 0; reject negative u-roots before solving y = ±√u.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to expand fractional powers like (y+3)^{3/2} directly, which is not a polynomial expansion and severs the link to part (a).
  • Choosing the wrong substitution variable, e.g. setting u = y+3 instead of u = (y+3)^{1/2}, which leaves fractional powers of u in the equation.
  • Ignoring the 'Hence' instruction and attempting to factorise the fractional/exponential equation from scratch.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting domain constraints on u (u ≥ 0 for square roots, u > 0 for exponentials, |u| ≤ 1 for sin/cos) and producing spurious solutions.
  • Forgetting to back-substitute — leaving the answer as values of u rather than the original variable.
  • Arithmetic slips when raising to powers during back-substitution, especially with negative roots (e.g. (-9)^3 = -729) or when squaring.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure scope (P1–P4): uses index laws, polynomial factorisation, and standard inverse operations (logs, roots, inverse trig). No formula booklet entry required. Avoid generalising to non-AP exponents — that needs more advanced techniques outside the syllabus.
