Trigonometry

Divide a sin–cos equation by cos to homogenise into tan form

Family: Trig Ratio Conversion Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The current equation contains sin(θ) and cos(θ) of the SAME argument as separate additive terms (e.g. A sin θ + B cos θ = 0, or 2cos(x/2) = ½x sin(x/2)), and the target/required form involves tan θ, arctan, or k = something extracted from a tan equation. Visual cue: a 'show that tan θ + … = 0' or 'show that arctan(…) = …' instruction with no tan visible in the working line. Equivalently: a mixed sin/cos equation where you can see that one division would collapse it into a single trig ratio. The presence of arctan in a target is a strong fingerprint that tan must be manufactured.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity sin θ / cos θ = tan θ is the cleanest bridge from a mixed sin–cos equation to a single-ratio tan equation. Dividing every term by cos θ turns sin θ terms into tan θ and cancels cos θ from any term it multiplies, homogenising the equation in tan. This is the standard 'show that' alignment move: the target form dictates the algebraic operation. The same principle (divide by cosⁿ) generalises to producing sec², or quadratics in tan, when needed.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the target form — it contains tan θ or arctan(…), so a tan ratio must appear in the working.
  2. Inspect the current equation: it has sin(θ) and cos(θ) of the same argument as separate terms, with no tan in sight.
  3. Rule out alternatives: double-angle identities would complicate, not simplify; squaring would introduce extraneous roots; dividing by sin θ would produce cot, moving AWAY from the arctan target.
  4. Commit to dividing every term by cos θ — this is the unique move that converts sin/cos to tan and cancels cos from cos-multiplied terms in one stroke.
  5. Justify the division: check cos θ ≠ 0 in the relevant domain or at the relevant point. If cos θ = 0 would force a contradiction with the original equation (e.g. it would force sin θ = 0 too, violating sin² + cos² = 1), or if the domain restricts θ so cos θ > 0, the division is safe.
  6. Carry out the division term-by-term, keeping signs intact, and read off the resulting tan equation in the form the target demands.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the equation with all terms on one side: A sin θ + B cos θ = 0 (or analogous).
  2. Confirm cos θ ≠ 0 on the domain/at the point of interest, with a one-line justification.
  3. Divide every term by cos θ: (A sin θ)/cos θ + (B cos θ)/cos θ = 0.
  4. Simplify: A tan θ + B = 0 (or, for a product like kx cos θ, the term becomes kx).
  5. Rearrange to the exact target form (e.g. tan θ = −B/A, or tan(x/2) = 4/x, ready for arctan inversion).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\sin 4x + 8x\cos 4x = 0$, divide by $\cos 4x$ (nonzero, since $\cos 4x = 0 \Rightarrow \sin 4x = 0$, contradicting $\sin^2 + \cos^2 = 1$): $\frac{\sin 4x}{\cos 4x} + 8x = 0 \Rightarrow \tan 4x + 8x = 0$, so $k = 8$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Target involves arctan(f(x)) and equation has sin(x/2), cos(x/2)
Divide by cos(x/2); justify cos(x/2) > 0 on the given x-domain (e.g. 0 < x ≤ π gives x/2 ∈ (0, π/2]).
Equation is quadratic in sin and cos (e.g. a sin²θ + b sin θ cos θ + c cos²θ = 0)
Divide by cos²θ to get a quadratic in tan θ: a tan²θ + b tan θ + c = 0.
Integration with mixed sin/cos in numerator/denominator
Divide numerator and denominator by cos²x (or cos x) to rewrite in tan x and sec²x, ready for u = tan x substitution.
Need cot form instead
Divide by sin θ instead — same principle, opposite ratio.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading sin 4x and cos 4x as candidates for a double-angle expansion, instead of recognising the simple division-by-cos route.
  • Treating the equation as needing to be 'solved' numerically, missing that the question only asks to rewrite in tan form.
  • Assuming the move is illegal because cos θ might be zero, without checking the domain or the consequences.

Pitfalls

  • Dividing by cos θ without justifying cos θ ≠ 0 on the relevant domain — markers expect an explicit note.
  • Sign error: rearranging A sin θ = −B cos θ before dividing and losing track of the minus sign.
  • Dividing by sin θ instead of cos θ, producing cot, and moving away from the arctan target.
  • Forgetting to divide EVERY term — leaving a stray cos θ in one term.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P2/P3): uses only the identity tan θ ≡ sin θ / cos θ (formula booklet, trig identities). No out-of-scope generalisations needed; avoid invoking complex-exponential forms of sin/cos which lie outside IAL scope.
