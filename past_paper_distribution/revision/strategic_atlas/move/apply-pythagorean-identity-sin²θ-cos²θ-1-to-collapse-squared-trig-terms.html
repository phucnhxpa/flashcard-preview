Trigonometry

Apply Pythagorean identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 to collapse squared trig terms

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

After expanding a product, applying the quotient rule, combining fractions, or computing a squared distance involving parametric points (a cosθ, b sinθ), the working contains both sin²θ and cos²θ as separate additive terms alongside (or instead of) linear trig terms. The target form is either a constant, a linear expression A cosθ + B sinθ + C, or a perfect square in one trig function — none of which can accommodate the squared trig terms as they stand. Equivalent cues: 'I see sin² and cos² floating around with a coefficient mismatch', 'the printed answer has no squared trig terms but my expansion does', or 'I need to reduce two trig variables to one'.

Motivation — Why this works

The Pythagorean identity sin²θ + cos²θ ≡ 1 is the unique tool that converts two variable-dependent squared terms into a single constant, eliminating one trig function entirely. When the coefficients of sin²θ and cos²θ are equal, direct substitution gives +1; when they differ, rewriting sin²θ = 1 − cos²θ (or vice versa) reduces the expression to a polynomial in one trig function, which often factors as a perfect square. This identity is the algebraic engine behind the focus–directrix property of conics, the R-formula reductions of differentiated quotients, and most 'show that' trig proofs at IAL level.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the expanded expression contains both sin²θ and cos²θ as separate additive terms — a structural red flag for the Pythagorean identity.
  2. Rule out double-angle (cos 2θ = cos²θ − sin²θ), sum-to-product, and half-angle identities: these would introduce new trig functions rather than reduce variables, and the target form has no 2θ terms.
  3. Commit to sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 because it is the only identity that produces a constant from squared trig terms, matching the structure of the target.
  4. Decide on the form: if the coefficients of sin²θ and cos²θ match, factor them out and replace the bracket with 1; if they differ, substitute sin²θ = 1 − cos²θ (choose whichever variable is already 'dominant' in the linear terms) to leave a single-variable polynomial.
  5. After substitution, check whether the resulting quadratic is a perfect square — for ellipse focal distances this is guaranteed by the relation b² = a²(1 − e²), so the discriminant vanishes.
  6. When taking a square root of (A cosθ − B)², determine the sign on the relevant range of θ before writing the simplified expression.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Fully expand the expression so sin²θ and cos²θ appear as separate additive terms (not inside brackets).
  2. Identify the coefficients: if equal, factor out and apply sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 directly; if unequal, write sin²θ = 1 − cos²θ (or cos²θ = 1 − sin²θ).
  3. Substitute and collect like terms to produce either a linear trig expression A cosθ + B sinθ + C or a single-variable quadratic.
  4. If a quadratic results, attempt to factor as a perfect square (a cosθ ± b)².
  5. Take the principal square root with explicit sign analysis on the given range of θ.

Worked Micro-Example

Simplify $PF^2 = (5\cos\theta - 3)^2 + (4\sin\theta)^2$ for $0<\theta<\pi/2$.\\Expand: $25\cos^2\theta - 30\cos\theta + 9 + 16\sin^2\theta$.\\Use $\sin^2\theta = 1-\cos^2\theta$: $25\cos^2\theta - 30\cos\theta + 9 + 16 - 16\cos^2\theta = 9\cos^2\theta - 30\cos\theta + 25 = (3\cos\theta - 5)^2$.\\Hence $PF = 5 - 3\cos\theta$ (since $3\cos\theta < 5$).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Coefficients of sin²θ and cos²θ are equal (e.g. after a quotient-rule expansion gives cos²x + sin²x as adjacent terms)
Replace the pair directly with 1; the numerator collapses to A cos x + B sin x + C and you read off A, B, C.
Coefficients differ (e.g. ellipse focal-distance with a²cos²θ and b²sin²θ)
Substitute sin²θ = 1 − cos²θ to leave a polynomial in cosθ alone; expect a perfect square via b² = a²(1 − e²).
Hyperbolic parametrisation (a coshθ, b sinhθ)
Use cosh²θ − sinh²θ = 1 instead; the same collapse logic applies.
Expression contains 1 + tan²x or 1 + cot²x
Use sec²x = 1 + tan²x or cosec²x = 1 + cot²x — Pythagorean variants serving the same collapsing role.
Reverse direction in integration
Replace 1 with sin²θ + cos²θ to split an integrand into pieces amenable to substitution.

Common Misreadings

  • Failing to spot the identity when sin²θ and cos²θ are separated by other terms in the expression — students often only recognise it when the two terms are adjacent.
  • Reaching for a double-angle identity (cos 2θ = cos²θ − sin²θ) because the squared terms 'look like' a double-angle setup, when in fact the target has no 2θ terms.
  • Trying to use sin 2θ = 2 sinθ cosθ to combine terms when no sinθ cosθ product is present.

Pitfalls

  • Taking √((3cosθ − 5)²) = 3cosθ − 5 without a sign check — on the relevant range this is negative, so the correct root is 5 − 3cosθ; getting the sign wrong flips the final answer (e.g. ratio becomes −e).
  • Misremembering the identity as cos²θ − sin²θ = 1 or sin²θ + cos²θ = 0.
  • Applying the identity before fully expanding, so sin²θ and cos²θ remain trapped inside brackets and never combine.
  • Choosing the wrong substitution direction (eliminating cosθ when sinθ is the dominant variable in the linear terms), leading to an unfactorable quadratic.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 and Further Pure scope. The identity sin²θ + cos²θ ≡ 1 is assumed knowledge (not in the formula booklet); the variants sec²x = 1 + tan²x and cosec²x = 1 + cot²x are also assumed. Avoid generalising to complex-valued or non-real arguments, which is out of scope.
