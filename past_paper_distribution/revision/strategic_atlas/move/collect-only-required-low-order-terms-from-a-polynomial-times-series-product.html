Binomial Expansion

Collect only required low-order terms from a polynomial-times-series product

Family: Targeted Product Collection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a function is presented as a product of a short polynomial (often linear or quadratic) and a binomial expansion, and the question only asks about a specific low-order coefficient or imposes a condition on a few coefficients (e.g. 'constant term equals 3 times coefficient of x'). Cues include phrases like 'find the coefficient of x^n', 'show that...', or a relation between two specific coefficients. There is no need to expand fully — only the targeted x^n contributions matter.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplication of series distributes term-by-term, so the coefficient of x^n in (a_0 + a_1 x + ...)(b_0 + b_1 x + ...) is the convolution sum \sum_{i+j=n} a_i b_j. If only one or two coefficients are needed, only the corresponding convolution slices need to be computed. This saves time, reduces algebra, and minimises arithmetic slips that typically arise when expanding higher unused powers.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the function is a product (linear/quadratic) × (binomial series) and the question only constrains specific low-order coefficients.
  2. Rule out doing a full expansion to x^3 or x^4 — those higher powers are irrelevant to the stated condition.
  3. Commit to the convolution view: coefficient of x^n equals the sum of products a_i b_j with i+j=n.
  4. Bridge to action by first writing out the series terms up to x^n only, then assembling each required coefficient via the convolution rule.
  5. Tactically factor out common surds or constants (e.g. \sqrt{2}) early to keep the algebra clean before forming or solving any equation.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Expand the binomial factor only up to the highest required power x^n.
  2. Identify which coefficients of f(x) the question actually needs (e.g. constant and coefficient of x).
  3. For each required coefficient of x^k, write the convolution: sum of (coefficient from polynomial of x^i) × (coefficient from series of x^{k-i}) for i+j=k.
  4. Simplify each contribution, factoring out common constants/surds.
  5. Substitute into the given condition or quoted value to form an equation in the unknown.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $f(x)=(5-3x)(8-kx)^{1/2}$. Expanding $(8-kx)^{1/2}=2\sqrt{2}-\tfrac{\sqrt{2}\,k}{8}x+\cdots$. Constant of $f$: $5\cdot 2\sqrt{2}=10\sqrt{2}$. Coefficient of $x$: $5\cdot\left(-\tfrac{\sqrt{2}\,k}{8}\right)+(-3)(2\sqrt{2})=\sqrt{2}\left(-\tfrac{5k}{8}-6\right)$. The condition 'constant $=3\times$ coefficient of $x$' gives $10\sqrt{2}=3\sqrt{2}\left(-\tfrac{5k}{8}-6\right)$, solvable for $k$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic × binomial series, asked for coefficient of x^2
Use three convolution contributions: a_0 b_2 + a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_0; expand the series only to x^2.
(1+ax+bx^2) × (1+x)^n with a stated coefficient of x^3
Compute only b_3 + a·b_2 + b·b_1 from the binomial coefficients of (1+x)^n; ignore higher binomial terms.
Two binomial series multiplied, asked for coefficient of x^k
Expand each only up to x^k, then take the convolution sum \sum_{i=0}^{k} a_i b_{k-i}.
Linear × series, asked to 'show the expansion begins ...'
Compute coefficients of x^0, x^1, x^2 only via convolution; do not expand beyond.

Common Misreadings

  • Thinking the coefficient of x in the product comes solely from multiplying the x-term of one factor by the constant of the other, missing the second cross-product.
  • Believing a full expansion is required because both factors are visible, when in fact only a convolution slice is needed.
  • Confusing 'constant term = 3 × coefficient of x' with 'coefficient of x = 3 × constant term', reversing the condition.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the minus sign on the linear factor's x-coefficient (e.g. using +3 instead of -3 from (5-3x)).
  • Errors in the binomial coefficient when the bracket is not in standard form, e.g. forgetting to factor (8-kx)^{1/2}=2\sqrt{2}(1-kx/8)^{1/2} before expanding.
  • Mishandling the surd \sqrt{2} when combining contributions, especially when forming the final equation.
  • Including an x^2 contribution by accident when only constant and x are needed.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 binomial expansion for rational/negative indices, where |x|< validity bound; convolution is used informally as 'collecting like powers'.
