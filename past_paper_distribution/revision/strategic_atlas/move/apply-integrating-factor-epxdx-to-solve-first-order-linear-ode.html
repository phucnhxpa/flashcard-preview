Strategic Atlas / Differential Equations / Integrating Factor Method
Differential Equations

Apply integrating factor e^{∫P(x)dx} to solve first-order linear ODE

Family: Integrating Factor Method Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the ODE is first-order, linear in the dependent variable (say y), and contains both dy/dx and y but no nonlinear couplings like y² or sin(y). Visual cues: an equation of the form a(x)dy/dx + b(x)y = c(x), or already-normalised dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x). Variable separation fails because of the mixed term involving both x and y. Also fires after a Bernoulli substitution v = y^{1-n} reduces a nonlinear ODE to a linear one in v. Constant-coefficient versions (dy/dx + ky = Q(x)) are equally signalled.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying by μ = e^{∫P(x)dx} engineers the LHS into an exact derivative: d/dx[μ y] = μ(dy/dx + P y), which is the product rule run in reverse. This converts the ODE into μ y = ∫ μ Q(x) dx + C, reducing a differential equation to a single integration. The technique works precisely because (d/dx)(e^{∫P dx}) = P · e^{∫P dx}, so the cross-term in the product rule matches the Py term in the ODE. It is the default — and on the IAL syllabus, essentially the only — method for first-order linear ODEs.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation is first-order and linear: y and dy/dx appear, but no y², no e^y, no products like y·dy/dx.
  2. Rule out separation of variables: the term P(x)y mixed with the dy/dx term cannot be split into f(x)dx = g(y)dy form.
  3. Rule out direct integration: the LHS is not yet an exact derivative because the coefficient of dy/dx isn't 1, or the y-coefficient isn't μ'(x)/μ(x) yet.
  4. Commit to the integrating factor method — the standard A-level/IAL tool for this class.
  5. First concrete operation: if the coefficient of dy/dx is not 1, divide the entire equation by it to reach the standard form dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x). Read off P(x) and Q(x).
  6. Compute ∫P(x)dx (no constant needed here — any one antiderivative suffices), then form μ = e^{∫P dx}. Watch for clean simplifications: e^{k ln x} = x^k, e^{kx} stays as is.
  7. Multiply the whole ODE by μ, verify the LHS collapses to d/dx[μ y], then integrate both sides and add the constant C.
  8. Solve algebraically for y, applying any initial/boundary condition to fix C.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Normalise: divide through by the coefficient of dy/dx so the equation reads dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x).
  2. Identify P(x) and Q(x) by inspection of the normalised form.
  3. Compute ∫P(x)dx (omit the integration constant).
  4. Form the integrating factor μ(x) = e^{∫P(x)dx}, simplifying logarithmic exponents to powers where possible.
  5. Multiply every term of the ODE by μ(x); the LHS becomes d/dx[μ(x)·y].
  6. Integrate both sides: μ(x)·y = ∫ μ(x)·Q(x) dx + C.
  7. Divide by μ(x) to isolate y, then apply any given condition to determine C.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\frac{dy}{dx} - \frac{2}{x}y = x^3$. Here $P(x) = -2/x$, so $\int P\,dx = -2\ln x$ and $\mu = e^{-2\ln x} = x^{-2}$. Multiplying: $\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[x^{-2}y\right] = x$. Integrate: $x^{-2}y = \tfrac{1}{2}x^2 + C$, giving $y = \tfrac{1}{2}x^4 + Cx^2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

P(x) = k/x (rational, common after Bernoulli substitution)
Integrating factor simplifies to a power of x: μ = x^k. Most frequent IF form in FP2-style problems.
P(x) constant, say P = k
μ = e^{kx}; identical logic, exponential surface instead of power.
Leading coefficient is a function like (x+1) or e^x
Divide every term (including the RHS) by that coefficient first to reach standard form before reading off P(x).
After Bernoulli substitution v = y^{1-n}
The transformed ODE in v is linear; apply the integrating factor to v, solve for v, then back-substitute to recover y.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading P(x) off the equation before normalising the leading coefficient to 1 — gives the wrong P and wrong μ.
  • Sign errors on P(x): treating dy/dx − (k/x)y as having P = +k/x rather than −k/x, producing the reciprocal IF.
  • Trying to separate variables when a mixed P(x)y term forbids it.
  • Mistaking a nonlinear ODE (containing y², √y, etc.) for a linear one and applying the IF directly without first using a Bernoulli substitution.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to multiply the RHS Q(x) by μ(x) — only multiplying the LHS.
  • Algebraic slip in e^{k ln x}: writing x^{−k} when it should be x^{k} or vice versa.
  • Omitting the constant of integration C after integrating both sides.
  • Forgetting to divide the original RHS by the leading coefficient when normalising.
  • Dropping the absolute value in ln|x| — usually harmless in IAL contexts but conceptually significant.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2) scope; the integrating factor method is the prescribed technique for first-order linear ODEs. The formula μ = e^{∫P dx} is not in the formula booklet — it must be remembered. Avoid invoking exact-equation tests or Laplace transforms, which are out of scope.
