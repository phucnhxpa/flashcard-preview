Integration

Collapse 1+(dy/dx)² into a perfect square to kill the arc-length radical

Family: Arc Length Simplification Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

An arc length integral $L = \int\sqrt{1+(dy/dx)^2}\,dx$ (or surface-area integral $2\pi\int y\sqrt{1+(dy/dx)^2}\,dx$) appears, and the curve $y=f(x)$ contains a sum of two terms with reciprocal/opposite-power structure — typical signatures are $y = \tfrac{x^{n+1}}{a} \pm \tfrac{1}{bx^{n-1}}$, $y$ involving $\cosh$/$\sinh$, or $y$ involving $\ln x$ alongside $x^2$. The hallmark is that after differentiating, $(dy/dx)^2$ produces a 'middle constant' (often $\pm \tfrac{1}{2}$) that, when combined with the $+1$, completes a square. If you spot symmetric power pairs like $x^{2n}$ and $x^{-2n}$ in the squared derivative, this move is almost certainly intended.

Motivation — Why this works

A generic $\sqrt{1+(f'(x))^2}$ is intractable, requiring trig/hyperbolic substitution. FP3 examiners engineer the curve so that $1+(f'(x))^2 = (g(x))^2$ for an elementary $g(x)$, exploiting the algebraic identity $(A+B)^2 = A^2 + 2AB + B^2$ where the cross-term $2AB$ equals exactly $\pm 1$ minus the $-1$ already present. Recognising this design lets the radical collapse to $|g(x)|$, leaving an elementary integrand. The same identity also underlies $1+\sinh^2 x = \cosh^2 x$ for hyperbolic curves.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the integrand is the arc-length form $\sqrt{1+(dy/dx)^2}$, and I see $y$ has a sum-of-two-terms structure with reciprocal powers (or hyperbolic functions) — a strong hint of designer simplification.
  2. I rule out trig/hyperbolic substitution as a first attempt: FP3 questions almost never require integrating an unsimplified $\sqrt{1+u^2}$, so I should test the perfect-square hypothesis first.
  3. I commit to computing $dy/dx$, squaring it, and looking for the pattern $A^2 \pm 2AB + B^2$ after adding 1.
  4. Concretely, after squaring I expect a middle constant like $-\tfrac{1}{2}$; adding 1 turns it into $+\tfrac{1}{2}$, which is the cross-term of $(A+B)^2$ where $A,B$ are the outer power-pair terms.
  5. I take the positive square root, checking that $g(x) > 0$ on the interval (usually obvious since terms are positive powers of positive $x$, or $\cosh$).
  6. I integrate the resulting elementary expression term-by-term.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Differentiate to obtain $dy/dx$.
  2. Compute $(dy/dx)^2$ and expand fully — do not skip cross terms.
  3. Form $1 + (dy/dx)^2$ and combine the constants/middle terms.
  4. Identify the perfect-square structure: $A^2 + 2AB + B^2 = (A+B)^2$ (or with a minus, depending on the sign of the cross term).
  5. Take the positive square root: $\sqrt{1+(dy/dx)^2} = g(x)$, verifying $g(x) > 0$ on $[a,b]$.
  6. Integrate $\int_a^b g(x)\,dx$ as a sum of standard power/log/hyperbolic integrals.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $y = \tfrac{x^3}{3} + \tfrac{1}{4x}$. Then $\tfrac{dy}{dx} = x^2 - \tfrac{1}{4x^2}$, so $\left(\tfrac{dy}{dx}\right)^2 = x^4 - \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{16x^4}$. Adding 1: $1+(dy/dx)^2 = x^4 + \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{16x^4} = \left(x^2 + \tfrac{1}{4x^2}\right)^2$. Hence $\sqrt{1+(dy/dx)^2} = x^2 + \tfrac{1}{4x^2}$, which integrates directly to $\tfrac{x^3}{3} - \tfrac{1}{4x} + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Parametric arc length $\int\sqrt{(dx/dt)^2+(dy/dt)^2}\,dt$
Hunt for $(dx/dt \pm dy/dt)^2$ structure inside the sum of squares; the cross-term 'extra' provided by adding the two squares plays the role of the +1.
Surface area of revolution $2\pi\int y\sqrt{1+(dy/dx)^2}\,dx$
Identical perfect-square collapse for the radical; afterwards multiply the simplified $g(x)$ by $y$ before integrating.
Polar arc length $\int\sqrt{r^2+(dr/d\theta)^2}\,d\theta$
Same algebraic hunt — look for $(r\pm dr/d\theta)^2$ or use $r=a(1+\cos\theta)$ type identities to collapse.
Hyperbolic curves like $y = a\cosh(x/a)$
Use $1+\sinh^2 u = \cosh^2 u$ directly — the identity is the perfect-square collapse in disguise.

Common Misreadings

  • Jumping immediately to a hyperbolic or trig substitution for $\sqrt{1+u^2}$ without first checking whether the curve was designed to collapse algebraically.
  • Trying to simplify $(dy/dx)^2$ alone and forgetting to add the $+1$ before factorising — the $+1$ is essential to completing the square.
  • Not expanding $(dy/dx)^2$ fully, missing the cross-term that reveals the perfect-square pattern.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when squaring a difference: $(x^n - x^{-n})^2$ has a $-2$ middle term, not $+2$ — the sign of the cross term in $y$ flips the structure.
  • Forgetting that $\sqrt{A^2} = |A|$; must verify $g(x) > 0$ on the integration interval before dropping the absolute value.
  • Algebraic slips with fractional/negative exponents when combining $(dy/dx)^2$ and 1 over a common denominator.
  • Mis-identifying the perfect square — e.g. writing $(A+B)^2$ when the middle term sign actually requires $(A-B)^2$.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL Further Pure 3 (FP3) arc length and surface area of revolution; the formulae $L=\int\sqrt{1+(dy/dx)^2}\,dx$ and $S=2\pi\int y\sqrt{1+(dy/dx)^2}\,dx$ are in the FP3 formula booklet. Avoid invoking general non-FP3 techniques like Simpson's rule or numerical methods — the algebraic collapse is always the intended route.
