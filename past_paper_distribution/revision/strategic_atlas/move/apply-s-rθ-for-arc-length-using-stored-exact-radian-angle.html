Trigonometry

Apply s = rθ for arc length using stored exact radian angle

Family: Radian Formula Application Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A perimeter, total length, or path involves a circular arc, and the central angle (in radians) was either given or computed earlier in the question — typically via the cosine rule, right-triangle trig, or arccos/arcsin. Cue phrases include 'find the perimeter of the shaded region', 'arc AB', or 'sector OAB'. The radius is known and the angle is in radians (or convertible). The move fires whenever an arc length is the missing ingredient in a composite-figure calculation.

Motivation — Why this works

In radian measure, arc length is defined by s = rθ, which is the cleanest and most accurate formula available. Reusing the unrounded angle stored from a previous part avoids compounding rounding error, which matters when the final answer is required to 3 s.f. Re-deriving θ wastes time and risks introducing inconsistency between parts.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that one boundary of the region is a circular arc whose length is not yet known.
  2. Rules out re-deriving θ from scratch (already computed) and rules out the degree formula πrθ/180 since θ is already in radians.
  3. Commits to s = rθ because radius and radian angle are both available.
  4. Bridges to computation by recalling the stored full-precision value of θ rather than the rounded display value.
  5. Decides to keep the arc length unrounded until it is summed with the straight-edge contributions to the perimeter.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the radius r and the central angle θ (in radians) subtended by the arc.
  2. Recall the unrounded stored value of θ from the calculator's memory or from earlier working.
  3. Compute s = r × θ to full calculator precision.
  4. Add s to the other (straight-line) perimeter components.
  5. Round the final perimeter — not the intermediate arc length — to the requested accuracy.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $r = 4$ m and $\theta = 1.18635\ldots$ rad from part (a), the arc $AB$ has length $s = r\theta = 4 \times 1.18635\ldots = 4.7454\ldots$ m. Adding the four straight edges (e.g. totalling $7.20$ m) gives perimeter $\approx 11.9$ m to 3 s.f.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Sector area in the same figure
Use $A = \tfrac{1}{2}r^{2}\theta$ with the same stored θ.
Segment area (sector minus triangle)
Compute $\tfrac{1}{2}r^{2}(\theta - \sin\theta)$, again reusing the stored θ in radians.
Angle originally given in degrees
Convert via $\theta_{\text{rad}} = \theta_{\deg} \times \pi/180$ before applying $s = r\theta$, or use $s = \tfrac{\theta}{360} \times 2\pi r$.
Arc length on a unit circle
$r=1$ so $s = \theta$ directly — useful for parametric or circular-motion contexts.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating θ as if it were in degrees and multiplying directly, giving an arc length too large by a factor of ~57.
  • Confusing arc length with chord length and applying $2r\sin(\theta/2)$ instead.
  • Believing the formula needs the diameter rather than the radius.

Pitfalls

  • Rounding θ to 3 s.f. before multiplying by r, producing a perimeter that fails the 3 s.f. accuracy requirement.
  • Using the calculator in degree mode when computing θ via arccos, then applying s = rθ regardless.
  • Forgetting to add the straight edges, or double-counting a radius that already lies along a straight edge of the region.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P2/P3 radian-measure content: arc length $s = r\theta$ and sector area $A = \tfrac{1}{2}r^{2}\theta$ with θ in radians.
