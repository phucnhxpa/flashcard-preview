Integration

Iterate a reduction formula along the parity chain to the matching base case

Family: Reduction Formula Ibp Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires once a reduction formula of the form $nI_n = a(n-1)I_{n-2} + c$ (or similar step-2 recurrence) has been established and a specific $I_N$ is requested. The index drop is 2, so the chain lands on $I_0$ when $N$ is even and on $I_1$ when $N$ is odd. Look for cues like 'hence find $I_5$' or 'use the result of part (a) to evaluate'. The required base case ($I_0$ or $I_1$) is usually computed in an earlier sub-part.

Motivation — Why this works

A reduction formula is precisely a tool for trading a hard integral for a lower-index one, so its purpose is realised only by iterating it down to a base case that can be evaluated directly. Because the recurrence steps by 2, parity of the target index dictates which base case is needed. Each iteration is pure arithmetic once the formula and base case are in hand, which is why the question structure rewards careful, exact-form bookkeeping rather than fresh integration.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the recurrence drops $n$ by 2, so plan the chain $I_N \to I_{N-2} \to \cdots$ and count how many applications are needed.
  2. Rule out re-deriving from scratch or using by-parts again — the formula already encodes the IBP work.
  3. Commit to iteration once parity is checked: odd $N$ terminates at $I_1$, even $N$ at $I_0$; confirm the relevant base value is available.
  4. Bridge to action by substituting $n = N-2k$ values in increasing order (smallest first), so each new $I_n$ is expressed via a value already known.
  5. Tactically keep surds and fractions in exact form, simplify boundary terms once and reuse, and only combine into a single fraction at the final step.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the base case dictated by parity of $N$ (odd $\Rightarrow I_1$, even $\Rightarrow I_0$) and write down its exact value.
  2. Apply the reduction formula with the smallest needed index first to obtain $I_{N-2k+2}$ in exact form.
  3. Re-apply with the next index, substituting the previous result; keep numerator/denominator structure clean.
  4. Continue until $I_N$ is reached; divide through by the leading coefficient $N$ at the final step.
  5. Simplify the surd expression and, if useful, sanity-check numerically against the original integral.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $nI_n = 10(n-1)I_{n-2} - 3$ with $I_1 = \sqrt{10} - 3$. For $I_5$: take $n=3$ to get $3I_3 = 20(\sqrt{10}-3) - 3 = 20\sqrt{10} - 63$, so $I_3 = \tfrac{20\sqrt{10}-63}{3}$. Then $n=5$: $5I_5 = 40 \cdot \tfrac{20\sqrt{10}-63}{3} - 3 = \tfrac{800\sqrt{10}-2529}{3}$, giving $I_5 = \tfrac{800\sqrt{10}-2529}{15}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Even target index, e.g. $I_6$ from the same recurrence
Chain $I_6 \to I_4 \to I_2 \to I_0$, with $I_0 = \int_0^1 \tfrac{dx}{\sqrt{10-x^2}} = \arcsin(1/\sqrt{10})$ as the base.
Trig powers $I_n = \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^n x\,dx$ with $nI_n = (n-1)I_{n-2}$
Same parity logic: odd $n$ ends at $I_1 = 1$, even $n$ ends at $I_0 = \pi/2$; no boundary constant to track.
Recurrence with index drop of 1, e.g. $I_n = nI_{n-1} + f(n)$
Every step is used (no parity split); iterate $N$ times down to $I_0$.
Recurrence expressing $I_{n-2}$ in terms of $I_n$
Rearrange first to make $I_n$ the subject before iterating, otherwise the chain runs the wrong way.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to use the reduction formula in the increasing direction instead of the decreasing one it is designed for.
  • Picking the wrong base case for the parity of the target index (using $I_0$ when $N$ is odd, or vice versa).
  • Treating the constant boundary term as if it appears only once, rather than at every iteration.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips in one step propagate to the final answer; verifying $I_3$ numerically before computing $I_5$ catches most errors.
  • Combining surds and rationals prematurely, leading to messy fractions; keep $\sqrt{10}$ symbolic until the last line.
  • Forgetting to divide by the leading coefficient $n$ at the final step, leaving an answer for $nI_n$ rather than $I_n$.

A-level Scope Notes

Reduction formulae and their iteration to base cases $I_0$ or $I_1$ are standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure content; all base integrals here ($\arcsin$, surd evaluation, simple powers) lie within the syllabus.
