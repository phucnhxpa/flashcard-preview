Apply quadratic formula to obtain exact surd roots

Family: Quadratic Formula Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0 must be solved and the question demands 'exact values' or 'in surd form'. Trial factorisation fails: no integer pair multiplies to ac and sums to b. The discriminant b² − 4ac is positive but not a perfect square, signalling irrational roots. Decimal approximations are explicitly ruled out by the wording.

Motivation — Why this works

The quadratic formula x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac))/(2a) is the universal closed-form solution and preserves exactness through surds. When the discriminant is a non-square positive integer, the roots are guaranteed irrational, so any decimal answer would be lossy. Simplifying the resulting surd (e.g. √20 = 2√5) and cancelling common factors with the denominator yields the canonical exact form expected in mark schemes.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the instruction 'exact value' or 'in the form p + q√n' — this rules out decimals.
  2. Test factorisation mentally: search for integer factor pairs of ac summing to b; if none exists, factorisation is dead.
  3. Compute the discriminant; a non-square positive value commits you to the formula (or completing the square).
  4. Substitute a, b, c carefully — sign errors on b and c are the dominant risk — and write √Δ before simplifying.
  5. Simplify the surd by extracting square factors, then cancel any common numerical factor between numerator and denominator; finally apply contextual constraints (e.g. positivity of a length) to discard the invalid root.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify a, b, c from the standard form ax² + bx + c = 0.
  2. Compute discriminant Δ = b² − 4ac and confirm Δ > 0 and non-square.
  3. Substitute into x = (−b ± √Δ)/(2a).
  4. Simplify √Δ by factoring out perfect squares.
  5. Cancel any common factor between numerator and 2a to reach simplest exact form.
  6. Apply context (positivity, range, geometry) to reject the extraneous root.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $p^2 + 2p - 4 = 0$ for $p > 0$. Discriminant: $\Delta = 2^2 - 4(1)(-4) = 20$. Then $p = \dfrac{-2 \pm \sqrt{20}}{2} = \dfrac{-2 \pm 2\sqrt{5}}{2} = -1 \pm \sqrt{5}$. Since $p > 0$, take $p = \sqrt{5} - 1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Discriminant is a perfect square
Switch to factorisation or inspection — the formula still works but is slower and the answer is rational.
Question says 'show that' the root has a specific surd form
Prefer completing the square: (p + b/2a)² = (b² − 4ac)/(4a²) makes the structure transparent and matches the target form directly.
Coefficients involve a parameter k
Apply the formula symbolically; the discriminant becomes a function of k, and you may need to impose Δ ≥ 0 for real roots.
Quadratic in a substituted variable (e.g. let u = x²)
Solve for u via the formula, then back-substitute and reject negative u-values before square-rooting.
Roots required in form a + b√n with rational a, b
After applying the formula, ensure the surd is fully simplified and split the fraction into rational and irrational parts.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'exact value' as 'to 3 significant figures' and giving a decimal.
  • Assuming factorisation must work and forcing non-integer factors.
  • Mistaking a non-square discriminant for 'no real solutions'.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when substituting a negative c into −4ac.
  • Failing to simplify √20 to 2√5 before cancelling, leaving an unsimplified answer.
  • Cancelling only one term of the numerator with the denominator (e.g. (−2 + 2√5)/2 → −2 + √5).
  • Forgetting to apply the contextual constraint and quoting both roots when only one is valid.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard P1/C1 content in Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics; surd manipulation and rejection of extraneous roots are core skills assessed throughout Pure and Further Pure modules.
