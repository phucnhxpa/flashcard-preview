Trigger — When to use

The move fires whenever a quadratic equation or inequality is presented with decimal (or fractional) coefficients that would make the quadratic formula or discriminant arithmetic messy. Visual cues include leading coefficients like 0.25, 0.4, 0.1, or mixed decimal coefficients such as 9.75 alongside whole-number constants. Equivalent phrasings: 'clear the decimals', 'make the quadratic monic', 'scale up by a power of 10', 'multiply by the LCM of denominators'. If you see a quadratic that isn't already in tidy integer form, ask whether a single multiplicative scaling will fix it before reaching for the formula.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying or dividing every term by a suitable positive constant produces an equivalent equation/inequality with integer (and often monic) coefficients, dramatically simplifying the discriminant b² − 4ac and any factorisation attempts. The underlying principle is that multiplying both sides of an equation — or every term of an inequality by a positive constant — preserves the solution set. Cleaner coefficients mean fewer arithmetic slips in the quadratic formula and make integer factor pairs visible for inspection.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the coefficients are decimals (e.g. 0.25, 0.4, 9.75) or fractions, so the quadratic formula will involve squaring decimals and multiplying decimals by negatives — a common source of error.
  2. Consider alternatives: applying the quadratic formula directly with decimals, or completing the square with decimals. Reject both because the discriminant arithmetic is messy and factorisation is invisible.
  3. Identify the smallest positive constant k that converts every coefficient to an integer — typically the reciprocal of the leading coefficient (e.g. divide by 0.4) or the LCM of the denominators when written as fractions (e.g. multiply by 4 when 0.25 = 1/4 appears).
  4. Confirm k > 0 so that the inequality direction is preserved (no sign flip needed). For an equation, sign of k is irrelevant but positive is conventional.
  5. Apply the scaling to ALL terms simultaneously, including the constant term, then read off the new a, b, c and proceed with the standard solution method (factorise, formula, or complete the square).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the scaling constant k: either k = 1/(leading coefficient) to make it monic, or k = 10ⁿ / LCM of denominators to clear decimals/fractions.
  2. Multiply (or divide) every term of the equation/inequality by k.
  3. Rewrite in the form at² + bt + c = 0 (or < 0, > 0) with the new integer coefficients.
  4. Verify by substituting one term back: e.g. check 0.4 · k = new leading coefficient.
  5. Proceed to solve the cleaned quadratic by factorisation, the quadratic formula, or completing the square.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $0.4t^2 - 8t + 16.8 < 0$. Divide every term by $0.4$ (positive, so inequality preserved): $t^2 - 20t + 42 < 0$. Discriminant: $400 - 168 = 232$, giving $t = 10 \pm \sqrt{58}$, so $10-\sqrt{58} < t < 10+\sqrt{58}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Leading coefficient is a 'nice' decimal like 0.25, 0.5, 0.2
Multiply through by 4, 2, or 5 respectively to obtain a monic quadratic.
All coefficients have one decimal place (e.g. 0.4, −8, 16.8)
Multiply through by 10 to clear decimals (gives 4t² − 80t + 168), then optionally divide by the GCD (4) to reach the monic form.
Fractional coefficients (e.g. ⅓x² + ½x − 2 = 0)
Multiply through by the LCM of denominators (here, 6) to obtain 2x² + 3x − 12 = 0.
Inequality with negative leading coefficient
If you choose to divide by a negative to make it monic, you MUST flip the inequality sign; usually safer to factor out −1 first or multiply by a positive constant only.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating decimal coefficients as 'fine' and ploughing into the quadratic formula, leading to messy discriminants and arithmetic slips.
  • Confusing this with 'dividing both sides' as in linear equations, and forgetting it must apply to every term including the constant.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to scale ALL terms — especially the constant — by the same factor.
  • Dividing by a negative number in an inequality without flipping the inequality sign.
  • Choosing a scaling constant that doesn't actually clear all the decimals (e.g. multiplying 0.25n² + 9.75n − 700 by 2 instead of 4).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics P1/P2 (quadratic equations and inequalities). The quadratic formula x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac))/(2a) is in the formula booklet. No out-of-scope generalisations needed; this is purely an arithmetic preparation step.
