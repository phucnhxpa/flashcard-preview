Algebra & Functions

Multiply through by the LCD to clear fractions and reach polynomial form

Family: Fraction Clearing Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

The equation contains one or more rational terms (e.g. 1/t, 1/(1−r), or several fractions with denominators like t and 1−t²) but the target form is a polynomial equation, a tidy linear/quadratic relation, or a 'standard bookwork' result with no compound fractions. Visual cues: a 1/(...) factor multiplying a bracket, two terms with different algebraic denominators summed to zero, or an answer template that has integer/polynomial coefficients only. Equivalent phrasings: 'clear the fractions', 'multiply up', 'remove denominators', 'multiply by the LCM/LCD'. The move fires whenever the obstacle to reaching the target form is purely the presence of a denominator.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying both sides by the lowest common denominator (LCD) eliminates every fraction in a single, controlled step, turning a rational equation into a polynomial one that can then be expanded and rearranged. This works because multiplication by a non-zero quantity preserves equality, and the LCD is engineered to cancel exactly with each denominator on the page. The payoff is often more than just tidiness: structural identities such as the difference of squares (1+t²)(1−t²) = 1−t⁴ frequently emerge, delivering the precise polynomial form required. It is the canonical bridge between rational and polynomial representations and is preferred over piecewise cross-multiplication, which is more error-prone.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the obstacle: the equation has fraction(s), but the target form (quadratic, quartic, or 'standard' linear form) is fraction-free.
  2. Rule out alternatives: cross-multiplying term-by-term risks losing a term; combining over a common denominator first and then equating numerators is valid but adds a step; manipulating each fraction separately is messier.
  3. Commit to the LCD approach: identify every distinct denominator, take their product (or LCM if they share factors) — e.g. denominators t and (1−t²) give LCD t(1−t²); a single denominator (1−r) is its own LCD.
  4. Note domain restrictions before multiplying: any value making the LCD zero (t = 0, t = ±1, r = 1, etc.) must be excluded, and any final solution checked against these restrictions to avoid spurious roots.
  5. Multiply EVERY term on BOTH sides by the LCD — this is the most common point of error; write the multiplication out term-by-term rather than mentally.
  6. Anticipate useful structure that will appear after clearing: e.g. (1+t²)(1−t²) collapsing via difference of squares to (1−t⁴), or partial cancellations like −at² + 2at² = +at². Use these to steer the simplification toward the target form.
  7. Expand, collect, and rearrange (often multiplying by −1 at the end) to match the requested format exactly.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify every denominator and form the LCD as their LCM.
  2. State excluded values (where LCD = 0) as domain restrictions.
  3. Multiply each term of the equation by the LCD, distributing carefully.
  4. Cancel each denominator against the matching factor of the LCD to leave polynomial terms only.
  5. Expand brackets, applying any standard identities (difference of squares, etc.) where they shortcut the algebra.
  6. Collect like terms and move everything to one side to match the required '= 0' form.
  7. If the leading coefficient has the wrong sign for the target, multiply the whole equation by −1.
  8. Verify any solutions later against the excluded values to discard spurious roots.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\dfrac{24t}{1-t^{2}} + \dfrac{5}{t} = 0$. LCD is $t(1-t^{2})$. Multiplying through: $24t\cdot t + 5(1+t^{2})(1-t^{2}) = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; 24t^{2} + 5(1-t^{4}) = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; -5t^{4} + 24t^{2} + 5 = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; 5t^{4} - 24t^{2} - 5 = 0.$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Single reciprocal parameter, e.g. tangent equation y − 2at = (1/t)(x − at²)
Multiply by t alone to get ty − 2at² = x − at², then rearrange to the standard ty = x + at².
Single linear denominator, e.g. 1/(1−r) = 1 + 8r
Multiply both sides by (1−r); expand and collect to reach the quadratic 4r² − 4r + 1 = 0.
Surd denominators such as 1/(√a − √b)
Multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate (√a + √b) — same difference-of-squares logic, applied as rationalisation rather than equation-clearing.
Partial fractions identity like A/(x−1) + B/(x+2) = (3x+1)/((x−1)(x+2))
Multiply through by the full denominator (x−1)(x+2) to obtain a polynomial identity, then equate coefficients or substitute strategic x-values.

Common Misreadings

  • Cross-multiplying only two of several terms, leaving stray fractions on the other side.
  • Treating the equation as if only one denominator existed and multiplying by that, missing fractions elsewhere.
  • Combining fractions over a common denominator and then forgetting that the numerator (not the equation) is what must vanish.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to multiply EVERY term by the LCD — especially constant terms that look like they have no denominator.
  • Missing a structural shortcut such as (1+t²)(1−t²) = 1−t⁴ and expanding the long way, inviting sign errors.
  • Forgetting to exclude values that make the LCD zero, leading to spurious solutions (e.g. r = 1 surviving the algebra but being invalid).
  • Sign errors when combining like terms after distribution (e.g. −at² + 2at² mis-evaluated as 3at² or −at²).
  • Leaving the equation with the wrong leading-coefficient sign and not multiplying by −1 to match the required form.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard manipulation throughout Edexcel IAL Pure (P1–P4), Further Pure (FP1–FP3), and Core algebra; no formula-booklet entry is needed. Stay clear of out-of-scope ideas like multiplying inequalities by expressions whose sign is unknown — that is a separate, more delicate move.
