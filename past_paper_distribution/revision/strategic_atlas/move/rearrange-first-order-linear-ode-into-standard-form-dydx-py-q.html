Differential Equations

Rearrange first-order linear ODE into standard form dy/dx + Py = Q

Family: Standard Form Rearrangement Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a first-order ODE is linear in y and dy/dx but not yet presented in the canonical shape dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x). Typical cues: a non-unit coefficient multiplies dy/dx (e.g. x·dy/dx), the y-term sits on the wrong side, or the equation appears as a product/quotient that hides the linear structure. The move is needed before any integrating factor can be computed. Also fires when an equation is linear in x as a function of y, requiring the dx/dy + P(y)x = Q(y) version.

Motivation — Why this works

The integrating factor μ(x) = e^{∫P dx} is derived assuming dy/dx has coefficient 1 and the y-term has coefficient exactly P(x). If dy/dx still carries a coefficient, multiplying by μ does not produce d/dx(μy), so the method silently fails. Normalising the equation makes P(x) and Q(x) unambiguous and aligns the LHS with the product-rule template that the integrating factor exploits.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation is first order and every occurrence of y or dy/dx is to the first power with no products like y·dy/dx — so it is linear in y.
  2. Rule out separation (variables won't split), exactness (not in M dx + N dy = 0 with M_y = N_x), and substitution methods (no Bernoulli y^n term, no homogeneous structure).
  3. Commit to the integrating factor route because linearity in y is the decisive feature, and that route requires the canonical form.
  4. First concrete operation: divide every term by the coefficient of dy/dx so dy/dx stands alone with coefficient 1.
  5. Then collect the y-term on the LHS and everything else on the RHS, and carefully read off P(x) (coefficient of y, with sign) and Q(x).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the coefficient of dy/dx; divide every term in the equation by it.
  2. Move all terms containing y to the LHS and all remaining terms to the RHS.
  3. Write the equation in the form dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x).
  4. Read off P(x) as the coefficient of y (including its sign) and Q(x) as the RHS.
  5. Sanity check: substitute a simple value or check dimensions/units of x in P and Q.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $x\frac{dy}{dx} - 2y = x^4$. Divide through by $x$: $\frac{dy}{dx} - \frac{2}{x}y = x^3$. Now in standard form with $P(x) = -\frac{2}{x}$ and $Q(x) = x^3$, ready for the integrating factor $\mu = e^{\int -2/x\,dx} = x^{-2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation linear in x rather than y, e.g. $\frac{dx}{dy} + \tan(y)\,x = \sec(y)$
Treat x as the dependent variable; rearrange to $\frac{dx}{dy} + P(y)x = Q(y)$ and apply the integrating factor in y.
Coefficient of dy/dx is a function, e.g. $(1+x^2)\frac{dy}{dx} + 2xy = 1$
Divide through by $(1+x^2)$ to obtain $\frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{2x}{1+x^2}y = \frac{1}{1+x^2}$.
Mixed form with y-term on RHS, e.g. $\frac{dy}{dx} = 3y + e^{x}$
Subtract $3y$ to bring it to the LHS: $\frac{dy}{dx} - 3y = e^{x}$, giving $P(x) = -3$.
Product form, e.g. $\frac{d}{dx}(xy) = x^2$
Expand the LHS via product rule to $x\frac{dy}{dx} + y = x^2$, then divide by x to get standard form.

Common Misreadings

  • Mistaking a Bernoulli equation $\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x)y^n$ for linear and applying the integrating factor without first substituting $u = y^{1-n}$.
  • Treating an equation that is nonlinear in y (e.g. contains $y^2$ or $y\frac{dy}{dx}$) as linear because it 'looks tidy'.
  • Reading P(x) from the original unnormalised equation when the coefficient of dy/dx is not 1.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to divide every term (including the RHS) by the coefficient of dy/dx, producing an incorrect P(x) and Q(x).
  • Sign error when transposing the y-term: writing $+P(x)y$ instead of $-P(x)y$ or vice versa.
  • Dividing by a coefficient that can vanish (e.g. dividing by x without noting x ≠ 0), which can cause issues at boundary conditions.
  • Leaving y-terms on both sides, so P(x) is computed from only one of them.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard for Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) first-order linear ODEs solved by integrating factor; Bernoulli equations are out of scope unless an explicit substitution is given in the question.
