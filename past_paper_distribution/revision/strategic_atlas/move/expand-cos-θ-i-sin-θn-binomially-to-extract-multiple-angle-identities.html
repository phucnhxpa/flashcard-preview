Complex Numbers

Expand (cos θ + i sin θ)^n binomially to extract multiple-angle identities

Family: De Moivre Expansion Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the question asks to express cos nθ, sin nθ, or tan nθ in terms of powers of cos θ and sin θ for a specific integer n (typically n = 3, 4, 5). Strong cues: the chapter is 'Further complex numbers', the target identity has a multiple angle on one side and single-angle powers on the other, or a derivation of tan nθ as a rational function of tan θ is requested. Phrases like 'show that', 'prove', or 'hence find' alongside multiple-angle trig in a complex-numbers context all point here.

Motivation — Why this works

De Moivre's theorem states (cos θ + i sin θ)^n = cos nθ + i sin nθ, so a single binomial expansion of the LHS produces real and imaginary parts that must equal cos nθ and sin nθ respectively. This delivers both identities simultaneously and avoids the compounding mess of iterating double-angle or compound-angle formulae. For tan nθ, dividing the imaginary part by the real part and then dividing numerator and denominator by cos^n θ converts everything to tan θ cleanly.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the target involves cos nθ, sin nθ, or tan nθ expressed in powers of single-angle trig — a classic De Moivre signature.
  2. Rule out repeated compound-angle expansion: it works but is error-prone and slow for n ≥ 3.
  3. Commit to De Moivre because one binomial expansion yields both real and imaginary parts at once.
  4. Set up (c + is)^n with c = cos θ, s = sin θ, and write out binomial coefficients C(n,r) for r = 0,…,n.
  5. Track powers of i carefully (i, -1, -i, 1 cycling) and split the expansion into real (even r) and imaginary (odd r) parts before equating to cos nθ and sin nθ.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write (cos θ + i sin θ)^n = cos nθ + i sin nθ by De Moivre.
  2. Expand the LHS as Σ_{r=0}^{n} C(n,r) (cos θ)^{n-r} (i sin θ)^r.
  3. Simplify (i sin θ)^r using i^0=1, i^1=i, i^2=-1, i^3=-i, i^4=1, …
  4. Collect the real part (even r terms) and equate to cos nθ.
  5. Collect the imaginary part (odd r terms, factor out i) and equate to sin nθ.
  6. If tan nθ is required, divide sin nθ by cos nθ, then divide numerator and denominator by cos^n θ to convert to tan θ.

Worked Micro-Example

For tan 4θ: $(c+is)^4 = c^4 + 4ic^3 s - 6c^2 s^2 - 4ics^3 + s^4$. Real: $\cos 4\theta = c^4 - 6c^2 s^2 + s^4$. Imag: $\sin 4\theta = 4c^3 s - 4cs^3$. Dividing and then dividing top and bottom by $c^4$: $\tan 4\theta = \dfrac{4\tan\theta - 4\tan^3\theta}{1 - 6\tan^2\theta + \tan^4\theta}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Deriving tan 3θ in terms of tan θ
Expand (c+is)^3 with coefficients 1, 3, 3, 1; obtain cos 3θ = c^3 - 3cs^2, sin 3θ = 3c^2 s - s^3, then divide and normalise by c^3.
Deriving tan 5θ or sin 5θ identity
Expand (c+is)^5 with coefficients 1, 5, 10, 10, 5, 1; real part gives cos 5θ, imaginary part gives sin 5θ.
Expressing cos nθ purely in powers of cos θ
After extracting the real part, replace any remaining sin^{2k} θ using sin^2 θ = 1 - cos^2 θ.
Expressing sin nθ purely in powers of sin θ (n odd)
After extracting the imaginary part, replace cos^{2k} θ using cos^2 θ = 1 - sin^2 θ.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the problem as a pure trigonometry question and iterating compound-angle formulae instead of invoking De Moivre.
  • Confusing this move with the reverse direction (using z + 1/z = 2cos θ to express cos^n θ in terms of multiple angles), which is a different technique.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors from powers of i: (is)^2 = -s^2, (is)^3 = -is^3, (is)^4 = +s^4.
  • Misallocating terms — putting an odd-r term into the real part or vice versa.
  • Forgetting to divide numerator and denominator by cos^n θ when converting the tan nθ expression into one purely in tan θ.
  • Dropping the binomial coefficient on a middle term (e.g. writing 6 as 4 for C(4,2)).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (Further Complex Numbers): standard application of De Moivre's theorem with the binomial theorem to derive multiple-angle identities, typically for n ≤ 5.
