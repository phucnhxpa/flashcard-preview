Coordinate Geometry

Equate y-expressions to reduce intersection to a single equation in x

Family: Elimination By Equating Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when two curves or a line and a curve are both given in the explicit form y = f(x) and y = g(x), and the question asks where they 'meet', 'intersect', 'cross', 'touch', or asks for the intersection point(s). Visual cue: both equations are already solved for y. Alternative phrasings include 'find the coordinates of intersection', 'show that the line cuts the curve at...', or 'the curve and line touch' (the latter signalling a repeated root condition to be applied afterwards). The same trigger applies whether one curve carries an unknown constant (k, c) or not.

Motivation — Why this works

At any intersection point both equations share the same (x, y), so the two y-expressions must agree there. Setting f(x) = g(x) eliminates y immediately and reduces a two-variable geometric problem (where do these graphs meet?) to a one-variable algebraic problem (solve a polynomial in x). This is the most direct route because no rearrangement or substitution is needed — y is already isolated in both. The resulting polynomial's degree is dictated by the highest-degree curve, and its discriminant/roots then govern the geometry (number of intersections, tangency, etc.).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that both equations are already in the form y = ..., so y is isolated in each.
  2. Recognise that the question is about intersection (keywords: 'meets', 'touches', 'crosses', 'intersection') — geometrically this means both equations hold at the same point.
  3. Rule out substitution gymnastics: I could substitute y from one into the other, but since y is already the subject in both, equating right-hand sides is equivalent and cleaner.
  4. Rule out equating derivatives: 'touch' means intersect with equal gradient — but the intersection condition must come first; tangency is then enforced via the discriminant being zero, not by equating f'(x) = g'(x) directly.
  5. Commit to setting f(x) = g(x), then move every term to one side to obtain a clean polynomial = 0 form, which is essential for applying the quadratic formula or discriminant.
  6. If a parameter (like c or k) is present, keep it symbolic in the coefficients — it will become the unknown determined by a later condition (e.g. discriminant = 0 for tangency).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the two equations in the form y = f(x) and y = g(x).
  2. Set the right-hand sides equal: f(x) = g(x).
  3. Move every term to one side to obtain f(x) − g(x) = 0.
  4. Simplify and collect like terms to get a standard polynomial form, e.g. Ax² + Bx + C = 0, with coefficients possibly involving any parameter (c, k).
  5. Hand off the resulting equation to the next stage: solve for x, or impose b² − 4ac = 0 for tangency, or b² − 4ac > 0 for two distinct intersections, etc.

Worked Micro-Example

Find where $y = 4x + c$ meets $y = x^2 - 3x$. Equate: $4x + c = x^2 - 3x$. Rearrange: $x^2 - 7x - c = 0$. This single equation in $x$ now governs the intersection; for tangency, set the discriminant $49 + 4c = 0$, giving $c = -\tfrac{49}{4}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Both equations given as x = g(y) and x = h(y)
Equate the x-expressions instead, producing a single equation in y; same logic, swapped roles.
Line y = mx + c meets a curve given implicitly (e.g. circle x² + y² = r²)
Substitute y = mx + c into the curve equation to get a polynomial in x — same elimination principle, executed via substitution rather than equating.
Parametric curves (x = p(t), y = q(t)) and (x = P(s), y = Q(s))
Equate x-components and y-components separately, giving a simultaneous system in the two parameters t and s.
'Touch' / tangency problems
After equating, impose discriminant = 0 on the resulting quadratic to enforce a repeated root.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to solve each equation separately for x and then equating those x-expressions — this is harder and unnecessary when y is already isolated.
  • Equating derivatives f'(x) = g'(x) when reading 'curves touch' — this gives equal gradients but ignores the requirement that they actually meet; both conditions are needed and the intersection equation comes first.
  • Setting x-expressions equal when the equations are not both solved for x.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when moving all terms to one side — particularly when a parameter like c or k carries its own sign.
  • Failing to collect ALL terms to one side, leaving a non-zero right-hand side, which then leads to wrong coefficients A, B, C for the discriminant.
  • Forgetting to substitute the x-roots back into one of the original equations to recover the y-coordinates of the intersection point(s).

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1–P4) scope. No formula booklet entry is invoked directly; the technique relies only on the definition of intersection and standard quadratic methods (discriminant, factorising, quadratic formula). Avoid generalising to systems requiring resultants or elimination theory — these are out of scope.
