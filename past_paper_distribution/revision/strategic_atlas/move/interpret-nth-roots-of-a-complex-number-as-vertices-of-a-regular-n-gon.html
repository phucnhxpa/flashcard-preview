Complex Numbers

Interpret nth roots of a complex number as vertices of a regular n-gon

Family: Geometric Interpretation Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question involving the equation z^n = w (or a translated form (z − a)^n = w) asks you to 'show the roots on an Argand diagram', 'describe the geometric relationship between the roots', 'find the area of the polygon formed by the roots', or 'find the distance between two of the roots'. The structural cue is that all n solutions share the same modulus r^{1/n} and have arguments that differ by exactly 2π/n. Equivalent phrasings include 'sketch the points representing the roots', 'find the perimeter of the figure', or 'state the locus on which all roots lie'.

Motivation — Why this works

By De Moivre's theorem, every nth root of w = r(cos θ + i sin θ) has modulus r^{1/n} and arguments (θ + 2kπ)/n for k = 0,…,n−1. Equal modulus places them on a single circle of radius r^{1/n} centred at the origin; equal angular spacing 2π/n then forces them to be the vertices of a regular n-gon inscribed in that circle. This converts what would be a messy Cartesian-coordinate computation (subtracting roots, summing triangle areas) into one-line trigonometric formulas based on the polygon's symmetry, and earns the 'geometric description' marks directly.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question asks for a geometric output — a diagram, an area, a distance, or a description — rather than just a list of roots in a + bi form.
  2. I rule out brute-force conversion of every root to Cartesian form followed by distance/shoelace calculations: that is correct but slow and error-prone, and it ignores the symmetry the marker is rewarding.
  3. I recall that De Moivre gives every root the same modulus r^{1/n} and arguments separated by 2π/n; equal modulus + equal angular spacing is the definition of a regular polygon's vertices on a circle.
  4. I commit to stating this geometric interpretation explicitly: 'the roots are the vertices of a regular n-gon inscribed in the circle |z| = r^{1/n} centred at the origin'.
  5. From this I read off the standard polygon formulas: side length 2R sin(π/n), area (n/2)R² sin(2π/n), and chord between roots z_j, z_k equal to 2R sin(|j−k|π/n), with R = r^{1/n}.
  6. I also check whether the equation has been translated to (z − a)^n = w, in which case the polygon is centred at a rather than 0, but the same formulas apply with that shifted centre.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write w in modulus-argument form r(cos θ + i sin θ) and identify R = r^{1/n} as the common modulus of every root.
  2. State that the n roots all lie on the circle |z| = R centred at the origin (or at a, for (z − a)^n = w).
  3. State that consecutive roots are separated by angle 2π/n, so they are the vertices of a regular n-gon.
  4. For the diagram: mark the first root z_0 at argument θ/n, then step around the circle in increments of 2π/n.
  5. For area: A = (n/2)·R²·sin(2π/n).
  6. For side length / adjacent-root distance: ℓ = 2R sin(π/n).
  7. For non-adjacent roots: |z_j − z_k| = 2R sin(|j − k|π/n).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve z^6 = 64 and find the area of the hexagon formed. Roots have modulus R = 64^{1/6} = 2 and arguments 2kπ/6 for k = 0,…,5, so they form a regular hexagon inscribed in |z| = 2. Area = (6/2)·2²·sin(2π/6) = 12·(√3/2) = 6√3.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation of the form (z − a)^n = w
The polygon is inscribed in the circle |z − a| = |w|^{1/n} centred at a; all length/area formulas are unchanged.
Question asks only for the locus of the roots
Answer with the circle |z| = r^{1/n} (or |z − a| = r^{1/n}); no need to invoke polygon formulas.
Distance between two specified non-adjacent roots z_j and z_k
Use the chord formula |z_j − z_k| = 2R sin(|j − k|π/n) instead of the side-length formula.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating z^n = w as requiring full Cartesian expansion of each root before any geometric reasoning.
  • Assuming the polygon is centred at the origin even when the equation is (z − a)^n = w.
  • Confusing the circumradius R with the side length ℓ in area formulas.

Pitfalls

  • Using r instead of r^{1/n} as the circumradius — forgetting to take the nth root of the modulus of w.
  • Using the diameter 2R in place of the radius R inside the sin(π/n) formula.
  • Getting the starting argument θ/n wrong, which rotates the polygon and misplaces every labelled vertex.
  • Drawing roots with visibly unequal angular gaps despite the equation guaranteeing equal spacing.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) coverage of De Moivre's theorem and nth roots of a complex number. The polygon formulas A = (n/2)R² sin(2π/n) and ℓ = 2R sin(π/n) are not in the formula booklet but follow from the standard triangle-area formula (½ab sin C) applied to the n isosceles triangles from the centre — students should derive them rather than quote them. Avoid invoking roots of unity group structure or cyclotomic identities, which are out of scope.
