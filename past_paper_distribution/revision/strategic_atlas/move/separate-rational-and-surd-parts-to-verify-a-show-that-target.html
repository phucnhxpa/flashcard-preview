Algebra & Functions

Separate rational and surd parts to verify a 'show that' target

Family: Rational Irrational Grouping Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a 'show that' or 'prove' question reduces to an expression mixing rational integers/fractions with surd terms (typically multiples of √2, √3, √5) and the target value is purely rational (often an integer). Cues include a final line of the form $a + b\sqrt{n} + c + d\sqrt{n} + \ldots$ that must equal a rational RHS. Also fires when the surds visibly appear with opposite signs, hinting at cancellation. The move is the standard finishing step after surd manipulation, binomial expansion of $(p+q\sqrt{n})^k$, or rationalising denominators.

Motivation — Why this works

Rational numbers and $\sqrt{n}$ (for non-square $n$) are linearly independent over $\mathbb{Q}$: if $a + b\sqrt{n} = 0$ with $a,b \in \mathbb{Q}$ then $a = b = 0$. Grouping the two types separately exploits this independence to produce two cleanly verifiable sub-claims (rationals sum correctly; surds cancel). It is examiner-friendly because each group is a single short calculation, eliminates sign-tracking errors that arise from left-to-right combination, and makes the proof structure transparent for method marks.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the working has collapsed to an expression of the form (rationals) + (multiples of $\sqrt{n}$), and the target on the RHS is purely rational.
  2. Rule out continuing to simplify left-to-right: this risks sign slips on the surd coefficients and obscures the cancellation that the question is designed around.
  3. Commit to splitting because rational/surd independence guarantees that if both groups give the right value, the proof is complete and unambiguous.
  4. Bridge to action: list every term with its sign, partition into a 'rational pile' and a '$\sqrt{n}$ pile'.
  5. Inside the move, factor $\sqrt{n}$ out of the surd pile, sum the rational coefficients, and present the two one-line totals before combining.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the expression as a single sum with explicit signs on every term, removing brackets carefully.
  2. Partition into rational terms and surd terms (collecting like surds, e.g. all $\sqrt{2}$ together).
  3. Sum the rational coefficients to a single rational number.
  4. Factor the surd and sum its coefficients; check this gives 0 (or the required surd part).
  5. Combine the two results and state equality with the RHS to conclude.

Worked Micro-Example

Show that $(6 - 4\sqrt{2}) + 1 + (4\sqrt{2} - 5) = 2$.\\ Rational part: $6 + 1 - 5 = 2$.\\ Surd part: $-4\sqrt{2} + 4\sqrt{2} = 0$.\\ Total: $2 + 0 = 2$, as required.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Complex number 'show that' proofs (FP1)
Separate real and imaginary parts; verify each equals the corresponding part of the RHS, using independence of $1$ and $i$ over $\mathbb{R}$.
Equating coefficients to find unknowns, e.g. $a + b\sqrt{3} = 5 + 2\sqrt{3}$
Use the same partition but read off $a = 5$ and $b = 2$ from the two groups instead of summing to a target.
Mixed surds such as $p + q\sqrt{2} + r\sqrt{3} + s\sqrt{6}$
Treat $1, \sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}, \sqrt{6}$ as four independent groups and sum coefficients within each.
Binomial expansion of $(a+b\sqrt{n})^k$ then 'show that the result is rational'
After expanding, group even-power (rational) and odd-power (surd) terms; show the surd group cancels.
After rationalising a denominator like $\dfrac{p+q\sqrt{n}}{r+s\sqrt{n}}$
Once the denominator is rational, split the numerator into rational and surd parts to match the form $A + B\sqrt{n}$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the expression as something to simplify left-to-right, generating intermediate mixed terms and losing track of which surds should cancel.
  • Believing one must rationalise or manipulate further before grouping, when in fact the grouping is the final step.
  • Confusing $\sqrt{n}$ terms with different $n$ (e.g. lumping $\sqrt{2}$ with $\sqrt{8}$ without first writing $\sqrt{8}=2\sqrt{2}$).

Pitfalls

  • Dropping a minus sign when removing brackets, e.g. reading $-(4\sqrt{2}-5)$ as $-4\sqrt{2}-5$ instead of $-4\sqrt{2}+5$.
  • Forgetting to simplify a surd to standard form before grouping (e.g. leaving $\sqrt{8}$ instead of $2\sqrt{2}$), so 'like' surds are not recognised.
  • Stating the answer is correct without explicitly showing the surd group equals 0, costing the communication mark on a 'show that' question.
  • Arithmetic slip in the rational sum because the surd cancellation distracts attention.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure (surds in P1, complex-number analogue in FP1). Uses only that $\{1, \sqrt{n}\}$ (or $\{1,i\}$) are linearly independent over $\mathbb{Q}$ (resp. $\mathbb{R}$), which is assumed implicitly at A-level.
