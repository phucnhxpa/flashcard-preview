Differentiation

Bridge volume and depth rates via chain rule dh/dt = (dV/dt)/(dV/dh)

Family: Chain Rule Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The problem supplies volumetric inflow/outflow rates (units like m³/min or cm³/s) but asks for the rate of change of a linear dimension such as depth, radius, or height. A geometric formula (cylinder, cone, sphere) links V to that linear variable. Cue phrases include 'water flows in at... and leaks out at...', 'find the rate at which the depth is rising', or 'show that dh/dt = ...'. Three quantities are in play: V(h), dV/dt, and the unknown dh/dt.

Motivation — Why this works

The chain rule dV/dt = (dV/dh)(dh/dt) is the only consistent way to convert between a volumetric rate and a linear rate — they have different units, so a geometric conversion factor dV/dh (an area-like quantity) must mediate them. Rearranging gives dh/dt = (dV/dt)/(dV/dh), which is exactly the bridge needed. Because V(h) is known explicitly from the geometry, dV/dh is a routine derivative, making the division mechanical.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the 'currency mismatch': data is in volume per time but the target is depth per time, so a direct equation is impossible.
  2. Rule out trying to express inflow/outflow directly as dh/dt — there is no physical reason flow rate equals depth rate without the cross-section.
  3. Commit to introducing V as the bridge variable because the geometry gives V as an explicit function of h, so dV/dh is computable.
  4. First concrete operation: write V from the geometric formula, then differentiate with respect to h to obtain dV/dh.
  5. Assemble dV/dt from inflow minus outflow, then apply dh/dt = (dV/dt)/(dV/dh); keep π symbolic so it cancels cleanly.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the geometry and write V as a function of the linear variable, e.g. V = πr²h = 16πh for a cylinder of radius 4 m.
  2. Differentiate to get dV/dh (here dV/dh = 16π).
  3. Express dV/dt from the physical setup: dV/dt = (inflow rate) − (outflow rate), e.g. 0.6π − 0.15πh.
  4. Apply the chain rule: dh/dt = (dV/dt)/(dV/dh).
  5. Simplify, cancelling common factors such as π, to obtain a clean expression in h.

Worked Micro-Example

A cylindrical tank of radius $4$ m has water flowing in at $0.6\pi$ m³/min and leaking out at $0.15\pi h$ m³/min. Then $V = 16\pi h$, so $\dfrac{dV}{dh} = 16\pi$ and $\dfrac{dV}{dt} = 0.6\pi - 0.15\pi h$. Hence $\dfrac{dh}{dt} = \dfrac{0.6\pi - 0.15\pi h}{16\pi} = \dfrac{0.6 - 0.15h}{16}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Conical tank with water depth h, where the radius scales with h (e.g. r = h/2)
V = (1/3)πr²h becomes V = (π/12)h³, so dV/dh = (π/4)h² is a function of h, not a constant; the bridge structure is identical but algebra is messier.
Expanding sphere (e.g. balloon) with volume inflated at a given rate, asked for dr/dt
Use V = (4/3)πr³, dV/dr = 4πr², then dr/dt = (dV/dt)/(4πr²).
Spreading circular oil slick where area increases at a given rate and dr/dt is wanted
Bridge via A = πr², so dA/dr = 2πr and dr/dt = (dA/dt)/(2πr) — same chain-rule pattern with area replacing volume.
Cuboid tank with uniform cross-section A, depth h
V = Ah gives dV/dh = A, so dh/dt = (dV/dt)/A — the simplest instance of the bridge.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating dV/dt as if it were dh/dt directly, losing the geometric conversion factor entirely.
  • Forgetting the cross-sectional area must appear when converting a volume rate to a depth rate.
  • Confusing dV/dh (a geometric derivative) with dV/dt (a physical rate) and substituting one for the other.

Pitfalls

  • Using diameter instead of radius in πr² (e.g. writing 64π instead of 16π for r = 4).
  • Inverting the chain rule as dh/dt = (dV/dh)(dV/dt) instead of dividing.
  • Dropping π prematurely or failing to cancel it, leaving an unsimplified answer.
  • Sign error in dV/dt by adding outflow instead of subtracting it.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P3/P4 connected-rates-of-change material; uses only chain rule, basic mensuration formulae, and elementary differentiation, all firmly within the syllabus.
