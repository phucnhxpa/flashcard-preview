Complex Numbers

Filter intersection points using half-line directional constraint from arg locus

Family: Domain Restriction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an arg(z − z₀) = θ locus has been combined algebraically with another locus (circle, line, perpendicular bisector) and the resulting equation yields two candidate points. The arg condition defines a half-line (ray) from z₀, not the full line through z₀, so candidates on the opposite ray must be rejected. Cue phrases: 'find the point of intersection', 'the half-line meets the circle at R', or any setup where squaring/eliminating arg has lost the directional information.

Motivation — Why this works

The equation arg(z − z₀) = θ specifies both a slope AND a direction from z₀: it is the open ray starting at z₀ heading at angle θ. When we replace arg by its tan or by the Cartesian line equation y − y₀ = m(x − x₀), we silently extend the ray to a full line, introducing spurious solutions on the opposite ray (angle θ + π). Restoring the directional constraint — sign of (x − x₀) and (y − y₀) consistent with cos θ and sin θ — eliminates these phantom intersections.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the algebra produced two candidate points, but my locus was arg(z − z₀) = θ, a half-line — one candidate may sit on the opposite ray.
  2. I rule out using the line equation alone as sufficient: squaring or substituting tan θ has discarded the sign information, so I must reimpose direction.
  3. I commit to a directional sign test based on the quadrant of θ: e.g. for θ = −3π/4, both (x − x₀) < 0 and (y − y₀) < 0 are required.
  4. I evaluate each candidate's coordinates against the fixed point z₀ = x₀ + iy₀, using decimals if the surds are close to the boundary.
  5. Tactically, I check both inequalities (x and y), not just one, since a single coordinate can be ambiguous when θ is near ±π/2 or 0.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the fixed point z₀ = x₀ + iy₀ and the angle θ of the half-line.
  2. Determine the required signs of (x − x₀) and (y − y₀) from cos θ and sin θ.
  3. For each algebraic candidate, compute (numerically if needed) x − x₀ and y − y₀.
  4. Reject any candidate whose signs do not match; accept those that do.
  5. State the surviving point(s) as the valid intersection, in the form a + bi.

Worked Micro-Example

Half-line: $\arg(z - (8+6i)) = -\tfrac{3\pi}{4}$ meets circle $|z - (8+6i)| = 4$. Substituting $y - 6 = x - 8$ gives $(x-8)^2 = 8$, so $x = 8 \pm 2\sqrt{2}$. Direction $-3\pi/4$ requires $x < 8$ and $y < 6$, so reject $x = 8 + 2\sqrt{2}$. Valid point: $z = (8 - 2\sqrt{2}) + (6 - 2\sqrt{2})i$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Half-line meets a perpendicular bisector |z − a| = |z − b|
Solve the bisector's Cartesian equation simultaneously with the line through z₀ of slope tan θ; usually one intersection, but still verify direction.
Half-line meets another half-line
Apply the directional sign test for BOTH rays; intersection exists only if the unique algebraic point satisfies both direction constraints.
Half-line tangent to a circle
Discriminant zero gives one algebraic point; still verify it lies on the correct ray, otherwise the half-line misses the circle entirely.
θ = 0, π/2, π, or −π/2 (axis-aligned rays)
Direction reduces to a single inequality (e.g. θ = 0 needs only x > x₀); the other coordinate equals y₀ automatically.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating arg(z − z₀) = θ as a full line and accepting both algebraic intersections.
  • Applying the wrong directional test, e.g. confusing θ = −3π/4 with θ = π/4 and checking the wrong sign.
  • Checking only the x-inequality when θ is near ±π/2 (where x − x₀ ≈ 0 is uninformative).
  • Forgetting that arg is undefined at z = z₀, so z₀ itself is excluded even if it satisfies all other equations.

Pitfalls

  • If both algebraic solutions lie on the correct ray (e.g. the ray enters and exits a circle on the same side), both are valid — never assume exactly one is rejected.
  • When candidates are close to x₀ or y₀, surd values can be deceptive; use decimal approximations to confirm sign.
  • Sign of sin θ and cos θ must be read from the actual angle, not from tan θ (which loses sign information).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL FP2/FP3 Argand-diagram material: arg loci as half-lines, intersections with circles or perpendicular bisectors, and exclusion of spurious points by direction.
