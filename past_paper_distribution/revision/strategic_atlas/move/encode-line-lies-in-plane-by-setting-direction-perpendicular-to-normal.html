Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a problem requires a line to lie entirely within a given plane Π, or asks for a line in Π through a specified point with some additional condition (e.g., perpendicular to another line, intersecting a given line). Look for phrases like 'l lies in the plane', 'line contained in Π', or 'line in Π that also...'. Often paired with a base-point check: the point on the line must already be in Π.

Motivation — Why this works

A line lies in a plane iff (i) one of its points is in the plane, and (ii) its direction vector is parallel to the plane — equivalently perpendicular to the plane's normal n. The dot-product condition e·n = 0 converts the geometric containment into a single scalar linear equation in the components of e. This collapses three unknown direction components to two, leaving just enough freedom to satisfy one further geometric requirement.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the line is required to lie in plane Π, not merely to meet it — this is a containment condition, not an intersection condition.
  2. Rule out using the Cartesian equation of Π directly on a parametric point (it works but produces an identity in the parameter, which is messier than the normal-dot-product shortcut).
  3. Commit to the direction-perpendicular-to-normal encoding because it instantly gives one clean linear equation in the direction components.
  4. Bridge: write the unknown direction as e = (a,b,c), read the normal n off the plane equation, and form e·n = 0.
  5. Tactically, solve the resulting equation for the 'ugliest' component (largest coefficient or the one that simplifies later substitution), then carry the two-parameter family into the next constraint.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the normal n to the plane Π from its Cartesian form ax + by + cz = d.
  2. Write the unknown line direction as e = (a, b, c) (or with other letters).
  3. Impose e · n = 0, giving one linear equation in a, b, c.
  4. Solve for one component in terms of the other two (e.g., c = 2a + 4b).
  5. Verify (or invoke) that the chosen base point of the line lies in Π so containment is fully secured.
  6. Substitute the reduced direction into any remaining constraint (perpendicularity, intersection, angle) to fix the remaining ratio.

Worked Micro-Example

Plane Π: $2x + 4y - z = 7$, so $\mathbf{n} = (2,4,-1)$. Find direction $\mathbf{e}=(a,b,c)$ of a line in Π. Impose $\mathbf{e}\cdot\mathbf{n}=0$: $2a+4b-c=0$, giving $c=2a+4b$. Hence $\mathbf{e}=(a,b,2a+4b)=a(1,0,2)+b(0,1,4)$, a 2-parameter family of admissible directions, ready for the next condition.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line must be perpendicular to another line l₁ while lying in Π
Combine e·n = 0 with e·d₁ = 0; two equations leave a 1-parameter direction (take cross product n × d₁).
Line in Π passing through a given external point Q
First project Q onto Π or check Q ∈ Π; if Q ∉ Π, no line through Q can lie in Π.
Reflected line in a mirror plane Π
The reflected direction's component along n flips sign; the in-plane component (perpendicular to n) is preserved.
Line of intersection of two planes Π₁, Π₂
Direction must satisfy e·n₁ = 0 and e·n₂ = 0, so e = n₁ × n₂.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'line lies in Π' as merely 'line meets Π', and only enforcing one point on the plane.
  • Setting the direction equal to the normal rather than perpendicular to it.
  • Using the plane's Cartesian constant d in the dot-product equation (the constraint on direction is homogeneous: e·n = 0, not e·n = d).

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to verify the base point P lies in Π — without this, e·n = 0 only makes the line parallel to Π, not contained in it.
  • Algebra slip when reading off n: missing the sign on the −z term.
  • Eliminating a component but then reintroducing it inconsistently in a later substitution.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure vectors: planes given in scalar/Cartesian form, lines in vector form, and the scalar product condition a·b = 0 for perpendicularity.
