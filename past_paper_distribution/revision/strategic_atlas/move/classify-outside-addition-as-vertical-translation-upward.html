Algebra & Functions

Classify outside-addition as vertical translation upward

Family: Graph Transformation Analysis Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a transformation is written in the form $y = f(x) + a$ with the constant added (or subtracted) outside the function bracket. Typical cues: a question asks to sketch $y = f(x) + 3$, $y = \sqrt{x} - 2$, or to state the image of a labelled point under such a transformation. Must be distinguished from $y = f(x + a)$, where the constant sits inside the bracket and produces a horizontal shift. Look for the position of the constant relative to the $f(\cdot)$ argument as the decisive cue.

Motivation — Why this works

Adding $a$ outside $f$ replaces every output $y$ by $y + a$ while leaving inputs $x$ unchanged, which is exactly the geometric action of sliding the whole graph vertically by $a$. Outside transformations act in the 'obvious' direction (the sign of $a$ matches the direction of the shift), in contrast to inside transformations which act in the opposite direction. This makes $y = f(x) + a$ a translation by the vector $\binom{0}{a}$. Recognising this preserves the shape of the curve and gives a fast, error-resistant way to compute image points and intercepts.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the constant $+3$ sits outside $f$, attached to the output, not to the argument $x$.
  2. Rule out a horizontal shift: that would require $f(x+3)$ with the constant inside the bracket.
  3. Commit to a vertical translation by $\binom{0}{3}$ because outside-additive constants shift $y$-values directly.
  4. Bridge to operations: every point $(x,y)$ on $y=f(x)$ maps to $(x, y+3)$, so the shape is preserved and only the height changes.
  5. Tactically transform the key features (starting point, intercepts, labelled points) by adding 3 to their $y$-coordinates and re-label the sketch.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the form: $y = f(x) + a$ with constant outside $\Rightarrow$ vertical translation by $\binom{0}{a}$.
  2. Apply the map $(x,y) \mapsto (x, y+a)$ to each labelled point on the original curve.
  3. Update key features: starting point, $y$-intercept (raises by $a$), and any given marked points.
  4. Write the explicit transformed equation, e.g. $y = \sqrt{x} + 3$.
  5. Sketch the new curve with identical shape, shifted up by $a$ units, and label transformed points clearly.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $y = \sqrt{x}$ with $P(9,3)$ on the curve, sketch $y = \sqrt{x} + 3$. The transformation is $y = f(x) + 3$, a translation by $\binom{0}{3}$. The starting point $(0,0)$ maps to $(0,3)$ and $P(9,3)$ maps to $P'(9,6)$. The new curve has the same shape, starts on the $y$-axis at $(0,3)$, and passes through $(9,6)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$y = f(x) - 2$
Translation by $\binom{0}{-2}$: shift the whole graph down by 2; subtract 2 from each $y$-coordinate.
$y = f(x+3)$ (inside-addition)
Horizontal translation by $\binom{-3}{0}$ (left by 3); changes $x$-coordinates not $y$-coordinates — the move students most often confuse with the outside case.
$y = 3f(x)$
Vertical stretch (scale factor 3 from the $x$-axis), still an outside transformation but multiplicative; multiply $y$-coordinates by 3.
$y = -f(x) + a$
Apply the reflection in the $x$-axis first ($(x,y)\mapsto(x,-y)$), then translate up by $a$.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading $f(x)+3$ as $f(x+3)$ and shifting left instead of up.
  • Treating $+3$ as a stretch (e.g. confusing with $3f(x)$) and altering the shape rather than the position.
  • Applying the shift to $x$-coordinates because of muscle memory from inside-transformation questions.

Pitfalls

  • Drawing the new curve still starting at the origin instead of at $(0,a)$.
  • Forgetting to relabel transformed points (e.g. leaving $P(9,3)$ instead of $P'(9,6)$).
  • Distorting the curve's shape rather than rigidly translating it.
  • Omitting the new $y$-intercept value when the question asks for coordinates of intersections with axes.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1/P2) graph transformations: single translations of the form $y=f(x)+a$, with image points and equations expected to be stated explicitly.
