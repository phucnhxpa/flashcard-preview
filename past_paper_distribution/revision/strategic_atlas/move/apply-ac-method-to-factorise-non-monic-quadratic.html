Algebra & Functions

Apply ac-method to factorise non-monic quadratic

Family: Strategic Factorisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A quadratic $ax^2+bx+c$ has leading coefficient $a\neq 1$ (e.g. $6x^2-7x-3$) and the question explicitly demands solving or factorising 'by factorisation' rather than via the quadratic formula or completing the square. The discriminant looks like a perfect square (signalling rational roots exist), so factorisation is feasible. The factor pairs of $a$ and $c$ are numerous enough that blind trial of $(mx+p)(nx+q)$ would be slow.

Motivation — Why this works

If $ax^2+bx+c$ factorises over the integers as $(mx+p)(nx+q)$, then $mn=a$, $pq=c$, and $mq+np=b$, which forces the two integers $mq$ and $np$ to satisfy $(mq)(np)=mnpq=ac$ and $(mq)+(np)=b$. So the existence of an integer factorisation is equivalent to finding two integers with product $ac$ and sum $b$. This collapses a two-dimensional search (over factor pairs of $a$ and of $c$) into a one-dimensional search over factor pairs of $ac$, after which grouping mechanically recovers the brackets.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Student notices $a\neq 1$, so the simple monic template $(x+p)(x+q)$ is unavailable.
  2. They rule out the quadratic formula and completing the square because the question stipulates factorisation.
  3. They commit to the ac-method because it replaces two-bracket trial-and-error with a single search for a number pair $(p_1,p_2)$ with $p_1p_2=ac$ and $p_1+p_2=b$.
  4. They bridge to the concrete computation by evaluating $ac$ and listing factor pairs of $|ac|$, then assigning signs to match $b$.
  5. Tactically, once the split is found, they choose a grouping order that visibly produces a common binomial, and check the sign extracted from the second group.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute $ac$.
  2. Find integers $p_1,p_2$ with $p_1p_2=ac$ and $p_1+p_2=b$ by scanning factor pairs of $|ac|$ and adjusting signs.
  3. Split the middle term: $ax^2+p_1x+p_2x+c$.
  4. Group into two pairs and factor out the HCF of each pair, ensuring the same binomial appears.
  5. Extract the common binomial factor to obtain $(mx+p)(nx+q)$.
  6. If solving, set each bracket to zero and read off the roots.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $6x^2-7x-3=0$ by factorisation. Here $ac=6\times(-3)=-18$ and $b=-7$; the pair $(2,-9)$ works since $2\cdot(-9)=-18$ and $2+(-9)=-7$. Split: $6x^2+2x-9x-3=0$, group: $2x(3x+1)-3(3x+1)=0$, factor: $(3x+1)(2x-3)=0$. Hence $x=-\tfrac{1}{3}$ or $x=\tfrac{3}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Monic quadratic $x^2+bx+c$
$ac=c$, so the method degenerates to finding two numbers with product $c$ and sum $b$, written directly as $(x+p_1)(x+p_2)$ without grouping.
Quadratic in a substituted variable, e.g. $6u^2-7u-3$ where $u=x^2$ or $u=e^x$
Apply ac-method in $u$, factorise, then back-substitute and solve the resulting equations in $x$.
Hidden quadratic across two terms, e.g. $6\sin^2\theta-7\sin\theta-3=0$
Treat $\sin\theta$ as the variable; ac-method gives $(3\sin\theta+1)(2\sin\theta-3)=0$, then solve trigonometric equations on the given interval.
Quadratic with a common numerical factor, e.g. $12x^2-14x-6$
Extract the overall factor first ($2$ here) to reduce $ac$ before searching, keeping coefficients smaller.
Grid/box layout
Same arithmetic ($ac$ and the pair $(p_1,p_2)$), but the split terms are placed in a 2x2 grid and HCFs are read off rows and columns instead of by algebraic grouping.

Common Misreadings

  • Searching for two numbers with product $c$ instead of $ac$, ignoring the leading coefficient.
  • Reading 'solve' as licence to use the quadratic formula even when the question specifies factorisation.
  • Assuming the quadratic must factorise; if $ac$ has no integer pair summing to $b$, the student should conclude the quadratic does not factorise over $\mathbb{Z}$ rather than force a wrong split.

Pitfalls

  • Choosing a grouping order that fails to produce a common binomial, e.g. grouping $(6x^2-3)+(2x-9x)$ instead of $(6x^2+2x)+(-9x-3)$.
  • Sign error when factoring a negative HCF from the second group: writing $-3(3x+1)$ as $-3(3x-1)$.
  • Forgetting to set each bracket to zero after factorising when the question asks to solve.
  • Mis-pairing signs of $p_1,p_2$: e.g. taking $(-2,9)$ which sums to $+7$ rather than $-7$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics P1 (quadratics) and reused in P2/P3 for substituted/hidden quadratics in indices, logs, and trigonometry; no content beyond standard A-level algebra is required.
