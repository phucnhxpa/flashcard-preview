Algebra & Functions

Classify graph transformation by inside-vs-outside position relative to f

Family: Transformation Classification Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question presents a new function defined in terms of f, such as y = f(x) + a, y = f(x) − 2, y = af(x), y = −f(x), y = f(x + p), y = f(kx), or y = f(−x), and asks for a sketch, image of key points, or coordinates of transformed features. The visual cue is a single symbol or constant attached to f either OUTSIDE the bracket (acting on the output) or INSIDE the bracket (acting on the input). Equivalent phrasings: 'describe the transformation', 'sketch the curve with equation y = ...', 'state the coordinates of the image of point A under …'. The trigger is the structural choice: where, relative to f's bracket, has the modification been placed?

Motivation — Why this works

The position of the modification relative to f completely determines whether the transformation is vertical (acts on y-coordinates, behaves intuitively) or horizontal (acts on x-coordinates, behaves with the 'opposite/reciprocal' rule). This dichotomy is the foundation of every transformation question in IAL Pure: every subsequent calculation — direction of shift, scale factor, axis of reflection — is fixed by this single classification. Getting it right reduces the problem to a mechanical coordinate mapping; getting it wrong produces an answer that is reliably reversed. The principle underlying the 'opposite' rule for inside-bracket changes is that we are asking 'what new x makes the bracket equal to the old x?' — i.e. solving for the input that reproduces the original output.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. First I LOOK at where the modification sits: is the constant/sign/multiplier attached after f has produced its value (outside the bracket) or applied to x before f acts (inside the bracket)?
  2. If it is OUTSIDE f, I commit to a vertical transformation acting directly on y, with no sign or reciprocal reversal — the change behaves exactly as written.
  3. If it is INSIDE f, I commit to a horizontal transformation acting on x, but I deliberately flag that the effect is the 'opposite' of what the symbol naively suggests: +p inside means shift LEFT by p, multiplier k inside means stretch by factor 1/k.
  4. I rule out treating f(x) − 2 and f(x − 2) as equivalent, and I rule out treating −f(x) and f(−x) as equivalent — these are the two classic confusions and the inside/outside test resolves both.
  5. Before touching any specific point, I write down the coordinate mapping (x, y) → (x', y') for the transformation. Only then do I substitute the given key points.
  6. If the question combines transformations (e.g. y = af(x) + b or y = f(kx + p)), I separate them: each constant is classified individually by its position, and I apply inside-bracket changes first when reading from the bracket outward.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Locate every modification and label it 'inside' (within f(...) ) or 'outside' (acting on f(...) as a whole).
  2. For each OUTSIDE modification, write the vertical mapping: y = f(x) + a gives (x, y) → (x, y + a); y = af(x) gives (x, y) → (x, ay); y = −f(x) gives (x, y) → (x, −y).
  3. For each INSIDE modification, write the horizontal mapping with the reversed/reciprocal rule: y = f(x + a) gives (x, y) → (x − a, y); y = f(kx) gives (x, y) → (x/k, y); y = f(−x) gives (x, y) → (−x, y).
  4. Apply the mapping to each named point on the original graph to obtain its image.
  5. State the transformation in words (e.g. 'translation by vector (−p, 0)', 'horizontal stretch, scale factor 1/2, about the y-axis', 'reflection in the x-axis') to match the mark scheme's required language.

Worked Micro-Example

Given that the curve y = f(x) passes through A(4, 3), find the image of A on y = f(2x) − 1. The '2x' is INSIDE f → horizontal stretch, scale factor $\tfrac{1}{2}$: $(x,y) \to (x/2, y)$, so $(4,3) \to (2,3)$. The '−1' is OUTSIDE f → vertical translation down 1: $(2,3) \to (2,2)$. Image: $A'(2, 2)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

y = f(x − a) with a > 0 (inside, additive)
Horizontal translation RIGHT by a; mapping (x, y) → (x + a, y) — sign flips because the modification is inside.
y = af(x) with a > 1 (outside, multiplicative)
Vertical stretch, scale factor a, about the x-axis; mapping (x, y) → (x, ay) — no reciprocal because outside.
y = f(kx) with 0 < k < 1 (inside, multiplicative)
Horizontal stretch, scale factor 1/k > 1 (an expansion), about the y-axis; mapping (x, y) → (x/k, y).
y = f(−x) vs y = −f(x) (sign change, opposite positions)
f(−x) reflects in the y-axis: (x, y) → (−x, y). −f(x) reflects in the x-axis: (x, y) → (x, −y). Position of the minus sign decides which axis.
Combined y = af(x + p) + q
Treat each constant separately by position: inside (+p) gives horizontal shift by −p; outside multiplier a gives vertical stretch ×a; outside (+q) gives vertical shift +q. Mapping (x, y) → (x − p, ay + q).

Common Misreadings

  • Reading y = f(x) − 2 as y = f(x − 2): the first is vertical (down 2), the second is horizontal (right 2).
  • Reading y = f(2x) as a horizontal stretch by factor 2 (doubling x): inside-bracket scaling uses the RECIPROCAL, so the factor is 1/2.
  • Reading y = f(x + p) as a shift to the right by p: inside-bracket additions reverse sign, so it's a shift LEFT by p.
  • Treating y = −f(x) and y = f(−x) as the same reflection: they reflect in different axes.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the 'opposite-direction' rule for inside-bracket transformations and shifting/stretching the wrong way.
  • Applying the reciprocal rule to outside modifications by mistake (e.g. claiming 2f(x) is a vertical stretch by 1/2).
  • When combining transformations, applying them in the wrong order — inside-bracket changes should be processed first when reading the formula from the argument outward.
  • Mapping a single point correctly but forgetting that asymptotes, intercepts, and turning points must all be transformed by the same mapping.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 (graphs and transformations). The transformations f(x)+a, f(x+a), af(x), f(ax), −f(x), f(−x) and simple combinations are examinable; matrix-based transformations of the plane (FP1) and non-linear distortions are out of scope here and should not be invoked. No formula booklet entry is required — these mappings are expected to be memorised.
