Sequences & Series

Set up simultaneous equations from two geometric series properties

Family: Model To Equations Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a geometric series problem supplies two independent pieces of information (e.g. $S_\infty$ and a specific term, or two specific terms, or $S_n$ and a term) and asks you to find or 'show that' a relation in $a$ or $r$. Cues include phrases like 'a geometric series has sum to infinity ... and second term ...' or 'the third term is ... and the sixth term is ...'. Two unknowns ($a$ and $r$) need pinning down, so two equations must be formed before any elimination can begin.

Motivation — Why this works

Each standard geometric formula — $u_n = ar^{n-1}$, $S_n = a(1-r^n)/(1-r)$, $S_\infty = a/(1-r)$ for $|r|<1$ — produces one equation in the two unknowns $a$ and $r$. A single equation is underdetermined; two independent equations form a solvable system. Translating the worded data into algebra is the precondition for eliminating $a$ (typically by substitution or division) to obtain a single equation in $r$, which is what 'show that' parts usually demand.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice two unknowns ($a$ and $r$) and two distinct facts about the series, so the problem is exactly determined.
  2. I rule out trying to solve from one equation alone, and I rule out guessing $r$ — both equations must be written first.
  3. I commit to the simultaneous-equations route because each given fact maps cleanly onto one standard GP formula.
  4. I pick the right formula for each fact: $S_\infty=75$ gives $a/(1-r)=75$; $u_2=18$ gives $ar=18$.
  5. I anticipate the next move: the simpler equation ($ar=18$) is the better one to rearrange for $a$, ready for substitution.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two given pieces of data and the two unknowns ($a$ and $r$).
  2. Match each piece of data to its standard GP formula: $u_n=ar^{n-1}$, $S_n=a(1-r^n)/(1-r)$, or $S_\infty=a/(1-r)$.
  3. Write Equation 1 from the first fact, e.g. $\dfrac{a}{1-r}=75$.
  4. Write Equation 2 from the second fact, e.g. $ar=18$.
  5. Note any domain constraint (e.g. $|r|<1$ if $S_\infty$ is used) to carry forward.

Worked Micro-Example

A GP has $S_\infty=75$ and $u_2=18$. From $S_\infty=\dfrac{a}{1-r}=75$ we get $a=75(1-r)$. From $u_2=ar=18$ we get $a=\dfrac{18}{r}$. Equating: $75(1-r)=\dfrac{18}{r}$, i.e. $75r(1-r)=18$, leading to $25r^2-25r+6=0$ (a single equation in $r$, ready to solve).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Given $S_n$ (finite) and a term $u_k$.
Use $S_n=a(1-r^n)/(1-r)$ for one equation and $u_k=ar^{k-1}$ for the other; no $|r|<1$ constraint required.
Given two specific terms, e.g. $u_3$ and $u_6$.
Write $ar^2=\ldots$ and $ar^5=\ldots$, then divide to eliminate $a$ immediately, giving $r^3$ directly.
Given $S_\infty$ and $S_n$ for a specific $n$.
Form $a/(1-r)=S_\infty$ and $a(1-r^n)/(1-r)=S_n$; dividing gives $1-r^n=S_n/S_\infty$, a one-variable equation.
Arithmetic series analogue.
Replace GP formulas with $u_n=a+(n-1)d$ and $S_n=\tfrac{n}{2}(2a+(n-1)d)$; the simultaneous-equations strategy is identical.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading $u_2$ as $ar^2$ instead of $ar$ (off-by-one in the exponent of $ar^{n-1}$).
  • Treating 'second term' as the second partial sum $S_2$ rather than the term $u_2$.
  • Using $S_\infty=a/(1-r)$ without checking that $|r|<1$ is even plausible from the data.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the constraint $|r|<1$ when $S_\infty$ is involved, leading to a spurious root being kept later.
  • Algebraic slip when clearing the fraction $a/(1-r)=75$ — failing to multiply through by $(1-r)$ on both sides.
  • Choosing the messier equation to rearrange for $a$, producing avoidable algebra in the elimination step.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 geometric series content: standard formulas $u_n=ar^{n-1}$, $S_n=a(1-r^n)/(1-r)$, and $S_\infty=a/(1-r)$ for $|r|<1$. No tools beyond GCSE-level simultaneous equations are needed.
