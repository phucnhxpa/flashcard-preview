Substitute a single repeated sub-expression to reduce to a standard quadratic

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 5 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fire this move when the equation contains exactly one repeated sub-expression appearing in two powers with a 2:1 ratio plus a constant term — i.e. it has the shape a·[E(x)]² + b·[E(x)] + c = 0. Common surface forms: even-power-only polynomials (x⁴ and x²), surds paired with their squares (x and √x), exponentials with paired exponents (2^{2x} and 2^x, or e^{2x} and e^x), and trig powers (sin²x and sin x). The visual cue is 'three terms, one repeated chunk, the higher power is exactly the square of the lower'. Equivalently: the equation looks transcendental or quartic, but algebraically it is a 'quadratic in disguise'.

Motivation — Why this works

Letting u = E(x) collapses the equation into a standard quadratic au² + bu + c = 0, unlocking the full quadratic toolkit (factorisation, quadratic formula, discriminant, Vieta's formulas, completing the square). This bridges a hard surface (quartic, surd, exponential, trigonometric) to a problem class the student has already mastered. The substitution is valid because E(x) is a single-valued expression, and any range restriction on E(x) (e.g. u > 0 for u = √x or u = 2^x; −1 ≤ u ≤ 1 for u = sin x) becomes a filter for which roots in u correspond to real solutions in x.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the equation has only three terms and that one sub-expression E(x) appears in two powers, with the higher power being exactly the square of the lower (e.g. x⁴ = (x²)², x = (√x)², 2^{2x} = (2^x)²).
  2. Rule out direct attacks: factorising the quartic in x, isolating-and-squaring the surd, or taking logs of the exponential equation are all messier and error-prone because the constant term blocks clean isolation.
  3. Commit to substitution because the 2:1 power ratio is the diagnostic signature of a 'disguised quadratic' — the standard, lowest-risk route.
  4. Bridge to action: define u = E(x) (the lower-power piece), so the higher-power piece becomes u², and rewrite every occurrence to obtain au² + bu + c = 0.
  5. Tactical record-keeping: immediately write down the domain restriction on u induced by the original variable (u > 0 for √x or 2^x; u ≠ 0 for x²; −1 ≤ u ≤ 1 for sin x), so that spurious quadratic roots can be rejected.
  6. After solving for u, plan the back-substitution u = E(x) to recover x, and check each candidate against the original equation.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the repeated sub-expression E(x) and verify the 2:1 power ratio.
  2. Let u = E(x); deduce u² replaces the higher-power term.
  3. Rewrite the equation as au² + bu + c = 0.
  4. Record the domain restriction on u (e.g. u > 0, u ≥ 0, or u ∈ [−1,1]).
  5. Solve the quadratic by factorisation or the quadratic formula.
  6. Discard any u-roots that violate the domain restriction.
  7. Back-substitute E(x) = u to solve for x, and verify in the original equation.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $2(2^x)^2 - 7(2^x) + 6 = 0$. Let $y = 2^x$ with $y>0$. Then $2y^2 - 7y + 6 = 0 \Rightarrow (2y-3)(y-2)=0 \Rightarrow y=\tfrac{3}{2}$ or $y=2$. Both are positive, so $2^x = \tfrac{3}{2}$ giving $x = \log_2\tfrac{3}{2}$, and $2^x = 2$ giving $x = 1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quartic with only even powers, e.g. $kx^4 + 9x^2 - 1 = 0$
Let $u = x^2$ with $u \ge 0$; solve the quadratic in $u$, then $x = \pm\sqrt{u}$ for each non-negative root.
Surd equation, e.g. $2x - 3\sqrt{x} - 14 = 0$
Let $u = \sqrt{x}$ with $u \ge 0$; then $x = u^2$ and the equation becomes $2u^2 - 3u - 14 = 0$. Reject any negative $u$-root before squaring back.
Exponential equation, e.g. $e^{2x} - 5e^x + 6 = 0$
Let $y = e^x$ with $y > 0$; solve the quadratic, then take $\ln$ of each positive root.
Trigonometric equation, e.g. $2\sin^2 x - 3\sin x - 2 = 0$
Let $u = \sin x$ with $-1 \le u \le 1$; solve and discard any out-of-range roots before solving $\sin x = u$.
Fractional-index equation, e.g. $x^{2/3} - x^{1/3} - 6 = 0$
Let $u = x^{1/3}$ (no sign restriction since cube roots accept negatives); solve and cube each root.

Common Misreadings

  • Choosing the wrong sub-expression — e.g. letting $u = x$ instead of $u = x^2$ in a quartic, or $u = 2^{2x}$ instead of $u = 2^x$, which fails to collapse the middle term.
  • Failing to spot the 2:1 ratio when terms are written as surds rather than fractional indices (e.g. $\sqrt{x}$ vs $x^{1/2}$).
  • Trying to square the whole equation or take logs term-by-term instead of substituting, which produces spurious solutions or invalid manipulations.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the domain restriction on $u$ (e.g. $u > 0$ for $u = 2^x$ or $u = \sqrt{x}$), and accepting a negative $u$-root that has no real $x$-preimage.
  • Forgetting to back-substitute from $u$ to $x$ at the end and reporting $u$-values as final answers.
  • Arithmetic slip in back-substitution — e.g. writing $x = 7/2$ instead of $x = 49/4$ when $\sqrt{x} = 7/2$.
  • Losing solutions in the $x^2$ case by forgetting the $\pm$ when taking the square root.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1–P4) and Further Pure scope. The technique appears for quadratic-in-disguise equations across polynomial, surd, exponential, and trigonometric contexts. No formula-booklet entry is invoked beyond the standard quadratic formula. Avoid extending to multi-variable substitutions or non-elementary substitutions, which lie outside IAL scope.
