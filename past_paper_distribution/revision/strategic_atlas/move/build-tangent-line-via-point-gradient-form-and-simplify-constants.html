Coordinate Geometry

Build tangent line via point-gradient form and simplify constants

Family: Tangent Construction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger â€” When to use

Fires whenever a point of tangency $(x_1,y_1)$ and a gradient $m$ are both already known explicitly â€” typically after differentiating implicitly or applying the chain rule and substituting the point. Cue phrases include 'find the equation of the tangent at P', 'show that the tangent is...', or any follow-up part that needs intercepts of the tangent. Choose this over $y=mx+c$ whenever solving for $c$ would be an extra unmotivated step.

Motivation â€” Why this works

Point-gradient form $y-y_1=m(x-x_1)$ is algebraically equivalent to $y=mx+c$ but skips the intermediate step of computing $c$ separately. When the point coordinates contain surds or parameters that combine cleanly with the gradient (e.g. $m\cdot x_1$ collapses with $y_1$), the simplification reveals symmetric structure â€” for hyperbolas a factor of 2 typically emerges in the y-intercept. This clean closed form then makes axis intercepts, midpoints, and area calculations in later parts immediate.

Thought Process â€” The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice I already have both the point $(x_1,y_1)$ and the gradient $m$ in hand â€” no missing data.
  2. I rule out $y=mx+c$ because it would force me to substitute and solve for $c$ as a separate algebraic step.
  3. I commit to $y-y_1=m(x-x_1)$ because it is a one-line template that absorbs both pieces of data simultaneously.
  4. Bridge: I write the template, then expand the RHS carefully â€” the product $m\cdot x_1$ is where surd/parameter cancellation usually happens.
  5. Tactical: I keep the constant terms separate during expansion so I can see the cancellation/combination, then collect into $y=mx+(\text{simplified constant})$ for downstream use.

Mechanic â€” Concrete operations

  1. Write $y-y_1=m(x-x_1)$ with the known point and gradient substituted.
  2. Expand the RHS: distribute $m$ across $(x-x_1)$ to get $mx-mx_1$.
  3. Simplify the constant $-mx_1$ using surd/index laws if applicable.
  4. Move $y_1$ to the RHS and combine like constant terms.
  5. Present the tangent in the form $y=mx+c$ (or rearrange to $ax+by=c$ if the question demands).

Worked Micro-Example

At $P\left(\tfrac{a}{\sqrt{k}},\,a\sqrt{k}\right)$ on $xy=a^2$, with gradient $m=-k$:\\ $y-a\sqrt{k}=-k\!\left(x-\tfrac{a}{\sqrt{k}}\right)=-kx+\tfrac{ka}{\sqrt{k}}=-kx+a\sqrt{k}$.\\ Hence $y=-kx+2a\sqrt{k}$, giving x-intercept $\tfrac{2a}{\sqrt{k}}$ and y-intercept $2a\sqrt{k}$ at once.

Variants â€” Same move, different surface

Parabola $y^2=4ax$ at parametric point $(at^2,2at)$ with gradient $m=1/t$
Point-gradient form gives $y-2at=\tfrac{1}{t}(x-at^2)$, which simplifies to $ty=x+at^2$ â€” the standard parametric tangent.
Ellipse $\tfrac{x^2}{a^2}+\tfrac{y^2}{b^2}=1$ at $(a\cos\theta,b\sin\theta)$ with $m=-\tfrac{b\cos\theta}{a\sin\theta}$
Same template; constants combine via $\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta=1$ to give $\tfrac{x\cos\theta}{a}+\tfrac{y\sin\theta}{b}=1$.
Circle $(x-h)^2+(y-k)^2=r^2$ at point $P$ with gradient from perpendicular-radius
Compute $m=-\tfrac{x_1-h}{y_1-k}$, then apply $y-y_1=m(x-x_1)$ identically.
Curve given parametrically with $\tfrac{dy}{dx}=\tfrac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}$
Evaluate gradient at parameter value, substitute the parametric point, then apply point-gradient form unchanged.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating point-gradient form as only valid for straight-line questions, not realising it is the standard tool for tangents to curves.
  • Believing $y=mx+c$ is required and re-deriving $c$ via substitution, doubling the work.
  • Confusing the tangent point $(x_1,y_1)$ with a generic $(x,y)$ on the curve and substituting curve equation prematurely.

Pitfalls

  • Simplifying $k\cdot\tfrac{a}{\sqrt{k}}$: students drop a factor and write $\tfrac{a}{\sqrt{k}}$ instead of $a\sqrt{k}$.
  • Sign error when distributing the negative gradient across $(x-x_1)$.
  • Forgetting to add $y_1$ back after expanding, leaving a tangent that misses the point of tangency.
  • Combining $-mx_1$ and $y_1$ before simplifying surds, which obscures the cancellation.
