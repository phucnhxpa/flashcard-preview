Algebra & Functions

Collect the unknown integral on both sides to close the reduction formula

Family: Self Referencing Rearrangement Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

After applying integration by parts (often with a Pythagorean or algebraic identity substitution), the original integral I_n reappears on the right-hand side of an equation whose left-hand side is also I_n. The equation has the schematic form I_n = [boundary term] + α·I_{n-k} + β·I_n where α, β depend on n. Visual cue: 'I_n is on both sides' — this looks circular but is actually a linear equation in the unknown I_n. Alternative phrasings: 'the integral reappears', 'IBP gave back what I started with', 'the recurrence is implicit'.

Motivation — Why this works

The reappearance of I_n is a feature, not a failure: it means we have a linear equation in I_n which can be solved algebraically to make I_n explicit in terms of I_{n-k}. Mathematically, this converts an implicit, self-referential equation into a usable two-step (or three-step) reduction formula by isolating I_n. The underlying principle is simply solving a linear equation aX = b + cX by collecting the X-terms — the same logic as 'move x's to one side' in elementary algebra. Without this step, the recurrence cannot be iterated to a base case.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that I_n appears on the LHS and also inside the RHS — initial instinct might be 'this is circular, IBP failed', but recognise instead that it is a linear equation in I_n.
  2. Rule out re-doing the integration by parts with a different choice of u/dv, or trying a substitution — these are unnecessary because the algebra closes by itself.
  3. Commit to treating I_n as an algebraic unknown: the goal is to gather all I_n terms on the left and everything else on the right.
  4. Concrete first operation: add or subtract the I_n term on the RHS to/from both sides, watching the sign carefully (sign errors here are the most common pitfall).
  5. Factor I_n from the LHS to get (1 + α)I_n = [boundary term] + β·I_{n-k}, then simplify the coefficient (1+α) into a single fraction so dividing is clean.
  6. Divide both sides by the simplified coefficient to obtain I_n = [...] + [...]·I_{n-k}, an explicit reduction formula ready to iterate.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Start from the post-IBP equation, e.g. I_n = B(x) + α(n)·I_{n-k} + β(n)·I_n.
  2. Move β(n)·I_n to the left: I_n − β(n)·I_n = B(x) + α(n)·I_{n-k}.
  3. Factor the LHS: (1 − β(n))·I_n = B(x) + α(n)·I_{n-k}.
  4. Simplify 1 − β(n) into a single fraction (common denominator).
  5. Divide through by this coefficient to isolate I_n explicitly.
  6. State the final reduction formula clearly, noting any restriction on n (e.g. denominator ≠ 0).

Worked Micro-Example

From IBP on $I_n=\int \sin^n x\,dx$, one gets $I_n = -\sin^{n-1}x\cos x + (n-1)I_{n-2} - (n-1)I_n$. Add $(n-1)I_n$ to both sides: $nI_n = -\sin^{n-1}x\cos x + (n-1)I_{n-2}$. Divide by $n$: $I_n = -\tfrac{1}{n}\sin^{n-1}x\cos x + \tfrac{n-1}{n}I_{n-2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Reduction for $\int \sin^n x\,dx$ or $\int \cos^n x\,dx$ (Wallis-type)
Coefficient is (n−1)/n; reduces index by 2; same collect-and-divide algebra.
Reduction for $\int \sec^n x\,dx$
After IBP and using $\tan^2 x = \sec^2 x - 1$, I_n reappears with coefficient (n−2); collect to get $(n-1)I_n = \sec^{n-2}x\tan x + (n-2)I_{n-2}$.
Reduction for $\int x^{n-2}(x^3+1)^{1/2}\,dx$ type with index drop of 3
Coefficient becomes (2n+5)/9; reduces index by 3 instead of 2; algebra is identical — factor and divide.
$\int (\ln x)^n\,dx$ or $\int x^n e^x\,dx$
If IBP recreates I_n (less common here, usually it reduces directly), apply the same collect-and-isolate procedure.

Common Misreadings

  • Seeing I_n on both sides and concluding 'IBP didn't work' — abandoning the method instead of solving algebraically.
  • Treating the equation as a definition (substituting back into itself) rather than as a linear equation to solve.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when moving the I_n term across — subtracting instead of adding (or vice versa) flips the sign of the final coefficient.
  • Arithmetic slip when combining 1 + α(n) into a single fraction (e.g. mis-expanding $1 + \tfrac{2(n-2)}{9}$ as $\tfrac{2n-3}{9}$ instead of $\tfrac{2n+5}{9}$).
  • Forgetting to divide the boundary term by the coefficient as well — only dividing the I_{n-k} term.
  • Not noting the restriction on n that makes the denominator nonzero.

A-level Scope Notes

This move is core to FP2/FP3 reduction-formula questions in Edexcel IAL Further Pure. No formula booklet entry is needed — it is pure algebraic rearrangement following IBP (booklet entry: integration by parts). Avoid generalising to improper integrals or convergence arguments, which are out of A-level scope.
