Integration

Flip integration limits to absorb a leading negative sign

Family: Limit Reversal Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires immediately after a u-substitution (or trig substitution) where the new variable is a DECREASING function of the original — so the new lower limit is numerically larger than the new upper limit — AND the substitution introduces a leading minus sign (e.g. du = −32t dt, or dx = −½ sin θ dθ, or u = cos x). Visual cues: the integral now reads ∫_{larger}^{smaller} with a negative coefficient out front, or a negative integrand. Equivalently: 'two negatives sitting in the working' — one from the reversed limits, one from the differential. Whenever you see this pairing, the move applies.

Motivation — Why this works

It uses the identity ∫_a^b f(x) dx = −∫_b^a f(x) dx. Swapping the limits introduces a minus sign which cancels the minus sign already present from the substitution, leaving a clean integral with conventional orientation (lower < upper) and a positive coefficient. Mathematically nothing changes, but cognitively it is a major win: every subsequent line carries one fewer sign to track, drastically reducing the chance of a sign error during evaluation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that after substitution the limits are 'backwards' (lower limit numerically greater than upper) AND there is a leading negative from du or dx.
  2. Rule out blindly evaluating as-is: carrying both negatives through partial fractions, surd manipulation, or trig identities is error-prone.
  3. Rule out 'just compute and trust the sign at the end' — students often miscount how many minus signs they have absorbed.
  4. Commit to the limit-reversal identity because it is a single, atomic housekeeping step that resolves both sign anomalies simultaneously.
  5. Execute by swapping the limits and dropping the leading minus in one motion — explicitly write 'two negatives cancel' to make the bookkeeping auditable.
  6. Confirm the result has lower limit < upper limit and a positive coefficient before continuing with the substantive integration.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two sign anomalies: leading negative coefficient (from du/dx) AND reversed limits ∫_{larger}^{smaller}.
  2. Apply ∫_{a}^{b} (−f) du = ∫_{b}^{a} f du to swap limits and remove the minus simultaneously.
  3. Rewrite the integral with limits in standard order (lower < upper) and positive coefficient.
  4. Proceed with the remaining integration on the cleaner expression.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $u = 81 - 16t^2$, so $du = -32t\,dt$ and the limits $t:0 \to 9/4$ become $u:81 \to 0$. Then $\int_0^{9/4} \frac{t}{\sqrt{81-16t^2}}\,dt = -\frac{1}{32}\int_{81}^{0} u^{-1/2}\,du = +\frac{1}{32}\int_{0}^{81} u^{-1/2}\,du$, where flipping the limits cancelled the leading $-1/32$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Trig substitution with x = a cos θ giving dx = −a sin θ dθ
The negative from dx pairs with reversed θ-limits (since cos is decreasing on [0,π/2]); flip to obtain a positive integrand on standard-direction limits.
u-sub where u = cos x on [0, π] or u = a − x
Same principle: u is a decreasing function of x, so du is negative and limits invert; flip to absorb the minus.
Surd-form integrals like ∫ t/√(a²−b²t²) dt with u = a²−b²t²
Identical move; the 1/√u or √u inside the integrand does not affect the limit-reversal logic.

Common Misreadings

  • Mistaking the reversed limits as a signal to redo the substitution differently (e.g. picking u = 16t²−81 instead) — unnecessary; limit-reversal is cleaner.
  • Thinking the negative from du must remain attached and cannot be 'moved' — it can, via the reversal identity.

Pitfalls

  • Flipping the limits but forgetting to cancel the existing negative — effectively applying the sign change twice and getting the wrong sign.
  • Cancelling the negative but forgetting to actually swap the limits — leaving an incorrect oriented integral.
  • Carrying both negatives through several lines before cancelling, increasing the chance of dropping one mid-calculation.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 (integration by substitution) and FP-level integration. The identity ∫_a^b f = −∫_b^a f is standard and not in the formula booklet — students are expected to know it. No generalizations to improper or complex integrals are required.
