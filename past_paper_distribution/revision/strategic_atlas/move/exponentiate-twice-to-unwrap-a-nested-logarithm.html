Exponentiate twice to unwrap a nested logarithm

Family: Inverse Power Operation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation contains a logarithm whose argument itself contains another logarithm of the unknown, e.g. $\ln(a+b\ln y)=f(x)$ or $\ln(\ln y)=g(x)$. Also triggered when the target form expresses $y$ as an exponential (or double exponential) of a given function of $x$, so all log layers around $y$ must be removed. Look for the cue 'isolate $y$' combined with two stacked $\ln$ symbols. Common in particular-solution work for separable ODEs whose integration produces $\ln$ on a side already containing $\ln y$.

Motivation — Why this works

Exponentiation with base $e$ is the exact inverse of $\ln$, and $e^{\ln u}=u$ peels off exactly one logarithmic layer. With two nested logarithms, two successive exponentiations are required, each preceded by algebraic tidying so the side being exponentiated is a single $\ln(\cdot)$. Between the two exponentiations one must isolate the inner $\ln y$ (subtract constants, divide coefficients), otherwise the second exponentiation will not act on $\ln y$ alone. The procedure is reversible and loses no solutions provided the original logs are defined.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that $y$ is buried under two layers of $\ln$, e.g. $\ln(1+2\ln y)=\dots$, so a direct rearrangement cannot reach $y$.
  2. Rule out taking a single exponential or trying to combine logs: log laws cannot collapse $\ln(1+2\ln y)$ because the inside is a sum, not a product.
  3. Commit to a two-stage unwrap: each $\ln$ is removed by one application of $e^{(\cdot)}$, and between them the inner $\ln y$ must be made to stand alone.
  4. First, clear any coefficient on the outer $\ln$ so the equation reads $\ln(\text{stuff with }\ln y)=h(x)$, then exponentiate to get $\text{stuff}=e^{h(x)}$.
  5. Isolate $\ln y$ algebraically, exponentiate again to obtain $y=e^{(\dots)}$, and finally massage the exponent (e.g. $1-2\cos 2x$ over $6$ rewritten via $\sec 2x$) to match the requested form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Clear any multiplicative constant on the outermost $\ln$ so the equation is $\ln(U(y,x))=h(x)$.
  2. Exponentiate both sides: $U(y,x)=e^{h(x)}$.
  3. Solve algebraically for $\ln y$, giving $\ln y = G(x)$.
  4. Exponentiate again: $y=e^{G(x)}$.
  5. Simplify $G(x)$ using identities (e.g. $1/\cos\theta=\sec\theta$) to match the target form and read off any required constants.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\tfrac12\ln(1+2\ln y)=-\tfrac16\cos 2x+\tfrac1{12}$ for $y$.\\ Multiply by $2$: $\ln(1+2\ln y)=\tfrac{1-2\cos 2x}{6}$.\\ Exponentiate: $1+2\ln y=e^{(1-2\cos 2x)/6}$, so $\ln y=\tfrac12\!\left(e^{(1-2\cos 2x)/6}-1\right)$.\\ Exponentiate again: $y=\exp\!\left(\tfrac{e^{(1-2\cos 2x)/6}-1}{2}\right)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Single nested log $\ln(\ln y)=x$
Exponentiate once to get $\ln y=e^x$, then once more to get $y=e^{e^x}$; no intermediate algebra needed.
Outer log with coefficient and constant: $a\ln(b+c\ln y)+d=f(x)$
First subtract $d$ and divide by $a$ to isolate the outer $\ln$, then proceed with the two exponentiations.
Log of a power: $\ln(1+y^2)=f(x)$
Only one exponentiation is needed, followed by solving $y^2=e^{f(x)}-1$ and taking a square root with sign chosen by initial conditions.
Particular solution from separable ODE giving $\ln|1+2\ln y|=\dots$
Drop the modulus using the initial condition's sign, then unwrap with two exponentiations as above.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $\ln(1+2\ln y)$ as $\ln 1+\ln(2\ln y)$ and trying to apply log laws to a sum.
  • Reading the equation as having a single $\ln$ and exponentiating only once, leaving $\ln y$ in the answer.
  • Mistaking the target $y=e^{(A\sec 2x-1)/2}$ as requiring a different identity rather than recognising $e^{-2\cos 2x/6}$ rewritten via $\sec$.

Pitfalls

  • Exponentiating only once and presenting an expression still containing $\ln y$.
  • Forgetting to subtract the constant ($1$) before dividing by $2$ when isolating $\ln y$.
  • Writing $e^{a+b}=e^a+e^b$ when splitting the exponent.
  • Mishandling the coefficient $\tfrac12$ on the outer $\ln$, leading to a wrong $h(x)$ before the first exponentiation.
  • Failing to convert $1/\cos 2x$ to $\sec 2x$ and so not matching the requested final form.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the inverse relationship $e^{\ln u}=u$ and standard log/exponent manipulation from Edexcel IAL Pure (P2/P3), typically arising in separable first-order ODE particular solutions in P3/FP.
