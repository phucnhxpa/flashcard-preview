Assign linear numerator Bx+C over irreducible quadratic factor

Family: Partial Fractions Setup Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when setting up partial fractions and the denominator contains a quadratic factor with negative discriminant (e.g. x²+1, x²+4, x²+x+1) that cannot be factored further over the reals. Cue phrases: 'express in partial fractions' combined with a visibly irreducible quadratic. Also fires when a quadratic factor has no rational roots and the rational-root test fails.

Motivation — Why this works

The partial fraction theorem states each factor of degree n in the denominator contributes a numerator of degree at most n−1. An irreducible quadratic (degree 2) therefore needs a general linear numerator Bx+C, not a single constant. Counting degrees of freedom: matching coefficients of x², x¹, x⁰ in the cleared identity yields three equations, requiring three unknowns. A constant-only numerator leaves the system underdetermined and the identity cannot hold for all x.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the denominator contains a quadratic factor and check its discriminant — if b²−4ac<0, it is irreducible over ℝ.
  2. Rule out the constant-numerator ansatz B/(x²+…): this gives too few unknowns to match all coefficient powers after clearing denominators.
  3. Commit to (Bx+C)/(quadratic) because the numerator must have degree strictly less than the denominator factor.
  4. Write the full decomposition combining a constant A over each linear factor with a Bx+C term over each irreducible quadratic.
  5. Decide solution tactic: substitute the real root from the linear factor to find A quickly, then equate coefficients of x² and x⁰ (or sub convenient values) to pin down B and C.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Factor the denominator fully and identify any quadratic with negative discriminant as irreducible.
  2. Set up the ansatz with constants over linear factors and Bx+C over each irreducible quadratic, e.g. f(x)/[(x−a)(x²+px+q)] = A/(x−a) + (Bx+C)/(x²+px+q).
  3. Multiply through by the full denominator to obtain a polynomial identity.
  4. Substitute x=a to isolate A, then equate coefficients of x² and the constant term (or substitute two further values) to solve for B and C.

Worked Micro-Example

Express $\dfrac{4x}{(x-1)(x^2+1)}$ in partial fractions. Discriminant of $x^2+1$ is $-4<0$, so write $\dfrac{4x}{(x-1)(x^2+1)} = \dfrac{A}{x-1} + \dfrac{Bx+C}{x^2+1}$. Clearing: $4x = A(x^2+1) + (Bx+C)(x-1)$. Set $x=1$: $4 = 2A \Rightarrow A=2$. Coefficient of $x^2$: $0 = A+B \Rightarrow B=-2$. Constant: $0 = A - C \Rightarrow C=2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Repeated irreducible quadratic $(x^2+a^2)^2$
Include both $(Bx+C)/(x^2+a^2)$ and $(Dx+E)/(x^2+a^2)^2$ — typically Further Pure territory.
Reducible quadratic $x^2-1=(x-1)(x+1)$
Do not use Bx+C; split into two constant-numerator fractions $A/(x-1)+B/(x+1)$.
Improper rational function with irreducible quadratic in denominator
Polynomial-divide first to obtain quotient + proper remainder, then apply the Bx+C ansatz to the proper part.
Mixed denominator $(x-a)^2(x^2+bx+c)$ with irreducible quadratic
Use $A/(x-a) + B/(x-a)^2 + (Cx+D)/(x^2+bx+c)$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $x^2+1$ as if it factors and trying to write $A/(x-i)+B/(x+i)$ — complex factors are out of A-level scope.
  • Writing only $B/(x^2+1)$ with a single constant, producing too few unknowns.
  • Assuming any quadratic needs Bx+C without first checking the discriminant — reducible quadratics should be split into linear factors.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the +C term and writing $Bx/(x^2+1)$, leaving the system underdetermined.
  • Forgetting to check the discriminant and missing that a quadratic actually factors over ℚ or ℝ.
  • Mis-expanding $(Bx+C)(x-a)$ when collecting coefficients, producing sign errors in B and C.

A-level Scope Notes

Edexcel IAL Core/Pure includes partial fractions with linear and irreducible quadratic factors; repeated irreducible quadratics appear in Further Pure. Complex factorisation is not used in this setup.
