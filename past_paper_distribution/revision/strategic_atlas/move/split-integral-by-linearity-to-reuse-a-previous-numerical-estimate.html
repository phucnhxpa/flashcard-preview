Integration

Split integral by linearity to reuse a previous numerical estimate

Family: Integral Linearity Reuse Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in multi-part numerical-integration questions (typically Trapezium Rule) where a later part asks for ∫ₐᵇ g(x) dx and g(x) differs from the earlier integrand f(x) by a constant or simple scalar factor. Look for the phrase 'use your answer to part (a)/(b)' or 'hence find', and check whether g(x) − f(x) simplifies to a constant, a simple polynomial, or kf(x). Equivalent surface cues: a logarithm whose argument has been rescaled (log₂(5x) vs log₂(2x)), or an exponential plus a different constant term. The hallmark is that re-applying the Trapezium Rule would be wasteful and the examiner clearly signals reuse.

Motivation — Why this works

Linearity of the definite integral, ∫[αf(x) + βg(x)] dx = α∫f(x) dx + β∫g(x) dx, lets us decompose the new integral into (i) the already-estimated piece and (ii) an exactly evaluable piece (usually ∫ₐᵇ k dx = k(b−a)). This converts an apparently new numerical-estimation problem into a single arithmetic step, eliminating fresh trapezium computation and the rounding errors it would introduce. The move rewards conceptual understanding of integral properties over mechanical re-application of a rule.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the new integrand can be rewritten as the old integrand ± a constant (or × a constant) — e.g. log₂(5x) = log₂(2x) + log₂(5/2), or e^(x/2) + ½x − 2 = [e^(x/2) + ½x] − 2.
  2. Rule out re-applying the Trapezium Rule: it would mean recomputing every y-value and re-summing, which is slow, error-prone, and ignores the 'use your answer' instruction.
  3. Commit to linearity: ∫[f(x) + c] dx = ∫f(x) dx + c(b − a), because the constant integral is exact and the f(x) integral is already done.
  4. Bridge to operation: write the explicit decomposition, substitute the (unrounded) previous estimate, and compute the constant piece as k × (interval width).
  5. Tactical care: use the unrounded value from the previous part to avoid compounding rounding error, and check the sign of the constant correction against the original decomposition.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the constant (or scalar) c such that g(x) = f(x) + c (or g(x) = αf(x) + c).
  2. Write ∫ₐᵇ g(x) dx = ∫ₐᵇ f(x) dx + c·(b − a) (or α∫ₐᵇ f(x) dx + c·(b − a)).
  3. Substitute the previous-part estimate for ∫ₐᵇ f(x) dx, using the unrounded value.
  4. Evaluate c·(b − a) exactly — no approximation.
  5. Combine: add or subtract per the sign in the decomposition; round only at the final step to the precision required.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\int_2^{14} \log_2(2x)\,dx \approx 45.6$, find $\int_2^{14} \log_2(x/2)\,dx$. Write $\log_2(x/2) = \log_2(2x) - 2$, so $\int_2^{14} \log_2(x/2)\,dx = 45.6 - 2(14-2) = 45.6 - 24 = 21.6$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

New integrand is a scalar multiple of the old: ∫ₐᵇ k·f(x) dx
Pull the constant out: answer = k × (previous estimate). No interval-width correction needed.
Logarithm with rescaled argument, e.g. log₂(5x) vs log₂(2x)
Use log laws to write log₂(5x) = log₂(2x) + log₂(5/2); then apply linearity with c = log₂(5/2).
New integrand differs by a simple polynomial, e.g. f(x) − x
Split off ∫ₐᵇ x dx = ½(b² − a²) and evaluate exactly; subtract from the previous estimate.
Area-between-curves where one curve is numerically estimated and the other is elementary
Subtract the exact integral of the elementary curve from the numerical estimate of the harder one.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the question as a fresh Trapezium Rule problem and recomputing all y-values, ignoring the 'use your answer' instruction.
  • Subtracting the constant once (e.g. 13.83 − 2) instead of integrating it over the interval (13.83 − 2(b−a)).
  • Failing to spot the log-law rewrite that exposes the constant difference (e.g. not seeing log₂(5x) − log₂(2x) = log₂(5/2)).

Pitfalls

  • Using the wrong interval length, e.g. (b − a) miscounted (14 − 2 = 12, not 14; 4 − 2 = 2, not 4).
  • Sign error: adding the constant correction when the decomposition requires subtraction (or vice versa).
  • Using the rounded value of the previous estimate rather than the unrounded one, propagating avoidable error.
  • Forgetting that ∫ₐᵇ k dx = k(b − a), not k.

A-level Scope Notes

Strictly within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 Trapezium Rule contexts and the standard linearity property of definite integrals (no formula-booklet entry required — linearity is assumed). Do not invoke change-of-variable or integration by parts here; the move is purely algebraic decomposition followed by reuse of a numerical estimate.
