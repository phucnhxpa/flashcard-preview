Strategic Atlas / Differentiation / Stationary Point Condition
Differentiation

Differentiate quadratic and solve dy/dx = 0 to locate vertex

Family: Stationary Point Condition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question asks for the minimum (or maximum) point, vertex, or turning point of a quadratic curve y = ax² + bx + c, especially when those coordinates are needed downstream (e.g. as a point on a line, a distance calculation, or a range of values). Phrases like 'the minimum point of C', 'the vertex of the curve', or 'the turning point' are direct cues. Also fires when a later part of the question refers back to 'this minimum point' without giving its coordinates.

Motivation — Why this works

For a differentiable curve, stationary points satisfy dy/dx = 0. For a quadratic, dy/dx is linear, so this collapses to a single linear equation in x, giving the vertex's x-coordinate immediately. The sign of the leading coefficient determines max vs min without needing a second-derivative test: a > 0 gives a minimum, a < 0 a maximum. Substituting back into y produces the full coordinate pair.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question wants the minimum point of a quadratic — a vertex, not a root or intercept.
  2. I rule out solving y = 0 (that gives x-intercepts, not the vertex) and rule out a graphical/numerical estimate.
  3. I commit to dy/dx = 0 because for a quadratic this is a one-line linear solve, faster than completing the square here.
  4. Bridge: differentiate term-by-term using the power rule to obtain dy/dx as a linear expression in x.
  5. Set dy/dx = 0, solve for x, substitute back into the original y to get the y-coordinate, and note that a > 0 confirms it is a minimum.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Differentiate y = ax² + bx + c to get dy/dx = 2ax + b.
  2. Set dy/dx = 0 and solve the linear equation for x.
  3. Substitute the x-value into the original equation to find y.
  4. State the coordinates of the minimum/maximum point and (if asked) justify nature via sign of a or d²y/dx².

Worked Micro-Example

For $y = 2x^2 - 12x + 14$: $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 4x - 12$. Setting $4x - 12 = 0$ gives $x = 3$. Then $y = 2(3)^2 - 12(3) + 14 = 18 - 36 + 14 = -4$. Since the coefficient of $x^2$ is $2 > 0$, the point $(3, -4)$ is a minimum.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Maximum point of a quadratic with a < 0 (e.g. y = -x² + 6x - 5)
Same dy/dx = 0 procedure; negative leading coefficient guarantees a maximum instead of a minimum.
Question explicitly says 'by completing the square'
Switch technique: write y = a(x - h)² + k and read vertex (h, k) directly; do not use differentiation.
Cubic or higher polynomial turning points
dy/dx = 0 may give multiple solutions; use second-derivative test or sign change of dy/dx to classify each stationary point.
Quadratic with parameters (e.g. y = x² - 2kx + 3)
Vertex x-coordinate comes out in terms of k; substitute back to get y in terms of k as well.
Minimum value (not point) requested
Only the y-coordinate is needed after solving dy/dx = 0.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing 'minimum point' with an x-intercept and solving y = 0 instead.
  • Assuming the minimum point's coordinates were given earlier in the question and skipping the calculation.
  • Reporting only the x-coordinate as 'the minimum point' rather than the full ordered pair.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to substitute x back into y, leaving only the x-coordinate.
  • Arithmetic slips in the substitution, particularly with negative middle terms like -12(3) = -36.
  • Differentiating the constant incorrectly or mishandling the coefficient (e.g. writing dy/dx = 2x - 12 instead of 4x - 12).
  • Failing to justify nature of the stationary point when the question asks to 'show' it is a minimum.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics P1/P2: power-rule differentiation of polynomials and stationary points via dy/dx = 0, with nature determined by leading coefficient or second derivative.
