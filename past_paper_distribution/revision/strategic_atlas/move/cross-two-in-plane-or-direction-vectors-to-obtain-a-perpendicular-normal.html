Strategic Atlas / Vectors / Cross Product Normal
Vectors

Cross two in-plane (or direction) vectors to obtain a perpendicular normal

Family: Cross Product Normal Frequency: 9 questions

Trigger — When to use

This move fires whenever you need a vector perpendicular to two known directions simultaneously. Typical cues: (i) three points A, B, C are given and you must find the equation of the plane through them; (ii) two lines/direction vectors are stated to lie in a single plane and you need that plane's normal; (iii) you need the common perpendicular direction to two skew lines; (iv) you need the direction of the line of intersection of two planes (cross the two normals). Phrasings include 'find the equation of the plane through…', 'find a vector perpendicular to both…', and 'find the direction of the line of intersection of the planes…'.

Motivation — Why this works

By definition, a × b is perpendicular to both a and b, so a single determinant computation produces the unique direction (up to scale) orthogonal to two given vectors. This is precisely what a plane's normal requires — it must be perpendicular to every vector in the plane, hence to any two spanning vectors. The same algebraic identity solves several geometrically distinct problems (plane normal, common perpendicular to skew lines, intersection-line direction). It avoids the alternative of solving a 3×3 simultaneous system from n·u = 0, n·v = 0, which is slower and more error-prone.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the goal — a plane equation, a common perpendicular, or an intersection direction — reduces to finding a vector orthogonal to two given vectors.
  2. Rule out setting up dot-product equations n·u = 0 and n·v = 0 as simultaneous equations: this works but is slower and invites algebra slips.
  3. Rule out substituting three points into ax + by + cz = d to solve a 3×3 system: again valid but cumbersome.
  4. Commit to the cross product because it encodes both orthogonality conditions in one operation and outputs the normal directly.
  5. Bridge to action: identify the two in-plane (or direction) vectors. If three points A, B, C are given, form AB = B − A and AC = C − A. If lines/planes are given, extract their direction vectors or normals directly.
  6. Tactical decision: use the i, j, k determinant layout for systematic expansion, and remember to apply n·a = d with any one given point to finish the plane equation.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify or construct two vectors u and v that span the relevant plane (e.g. u = AB = b − a, v = AC = c − a), or take u, v as the two given direction vectors / plane normals.
  2. Set up the determinant n = u × v = |i j k ; u₁ u₂ u₃ ; v₁ v₂ v₃|.
  3. Expand by cofactors along the top row: n = i(u₂v₃ − u₃v₂) − j(u₁v₃ − u₃v₁) + k(u₁v₂ − u₂v₁), being careful with the minus sign on the j-component.
  4. Verify orthogonality as a check: confirm n · u = 0 and n · v = 0.
  5. If a plane equation is required, compute d = a · n using any known point on the plane, then write n₁x + n₂y + n₃z = d (or r · n = d).

Worked Micro-Example

Plane through A=(1,0,2), B=(2,1,0), C=(0,3,1). Then AB = (1,1,−2), AC = (−1,3,−1). \( \mathbf{n} = AB \times AC = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ 1 & 1 & -2 \\ -1 & 3 & -1 \end{vmatrix} = (1\cdot(-1) - (-2)\cdot 3)\mathbf{i} - (1\cdot(-1) - (-2)\cdot(-1))\mathbf{j} + (1\cdot 3 - 1\cdot(-1))\mathbf{k} = 5\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} + 4\mathbf{k}. \) Then d = A·n = 5 + 0 + 8 = 13, giving 5x + 3y + 4z = 13.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Plane given by a point and two direction vectors (parametric form r = a + λu + μv)
Cross the two parametric direction vectors directly: n = u × v; no need to construct AB, AC.
Plane passes through the origin
d = 0 automatically, so only the cross product is needed; equation is n · r = 0.
Direction of the line of intersection of two planes
Cross the two plane normals: direction = n₁ × n₂ (the line lies in both planes, so it is perpendicular to both normals).
Common perpendicular direction to two skew lines
Cross the two line direction vectors d₁ × d₂; this vector is perpendicular to both lines.
Area of triangle/parallelogram from two edge vectors
Same cross product; area of parallelogram = |u × v|, area of triangle = ½|u × v|.

Common Misreadings

  • Using the position vectors of A, B, C directly in the cross product instead of first forming in-plane direction vectors AB and AC.
  • Confusing 'perpendicular to both lines' with needing two separate dot-product equations, missing that the cross product captures both conditions at once.
  • Treating the direction of intersection of two planes as something requiring solving the planes simultaneously when n₁ × n₂ gives the direction immediately.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the minus sign on the j-component of the cofactor expansion — the most common arithmetic error.
  • Reversing the order (v × u instead of u × v) flips the sign of n; harmless for a plane equation but matters when orientation is significant.
  • Forgetting to substitute a point to find d, leaving the plane equation as just 'n₁x + n₂y + n₃z = ?'.
  • Putting the two vectors in the wrong rows of the determinant, producing a sign-flipped or incorrect n; mitigate by checking n · u = 0 and n · v = 0.

A-level Scope Notes

Cross product (vector product) is in the Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP3) formula booklet, including the determinant form and the identity |a × b| = |a||b| sinθ. Stay within 3D Cartesian vectors; do not invoke generalisations such as the wedge product or higher-dimensional analogues.
