Algebra & Functions

Translate a bounded planar region into simultaneous inequalities

Family: Region Definition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question says 'define the region R by inequalities' or 'write down the inequalities that define R', after a sketch involving a curve, a line, and possibly an axis. R is shown shaded, enclosed by two or three boundaries that meet at identifiable intersection points. Cues include phrases like 'the shaded region', 'bounded by', or 'enclosed between'. The student already has equations of the boundaries and the coordinates of intersection points.

Motivation — Why this works

Each boundary contributes exactly one inequality, whose direction (<, >, ≤, ≥) is fixed by which side of that boundary R lies on. Vertical boundaries (axes, x = a) and the x-coordinates of intersection points pin down the horizontal extent, preventing R from extending to infinity. Testing one interior sample point against each candidate inequality confirms all directions simultaneously, because a region is the intersection of half-planes/half-regions cut out by its boundaries.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that R is enclosed and identify every boundary curve/line/axis touching it, plus the intersection points where consecutive boundaries meet.
  2. Rule out using equations alone — R is two-dimensional, so each boundary must become an inequality, and the count of inequalities must match the count of boundaries.
  3. Commit to the half-plane viewpoint: for each boundary, decide whether R lies on the 'less than' or 'greater than' side by inspecting the shaded sketch.
  4. Bridge: write each boundary as y = … or x = … in the form most convenient for the inequality, then attach the chosen direction.
  5. Tactical check: pick one obvious interior point of R and substitute into every inequality; if all hold, the system is correct, otherwise flip the offending sign.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. List the boundaries of R and the intersection points relevant to its extent.
  2. For the curve boundary y = f(x), write y < f(x) or y > f(x) according to whether R is below or above the curve.
  3. For the straight-line boundary y = mx + c, write y > mx + c or y < mx + c similarly.
  4. For vertical boundaries (y-axis, x = a, intersection x-coordinate), write the x-range as a ≤ x ≤ b.
  5. Verify by substituting an interior point into all inequalities.

Worked Micro-Example

Curve $C: y = x^2 - 5x + 13$, line $l: y = 2.7x$, meeting at $M(2.5, 6.75)$; $R$ is enclosed by $C$, $l$ and the $y$-axis. Region lies below $C$, above $l$, right of $x=0$, left of $M$, giving $y \le x^2 - 5x + 13$, $y \ge 2.7x$, $0 \le x \le 2.5$. Check at $(1,3)$: $3 \le 9$, $3 \ge 2.7$, $0 \le 1 \le 2.5$ — all satisfied.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Region bounded by two curves only (no axis)
Horizontal extent comes from the two intersection x-values, e.g. $a \le x \le b$ where $a,b$ solve $f(x)=g(x)$.
Region above the curve and below the line
Reverse both directional signs: $y \ge f(x)$ and $y \le mx+c$.
Boundary is a circle $x^2+y^2 = r^2$
Use $x^2+y^2 \le r^2$ for the interior or $\ge r^2$ for the exterior.
Linear programming feasible region (FM/Decision style)
Each straight edge becomes $ax+by \le c$ or $\ge c$; non-negativity gives $x \ge 0$, $y \ge 0$.

Common Misreadings

  • Reversing an inequality by misjudging which side of the boundary R lies on without checking an interior point.
  • Omitting the x-range, so the system describes an unbounded strip instead of a closed region.
  • Treating the boundaries as equations only and writing y = f(x), y = mx+c instead of inequalities.

Pitfalls

  • Mixing strict and non-strict inequalities inconsistently when the question specifies whether boundary points belong to R.
  • Using only two inequalities when three boundaries are present (forgetting the y-axis or the vertical line through the meeting point).
  • Reading the meeting-point x-coordinate off the sketch rather than solving f(x) = mx+c algebraically.

A-level Scope Notes
