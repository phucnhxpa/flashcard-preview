Trigger — When to use

A polynomial equation is presented with all coefficients explicitly real (often phrased as 'a, b are real constants' or with numeric real coefficients), and one non-real complex root α = a + bi is given. The question typically asks to 'show that' or 'write down' another specific complex number (the conjugate a − bi) as a root, or asks for 'the other complex root'. The phrasing 'write down' is a strong cue that no computation is intended — just a one-line theorem citation. This trigger also fires on quartics where one complex root is given and a conjugate must be deduced before further factoring.

Motivation — Why this works

For any polynomial P(z) with all real coefficients, if P(α) = 0 then P(ᾱ) = 0, because conjugation commutes with real-coefficient arithmetic: taking conjugates of both sides of P(α) = 0 yields P(ᾱ) = 0. Hence non-real roots of real-coefficient polynomials always occur in conjugate pairs. This delivers a second root with zero algebraic work, which is crucial because at this stage the unknown coefficients (e.g. a, b, p, q) are not yet known, so direct substitution would be circular. For a cubic it halves the remaining work; for a quartic it can immediately produce a quadratic factor.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question gives one non-real root α = a + bi and asks for another root or to 'show' that ᾱ = a − bi is a root — and the target value differs from α only in the sign of the imaginary part.
  2. Check the hypothesis carefully: are ALL coefficients of the polynomial real? Scan for phrases like 'a, b ∈ ℝ' or numeric real coefficients on every term including the leading and constant terms.
  3. Rule out direct substitution of the target root into P(z): this would require knowing every coefficient, but the unknown parameters are exactly what we are trying to find later — circular.
  4. Rule out the quadratic formula or polynomial division at this stage — they are tools for the *next* part of the question, not for justifying the conjugate root.
  5. Commit to the Conjugate Root Theorem: a one-line citation suffices. The 'write down' or 'show that' wording confirms the examiner expects this single deduction.
  6. Execute by writing the conjugate carefully: negate ONLY the imaginary part, preserving the real part and any surd structure inside the imaginary component.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. State explicitly that all coefficients of P(z) are real (cite the given conditions, e.g. 'since a, b ∈ ℝ and the other coefficients are real numbers').
  2. Cite the Conjugate Root Theorem: 'for a polynomial with real coefficients, non-real complex roots occur in conjugate pairs'.
  3. Form the conjugate of the given root α = a + bi by negating the imaginary part: ᾱ = a − bi.
  4. Conclude: 'therefore ᾱ is also a root of P(z) = 0'.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $P(z) = z^3 + az^2 + bz + 10$ with $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$, and $z_1 = 1 - 2i$ is a root. Since all coefficients of $P$ are real, by the Conjugate Root Theorem $\overline{z_1} = 1 + 2i$ is also a root. (No substitution needed; $a, b$ are still unknown at this stage.)

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quartic with real coefficients and one complex root given
Same theorem yields the conjugate immediately, producing a conjugate-pair quadratic factor $(z - \alpha)(z - \bar\alpha) = z^2 - 2\mathrm{Re}(\alpha)z + |\alpha|^2$ which can then be divided out.
Coefficients involve parameters not yet shown to be real
First establish that the parameters are real (often given in the question stem) before invoking the theorem; otherwise the theorem does not apply.
Rational coefficients with a surd root $a + \sqrt{b}$
Analogous surd-conjugate result: $a - \sqrt{b}$ is also a root. Same pairing logic, different conjugation operation.
Imaginary part contains a surd, e.g. $\tfrac{3}{8} + \tfrac{\sqrt{71}}{8}i$
Negate only the imaginary part; preserve the surd intact: conjugate is $\tfrac{3}{8} - \tfrac{\sqrt{71}}{8}i$.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to substitute the target conjugate into P(z) to 'verify' it — this requires the unknown coefficients and is circular at this stage.
  • Confusing 'conjugate' with 'negative': writing $-1 + 2i$ or $-a - bi$ instead of $a - bi$.
  • Negating both the real and imaginary parts (writing $-a - bi$ for the conjugate of $a + bi$).
  • Failing to notice the question says 'write down', and instead launching into long algebraic computation.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to explicitly state that all coefficients are real — this is the key hypothesis and 'show that' questions lose marks without it.
  • Negating the wrong part: only the imaginary part flips sign; the real part is unchanged.
  • Mishandling surds inside the imaginary part when writing the conjugate.
  • Applying the theorem to polynomials with complex (non-real) coefficients — the theorem fails there.

A-level Scope Notes

This is core Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP1/FP2) material on roots of polynomials. The Conjugate Root Theorem is not in the formula booklet but is a named result students are expected to cite. Avoid generalising to polynomials over ℂ with complex coefficients (out of scope) and avoid invoking Galois-theoretic justifications.
