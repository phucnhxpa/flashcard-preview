Coordinate Geometry

Use radius⊥tangent to form right triangle and apply Pythagoras

Family: Geometric Property Exploitation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a line is tangent to a circle at point B, an external point A lies on that tangent, and the centre X is known (or parametric). Typical cues: 'tangent meets the circle at B', 'find the length AB', or a chord-of-contact / tangent-length question. Whenever you need a length involving the tangent point but don't want to solve for B explicitly, this move applies. Especially powerful when A, X have known/parametric coordinates and r appears as an unknown.

Motivation — Why this works

The circle theorem 'radius is perpendicular to tangent at the point of contact' guarantees ∠XBA = 90°, so triangle AXB is right-angled at B with hypotenuse AX. Pythagoras then gives AB² = AX² − r², bypassing the need to compute B's coordinates by solving a quadratic. This converts a geometric configuration into a clean algebraic equation linking known coordinates, the radius r, and any parameters. It is the standard 'tangent length from external point' identity.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that B is a tangent point and X is the centre, so the radius XB is perpendicular to the tangent line at B.
  2. Rule out finding B by solving the line–circle intersection quadratic — that entangles parameters and is far messier.
  3. Commit to the right-triangle approach because AX is computable directly from coordinates and XB = r is immediate.
  4. Bridge: write AX² using the distance formula, then state Pythagoras AB² + r² = AX².
  5. Tactically, substitute any given length condition on AB (e.g., AB = 2r, or a numerical value) to obtain a single equation in the remaining unknowns.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the external point A (often by intersecting the tangent line with an axis) and the centre X.
  2. Compute AX² using the distance formula in terms of any parameters.
  3. State the right angle at B from radius⊥tangent, with XB = r.
  4. Apply Pythagoras: AB² = AX² − r².
  5. Substitute the given condition on AB (length value or relation to r) to obtain the required equation.

Worked Micro-Example

Circle has centre $X=(3,5)$, radius $r$. Line $y=2x+k$ is tangent at $B$, and $A=(0,k)$. Then $AX^2 = 9+(5-k)^2 = k^2-10k+34$. Since $XB\perp AB$, Pythagoras gives $AB^2 = AX^2 - r^2$. If $AB=2r$, then $4r^2 = k^2-10k+34 - r^2$, so $5r^2 = k^2-10k+34$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Tangent length from a fixed external point P to a circle of centre C, radius r
Tangent length $= \sqrt{|PC|^2 - r^2}$ directly; no parameters needed.
Two tangents from an external point meeting the circle at B₁, B₂
Both tangent lengths are equal by the same Pythagoras argument; use the right triangles to find angles via trigonometry.
Shortest distance from a point to a circle along a tangent direction
Same identity; the tangent length is the leg, the centre-to-point distance is the hypotenuse.
Finding r given a tangent line and external point
Use perpendicular distance from centre to line equals r, often combined with this Pythagoras setup.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to find B's coordinates by solving the line–circle quadratic, introducing unnecessary parameter entanglement.
  • Forgetting the right angle is at B (the tangent point), not at A or X, and misapplying Pythagoras.
  • Confusing AB (tangent segment) with AX (centre-to-external distance) in the Pythagoras relation.

Pitfalls

  • Placing the right angle at the wrong vertex — it must be at B, making AX the hypotenuse.
  • Reading off A's coordinates incorrectly (e.g., using $(k,0)$ instead of $(0,k)$ for the y-intercept).
  • Sign or expansion errors when computing $AX^2$ with a parameter, e.g. mishandling $(5-k)^2$.
  • Forgetting to square the length condition (e.g., using $AB=2r$ as $AB^2=2r^2$ instead of $4r^2$).

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the Edexcel IAL circle theorem 'tangent perpendicular to radius at point of contact', the distance formula, and Pythagoras — all within Pure Maths P1/P2 coordinate geometry.
