Algebra & Functions

Equate coefficients of like powers to solve for unknown parameter

Family: Coefficient Matching Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an expansion (binomial, Maclaurin, partial-fraction recombination) yields symbolic coefficients in an unknown parameter, and the question states a numerical value for one or more specific coefficients. Cues include phrases like 'the coefficient of $x$ is $-12$', 'given that the coefficient of $x^2$ equals...', or 'find the value of $p$ such that...'. Also fires when two algebraic expressions are asserted equal as polynomials/series and unknowns must be pinned down.

Motivation — Why this works

Two polynomials or convergent power series are equal if and only if their coefficients agree term-by-term — this is the uniqueness of polynomial/series representation. Equating a single symbolic coefficient with its given numerical value collapses the identity into one equation in the unknown, which is usually linear and immediately solvable. Once the parameter is fixed, every other coefficient can be evaluated by direct substitution, so the same principle services subsequent parts of the question.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question supplies a numerical value for a coefficient I have just expressed symbolically in $p$ — these must be the same number.
  2. Rule out treating the $x$-term as an equation in $x$ (i.e. $-4px = -12$); only the coefficient, stripped of $x$, is to be equated.
  3. Commit to coefficient-matching because the symbolic coefficient is linear in $p$, so the equation will solve cleanly with no extraneous roots.
  4. Translate '$x$-coefficient $=-12$' into the scalar equation $-4p = -12$ and solve.
  5. Reuse the now-known value of $p$ to evaluate any further requested coefficients (e.g. $Q = 10p^2$), keeping higher-order coefficients symbolic if they will be needed in later parts.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the symbolic coefficient of the relevant power of $x$ from the expansion (here, coefficient of $x$ is $-4p$).
  2. Set this equal to the given numerical coefficient: $-4p = -12$.
  3. Solve the resulting (usually linear) equation: $p = 3$.
  4. Substitute the found value into other symbolic coefficients to answer remaining sub-parts: $Q = 10(3)^2 = 90$.
  5. Retain higher-order coefficients (e.g. $-20p^3$) symbolically or evaluate as needed for later parts.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose $(1+px)^5 = 1 - 4px + 10p^2 x^2 - 20p^3 x^3 + \dots$ wait — using the correct expansion $(1-px)^{-2}$-style or binomial form, the coefficient of $x$ is $-4p$. Given coefficient of $x$ is $-12$: $-4p=-12 \Rightarrow p=3$. Then coefficient of $x^2$ is $10p^2 = 10(9) = 90$, so $Q=90$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Partial fractions identity, e.g. $\tfrac{3x+1}{(x-1)(x+2)} = \tfrac{A}{x-1}+\tfrac{B}{x+2}$
Multiply through by the common denominator and equate coefficients of like powers of $x$ on both sides to obtain simultaneous equations in $A,B$.
Two given coefficients with two unknowns (e.g. unknown $p$ and unknown $n$ in $(1+px)^n$)
Equate both coefficients to give a 2×2 system; eliminate by ratio or substitution rather than solving each independently.
Maclaurin series matching, e.g. $f(x) = a + bx + cx^2 + \dots$ given a closed form for $f$
Compare derivatives at $0$ or expand and match coefficients to identify $a,b,c$.
Polynomial identity such as $x^3 + ax + b \equiv (x-1)(x^2+cx+d)$
Expand the right-hand side and equate coefficients of $x^3, x^2, x^1, x^0$ to solve for $a,b,c,d$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the term as an equation including $x$, e.g. writing $-4px = -12$ rather than $-4p = -12$.
  • Confusing 'coefficient of $x^2$' with 'value of the $x^2$ term at some point', leading to spurious substitutions of $x$.
  • Misidentifying which power of $x$ the given numerical coefficient refers to (e.g. matching it against the $x^2$ coefficient instead of the $x$ coefficient).

Pitfalls

  • If the matched coefficient is quadratic in $p$ (e.g. $10p^2 = 90$), forgetting to consider both signs $p = \pm 3$ and to reject any root violating the validity condition $|px|<1$.
  • Substituting $p$ before fully simplifying the symbolic coefficient, increasing the chance of arithmetic slips.
  • Carrying the wrong sign from the binomial expansion (e.g. dropping the negative on $-4p$) and obtaining $p=-3$ instead of $p=3$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3/P4 scope: binomial expansions for rational/negative indices, partial fractions, and polynomial identities. No formal uniqueness theorems are invoked — coefficient comparison is treated as a standard technique.
