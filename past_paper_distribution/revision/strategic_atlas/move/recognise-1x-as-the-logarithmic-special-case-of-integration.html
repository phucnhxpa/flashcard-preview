Integration

Recognise 1/x as the logarithmic special case of integration

Family: Special Case Recognition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integrand contains a term of the form k/x or kx⁻¹ (i.e. a power of x with exponent exactly −1). This fires whenever you spot x in a denominator with no other x-factors, or after rewriting a fraction such as 9/x as 9x⁻¹. The cue is that applying the power rule ∫xⁿ dx = xⁿ⁺¹/(n+1) would give division by zero at n = −1.

Motivation — Why this works

The power rule formula has (n+1) in its denominator, which collapses at n = −1. The antiderivative of x⁻¹ is instead ln|x|, a distinct standard result on the Edexcel formula list. Recognising this is essential because mechanically forcing the power rule yields the undefined expression x⁰/0, while ln|x| + c is the correct primitive whose derivative recovers 1/x.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice a term k/x in the integrand and rewrite it as kx⁻¹ so the power of x is explicit.
  2. I rule out the power rule because n = −1 would force division by zero in xⁿ⁺¹/(n+1).
  3. I commit to the logarithmic standard result ∫x⁻¹ dx = ln|x| + c, with the coefficient k carried through as k ln|x|.
  4. I integrate the remaining terms by the power rule as normal, treating this 1/x term separately.
  5. I add a single constant of integration c at the end and include the modulus on x unless the domain is restricted to x > 0.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify any term of the form k/x and rewrite as kx⁻¹.
  2. Apply the special case: ∫kx⁻¹ dx = k ln|x|.
  3. Integrate all other xⁿ terms by the power rule (n ≠ −1).
  4. Sum the antiderivatives and append a single + c.
  5. Drop the modulus only if the question states x > 0.

Worked Micro-Example

Find $\int \left(3x^2 - \tfrac{9}{x} + 3\right)\,dx$ for $x>0$.\\ Rewrite the middle term as $-9x^{-1}$. Then $\int 3x^2\,dx = x^3$, $\int -9x^{-1}\,dx = -9\ln x$, and $\int 3\,dx = 3x$.\\ Combine: $x^3 - 9\ln x + 3x + c$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Integrand of the form f'(x)/f(x)
Use the generalised log result ∫f'(x)/f(x) dx = ln|f(x)| + c, e.g. ∫2x/(x²+1) dx = ln(x²+1) + c.
Coefficient hidden inside a fraction, e.g. 1/(2x)
Factor out the constant: ∫1/(2x) dx = ½ ∫1/x dx = ½ ln|x| + c.
Linear denominator, e.g. ∫1/(ax+b) dx
Apply the reverse-chain version: ∫1/(ax+b) dx = (1/a) ln|ax+b| + c.
Definite integral with x > 0 throughout
The modulus can be dropped: ∫ₐᵇ k/x dx = k(ln b − ln a) = k ln(b/a).

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to force the power rule on x⁻¹ and writing x⁰/0 or simplifying to 1.
  • Treating 9/x as a constant because '9' is in the numerator, and integrating it as 9x.
  • Confusing ∫1/x dx = ln|x| with ∫ln x dx (which requires integration by parts).

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the modulus |x| when the domain includes or could include negative values.
  • Omitting the constant of integration c — a standard Edexcel accuracy mark.
  • Mishandling the sign of the coefficient, e.g. writing +9 ln x instead of −9 ln x when the term was −9/x.
  • Forgetting that for ∫1/(ax+b) dx the answer is (1/a) ln|ax+b|, not ln|ax+b|.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics: ∫1/x dx = ln|x| + c is a listed standard integral, and the f'(x)/f(x) extension is on-spec for P3/P4 integration.
