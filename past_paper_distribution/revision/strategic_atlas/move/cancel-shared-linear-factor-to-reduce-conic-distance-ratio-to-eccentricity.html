Strategic Atlas / Proof / Ratio Simplification
Proof

Cancel shared linear factor to reduce conic distance ratio to eccentricity

Family: Ratio Simplification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires at the closing step of a 'show that QF/PF = e' (or analogous focus/directrix/normal-foot ratio) proof on a conic. Both distances have just been written in terms of the parameter (typically cos θ or t) and one of them appears as a constant multiple of the other. A common factor of the form (a − e cos θ) or similar is visible in numerator and denominator. Also fires whenever a parametric ratio simplifies to a constant after a single cancellation.

Motivation — Why this works

Focus–directrix and focus–normal-foot ratios on a conic are constant (= e) by definition/geometry, so any correct parametric expression for the two distances must share a θ-dependent factor that cancels. Keeping both distances in factored form (rather than expanding) preserves the shared block (a − e cos θ) so the cancellation is mechanical. The successful cancellation also acts as a self-check: if no common factor appears, an earlier algebraic step is wrong.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that QF and PF, just computed, both contain the factor (5 − 3cos θ).
  2. Rule out expanding or rationalising further — that would obscure the shared factor and create unnecessary algebra.
  3. Commit to forming the ratio QF/PF directly in factored form, since the answer is supposed to be a constant e.
  4. Bridge: write QF/PF as a single fraction and identify (5 − 3cos θ) as the cancellable block.
  5. Tactically, justify that (5 − 3cos θ) ≠ 0 (since |cos θ| ≤ 1 < 5/3) before cancelling, then read off the remaining constant 3/5 = e.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the ratio as QF/PF = [3(5 − 3cos θ)/5] ÷ (5 − 3cos θ).
  2. Note (5 − 3cos θ) > 0 for all θ since 5 > 3 ≥ 3cos θ, so cancellation is valid.
  3. Cancel the common factor (5 − 3cos θ).
  4. Obtain QF/PF = 3/5.
  5. Identify 3/5 as the eccentricity e found earlier and conclude the proof.

Worked Micro-Example

Given QF = \tfrac{3(5-3\cos\theta)}{5} and PF = 5-3\cos\theta, then \[\frac{QF}{PF}=\frac{3(5-3\cos\theta)/5}{5-3\cos\theta}=\frac{3}{5},\] valid since 5-3\cos\theta\ge 2>0. Hence QF/PF = 3/5 = e. ▢

Variants — Same move, different surface

Ellipse focal-chord problem with PF = a − e x and PF' = a + e x in parametric form a − e a cos θ.
Cancel the factor a after factoring; ratio reduces to a constant or to a simple cos θ expression.
Parabola y² = 4ax proof that focal distance equals x + a using parameter t.
Both sides simplify to a(1+t²); cancellation isn't needed, but the same 'keep factored' discipline applies.
Hyperbola proof that PS/PM = e using directrix x = a/e.
Shared factor is (e x − a) or (a − e x); cancel after checking sign on the relevant branch.
Rectangular hyperbola xy = c² ratios involving parameter t (e.g. tangent/normal length ratios).
Shared factor is typically (1+t⁴) or (t²±1); cancel after verifying nonzero.
Trig identity proofs where LHS/RHS share a factor like (1−cos θ) or sin θ.
Same move: factor both sides, cancel the common bracket, state the nonzero domain restriction.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating QF/PF = e as something requiring an independent geometric argument rather than just algebraic cancellation.
  • Believing the factor must be expanded and recombined to 'show working', losing the visible cancellation.
  • Confusing (5 − 3cos θ) in PF with the expression for the directrix distance and trying to invoke the focus–directrix property directly.

Pitfalls

  • Leaving PF as √(expression) so the shared factor is hidden — the perfect-square step beforehand is essential.
  • Cancelling without stating (5 − 3cos θ) ≠ 0; in a 'prove' question this can cost a rigour mark.
  • Arithmetic slip when reducing 3/5 (e.g. writing 5/3) — always cross-check against the e computed in the earlier part.
  • Forgetting to conclude with '= e' explicitly; the question asks for the ratio to equal e, not just 3/5.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL Further Pure conic-sections work (parametric form of ellipse/hyperbola, focus–directrix property, eccentricity). Uses only basic algebraic cancellation and the bound |cos θ| ≤ 1, both standard at A-level.
