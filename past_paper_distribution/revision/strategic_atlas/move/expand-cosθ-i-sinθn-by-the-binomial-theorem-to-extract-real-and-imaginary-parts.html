Complex Numbers

Expand (cosθ + i sinθ)^n by the binomial theorem to extract real and imaginary parts

Family: Binomial Complex Expansion Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

You have applied De Moivre to write cos(nθ) + i sin(nθ) = (cosθ + i sinθ)^n and now need explicit polynomial expressions for cos(nθ) and sin(nθ) in terms of cosθ and sinθ. Typical cues: 'show that cos(5θ) = 16cos^5θ − 20cos^3θ + 5cosθ', 'express sin(4θ) in terms of sinθ and cosθ', or any prompt linking a multiple-angle trig function to powers of single-angle trig. It also fires whenever a question demands matching real/imaginary parts after a De Moivre step.

Motivation — Why this works

The binomial theorem expands (a + b)^n term by term, and choosing a = cosθ, b = i sinθ makes the powers of i partition the sum cleanly into real and imaginary parts. Equating real parts on both sides of De Moivre gives cos(nθ) as a polynomial in cosθ and sinθ; equating imaginary parts gives sin(nθ). This is the standard A-level bridge between multiple-angle identities and powers of trig functions, and it is the only systematic route at this level.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice (cosθ + i sinθ)^n on one side and cos(nθ) + i sin(nθ) on the other — I need a second form of the LHS to compare.
  2. I rule out repeated angle-addition formulae (too messy for n ≥ 4) and I rule out using e^{iθ} expansions beyond what De Moivre already gives.
  3. I commit to the binomial theorem because it generates every term mechanically and the powers of i automatically tag each term as real or imaginary.
  4. First concrete operation: write the full Σ C(n,k) cos^{n−k}θ (i sinθ)^k expansion, deferring simplification.
  5. Tactically, I track i^k mod 4 in a margin column, then collect even-k terms (real) and odd-k terms (imaginary, with one factor of i pulled out).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write (cosθ + i sinθ)^n = Σ_{k=0}^{n} C(n,k) cos^{n−k}θ · (i sinθ)^k.
  2. Replace i^k using i^0=1, i^1=i, i^2=−1, i^3=−i, i^4=1, … attaching the correct sign to each term.
  3. Collect terms with even k (real part) — these give cos(nθ).
  4. Collect terms with odd k, factor out i (imaginary part) — these give sin(nθ).
  5. Equate Re(LHS) = cos(nθ) and Im(LHS) = sin(nθ); label these as the required identities.

Worked Micro-Example

For n = 3: $(\cosθ + i\sinθ)^3 = \cos^3θ + 3i\cos^2θ\sinθ + 3i^2\cosθ\sin^2θ + i^3\sin^3θ$ $= (\cos^3θ - 3\cosθ\sin^2θ) + i(3\cos^2θ\sinθ - \sin^3θ)$. Equating with $\cos 3θ + i\sin 3θ$: $\cos 3θ = \cos^3θ - 3\cosθ\sin^2θ$ and $\sin 3θ = 3\cos^2θ\sinθ - \sin^3θ$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Expressing cos^n θ or sin^n θ as a sum of multiple-angle terms
Instead use z = e^{iθ} with z + z^{-1} = 2cosθ (or z − z^{-1} = 2i sinθ) and binomially expand (z + z^{-1})^n; pair terms z^k + z^{-k} = 2cos(kθ) — this is the reverse direction.
Deriving tan(nθ) in terms of tanθ
Form sin(nθ)/cos(nθ) from the binomial expansion, then divide numerator and denominator by cos^n θ to convert all sin/cos ratios to tanθ.
Only one of cos(nθ) or sin(nθ) is required
Write only the even-k (for cos) or odd-k (for sin) terms of the binomial expansion to save work.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating (cosθ + i sinθ)^n as a single trig identity to be expanded by repeated angle-addition rather than as a binomial in two terms.
  • Believing the answer must be in cosθ alone — students force premature use of sin^2θ = 1 − cos^2θ before separating real/imaginary parts.
  • Confusing this with the (z + z^{-1})^n route, which solves the opposite problem.

Pitfalls

  • Miscounting powers of i, especially mixing i^2 = −1 with i^3 = −i; track i^k mod 4 explicitly.
  • Arithmetic slips in C(n,k) for n ≥ 5 — write out Pascal's row first.
  • Forgetting to factor out the single i from the imaginary part before equating with sin(nθ).
  • Substituting sin^2θ = 1 − cos^2θ too early and losing the clean real/imaginary split.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) — binomial theorem for positive integer n combined with De Moivre's theorem. No use of generating functions or complex analysis beyond standard powers of i.
