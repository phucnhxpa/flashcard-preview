Compare coefficients of like powers to solve unknowns in cascade

Family: Coefficient Comparison Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Two polynomial (or truncated series) expressions are asserted to be identical, e.g. an expansion in $k$ equals a given series $1+\tfrac{1}{8}x+Ax^2+Bx^3$. Multiple unknowns appear (a parameter inside the expansion plus labelled coefficients $A$, $B$). The lowest non-trivial power's coefficient depends on only one unknown, so the system decouples. Phrases like 'identical for all $x$', 'find the values of', or '$\equiv$' are cues.

Motivation — Why this works

If two polynomials agree for all $x$, their coefficients of each power of $x$ must be equal — this is the identity principle. It converts one polynomial identity into independent scalar equations, one per power. When the parameter appears at low order and the labelled unknowns at higher orders, the equations form a triangular system: solve for the parameter from the lowest power, then substitute forward. No simultaneous solving is required.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that the LHS expansion has $k$ baked into each coefficient, while the RHS shows a clean numerical coefficient at $x^1$ but unknowns $A$, $B$ at $x^2$, $x^3$.
  2. Rules out a simultaneous-equations approach because the $x^1$ equation contains only $k$ — there is no need to couple it with the $A$, $B$ equations.
  3. Commits to the identity principle: equate coefficients power-by-power and exploit the triangular structure to solve in cascade.
  4. Bridges to action by writing down the $x^1$ equation first, isolating $k$, then plugging this value into the $x^2$ and $x^3$ coefficient expressions.
  5. Tactically watches for fraction simplification (cancelling common factors before multiplying) and keeps signs from $(1+u)^n$ expansions consistent.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write each coefficient of the expansion as an expression in the parameter $k$.
  2. Equate the $x^1$ coefficients: $\tfrac{1}{3}k=\tfrac{1}{8}\Rightarrow k=\tfrac{3}{8}$.
  3. Substitute $k=\tfrac{3}{8}$ into the $x^2$ coefficient to obtain $A=-\tfrac{1}{9}\left(\tfrac{3}{8}\right)^2=-\tfrac{1}{64}$.
  4. Substitute $k=\tfrac{3}{8}$ into the $x^3$ coefficient to obtain $B=\tfrac{5}{81}\left(\tfrac{3}{8}\right)^3=\tfrac{5}{1536}$.
  5. State all three values explicitly as the final answer.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose $(1+kx)^{1/3}=1+\tfrac{1}{8}x+Ax^2+Bx^3+\dots$. The expansion gives coefficients $\tfrac{k}{3}$, $-\tfrac{k^2}{9}$, $\tfrac{5k^3}{81}$. From $\tfrac{k}{3}=\tfrac{1}{8}$, $k=\tfrac{3}{8}$. Then $A=-\tfrac{1}{9}\cdot\tfrac{9}{64}=-\tfrac{1}{64}$ and $B=\tfrac{5}{81}\cdot\tfrac{27}{512}=\tfrac{5}{1536}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Partial fractions, e.g. $\tfrac{3x+2}{(x-1)(x+2)}\equiv\tfrac{A}{x-1}+\tfrac{B}{x+2}$
Multiply through by the common denominator and compare coefficients of each power of $x$ to obtain a triangular or simple linear system in $A$, $B$.
Polynomial identity such as $x^3+px+q\equiv (x-a)(x^2+bx+c)$
Match coefficients of $x^2$, $x^1$, $x^0$ to relate $a,b,c$ to $p,q$; the $x^2$ equation often isolates one unknown immediately.
Binomial expansion matched to a given series with a parameter inside, e.g. $(a+bx)^n$ given the first two coefficients
Use the $x^0$ coefficient to fix $a^n$, then the $x^1$ coefficient to fix $b$ in terms of $a$, cascading through higher powers.
Method of undetermined coefficients for a particular integral in differential equations (FM)
Substitute the trial solution, expand, and compare coefficients of each power of $x$ (or each $\sin/\cos$ term) to determine the constants.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to solve for $A$ and $B$ before finding $k$, ending up with answers still containing $k$.
  • Treating the identity as an equation valid only at one specific $x$ rather than for all $x$, and substituting a convenient value instead of comparing coefficients.
  • Reading $A$ and $B$ as the full $x^2$ and $x^3$ terms rather than just the coefficients.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips with nested fractions, e.g. failing to cancel $27$ against $81$ in $\tfrac{5}{81}\cdot\tfrac{27}{512}$.
  • Writing $(kx)^2=kx^2$ instead of $k^2x^2$, or $(kx)^3=kx^3$ instead of $k^3x^3$.
  • Sign errors from binomial coefficients $\binom{n}{r}$ when $n$ is a negative or fractional index.
  • Forgetting to state all three required values at the end.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure scope: binomial series for fractional/negative indices (P3/P4) and polynomial identities/partial fractions (P3); the cascade idea also appears in Further Pure when matching coefficients in particular integrals.
