Trigger — When to use

The question asks to plot all solutions of $z^n = w$ on an Argand diagram, or to describe their geometric configuration. Cues include 'show the roots on an Argand diagram', 'describe the shape formed by the roots', or follow-up parts asking about distances/areas between roots. The roots share modulus $|w|^{1/n}$ with arguments in arithmetic progression of common difference $2\pi/n$.

Motivation — Why this works

All nth roots of a fixed complex number $w$ lie on the circle $|z|=|w|^{1/n}$ and have arguments $(\arg w + 2k\pi)/n$, which differ consecutively by $2\pi/n$. Equal modulus plus equal angular spacing is precisely the definition of vertices of a regular n-gon inscribed in that circle. Recognising this turns a multi-step plotting task into a single rotation-by-$2\pi/n$ procedure and supports clean geometric arguments for areas, perimeters, and loci in later parts.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice that after applying de Moivre I have n roots all with the same modulus $r^{1/n}$.
  2. I rule out plotting each root from scratch via Cartesian conversion — that wastes time and accumulates rounding error.
  3. I commit to a polygon construction because equal modulus and equal angular spacing $2\pi/n$ force a regular n-gon.
  4. Concretely, I draw the circle of radius $r^{1/n}$, place the principal root at angle $\theta/n$, then step around by $2\pi/n$.
  5. I check whether the equation is $z^n=w$ (centre origin) or $(z-a)^n=w$ (centre $a$), and whether $n$ is small enough to label every vertex clearly.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write $w = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)$ and identify the common modulus $r^{1/n}$ of all roots.
  2. Draw a circle of radius $r^{1/n}$ centred at the appropriate point (origin for $z^n=w$).
  3. Plot the principal root $z_0$ at argument $\theta/n$ on this circle.
  4. Mark the remaining $n-1$ roots by rotating $z_0$ successively by $2\pi/n$.
  5. Label each root (e.g. $z_0,z_1,\ldots,z_{n-1}$) and, if asked, state that they form a regular n-gon inscribed in the circle.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $z^4 = 16i$ and plot the roots. Here $|w|=16$, $\arg w=\pi/2$, so $|z|=2$ and arguments are $\pi/8, 5\pi/8, 9\pi/8, 13\pi/8$. Draw the circle $|z|=2$, mark $z_0=2\operatorname{cis}(\pi/8)$, then rotate by $\pi/2$ three times to obtain a square inscribed in the circle.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation of the form $(z-a)^n = w$
The regular n-gon is inscribed in a circle of radius $|w|^{1/n}$ centred at $a$, not at the origin.
Pure roots of unity $z^n = 1$
Circle is the unit circle and one vertex sits at $z=1$; remaining vertices are obtained by successive rotations of $2\pi/n$.
Equation $z^n = -k$ with $k>0$ real
Principal argument is $\pi/n$ rather than $0$; the polygon is the unit-circle one rotated by $\pi/n$.
Follow-up asks for area or perimeter
Use regular n-gon formulas with circumradius $R=|w|^{1/n}$, e.g. area $=\tfrac12 n R^2 \sin(2\pi/n)$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the n roots as unrelated points needing individual Cartesian computation.
  • Reading $(z-a)^n=w$ as if it were $z^n=w$ and centring the polygon at the origin.
  • Confusing the polygon's circumradius $|w|^{1/n}$ with $|w|$ itself.

Pitfalls

  • Inaccurate placement of the first root's argument $\theta/n$, which rotates the entire polygon.
  • Using $2\pi/n$ in degrees vs radians inconsistently when stepping between vertices.
  • Forgetting to draw or indicate the circle, losing the 'regular polygon' descriptive mark.
  • Stopping after fewer than $n$ roots or going past, producing the wrong polygon.
