Strategic Atlas / Complex Numbers / Real Imaginary Separation
Complex Numbers

Equate real and imaginary parts to solve for a real parameter

Family: Real Imaginary Separation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a complex equation must hold with a real unknown (parameter, coordinate, or coefficient) appearing on one side. Cues: an equation of the form a+bi = c+di where one side is purely real or purely imaginary, or where the unknown is stated to be real. Also triggers in locus work when w = u+iv is set equal to an expression in x,y, or when finding real/imaginary parts of a quotient. Any phrase like 'find the real value of p such that...' is a strong signal.

Motivation — Why this works

Two complex numbers are equal iff their real parts are equal AND their imaginary parts are equal — this is the defining property of C as R^2 with the chosen basis {1,i}. So one complex equation encodes two simultaneous real equations. When the unknown is real, this typically gives one determining equation plus one consistency condition. The method converts complex algebra into ordinary real algebra without losing information.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation is complex-valued but the unknown p is declared real, so p contributes only to the real part of any expression it sits in.
  2. Rule out brute manipulation (e.g. dividing both sides by i or taking modulus) — these can lose information or introduce extraneous solutions.
  3. Commit to writing each side in the canonical a+bi form so that matching is unambiguous.
  4. Bridge: identify which terms are real and which carry an explicit i, treating √3, π, etc. as real constants.
  5. Tactically: solve the simpler of the two resulting equations first; use the other as a consistency check, and if it fails, suspect a misread or algebraic slip upstream.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rearrange so each side is in the form (real expression) + i·(real expression).
  2. Read off Re(LHS), Im(LHS), Re(RHS), Im(RHS).
  3. Set Re(LHS) = Re(RHS) — this is one real equation.
  4. Set Im(LHS) = Im(RHS) — this is the second real equation.
  5. Solve the equation containing the unknown p; substitute into the other to verify consistency.
  6. If the consistency equation fails identically, return to the source equation and check for a misread or error.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $(2+i)p + 3i = 4 + 5i$ for real $p$.\\ LHS $= 2p + i(p+3)$, RHS $= 4 + 5i$.\\ Real parts: $2p = 4 \Rightarrow p = 2$.\\ Imaginary parts: $p+3 = 5 \Rightarrow p = 2$. ✓ Consistent, so $p=2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Locus problem: w = f(z) with z = x+iy, find Cartesian equation of image
Compute u = Re(w), v = Im(w) in terms of x,y, then eliminate x,y between the two real equations.
Finding real and imaginary parts of a quotient (a+bi)/(c+di)
Multiply by conjugate first to put in a+bi form, then read off parts; no equating needed but the same separation principle.
Polynomial with real coefficients: substituting z = x+iy and demanding the result be real
Set Im of the expression to zero; this single equation defines a curve or condition on x,y.
Solving z² = a+bi for z = x+iy with x,y real
Equate real parts (x²−y² = a) and imaginary parts (2xy = b), then solve the simultaneous system.
Determining a real constant k so that an expression is purely real (or purely imaginary)
Set Im (or Re) of the expression equal to zero — only one equation arises since only one constraint is imposed.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating √3, π, or e^a (with a real) as if they had imaginary content because they look unfamiliar.
  • Assuming the unknown can absorb an imaginary mismatch when it is declared real.
  • Checking only one part (real or imaginary) and missing a contradiction in the other.
  • Confusing 'purely imaginary' (Re = 0) with 'imaginary part' (the coefficient of i).

Pitfalls

  • If the unknown is allowed to be complex, separation gives two equations in two real unknowns Re(p), Im(p), not one.
  • A contradiction between the two parts indicates a misread question or arithmetic slip upstream — do not force an answer.
  • Failing to fully simplify each side into a+bi form before reading off parts (e.g. leaving an i² unsimplified).
  • Dividing by i or by a complex factor instead of separating — risks sign errors and obscures the two-equation structure.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Core/Further Pure technique used throughout complex numbers (FP1/FP2): solving equations, loci, square roots of complex numbers, and identifying real/imaginary parts of expressions.
