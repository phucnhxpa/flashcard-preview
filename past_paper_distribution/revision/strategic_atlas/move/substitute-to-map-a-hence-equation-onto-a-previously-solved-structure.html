Algebra & Functions

Substitute to map a 'Hence' equation onto a previously solved structure

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a later part of a question contains an equation whose algebraic skeleton is identical to an earlier part, with a single repeated expression (e.g. y−5, eˣ, sinθ, x²) playing the role of the original variable. The keyword 'Hence' (or 'Hence, or otherwise') is the strongest cue, signalling that the examiner expects reuse rather than a fresh solve. Recognising the structural match — same coefficients, same powers, same right-hand side — is the trigger to introduce a substitution variable.

Motivation — Why this works

Letting u equal the repeated expression converts the new equation into a verbatim copy of the already-solved one, so its roots are known immediately. This avoids expanding powers of a binomial (or transcendental) expression, which would generate a fresh polynomial requiring independent factorisation. The bridge works because polynomial identity in u is preserved under any invertible substitution u = g(variable), and the back-map u → variable recovers the required solutions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Student notices the new equation has the same coefficients and powers as part (a), with one expression (y−5) consistently replacing x.
  2. They rule out brute-force expansion of (y−5)³ and (y−5)², recognising that this would discard part (a)'s work and contradict the 'Hence' instruction.
  3. They commit to the substitution u = y−5 because it makes the two equations literally identical and therefore reuses every root for free.
  4. Bridge: rewrite the equation in u, quote the roots from part (a), then prepare to invert via y = u + 5.
  5. Tactically, they list all u-roots before back-substituting, and check each y-value satisfies any domain restriction stated in the question.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the repeated expression and let u equal it (here u = y − 5).
  2. Rewrite the equation in u; verify it matches the earlier part exactly: 2u³ + 3u² − 35u = 0.
  3. Quote the roots from part (a): u = 0, u = 7/2, u = −5.
  4. Reverse-substitute using y = u + 5 for each root.
  5. List all final y-values: y = 5, y = 17/2, y = 0.

Worked Micro-Example

Given part (a) solved $2x^3 + 3x^2 - 35x = 0$ with roots $x = 0, \tfrac{7}{2}, -5$. For (b) hence solve $2(y-5)^3 + 3(y-5)^2 - 35(y-5) = 0$. Let $u = y-5$: equation becomes $2u^3 + 3u^2 - 35u = 0$, so $u \in \{0, \tfrac{7}{2}, -5\}$. Then $y = u + 5$ gives $y \in \{5, \tfrac{17}{2}, 0\}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Exponential bridge: 'Hence solve 2e^{3x} + 3e^{2x} − 35e^{x} = 0'.
Let u = eˣ; reject u ≤ 0; invert with x = ln u for admissible roots only.
Trigonometric bridge: 'Hence solve 2sin³θ + 3sin²θ − 35sinθ = 0' on a given interval.
Let u = sinθ; reject |u| > 1; invert with θ = arcsin u plus symmetry/periodicity within the stated range.
Power bridge: 'Hence solve 2x⁶ + 3x⁴ − 35x² = 0'.
Let u = x²; require u ≥ 0; invert with x = ±√u (drop negative-u roots).
Logarithmic bridge: 'Hence solve 2(ln x)³ + 3(ln x)² − 35 ln x = 0'.
Let u = ln x; invert with x = eᵘ; all real u give valid x > 0.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the second equation as unrelated and expanding (y−5)³ and (y−5)² fully, ignoring the 'Hence' cue.
  • Substituting correctly but quoting only one root, forgetting that all three u-values must be back-mapped.
  • Mistaking the substitution as merely cosmetic and skipping the formal 'Let u = …' line, losing method marks.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to reverse-substitute, leaving final answers as u-values.
  • Arithmetic slips converting u + 5 for fractional roots (e.g. 7/2 + 5 = 17/2).
  • In domain-restricted variants, forgetting to discard inadmissible u-values (u ≤ 0 for eˣ, |u| > 1 for sinθ).
  • Not checking that any stated solution interval (e.g. 0 ≤ θ < 2π) yields all required θ-values per admissible u.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths: relies only on polynomial factorisation from P1/P2 and standard inverse functions (ln, arcsin, square root) seen in P2–P4. No content beyond A-level.
