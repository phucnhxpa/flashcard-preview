Flip log argument to absorb a leading negative sign

Family: Log Law Simplification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a result of the form k = -c·ln(a/b) (with a/b < 1 or with an unwanted minus) must be presented as p·ln(q) with p, q rational and 'cleanly' positive. Cues include the question's wording 'in the form p ln q', a negative coefficient sitting in front of a logarithm, or a log argument that is a proper fraction. Also fires when an integration or solving-an-equation step yields ln(small/large) and the mark scheme expects ln(large/small).

Motivation — Why this works

The identity -ln(a/b) = ln(b/a) lets a leading minus be reabsorbed by inverting the argument, since ln(x^{-1}) = -ln(x). This swaps a negative coefficient for a reciprocal inside the log without changing the value. The result then matches the canonical p·ln(q) presentation expected by the mark scheme, with both p and q rational and 'natural'.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice my answer is k = -(1/5)ln(3/5): correct in value, but the question wants p ln q with the minus absent and the argument tidy.
  2. I rule out distributing as ln 3 - ln 5 (loses the p ln q shape) and rule out leaving p = -1/5, q = 3/5 (technically valid but not the expected presentation).
  3. I commit to using -ln(a/b) = ln(b/a), since this single law removes the minus and inverts the fraction in one stroke.
  4. Concretely, I rewrite -ln(3/5) as ln(5/3), then re-attach the 1/5 coefficient.
  5. I read off p = 1/5 and q = 5/3 and check both are rational and positive, matching the required form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the offending structure: a negative coefficient times ln(fraction).
  2. Apply -ln(a/b) = ln(b/a) to invert the argument and absorb the minus.
  3. Re-attach any remaining positive coefficient to obtain p·ln(q).
  4. Read off p and q and verify both are rational and the argument exceeds 1 (if expected).

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose solving $5e^{k} = 3$ gives $k = \ln(3/5) \cdot 1 = -(1/5)\ln(3/5)$ after a prior $1/5$ factor. Then $k = -\tfrac{1}{5}\ln\tfrac{3}{5} = \tfrac{1}{5}\ln\tfrac{5}{3}$, so $p = \tfrac{1}{5}$ and $q = \tfrac{5}{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Definite integral result $\int_a^b \tfrac{1}{x}\,dx = \ln(a/b)$ with $a<b$
Rewrite as $-\ln(b/a)$ if a positive log of a value $>1$ is wanted, or vice versa.
Solving $a^x = b$ with $b<a$ giving $x = \log_a b < 0$
Express as $-\log_a(1/b)$ or use change of base; same flip principle.
Series/log expressions like $-\ln(1-x)$
Rewrite as $\ln\bigl(1/(1-x)\bigr)$ when a positive-log presentation is required.
Coefficient is itself negative rational, e.g. $-\tfrac{2}{3}\ln(4/9)$
Flip to $\tfrac{2}{3}\ln(9/4)$; works because the minus, not the coefficient's size, is what gets absorbed.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $-\ln(3/5)$ as $\ln(-3/5)$, which is undefined.
  • Thinking the flip changes the numerical value of $k$ (it does not).
  • Believing p must be a positive integer; here $p = 1/5$ is acceptable because the question only requires rational.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to keep the external coefficient $1/5$ when flipping the argument.
  • Splitting $\ln(5/3)$ as $\ln 5 - \ln 3$ and losing the required $p\ln q$ shape.
  • Flipping the argument but also (wrongly) flipping the sign of the coefficient, producing $-(1/5)\ln(5/3)$.
  • Applying the flip when the question actually wants $p\ln q$ with $q<1$ — read the form carefully.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the standard log law $\ln(a/b) = -\ln(b/a)$ from Edexcel IAL P2/P3 logarithms; no results beyond A-level scope are required.
