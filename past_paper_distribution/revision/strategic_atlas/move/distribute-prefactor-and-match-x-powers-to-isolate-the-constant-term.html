Binomial Expansion

Distribute prefactor and match x-powers to isolate the constant term

Family: Power Matching Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question asks for the 'term independent of x' (or 'coefficient of x^k') in a product of a short prefactor (polynomial or sum of negative powers of x) and a binomial expansion $(a+bx^m)^n$ or $(a+b/x^m)^n$. Cue phrases include 'find the term independent of x in $(\tfrac{1}{x^3}+\tfrac{8}{x^5})(1+\tfrac{x}{4})^{12}$' or 'coefficient of $x^2$ in $(2-x)(1+x)^{10}$'. The prefactor has only 2-3 terms, so only 2-3 specific terms of the expansion can contribute. The move fires whenever expanding the full product would be wasteful.

Motivation — Why this works

By linearity, $(A+B)\sum T_r = A\sum T_r + B\sum T_r$, so each prefactor monomial $cx^j$ multiplied against the expansion contributes only the single term whose $x$-power complements $j$ to the desired total power. This converts a long multiplication into a small set of independent power-matching equations, one per prefactor term. The general term of $(a+bx^m)^n$ is $\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}(bx^m)^r$, so picking the right $r$ for each prefactor piece pins down the contribution exactly.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the product has a short prefactor and a full binomial expansion, and the target is a single power of x (often $x^0$).
  2. Rule out expanding everything: only one term per prefactor monomial can land on the target power, so most products are wasted.
  3. Commit to the linearity decomposition: handle each prefactor monomial $cx^j$ separately and solve $j + (\text{power from expansion}) = \text{target}$ for $r$.
  4. Write the general term of the expansion explicitly, equate the exponent to the required value, and solve for $r$; check $0\le r\le n$ and $r\in\mathbb{Z}$.
  5. Compute each surviving contribution as $c\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r$ (with the correct sign), then sum them.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the general term of the binomial: $T_{r+1}=\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}(bx^m)^r$ with explicit power of $x$.
  2. For each prefactor term $cx^j$, set the total power of $x$ equal to the target and solve for $r$.
  3. Discard any branch whose $r$ is not a non-negative integer $\le n$.
  4. Compute each surviving contribution $c\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r$, keeping signs and the prefactor coefficient.
  5. Add the contributions to obtain the required term/coefficient.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the term independent of $x$ in $\left(\tfrac{1}{x^3}+\tfrac{8}{x^5}\right)(1+\tfrac{x}{4})^{12}$. General term: $\binom{12}{r}(x/4)^r$. From $\tfrac{1}{x^3}$: need $r=3$, giving $\binom{12}{3}/4^3=220/64=55/16$. From $\tfrac{8}{x^5}$: need $r=5$, giving $8\cdot\binom{12}{5}/4^5=8\cdot 792/1024=99/16$. Sum: $55/16+99/16=77/8$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Polynomial prefactor such as $(2-3x+x^2)(1+x)^{10}$ asking for coefficient of $x^k$
Solve $r=k$, $r=k-1$, $r=k-2$ separately; combine with coefficients $2,-3,1$ respectively.
Prefactor mixes positive and negative powers, e.g. $(x^2+1/x)(1+2x)^n$
Each prefactor term yields its own equation for $r$; some branches may be discarded if $r$ falls outside $0\le r\le n$.
Expansion uses $(a+bx)^n$ with $a\ne 1$
Keep the $a^{n-r}$ factor; the power-matching step is unchanged but the coefficient computation involves $a^{n-r}b^r$.
Series expansion via $(1+x)^n$ with $n$ negative or fractional (valid in IAL FP/Pure for $|x|<1$)
Same matching logic, but use the generalized binomial coefficient and respect the convergence condition.

Common Misreadings

  • Finding only one contribution and stopping, missing that each prefactor term can produce a contribution.
  • Misreading the prefactor (e.g. swapping $1/x^3$ for $x^3$), giving the wrong sign of $j$ and wrong $r$.
  • Treating 'term independent of x' as 'first term' or 'middle term' rather than the $x^0$ term.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to multiply by the prefactor's numerical coefficient (e.g. the 8 in $8/x^5$).
  • Sign errors when the binomial is $(a-bx)^n$ or the prefactor contains subtraction; remember $(-b)^r$.
  • Not verifying $r\in\mathbb{Z}$ with $0\le r\le n$ before computing — invalid $r$ means that branch contributes 0.
  • Arithmetic slips with powers of the denominator, e.g. $4^r$ in $(x/4)^r$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (binomial expansion of $(a+bx)^n$ for positive integer $n$, and the generalized expansion for rational $n$ with $|x|<1$); no content beyond standard binomial theorem is required.
