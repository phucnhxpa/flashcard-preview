Algebra & Functions

Select geometrically valid root using diagram or context

Family: Root Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an algebraic equation (typically quadratic, but also higher-degree, trigonometric, or from simultaneous equations) yields two or more candidate solutions, but the problem requests one specific point, time, or value. Cues include phrases like 'the point P shown in the diagram', 'the point in the first quadrant', 'the time at which', or 'the other intersection'. A diagram, stated domain, or physical/geometric constraint disambiguates which root to keep. Common in coordinate-geometry intersection problems and curve-sketching tasks.

Motivation — Why this works

Polynomial and simultaneous equations are blind to the geometry of the original problem — they return all algebraic solutions, including ones that violate the question's stated configuration. The discriminating information lives in the diagram, the labelling, or an inequality (e.g. x > 0, t > 0, point lies in a named region). Reading that information back into the algebra is essential to score the final mark; otherwise a fully correct algebraic solution still yields the wrong coordinate.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that the intersection/equation has produced more than one candidate root, but the question singles out one specific point or value.
  2. Rules out the root that contradicts the diagram or constraint — e.g. the candidate lying in the wrong quadrant, on the wrong branch, or outside the stated domain.
  3. Commits to the remaining root because it matches the position, sign, or region indicated by the labelling of P (or by an inequality the problem imposes).
  4. Bridges to computation by substituting the chosen x-value into the simpler of the two curves to obtain the corresponding y-coordinate.
  5. Decides tactically to use the function with fewer terms (or fewer fractions) to minimise arithmetic risk, since both curves give the same y at an intersection.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. List all candidate roots produced by the equation.
  2. Read the diagram / stated constraint to identify which root corresponds to the named point.
  3. Discard the candidate(s) that violate the geometric configuration.
  4. Substitute the surviving root into the simpler curve equation to compute the other coordinate.
  5. State the required point or value in the form requested (e.g. coordinates of P).

Worked Micro-Example

A line meets the curve $f(x) = -5x(x-4)$ at points giving $(x+8)(x-3)=0$, so $x=-8$ or $x=3$. The diagram shows $P$ far to the left of the y-axis, so take $x=-8$. Then $y = -5(-8)(-8-4) = -5(-8)(-12) = -480$. Hence $P = (-8,\,-480)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Circle–line intersection where the question asks for the point in a stated quadrant
Select the root whose coordinates satisfy the quadrant's sign conditions; discard the other.
Projectile or kinematics problem yielding two times from a quadratic in t
Reject the negative or pre-launch root using the constraint $t \ge 0$ (or $t >$ launch time).
Tangent problem where a quadratic in x has a known root and a second 'other' intersection
Discard the known repeated/tangent root and keep the new x-value as the required point.
Trigonometric equation on a stated interval producing multiple solutions
Filter solutions to those lying in the given range; context (e.g. 'first time after t=0') picks the smallest valid one.
Logarithmic or surd equation with an extraneous root
Reject roots that make a logarithm undefined or a surd negative; keep the algebraically admissible one.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating both algebraic roots as acceptable answers and giving two points when only one is required.
  • Assuming the 'first' or 'positive' root is automatically the answer without checking the diagram.
  • Confusing P with another labelled point (e.g. Q) on the same diagram and selecting that root instead.

Pitfalls

  • Choosing the wrong root because the diagram was not consulted or P was misidentified.
  • Substituting the chosen x into the more complicated curve and making an arithmetic slip when the simpler one would do.
  • Forgetting to state the y-coordinate, leaving the answer as just an x-value.
  • Sign errors when evaluating $f(-8)$ or similar — mishandling the product of negatives.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard across Edexcel IAL Pure (P1–P4) coordinate geometry, curve-sketching, and intersection problems, and applies equally in Mechanics (kinematics root selection) and Further Pure contexts.
