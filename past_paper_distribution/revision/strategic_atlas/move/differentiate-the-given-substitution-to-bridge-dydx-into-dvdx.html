Differential Equations

Differentiate the given substitution to bridge dy/dx into dv/dx

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The ODE is non-linear in y (e.g. it contains y², a product y·(y−4x), or y·dy/dx) so no integrating factor or separation works directly. The question explicitly hands you a substitution such as v = y − 2x, z = y², v = y/x, or z = 1/y. The expected output is a tidy reduced ODE in the new variable (linear, separable, or homogeneous-reduced). Equivalent phrasing: 'Use the substitution … to show that …', 'Hence, by means of the substitution …, find …'.

Motivation — Why this works

An ODE in y and x can only be transformed into one in v (or z) and x if BOTH y and dy/dx are re-expressed in the new variable. Differentiating the given substitution with respect to x — invoking the chain/product rule as needed — produces the bridge equation linking dy/dx to dv/dx. Combined with the algebraic rearrangement of y in terms of v and x, every occurrence of y in the original ODE can be eliminated. The underlying principle is the chain rule applied to a defining identity, which guarantees the new ODE is equivalent to the original.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the ODE is non-linear in y: a y² term, a product like y(y−4x), or a y^n on the right-hand side blocks the standard linear-ODE toolkit (integrating factor, exact-form test).
  2. Rule out direct attacks: integrating factor needs linearity in y; separation needs a clean f(x)g(y) split; neither is available here.
  3. Recognise that the question has supplied a substitution — this is a strong directive that the intended route is reduction via substitution, not a clever rearrangement.
  4. Commit to the bridge: differentiate the defining equation v = … with respect to x to obtain dv/dx in terms of dy/dx, then solve for dy/dx so it can be slotted into the ODE.
  5. Simultaneously rearrange the substitution algebraically to express y (and any composite like y − 4x) purely in v and x, so that EVERY y in the ODE can be replaced — leaving no mixed v–y terms.
  6. After substituting, simplify and check that the resulting equation is in the expected reduced form (linear in v, separable, or homogeneous); if y still appears, a substitution was missed.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write down the given substitution, e.g. v = y − 2x (or z = y², v = y/x, z = 1/y).
  2. Differentiate both sides with respect to x using chain/product rule: e.g. dv/dx = dy/dx − 2, or dz/dx = 2y·dy/dx, or dv/dx = (1/x)·dy/dx − y/x².
  3. Solve this bridge equation for dy/dx in terms of dv/dx (and possibly v, x): e.g. dy/dx = dv/dx + 2.
  4. Rearrange the substitution to express y in terms of v and x (e.g. y = v + 2x), and rewrite any composite expressions appearing in the ODE (e.g. y − 4x = v − 2x).
  5. Substitute the expressions for dy/dx and y into the original ODE, replacing EVERY occurrence of y.
  6. Simplify algebraically; the result should be a standard reduced ODE in v (or z) and x ready for the next technique (integrating factor, separation, etc.).

Worked Micro-Example

ODE: $\dfrac{dy}{dx} + 2x\,y(y-4x) = 2 - 8x^3$, with $v = y - 2x$.\\ Differentiate: $\dfrac{dv}{dx} = \dfrac{dy}{dx} - 2 \Rightarrow \dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{dv}{dx} + 2$.\\ Rewrite: $y = v + 2x$, and $y - 4x = v - 2x$, so $y(y-4x) = (v+2x)(v-2x) = v^2 - 4x^2$.\\ Substitute: $\dfrac{dv}{dx} + 2 + 2x(v^2 - 4x^2) = 2 - 8x^3 \;\Rightarrow\; \dfrac{dv}{dx} = -2x v^2$, a separable ODE in $v$ and $x$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Homogeneous ODE with substitution v = y/x
Differentiate using product rule: y = vx ⇒ dy/dx = v + x·dv/dx. Substitute to obtain a separable ODE in v and x.
Bernoulli-type equation dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x)y^n with substitution z = y^(1−n)
Differentiate: dz/dx = (1−n)y^(−n)·dy/dx; multiply the ODE through by (1−n)y^(−n) to obtain a linear ODE in z.
Reciprocal substitution z = 1/y to linearize
Differentiate: dz/dx = −(1/y²)·dy/dx ⇒ dy/dx = −y²·dz/dx = −(1/z²)·dz/dx. Watch the sign carefully.
Square substitution z = y² to clear a y·dy/dx term
Differentiate: dz/dx = 2y·dy/dx, so y·dy/dx = (1/2)·dz/dx. Substitute directly.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to apply an integrating factor to the original non-linear ODE without first substituting.
  • Replacing y in some terms but leaving y in others, producing a mixed v–y equation that cannot be solved.
  • Treating the substitution as purely algebraic and forgetting that dy/dx must also be transformed.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when differentiating: e.g. for v = y − 2x writing dv/dx = dy/dx + 2 instead of dy/dx − 2.
  • Forgetting the chain-rule factor: for z = y², dz/dx = 2y·dy/dx, NOT dy/dx.
  • For z = 1/y, missing the minus sign: dz/dx = −(1/y²)·dy/dx.
  • Not simplifying composite expressions (e.g. y − 4x in terms of v) before substituting, leading to algebraic clutter and missed cancellations.

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) first-order ODE work. The substitution is always GIVEN in the question stem at IAL level — students are not expected to invent it. Standard linear-ODE solution via integrating factor (formula booklet: e^{∫P dx}) follows after the substitution. Avoid invoking general Bernoulli theory by name; just execute the supplied substitution.
