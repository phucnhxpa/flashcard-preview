Complex Numbers

Recognise standard complex loci to bypass Cartesian expansion

Family: Pattern Recognition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a locus question presents an equation in z of the form |z − a| = r, |z − a| = |z − b|, |z − a| = k|z − b|, arg(z − a) = θ, or arg((z − a)/(z − b)) = θ. Also fires for inequality versions describing regions, or when the question asks for a sketch on an Argand diagram. Strong cue: the equation is written purely in terms of |·| or arg(·) with no real/imaginary parts split out. Weak cue: a follow-up part asks for the Cartesian equation, suggesting the geometric form is expected first.

Motivation — Why this works

Each canonical form corresponds to a named geometric object on the Argand plane, derivable once and reused: |z − a| = r is a circle (centre a, radius r); |z − a| = |z − b| is the perpendicular bisector of the segment ab; |z − a| = k|z − b| with k ≠ 1 is an Apollonius circle; arg(z − a) = θ is a half-line from a (excluding a) at angle θ to the positive real axis; arg((z − a)/(z − b)) = θ is an arc of a circle through a and b on which the chord ab subtends angle θ. Pattern-matching avoids the costly substitution z = x + iy and squaring, which often introduces sign errors or extraneous points.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the equation is expressed entirely in modulus or argument language about (z − a)-type blocks, not in x and y.
  2. I rule out a Cartesian-from-the-start approach because the algebra would re-derive a result I already know geometrically.
  3. I commit to pattern recognition once I match the form to one of the five canonical templates (circle, perpendicular bisector, Apollonius circle, half-line, arc).
  4. I read off the geometric data: centre and radius, or endpoints and angle, directly from a, b, r, k, θ.
  5. I decide whether the question wants a sketch (annotate key points), a description (state the locus by name), or a Cartesian equation (convert at the end only).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Classify the form: |z − a| = r, |z − a| = |z − b|, |z − a| = k|z − b|, arg(z − a) = θ, or arg((z − a)/(z − b)) = θ.
  2. Read off the parameters a, b, r, k, θ from the equation.
  3. State the locus geometrically: name (circle / perpendicular bisector / Apollonius circle / half-line / arc) plus defining features (centre, radius, endpoints, direction, included angle).
  4. Sketch on the Argand diagram, marking a, b, the midpoint of ab where relevant, the direction of any half-line, and whether endpoints are included or excluded.
  5. If a Cartesian equation is required, substitute z = x + iy only at this final stage and simplify.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the locus of z satisfying $|z - 2| = |z + 2i|$. Recognise this as the perpendicular bisector of the segment joining $2$ and $-2i$. Midpoint is $1 - i$; gradient of segment is $\tfrac{0-(-2)}{2-0} = 1$, so bisector has gradient $-1$. Equation: $y - (-1) = -1(x - 1)$, i.e. $y = -x$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

|z − a| = k|z − b| with k ≠ 1
Locus is an Apollonius circle; in A-level scope usually solved by squaring once to find centre and radius in Cartesian form.
arg((z − a)/(z − b)) = θ
Locus is a major or minor arc of a circle through a and b; the chord ab subtends angle θ at points on the arc, with a and b themselves excluded.
Inequalities such as |z − a| ≤ r or α ≤ arg(z − a) ≤ β
Locus becomes a region (disc, sector, or wedge); shade it and use solid/dashed boundaries to indicate inclusion.
Intersection of two loci, e.g. |z − a| = r and arg(z − b) = θ
Sketch both, then find intersection points algebraically or geometrically; usually 0, 1, or 2 points.
|z| = r or arg z = θ
Special case with a = 0; circle centred at origin, or half-line from origin.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading arg(z − a) = θ as a full line through a rather than a half-line from a.
  • Treating |z − a| = k|z − b| as a perpendicular bisector when k ≠ 1 (it is actually a circle).
  • Misreading arg((z − a)/(z − b)) = θ as a straight line rather than a circular arc.
  • Confusing |z − a| = |z − b| with |z| = |a − b| or similar surface-level matches.

Pitfalls

  • Drawing a full line through a instead of a half-line from a for arg(z − a) = θ.
  • Computing the perpendicular bisector's gradient incorrectly: it must be the negative reciprocal of the gradient of ab.
  • Including the point a in arg(z − a) = θ; it must be excluded since arg 0 is undefined.
  • For arc loci, drawing the wrong arc (major vs minor) — the side is determined by the sign and size of θ.
  • When converting to Cartesian by squaring, forgetting to check that no extraneous points were introduced.

A-level Scope Notes

All five canonical loci (circle, perpendicular bisector, Apollonius circle, half-line, arc) are explicitly within Edexcel IAL Further Pure complex numbers; sketching on an Argand diagram and converting to Cartesian form when asked are standard.
