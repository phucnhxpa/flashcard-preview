Trigonometry

Resolve square-root sign ambiguity using the range of arcsin

Family: Domain Restriction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when implicit differentiation of an inverse trig relation (e.g. y = arcsin(x/2)) produces an equation like cos²y = (4−x²)/4, and a square root must be taken to express dy/dx in terms of x. The ± ambiguity from √ must be resolved before the final single-valued derivative can be written. Especially critical in 'show that' or 'prove' questions where every sign choice must be justified. Also fires whenever a Pythagorean identity is used to convert between sin/cos/tan of an inverse trig output.

Motivation — Why this works

By definition, arcsin returns values only in [−π/2, π/2], and cosine is non-negative on this entire interval. So even though cos²y = k yields cos y = ±√k algebraically, the restricted range of arcsin forces the positive branch. This argument turns a two-valued algebraic step into a single-valued one and is the standard justification examiners expect for inverse-trig derivative proofs.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that √ on both sides of cos²y = (4−x²)/4 introduces a ± that the target identity does not contain.
  2. Rules out blindly choosing the positive root: in a 'show that' an unjustified sign loses the proof mark.
  3. Commits to the range argument because y is defined as arcsin, whose principal range fixes the sign of cos y.
  4. Bridges to the concrete step by recalling: range of arcsin is [−π/2, π/2], on which cos y ≥ 0.
  5. Tactically writes one explicit sentence stating the range, then concludes cos y = +√(…)/2 before substituting into dy/dx.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the inverse trig function defining y (here y = arcsin(x/2)) and quote its principal range: −π/2 ≤ y ≤ π/2.
  2. State the sign of the trig function appearing under the root on this range (here cos y ≥ 0).
  3. Take the positive square root only: cos y = +√(4−x²)/2.
  4. Substitute back into the differentiated relation to obtain the required single-valued dy/dx.

Worked Micro-Example

Given y = \arcsin(x/2), differentiate: \cos y \, \frac{dy}{dx} = \tfrac{1}{2}. From \sin y = x/2, \cos^2 y = 1 - x^2/4 = (4-x^2)/4. Since -\pi/2 \le y \le \pi/2, \cos y \ge 0, so \cos y = +\tfrac{1}{2}\sqrt{4-x^2}. Hence \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4-x^2}}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

y = arccos(x), needing sin y from sin²y = 1−x²
Range is [0, π], on which sin y ≥ 0, so sin y = +√(1−x²); leads to dy/dx = −1/√(1−x²).
y = arctan(x), needing sec y or cos y
Range is (−π/2, π/2), on which cos y > 0 and sec y > 0; take positive root of sec²y = 1+x².
y = arcsec(x) or arccsc(x) (Further Pure)
Use the conventional restricted range to fix the sign of the relevant trig function before taking √.
Integration via inverse trig substitution e.g. x = a sin θ
Restrict θ to [−π/2, π/2] so that √(a²−x²) = a cos θ (positive), justifying dropping the modulus.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the ± as a stylistic choice and silently picking + without citing the range of arcsin.
  • Confusing 'range of arcsin' with 'domain of sin', and quoting the wrong interval.
  • Believing cos y could be negative because x can be negative — forgetting that y, not x, controls the sign.

Pitfalls

  • Mixing up domain ([−1,1] for arcsin) with range ([−π/2, π/2]); only the range determines the sign.
  • Forgetting the factor of 2 when simplifying √((4−x²)/4) to √(4−x²)/2.
  • Applying the same range argument to arccos without updating: there sin y ≥ 0 because y ∈ [0, π], not because of arcsin.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL P3/P4 differentiation of inverse trig functions and FP integration via trig substitution; uses only the standard principal ranges of arcsin, arccos, arctan.
