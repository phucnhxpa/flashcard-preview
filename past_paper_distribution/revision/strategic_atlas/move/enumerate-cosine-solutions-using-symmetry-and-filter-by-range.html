Trigonometry

Enumerate cosine solutions using symmetry and filter by range

Family: Solution Enumeration Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires once an equation has been reduced to the form $\cos u = k$ (often after a substitution $u = ax+b$) with $u$ restricted to a transformed interval. Cues include: a quadratic-in-$\cos$ that yielded two values of $k$, or a linear substitution that maps $x \in [a,b]$ to a non-standard $u$-range. Whenever the question asks for ALL solutions in an interval, this move is the systematic enumeration step.

Motivation — Why this works

Cosine is even and has period $360°$, so $\cos u = k$ has exactly two solutions per period: the principal value $\alpha = \arccos(k) \in [0°,180°]$ and its reflection $360°-\alpha$. Listing both candidates per period and shifting by $\pm 360°$ guarantees completeness, while filtering against the $u$-interval guarantees no spurious solutions. This symmetry follows directly from $\cos(-\theta)=\cos\theta$ and $\cos(\theta+360°)=\cos\theta$.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation is now $\cos u = k$ on a transformed interval — the structure is standard enumeration.
  2. Rule out a single-answer approach: arccos returns only the principal value in $[0°,180°]$, which would miss the partner.
  3. Commit to listing both $\alpha$ and $360°-\alpha$, then translating by $\pm 360°$ until candidates leave the $u$-range.
  4. Bridge: compute $\alpha = \arccos(k)$ to full precision, then write down the partner $360°-\alpha$.
  5. Tactical decision: for each $k$ value, tabulate candidates, tick those inside the $u$-interval, then back-substitute to $x$ and round only at the end.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the $u$-range induced by the substitution (e.g. $x\in[10°,100°)$ with $u=2x+10$ gives $u\in[30°,210°)$ — recompute carefully).
  2. For each value of $k$, compute $\alpha = \arccos(k)$ keeping full calculator precision.
  3. List candidate solutions: $\alpha,\; 360°-\alpha,\; \alpha\pm360°,\; (360°-\alpha)\pm 360°$, continuing until clearly outside the $u$-range.
  4. Tick the candidates lying in the $u$-range; discard the rest.
  5. Back-substitute each surviving $u$ to recover $x$, then round to the requested precision.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\cos u = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$ for $u\in[10°,190°)$. Principal value: $\alpha = \arccos(1/\sqrt{3}) \approx 54.7356°$ ✓. Partner: $360°-\alpha \approx 305.26°$ ✗. Period shifts $\alpha\pm360°$ also lie outside. So $u\approx 54.7356°$ is the unique solution; back-substituting $u=2x+10$ gives $x\approx 22.4°$ (1 d.p.).

Variants — Same move, different surface

$\sin u = k$ on a restricted interval
Use the pair $\alpha = \arcsin(k)$ and $180°-\alpha$, then add $\pm 360°$ shifts.
$\tan u = k$ on a restricted interval
Single family $u = \arctan(k) + 180°n$ since $\tan$ has period $180°$ — no reflection partner.
Quadratic in $\cos x$ giving two values of $k$
Apply the cosine enumeration twice, once per $k$, and union the surviving solutions.
Radian interval e.g. $u\in[0,2\pi)$
Replace $360°$ with $2\pi$ and $\alpha$ stays as $\arccos(k)\in[0,\pi]$, partner $2\pi-\alpha$.

Common Misreadings

  • Reporting only the principal arccos value and missing the $360°-\alpha$ partner.
  • Applying the sine rule $180°-\alpha$ to a cosine equation (or vice versa).
  • Filtering against the original $x$-range instead of the substituted $u$-range.
  • Stopping after one period without checking whether $\pm 360°$ shifts also lie in a wide $u$-interval.

Pitfalls

  • Rounding $\alpha$ early; subsequent division (e.g. by $2$) magnifies the error — keep full precision until the final $x$.
  • Forgetting that endpoints may be excluded: check whether the $u$-interval is open or closed before accepting boundary solutions.
  • Mis-transforming the interval: when $u=ax+b$, the $u$-range is $[a\cdot\text{min}+b,\ a\cdot\text{max}+b]$ — easy to compute incorrectly.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure (C2/P2 and P3) trig-equation technique; uses only $\arccos$ on a calculator and the symmetry/periodicity of cosine, with no identities beyond syllabus.
