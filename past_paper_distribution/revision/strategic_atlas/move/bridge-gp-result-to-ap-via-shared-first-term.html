Sequences & Series

Bridge GP result to AP via shared first term

Family: Parameter Linking Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in mixed AP/GP problems where the question explicitly states both sequences share a parameter (most often the first term $a$). A 'Hence' or 'Hence find' in a later part is the strongest cue that an earlier-determined value must be carried forward. Also fires when an AP equation alone is under-determined (one equation, two unknowns $a$ and $d$) but a previous part has pinned down $a$ from GP conditions.

Motivation — Why this works

Mixed sequence problems are deliberately stitched together through a shared parameter so that information flows from one sub-problem to the next. The GP side, with conditions on terms or sums, typically determines $a$ uniquely; that value is then injected into the AP relation to collapse it from two unknowns to one. Recognising the bridge is what makes the chain of parts solvable — without it, the final part is genuinely under-determined.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that part (c)'s AP equation, e.g. $S_5 = \tfrac{5}{2}(2a+4d) = 65$, has two unknowns $a$ and $d$.
  2. Rule out solving the AP independently — there is not enough information in part (c) alone.
  3. Commit to reusing the value of $a$ produced by parts (a)–(b), prompted by the keyword 'Hence'.
  4. Bridge: substitute the GP-derived $a$ into the AP formula, turning it into a single-variable equation.
  5. Tactically, write $a = 4$ once at the top of part (c) so the substitution is clean and the remaining algebra is just solving for $d$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the shared parameter declared in the stem (typically 'the same first term $a$').
  2. From the GP sub-parts, record the numerical value of $a$.
  3. Write the AP relation required in the current part using $a$ and $d$.
  4. Substitute the known $a$ to reduce to one unknown.
  5. Solve the resulting linear (or quadratic) equation for the remaining parameter.

Worked Micro-Example

GP with first term $a$ and ratio $r=\tfrac{1}{2}$ has $S_\infty = 8$, so $a/(1-\tfrac{1}{2}) = 8 \Rightarrow a = 4$. The AP shares this first term and satisfies $S_5 = 65$: $\tfrac{5}{2}(2a + 4d) = 65 \Rightarrow 2a + 4d = 26$. Substituting $a=4$: $8 + 4d = 26 \Rightarrow d = 4.5$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Shared common ratio/difference instead of first term
One sub-problem fixes $r$ (or $d$); inject it into the other sequence's term/sum equation.
A specified term is shared, e.g. $u_3$ of AP equals $u_2$ of GP
Form an equation $a + 2d = ar$ linking the parameters and combine with one further condition.
Shared sum condition, e.g. $S_n^{AP} = S_n^{GP}$
Equate the two sum formulas and solve, treating one parameter as already known from a prior part.
Three terms in AP that are also in GP (or vice versa)
Use both AP property $2b = a+c$ and GP property $b^2 = ac$ as the bridging equations.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'Hence' as optional and trying to solve the final part from scratch, leaving an under-determined equation.
  • Reading 'same first term' as 'same first two terms', importing GP structure into the AP.
  • Assuming the shared parameter is the common difference/ratio when the stem actually shares only $a$.

Pitfalls

  • Substituting $a$ into the wrong formula (using $ar^{n-1}$ in place of $a + (n-1)d$).
  • Arithmetic slip in $\tfrac{n}{2}(2a+(n-1)d)$ after substitution, especially with the factor of $n/2$.
  • Carrying forward an incorrect $a$ from an earlier part and not sanity-checking against given sums.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 sequences and series: AP sum $S_n = \tfrac{n}{2}(2a+(n-1)d)$, GP sum $S_n = a(1-r^n)/(1-r)$ and $S_\infty = a/(1-r)$ for $|r|<1$.
