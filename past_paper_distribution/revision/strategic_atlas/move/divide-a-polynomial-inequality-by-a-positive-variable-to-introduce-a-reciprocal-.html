Strategic Atlas / Proof / Inequality Division By Sign
Proof

Divide a polynomial inequality by a positive variable to introduce a reciprocal term

Family: Inequality Division By Sign Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The target inequality mixes a variable with its reciprocal (e.g. k + 9/k, a/b + b/a, x + 1/x), but a known/derived non-negative expression — typically a perfect square such as (k − 3)² ≥ 0 — gives only polynomial terms. A positivity condition (k > 0, ab > 0, x > 0) is explicitly stated. The move fires whenever you need to bridge a degree-2 polynomial inequality to one containing a degree −1 term.

Motivation — Why this works

Dividing every term of a polynomial inequality by a positive quantity reduces each exponent by 1 while preserving the inequality's direction. So k² − 6k + 9 ≥ 0 becomes k − 6 + 9/k ≥ 0, exactly the shape needed for a reciprocal-style target. The positivity hypothesis is precisely what legitimises this division — without it the inequality could flip or be undefined. This is the canonical bridge between 'expand a square' and 'reciprocal inequality'.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the target k + 9/k ≥ 6 has terms of degree 1 and −1, while the square (k − 3)² ≥ 0 expands to a degree-2 polynomial — there is a one-step exponent gap.
  2. Rule out factoring or AM-GM directly: the cleanest bridge is to lower every exponent by 1, i.e. divide through by k.
  3. Commit because the condition k > 0 is given, so dividing by k is both well-defined and direction-preserving — and this is exactly why that hypothesis was supplied.
  4. Operationally: divide k² − 6k + 9 ≥ 0 termwise by k to get k − 6 + 9/k ≥ 0, remembering 0/k = 0 on the RHS.
  5. Rearrange by adding 6 to both sides to land precisely on the required form k + 9/k ≥ 6, and explicitly cite 'since k > 0' at the division step to secure the justification mark.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Start from the established polynomial inequality, e.g. k² − 6k + 9 ≥ 0 (from (k − 3)² ≥ 0).
  2. State 'since k > 0' to justify division preserving the inequality.
  3. Divide every term by k: k − 6 + 9/k ≥ 0 (note RHS: 0/k = 0).
  4. Add 6 to both sides to isolate the required expression: k + 9/k ≥ 6.
  5. Conclude, optionally noting equality holds when k = 3.

Worked Micro-Example

Prove that for $k > 0$, $k + \dfrac{9}{k} \geq 6$.\\ Since $(k-3)^2 \geq 0$, expanding gives $k^2 - 6k + 9 \geq 0$.\\ As $k > 0$, divide both sides by $k$: $k - 6 + \dfrac{9}{k} \geq 0$.\\ Adding $6$ to both sides yields $k + \dfrac{9}{k} \geq 6$, with equality when $k = 3$. $\blacksquare$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Proving a/b + b/a ≥ 2 for a, b > 0
Start from (a − b)² ≥ 0, then divide by ab (positive since a, b > 0) to get the reciprocal pair.
Proving x + 1/x ≥ 2 for x > 0
Start from (x − 1)² ≥ 0, divide through by x.
Inequalities involving √k for k > 0
Divide by √k (positive) instead of k to halve, rather than reduce by 1, the effective exponent.
Negative-variable case, e.g. k < 0
Dividing by k reverses the inequality, so the conclusion becomes k + 9/k ≤ −6.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the division as a routine algebraic step and omitting the explicit 'since k > 0' justification.
  • Believing the inequality (k − 3)² ≥ 0 alone is the proof, without performing the bridging division.
  • Confusing this with AM-GM and quoting AM-GM as the justification rather than the perfect-square argument expected at A-level.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to cite k > 0 at the moment of division — examiners deduct the justification mark even if the algebra is correct.
  • Forgetting to divide the RHS (writing k − 6 + 9/k ≥ 0/k incorrectly, or leaving a stray constant).
  • Dividing only some terms (e.g. dividing 9 but not k² by k).
  • Using this move when the positivity hypothesis is absent — the direction may flip for negative values.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (proof by deduction from a perfect square) and a staple Further Pure technique; no AM-GM or advanced inequality machinery is invoked.
