Factorise quadratic to extract x-intercepts for sketch

Family: Factorisation For Roots Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks you to sketch a quadratic y = ax² + bx + c (or solve ax² + bx + c = 0) and the coefficients suggest integer roots — typically when a = 1 and you can spot two integers multiplying to c and summing to b. Cues include phrases like 'sketch the curve', 'state the coordinates where the curve meets the x-axis', or 'find the roots'. Also fires whenever a quadratic factor must be split to expose zeros (e.g. inside a cubic that has already been reduced). It is the default first attempt before reaching for the formula or completing the square.

Motivation — Why this works

If (x − p)(x − q) = 0 then x = p or x = q by the zero-product principle, so factorised form makes the x-intercepts immediately readable. For a sketch, the two roots plus the y-intercept and the upward/downward orientation pin down the shape with minimum work. The quadratic formula and completing the square recover the same information but require more arithmetic; when integer factors exist, factorisation is strictly faster and less error-prone. The vertex sits midway between the roots at x = (p + q)/2, so factorisation also accelerates the symmetry argument for the turning point.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the task is a sketch of a quadratic, so I need intercepts, vertex, and orientation — roots are the priority.
  2. I rule out the quadratic formula and completing the square once I scan b and c and see integer factors of c that add to b are visible.
  3. I commit to factorisation because the leading coefficient is 1 and the discriminant looks like a perfect square (small integer roots likely).
  4. I bridge to the concrete step: set the expression equal to zero and search for the integer pair (p, q) with pq = c and p + q = b.
  5. Tactically, I also read off the y-intercept from c, use orientation from the sign of a, and locate the vertex via x = (p+q)/2 rather than −b/(2a) for speed.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Set the quadratic equal to zero: ax² + bx + c = 0.
  2. Find integers p, q with pq = c and p + q = b (for a = 1); factorise as (x − p)(x − q) = 0.
  3. Read off roots x = p, x = q, giving x-intercepts (p, 0) and (q, 0).
  4. Substitute x = 0 to get y-intercept (0, c).
  5. Locate the vertex at x = (p + q)/2 and evaluate y there.
  6. Use the sign of a to determine U-shape (a > 0) or ∩-shape (a < 0) and assemble the sketch.

Worked Micro-Example

Sketch y = x² − x − 2. Solve x² − x − 2 = 0: seek integers with product −2 and sum −1, giving (x − 2)(x + 1) = 0, so x = 2 or x = −1. y-intercept: (0, −2). Vertex at x = (2 + (−1))/2 = 1/2, y = (1/2)² − 1/2 − 2 = −9/4, so vertex (1/2, −9/4). Since a = 1 > 0, draw a U-shape through (−1, 0), (2, 0), (0, −2) with minimum at (1/2, −9/4).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Leading coefficient a ≠ 1, e.g. 2x² + x − 6
Use the AC method: find factors of ac = −12 summing to b = 1 (4 and −3), split the middle term, and factor by grouping to get (2x − 3)(x + 2).
Difference of two squares, e.g. x² − 9
Factor directly as (x − 3)(x + 3); roots are ±√c with vertex on the y-axis.
Discriminant not a perfect square
Abandon factorisation; apply x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac))/(2a) and plot approximate intercepts, keeping exact surd form for any 'exact' marks.
Question asks for vertex/range or minimum value
Switch to completing the square to read the vertex directly from y = a(x − h)² + k.
Quadratic factor inside a cubic after factor theorem
Apply the same factorisation move to the residual quadratic to expose remaining real roots.
Quadratic in disguise, e.g. x⁴ − x² − 2
Substitute u = x², factorise (u − 2)(u + 1), then back-substitute and discard u = −1 to get x = ±√2.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the sketch as needing only roots and omitting the y-intercept and vertex.
  • Reaching for completing the square out of habit when integer factors are visible.
  • Reading the constant term as a root, e.g. claiming x = −2 is a root of x² − x − 2 directly from the −2.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when pairing factors: writing (x + 2)(x − 1) instead of (x − 2)(x + 1).
  • Forgetting the y-intercept (0, c), which is often a free mark on Edexcel sketch questions.
  • Mislabelling the vertex by computing y at the wrong x, especially miscomputing (p + q)/2 with negative roots.
  • Drawing the parabola opening the wrong way after misreading the sign of a.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 quadratic content: factorisation, roots, sketches, and the link between factor form and intercepts. No discriminant theory or transformations beyond the standard P1 toolkit are required.
