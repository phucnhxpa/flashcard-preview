Reject non-positive substitution values when solving quadratics in an exponential

Family: Domain Validation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires after a substitution like $y = a^x$ (with $a>0$, $a\neq 1$) has reduced an equation to a quadratic in $y$, and that quadratic has been solved. Cue phrases: 'let $y = 2^x$', 'hence find $x$', or any equation involving $a^{2x}$ and $a^x$. Once roots $y_1, y_2$ are obtained, the check $y > 0$ must be performed before taking logarithms. Also fires for $y = e^x$ substitutions.

Motivation — Why this works

An exponential $a^x$ with $a>0$ is strictly positive for every real $x$, so $a^x = c$ has no real solution when $c \le 0$. Any non-positive root of the auxiliary quadratic therefore corresponds to no real $x$ and must be discarded. Skipping this check leads to attempts to evaluate $\ln$ of a non-positive number, costing accuracy marks and producing spurious 'solutions'. Examiners explicitly reward stating the rejection with reason.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the quadratic in $y$ has been solved and I now have candidate values of $y$.
  2. I rule out any $y \le 0$ because $y = a^x$ cannot be non-positive for real $x$.
  3. I commit to filtering the roots before logarithming, since $\ln$ of a non-positive number is undefined.
  4. I write each root next to a tick or cross with a brief reason ('$2^x>0$, reject').
  5. For surviving roots I proceed to $x = \log_a y$ (or $\ln y$ for $e^x$).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. List the roots $y_1, y_2$ of the quadratic.
  2. For each root, test $y_i > 0$.
  3. If $y_i \le 0$, write 'reject since $a^x > 0$' and discard.
  4. For each retained $y_i$, solve $a^x = y_i$ by taking $\log_a$ (or $\ln$).
  5. State the final $x$-values clearly.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $4^x - 2^x - 6 = 0$. Let $y = 2^x$, giving $y^2 - y - 6 = 0$, so $(y-3)(y+2)=0$ and $y = 3$ or $y = -2$. Reject $y = -2$ since $2^x > 0$ for all real $x$. Hence $2^x = 3$, giving $x = \log_2 3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic in $e^x$, e.g. $e^{2x} - 5e^x + 4 = 0$
Reject any non-positive root of the quadratic in $y = e^x$; solve $x = \ln y$ for survivors.
Quadratic in $\sin x$ or $\cos x$
Reject roots with $|y| > 1$ since $\sin x, \cos x \in [-1,1]$.
Quadratic in $\sqrt{x}$
Reject negative roots since $\sqrt{x} \ge 0$ by convention.
Quadratic in $\ln x$
All real roots are admissible (since $\ln x$ ranges over $\mathbb{R}$), but the resulting $x = e^{y}$ must still be checked against any original domain restriction.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating both quadratic roots as automatically valid without checking the range of the substitution.
  • Confusing the substitution's range (here $y>0$) with the original variable's range.

Pitfalls

  • Attempting $\log$ of a negative number and producing nonsense.
  • Forgetting to state the rejection explicitly, losing the reasoning mark.
  • Rejecting a perfectly valid positive but small root (e.g. $y = \tfrac{1}{2}$) by confusing 'small' with 'invalid'.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P2/P3 technique for exponential equations reducible to quadratics; uses only the property $a^x>0$ for $a>0$ and basic logarithms.
