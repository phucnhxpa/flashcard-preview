Collapse e^(k ln a) to a^k at logarithmic bounds

Family: Log Law Simplification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an exponential expression of the form e^(k ln a) appears — typically when substituting a logarithmic bound (e.g. x = 4 ln 2) into an antiderivative containing e^(x/2), or when simplifying answers that mix exponentials and logarithms. The cue is any product of a constant with ln(of a positive number) sitting inside an exponent. Alternative phrasings include e^(ln a^k), exp(k ln a), or a^k written via a logarithm. Bounds like ln k, 2 ln k, or ½ ln k almost always signal that this collapse is intended.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity e^(k ln a) = a^k follows directly from the inverse relationship between exp and ln: e^(ln a) = a, and raising both sides to the k-th power gives e^(k ln a) = a^k. Applying this turns transcendental expressions into rational numbers, removing the exponential entirely. Examiners design problems with logarithmic bounds precisely so this collapse occurs cleanly, and mark schemes typically require the simplified integer form. Leaving e^(2 ln 2) unsimplified usually loses the final accuracy mark.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the exponent on e is a constant multiple of ln(something positive) — a structural flag for the identity e^(k ln a) = a^k.
  2. Rule out trying to evaluate numerically (decimals lose exactness) and rule out misapplying the index law as k·e^(ln a).
  3. Commit to the rewrite because the bound was clearly engineered: the coefficient of ln a inside the exponent is a small integer or simple fraction.
  4. Bridge: rewrite e^(k ln a) as (e^(ln a))^k = a^k, then substitute this integer back into the antiderivative.
  5. Tactically, check the lower bound too — e^0 = 1, not 0 — and keep any standalone ln a terms unsimplified so the final answer reads as p + q ln a.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the exponent as k · ln a for some constant k and positive a.
  2. Rewrite e^(k ln a) = (e^(ln a))^k = a^k.
  3. Substitute the resulting integer (or rational) into the antiderivative at that bound.
  4. Repeat at the other bound, remembering e^0 = 1.
  5. Subtract F(upper) − F(lower) and collect the surviving ln a terms separately from the rational constants.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_0^{4\ln 2} e^{x/2}\,dx$. Antiderivative is $2e^{x/2}$. At $x=4\ln 2$: $2e^{2\ln 2} = 2\cdot 2^2 = 8$. At $x=0$: $2e^0 = 2$. Result: $8-2 = 6$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Bound is x = 3 ln 5 inside e^x
e^(3 ln 5) = 5^3 = 125 directly.
Bound is x = ½ ln 9 inside e^x
e^(½ ln 9) = 9^(1/2) = 3.
Negative coefficient, e.g. e^(−2 ln 3)
Collapses to 3^(−2) = 1/9; sign of k carries through as a reciprocal.
Mixed exponent like e^(ln 2 + ln 3)
Use ln 2 + ln 3 = ln 6 first, then e^(ln 6) = 6.
Differential equation solution where C = e^(k ln a)
Same collapse turns the arbitrary constant into a rational a^k for clean particular solutions.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading e^(k ln a) as k · e^(ln a) = k a (treating the coefficient as a multiplier outside the exponent).
  • Reading e^(2 ln 2) as 2 ln 4 or as ln 4 by confusing exp/ln structure.
  • Believing the coefficient k must be a positive integer for the identity to apply — it works for any real k.

Pitfalls

  • Arriving at the right value by wrong reasoning, e.g. e^(2 ln 2) = 2·e^(ln 2) = 4 — coincidentally correct here but fails for e^(3 ln 2).
  • Forgetting that e^0 = 1, so the lower-bound contribution is rarely zero when a constant multiplies the exponential.
  • Simplifying ln 2 to a decimal partway through, breaking the exact form required by the mark scheme.
  • Dropping the surviving ln a terms or absorbing them into the rational part.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the inverse relationship between e^x and ln x and standard index laws — fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 logarithm and exponential content, and routinely required in P3/P4 definite integration.
