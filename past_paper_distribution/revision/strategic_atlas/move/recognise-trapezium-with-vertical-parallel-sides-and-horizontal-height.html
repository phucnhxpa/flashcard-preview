Coordinate Geometry

Recognise trapezium with vertical parallel sides and horizontal height

Family: Trapezium Orientation Recognition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a quadrilateral in the coordinate plane has two sides lying on vertical lines x = a and x = b with different lengths, or more generally when the two parallel sides of a trapezium are not horizontal. Cues include vertices given with two pairs sharing the same x-coordinate, or a sketch where the 'top' and 'bottom' edges are slanted while the 'left' and 'right' edges are vertical. Also fires when applying A = ½(a+b)h to a region bounded by two vertical line segments and a curve or chord.

Motivation — Why this works

The trapezium area formula A = ½(a+b)h depends only on the two parallel sides and the perpendicular distance between them, regardless of orientation. If the parallel sides happen to be vertical, the perpendicular distance is the horizontal gap |b − a|, not a vertical length. Treating 'height' as always vertical is a habit, not a rule, and rotating the picture (or the formula) by 90° preserves the area. Identifying which pair of sides is parallel is therefore the first decision, not an afterthought.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice two vertices share x-coordinate 0 and two share x-coordinate 1.5, so two sides lie on vertical lines.
  2. I rule out the 'top and bottom' edges as the parallel pair because they connect points at different x-values and are slanted.
  3. I commit to OD and CB as the parallel sides because both are vertical, hence parallel to each other.
  4. Since the parallel sides are vertical, the perpendicular distance between them is the horizontal gap, h = 1.5.
  5. I read the parallel-side lengths as differences of y-coordinates and plug into A = ½(a+b)h.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. List the four vertices and group them by shared x-coordinate (or shared y-coordinate) to spot the parallel pair.
  2. Compute the lengths of the two parallel sides as |Δy| (if vertical) or |Δx| (if horizontal).
  3. Compute h as the perpendicular distance between the two parallel lines: |Δx| if sides are vertical, |Δy| if horizontal.
  4. Apply A = ½(a+b)h with the correct a, b, h.

Worked Micro-Example

Vertices $O(0,0)$, $D(0,6)$, $C(1.5,3.708)$, $B(1.5,6)$. Parallel sides are $OD$ (vertical, length $6$) and $BC$ (vertical, length $6-3.708=2.292$). Perpendicular distance: $h=1.5$. Area $=\tfrac{1}{2}(6+2.292)(1.5)\approx 6.22\,\text{m}^2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Trapezium with horizontal parallel sides (the standard orientation)
Parallel sides are read as |Δx|, and h is the vertical gap |Δy| between the two horizontal lines.
Trapezium with parallel sides on slanted lines y = mx + c₁ and y = mx + c₂
Use h = |c₂ − c₁|/√(1+m²) for the perpendicular distance between the two parallel lines.
Region bounded by a curve and two vertical ordinates, approximated by a single trapezium
The two ordinates are the parallel sides; h is the horizontal strip width — same orientation as this move.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming the 'top' and 'bottom' edges (e.g. DC and OA) must be the parallel pair simply because of their position in the sketch.
  • Treating the longest side as automatically the 'base' and the tallest vertical extent as the 'height'.

Pitfalls

  • Using a vertical length (e.g. 6) as h when the parallel sides are themselves vertical.
  • Confusing the non-parallel slanted sides with the parallel ones and averaging the wrong lengths.
  • Forgetting to take absolute differences, producing a negative 'length'.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits within Edexcel IAL Pure coordinate geometry; uses only the trapezium area formula and distances between parallel lines, both standard at AS level.
