Audit note

The atlas has ode/separation-of-variables for directly separable ODEs, but the strategic recognition that a substitution-reduced ODE is now separable — and the decision to separate rather than use an integrating factor — is a distinct post-substitution routing decision that fires repeatedly in FP2 Chapter 4.

Evidence: 4::Jan_2022::Q8, 4::Jan_2021::Q4

Trigger — When to use

Fires immediately after a given substitution (e.g. $v=y-2x$, or $z=y^{-2}$ for a Bernoulli equation) has reduced a non-linear ODE to the form $\frac{dv}{dx}=g(x)\,h(v)$. The cue: the transformed equation has the state variable and $x$ in a product/factorable shape rather than a linear $\frac{dv}{dx}+P(x)v=Q(x)$.

Motivation — Why this works

Once the substitution removes the non-linearity into a $g(x)h(v)$ product, the variables genuinely separate: $\frac{dv}{h(v)}=g(x)\,dx$ integrates each side independently — simpler than building an integrating factor, which is only needed when the reduced equation is linear in $v$.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Inspect the reduced ODE: is it $\frac{dv}{dx}=g(x)h(v)$ (separable) or $\frac{dv}{dx}+P(x)v=Q(x)$ (linear)?
  2. If the $v$-dependence factors out as a single $h(v)$ (e.g. $-2xv^2$), commit to separation, not an integrating factor.
  3. Divide both sides by $h(v)$ and multiply by $dx$ to get $\frac{dv}{h(v)}=g(x)\,dx$.
  4. Integrate each side with standard results (power rule, $\ln$, etc.).
  5. Solve for $v$, then back-substitute the original substitution to express the answer in $y$ and $x$.
  6. Apply any initial condition to fix the constant; absorb signs into the arbitrary constant cleanly.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Confirm the reduced form $\frac{dv}{dx}=g(x)h(v)$.
  2. Separate: $\dfrac{dv}{h(v)}=g(x)\,dx$.
  3. Integrate: $\displaystyle\int\frac{dv}{h(v)}=\int g(x)\,dx$.
  4. Rearrange for $v$, folding constants into one arbitrary $c$.
  5. Back-substitute (e.g. $y=v+2x$) to give the solution in the original variables.

Worked Micro-Example

After $v=y-2x$ reduces the ODE to $\frac{dv}{dx}=-2xv^2$, separate: $\frac{dv}{v^2}=-2x\,dx$. Integrate: $-\frac{1}{v}=-x^2+C$, so $\frac{1}{v}=x^2-C$, i.e. $v=\frac{1}{x^2+c}$. Back-substitute $y=v+2x$ to get $y=\frac{1}{x^2+c}+2x$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Bernoulli equation reduced by $z=y^{-2}$ to $\frac{dz}{dx}-4z=-6x$.
Here the reduced equation is LINEAR in $z$, so do NOT separate — use the integrating factor $e^{\int-4dx}=e^{-4x}$ instead; this is the routing decision to watch for.
Substitution leaves a separable but harder $h(v)$ (e.g. $\frac{1}{v^2}$ vs $\cot y$).
Same separation; just match $\int\frac{dv}{h(v)}$ to the right standard integral (power rule, $\int\cot y\,dy=\ln|\sin y|$, etc.).

Common Misreadings

  • Reaching for an integrating factor on a reduced equation that has factored into $g(x)h(v)$ and is already separable.
  • Failing to notice the reduced equation is linear in the new variable (Bernoulli case) and wrongly trying to separate $\frac{dz}{dx}-4z=-6x$.
  • Thinking the substitution itself solves the ODE, rather than just setting up the separable (or linear) form to integrate.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error after integrating $\int v^{-2}dv=-\frac{1}{v}$, or mishandling the arbitrary constant when absorbing $-C$ into $c$.
  • Forgetting to back-substitute (leaving the answer in $v$ or $z$ instead of $y$).
  • Forgetting that $\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{dv}{dx}+2$ when $v=y-2x$ (the constant term), corrupting the reduced equation.
  • Choosing separation when the reduced equation is actually linear (or vice versa), wasting time on the wrong tool.
