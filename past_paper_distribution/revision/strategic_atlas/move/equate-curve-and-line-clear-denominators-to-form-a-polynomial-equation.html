Algebra & Functions

Equate curve and line, clear denominators to form a polynomial equation

Family: Simultaneous To Polynomial Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question concerns intersection (or non-intersection, or tangency) of a rational curve such as y = a/(x − k) with a straight line y = mx + c. Cue phrases include 'find the values of k for which the line does not meet the curve', 'show that the line is tangent to the curve', or 'find the points of intersection'. Whenever discriminant analysis or factorisation is the eventual goal, you must first manufacture a polynomial in x.

Motivation — Why this works

Discriminant tests, factor theorem, and Vieta-style reasoning all require a polynomial equation set equal to zero. Setting the two y-expressions equal and multiplying through by the denominator converts the rational equation into a quadratic (or higher) polynomial. Multiplying by (x − k) is safe here because x = k is the curve's vertical asymptote and is automatically excluded from the solution set, so no spurious roots are introduced.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that 'no intersection / tangent / meets' translates into a simultaneous-equation problem in x.
  2. Rules out graphical or substitution-into-y approaches as messier, and rules out fearing the multiplication because x = k is already excluded by the asymptote.
  3. Commits to clearing the denominator because the eventual tool (discriminant) only works on polynomials of the form ax² + bx + c = 0.
  4. Bridges to the first operation by writing 4/(x − k) = 9 − x and multiplying both sides by (x − k).
  5. Tactically expands carefully, watches double-negatives, then rearranges everything to one side in standard quadratic form, ready for b² − 4ac.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Set the two y-expressions equal: 4/(x − k) = 9 − x.
  2. Multiply both sides by (x − k): 4 = (9 − x)(x − k).
  3. Expand the RHS: 4 = 9x − 9k − x² + kx.
  4. Move everything to one side: x² − (9 + k)x + (9k + 4) = 0.
  5. Identify coefficients a = 1, b = −(9 + k), c = 9k + 4 ready for discriminant work.

Worked Micro-Example

Find k for which y = 4/(x − k) and y = 9 − x do not meet. Set $\frac{4}{x-k} = 9 - x$, so $4 = (9-x)(x-k) = 9x - 9k - x^2 + kx$. Rearranging: $x^2 - (9+k)x + (9k+4) = 0$. This quadratic is the object on which the discriminant condition $b^2 - 4ac < 0$ will subsequently be imposed.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Curve y = a/(x − p) meets line y = mx + c
Multiply through by (x − p) to obtain a quadratic in x; coefficients depend on m, c, a, p.
Curve y = a/x² meets a line
Multiply through by x² to obtain a cubic; discriminant tools no longer apply directly — use factor theorem or calculus for tangency.
Two rational expressions equal: a/(x−p) = b/(x−q)
Cross-multiply (or multiply by LCM (x−p)(x−q)) to obtain a linear or quadratic equation; check x = p, x = q are not solutions.
Curve and line where line is given implicitly, e.g. x + y = 9
First rearrange to y = 9 − x, then proceed as standard.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'no intersection' as a geometric statement and trying to argue from sketches rather than algebra, missing exact bounds on k.
  • Substituting y = 9 − x into the curve as y(x − k) = 4 without realising this is the same move dressed up, then mis-expanding.
  • Believing that multiplying by (x − k) introduces a spurious root, and unnecessarily checking x = k separately.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors expanding (9 − x)(x − k); in particular (−x)(−k) = +kx is often dropped or signed wrongly.
  • Forgetting to bring the constant 4 across so the equation is set to zero before applying b² − 4ac.
  • Mis-identifying b as (9 + k) instead of −(9 + k) when reading off coefficients for the discriminant.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure content (P1/P2): simultaneous equations between a curve and a line, clearing fractions, and forming a quadratic in preparation for discriminant analysis.
