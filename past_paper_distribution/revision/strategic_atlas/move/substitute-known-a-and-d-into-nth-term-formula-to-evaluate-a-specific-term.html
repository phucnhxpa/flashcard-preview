Sequences & Series

Substitute known a and d into nth-term formula to evaluate a specific term

Family: Direct Evaluation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an arithmetic progression has its first term a and common difference d already determined (often from a previous part), and the question asks for a specific numbered term such as u_{20} or 'the 15th term'. Cues include phrases like 'find the value of', 'hence find the kth term', or 'state the nth term'. The work needed is a single substitution into u_n = a + (n-1)d, not the setting up of equations.

Motivation — Why this works

Once a and d are known, the AP is fully specified, so every term is given by the closed-form u_n = a + (n-1)d. This formula encodes the defining recurrence u_n = u_{n-1} + d in one step, making any term reachable without iterating. Direct substitution is the most efficient and least error-prone route, with no need for simultaneous equations or summation identities.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question asks for a single specific term of an AP and I already have numerical values for a and d.
  2. I rule out re-deriving a and d, listing terms one by one, or using the sum formula S_n — none are needed for a single term.
  3. I commit to u_n = a + (n-1)d because it is the direct closed form for the nth term of an AP.
  4. I bridge to computation by writing u_n with the target n substituted, keeping (n-1) explicit to avoid the classic off-by-one slip.
  5. Tactically, I evaluate (n-1)d first, then add a, and sanity-check the sign and magnitude against the trend of the sequence.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Recall u_n = a + (n-1)d.
  2. Substitute the known a, d, and target n.
  3. Compute (n-1) first, then multiply by d.
  4. Add a to obtain the value of u_n.
  5. State the answer with appropriate units/context.

Worked Micro-Example

Given a = 10 and d = 4, find u_{20}.\\ u_{20} = a + (20-1)d = 10 + 19 \times 4 \\ = 10 + 76 = 86.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Geometric progression with known a and r
Use u_n = a r^{n-1} instead; substitute n directly after computing r^{n-1}.
AP given by two conditions in earlier part
Carry forward the solved a and d; the evaluation step itself is unchanged.
Question asks for 'the kth term equals X — find k'
Set a + (n-1)d = X and solve for n rather than substituting a known n.
Sum of first n terms requested instead of u_n
Switch to S_n = (n/2)(2a + (n-1)d) or S_n = (n/2)(a + l).
Sequence defined recursively as u_{n+1} = u_n + d
First identify a = u_1 and d, then apply u_n = a + (n-1)d as usual.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the index as starting from 0 (so using n instead of n-1).
  • Confusing 'the 20th term' with 'the sum of the first 20 terms' and applying S_n.
  • Reading d as the second term rather than the common difference.

Pitfalls

  • Off-by-one error: using n in place of (n-1), e.g. u_{20} = 10 + 20\times 4 = 90 instead of 86.
  • Sign error when d is negative — failing to bracket (n-1)d before multiplying.
  • Arithmetic slip when (n-1)d is computed mentally for large n; safer to write the product explicitly.
  • Forgetting to substitute the latest values of a and d if they were updated in a later part of the question.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P2) coverage of arithmetic sequences and the standard formula u_n = a + (n-1)d; no techniques beyond A-level scope are required.
