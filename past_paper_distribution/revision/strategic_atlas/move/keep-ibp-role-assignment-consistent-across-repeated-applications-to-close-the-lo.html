Trigger — When to use

The integrand is a product of two factor types where one of them either self-reproduces under integration (like $e^{ax}$) or has a clean differentiation pattern (like $\sin^n x$, $\ln x$, $x^n$). A second (or third) integration by parts is needed and the new integral has the SAME structural form as the original — typically $\int e^{ax}\sin(bx)\,dx$, $\int e^{ax}\cos(bx)\,dx$, or a reduction-formula setup like $\int e^x \sin^n x\,dx$. The visual cue: after one IBP, you see a sibling integral of the same exp×trig (or exp×poly) family, and you must decide afresh which factor is $u$.

Motivation — Why this works

If you keep the SAME factor as $u$ on every pass (trig as $u$, exponential as $dv$, or log/poly as $u$, exponential as $dv$), the trig factor advances through its derivative cycle ($\sin\to\cos\to-\sin$) or the polynomial power steps down, while the exponential is reproduced unchanged. This makes the original integral $I$ re-emerge with a non-unity coefficient, giving a solvable linear equation in $I$. If you swap roles on the second pass, the second IBP exactly inverts the first — algebraically you get $0=0$ or $I=I$, a tautology with no progress. The principle is: consistency preserves the loop; inconsistency closes it trivially.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the integrand is exp×trig (or exp×poly, log×poly), and one IBP will not finish the job — the residual integral has the same structural shape.
  2. Rule out a u-substitution (no inner-derivative pattern) and rule out direct integration (no antiderivative of the product is known). IBP is the only tool.
  3. Apply LIATE on the FIRST pass: choose $u$ as the factor whose derivative is simpler/lower-degree (Log, Inverse trig, Algebraic, Trig) and $dv$ as the self-reproducing factor (Exponential).
  4. Anticipate that a second IBP is coming. Decide upfront: I will keep the SAME role assignment philosophy on every pass — the exponential always plays $dv$, the trig/poly/log always plays $u$.
  5. Rule out swapping on pass two by sanity-check: if I let $u = e^{ax}$ and $dv = \cos(bx)\,dx$ now, applying IBP would just undo pass one and return me to the starting integral. So consistency is forced.
  6. Commit: after the second IBP, the original integral $I$ reappears with a coefficient $\neq 1$. Move all $I$ terms to one side and solve algebraically.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. First IBP: set $u = $ (trig or log or poly), $dv = e^{ax}\,dx$, so $v = \tfrac{1}{a}e^{ax}$.
  2. Write $I = uv - \int v\,du$, producing a new integral of the same exp×trig (or exp×poly) shape.
  3. Second IBP on the new integral: KEEP the same convention — the (now-different) trig/poly factor is $u$, and $e^{ax}\,dx$ is again $dv$.
  4. Expand: the original $I$ reappears on the right-hand side with some coefficient $k$.
  5. Form the linear equation $I = (\text{boundary terms}) + kI$ and solve: $I = \dfrac{\text{boundary terms}}{1-k} + C$.

Worked Micro-Example

For $I = \int e^{2x}\sin x\,dx$: First IBP with $u=\sin x,\ dv=e^{2x}dx$ gives $I = \tfrac{1}{2}e^{2x}\sin x - \tfrac{1}{2}\int e^{2x}\cos x\,dx$. Second IBP — KEEP $u=\cos x,\ dv=e^{2x}dx$ — gives $\int e^{2x}\cos x\,dx = \tfrac{1}{2}e^{2x}\cos x + \tfrac{1}{2}I$. Substituting back: $I = \tfrac{1}{2}e^{2x}\sin x - \tfrac{1}{4}e^{2x}\cos x - \tfrac{1}{4}I$, so $\tfrac{5}{4}I = \tfrac{1}{4}e^{2x}(2\sin x - \cos x)$, giving $I = \tfrac{1}{5}e^{2x}(2\sin x - \cos x) + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Reduction formula like $I_n = \int e^x \sin^n x\,dx$
Same rule across BOTH IBPs in deriving the recurrence: keep $e^x$ as $dv$ (reproduces), trig power as $u$ (steps down). Switching on pass two collapses the recurrence.
Algebraic × logarithmic, e.g. $\int x^n \ln x\,dx$
LIATE picks $u=\ln x$, $dv=x^n dx$. Only one IBP is needed, but the same principle (log as $u$ because $du=1/x$ simplifies) applies if a second pass arises with $(\ln x)^2$.
Polynomial × exponential, e.g. $\int x^n e^{ax}\,dx$
Always assign $u = x^n$ (power drops by 1 each IBP) and $dv = e^{ax}dx$. Repeat $n$ times consistently until the polynomial vanishes.
$\int e^{ax}\cos(bx)\,dx$ with general constants
Same consistency rule; the final coefficient on $I$ becomes $-b^2/a^2$ (or similar), and you solve $I(1+b^2/a^2)=\ldots$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the second integral as a 'fresh' problem and re-applying LIATE blindly — leading to a role swap that undoes pass one.
  • Trying a u-substitution on the residual integral when its structure is genuinely IBP-only.
  • Believing the integral 'cannot be done' after the original $I$ reappears — failing to recognise the algebraic-equation trick.

Pitfalls

  • Swapping $u$ and $dv$ on the second pass — produces $0=0$ or $I=I$ and looks like progress until you simplify.
  • Sign errors when collecting the $I$ terms on one side — the IBP formula is $uv - \int v\,du$, and a missed minus flips the coefficient.
  • Forgetting the $+C$ once $I$ has been solved algebraically (the constant is not absorbed by the cancellation).
  • Mismanaging the $\tfrac{1}{a}$ from $v = \tfrac{1}{a}e^{ax}$ across two passes, giving the wrong final coefficient.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3 integration by parts (formula booklet: $\int u\,\dfrac{dv}{dx}\,dx = uv - \int v\,\dfrac{du}{dx}\,dx$). The 'solve for $I$' algebraic trick is standard at IAL. Reduction-formula derivations using this consistency rule appear in FP2/FP3; do not stray into complex-exponential shortcuts ($e^{(a+ib)x}$), which are out of scope.
