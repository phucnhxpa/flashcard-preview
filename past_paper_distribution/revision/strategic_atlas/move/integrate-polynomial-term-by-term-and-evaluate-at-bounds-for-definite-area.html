Integration

Integrate polynomial term-by-term and evaluate at bounds for definite area

Family: Polynomial Integration Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integrand is (or has been reduced to) a low-degree polynomial — typically a linear, quadratic, or cubic expression in x — and the bounds are explicit numbers, often integers or simple rationals. This is the default move once a 'difference of curves' or single-curve area has been written as a single polynomial integrand. No transcendental, rational, or composite factors are present, so no substitution, parts, or partial fractions are needed. Cue phrases include 'find the area', 'evaluate ∫…dx', or 'shaded region bounded by…'.

Motivation — Why this works

By the power rule ∫xⁿ dx = xⁿ⁺¹/(n+1) for n ≠ −1, every polynomial term has an elementary antiderivative, and the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus turns the definite integral into F(b) − F(a). Combining the curve and any subtracted line into a single polynomial before integrating halves the number of antiderivative and bound evaluations, reducing arithmetic risk. With integer bounds the cubic (or higher) antiderivative evaluates to clean rationals. This is the most efficient and least error-prone route whenever the integrand is already a polynomial.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand is a polynomial in x with no surds, fractions in x, or composite functions — direct integration is available.
  2. Rule out substitution, parts, or partial fractions: there is no inner function, no product needing differentiation, and no rational denominator.
  3. Commit to term-by-term power-rule integration because the bounds are explicit numbers, so a single F(b) − F(a) evaluation finishes the problem.
  4. If the area is a difference of curves, combine into one polynomial integrand first (top minus bottom) rather than integrating each separately and subtracting at the end.
  5. Evaluate the antiderivative carefully at the upper bound, then the lower bound, and subtract; check the sign matches the geometric expectation (area > 0).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Simplify the integrand into standard polynomial form a_nxⁿ + … + a_1x + a_0.
  2. Apply the power rule term-by-term: ∫xⁿ dx = xⁿ⁺¹/(n+1), keeping coefficients.
  3. Write the antiderivative F(x) inside square brackets with the bounds attached.
  4. Compute F(upper) by substituting the upper bound and simplifying to a single rational.
  5. Compute F(lower) similarly, then form F(upper) − F(lower).
  6. Check the sign: if negative when an area is expected, re-examine which curve is on top or whether the region crosses the x-axis.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_0^1 (x^2 - 4x + 3)\,dx$.\ Antiderivative: $\left[\tfrac{x^3}{3} - 2x^2 + 3x\right]_0^1$.\ At $x=1$: $\tfrac{1}{3} - 2 + 3 = \tfrac{4}{3}$. At $x=0$: $0$.\ Result: $\tfrac{4}{3} - 0 = \tfrac{4}{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Area between a curve y = f(x) and a line y = mx + c, both polynomials
Form the single integrand (f(x) − (mx+c)) (top minus bottom) before integrating, rather than computing two integrals and subtracting.
Region crosses the x-axis between bounds
Split the integral at the root(s); integrate each piece separately and take absolute values before summing for total area.
Bounds are roots of the integrand (e.g. area enclosed between curve and x-axis)
Find the roots first by factorising or solving f(x)=0, then use them as the limits of integration.
Integrand contains negative or fractional powers of x (e.g. x^{-2}, x^{1/2})
Still apply the power rule term-by-term, but watch n = −1 (would need ln) and ensure bounds avoid singularities.
Cubic or quartic integrand from a Further Pure context
Same procedure; the antiderivative is one degree higher, and arithmetic at the bounds becomes the main risk.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating a difference-of-curves area as needing two separate integrals when one combined polynomial integral suffices.
  • Reading a negative result as 'wrong' when the curve genuinely lies below the x-axis on that interval — the integral is correct, but |·| is needed for area.
  • Assuming substitution is required because the integrand has been written in factorised form, e.g. (x−1)(x−3); expanding first reveals it is a plain polynomial.
  • Confusing 'area between curves' with 'area under curve' and forgetting to subtract the lower function.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips evaluating cubics or quartics at integer bounds (e.g. miscomputing 3³/3 = 9).
  • Forgetting to subtract F(lower); if F(0)=0 students often omit it but should still write '− 0' to make the structure explicit.
  • Sign errors when distributing a leading minus sign across the antiderivative of −x² + 4x − 3.
  • Dropping the constant term's contribution (e.g. integrating 3 to give 3x but forgetting the 3x at the bound).
  • Using the bounds in the wrong order, producing the negative of the correct area.

A-level Scope Notes

Direct polynomial integration via the power rule is core C2/P2 material in Edexcel IAL Maths and reappears throughout pure modules; all techniques and notation here remain within syllabus.
