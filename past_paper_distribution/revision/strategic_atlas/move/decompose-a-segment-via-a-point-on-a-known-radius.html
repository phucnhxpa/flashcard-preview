Coordinate Geometry

Decompose a segment via a point on a known radius

Family: Segment Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A point D is stated to lie on a radius (or diameter) OC of length r, and a perimeter or path length requires the sub-segment CD. CD is not given directly, but OD can be obtained from a triangle whose other elements are already known. Cue phrases: 'D lies on OC', 'D is a point on the radius', or a diagram showing D between O and C. Fires whenever a known total length is split into a found part and an unknown part.

Motivation — Why this works

Because D lies between O and C on the same straight radius, the segment additivity OD + DC = OC holds, so DC = r − OD. This converts an awkward length DC (not adjacent to easily known angles) into a subtraction once OD is found from a triangle with sufficient known data. The Sine Rule or Cosine Rule in triangle AOD typically supplies OD, since after earlier parts of the question its angles and one side are usually accessible.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that the required perimeter contains CD, which has no direct formula from the given data.
  2. Rules out treating CD as an arc or as the full radius; recognises D is interior to OC.
  3. Commits to the decomposition CD = OC − OD because OC is the known radius r.
  4. Bridges to triangle AOD: identifies which angles/sides are already known from earlier parts and selects Sine or Cosine Rule accordingly.
  5. Decides which angle pairs with which side in the Sine Rule, computes OD, then subtracts from r to get CD.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify that D lies on radius OC so OD + DC = OC = r.
  2. Locate a triangle (here AOD) containing OD with enough known data.
  3. Find any missing angle using the angle sum of the triangle, e.g. ∠OAD = π − ∠AOD − ∠ADO.
  4. Apply Sine Rule: OD/sin(∠OAD) = AD/sin(∠AOD), solve for OD.
  5. Compute CD = r − OD.
  6. Substitute CD into the perimeter/path expression along with arc lengths and other known segments.

Worked Micro-Example

In a circle centre $O$ radius $6$, $D$ lies on $OC$ with $AD = 5$, $\angle AOD = 0.7$, $\angle ADO = 2.257$. Then $\angle OAD = \pi - 0.7 - 2.257 = 0.185$. By Sine Rule, $OD = \dfrac{5\sin 0.185}{\sin 0.7} \approx 1.426$. Hence $CD = 6 - 1.426 \approx 4.574$ cm.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Point on a diameter rather than a radius
Use the full diameter $2r$ as the total: $CD = 2r - OD$ with $O$ replaced by the appropriate endpoint.
Tangent from external point split by point of tangency
The external segment decomposes as (full length) − (internal piece), with the internal piece found via right-angled triangle and Pythagoras.
Chord crossed by a perpendicular from centre
Use the foot of perpendicular to split the chord into two equal halves; recover half-length via Pythagoras in the right triangle.
Point on a line segment in a vector/coordinate setup
Use position vector $\vec{OD} = \lambda \vec{OC}$ with $0<\lambda<1$; then $CD = (1-\lambda)|OC|$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating CD as equal to the full radius OC because D is 'on' OC.
  • Confusing CD with the arc from C to D rather than a straight segment.
  • Trying to compute CD directly from triangle ACD without realising OD is the natural bridge.

Pitfalls

  • Pairing the wrong angle with the wrong side in the Sine Rule (angle must be opposite the chosen side).
  • Rounding ∠OAD or OD prematurely, causing visible error in the final perimeter.
  • Forgetting to add DA and the arc length once CD is found, or double-counting OC.
  • Working in degrees when the question gives angles in radians (or vice versa).

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only segment additivity on a straight line and the Sine Rule with arc length $s = r\theta$ — all standard Edexcel IAL P2/P3 circular measure and trigonometry content.
