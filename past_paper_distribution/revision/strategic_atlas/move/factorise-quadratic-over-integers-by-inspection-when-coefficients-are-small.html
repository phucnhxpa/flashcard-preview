Factorise quadratic over integers by inspection when coefficients are small

Family: Factorisation Choice Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A quadratic ax² + bx + c = 0 has small integer coefficients with very few factor pairs for a and c (e.g. a ∈ {1,2,3}, |c| small). The discriminant b² − 4ac is a perfect square, signalling rational roots. The student wants exact roots quickly without invoking the quadratic formula or completing the square.

Motivation — Why this works

When a and c have only one or two factor pairs, the number of bracket combinations to test is tiny, so inspection is faster than the quadratic formula and avoids surd manipulation. Factorisation also delivers exact rational roots directly and exposes structure (e.g. for sketching or sign analysis). The underlying identity is (px + q)(rx + s) = prx² + (ps + qr)x + qs, so matching pr = a, qs = c, ps + qr = b suffices.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the coefficients are small integers and mentally computes the discriminant to check it is a perfect square.
  2. Rules out the quadratic formula (overkill for small coefficients) and completing the square (slower, introduces fractions when a ≠ 1).
  3. Commits to inspection because the factor pairs of a and c are minimal, so trial-and-error is bounded by 2–3 cases.
  4. Bridges to the first operation by writing the bracket skeleton (ax)(x) or similar, then deciding the signs from the sign of b and c.
  5. Tactically tests the most likely factor combination first (often the closest split of c that produces b) and verifies via the cross/middle term.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute discriminant b² − 4ac mentally; confirm it is a perfect square.
  2. List factor pairs of a (for the x-coefficients) and of c (for the constants).
  3. Determine the signs of the bracket constants from the signs of b and c.
  4. Write a candidate factorisation (px + q)(rx + s) and check ps + qr = b.
  5. If incorrect, swap or replace one factor pair and retest until the middle term matches.
  6. Set each factor to zero and solve for the two roots.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $2y^2 - 7y + 3 = 0$. Discriminant: $49 - 24 = 25$ ✓. Skeleton $(2y\ \Box)(y\ \Box)$ with both constants negative. Try $(2y - 1)(y - 3)$: middle term $-6y - y = -7y$ ✓. Hence $y = \tfrac{1}{2}$ or $y = 3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Monic quadratic x² + bx + c with integer roots
Find two integers multiplying to c and summing to b; brackets are (x + p)(x + q) with no leading-coefficient juggling.
Disguised quadratic in u = xⁿ or u = eˣ (e.g. e^{2x} − 5e^x + 6 = 0)
Substitute u, factorise the integer quadratic in u, then back-substitute to solve for x.
Quadratic in trig, e.g. 2sin²θ − 7sinθ + 3 = 0
Treat sinθ as the variable, factorise as (2sinθ − 1)(sinθ − 3); discard sinθ = 3 as out of range.
Difference of squares a²x² − c² (b = 0)
Use (ax − c)(ax + c) directly rather than searching factor pairs.
Negative leading coefficient
Factor out −1 first, or absorb the sign into one bracket, to reduce sign-error risk.

Common Misreadings

  • Jumping to the quadratic formula by default without checking whether the quadratic factorises easily — wastes time and increases arithmetic error risk.
  • Assuming a non-perfect-square discriminant means no factorisation is useful, when sometimes a quick factor over rationals still works.
  • Reading 2y² − 7y + 3 as needing the brackets (y − …)(y − …) and forgetting to split the leading 2.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when both bracket constants are negative: confusing (2y − 1)(y − 3) with (2y + 1)(y − 3).
  • Swapping the factor pair so the middle term has the right magnitude but wrong sign.
  • Forgetting to set BOTH factors to zero and reporting only one root.
  • Misallocating factors of a, e.g. trying (2y − 3)(y − 1) which gives −5y, not −7y.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard P1/Pure 1 content in Edexcel IAL; factorisation by inspection is the default first method for solving quadratics with small integer coefficients before introducing the formula or completing the square.
