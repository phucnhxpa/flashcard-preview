Factor expression to match a prescribed template form

Family: Reverse Engineer Form Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question states that an expression can be written in a specific form such as $A f(x)(p\,g(x) + q)$ and asks for the constants $p$ and $q$ (or similar parameters). A raw expression — often arising from differentiation, integration, or expansion — must be rearranged to expose this skeleton. Phrases like 'show that ... can be written in the form', 'hence find the values of $p$ and $q$', or 'where $a$, $b$ are constants to be found' are direct cues. The move applies whenever the target form is given before simplification is finished.

Motivation — Why this works

The prescribed template tells you exactly which common factor must be extracted, removing guesswork. Once the GCF matches the leading factor in the template, what remains inside the bracket can only be the $(p\cdot\text{stuff} + q)$ part, so $p$ and $q$ are read off directly. This is more reliable than free simplification followed by pattern-matching, because it forces you to align with the examiner's structure from the start. It also avoids algebraic detours that risk sign or coefficient slips.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question prescribes a target form, e.g. $8\tan x\sec^2 x(p\sec^2 x + q)$, so the answer's outer factor is fixed.
  2. Rule out free simplification or expanding everything out — that wastes time and risks losing the structure.
  3. Commit to treating the outer factor of the template as the GCF to extract from the raw expression.
  4. Bridge: divide each term of the raw expression by that outer factor and check the quotients are clean (integers or simple constants).
  5. Tactically, read $p$ from the coefficient of the variable part and $q$ from the constant inside the bracket; verify by mentally re-expanding.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Read the target form and identify its outer factor (the part outside the bracket).
  2. Confirm this outer factor divides every term of the raw expression — adjust constants if needed.
  3. Divide each term by the outer factor to obtain the contents of the bracket.
  4. Compare the bracket against $(p\cdot\text{variable part} + q)$ and read off $p$ and $q$.
  5. Sanity-check by expanding the factored form back to the original expression.

Worked Micro-Example

Raw: $\dfrac{d^3 y}{dx^3} = 24\sec^4 x\tan x - 8\sec^2 x\tan x$. Target: $8\tan x\sec^2 x(p\sec^2 x + q)$. Extract $8\sec^2 x\tan x$: $24\sec^4 x\tan x \div 8\sec^2 x\tan x = 3\sec^2 x$, and $-8\sec^2 x\tan x \div 8\sec^2 x\tan x = -1$. So the bracket is $(3\sec^2 x - 1)$, giving $p = 3$, $q = -1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

After differentiating a product, write $f'(x)$ in the form $(ax+b)e^{kx}$.
Extract $e^{kx}$ as the common factor; the remaining linear bracket reveals $a$ and $b$.
Integration result presented as $\ln|f(x)| + C$ where $f(x) = ax^2+bx+c$.
Factor inside the logarithm to match the prescribed quadratic, reading off $a,b,c$.
Partial fractions stated in form $\dfrac{A}{x-1} + \dfrac{B}{(x-1)^2}$.
Use cover-up or comparison after fixing the denominator structure dictated by the template.
Binomial expansion truncated to $a + bx + cx^2$.
Compute each term and read constants directly; do not over-simplify into a single fraction.
Trig identity rewrite into $R\sin(x+\alpha)$ form.
Outer factor is $R$; comparing coefficients of $\sin x$ and $\cos x$ pins down $R$ and $\alpha$.

Common Misreadings

  • Extracting only part of the outer factor (e.g. $\tan x\sec^2 x$ without the constant $8$) and then having to absorb the leftover constant into $p$ and $q$, leading to fractional answers.
  • Expanding the template and comparing coefficients term-by-term — valid but slower and more error-prone than direct factoring.
  • Mistaking $p$ and $q$ for being free unknowns to be solved from equations, rather than read off after factoring.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when extracting a common factor from a term with a leading minus.
  • Pulling out the wrong numerical GCF (e.g. $4$ instead of $8$), giving non-integer $p,q$ that conflict with the expected form.
  • Forgetting to factor a power fully (e.g. extracting $\sec x$ when $\sec^2 x$ was available).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard across Edexcel IAL Pure Maths — appears in differentiation (P3/P4), integration, binomial expansion, and partial fractions whenever a question says 'in the form ... where ... are constants to be found'.
