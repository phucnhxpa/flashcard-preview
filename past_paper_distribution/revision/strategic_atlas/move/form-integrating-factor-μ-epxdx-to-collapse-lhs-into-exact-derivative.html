Differential Equations

Form integrating factor μ = e^{∫P(x)dx} to collapse LHS into exact derivative

Family: Integrating Factor Method Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

The ODE is first-order linear and has been arranged into standard form dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x) (with coefficient 1 on dy/dx). The P(x)y term blocks direct integration and the equation is not separable, so a multiplier is needed to combine the LHS into a single derivative. Visual cues: a 'dy/dx + (something in x) · y' structure with a non-zero RHS, or any equation that becomes such after dividing through by the leading coefficient. Equivalently phrased: 'I have a linear ODE with a variable coefficient on y and need to integrate both sides.'

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying through by μ(x) = e^{∫P(x)dx} forces the LHS μ·dy/dx + μP(x)·y to equal d/dx[μy] exactly, by the product rule run in reverse. The exponential construction is the unique solution to μ' = P(x)μ, which is precisely the condition that makes the product rule collapse work. Once the LHS is an exact derivative, the equation reduces to d/dx[μy] = μQ(x), which is solvable by direct integration on both sides. This is the central machinery of the integrating-factor method and is listed in the Edexcel formula booklet.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the ODE is first-order linear in y with a variable coefficient P(x) multiplying y; separation of variables fails because Q(x) and P(x)y don't factor nicely.
  2. I rule out direct integration (the P(x)y term obstructs it) and separation (the +Q(x) term breaks it); this leaves the integrating-factor method as the natural tool.
  3. I commit to the IF approach because I want to rewrite the LHS as d/dx[μy]; the product rule tells me I need a μ satisfying μ' = P(x)μ, whose solution is μ = e^{∫P dx}.
  4. First concrete operation: ensure the ODE is in standard form (divide through if there is a coefficient on dy/dx), then read off P(x) as the coefficient of y.
  5. Tactical decision: when computing ∫P(x)dx I omit the constant of integration, since any constant just scales μ by e^C and cancels on both sides of the equation.
  6. Simplification decision: if ∫P dx produces k·ln|x|, I rewrite e^{k ln|x|} as x^k (taking the positive branch on the working domain) rather than leaving it in exponential form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rewrite the ODE in standard form dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x); if there is a coefficient a(x) on dy/dx, divide through so that P(x) = b(x)/a(x).
  2. Identify P(x) as the coefficient of y on the LHS (with its sign).
  3. Compute ∫P(x) dx without a constant of integration.
  4. Form μ(x) = e^{∫P(x) dx}.
  5. Simplify using log/exponent laws: e^{k ln x} = x^k, e^{ln f(x)} = f(x), e^{kx} stays as is.
  6. (Optional check) Verify μ'(x) = P(x)·μ(x).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\dfrac{dy}{dx} + \dfrac{2}{x}y = x^3$ on $x>0$: identify $P(x)=2/x$, compute $\int \tfrac{2}{x}\,dx = 2\ln x$, so $\mu(x) = e^{2\ln x} = x^2$. Multiplying gives $\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^2 y] = x^5$, ready to integrate.

Variants — Same move, different surface

P(x) is a constant k
μ = e^{kx} directly; common in growth/decay and modelling questions.
P(x) = k/x
μ simplifies to x^k after using e^{k ln x} = x^k; standard FP2/FP3 pattern.
P(x) = tan x
∫tan x dx = ln|sec x|, so μ = sec x.
ODE given with leading coefficient a(x) on dy/dx
Divide through by a(x) first so P(x) = b(x)/a(x); failing to do this gives the wrong μ.
Substitution problem (e.g. Bernoulli or z = y/x)
After substitution the equation becomes dz/dx + P(x)z = Q(x); apply the same IF formula to the new dependent variable z.

Common Misreadings

  • Using Q(x) instead of P(x) in the exponent of the integrating factor.
  • Forgetting to divide through by the leading coefficient, so P(x) is taken as b(x) instead of b(x)/a(x).
  • Including '+ C' in ∫P dx, producing an unnecessarily complicated μ.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors in P(x): for dy/dx − 2xy = Q, P(x) = −2x, giving μ = e^{−x²}, not e^{x²}.
  • Mis-simplifying e^{k ln x} as kx instead of x^k.
  • Failing to simplify e^{ln f(x)} back to f(x), leaving μ in unwieldy form.
  • Errors in integrating P(x) itself, e.g. ∫(2/x)dx written as 2x instead of 2 ln|x|.

A-level Scope Notes

This is the standard Edexcel IAL FP2 integrating factor method; the formula μ = e^{∫P dx} appears in the FP2 formula booklet. Stay with first-order linear ODEs in standard form; do not extend to non-linear ODEs (Bernoulli requires a substitution first) or to systems of ODEs, which are out of scope.
