Trigonometry

Apply cosine rule to find an angle from three known sides (SSS)

Family: Cosine Rule Angle Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a triangle has all three side lengths specified and an interior angle is required, with no angle information yet available (the classic SSS configuration). Visual cues: three numerical side labels on a triangle diagram, a question asking for 'angle ABC' or 'the angle at X', and the absence of any right-angle mark or given angle. Equivalent phrasings include 'find the size of angle …', 'show that cos θ = …', or 'calculate the angle in radians/degrees'. If even one angle were given, the sine rule would compete; here it cannot start the problem.

Motivation — Why this works

The cosine rule a² = b² + c² − 2bc cos A is the unique standard tool linking three sides to an angle in one step, since the sine rule requires a known angle–side pair to begin. Rearranging gives cos A = (b² + c² − a²)/(2bc), so the angle is recovered directly via arccos. This works because the cosine rule is essentially the Pythagorean theorem corrected by the projection term −2bc cos A, encoding how far the triangle deviates from a right angle. It also signals the angle's type automatically: a negative cosine flags an obtuse angle.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the data pattern: three sides are labelled, zero angles are given — this is SSS.
  2. Rule out the sine rule: it needs at least one (angle, opposite side) pair, which we do not have, so it cannot initiate the solution.
  3. Rule out right-angled trig (SOH-CAH-TOA): there is no right angle in the triangle.
  4. Commit to the cosine rule because it is the only triangle-solving tool that converts SSS data into an angle in one move.
  5. Identify which side sits opposite the target angle — that side must play the role of 'a' in a² = b² + c² − 2bc cos A. Mislabelling here finds the wrong angle.
  6. Decide the output mode: keep the calculator in radians if the question works in radians, otherwise degrees; do this BEFORE pressing arccos.
  7. Substitute, isolate cos A, then apply arccos; if cos A < 0, expect (and accept) an obtuse angle.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Label sides so that the side opposite the required angle is 'a'; the two enclosing sides are 'b' and 'c'.
  2. Write a² = b² + c² − 2bc cos A.
  3. Substitute the three numerical side lengths.
  4. Rearrange: cos A = (b² + c² − a²)/(2bc).
  5. Evaluate the right-hand side as an exact fraction or decimal.
  6. Set the calculator to the required mode (radians or degrees) and compute A = arccos(…).
  7. Sanity-check: if cos A < 0 the angle is obtuse (between π/2 and π); if cos A > 0 it is acute.

Worked Micro-Example

Triangle with sides $XY=4$, $XZ=6$, $YZ=9$; find angle $YXZ=\theta$. The side opposite $\theta$ is $YZ=9$, so $9^2 = 4^2 + 6^2 - 2(4)(6)\cos\theta$, giving $81 = 52 - 48\cos\theta$, hence $\cos\theta = -\tfrac{29}{48}$ and $\theta = \arccos(-29/48) \approx 2.22$ rad (obtuse, as expected from the negative cosine).

Variants — Same move, different surface

SAS configuration (two sides and the included angle known, third side required)
Use the same formula a² = b² + c² − 2bc cos A directly — no rearrangement, no arccos; just compute a.
At least one angle and its opposite side already known
Prefer the sine rule (lighter algebra); reserve the cosine rule for cases it cannot handle.
Question asks to 'show that cos θ = p/q' (exact form)
Stop after the rearrangement step — do not apply arccos; present cos θ as an exact fraction.
Answer required in radians
Switch the calculator to radian mode before evaluating arccos, or convert via × π/180 afterwards.

Common Misreadings

  • Placing the wrong side as 'a' (not opposite the target angle), which produces a different angle of the triangle.
  • Assuming the triangle is right-angled because two sides look perpendicular on the diagram and using SOH-CAH-TOA instead.
  • Trying the sine rule despite having no angle, by inventing one from the diagram.

Pitfalls

  • Algebraic sign slip when rearranging: writing cos A = (a² − b² − c²)/(−2bc) and dropping a minus sign.
  • Calculator left in degrees when the question is in radians (or vice versa), giving a numerically wrong but plausible-looking answer.
  • Discarding a negative value of cos A as an 'error' instead of recognising it as a legitimate obtuse angle.
  • Rounding cos A too early before applying arccos, magnifying error in the final angle.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (P2/P3) and Mechanics scope; the cosine rule a² = b² + c² − 2bc cos A is given in the formula booklet. Avoid generalising to spherical or non-Euclidean triangles, and do not invoke the dot-product derivation unless the question is explicitly vector-based.
