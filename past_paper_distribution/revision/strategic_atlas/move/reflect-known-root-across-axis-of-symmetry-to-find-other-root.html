Coordinate Geometry

Reflect known root across axis of symmetry to find other root

Family: Symmetry Of Parabola Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a quadratic's axis of symmetry (or turning point x-coordinate) is known together with exactly one root, and the other root is required. Typical phrasings: 'the curve has a minimum at x = h and passes through (r, 0), find the other x-intercept', or 'given f(r) = 0 and the vertex is at x = h, state k such that f(k) = 0'. Also fires when the quadratic is given in completed-square form $a(x-h)^2 + q$ with one root supplied. The cue is that no algebra is really needed — only the equal-distance property of a parabola.

Motivation — Why this works

A quadratic $y = A(x-r_1)(x-r_2)$ is symmetric about the vertical line $x = \tfrac{r_1+r_2}{2}$, which is exactly the axis through its turning point. Hence if $h$ is the axis of symmetry and $r_1$ is one root, the other root must satisfy $h - r_1 = r_2 - h$, giving $r_2 = 2h - r_1$. This bypasses solving the quadratic, factorising, or invoking calculus, and is exact regardless of the leading coefficient $A$.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question gives a root and a vertex/axis of symmetry, not two roots or full coefficients.
  2. Rule out forming and solving the quadratic from scratch — there isn't enough data without extra work, and it's unnecessary.
  3. Commit to the symmetry shortcut: every parabola has its two roots equidistant from the axis of symmetry.
  4. Translate this into the formula $r_2 = 2h - r_1$ where $h$ is the axis of symmetry.
  5. Sanity-check by computing the distance from the known root to $h$ and reflecting in the correct direction (the other side of $h$).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify $h$, the x-coordinate of the turning point / axis of symmetry.
  2. Identify the known root $r_1$.
  3. Compute the signed distance $d = h - r_1$.
  4. Apply $r_2 = h + d = 2h - r_1$.
  5. State the answer (and verify by checking $\tfrac{r_1+r_2}{2} = h$).

Worked Micro-Example

A quadratic has a minimum at $x = 7$ and one x-intercept at $x = 4$. Find the other x-intercept $k$. Distance from $4$ to the axis $x=7$ is $3$, so $k = 7 + 3 = 10$. Equivalently $k = 2(7) - 4 = 10$. Check: $\tfrac{4+10}{2} = 7$ ✓.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic given in standard form $ax^2 + bx + c$ with one root known
Use the axis $h = -\tfrac{b}{2a}$, then apply $r_2 = 2h - r_1$.
Quadratic given in completed-square form $a(x-h)^2 + q$
Read $h$ directly from the form; reflect the known root across $x = h$.
Two points on the parabola with equal $y$-values are given
Their x-coordinates are symmetric about $h$, so $h = \tfrac{x_1+x_2}{2}$; combine with a known root to get the other.
Vieta's route when the sum of roots is implied
Use $r_1 + r_2 = -\tfrac{b}{a} = 2h$ to recover $r_2$ — algebraically the same identity.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the turning point's x-coordinate as itself a root of the quadratic.
  • Assuming the y-coordinate of the vertex is needed — only the x-coordinate matters for locating roots.
  • Confusing 'axis of symmetry' with the y-axis when $h \neq 0$.

Pitfalls

  • Adding the distance to the known root instead of to the axis, e.g. $4 + 3 = 7$ instead of $7 + 3 = 10$.
  • Reflecting in the wrong direction when $r_1 > h$ (forgetting that signed distance handles this automatically via $2h - r_1$).
  • Forgetting that this only locates the other root — if the question wants the full quadratic, you still need a $y$-value to fix the leading coefficient $A$.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure content (P1 quadratics): axis of symmetry of $y = ax^2 + bx + c$ is $x = -b/(2a)$, and roots are symmetric about it. No tools beyond completing the square or Vieta-style sum of roots are required.
