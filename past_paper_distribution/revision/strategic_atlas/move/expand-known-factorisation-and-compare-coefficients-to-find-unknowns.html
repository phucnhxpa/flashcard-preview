Algebra & Functions

Expand known factorisation and compare coefficients to find unknowns

Family: Coefficient Comparison Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a polynomial $P(z)$ contains unknown coefficients (e.g. $a$, $b$) but a complete factorisation of $P(z)$ has already been obtained (typically from a previous part of the question, e.g. after using a given complex/real root). Cues include phrases like 'hence find the values of $a$ and $b$' immediately after a factorisation step. Also fires when one factor is given and the quadratic factor has just been deduced via root-pair conjugacy or polynomial division.

Motivation — Why this works

Two polynomials that are equal as expressions must have identical coefficients term by term (the identity theorem for polynomials). If the fully factored form is known, expanding it produces a unique standard-form polynomial whose coefficients can be read off directly against the target. This avoids reintroducing roots via substitution, which can require complex arithmetic or solving simultaneous equations.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that the factorisation is already complete and contains no unknowns, while the target polynomial still carries unknown coefficients $a$ and $b$.
  2. Rules out substituting the known root back into $P(z)=0$ and splitting real/imaginary parts — valid but heavier and error-prone with complex numbers.
  3. Commits to expand-and-compare because both sides are polynomials in the same variable and equality is identical, so coefficient matching is guaranteed to work.
  4. Bridges to the first operation: multiply the quadratic factor by the linear factor carefully, tracking signs.
  5. Inside the move, focuses only on the coefficients that carry unknowns (here the $z$-term and constant), but expands fully to self-check the $z^3$ and $z^2$ coefficients against the given values.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the factored form alongside the target polynomial in standard form.
  2. Expand the product term by term, distributing carefully.
  3. Collect like terms to obtain a single standard-form polynomial.
  4. Equate coefficients of each power of the variable with the target.
  5. Read off the unknowns; cross-check the leading and next coefficients agree as a sanity check.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $(z^2-4z+13)(2z-3) \equiv 2z^3-11z^2+az+b$. Expand: $2z^3-3z^2-8z^2+12z+26z-39 = 2z^3-11z^2+38z-39$. Comparing coefficients: $a=38$, $b=-39$. The $z^3$ and $z^2$ coefficients match ($2$ and $-11$), confirming the expansion.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cubic with given real linear factor and quadratic factor containing unknowns, e.g. $(z-2)(z^2+pz+q)\equiv z^3+\dots$
Expand and compare to find $p,q$; same mechanism, swap which factor holds the unknowns.
Partial fractions identity such as $\frac{1}{(x-1)(x+2)}\equiv \frac{A}{x-1}+\frac{B}{x+2}$
Multiply through, expand, and compare coefficients of powers of $x$ to find $A,B$ (alternative to cover-up).
Quartic factorising as a product of two quadratics with unknown coefficients
Expand the quadratic-times-quadratic product and match all four non-leading coefficients to obtain a system.
Polynomial division remainder problems where divisor and quotient are known up to constants
Use $P(x)=Q(x)D(x)+R(x)$, expand the right-hand side, and match coefficients to recover unknowns in $Q$ or $R$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the identity as an equation valid only at specific values of $z$ rather than for all $z$, leading to unnecessary substitution.
  • Attempting to substitute the complex root before using the factorisation already produced, doing twice the work.
  • Confusing 'comparing coefficients' with 'comparing values', and only checking one $z$-value.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when distributing the negative term in the linear factor (e.g. the $-3$ in $2z-3$).
  • Mis-collecting like terms across the $z^2$ or $z$ columns during expansion.
  • Reading off $b$ with the wrong sign when the target is written as $\dots+az+b$ versus $\dots+az-b$.
  • Forgetting to verify that the leading and second coefficients of the expansion match the given polynomial — a quick check that catches expansion errors.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard technique within Edexcel IAL Core/Further Pure: polynomial identities, factor theorem, complex roots in conjugate pairs (FP1/FP2), and partial fractions (P3/P4). No tools beyond expansion and coefficient matching are required.
