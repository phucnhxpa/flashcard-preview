Integration

Split sin^n x = sin^(n-1) x · sin x to set up IBP reduction

Family: Reduction Formula Ibp Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

You face an integral of the form $I_n = \int \sin^n x\, dx$ (typically $\int_0^{\pi/2}$) with a general integer power $n$, and the question asks you to derive a reduction formula linking $I_n$ to $I_{n-2}$. The cue is the phrase 'show that $I_n = \tfrac{n-1}{n} I_{n-2}$' or similar, combined with a generic power that cannot be integrated directly. The same trigger applies to $\int \cos^n x\, dx$ by symmetry. Recognising 'general power + need a recurrence' is the key.

Motivation — Why this works

A single application of integration by parts on $\sin^n x$ as a whole produces nothing useful, because there is no obvious $u, dv$ split. Rewriting $\sin^n x = \sin^{n-1} x \cdot \sin x$ creates an asymmetric product: $\sin^{n-1} x$ differentiates cleanly (lowering the power), and $\sin x$ integrates cleanly to $-\cos x$. Crucially, the resulting integral contains $\sin^{n-2} x \cos^2 x$, which is exactly the form needed to invoke the Pythagorean identity $\cos^2 x = 1 - \sin^2 x$ and produce the self-referential equation $I_n = (n-1)(I_{n-2} - I_n)$. This is the only split that makes both the IBP step and the subsequent identity work in tandem.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand has a generic power $\sin^n x$ — direct integration is impossible, so a recurrence relating $I_n$ to $I_{n-2}$ is required.
  2. Rule out $u$-substitution ($u = \sin x$ leaves a stray $\cos x$ that doesn't simplify the power) and rule out the symmetric split $\sin^{n-2}x \cdot \sin^2 x$ (because $\int \sin^2 x\, dx$ produces $x$ and $\sin 2x$, not a single clean term).
  3. Rule out $u = \sin^n x, dv = dx$ — this gives $v = x$ and a worse integral $\int x \sin^{n-1}x \cos x\, dx$.
  4. Commit to $u = \sin^{n-1} x$, $dv = \sin x\, dx$: differentiating $u$ drops the power and produces a $\cos x$ factor that pairs with $v = -\cos x$ to give $\cos^2 x$, ready for the Pythagorean identity.
  5. Anticipate the boundary term: $[-\sin^{n-1} x \cos x]_0^{\pi/2}$ vanishes at both ends (cos kills the upper bound, sin kills the lower for $n \geq 2$), so only the integral term survives.
  6. Plan ahead: after IBP, replace $\cos^2 x = 1 - \sin^2 x$ to express the new integral as $I_{n-2} - I_n$, then solve for $I_n$ algebraically.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rewrite $I_n = \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^{n-1} x \cdot \sin x\, dx$.
  2. Choose $u = \sin^{n-1} x$ and $dv = \sin x\, dx$.
  3. Differentiate and integrate: $du = (n-1)\sin^{n-2} x \cos x\, dx$, $\;v = -\cos x$.
  4. Apply IBP: $I_n = \bigl[-\sin^{n-1} x \cos x\bigr]_0^{\pi/2} + (n-1)\int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^{n-2} x \cos^2 x\, dx$.
  5. Evaluate boundary: at $x = \pi/2$, $\cos(\pi/2) = 0$; at $x = 0$, $\sin^{n-1}(0) = 0$ (for $n \geq 2$). Boundary term $= 0$.
  6. Apply $\cos^2 x = 1 - \sin^2 x$: $I_n = (n-1)(I_{n-2} - I_n)$, then solve to give $I_n = \tfrac{n-1}{n} I_{n-2}$.

Worked Micro-Example

For $I_4 = \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^4 x\, dx$: split as $\sin^3 x \cdot \sin x$, take $u = \sin^3 x$, $dv = \sin x\, dx$, so $du = 3\sin^2 x \cos x\, dx$, $v = -\cos x$. Then $I_4 = 0 + 3\int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^2 x \cos^2 x\, dx = 3(I_2 - I_4)$, giving $I_4 = \tfrac{3}{4} I_2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

For $I_n = \int_0^{\pi/2} \cos^n x\, dx$
Split as $\cos^{n-1} x \cdot \cos x$ with $u = \cos^{n-1} x$, $dv = \cos x\, dx$, $v = \sin x$. Mirror logic; boundary term still vanishes.
For $I_n = \int \tan^n x\, dx$
Split as $\tan^{n-2} x \cdot \tan^2 x$ and use $\tan^2 x = \sec^2 x - 1$ — same self-referencing recurrence idea but no IBP needed.
For $I_n = \int_0^1 x^n e^x\, dx$
Same 'differentiate the power, integrate the nice factor' principle: $u = x^n$, $dv = e^x\, dx$ gives $I_n = e - n I_{n-1}$.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying a $u$-substitution $u = \sin x$ on the whole integral — the leftover $\cos x$ doesn't simplify the power.
  • Choosing the symmetric split $\sin^{n-2} x \cdot \sin^2 x$ — $\int \sin^2 x\, dx$ doesn't give a single clean antiderivative.
  • Trying $u = \sin^n x$, $dv = dx$ — produces a worse integral involving $x$.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the $(n-1)$ coefficient from the chain rule when differentiating $\sin^{n-1} x$.
  • Sign error: $v = -\cos x$, and the IBP formula has its own minus, so the surviving integral term is $+(n-1)\int \sin^{n-2} x \cos^2 x\, dx$.
  • Forgetting that the boundary term vanishing requires $n \geq 2$; the formula does not apply to $I_1$.
  • Failing to apply $\cos^2 x = 1 - \sin^2 x$ at the end and so not closing the recurrence.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope (FP2/FP3 reduction formulae). The IBP formula $\int u\, dv = uv - \int v\, du$ is in the formula booklet. Avoid generalising to non-integer $n$ or to complex-analytic methods — those are out of scope.
