Sequences & Series

Substitute established closed forms to reduce a summation equation to algebra in n

Family: Result Substitution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A later part of a multi-part question presents an equation involving Σ-expressions whose closed forms are either proved in earlier parts or available from the formula book (e.g. Σr, Σr², Σr³, or a custom Σr(r+1)(r−1) just established). Phrasings include 'Hence find the value of n such that...', 'Using the result from part (b)...', or simply juxtaposing two summations with an '=' and a multiplier. The cue is that both sides are summations to n with known closed forms, so no further series technique is needed.

Motivation — Why this works

Edexcel IAL multi-part summation questions are deliberately scaffolded: an earlier part proves or quotes a closed form precisely so a later part can invoke it. Once every Σ is replaced by its polynomial-in-n form, the problem becomes a polynomial equation, which is fully within A-level algebra. Re-deriving the closed forms wastes time and risks arithmetic error; the 'Hence' wording signals that substitution, not derivation, is the intended pathway.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice both sides of the equation are Σ-expressions to n, and I have closed forms for each (one from part (b), one from the formula book).
  2. I rule out re-deriving by induction or telescoping — the 'Hence' or part-structure signals that work is already done.
  3. I commit to substitution because once every Σ becomes a polynomial in n, the problem is a routine equation-solve.
  4. First concrete operation: write out each closed form explicitly with brackets, including any external multiplier such as the '17' on the RHS.
  5. Tactically, I keep the common factor n(n+1) un-expanded — it will cancel and avoid expanding a quartic unnecessarily.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify which Σ on each side has a known closed form and where it comes from (earlier part vs formula book).
  2. Replace LHS Σ with its closed form, e.g. Σr(r+1)(r−1) = ¼n(n−1)(n+1)(n+2).
  3. Replace RHS Σ with its closed form including any multiplier, e.g. 17·Σr² = 17·⅙n(n+1)(2n+1).
  4. Write the resulting polynomial equation in n with brackets preserved.
  5. Cancel any common factor (often n or n(n+1)) after noting n ≠ 0, n ≠ −1 in context.
  6. Expand and rearrange to a standard polynomial equation; solve for n and reject non-positive-integer roots.

Worked Micro-Example

Given (from part (b)) Σ_{r=1}^{n} r(r+1)(r−1) = ¼n(n−1)(n+1)(n+2), solve Σr(r+1)(r−1) = 17·Σr². Substitute: ¼n(n−1)(n+1)(n+2) = 17·⅙n(n+1)(2n+1). Cancel n(n+1) (n ≥ 1 so nonzero): ¼(n−1)(n+2) = (17/6)(2n+1). Multiply by 12: 3(n−1)(n+2) = 34(2n+1), giving 3n² + 3n − 6 = 68n + 34, so 3n² − 65n − 40 = 0, hence n = (65 + √(4225+480))/6 = (65+√4705)/6... reject non-integer; example illustrates the algebraic reduction.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation mixes Σr³ (formula book) with a result like Σr(r+1) from earlier
Substitute ¼n²(n+1)² for Σr³ and the proved closed form for the other; cancel shared n(n+1) factor.
'Hence show that' rather than 'solve' — target is an identity in n
After substitution, expand both sides and verify equality rather than solving for n.
Σ from r=k to n (not from 1)
Use S_n − S_{k−1} for each closed form before substituting into the equation.
Method of differences result feeds the next part
Take the telescoped closed form from the earlier part and substitute it directly; no formula-book entry needed.
Inequality instead of equation, e.g. Σ... > 1000
Substitute closed forms, rearrange to polynomial inequality, solve and choose smallest integer n satisfying it.

Common Misreadings

  • Re-deriving Σr(r+1)(r−1) from scratch instead of quoting the part (b) result.
  • Trying to prove Σr² = ⅙n(n+1)(2n+1) by induction when it is a formula-book standard result.
  • Treating the equation as a series-manipulation problem and attempting telescoping or differences after the closed forms are already available.
  • Missing that the external multiplier (e.g. 17) attaches to the RHS closed form, not inside the Σ.

Pitfalls

  • Confusing Σr² = ⅙n(n+1)(2n+1) with Σr³ = ¼n²(n+1)².
  • Cancelling n or n(n+1) without noting it is nonzero, or cancelling a factor that is not actually common.
  • Arithmetic slips when clearing fractions ¼ and ⅙ — failing to multiply every term by the LCM 12.
  • Forgetting to reject non-positive or non-integer roots when n must be a positive integer.
  • Expanding the quartic prematurely instead of cancelling shared factors first.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only Edexcel IAL standard results Σr, Σr², Σr³ from the formula book together with results proved earlier in the same question (typically by induction or method of differences in FP1). Resulting equations are quadratic or cubic in n, solvable by factorising or the quadratic formula.
