Split the middle term via the ac-method to factorise a non-monic quadratic

Family: Strategic Factorisation Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when you face a quadratic ax² + bx + c with leading coefficient a ≠ 1 (e.g. 2x² − 7x + 3, 2x² + 3x − 35, 3x² + 5x − 2) that must be put into factorised form — typically as a step inside a cubic factorisation, an equation-solving question, or a 'show that … is a factor' problem. Visual cues: integer (or small rational) coefficients, a small/manageable value of |ac|, and a question that explicitly rewards factorised form (find roots, sketch, identify intercepts) rather than just numerical roots. Alternative phrasing of the same trigger: 'I need (px + q)(rx + s) form and inspection of factor pairs of a and c is awkward.' If |ac| has many factor pairs and none seem to sum to b after a quick scan, that itself is a counter-trigger pointing to the quadratic formula instead.

Motivation — Why this works

The ac-method exploits the identity ax² + bx + c = ax² + mx + nx + c whenever m + n = b and mn = ac, after which factor-by-grouping forces a common bracket and yields (px + q)(rx + s) directly. It works because, if ax² + bx + c factorises over the rationals as (px + q)(rx + s), then expanding shows the cross-terms ps·x and qr·x have ps + qr = b and (ps)(qr) = (pr)(qs) = ac — exactly the integers we are hunting for. This is more efficient than the quadratic formula when rational factors exist, because it yields the factorised form (which mark schemes reward) without converting roots back to factors. It also avoids unsystematic trial-and-error on factor pairs of a and c separately.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the quadratic is non-monic (a ≠ 1), so guessing (x + …)(x + …) is not enough; the leading coefficient must be split between the two brackets.
  2. Rule out the quadratic formula as the first weapon: it gives roots but you would still have to convert back to factor form, costing extra arithmetic; rule out blind trial of factor pairs of a and c, which is unsystematic.
  3. Commit to the ac-method: compute ac and b, and search for two integers with product ac and sum b — if such a pair exists, factorisation over the rationals is guaranteed.
  4. Bridge to operation: list factor pairs of |ac| mentally, pairing each with the correct sign pattern (same signs if ac > 0, opposite signs if ac < 0; the larger-magnitude factor takes the sign of b).
  5. Tactical choice: once the pair (m, n) is found, rewrite bx as mx + nx (either order works; pick the order that makes the first grouping have an obvious common factor).
  6. Group the four terms in pairs, factor each pair, and read off the common bracket — if the two brackets don't match, you've made a sign error in the split, not a conceptual error.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify a, b, c and compute the product P = ac.
  2. Find integers m, n with mn = P and m + n = b (signs: opposite if P < 0, same as b if P > 0).
  3. Rewrite the quadratic as ax² + mx + nx + c.
  4. Group: (ax² + mx) + (nx + c), and factor the HCF out of each pair.
  5. Both pairs now share a common linear bracket; factor it out to obtain (px + q)(rx + s).
  6. If the question asks for roots, set each bracket to zero and solve.

Worked Micro-Example

Factorise $2x^2 + 3x - 35$. Here $ac = 2\cdot(-35) = -70$ and $b = 3$. Seek $m,n$ with $mn=-70$, $m+n=3$: take $m=10, n=-7$. Rewrite: $2x^2 + 10x - 7x - 35 = 2x(x+5) - 7(x+5) = (2x-7)(x+5)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Monic quadratic (a = 1), e.g. x² − 5x + 6
ac reduces to c, so simply find two numbers with product c and sum b — the familiar 'sum-product' shortcut, no grouping needed.
Quadratic appears as the residual after polynomial division of a cubic by a known linear factor
Apply ac-method to the quotient quadratic to obtain the full three-bracket factorisation of the cubic.
ac is large or no integer pair sums to b after a quick scan
Abandon ac-method and use the quadratic formula; if the discriminant is a perfect square, convert roots r₁, r₂ back to a(x − r₁)(x − r₂).
Quadratic in a disguised variable, e.g. 2u² + 3u − 35 with u = x²
Factorise as a quadratic in u using ac-method, then substitute back and factor further if possible (e.g. difference of squares on (u − 5/2) once cleared).

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming the quadratic is irreducible just because a ≠ 1, and jumping straight to the quadratic formula without trying ac-method first.
  • Confusing the ac-method with the monic shortcut and searching for numbers that multiply to c (instead of ac) when a ≠ 1.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when ac < 0: forgetting that m and n must have opposite signs, or assigning the wrong sign to the larger-magnitude factor.
  • Choosing a split order that makes the first grouping awkward, then mis-factoring the second pair (especially forgetting to factor out −1 so the brackets match).
  • Dropping the leading coefficient: writing (x + q)(x + s) at the end instead of (px + q)(rx + s), forgetting that the a was distributed across the two brackets.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1 algebraic methods, factorising quadratics and cubics). No formula-booklet entry is needed; the quadratic formula is the only related booklet item and is the fallback, not the primary tool. Avoid generalising to factorisation over irrationals or complex numbers in a P1/P3 context — if the discriminant is not a perfect square, the ac-method will simply fail to find an integer pair, signalling a switch to the quadratic formula.
