Reject substitution roots that violate exponential positivity

Family: Domain Constraint Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires after substituting y = e^x (or y = a^x with a > 0) to reduce an equation in e^x and e^{2x} to a quadratic in y, and then solving that quadratic. If any root is zero or negative, it cannot correspond to a value of e^x for real x. The cue is a numerical root y ≤ 0 sitting alongside a positive root, often phrased as 'hence solve' or 'find the exact values of x'.

Motivation — Why this works

The exponential function e^x has range (0, ∞), so the equation e^x = k has a real solution only when k > 0. Roots of the auxiliary quadratic that violate this are extraneous artefacts of the substitution, not solutions of the original equation. Discarding them with explicit justification is required both for mathematical correctness and for examiner marks, since taking ln of a non-positive number is undefined in A-level scope.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the quadratic in y has produced two roots, and one of them is negative (or zero).
  2. I rule out the non-positive root because y was defined as e^x, and e^x is strictly positive for all real x.
  3. I commit to keeping only the positive root(s), recording the rejection in writing rather than silently dropping it.
  4. I bridge to the next step by setting e^x equal to the surviving positive root and preparing to take natural logs.
  5. Tactically, I phrase the rejection clearly — e.g. 'e^x > 0, so y = -1/2 has no real solution' — to secure the reasoning mark.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the substitution y = e^x and the constraint y > 0.
  2. List both quadratic roots.
  3. For each root, check the sign: if y ≤ 0, write an explicit rejection citing e^x > 0.
  4. For each positive root, set e^x = root and solve by taking ln.
  5. Report only the x-values arising from valid (positive) roots.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $e^{2x} - \tfrac{5}{2}e^x - \tfrac{3}{2} = 0$. Let $y = e^x$: $2y^2 - 5y - 3 = 0 \Rightarrow (2y+1)(y-3) = 0$, so $y = -\tfrac{1}{2}$ or $y = 3$. Since $e^x > 0$, reject $y = -\tfrac{1}{2}$. From $e^x = 3$, $x = \ln 3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation in $a^x$ with $a > 0, a \neq 1$ reduced to a quadratic in $y = a^x$
Same constraint $y > 0$; solve $a^x = y$ via $x = \log_a y$ or $x = \ln y / \ln a$.
Equation in $\sqrt{f(x)}$ substituted as $y = \sqrt{f(x)}$
Reject any negative root of the quadratic in $y$ since principal square roots are non-negative.
Hyperbolic substitution $y = \cosh x$ (Further Maths)
Reject roots with $y < 1$ since $\cosh x \geq 1$ for real $x$.
Trig substitution $y = \sin x$ or $y = \cos x$
Reject roots outside $[-1, 1]$ since sine and cosine are bounded.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating both quadratic roots as valid and attempting $\ln(-\tfrac{1}{2})$, then writing 'math error' or guessing.
  • Dropping the negative root silently without stating the reason, losing the justification mark.
  • Concluding the entire equation has no solution because one root failed, instead of only that branch.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the positivity check entirely under time pressure.
  • Writing $x = \ln(-\tfrac{1}{2})$ as if it were a valid expression.
  • Confusing the constraint $y > 0$ with $y \geq 0$ — note $e^x = 0$ also has no solution.
  • Applying the same rejection rule to substitutions where negatives are allowed (e.g. $y = \sinh x$ takes all real values).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard for Edexcel IAL P2/P3 exponential equations and reappears in Further Pure with hyperbolic substitutions; the explicit justification 'since $e^x > 0$' is expected in mark schemes.
