Sequences & Series

Factor S∞ out of the GP partial-sum formula to reuse a known value

Family: Structural Simplification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in multi-part GP questions where S∞ = a/(1−r) has already been computed in an earlier part and a later part asks for S_n with the same a and r. The cue is seeing S_n = a(1−r^n)/(1−r) and recognising that the prefactor a/(1−r) is exactly the previously found S∞. Also fires when a question asks 'how many terms are needed for S_n to reach a given fraction of S∞', since the ratio S_n/S∞ = 1−r^n drops out immediately.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity S_n = (a/(1−r))(1−r^n) = S∞·(1−r^n) holds whenever |r| < 1, because the partial-sum formula factors cleanly into the infinite-sum prefactor times the truncation factor (1−r^n). Reusing the known S∞ collapses two arithmetic operations into one multiplication, reducing the chance of sign or rounding errors. It also makes the structural meaning transparent: S_n is the fraction (1−r^n) of the total S∞.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice S_n = a(1−r^n)/(1−r) shares the prefactor a/(1−r) with S∞, which the previous part already pinned down.
  2. I rule out recomputing a/(1−r) from scratch — it would duplicate work and risk fresh arithmetic slips.
  3. I commit to the factorisation S_n = S∞·(1−r^n) because it preserves accuracy and exploits inter-part continuity.
  4. First concrete operation: substitute the numerical S∞ in front of (1−r^n).
  5. Tactically, I evaluate r^n only at the end, keeping the expression symbolic until the calculator step to avoid premature rounding.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rewrite S_n = a(1−r^n)/(1−r) as [a/(1−r)] × (1−r^n).
  2. Identify a/(1−r) as the previously computed S∞.
  3. Substitute the numerical value of S∞ to get S_n = S∞·(1−r^n).
  4. Evaluate r^n on the calculator, subtract from 1, then multiply by S∞.
  5. State the final answer to the required degree of accuracy.

Worked Micro-Example

Given a GP with $a = 3.5$, $r = 73/80$, part (b) gives $S_\infty = \frac{3.5}{1-73/80} = 40$. For $S_5$: $S_5 = S_\infty\bigl(1 - r^5\bigr) = 40\bigl(1 - (73/80)^5\bigr) \approx 40(1 - 0.6436) \approx 14.26$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Finding the smallest n with S_n > k·S∞ (e.g. 99% of the sum)
Use S_n/S∞ = 1−r^n, set 1−r^n > k, solve r^n < 1−k by taking logs (remembering to flip the inequality since log r < 0).
Comparing the difference S∞ − S_n
Write S∞ − S_n = S∞·r^n directly, bypassing the partial-sum formula entirely.
Earlier part gave a and r but not S∞ explicitly
Compute S∞ once, then still factor it out for any subsequent S_n evaluations to keep arithmetic centralised.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating S_n and S∞ as independent quantities and recomputing a/(1−r) from scratch each time.
  • Believing the factorisation requires |r| < 1 in the algebra (it doesn't — only S∞ existing does), and refusing to factor when r is negative or close to 1.

Pitfalls

  • Computing 3.5 ÷ (7/80) from scratch — correct but slower and more error-prone.
  • Mixing the two forms a(1−r^n)/(1−r) and a(r^n−1)/(r−1) and producing sign errors when r < 1.
  • Rounding S∞ before substituting, then propagating the rounding error into S_n.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL P2/Pure: geometric series, sum to infinity for |r|<1, and the standard partial-sum formula. No techniques beyond syllabus.
