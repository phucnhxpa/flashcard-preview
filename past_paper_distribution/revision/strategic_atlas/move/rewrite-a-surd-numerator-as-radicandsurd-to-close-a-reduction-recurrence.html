Integration

Rewrite a surd numerator as (radicand)/surd to close a reduction recurrence

Family: Rationalisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires during a reduction-formula derivation for $I_n = \int x^n/\sqrt{a-x^2}\,dx$ when, after integration by parts, you are left with an integral such as $\int x^{n-2}\sqrt{a-x^2}\,dx$ in which the surd has migrated to the numerator. The new integral does not match the $I_k$ template (surd in denominator), so you cannot yet write the recurrence. The cue is: 'I have $\sqrt{a-x^2}$ upstairs but my $I_k$'s have it downstairs.' Equivalent phrasings include 'after IBP I have a $\sqrt{\cdot}$ in the numerator I need to convert.'

Motivation — Why this works

The identity $\sqrt{a-x^2} = (a-x^2)/\sqrt{a-x^2}$ is just multiplying and dividing by the surd, but it strategically relocates the radical to the denominator, restoring the $I_k$ shape. Expanding the polynomial numerator then splits the integral into a linear combination of two $I_k$'s — typically $I_{n-2}$ and $I_n$. The reappearance of $I_n$ on the right-hand side is exactly what's needed: it produces a self-referential algebraic equation in $I_n$ which can be solved to express $I_n$ in terms of $I_{n-2}$, closing the recurrence.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that the post-IBP integral $\int x^{n-2}\sqrt{a-x^2}\,dx$ has the surd in the wrong place compared with the target $I_k = \int x^k/\sqrt{a-x^2}\,dx$.
  2. Rules out a substitution $u = a-x^2$ because it would absorb the $x^{n-2}$ factor into $du$ awkwardly and fail to produce $I_k$-shaped pieces.
  3. Commits to a purely algebraic rewrite: forcing $\sqrt{a-x^2}$ back into the denominator without changing the integrand's value.
  4. Bridges to the operation by writing $\sqrt{a-x^2} = (a-x^2)/\sqrt{a-x^2}$ and distributing $x^{n-2}$ across $a-x^2$ in the numerator.
  5. Tactically anticipates that $I_n$ will reappear on the RHS, plans to gather $I_n$ terms on the LHS, and solves for $I_n$ in terms of $I_{n-2}$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Replace $\sqrt{a-x^2}$ with $(a-x^2)/\sqrt{a-x^2}$ inside the integrand.
  2. Distribute: $x^{n-2}(a-x^2) = a\,x^{n-2} - x^n$.
  3. Split the integral: $\int x^{n-2}\sqrt{a-x^2}\,dx = a\,I_{n-2} - I_n$.
  4. Substitute this back into the post-IBP equation for $I_n$.
  5. Collect all $I_n$ terms on the left-hand side and solve algebraically for $I_n$ in terms of $I_{n-2}$.

Worked Micro-Example

Deriving $I_n = \int_0^1 x^n/\sqrt{10-x^2}\,dx$. After IBP, $I_n = -3 + (n-1)\int_0^1 x^{n-2}\sqrt{10-x^2}\,dx$. Rewrite $\sqrt{10-x^2} = (10-x^2)/\sqrt{10-x^2}$, so the remaining integral equals $10I_{n-2} - I_n$. Hence $I_n = -3 + (n-1)(10I_{n-2} - I_n)$, giving $nI_n = 10(n-1)I_{n-2} - 3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Reduction formula for $\int \sin^n x\,dx$
After IBP you reach $\int \sin^{n-2}x\cos^2 x\,dx$; replace $\cos^2 x = 1-\sin^2 x$ to split into $I_{n-2} - I_n$, then collect $I_n$.
$I_n = \int x^n\sqrt{1-x^2}\,dx$ (surd already in numerator)
Use $\sqrt{1-x^2} = (1-x^2)/\sqrt{1-x^2}$ to rewrite as $\int x^n/\sqrt{1-x^2}\,dx - \int x^{n+2}/\sqrt{1-x^2}\,dx$ and link two $I_k$'s.
Reduction for $\int \cos^n x\,dx$
Replace $\sin^2 x = 1 - \cos^2 x$ after IBP to produce the self-referential $I_n$ on the RHS.
$I_n = \int x^n/\sqrt{a^2+x^2}\,dx$
Use $\sqrt{a^2+x^2} = (a^2+x^2)/\sqrt{a^2+x^2}$ to split into $a^2 I_{n-2} + I_n$ (sign flipped because radicand has $+x^2$).

Common Misreadings

  • Trying $u = a-x^2$ at this stage, which mangles the $x^{n-2}$ factor and abandons the $I_k$ template.
  • Treating $\int x^{n-2}\sqrt{a-x^2}\,dx$ as a brand-new integral requiring its own IBP, instead of recognising it as a linear combination of existing $I_k$'s.
  • Believing the recurrence is broken because $I_n$ reappears on the RHS, rather than seeing this as the mechanism that closes it.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to move the self-referencing $I_n$ from the RHS to the LHS, leaving a circular formula.
  • Sign errors when distributing $(n-1)$ across $(a\,I_{n-2} - I_n)$, particularly losing the minus on $(n-1)I_n$.
  • Mis-pairing indices: writing $I_{n-1}$ instead of $I_{n-2}$ when the polynomial factor is $x^{n-2}$.
  • Dropping the constant term (e.g. the boundary contribution like $-3$) when collecting $I_n$ terms.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard technique in Edexcel IAL Further Pure reduction-formula questions for integrals involving $\sqrt{a\pm x^2}$ or trig powers; uses only IBP, polynomial expansion, and algebraic rearrangement.
