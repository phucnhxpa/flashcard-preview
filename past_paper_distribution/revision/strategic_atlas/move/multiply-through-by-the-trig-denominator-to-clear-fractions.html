Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation, after rewriting tan/cot/sec/cosec in terms of sin and cos, contains one or more trig fractions sharing a common denominator (typically sin θ or cos θ, or sin 2θ / cos 2θ for double-angle versions). Visual cues: a single dominant denominator like sin θ in one term plus polynomial trig terms elsewhere; or mixed tan θ and cot θ terms that, once expanded, share sin θ · cos θ as a common factor. Equivalently phrased: 'the equation is a trig-rational expression and you want a trig-polynomial expression'. Whenever you see fractions blocking factorisation or the use of sin²θ + cos²θ = 1, this move is the standard opening.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying every term by the common trig denominator removes the fractions and converts a trig-rational equation into a trig-polynomial one — i.e. an equation involving only products and powers of sin θ and cos θ. This is the prerequisite step for applying the Pythagorean identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1, for factoring, or for reducing to a single trig function. Without clearing denominators first, identities and factor methods cannot be applied cleanly. The principle is exactly the same as clearing fractions in algebra by multiplying by the LCD.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that after rewriting all trig functions in terms of sin and cos, the equation contains fractions with sin θ (or cos θ, or sin 2θ) in the denominator, blocking direct manipulation.
  2. Rule out: applying a Pythagorean identity directly — it doesn't help while fractions remain; squaring — would over-complicate; substituting u = sin θ — premature, fractions still present.
  3. Commit to multiplying through by the common denominator because this is the standard algebraic move to convert a rational equation into a polynomial one, and the resulting polynomial trig equation is exactly the form needed for sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 or factoring.
  4. Before multiplying, check the domain: is the denominator (e.g. sin θ) automatically nonzero in the original equation? If cot θ or cosec θ already appeared, then sin θ = 0 is excluded by definition, so no spurious roots are introduced. Otherwise, flag sin θ = 0 as a value to test against the original equation at the end.
  5. Multiply EVERY term — including any constant — by the chosen denominator, simplifying products like sin θ · (cos θ / sin θ) = cos θ as you go.
  6. Tactical choice: if two different denominators appear (e.g. sin 2θ and cos 2θ from tan 2θ and cot 2θ), multiply by whichever single factor clears the most fractions first, or by their product if needed.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the common trig denominator D (e.g. sin θ, cos θ, sin 2θ, or sin θ · cos θ).
  2. Check the domain: confirm D ≠ 0 is already implied by the original equation (e.g. cot θ present ⇒ sin θ ≠ 0); otherwise note that solutions with D = 0 must be checked separately.
  3. Multiply every term on both sides by D.
  4. Simplify each product: terms like (sin θ)(cos θ / sin θ) collapse to cos θ; (sin θ)(k sin θ) becomes k sin²θ; the constant term c becomes c·D.
  5. If a single Pythagorean substitution will reduce to one variable, apply sin²θ = 1 − cos²θ (or vice versa) now.
  6. Solve the resulting polynomial trig equation by factoring or the quadratic formula, then verify roots against the original domain.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $3\cot\theta - 2\sin\theta = 0$. Rewrite: $\dfrac{3\cos\theta}{\sin\theta} - 2\sin\theta = 0$. Multiply through by $\sin\theta$ (valid since $\cot\theta$ requires $\sin\theta\neq 0$): $3\cos\theta - 2\sin^2\theta = 0$. Substitute $\sin^2\theta = 1-\cos^2\theta$: $2\cos^2\theta + 3\cos\theta - 2 = 0$, giving $(2\cos\theta-1)(\cos\theta+2)=0$, so $\cos\theta = \tfrac12$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation contains tan 2θ and cot 2θ together (double-angle version)
Multiply by sin 2θ (and possibly cos 2θ) to clear both; same logic applies, just on the 2θ argument.
Equation contains sec θ and tan θ
Multiply through by cos θ instead — same difference-of-fractions logic, different denominator.
Equation contains cosec θ but no cot θ
Multiply by sin θ, but explicitly check whether sin θ = 0 gives a valid solution to the original equation, since cosec θ also excludes it but the form may differ.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating tan θ and cot θ as 'just functions' and trying to use a t = tan(θ/2) Weierstrass-type substitution — overkill for Edexcel-style problems.
  • Believing that sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 can be used directly while fractions are still present — it cannot, the fractions must be cleared first.
  • Misreading the move as 'divide by sin θ' (which loses solutions) instead of 'multiply by sin θ'.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to multiply the constant term (or RHS) by the denominator — a very common slip that produces a wrong equation.
  • Multiplying by sin θ when sin θ = 0 is actually a valid solution of the original equation — this loses roots. Always verify the domain restriction is already enforced by the original trig functions present.
  • Multiplying by sin θ when sin θ = 0 is NOT excluded — this introduces spurious roots; check candidate solutions back in the original equation.
  • Mixing up which substitution to make after clearing: choose sin²θ = 1 − cos²θ if the surviving linear term is in cos θ, and vice versa.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 trig chapters and the Further Pure trig identities. Uses only the booklet-listed identities sin²θ + cos²θ = 1, 1 + tan²θ = sec²θ, 1 + cot²θ = cosec²θ, and definitions of tan/cot/sec/cosec. Avoid introducing the half-angle (Weierstrass) substitution t = tan(θ/2) — that is out of scope and unnecessary here.
