Algebra & Functions

Rewrite symmetric functions of roots via elementary symmetric polynomials to use Vieta's outputs

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question asks for a value of a symmetric expression in the two roots α, β of a quadratic — typically α²+β², (α−β)², α³+β³, α⁴+β⁴, or 1/α+1/β — but only α+β and αβ are accessible via Vieta's formulas. Visual cues: powers, differences, or reciprocals of roots appearing alongside a quadratic whose coefficients are given (often with an irrational or complex discriminant that discourages solving for the roots individually). Equivalent phrasings: 'find α²+β² given a quadratic', 'evaluate this symmetric polynomial in the roots without solving the equation', 'compute the discriminant-like quantity (α−β)²'. The hallmark is that you cannot read the target directly off Vieta's, but the target IS symmetric.

Motivation — Why this works

By the fundamental theorem of symmetric polynomials, every symmetric polynomial in α, β can be written purely in terms of σ₁ = α+β and σ₂ = αβ — and these are exactly what Vieta gives you (−b/a and c/a). The standard bridges α²+β² = (α+β)²−2αβ, (α−β)² = (α+β)²−4αβ, and α³+β³ = (α+β)³−3αβ(α+β) come from expanding (α±β)ⁿ and rearranging. This converts an inaccessible quantity into an arithmetic computation, completely sidestepping the need to find α and β individually (which may be irrational or complex).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice: the target (e.g. α²+β² or (α−β)²) is symmetric in the roots but is not directly σ₁ or σ₂ — it's a higher-degree power sum or a squared difference.
  2. Rule out solving for α, β explicitly via the quadratic formula: the discriminant may be negative, irrational, or simply messy, making this route slow and error-prone.
  3. Rule out Newton's recursion pₙ = σ₁pₙ₋₁ − σ₂pₙ₋₂ for low n: it works but adds intermediate steps; for n ≤ 3 the direct identity is cleaner.
  4. Commit to the identity bridge: expand (α+β)² or (α+β)³ symbolically, then rearrange to isolate the target in terms of σ₁ and σ₂.
  5. Tactical choice: for (α−β)², go directly to (α+β)²−4αβ rather than routing through α²+β²−2αβ — fewer steps, fewer slip opportunities.
  6. Substitute the Vieta values for σ₁, σ₂ and simplify, taking care with fraction arithmetic and sign of σ₁ (which is −b/a, not b/a).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Read off σ₁ = α+β = −b/a and σ₂ = αβ = c/a from the quadratic ax²+bx+c = 0.
  2. Select the relevant identity: α²+β² = σ₁²−2σ₂; (α−β)² = σ₁²−4σ₂; α³+β³ = σ₁³−3σ₁σ₂; α⁴+β⁴ = (σ₁²−2σ₂)²−2σ₂²; 1/α+1/β = σ₁/σ₂.
  3. Substitute the numerical values of σ₁ and σ₂ into the identity.
  4. Simplify carefully — convert to a common denominator before combining fractions.
  5. Sanity-check the sign and magnitude (e.g. (α−β)² should be ≥ 0 when α, β are real).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $2x^2 - 8x + 3 = 0$ with roots $\alpha, \beta$: by Vieta's, $\alpha+\beta = 4$ and $\alpha\beta = 3/2$. Then $\alpha^2+\beta^2 = (\alpha+\beta)^2 - 2\alpha\beta = 16 - 3 = 13$, and $(\alpha-\beta)^2 = (\alpha+\beta)^2 - 4\alpha\beta = 16 - 6 = 10$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cubic power sum α³+β³
Use α³+β³ = (α+β)³ − 3αβ(α+β), or equivalently (α+β)((α+β)² − 3αβ) — same elementary-symmetric strategy, one level deeper.
Quartic power sum α⁴+β⁴
Chain the identity: α⁴+β⁴ = (α²+β²)² − 2(αβ)² = (σ₁² − 2σ₂)² − 2σ₂². Apply the degree-2 identity twice.
Reciprocal sum 1/α + 1/β or 1/α² + 1/β²
Combine fractions first: 1/α + 1/β = (α+β)/(αβ) = σ₁/σ₂; 1/α² + 1/β² = (α²+β²)/(αβ)² = (σ₁²−2σ₂)/σ₂².
Non-symmetric target like α³−β³
Factor as (α−β)(α²+αβ+β²); the second factor is symmetric and reduces to σ₁²−σ₂, but α−β is not symmetric, so you need (α−β)² = σ₁²−4σ₂ and then take ±√, with sign fixed by problem context.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating α²+β² as (α+β)² and forgetting the −2αβ correction.
  • Confusing (α−β)² with α²−β²: the latter is (α−β)(α+β), is not symmetric, and requires knowing which root is larger.
  • Confusing α³+β³ with (α+β)³, which overshoots by 3αβ(α+β).

Pitfalls

  • Using the wrong coefficient: −2αβ for α²+β² versus −4αβ for (α−β)² — easy to swap under time pressure.
  • Sign error on σ₁: forgetting that α+β = −b/a (with the minus sign) when b is negative.
  • Fraction arithmetic slips when σ₁ or σ₂ is rational, e.g. computing 2 × (3/2) = 3 (not 6), or failing to find a common denominator when combining (α+β)² with αβ.
  • Combining the middle term of α²−αβ+β² as (σ₁²−2σ₂)−σ₂ = σ₁²−3σ₂ but writing −2σ₂ instead.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP1) on roots of polynomial equations. The relations α+β = −b/a, αβ = c/a appear in the formula booklet; the symmetric-function identities (α²+β², (α−β)², α³+β³) are expected to be derived or memorised. Avoid invoking the general fundamental theorem of symmetric polynomials or Newton's identities by name in written solutions — derive the specific identity used.
