Sequences & Series

Iterate recurrence directly to reach a small-index term

Family: Direct Iteration Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a 'show that' question asks for a specific term $u_N$ with $N$ small (typically $N \le 6$) from a recurrence relation. Especially compelling when the recurrence contains an awkward feature like $(-1)^n$, a non-constant coefficient, or a parameter $k$ that blocks a clean closed form. Cues include phrasings such as 'given $u_1 = \ldots$, show that $u_5 = \ldots$' or 'find $u_4$ in terms of $k$'. The 'show that' wording itself signals that every intermediate term must be displayed.

Motivation — Why this works

For a small number of steps, repeatedly substituting into the recurrence is both faster and more transparent than deriving a general formula. It produces a visible chain of justified equalities, which is exactly what 'show that' rewards. Carrying any parameter (like $k$) symbolically through the iteration also yields expressions reusable in later parts of the question. Closed forms are unnecessary — and often impossible at A-level — when sign-alternating or variable-coefficient terms are present.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the recurrence contains an alternating or non-standard factor (e.g. $(-1)^n$) so no familiar closed form (AP/GP) applies.
  2. Rule out solving the recurrence in general — the target index is small, so the cost of a closed form outweighs the benefit.
  3. Commit to direct iteration: count how many substitutions are needed (here $n=1,2,3,4$ to reach $u_5$).
  4. Decide to keep the parameter $k$ symbolic at every stage so each $u_n$ is an explicit expression in $k$.
  5. Track the sign of $(-1)^n$ carefully step-by-step and bracket expressions before distributing to avoid sign slips.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the starting value $u_1$ and the recurrence form $u_{n+1} = f(n, u_n)$.
  2. Substitute $n=1$ to get $u_2$, simplifying with brackets around any composite expression.
  3. Substitute $n=2$ into the recurrence using the just-computed $u_2$ to get $u_3$.
  4. Continue substituting successive values of $n$ until the required $u_N$ is reached.
  5. Present each line as an equality chain so the 'show that' demand is met.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $u_1 = -7$ and $u_{n+1} = (-1)^n u_n + k$:\n$u_2 = (-1)^1(-7) + k = 7 + k$ — wait, recompute: $u_2 = -u_1 + k = -(-7)+k = 7+k$.\n$u_3 = (-1)^2 u_2 + k = (7+k)+k = 7+2k$.\n$u_4 = (-1)^3 u_3 + k = -(7+2k)+k = -7-k$.\n$u_5 = (-1)^4 u_4 + k = (-7-k)+k = -7$. (Sign of $u_1$ chosen here for illustration; the principle is the iteration itself.)

Variants — Same move, different surface

Recurrence $u_{n+1} = a u_n + b$ with numerical $a,b$ and small target index
Iterate arithmetically; no symbolic parameter to carry, but still write each line.
Recurrence with a parameter, e.g. $u_{n+1} = u_n^2 - p$, asked to express $u_4$ in terms of $p$
Keep $p$ symbolic; expand and simplify polynomials at each stage.
Periodic recurrence where the question asks for $u_{100}$
Iterate only until the period is detected, then use $n \bmod \text{period}$ rather than iterating 100 times.
Two-term recurrence $u_{n+2} = f(u_{n+1}, u_n)$ with both $u_1, u_2$ given
Same direct iteration, but each step uses the two most recent terms.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading $(-1)^n u_n$ as $(-1)^{n} \cdot n$ or attaching the sign to the wrong term.
  • Treating the recurrence as geometric because of the $(-1)^n$ factor and trying to write a closed form.
  • Misaligning the index: using $(-1)^{n+1}$ when computing $u_{n+1}$ instead of $(-1)^n$.

Pitfalls

  • Substituting the wrong power of $-1$ at each stage (off-by-one in the index).
  • Forgetting brackets when $(-1)^n = -1$ multiplies a sum, leading to sign errors on the second term.
  • Evaluating numerically too early and losing the symbolic $k$ that later parts need.
  • Stopping one step short or one step too far — miscounting how many substitutions reach $u_N$.

A-level Scope Notes

Direct iteration of recurrences is standard in Edexcel IAL P1/P2 sequences and series; no closed-form solution of recurrences is required at this level.
