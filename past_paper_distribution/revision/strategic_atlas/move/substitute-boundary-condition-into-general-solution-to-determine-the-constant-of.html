Differential Equations

Substitute boundary condition into general solution to determine the constant of integration

Family: Initial Condition Substitution Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

A first-order ODE has been solved (by separation, integrating factor, or substitution) yielding a general solution containing an arbitrary constant C — typically in the form y = f(x, C), μ(x)·y = F(x) + C, or an implicit form like G(y) = H(x) + C. The question then states a boundary/initial condition such as 'y = y₀ when x = x₀', or 'y(x₀) = y₀'. Often the chosen point is strategically clean (x = 0 collapses exponentials via e⁰ = 1; y = 1 collapses logarithms via ln 1 = 0; standard angles like x = π/6 give exact trig values), signalling that direct substitution will produce a tidy equation in C.

Motivation — Why this works

The general solution describes an entire family of solution curves indexed by C; the boundary condition selects the unique curve passing through the specified point. Substituting (x₀, y₀) yields a single algebraic equation in C, which can be solved and then back-substituted to give the particular solution the question demands. The examiner usually picks x₀ or y₀ to exploit a vanishing identity (ln 1 = 0, e⁰ = 1, sin 0 = 0), so the substitution should be done in the form where this simplification is visible — not after further algebraic rearrangement that hides it.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question gives a single point (x₀, y₀) and the general solution contains exactly one arbitrary constant C — so one equation in one unknown is sufficient.
  2. Rule out substituting into the original ODE (this gives a relation between dy/dx, x, y but does not determine C) and rule out exponentiating/rearranging the general solution before substitution (this often destroys the clean simplification the examiner engineered).
  3. Commit to substituting x = x₀ and y = y₀ directly into the general solution in its current form, especially if the chosen values make a logarithm, exponential, or trig term collapse.
  4. Evaluate each side carefully: compute μ(x₀), F(x₀), or any composite functions at x₀, and the LHS at y₀, watching for traps like cos(2·π/6) = cos(π/3) (not cos(π/6)).
  5. Solve the resulting linear equation for C, substitute C back into the general solution, and simplify (often by multiplying through to clear fractions, or rearranging to make y the subject) to present the particular solution cleanly.
  6. Optionally verify by checking the particular solution satisfies both the ODE and the boundary condition.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the general solution explicitly, e.g. G(y) = H(x) + C or μ(x)·y = F(x) + C.
  2. Substitute x = x₀ and y = y₀ into both sides.
  3. Evaluate special values: e⁰ = 1, ln 1 = 0, cos(π/3) = 1/2, etc.
  4. Solve the resulting equation for C.
  5. Replace C in the general solution with the determined numerical value.
  6. Simplify (clear fractions, rearrange for y if required) to give the particular solution in its cleanest form.

Worked Micro-Example

General solution: $\tfrac{1}{2}\ln(1+2\ln y) = -\tfrac{1}{6}\cos(2x) + C$. Boundary: $y=1$ at $x=\pi/6$. LHS: $\tfrac{1}{2}\ln(1+0) = 0$. RHS: $-\tfrac{1}{6}\cos(\pi/3) + C = -\tfrac{1}{12} + C$. So $0 = -\tfrac{1}{12} + C \Rightarrow C = \tfrac{1}{12}$, giving the particular solution $\tfrac{1}{2}\ln(1+2\ln y) = -\tfrac{1}{6}\cos(2x) + \tfrac{1}{12}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Condition given at x = 0 with an exponential factor in the solution
e⁰ = 1 collapses the exponential, leaving polynomial/constant terms — substitute before any further simplification.
Condition y = 1 with logarithmic terms in the solution
ln 1 = 0 zeros out the entire logarithmic side; substitute in log form, NOT after exponentiating.
Implicit condition (e.g. 'y is finite/bounded as x → 0' or 'y → 0 as x → ∞')
Choose C to eliminate the singular or unbounded term, rather than by direct numerical substitution.
General solution in implicit form G(y) = H(x) + C
Substitute (x₀, y₀) directly into the implicit form; do not first attempt to make y the subject.

Common Misreadings

  • Substituting the boundary condition into the original ODE rather than the general solution — this gives a relation but does not determine C.
  • Confusing y(x₀) = y₀ (a point condition) with dy/dx(x₀) = y₀ (a gradient condition).
  • Treating two conditions for a first-order ODE as both fixing C, when one of them is actually a check or used elsewhere.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips when evaluating μ(x₀), F(x₀), or composite trig like cos(2·π/6) = cos(π/3) = 1/2 (not cos(π/6) = √3/2).
  • Rearranging to exponential/explicit form before substituting, losing the clean ln 1 = 0 or e⁰ = 1 simplification.
  • Forgetting to substitute the value of C back into the general solution — leaving the answer with C still present forfeits the boundary-condition marks.
  • Forgetting to divide by μ(x₀) when the general solution has the form μ(x)·y = F(x) + C.
  • Failing to clear fractions or simplify at the end, losing presentation marks.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4/FP2 first-order ODE techniques (separation of variables and integrating factor). No formula-booklet entry is required for the substitution itself. Avoid generalising to second-order ODEs with two boundary conditions or to systems — those belong to FP-level material and use the same logic but applied repeatedly.
