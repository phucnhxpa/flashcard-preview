Rationalise a surd denominator to deliver the exact form expected by the mark scheme

Family: Rationalisation Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a final or intermediate exact answer contains an irrational denominator — typical surface forms are 1/√n, k/√n, 1/(a+√b), or coefficients like 1/√3 sitting in front of a function. Visual cues: a single surd √n in the denominator, or a binomial surd a±√b in the denominator, with the question demanding an 'exact value', 'exact form' or 'simplified surd form'. Edexcel mark schemes routinely award the final accuracy mark only for the rationalised presentation (e.g. √10/50, ±√5/5, √3/3), so any answer left as 1/(5√10) or 1/√5 is at risk.

Motivation — Why this works

Rationalising eliminates the surd from the denominator by exploiting either √n·√n = n (single surd) or the difference-of-squares identity (a−√b)(a+√b) = a²−b (binomial surd). The resulting fraction is mathematically equivalent (we multiply by √n/√n = 1) but is the conventional 'simplified' representation at A-level. This makes answers easier to compare with mark-scheme forms, removes ambiguity in subsequent algebraic manipulation, and prevents loss of the final mark for an unsimplified surd.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the answer carries an irrational denominator (e.g. 1/√5, 1/(5√10), 1/√3) — structurally the surd is in the wrong place.
  2. Rule out further algebraic simplification: the fraction is already in lowest terms numerically, so no cancellation, factoring, or trig identity will remove the surd; only multiplying by a cleverly chosen 1 will.
  3. Rule out leaving it as-is: at Edexcel IAL the mark scheme typically requires a rational denominator for the final accuracy mark, so this is not optional cosmetics.
  4. Commit to multiplying numerator and denominator by the surd factor (or its conjugate, for binomial denominators), recognising √n·√n = n is the engine that rationalises.
  5. Choose the multiplier carefully: for 1/√n use √n/√n; for 1/(a+√b) use (a−√b)/(a−√b); for 1/(√a−√b) use (√a+√b)/(√a+√b).
  6. After multiplying, simplify the resulting fraction (cancel common integer factors) so the answer matches the standard form, e.g. 2√10/100 must be reduced to √10/50.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the irrational denominator, e.g. 1/(5√10).
  2. Multiply numerator and denominator by the surd: (1/(5√10)) × (√10/√10).
  3. Simplify the denominator using √n·√n = n: 5√10·√10 = 5·10 = 50.
  4. Simplify the numerator: 1·√10 = √10.
  5. Combine: √10/50.
  6. Reduce the fraction to lowest terms if any common integer factor remains, and apply ± if the original answer carried both signs.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\cos\theta = \dfrac{1}{5\sqrt{10}}$, multiply top and bottom by $\sqrt{10}$: $\dfrac{1}{5\sqrt{10}} \cdot \dfrac{\sqrt{10}}{\sqrt{10}} = \dfrac{\sqrt{10}}{5\cdot 10} = \dfrac{\sqrt{10}}{50}$. This is the rationalised exact form.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Single surd denominator 1/√n or k/√n
Multiply numerator and denominator by √n; use √n·√n = n.
Binomial surd denominator 1/(a+√b) or 1/(√a−√b)
Multiply by the conjugate (a−√b) or (√a+√b); use difference of squares (a+√b)(a−√b) = a²−b.
Coefficient of a function, e.g. y = (1/√3)x^{-3/2}
Rationalise the coefficient first, giving y = (√3/3)x^{-3/2}; the rest of the expression is untouched.
Answer carries ± (e.g. x = ±1/√5)
Rationalise the magnitude and reattach the ±: x = ±√5/5.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating rationalisation as optional cosmetic tidying — at A-level it is typically required for the final accuracy mark.
  • Mistaking 1/√n for an already-simplified form because the numerator is 1.

Pitfalls

  • Multiplying only the denominator by √n and forgetting to multiply the numerator, which breaks equality.
  • Forgetting to simplify the resulting fraction (e.g. leaving 2√10/100 instead of √10/50).
  • Rationalising only the positive root and dropping the ± when both signs were present.
  • Erroneously collapsing √n/n to 1 by writing √(n/n), confusing the surd with the fraction.

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL Pure (P1–P4) surd manipulation; no formula booklet entry is invoked — the identities √n·√n = n and (a+√b)(a−√b) = a²−b are assumed knowledge. Avoid generalising to nested radicals or higher-order roots beyond cube roots, which sit outside the IAL syllabus.
