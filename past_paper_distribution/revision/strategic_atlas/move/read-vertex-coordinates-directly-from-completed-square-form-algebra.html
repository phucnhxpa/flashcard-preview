Read vertex coordinates directly from completed-square form

Family: Vertex Form Interpretation Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a quadratic has already been written (or just been written in a previous part) in completed-square form $y = a(x \pm b)^2 + c$, and the question asks for the minimum point, maximum point, vertex, turning point, or range of the function. Watch for the verb 'deduce' or 'hence' immediately after a 'complete the square' part — this is the strongest signal that no calculus is intended. Also fires when only the minimum/maximum value (not the full point) is requested, or when the question asks for the line of symmetry $x = -b$.

Motivation — Why this works

Because $(x \pm b)^2 \geq 0$ for all real $x$ with equality precisely when the bracket is zero, the term $a(x \pm b)^2$ contributes its extreme value (zero) at exactly one $x$. The sign of $a$ then determines whether this extreme is a minimum (if $a > 0$) or a maximum (if $a < 0$), and the constant $c$ is the corresponding $y$-value. This converts an optimisation question into a single sign check plus reading two numbers off the page — strictly faster and more reliable than differentiating, and it is the method the examiner is signalling with the word 'deduce'.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the quadratic is already (or has just been written) in the form $a(x \pm b)^2 + c$, and the question asks for a turning point, minimum/maximum value, or range.
  2. Rule out differentiation ($\tfrac{dy}{dx}=0$) and the symmetry-axis formula $x = -B/(2A)$: they would produce the same answer but cost extra work, and 'deduce'/'hence' indicates the examiner wants the algebraic reading.
  3. Commit to reading the vertex off directly, because $(x \pm b)^2 \geq 0$ pins the extreme down at one value of $x$.
  4. Bridge to action: check the sign of $a$ — if $a > 0$ the vertex is a minimum, if $a < 0$ it is a maximum. State this explicitly so the answer is justified, not just quoted.
  5. Set the squared bracket to zero to get the $x$-coordinate; be careful with the sign flip ($(x+b)^2=0 \Rightarrow x=-b$, while $(x-b)^2=0 \Rightarrow x=+b$).
  6. Substitute back (or just read off $c$) for the $y$-coordinate, and state the vertex as an ordered pair.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify $a$, $b$, $c$ in $y = a(x \pm b)^2 + c$ and check the sign of $a$ to decide minimum vs maximum.
  2. Set the squared bracket equal to zero: $(x \pm b) = 0 \Rightarrow x = \mp b$.
  3. Read $y = c$ directly (or substitute the $x$-value to verify).
  4. State the turning point as $(\mp b,\; c)$, declaring it as a minimum (if $a>0$) or maximum (if $a<0$).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $y = 3(x+1)^2 + 6$: since $a=3>0$, a minimum occurs where $(x+1)^2=0$, i.e. $x=-1$. Then $y = 3(0)+6 = 6$, so the minimum point is $(-1,\,6)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Coefficient $a < 0$, e.g. $y = -2(x-4)^2 + 5$
Same reading-off, but the vertex is a MAXIMUM. Here max point is $(4,\,5)$.
Only the minimum/maximum VALUE is asked for (not the point)
Quote $c$ alone (with sign-of-$a$ justification); no need to state the $x$-coordinate.
Trigonometric quadratic, e.g. $2(\sin\theta - 1)^2 - 4$
Same logic gives minimum when $\sin\theta = 1$, but check the achievable range $\sin\theta \in [-1,1]$ before declaring the extreme attainable.
Two-variable form $a(x+b)^2 + c(y+d)^2 + e$ (circles/ellipses in P2/P3)
The same reasoning identifies the CENTRE $(-b,-d)$, not a turning point.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'deduce' as 'differentiate' and wasting time finding $\tfrac{dy}{dx}=0$.
  • Confusing the vertex with the $y$-intercept (substituting $x=0$ instead of solving the bracket=0).

Pitfalls

  • Sign error: writing the vertex of $a(x+b)^2+c$ as $(b,c)$ instead of $(-b,c)$ — the bracket zero gives $x=-b$.
  • Declaring 'minimum' without checking that $a>0$ (or vice versa for maximum).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1 (completing the square, quadratic functions). No formula booklet entry is needed — the move is purely algebraic. Avoid invoking calculus justifications (second derivative test) at this stage; the inequality $(x \pm b)^2 \geq 0$ is the intended rigour.
