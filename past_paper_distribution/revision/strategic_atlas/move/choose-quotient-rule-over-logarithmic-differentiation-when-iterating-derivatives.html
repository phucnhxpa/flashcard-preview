Differentiation

Choose quotient rule over logarithmic differentiation when iterating derivatives

Family: Implicit Differentiation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when y is given as an explicit quotient (often with a surd or logarithm in the numerator) and the question asks for both dy/dx and d²y/dx². Cues include phrasings like 'find the first and second derivatives', or a ratio f(x)/g(x) where logarithmic differentiation would tempt you because of products of logs/roots. Also fires whenever keeping dy/dx as a single explicit fraction matters for later substitution (e.g. stationary points, second-derivative test).

Motivation — Why this works

The quotient rule outputs dy/dx as an explicit fraction p(x)/q(x), which can be fed straight back into the quotient rule a second time. Logarithmic differentiation, by contrast, gives (1/y)(dy/dx) = …, so dy/dx emerges implicitly multiplied by y; differentiating again requires the product rule plus back-substitution of y, doubling the bookkeeping. When the surface is already explicit (no implicit defining equation), the structural advantage of ln-differentiation — turning products into sums — is outweighed by the cost at the second-derivative stage.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice y is an explicit ratio with a composite surd numerator and the question demands a second derivative.
  2. Rule out logarithmic differentiation: it would yield dy/dx = y · (…), forcing back-substitution before a messy second differentiation.
  3. Commit to quotient rule because it keeps the answer as a single fraction p/q ready for iteration.
  4. Identify u = √(4+ln x) and v = x, and plan to use chain rule on u.
  5. Decide to leave the first derivative in unsimplified quotient form (or in a tidy single-fraction form with rationalised surd) so the second application of the quotient rule is mechanical.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Let u = (4+ln x)^{1/2}, v = x.
  2. Compute u' by chain rule: u' = (1/2)(4+ln x)^{-1/2} · (1/x).
  3. Compute v' = 1.
  4. Apply quotient rule: dy/dx = (u'v − uv')/v².
  5. Simplify to a single fraction, keeping surd factors intact for the next application.
  6. If d²y/dx² is required, set U = u'v − uv' and V = v², and apply quotient rule again.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $y = \dfrac{\sqrt{4+\ln x}}{x}$. With $u=(4+\ln x)^{1/2}$, $v=x$: $u' = \dfrac{1}{2x\sqrt{4+\ln x}}$, $v'=1$. Then $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{u'v - uv'}{v^2} = \dfrac{\frac{1}{2\sqrt{4+\ln x}} - \sqrt{4+\ln x}}{x^2} = \dfrac{1 - 2(4+\ln x)}{2x^2\sqrt{4+\ln x}} = \dfrac{-7-2\ln x}{2x^2\sqrt{4+\ln x}}$, ready for a second quotient-rule pass.

Variants — Same move, different surface

y = x^n · e^{f(x)} or y = x^x type expressions where only first derivative is required
Prefer logarithmic differentiation: ln converts the product/power to a sum, and there is no second-derivative penalty.
y = f(x)/g(x) where f, g are simple polynomials and a second derivative is needed
Quotient rule directly; no temptation to use logs since there is no product/power structure to exploit.
y = [f(x)]^{g(x)} (variable base and exponent)
Logarithmic differentiation is essentially forced for the first derivative; for a second derivative, differentiate the explicit form dy/dx = y·h(x) using the product rule.
Quotient where the numerator is itself a product, e.g. y = x ln x / (1+x²)
Quotient rule on the outside, product rule on the numerator; logarithmic differentiation usually less tidy when a second derivative is requested.

Common Misreadings

  • Rewriting as y = (4+ln x)^{1/2} · x^{-1} and using the product rule — correct but generates more loose terms to recombine before the second derivative.
  • Assuming logarithmic differentiation is always 'cleaner' whenever logs or roots appear, without weighing the second-derivative cost.
  • Treating √(4+ln x) as if its derivative were 1/(2√(4+ln x)), forgetting the inner 1/x.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the chain-rule factor 1/x when differentiating (4+ln x)^{1/2}.
  • Failing to combine to a single fraction before differentiating again, leading to algebraic chaos in d²y/dx².
  • Sign errors when subtracting uv' in the quotient rule numerator.
  • Cancelling surd factors prematurely and losing track of the (4+ln x)^{-1/2} factor needed at the next stage.

A-level Scope Notes

All techniques used — quotient rule, chain rule, derivative of ln x, and (where relevant) logarithmic differentiation — are within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P3/P4). No tools beyond the standard A-level differentiation toolkit are invoked.
