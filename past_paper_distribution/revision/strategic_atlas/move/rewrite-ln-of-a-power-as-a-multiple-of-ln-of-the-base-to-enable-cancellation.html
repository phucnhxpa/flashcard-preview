Trigger — When to use

Fires after substituting integration limits (or evaluating an expression) when two logarithmic terms appear whose arguments are related by a power, e.g. ln 4 and ln 2, ln 9 and ln 3, or ln e^2 and ln e. The question asks for an EXACT answer, signalling that logs should combine or cancel rather than be evaluated numerically. A typical cue is seeing both ln(a^k) and ln(a) with rational coefficients in the same expression. Equivalent phrasings: 'leave answer in exact form', 'in terms of ln 2', or simply that no calculator is permitted.

Motivation — Why this works

The power law ln(a^k) = k ln a re-expresses logarithms with related arguments in a common 'currency' (ln of the base). Once every log term is written as a rational multiple of the same ln a, like terms can be combined or cancelled by ordinary algebra. In limit-substitution problems the upper- and lower-limit log contributions are often engineered to be equal and opposite, so this rewrite exposes an exact rational answer. Without it the expression looks irrational and the structural cancellation is hidden.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the expression contains ln 4 and ln 2 with rational coefficients, and the question demands an exact value.
  2. I rule out numerical evaluation (calculator) and rule out trying to combine ln 4 and ln 2 directly via ln(a) − ln(b) = ln(a/b), since the coefficients differ.
  3. I commit to the power law because 4 = 2², so ln 4 = 2 ln 2 brings every log term onto the same ln 2 axis.
  4. First concrete operation: substitute ln 4 → 2 ln 2 in the offending term and simplify the coefficient.
  5. Tactically, I then group the ln 2 terms to check whether they cancel; if they do, only rational fractions remain and I finish by simple arithmetic.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the related arguments (e.g. 4 and 2 with 4 = 2²).
  2. Apply the power law: replace ln 4 with 2 ln 2 (or ln 9 with 2 ln 3, etc.).
  3. Simplify the resulting coefficient, e.g. −(2 ln 2)/4 = −(ln 2)/2.
  4. Collect ln 2 terms across the whole expression and check for cancellation.
  5. Combine remaining rational fractions to obtain the exact answer.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\left[\tfrac{x}{2} - \tfrac{\ln x}{x}\right]_{2}^{4}$. Substituting: $\left(2 - \tfrac{\ln 4}{4}\right) - \left(1 - \tfrac{\ln 2}{2}\right) = 1 - \tfrac{\ln 4}{4} + \tfrac{\ln 2}{2}$. Use $\ln 4 = 2\ln 2$: $= 1 - \tfrac{2\ln 2}{4} + \tfrac{\ln 2}{2} = 1 - \tfrac{\ln 2}{2} + \tfrac{\ln 2}{2} = 1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Limits e and e² in an integral producing ln(e²) and ln e
Use ln(e²) = 2 and ln e = 1 directly; the same 'rewrite power as multiple' principle applies but evaluates to integers.
Limits 3 and 9 with ln 9 and ln 3 appearing
Replace ln 9 with 2 ln 3 to enable cancellation or combination.
Equation-solving such as ln 8 − 3 ln 2 = 0
Rewrite ln 8 = 3 ln 2 to reveal the identity or to solve for an unknown coefficient.
Binomial/series contexts with ln(2^n) terms
Pull the exponent out as n ln 2 to factor or telescope the sum.
Differential equations whose solution has ln(kx) terms at related x-values
Split ln(kx) = ln k + ln x and apply the power law to any ln of a perfect power.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating ln 4 and ln 2 as independent constants and leaving the answer in mixed log form.
  • Reaching for a calculator and giving a decimal, losing the 'exact form' marks.
  • Trying to use ln 4 − ln 2 = ln 2 when the coefficients of ln 4 and ln 2 are not equal, which is invalid.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips combining rational parts, e.g. writing −1/4 + 1/2 = −1/4 instead of +1/4.
  • Failing to distribute the outer minus sign correctly when expanding F(b) − F(a), which flips the sign of the lower-limit log term.
  • Forgetting to simplify the coefficient after the rewrite, e.g. leaving −(2 ln 2)/4 instead of −(ln 2)/2, which can mask the cancellation.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the standard log power law ln(a^k) = k ln a from C2/P2 logarithms, applied within definite-integral evaluation or exact-form algebra in Edexcel IAL Maths and Further Maths. No change-of-base or complex logarithms required.
