Algebra & Functions

Classify y = f(kx) as horizontal stretch with scale factor 1/k

Family: Graph Transformation Analysis Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a transformation question presents y = f(kx) with a constant k multiplying x inside the function argument, e.g. y = f(2x), y = f(3x), y = f(x/2). Typical cues: 'sketch the graph of y = f(2x)', 'state the coordinates of the image of P under the transformation', or being given the graph of y = f(x) and asked to draw y = f(kx). The multiplier sits inside the bracket, acting on x, not outside acting on f(x).

Motivation — Why this works

Replacing x by kx means the function attains any given output at x-input divided by k, since f(k·(x/k)) = f(x). So horizontal coordinates scale by 1/k — the reciprocal of the inside multiplier. This is the canonical 'inside means opposite' rule for horizontal transformations: an inside factor of k > 1 compresses the graph toward the y-axis, while 0 < k < 1 stretches it away from the y-axis. Getting the reciprocal wrong flips compression and stretch and loses every shape mark.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the constant k sits inside f(…), multiplying x — so the transformation acts on x-coordinates, not y-coordinates.
  2. Rule out a vertical stretch (that would be y = k·f(x)) and rule out stretching by factor k (that would correspond to y = f(x/k)).
  3. Commit to: y = f(kx) is a horizontal stretch with scale factor 1/k about the y-axis.
  4. Bridge to operations: every point (x, y) on y = f(x) maps to (x/k, y) on the new curve; y-coordinates and y-axis intercepts are preserved.
  5. Tactically: transform the named labelled points first, check whether any points on the y-axis stay fixed, then sketch the same shape with horizontal coordinates rescaled.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the inside multiplier k in y = f(kx) and state: horizontal stretch, scale factor 1/k, about the y-axis.
  2. Map each labelled point (a, b) on y = f(x) to (a/k, b) on the transformed graph.
  3. Check fixed points: any point on the y-axis (x = 0) is unchanged since 0/k = 0.
  4. If an explicit form of f is given, write the new equation by substituting kx for x, e.g. y = √x becomes y = √(kx).
  5. Sketch the same overall shape as y = f(x), horizontally rescaled, and label the transformed coordinates on the diagram.

Worked Micro-Example

Given y = f(x) = √x passes through O(0,0) and P(9,3), sketch y = f(2x). Here k = 2, so horizontal stretch with SF 1/2. The origin is fixed, and P(9,3) maps to (9/2, 3) = (4.5, 3). The new equation is y = √(2x), giving the same √-shape compressed toward the y-axis through (4.5, 3).

Variants — Same move, different surface

y = f(3x) with k > 1
Horizontal compression with SF 1/3; multiply each x-coordinate by 1/3.
y = f(x/2), i.e. k = 1/2
Horizontal stretch with SF 2; multiply each x-coordinate by 2 — the reciprocal case students often confuse with f(2x).
y = f(-x)
Reflection in the y-axis (special case k = -1, SF |1/k| = 1 with sign flip).
y = a·f(x) (outside multiplier)
Vertical stretch SF a — y-coordinates scale by a directly, no reciprocal, contrast with the inside case.
y = f(x + a) (inside addition)
Horizontal translation by -a, not a stretch — but same 'inside means opposite' principle (sign flip rather than reciprocal).

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the factor k as if it were outside, scaling y-coordinates instead of x-coordinates.
  • Stretching by factor k instead of 1/k, forgetting the reciprocal rule for horizontal transformations.
  • Confusing y = f(2x) with y = f(x/2) and applying the wrong direction (compress vs stretch).

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting that points on the y-axis are fixed — and conversely, that points off the y-axis always move horizontally.
  • Failing to update labelled coordinates on the sketch (e.g. leaving P at (9,3) instead of (4.5,3)) — examiners require the new coordinates to be shown.
  • Mis-stating the description: must say 'horizontal stretch, scale factor 1/2' (or equivalent), not just 'compression by 2'.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure (Core Mathematics) graph transformation content; combinations of stretches and translations on y = f(x) are examined in P2/P3 and remain within the single-variable transformation toolkit.
