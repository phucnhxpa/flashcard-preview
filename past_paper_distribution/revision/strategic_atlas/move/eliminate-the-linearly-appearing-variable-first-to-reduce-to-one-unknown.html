Algebra & Functions

Eliminate the linearly-appearing variable first to reduce to one unknown

Family: Strategic Variable Elimination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A system of three equations in three unknowns where one variable appears linearly in only some equations while another appears non-linearly (e.g. as both r and r²). Cues include systems mixing arithmetic and geometric progression conditions, parametric setups, or any 3×3 system where variables have unequal 'degrees' of appearance. The move fires when you can spot one variable that is cheapest to isolate and substitute away, leaving a smaller system. The non-linear variable is the natural 'last survivor'.

Motivation — Why this works

Eliminating the linearly-occurring variable first costs only one substitution and introduces no new powers or products. Saving the non-linear variable for last keeps it confined to a single final equation, which is then solved by standard polynomial or rational methods. Choosing the wrong order forces you to substitute expressions involving r and r² into multiple equations, multiplying algebraic complexity. The principle is: eliminate in increasing order of non-linearity.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice which variable appears in the fewest equations and only to power 1 — that variable is the cheapest to eliminate.
  2. Rule out eliminating the variable that appears non-linearly (e.g. both r and r²), since substituting it would raise degrees elsewhere.
  3. Commit to eliminating the linear variable first because one substitution removes it entirely from the system.
  4. Isolate the linear variable from its simplest equation and substitute into the remaining equation(s) that contain it.
  5. From the resulting reduced system, isolate the next-easiest variable (often via the purely multiplicative equation) and substitute to obtain one equation in the non-linear unknown.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the variable that appears linearly in the fewest equations; isolate it from the simplest equation.
  2. Substitute that expression into every other equation containing the variable, eliminating it system-wide.
  3. From the reduced 2-equation system, isolate the next variable from the cleanest equation (typically a pure product like ar² = k giving a = k/r²).
  4. Substitute into the remaining equation to obtain a single equation in the non-linear unknown.
  5. Clear fractions and solve the resulting polynomial/rational equation.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $a + 3d = 10$, $ar^2 = 16$, $a + d = ar$. From Eq 1: $d = (10-a)/3$. Sub into Eq 3: $a + (10-a)/3 = ar \Rightarrow 2a + 10 = 3ar$. From Eq 2: $a = 16/r^2$. Substituting: $32/r^2 + 10 = 48/r$, i.e. $10r^2 - 48r + 32 = 0$, giving $r = 4$ or $r = 4/5$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

AP/GP combined problems where three terms satisfy both an arithmetic and a geometric condition
Eliminate the common difference $d$ first since it appears linearly, leaving a single equation in the common ratio $r$.
Parametric-to-Cartesian conversion with an auxiliary variable
Eliminate the parameter that appears linearly (e.g. $t$ from $x = 2t+1$) before tackling one that appears as $t^2$.
Simultaneous equations mixing linear and quadratic constraints
Isolate the variable from the linear equation and substitute into the quadratic, rather than the reverse.
Trigonometric systems with one identity linear in $\sin\theta$ and another quadratic
Eliminate the linearly-appearing trig term first to reduce to a single quadratic in the other.

Common Misreadings

  • Attempting to eliminate the non-linear variable (e.g. $r$ when it appears as both $r$ and $r^2$) first, which forces a quadratic substitution and inflates the algebra.
  • Treating all three equations symmetrically and trying matrix elimination, missing that one variable is structurally cheaper to remove.
  • Substituting $a = 16/r^2$ into every equation immediately, before first eliminating $d$, producing fractions throughout.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to multiply every term by 3 when clearing the fraction $(10-a)/3$.
  • Sign errors when distributing $3a + 10 - a$ to get $2a + 10$.
  • Multiplying $3a \cdot r$ incorrectly or losing the factor when combining $32/r^2 + 10 = 48/r$ into a polynomial.
  • Discarding a valid solution branch when the final equation factorises into two roots for $r$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure scope: simultaneous equations, arithmetic and geometric sequences (P1/P2), and standard polynomial/rational manipulation. No techniques beyond substitution and quadratic solving are required.
