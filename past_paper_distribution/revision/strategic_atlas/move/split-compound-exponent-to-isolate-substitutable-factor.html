Split compound exponent to isolate substitutable factor

Family: Exponent Separation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an exponential expression has a non-pure exponent — a sum or difference like $a^{x-2}$, $a^{x+1}$, or $a^{2x+3}$ — and you need to expose a clean $a^x$ (or $(a^x)^k$) factor. Typical cue: a substitution $y = a^x$ has been set up (often in a disguised quadratic) but the equation contains $a^{x \pm c}$ terms that don't directly become $y$. Also fires when comparing/combining exponentials with shifted exponents.

Motivation — Why this works

The index law $a^{m+n} = a^m \cdot a^n$ lets a single exponential with a compound exponent split into a product of two exponentials. One factor carries the variable ($a^x$, becoming $y$); the other is a pure numerical constant ($a^{\pm c}$). This cleanly separates the variable and constant parts so substitution becomes mechanical, and it is the standard preparation step before reducing to a polynomial in $y$.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the exponent is not just $x$ — it's $x-2$, so a direct substitution $y=3^x$ won't absorb it.
  2. I rule out trying to rewrite the base or take logs; the exponent is already a clean sum, so an index law will do it.
  3. I commit to $a^{m+n}=a^m\cdot a^n$ because it surgically peels off the constant part while leaving $3^x$ intact.
  4. First concrete operation: write $x-2 = x+(-2)$ and split $3^{x-2}=3^x\cdot 3^{-2}$.
  5. Tactically, I evaluate $3^{-2}=\tfrac{1}{9}$ immediately so the substitution gives $\tfrac{y}{9}$ — a tidy coefficient — rather than leaving an unsimplified $3^{-2}$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rewrite the compound exponent as an explicit sum: $x-2 = x + (-2)$.
  2. Apply $a^{m+n} = a^m \cdot a^n$: $3^{x-2} = 3^x \cdot 3^{-2}$.
  3. Evaluate the numerical factor: $3^{-2} = \tfrac{1}{9}$.
  4. Substitute $y = 3^x$ to obtain $\tfrac{y}{9}$ (or the analogous coefficient).
  5. Insert into the surrounding equation and clear fractions if needed (e.g. multiply through by $9$).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $3^{x-2} + 3^x = 10$. Split: $3^{x-2} = 3^x \cdot 3^{-2} = \tfrac{1}{9}\,3^x$. Let $y = 3^x$: $\tfrac{y}{9} + y = 10 \Rightarrow \tfrac{10y}{9} = 10 \Rightarrow y = 9$. Hence $3^x = 9$, so $x = 2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Exponent of the form $kx + c$, e.g. $3^{2x+1}$
Split as $3^{2x}\cdot 3^{1}$, then apply power-of-a-power: $3^{2x} = (3^x)^2 = y^2$, giving $3y^2$.
Disguised quadratic in $e^x$, e.g. $e^{x+1}$ inside $e^{2x} - e^{x+1} + e = 0$
Write $e^{x+1} = e\cdot e^x$; with $y=e^x$ the equation becomes $y^2 - ey + e = 0$.
Negative shift inside a logarithmic equation, e.g. $\log_2(2^{x-3})$
Split $2^{x-3} = 2^x\cdot 2^{-3}$ before applying log laws, so the constant $-3$ emerges cleanly.
Different bases sharing a common base after rewriting, e.g. $9^{x-1}$
Rewrite as $3^{2(x-1)} = 3^{2x}\cdot 3^{-2} = \tfrac{1}{9}(3^x)^2 = \tfrac{y^2}{9}$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $3^{x-2}$ as $3^x - 3^2$ (confusing exponent subtraction with subtraction of powers).
  • Reading $3^{x-2}$ as $\tfrac{3^x}{3^{-2}} = 9\cdot 3^x$ by misapplying the quotient rule (sign error on the constant exponent).
  • Assuming substitution $y=3^x$ cannot proceed because the exponent isn't exactly $x$, and abandoning the method.

Pitfalls

  • Leaving the answer as $y\cdot 3^{-2}$ instead of simplifying to $\tfrac{y}{9}$.
  • Forgetting the negative sign: writing $3^{x-2} = 3^x\cdot 3^{2} = 9\cdot 3^x$.
  • When the exponent is $kx+c$ with $k\neq 1$, splitting off the constant but neglecting the power-of-a-power step (writing $3^{2x}$ as $y$ instead of $y^2$).
  • Clearing the fraction inconsistently across the equation (multiplying only one term by $9$).
