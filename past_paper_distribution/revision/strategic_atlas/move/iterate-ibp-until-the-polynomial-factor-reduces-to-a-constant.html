Integration

Iterate IBP until the polynomial factor reduces to a constant

Family: Repeated Ibp Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a first IBP on ∫P(x)e^{ax} dx (or P(x)sin ax, P(x)cos ax) returns a residual integral of the same structural type but with polynomial degree reduced by one. The cue is: 'I still see polynomial × exponential/trig after one round.' This applies whenever deg P ≥ 2, signalling that one IBP is not enough. Alternative phrasings: 'apply IBP repeatedly', 'reduce the power of x step by step', 'iterate IBP n times for a degree-n polynomial'.

Motivation — Why this works

Each IBP with u = polynomial differentiates the polynomial once, lowering its degree by exactly one, while the exponential or trig part is preserved (up to constants) by integration. After n rounds for a degree-n polynomial, the polynomial factor becomes a constant and the remaining integral is elementary. Recognising this upfront prevents stopping prematurely or switching to an unhelpful method for the residual.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the residual integral after one IBP still has the form polynomial × exponential (or × sin/cos), just with the polynomial degree reduced by one.
  2. I rule out substitution and any change of technique — the residual is structurally identical to the original at lower degree, so a different method offers no advantage.
  3. I commit to repeating IBP with the same role assignment (u = polynomial part, dv/dx = exponential/trig part) because LIATE/structure dictates the same choice.
  4. Bridge: I queue up a second IBP and plan to substitute its result back into the first expression, watching the bracket sign carefully.
  5. Tactically, I track signs at each round and, for higher-degree cases, consider a tabular (DI) layout to avoid bookkeeping errors.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. After first IBP on ∫x²e^{2x} dx with u = x², dv/dx = e^{2x}: obtain ½x²e^{2x} − ∫xe^{2x} dx.
  2. For the residual ∫xe^{2x} dx, set u = x, du/dx = 1; dv/dx = e^{2x}, v = ½e^{2x}.
  3. Apply IBP: ∫xe^{2x} dx = ½xe^{2x} − ∫½e^{2x} dx = ½xe^{2x} − ¼e^{2x}.
  4. Substitute back: ∫x²e^{2x} dx = ½x²e^{2x} − (½xe^{2x} − ¼e^{2x}) + C = ½x²e^{2x} − ½xe^{2x} + ¼e^{2x} + C.
  5. Optionally factor: ¼e^{2x}(2x² − 2x + 1) + C.

Worked Micro-Example

Compute $\int x^{2}e^{2x}\,dx$. First IBP ($u=x^2$, $dv=e^{2x}dx$): $\tfrac12 x^2 e^{2x} - \int x e^{2x}\,dx$. Second IBP ($u=x$, $dv=e^{2x}dx$): $\int x e^{2x}\,dx = \tfrac12 x e^{2x} - \tfrac14 e^{2x}$. Combine: $\int x^2 e^{2x}\,dx = \tfrac12 x^2 e^{2x} - \tfrac12 x e^{2x} + \tfrac14 e^{2x} + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

∫x³e^{x} dx (higher-degree polynomial × exponential)
Three rounds of IBP are required; a tabular/DI table with alternating signs is recommended for clean bookkeeping.
∫x² sin x dx or ∫x² cos x dx
Two rounds of IBP are needed; the v column cycles sin → −cos → −sin, so sign tracking is even more critical.
∫x²(ax+b)^{n} dx via IBP rather than expansion
Same iterative logic with u = x² and dv = (ax+b)^n dx; degree of x falls by one each round until constant.
∫x² ln x dx
Only one IBP is needed (with u = ln x), since differentiating ln x removes it entirely; iteration does not arise.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying a substitution like u = xe^{2x} for the residual instead of recognising another IBP of the same type.
  • Believing one IBP must always suffice and concluding the integral is 'unsolvable' when the residual still has an x factor.
  • Switching role assignments mid-process (e.g., letting u = e^{2x} on the second round), which undoes the progress of the first round.

Pitfalls

  • Losing track of the alternating −∫v du sign across rounds.
  • Dropping the bracket when substituting the second result into the first, e.g., writing ½x²e^{2x} − ½xe^{2x} − ¼e^{2x} instead of ½x²e^{2x} − ½xe^{2x} + ¼e^{2x}.
  • Forgetting the constant of integration after the final round.
  • Errors in v when dv/dx = e^{ax} or sin/cos(ax) — missing the 1/a factor compounds across rounds.

A-level Scope Notes

Repeated integration by parts for polynomial × exponential or polynomial × sine/cosine is standard in Edexcel IAL P3/P4 and Further Pure; tabular DI is acceptable as scratch work but final answers should display the IBP formula application.
