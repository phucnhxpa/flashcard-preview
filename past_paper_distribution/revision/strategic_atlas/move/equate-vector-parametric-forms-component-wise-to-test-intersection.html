Vectors

Equate vector parametric forms component-wise to test intersection

Family: Parametric Elimination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Two 3D lines are given in vector parametric form with distinct parameters (typically λ and μ), and the question asks to show they intersect or to find the point of intersection. Phrasings include 'show that the lines meet', 'find the position vector of the point of intersection', or 'prove the lines are not skew'. The presence of two independent parameters and a 'show'/'verify' instruction is the key cue.

Motivation — Why this works

Setting r₁ = r₂ converts one vector equation into three scalar equations in just two unknowns (λ and μ). This deliberate over-determination is the whole point: two components pin down the parameters, and the third component acts as a consistency check that distinguishes intersecting lines from skew lines. Without checking the third equation, you have only solved a 2×2 system and have not actually proved intersection.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice two lines in 3D with different parameters — they could intersect, be parallel, or be skew, so a verification step is required.
  2. Rule out shortcuts like cross-product distance formulas or pure geometric reasoning, since the mark scheme rewards an algebraic intersection proof and an explicit point.
  3. Commit to equating r₁ = r₂ component-by-component because this directly produces the over-determined 3-equations-in-2-unknowns system needed to both solve and verify.
  4. Bridge to the first operation: write the vector equation, then split into x, y, z scalar equations labelled (1), (2), (3).
  5. Tactically pick the two simplest equations (often the one with a single parameter) to solve for λ and μ, then substitute into the third as the consistency check; finally substitute back into either line to state the point of intersection.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write r₁ = r₂, e.g. (1, 0, −1) + λ(2, 1, 3) = (4, 0, 2) + μ(1, −1, 0).
  2. Extract the x-component equation: 1 + 2λ = 4 + μ …(1).
  3. Extract the y-component equation: λ = −μ …(2).
  4. Extract the z-component equation: −1 + 3λ = 2 …(3).
  5. Solve two of the equations simultaneously for λ and μ.
  6. Substitute into the remaining equation; if it is satisfied, the lines intersect — state this conclusion explicitly.
  7. Substitute λ (or μ) back into r₁ (or r₂) to give the position vector of the intersection point.

Worked Micro-Example

From (3): $-1+3\lambda=2\Rightarrow \lambda=1$. From (2): $\mu=-\lambda=-1$. Check (1): LHS $=1+2(1)=3$, RHS $=4+(-1)=3$ ✓. Hence the lines meet, and substituting $\lambda=1$ into $r_1$ gives the point of intersection $(3,1,2)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Lines given in Cartesian symmetric form $(x-a)/p=(y-b)/q=(z-c)/r$
First introduce a parameter for each line (set each chain equal to λ, then μ) to recover vector parametric form, then equate components as usual.
Line meets a plane given in vector/Cartesian form
Substitute the parametric components of the line into the plane equation to get a single equation in λ; no second parameter, no consistency check needed.
Showing a specific point lies on a line
Equate the point's coordinates to the parametric components, solve for λ from one equation, and check the other two components agree.
Two lines in 2D
Only two scalar equations in two unknowns are produced; the system is square, so any solution gives an intersection — no separate consistency check is possible (lines in 2D cannot be skew).

Common Misreadings

  • Using the same parameter letter for both lines, which falsely forces them to meet at equal parameter values and only detects coincident parametrisations.
  • Stopping after solving two of the three equations and declaring intersection without checking the third — this fails to rule out skew lines.
  • Confusing this with the parallel/skew test: students sometimes try to compare direction vectors instead of equating position vectors.

Pitfalls

  • Algebraic slips when extracting components, especially sign errors on negative direction entries.
  • Forgetting to state the intersection point at the end after verifying consistency.
  • Substituting λ into the wrong line or mixing λ and μ when computing the final position vector.
  • Concluding 'no intersection' from an inconsistent third equation without explicitly stating the lines are therefore skew.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Core Pure / FP3 vector geometry: 3D lines in parametric vector form, intersection vs skew classification, and stating points of intersection — no use of matrix rank or higher linear-algebra machinery.
