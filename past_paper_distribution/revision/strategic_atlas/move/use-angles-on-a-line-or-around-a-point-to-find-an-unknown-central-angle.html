Trigonometry

Use angles-on-a-line or around-a-point to find an unknown central angle

Family: Angle Chasing Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in circle/sector problems where you need a central angle (for sector area, triangle area via ½ab sin C, or arc length) but it is not given directly. The visual cues are: (i) a configuration where multiple radii meet at the centre O, (ii) a diameter or straight line through O (giving a π-sum), or (iii) several sectors/triangles filling the full turn at O (giving a 2π-sum), often combined with a symmetry or congruence condition that forces two of the unknown angles to be equal. Equivalent phrasings: 'D, O, C collinear so ∠AOD + ∠AOC = π', 'angles round O sum to 2π', 'by symmetry the two flanking angles are equal'.

Motivation — Why this works

This move exploits two elementary identities — angles on a straight line sum to π, and angles around a point sum to 2π — to extract an unknown angle from a single linear equation, avoiding heavier machinery (dot products, cosine rule, coordinates). It works because the geometric configuration constrains the angles globally, so once you know all-but-one (or all-but-two equal ones), the missing angle drops out. The resulting expression often has a clean form like π − θ or (2π − α)/2, and identities such as sin(π − θ) = sin θ keep downstream area/length calculations tidy.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the quantity needed downstream (sector area, triangle area, arc length) requires a central angle at O that the question has not stated explicitly.
  2. Consider alternatives — cosine rule on the triangle, dot product with coordinates, or arccos from a known ratio — and rule them out as heavier than necessary once a straight-line or full-turn constraint at O is spotted.
  3. Commit to angle-chasing: scan the figure for (a) any diameter or three collinear points through O (gives a π-sum on each side), or (b) the fact that all angles at O must close up to 2π, plus any symmetry/congruence that pairs two unknown angles.
  4. Translate the geometric constraint into a linear equation: e.g. ∠AOD + ∠AOC = π, or ∠AOB + ∠AOC + ∠BOC = 2π with ∠AOC = ∠BOC.
  5. Solve for the unknown angle and decide whether the minor or major version is needed (critical if the sector wraps the 'long way' round); if the major sector is wanted, take 2π minus the minor angle just found.
  6. Sanity-check the value lies in (0, 2π) and, where possible, cross-verify against a quick dot-product or cosine-rule check (e.g. cos θ = −1/3 ↔ θ = π − arccos(1/3)).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the constraint: straight line through O ⇒ adjacent angles sum to π; full turn at O ⇒ all angles at O sum to 2π.
  2. Label unknown angles, applying any congruence/symmetry to equate pairs (reducing to one unknown).
  3. Write the linear equation, e.g. α + 2β = 2π or β = π − α.
  4. Solve for the unknown angle in radians.
  5. If the major sector/angle is required, compute 2π − (minor angle) as a final step.

Worked Micro-Example

Three radii OA, OB, OC meet at centre O with $\angle AOB = 1.6$ rad, and triangles $\triangle AOC \cong \triangle BOC$. Then $\angle AOC = \angle BOC$, so $1.6 + 2\angle AOC = 2\pi \Rightarrow \angle AOC = \pi - 0.8$. The triangle area is $\tfrac12 r^2 \sin(\pi - 0.8) = \tfrac12 r^2 \sin(0.8)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Diameter / three collinear points through O (D-O-C straight)
Use the π-sum: ∠AOD + ∠AOC = π, so ∠AOC = π − ∠AOD.
Major sector wrapping 'the long way' from OA to OC
After finding the minor ∠AOC, take major angle = 2π − ∠AOC (minor).
Three or more sectors/triangles meeting at O with k-fold symmetry
Generalise the full-turn equation: α + kβ = 2π, solve for β = (2π − α)/k.
Coordinate/vector setup available
Cross-check via cos(∠AOC) = (OA·OC)/(|OA||OC|); the angle-chase should agree.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming ∠AOC = π − α when in fact the remaining (2π − α) is shared between two equal angles, giving (2π − α)/2.
  • Treating the required sector as the minor one when the diagram shows the lawn/region wrapping the outside (major sector).

Pitfalls

  • Mixing degrees and radians — using 360° in one step and π elsewhere; keep everything in radians.
  • Confusing major vs minor sector angle: using 1.91 rad instead of 4.37 rad (or vice versa) gives a wildly wrong area.
  • Forgetting to halve when symmetry pairs two unknown angles, leading to a doubled answer.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 sector and triangle work; uses only 'angles on a straight line = π', 'angles round a point = 2π', and the formula-booklet entries for sector area ½r²θ, arc length rθ, and triangle area ½ab sin C. No directed-angle or oriented-angle subtleties are needed.
