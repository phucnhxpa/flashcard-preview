Binomial Expansion

Pair only the cross-terms that hit the target power

Family: Selective Multiplication Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A question asks for the coefficient of $x^k$ in a product $(A + Bx)\times(\text{series})$ or $(A + Bx + Cx^2)\times(\text{series})$, where the series is typically a binomial expansion truncated to a few terms. Phrases like 'find the coefficient of $x^3$', 'hence find...', or 'using your expansion from part (a)' are the giveaway. The full product is not required — only one specific power. Common in Edexcel IAL C12/P3 binomial expansion follow-up parts.

Motivation — Why this works

In a product $f(x)g(x)$, the coefficient of $x^k$ is $\sum_{i+j=k} a_i b_j$ (the Cauchy product). When one factor has very few terms, this sum collapses to as many contributions as there are non-zero terms in the short factor. Listing only those pairings whose degrees add to $k$ avoids redundant multiplication and reduces arithmetic error. It is the most efficient route for any 'coefficient of $x^k$ in a product' task at A-level.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question only asks for one specific coefficient (e.g. $x^3$), not the full expansion of the product.
  2. I rule out multiplying every term against every term — most products would be discarded anyway.
  3. I commit to the Cauchy-pair strategy: list only the index pairs $(i,j)$ with $i+j=k$.
  4. Bridge to operation: write down the short factor's terms with their degrees, and the relevant series terms with their degrees, then match.
  5. Tactically, I keep coefficients as exact fractions, and check at the end that every contributing pair has been counted exactly once.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the target power $x^k$ and the two factors $f(x)$ and $g(x)$.
  2. List the terms of the short factor with their degrees (e.g. $A$ at degree 0, $Bx$ at degree 1).
  3. For each term of degree $i$ in the short factor, locate the term of degree $k-i$ in the expansion.
  4. Form the products of (coefficient $\times$ coefficient) for each valid pairing.
  5. Sum these contributions, working over a common denominator if fractions are involved.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the coefficient of $x^2$ in $(2+5x)(1+x)^4$. The expansion gives $1+4x+6x^2+\dots$. Pairings for $x^2$: $2\times 6x^2$ and $5x\times 4x$. Coefficient $= 2(6) + 5(4) = 12 + 20 = 32$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Pre-multiplier is a quadratic $(A+Bx+Cx^2)$
Three pairings are needed: $A\cdot[x^k] + B\cdot[x^{k-1}] + C\cdot[x^{k-2}]$ from the expansion.
Both factors are full binomial series (e.g. $(1+x)^n(1-2x)^m$)
Use the Cauchy convolution $\sum_{i=0}^{k}a_i b_{k-i}$, taking only the indices needed up to $i+j=k$.
Target power is $x^0$ (the constant) or $x^1$
Only one or two pairings exist; the method is even shorter, but check the constant term of each factor carefully.
Pre-multiplier contains a negative power, e.g. $(1+\tfrac{1}{x})\cdot(\dots)$ in a Further Maths context
Same principle, but degrees can be negative; pair so that degrees still sum to $k$.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading the question as 'expand fully' rather than 'find one coefficient', leading to wasted work.
  • Missing one of the cross-product contributions, especially the one coming from the $x$-term of the short factor.
  • Treating the truncated series as exact and including spurious higher-order terms that the expansion did not provide.

Pitfalls

  • Pairing the wrong degrees: the constant pairs with the $x^k$ coefficient, the $x$-term pairs with the $x^{k-1}$ coefficient — swapping these is the most common slip.
  • Forgetting to multiply by the numerical coefficient of the $x$-term (using $1$ instead of $B$).
  • Sign errors when the expansion has alternating signs — keep brackets around negative coefficients.
  • Arithmetic slips with fractions; convert to a common denominator before summing.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Maths (C12/P3 binomial expansion) and Further Maths; no generating-function machinery beyond the finite Cauchy-product idea is required.
