Algebra & Functions

Insert zero-power factor to match prescribed monomial template

Family: Coefficient Matching Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a simplified expression appears to be missing a variable that the question's required form (e.g. $ap^b q^c$, $kx^m y^n$) explicitly contains. Cues: 'Express in the form ...' followed by a template with more variables than your simplified answer shows; the question asks for the values of every constant including one that seems to have vanished. Also triggers when a binomial or product expansion collapses one variable's contribution to a constant.

Motivation — Why this works

For any non-zero base, $x^0 = 1$, so multiplying by $q^0$ leaves the value unchanged while restoring the variable to the written form. This lets the simplified expression be aligned term-by-term with the prescribed template, yielding a legitimate value (often $0$) for the exponent that looked absent. The move is justified by an identity, not a fudge, and it secures the mark for the missing constant.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice my simplified answer (e.g. $162p^5$) has fewer variables than the target form $ap^b q^c$ demands.
  2. I rule out the idea that $q$ has 'cancelled' in a way that makes $c$ undefined — the template still expects a numerical $c$.
  3. I commit to the identity $q^0 = 1$, which lets me reintroduce $q$ without altering the value.
  4. I rewrite $162p^5$ as $162p^5 q^0$ so the structure matches $ap^b q^c$ exactly.
  5. I read off $a=162$, $b=5$, $c=0$ by direct comparison of corresponding factors.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compare your simplified result to the prescribed form and identify the missing variable(s).
  2. Multiply by that variable raised to the power $0$, using $x^0 = 1$.
  3. Write the result in full template order, e.g. $162p^5 q^0$.
  4. Match coefficient and each exponent against $ap^b q^c$ to extract every constant.
  5. State all required values explicitly, including the zero exponent.

Worked Micro-Example

Simplify $\dfrac{(3p^2 q)^2 \cdot 18 p}{q^2}$ and express in the form $a p^b q^c$.\\ $= \dfrac{9p^4 q^2 \cdot 18 p}{q^2} = \dfrac{162 p^5 q^2}{q^2} = 162 p^5$.\\ Rewrite as $162 p^5 q^0$, so $a=162$, $b=5$, $c=0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

A simplification in a question demanding $kx^n$ form leaves a pure constant.
Write the constant as $k x^0$ to expose $n=0$.
Binomial expansion asked to be written as $A + Bx + Cx^2$ where the $x^2$ coefficient happens to be $0$.
Explicitly include the $0 \cdot x^2$ term so $C=0$ can be stated.
Partial fractions or polynomial identity matching where one power of $x$ is absent on one side.
Insert $0 \cdot x^k$ as a placeholder so coefficient comparison gives that constant explicitly.
Logarithm/index questions requiring an answer as $a + b\ln x$ when the answer is purely numerical.
Write the answer as $a + 0\cdot \ln x$ to record $b=0$.

Common Misreadings

  • Believing the variable has 'cancelled out' and leaving the corresponding constant blank or writing 'undefined'.
  • Assuming every required constant must be non-zero and forcing a wrong non-zero value.
  • Treating the missing variable as a sign of an algebraic error and redoing the simplification incorrectly.

Pitfalls

  • Writing $c = 1$ by confusing the value of $q^0$ (which is $1$) with the exponent itself (which is $0$).
  • Omitting $c$ from the final answer and losing the mark.
  • Introducing $q^0$ when $q$ could be zero (not an issue in IAL index questions, but worth noting if the domain restricts $q$).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1 indices and form-matching questions); the identity $x^0 = 1$ for $x \neq 0$ is standard and the technique is a routine examiner-expected step.
