Trigger — When to use

Fires when an expression (product, sum, or quotient) involves several variables, but the problem supplies relations that express all but one of them in terms of a single chosen variable. Cues include phrases like 'given that a = ...' and 'c = ... in terms of b', or recursive/parametric definitions of sequence terms. Also triggered when a divisibility, parity, sign, or factorisation property must be proved about a multi-letter expression — proving such a property usually requires a single-variable form first.

Motivation — Why this works

Reducing to one variable exposes structural features (common factors, perfect squares, sign of a quadratic) that are invisible while the expression is fragmented across several letters. Once written as a single polynomial in b, standard tools — factoring out constants, completing the square, discriminant tests — become directly applicable. The underlying principle is simply that a system with k equations in k+1 unknowns has one degree of freedom, so any derived quantity is a function of one parameter.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the target expression mixes several letters (e.g. abc) while the givens pin down all but one of them.
  2. Rule out eliminating the 'free' variable in favour of the others — that direction is harder because the givens are not solved that way.
  3. Commit to substitution because it is mechanical, reversible, and produces a single-variable polynomial ready for factoring.
  4. Bridge: literally write a = ... and c = ... and plug into the target, keeping brackets intact to avoid sign slips.
  5. Inside the move, decide whether to expand fully or keep factored form — for divisibility/parity proofs, keep factored; for differentiation or comparison, expand.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the free variable (the one in terms of which the others are expressed).
  2. Write the target expression and replace each constrained letter with its given expression, using brackets.
  3. Leave the result in factored form if a structural property (divisibility, sign, parity) is sought; otherwise expand.
  4. Inspect for an obvious common factor or sign that answers the question.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $a = 8 - 2b$ and $c = b + 2$, show $abc$ is even. Substitute: $abc = (8-2b)(b)(b+2) = 2(4-b)(b)(b+2)$. The factor $2$ is explicit, so $abc$ is even for every integer $b$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Sequence terms defined by $u_{n+1} = f(u_n)$ with a property to prove about $u_n u_{n+1}$
Substitute the recurrence to write the product as a single expression in $u_n$, then factor.
Parametric curve with $x = g(t)$, $y = h(t)$ and a relation like $xy$ or $x+y$ to simplify
Substitute both into the target to obtain a function of $t$ alone before differentiating or finding stationary values.
Two simultaneous equations where one expression (e.g. $x^2 + y^2$) is required
Solve one equation for $y$ in terms of $x$, substitute, and reduce to a single-variable polynomial.
Binomial/Pascal problems where two coefficients are linked by a given ratio
Use the ratio to express one coefficient in terms of the other, collapsing the unknown $n$ or $r$ into one equation.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to argue about each variable separately (case-split on parity of $b$) instead of substituting first.
  • Treating $a$, $b$, $c$ as independent and attempting a general proof, ignoring that the givens already tie them together.
  • Eliminating the wrong variable, producing messier expressions when the natural free variable is staring at you.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping brackets during substitution, e.g. writing $8 - 2b \cdot b$ instead of $(8-2b) \cdot b$, leading to sign/precedence errors.
  • Expanding prematurely and losing the factored form needed for the divisibility/parity argument.
  • Forgetting to substitute in every occurrence of the constrained letter.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths: routine substitution, polynomial manipulation, and elementary factor extraction. No techniques beyond standard algebra are required.
