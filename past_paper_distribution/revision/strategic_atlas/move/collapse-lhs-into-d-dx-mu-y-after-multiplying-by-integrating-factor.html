Trigger — When to use

This move fires immediately after a first-order linear ODE in standard form dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x) has been multiplied through by the integrating factor μ(x) = e^{∫P(x)dx}. The visual cue is a left-hand side of the form μ(x)·dy/dx + μ(x)P(x)·y — two terms, one containing dy/dx and one containing y, with a common μ factor and the y-coefficient matching μ'. Equivalent phrasings of the trigger: 'the LHS matches the product rule expansion of μy', 'μ' = μP by construction so LHS = (μy)''. The move also fires when an examiner explicitly cues it with 'show that the equation can be written as d/dx[μy] = …'.

Motivation — Why this works

The integrating factor μ is constructed precisely so that μ' = μP(x). By the product rule (read in reverse), μ·dy/dx + μ'·y = d/dx[μy], so the two-term LHS collapses into a single exact derivative. This is the entire payoff of the IF method: the ODE becomes d/dx[μy] = μQ(x), which is directly integrable to give μy = ∫μQ dx + C — a single algebraic equation for y. Without this recognition, the student is stuck with a product of two unknown functions and the method stalls.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the LHS, after multiplying by μ, has exactly two terms: μ·dy/dx and μP·y. This is the structural fingerprint of a product-rule expansion.
  2. I rule out trying to integrate the LHS term by term — that would treat μ·dy/dx and μP·y as independent integrals, which is circular because y is unknown.
  3. I commit to the exact-derivative recognition because by construction μ' = μP, so the product rule guarantees μ·dy/dx + μ'·y = d/dx[μy]. This is not a guess; it is forced by how μ was chosen.
  4. As a sanity check (especially under exam pressure), I expand d/dx[μy] via the product rule and verify it matches the LHS — a mismatch signals an algebra error in computing μ, which I trace back and fix rather than ignore.
  5. I rewrite the equation as d/dx[μy] = μQ(x) and integrate both sides with respect to x, attaching the constant +C only on the RHS.
  6. I then evaluate ∫μQ dx using whatever standard technique the integrand demands (direct integration, by parts, substitution, partial fractions), and finally divide through by μ to isolate y.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Confirm the ODE is in standard form dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x) and that μ(x) = e^{∫P dx} has been computed.
  2. Multiply every term by μ(x): μ·dy/dx + μP·y = μQ.
  3. (Optional check) Verify μ'(x) = μ(x)·P(x) so that the product rule gives d/dx[μy] = μ·dy/dx + μP·y.
  4. Replace the LHS with the exact derivative: d/dx[μ(x)·y] = μ(x)·Q(x).
  5. Integrate both sides with respect to x: μ(x)·y = ∫μ(x)Q(x) dx + C.
  6. Evaluate the RHS integral using the appropriate technique.
  7. Divide through by μ(x) to give y explicitly.

Worked Micro-Example

Given dy/dx − y/(x+1) = e^{2x}/(x+1) with μ = (x+1)e^{-x}: multiplying through gives (x+1)e^{-x}·dy/dx − xe^{-x}y = e^{2x}. Recognise LHS = d/dx[(x+1)e^{-x}·y]. Integrate: (x+1)e^{-x}·y = ½e^{2x} + C. Divide by μ: y = e^{3x}/[2(x+1)] + Ce^{x}/(x+1).

Variants — Same move, different surface

RHS integral ∫μQ dx requires integration by parts (e.g. μQ = xe^{x}).
Same strategic move; nest IBP inside step 6 of the mechanics. Recognition of the LHS as d/dx[μy] is unchanged.
Examiner pre-states the exact-derivative form, e.g. 'show the equation can be written as d/dx[x²y] = …'.
The recognition is cued; the student must verify by expanding d/dx[x²y] via the product rule and matching to the given LHS, rather than discovering μ from scratch.
Dependent variable is z (or another letter) instead of y, or the independent variable is t.
Pure relabelling — LHS collapses to d/dx[μz] (or d/dt[μz]); no change to the principle.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating μ·dy/dx and μP·y as two separate integrals to be tackled term by term, rather than as the two halves of a product-rule expansion.
  • Believing μ must be re-derived or re-checked at this stage, when in fact the product-rule identity is guaranteed by the construction of μ.
  • Confusing this with the exact-equation test ∂M/∂y = ∂N/∂x for M dx + N dy = 0 (a related but distinct technique outside IAL core scope).

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the constant of integration +C on the RHS — fatal for general solutions and any subsequent boundary-condition step.
  • Algebraic slip in computing μ' so that μ' ≠ μP; the LHS then does not collapse cleanly, and ignoring the mismatch propagates the error.
  • Errors when finally dividing by μ — particularly with exponential factors (dividing by e^{-x} means multiplying by e^{x}, which shifts exponents).
  • Errors inside ∫μQ dx, especially when integration by parts or substitution is required as a sub-technique.

A-level Scope Notes

This is the standard integrating-factor method for first-order linear ODEs as taught in Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3, depending on specification edition). The formula μ = e^{∫P dx} and the resulting d/dx[μy] = μQ structure are core syllabus. Avoid extending to exact equations in two variables (M dx + N dy = 0 with ∂M/∂y = ∂N/∂x), which is outside IAL scope and may confuse students.
