Strategic Atlas / Differential Equations / Separation Of Variables
Differential Equations

Separate variables to isolate y and x on opposite sides

Family: Separation Of Variables Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires on first-order ODEs of the form dy/dx = g(y)·h(x) or dy/dx = h(x)/g(y), where the right-hand side cleanly factors into a y-only piece and an x-only piece. Concrete cues: y appears only as a multiplicative or reciprocal factor (e.g. dy/dx = 6x²e^(-x/3)/y, dy/dx = xy, dy/dx = y cos x), with no mixed terms like x+y or e^(x+y) that resist factoring. If the equation already has the structure f(y) dy = g(x) dx after one rearrangement, separation is the intended route.

Motivation — Why this works

Once the equation is written f(y) dy = g(x) dx, both sides become independent single-variable integrals, reducing the ODE to two standard integration problems. This works because dy/dx is treated formally as a ratio of differentials, valid whenever the variables genuinely decouple. It is the most direct first-order technique in the A-level toolkit and should be tried before integrating factors or substitutions whenever the RHS factors.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Student notices the RHS factors cleanly: y appears only in one place (here as a denominator), and x-terms are grouped together.
  2. Student rules out integrating factors (no dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x) structure) and substitutions (no homogeneous or awkward composite term).
  3. Student commits to separation because f(y) dy = g(x) dx can be obtained by a single algebraic step.
  4. Bridge to operation: multiply or divide to gather all y-terms with dy on one side and all x-terms with dx on the other.
  5. Tactical decision: choose which side to attach the constant of integration (conventionally the x-side), and check that both resulting integrals are tractable by A-level methods (standard forms, parts, or substitution).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the y-factor and x-factor on the RHS.
  2. Rearrange algebraically so all y-dependence multiplies dy and all x-dependence multiplies dx, e.g. y dy = 6x²e^(-x/3) dx.
  3. Integrate both sides: ∫f(y) dy = ∫g(x) dx.
  4. Add a single constant of integration C on the x-side.
  5. If a boundary/initial condition is given, substitute to find C; otherwise leave the implicit general solution or solve for y explicitly if required.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve dy/dx = 6x²e^{-x/3}/y. Separate: y\,dy = 6x²e^{-x/3}\,dx. Integrate LHS: ∫y\,dy = y²/2. Integrate RHS by parts twice: ∫6x²e^{-x/3}\,dx = -18x²e^{-x/3} - 108xe^{-x/3} - 324e^{-x/3} + C. Hence y²/2 = -18e^{-x/3}(x² + 6x + 18) + C.

Variants — Same move, different surface

dy/dx = xy
Divide by y to get (1/y)dy = x\,dx; integrate to ln|y| = x²/2 + C, then exponentiate.
dy/dx = y cos x with y(0)=1
Separate to (1/y)dy = cos x\,dx; integrate to ln|y| = sin x + C; apply initial condition to fix C=0.
dy/dx = (1+y²)/x
Separate to dy/(1+y²) = dx/x; LHS integrates to arctan y, RHS to ln|x| + C.
Rate problems like dV/dt = -k√V
Separate V^{-1/2} dV = -k\,dt; integrate and apply initial volume to determine C.
dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x) with Q(x) ≠ 0
Separation fails because the RHS does not factor; switch to integrating factor e^{∫P dx}.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating an already-separable equation as needing an integrating factor, overcomplicating the problem.
  • Forgetting that y appears in a denominator and reading the RHS as purely a function of x.
  • Mistaking dy/dx = f(x) + g(y) (sum, not product) as separable — it is not.
  • Confusing dy/dx = e^{x+y} as non-separable when it factors as e^x·e^y.

Pitfalls

  • Omitting the constant of integration, or adding one on each side instead of combining into a single C.
  • Dropping absolute value signs when integrating 1/y to ln|y|, then mishandling sign of the exponentiated solution.
  • Algebra slip when multiplying through: failing to move every y-term to the LHS.
  • Applying the initial condition before integrating, instead of after.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P4 first-order ODEs). Resulting integrals stay within standard techniques: direct integration, substitution, integration by parts, and standard forms like 1/(1+x²).
