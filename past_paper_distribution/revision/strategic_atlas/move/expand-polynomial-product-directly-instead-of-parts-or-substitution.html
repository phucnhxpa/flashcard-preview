Integration

Expand polynomial product directly instead of parts or substitution

Family: Partial Fractions Then Integrate Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integrand is a product of two polynomial factors, e.g. $x^2(2x-3)^3$, $x(x+1)^4$, or $(x-1)^2(x+2)^3$. The combined degree after expansion is modest (typically ≤ 6 or 7), so term-by-term integration via the power rule is feasible. Cues include a low integer power on a linear bracket and a small monomial multiplier. Substitution and integration by parts both look applicable but neither truly avoids expansion.

Motivation — Why this works

A product of polynomials is itself a polynomial, and polynomials are integrated trivially by the power rule on each term. Integration by parts on $x^n \times (\text{cubic})$ would require $n$ successive applications, while substitution $u = ax+b$ still leaves a $(u+c)^n u^m$ expression that must be expanded. Direct expansion compresses the work into one binomial expansion plus routine integration. It is the dominant strategy whenever the total degree stays manageable.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand is a product of two polynomial factors — both expandable in closed form.
  2. Rule out integration by parts: $u=x^2,\,dv=(2x-3)^3\,dx$ would need three cycles of parts to clear $x^2$.
  3. Rule out substitution $u=2x-3$: it converts to $\tfrac{1}{8}\int(u+3)^2 u^3\,du$, which still requires expansion.
  4. Commit to expansion because the result is a degree-5 polynomial with only four terms — the cleanest path.
  5. Tactically: expand the higher-power bracket first, then distribute the monomial, then integrate term by term.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Expand the bracketed power using the binomial theorem, e.g. $(2x-3)^3 = 8x^3 - 36x^2 + 54x - 27$.
  2. Multiply through by the monomial factor: $x^2(8x^3 - 36x^2 + 54x - 27) = 8x^5 - 36x^4 + 54x^3 - 27x^2$.
  3. Integrate term by term using $\int x^n\,dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}$.
  4. Simplify each coefficient (e.g. $8/6 = 4/3$, $54/4 = 27/2$).
  5. For a definite integral, evaluate each limit as a single combined fraction (using a common denominator) before subtracting.

Worked Micro-Example

Compute $\int_2^3 x^2(2x-3)^3\,dx$. Expand: $(2x-3)^3 = 8x^3-36x^2+54x-27$, so the integrand is $8x^5-36x^4+54x^3-27x^2$. Antiderivative: $F(x) = \tfrac{4}{3}x^6 - \tfrac{36}{5}x^5 + \tfrac{27}{2}x^4 - 9x^3$. Then $\int_2^3 = F(3) - F(2)$, evaluated by combining each over LCD $30$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$\int x(x+1)^4\,dx$
Expand $(x+1)^4$ via binomial; multiply by $x$; integrate the resulting degree-5 polynomial.
$\int x^3(1-x)^2\,dx$
Expand $(1-x)^2 = 1-2x+x^2$; multiply by $x^3$; integrate the degree-5 polynomial term by term.
$\int (x-1)^2(x+2)^3\,dx$
Expand each bracket separately, multiply the two expansions, then integrate; total degree 5.
Integrand contains $e^x$ or $\sin x$ instead of a polynomial factor
Expansion no longer applies — switch to integration by parts (LIATE).

Common Misreadings

  • Reflexively reaching for integration by parts whenever a product is seen, without first checking if both factors are polynomial.
  • Attempting substitution $u = 2x-3$ and not realising expansion of $(u+3)^2 u^3$ is still required, plus extra fraction arithmetic.
  • Worrying that the bracketed power must be 'kept intact' — students sometimes feel expanding 'destroys structure' when in fact it simplifies the problem.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors in binomial expansion, especially $(-3)^2 = +9$ vs $(-3)^3 = -27$.
  • Forgetting to multiply every expanded term by the monomial factor (commonly missing the last term).
  • Arithmetic slips evaluating high powers at the limits, e.g. $3^6 = 729$, $2^6 = 64$ — one error cascades.
  • LCD errors when combining several fractions at the evaluation stage; safer to compute $F(3)$ and $F(2)$ each as a single fraction first.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (C2/C4): binomial expansion of $(a+b)^n$ for positive integer $n$ and power-rule integration of polynomials. No techniques beyond A-level are required.
