Coordinate Geometry

Parametrise point on conic to collapse two unknowns to one

Family: Parametric Representation Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question names a 'general point P' on an ellipse, hyperbola, parabola or rectangular hyperbola and then asks for a tangent, normal, chord, intercept or locus. Equivalent phrasings include 'a point on the curve with coordinates (x₁, y₁)', 'P lies on the ellipse and the tangent at P meets the axes at...', or 'find the locus of the midpoint as P varies'. The visual cue is a Cartesian point that would otherwise have to be carried alongside the conic equation as a side-constraint. If you find yourself about to write x₁²/a² + y₁²/b² = 1 as a working line, you should switch to a parameter instead.

Motivation — Why this works

Replacing (x₁, y₁) by a parametric pair such as (a cos θ, b sin θ) for an ellipse, or (a sec t, b tan t) for a hyperbola, absorbs the conic equation into a Pythagorean-type identity (cos²θ + sin²θ = 1, sec²t − tan²t = 1). This converts a two-variable constrained problem into a single-variable unconstrained one: every subsequent gradient, tangent line, intercept, and locus expression becomes a clean function of the single parameter. The identity is then exactly the algebraic tool needed to eliminate the parameter at the locus stage, which has no analogue if Cartesian coordinates are kept.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question refers to a general point P on the conic and will demand follow-up algebra (tangent, normal, locus) — so I need a representation of P that is automatically on the curve.
  2. Consider the Cartesian option (x₁, y₁) with side-condition x₁²/a² + y₁²/b² = 1: rule it out because the constraint must be re-substituted at every step and there is no clean way to eliminate it later.
  3. Match the conic's algebraic structure to a known identity: x²/a² + y²/b² = 1 mirrors cos²θ + sin²θ = 1, while x²/a² − y²/b² = 1 mirrors sec²t − tan²t = 1. This pattern-match tells me the right parameter family.
  4. Commit to the parametric form because Edexcel FP3 tangent/normal formulae are stated parametrically and the available identity will drive the eventual elimination.
  5. Choose conventions deliberately: x-coordinate gets cos θ (or sec t) because the positive x²/a² term pairs with the positive squared trig function; this matches the formula booklet so signs in tangent/normal results come out right.
  6. Write P explicitly with the chosen parameter and verify by substitution that the conic equation reduces to the identity, then proceed with all later working in θ (or t) only.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the conic and its semi-axes: e.g. ellipse x²/a² + y²/b² = 1, hyperbola x²/a² − y²/b² = 1, parabola y² = 4ax, rectangular hyperbola xy = c².
  2. Select the matching parametrisation: ellipse → P(a cos θ, b sin θ); hyperbola → P(a sec t, b tan t); parabola → P(at², 2at); rectangular hyperbola → P(ct, c/t).
  3. Verify by substitution: e.g. (a cos θ)²/a² + (b sin θ)²/b² = cos²θ + sin²θ = 1 ✓.
  4. Replace every (x₁, y₁) reference in the working with the parametric coordinates and continue all later derivations (gradient dy/dx, tangent equation, intercepts) as functions of the single parameter.

Worked Micro-Example

Ellipse $\frac{x^2}{9}+\frac{y^2}{4}=1$, find tangent at general point $P$. Set $P=(3\cos\theta,\,2\sin\theta)$. Check: $\frac{9\cos^2\theta}{9}+\frac{4\sin^2\theta}{4}=\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta=1$ ✓. Tangent (formula booklet): $\frac{x\cos\theta}{3}+\frac{y\sin\theta}{2}=1$ — now a single-parameter object ready for intercepts or locus elimination.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Hyperbola x²/a² − y²/b² = 1
Use P(a sec t, b tan t); the companion identity is sec²t − tan²t = 1. (Hyperbolic form (a cosh u, b sinh u) is mathematically equivalent but only used when the question is set in hyperbolic coordinates.)
Parabola y² = 4ax
Use P(at², 2at); elimination later uses direct algebraic substitution rather than a trig identity.
Rectangular hyperbola xy = c²
Use P(ct, c/t); the identity baked in is simply (ct)(c/t) = c².
Two distinct points on the same conic (chord problems)
Use distinct parameters, e.g. P(a cos α, b sin α) and Q(a cos β, b sin β), then exploit sum-to-product identities for the chord equation.

Common Misreadings

  • Using (a sin θ, b cos θ) instead of (a cos θ, b sin θ): mathematically valid but inconsistent with the formula booklet, leading to sign errors in tangent/normal formulae.
  • Reaching for (a cosh u, b sinh u) on a hyperbola when the Edexcel-standard (a sec t, b tan t) is expected.
  • Mistaking a 'point on the curve' question for one needing implicit differentiation in (x, y) — implicit differentiation works but produces messier algebra than parametric differentiation here.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting that sec t and tan t are undefined at t = π/2, so any limiting/asymptotic case for the hyperbola must be argued separately.
  • Mixing up which coordinate carries cos and which carries sin: the x-coordinate matches the positive x²/a² term and so receives a cos θ (or sec t) by convention.
  • Dropping the negative-quadrant cases: as θ ranges over [0, 2π), cos θ and sin θ change sign, so one must not assume P is restricted to the first quadrant unless the question says so.
  • After parametrising, forgetting to eliminate the parameter at the final locus step — the whole point of choosing trig was to use the Pythagorean identity to eliminate θ.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP3) coordinate-geometry of conics; the parametric forms (a cos θ, b sin θ), (a sec t, b tan t), (at², 2at), (ct, c/t) and the corresponding tangent/normal equations are in the FP3 formula booklet. Avoid introducing hyperbolic parametrisations (a cosh u, b sinh u) unless the question is explicitly framed in hyperbolic terms, as this can confuse students about which formula booklet entry to invoke.
