Differential Equations

Rewrite tan y as sin y / cos y to separate variables

Family: Separation Of Variables Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The ODE has the form dy/dx = (trig function of y) · h(x), e.g. dy/dx = tan y / [(1+3x)(1−x)], where the RHS factorises cleanly into a pure-y part and a pure-x part. This is the hallmark of a separable equation. The trig factor in y (tan, cot, sec, etc.) signals that rewriting in terms of sin y and cos y will produce a recognisable standard integral after separation. An integrating factor is unavailable because the equation is nonlinear in y.

Motivation — Why this works

Any separable ODE f(y) dy/dx = g(x) integrates directly as ∫f(y) dy = ∫g(x) dx. Dividing by tan y (i.e. multiplying by cot y = cos y / sin y) places all y-terms on the left and all x-terms on the right, reducing the ODE to two independent integrals. The form cos y / sin y is preferred because ∫cos y / sin y dy = ln|sin y| is a standard f'(y)/f(y) integral. Attempting a linear-ODE method would fail since tan y is nonlinear.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the RHS factorises as (function of y) × (function of x) — a textbook separable structure.
  2. I rule out an integrating factor: the ODE is nonlinear in y because tan y appears, so the linear-ODE machinery does not apply.
  3. I commit to separation because both sides will yield standard integrals — the x-side via partial fractions, the y-side via the f'/f form.
  4. To bridge to computation, I divide by tan y, writing cot y dy = h(x) dx, and rewrite cot y as cos y / sin y to expose the f'(y)/f(y) pattern.
  5. I check the initial condition does not lie on a singularity of cot y (i.e. sin y ≠ 0 there), so the separation is valid on the relevant interval.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Divide both sides by tan y to get cot y dy/dx = 1/[(1+3x)(1−x)].
  2. Rewrite cot y as cos y / sin y so the y-integrand has the form f'(y)/f(y).
  3. Multiply by dx to obtain (cos y / sin y) dy = 1/[(1+3x)(1−x)] dx.
  4. Apply the partial fractions from part (a): (cos y / sin y) dy = [3/(4(1+3x)) + 1/(4(1−x))] dx.
  5. Integrate both sides: ln|sin y| = (1/4)ln|1+3x| − (1/4)ln|1−x| + C.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve dy/dx = tan y / [(1+3x)(1−x)] with y = π/2 at x = 0. Separate: (cos y / sin y) dy = [3/(4(1+3x)) + 1/(4(1−x))] dx. Integrate: ln|sin y| = (1/4)ln|(1+3x)/(1−x)| + C. At x=0, y=π/2: ln 1 = 0, so C = 0, giving sin^4 y = (1+3x)/(1−x).

Variants — Same move, different surface

dy/dx = cot y · g(x)
Multiply by tan y = sin y / cos y; the y-integral becomes −ln|cos y|.
dy/dx = sec y · g(x) or sec²y · g(x)
Move sec y across by multiplying by cos y; integrate cos y dy = sin y, or for sec² y use 1/sec²y = cos²y and double-angle.
dy/dx = sin y cos y · g(x)
Separate to dy/(sin y cos y) = g(x) dx; rewrite using 2/sin(2y) or split as (sec²y / tan y) dy → ln|tan y|.
dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x) (linear)
Not separable in general — use integrating factor e^{∫P dx} instead.
dy/dx = f(y/x) (homogeneous)
Substitute v = y/x to convert to a separable ODE in v and x.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating tan y as if it were tan x and trying to integrate it on the x-side.
  • Attempting an integrating factor because the ODE 'looks linear', missing the nonlinearity in y.
  • Substituting u = tan y unnecessarily when direct separation already gives a standard integral.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the constant of integration before applying the initial condition.
  • Dropping absolute value signs in ln|sin y|, which can cause sign errors when solving for y.
  • Failing to check that sin y ≠ 0 at the initial condition, so that division by tan y is legitimate.
  • Mishandling the chain rule: ∫cos y / sin y dy = ln|sin y|, not ln|cos y|.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P4/FP-level work on first-order separable ODEs, standard trig identities, partial fractions, and ln-type integrals.
