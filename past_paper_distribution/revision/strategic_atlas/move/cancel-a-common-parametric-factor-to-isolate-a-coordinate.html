Algebra & Functions

Cancel a common parametric factor to isolate a coordinate

Family: Parameter Cancellation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

After substituting a coordinate condition (e.g. $y=0$) into a parametric tangent or normal equation, every term carries a common factor of the parameter $t$ (or $-t$, $ct$, etc.). The equation typically looks like $-6t = -t(x - 3t^2)$ or $ty = 3t^2$, with the parameter appearing once in each term. The goal is to solve for the remaining spatial variable $x$ or $y$ in terms of $t$, and brute expansion would reintroduce avoidable algebra.

Motivation — Why this works

Dividing both sides by the common parametric factor collapses a product-form equation into a clean linear equation in the target coordinate. This works because the parameter is a non-zero scalar on a generic point of the curve, so the cancellation is reversible. The move is essentially the distributive law run in reverse, exploiting that the substitution $y=0$ (or similar) does not itself force $t=0$. Stating $t\neq 0$ before dividing is mandatory in 'show that' questions for full marks.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice every term in the equation contains $t$ as a factor, so the equation is degenerate in $t$.
  2. I rule out expanding the bracket and collecting like powers of $t$ — that path is longer and invites sign errors.
  3. I commit to dividing through by the common factor because it directly produces the target form for $x$ (or $y$).
  4. Before dividing, I check that $t\neq 0$ is consistent with the geometry (the point of tangency is not the vertex/origin) and state it explicitly.
  5. I track the sign of the divisor carefully: dividing $-6t$ by $-t$ gives $+6$, not $-6$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the common parametric factor on both sides (here $-t$).
  2. State the assumption $t\neq 0$ explicitly.
  3. Divide every term by that factor, watching signs.
  4. Rearrange the resulting linear equation to express the target coordinate in terms of $t$.
  5. Compare with the required form to confirm.

Worked Micro-Example

Given the normal $y - 3t^2 \cdot \tfrac{1}{?} \ldots$ reduces to $-6t = -t(x - 3t^2)$ at $y=0$. Assuming $t\neq 0$, divide by $-t$: $6 = x - 3t^2$. Hence $x = 3t^2 + 6$, as required.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Tangent to parabola $y^2 = 4ax$ meeting an axis: $ty = x + at^2$ with $x=0$ gives $ty = at^2$.
Divide by $t$ (with $t\neq 0$) to get $y = at$.
Rectangular hyperbola $xy=c^2$ with parametrisation $(ct, c/t)$: tangent equations often share a factor of $t$ or $c$.
Cancel $c$ or $t$ after stating non-zero, then solve for the remaining coordinate.
Chord of contact equations where both sides carry a parameter difference $(t_1 - t_2)$.
State $t_1\neq t_2$ (distinct points) and divide through to obtain a linear relation.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $-t(x-3t^2)$ as $-tx - 3t^3$ (sign error on the second term).
  • Believing the substitution $y=0$ forces $t=0$, and so refusing to divide by $t$.
  • Dividing only one term by $-t$ instead of the whole equation.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to state $t\neq 0$ before dividing — typically costs the final 'show that' mark.
  • Sign error: $-6t \div (-t) = 6$, not $-6$.
  • Dropping the constant on the right (e.g. forgetting to divide $3t^3$ by $-t$ to get $-3t^2$).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard for Edexcel IAL Further Pure parametric coordinate geometry (parabola, ellipse, hyperbola, rectangular hyperbola). The non-zero parameter caveat is routinely required in 'show that' style questions.
