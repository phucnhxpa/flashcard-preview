Trigger — When to use

A question explicitly asks to 'show on an Argand diagram' or 'represent on an Argand diagram' a set of complex numbers, typically the roots of a polynomial just found in earlier parts. The cue is a list of complex values $a+bi$ to be displayed geometrically. May also appear after solving $z^n = w$ or factorising a quartic over $\mathbb{C}$.

Motivation — Why this works

An Argand diagram identifies the complex number $a+bi$ with the Cartesian point $(a,b)$, where the real part is the horizontal coordinate and the imaginary part is the vertical coordinate. This bijection between $\mathbb{C}$ and $\mathbb{R}^2$ turns algebraic features into geometric ones: conjugate pairs become reflections in the real axis, real numbers lie on the horizontal axis, and purely imaginary numbers lie on the vertical axis. Mark schemes reward correctly placed and labelled points together with axis labels.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices each root is given (or can be written) in the form $a+bi$ with real $a,b$.
  2. Rules out polar/modulus-argument plotting since the question gives Cartesian-form roots and only asks for placement, not arcs or circles.
  3. Commits to the standard convention: horizontal axis = Re, vertical axis = Im, so $a+bi \mapsto (a,b)$.
  4. Bridges to drawing axes labelled Re and Im, then placing each root one at a time with its label written next to the point.
  5. Tactically chooses a scale that fits the largest $|a|$ and $|b|$ values, and visually checks that conjugate pairs appear symmetric about the real axis as a self-consistency check.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Draw two perpendicular axes; label horizontal as Re (or Re($z$)) and vertical as Im (or Im($z$)).
  2. Choose a scale that accommodates the largest real and imaginary parts among the roots.
  3. For each root $a+bi$, mark the point $(a,b)$ with a clear dot or cross.
  4. Label each plotted point with the corresponding complex value (e.g. $3i$, $-3i$, $8$, $2$).
  5. Verify visually: conjugate pairs should be mirror images in the Re axis; real roots should lie on the Re axis.

Worked Micro-Example

Roots of $z^4 - 10z^3 + 25z^2 - 90z + 144 = 0$ are $z = 8,\ 2,\ 3i,\ -3i$. On the Argand diagram with Re horizontal and Im vertical, plot $(8,0)$ labelled $8$, $(2,0)$ labelled $2$, $(0,3)$ labelled $3i$, and $(0,-3)$ labelled $-3i$. The pair $3i,\ -3i$ is symmetric about the Re axis, confirming the conjugate root theorem.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Roots with both nonzero real and imaginary parts, e.g. $2 \pm 3i$
Plot $(2,3)$ and $(2,-3)$ in the interior of the right half-plane; they remain mirror-symmetric about the Re axis.
$n$th roots of a complex number $z^n = w$
Plot the $n$ roots; they lie equally spaced on a circle of radius $|w|^{1/n}$ centred at the origin — optionally sketch the circle as a visual aid.
Loci such as $|z - a| = r$ or $\arg(z - a) = \theta$
Switch from plotting discrete points to drawing the corresponding circle, half-line, or perpendicular bisector on the same Re-Im axes.
A single complex number to be illustrated as a vector
Draw an arrow from the origin to $(a,b)$ rather than a single point, and label the tip with $z = a+bi$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the Argand diagram as a normal $xy$-plane and writing coordinates as $(\mathrm{Re}, \mathrm{Im})$ but labelling the axes $x$ and $y$ instead of Re and Im.
  • Reading $bi$ as a real-axis quantity and plotting $(b,0)$ for a purely imaginary root.
  • Assuming polar form is required and trying to mark modulus/argument instead of Cartesian coordinates.

Pitfalls

  • Swapping the axes (placing Re vertically and Im horizontally).
  • Forgetting to label the axes Re and Im, or omitting point labels — both cost presentation marks.
  • Plotting $(3,0)$ for $3i$ instead of $(0,3)$, i.e. confusing real and imaginary components.
  • Using inconsistent or unmarked scales so conjugate symmetry is not visually evident.
  • Drawing only dots without writing the corresponding complex values next to them.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope (FP1/FP2): Argand diagram representation of complex numbers in Cartesian form, with conjugate-pair symmetry as a standard visual check.
