Trigonometry

Bridge tan t and sec²t parametric equations via 1+tan²t=sec²t

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when parametric equations have one component containing tan t (often linearly, like x = a + b tan t) and the other containing sec²t (like y = c sec²t or y = c + d sec²t), and the task asks to eliminate t for a Cartesian equation. Also triggers when only sec t appears but it can be squared cleanly. The cue is the simultaneous presence of tan and sec — the Pythagorean pairing — rather than sin/cos.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity 1 + tan²t = sec²t is the unique Pythagorean relation linking exactly tan and sec, so it eliminates t in one substitution without introducing fresh trig functions. Because tan t can be solved linearly from the x-equation, squaring it slots directly into sec²t, producing a polynomial (typically quadratic) Cartesian form. Any other identity would either reintroduce sin/cos or require an extra elimination step.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the parametric pair uses tan t in one equation and sec²t in the other — a Pythagorean pairing.
  2. Rules out converting both to sin/cos (messier) and rules out double-angle/compound-angle identities (wrong functions).
  3. Commits to 1 + tan²t = sec²t because it bridges exactly these two functions in one step.
  4. Plans to isolate tan t from the x-equation, then substitute tan²t into the rewritten y-equation.
  5. Decides to keep the (x − h)/k form un-expanded if the target answer is in completed-square form a(x+b)² + c.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Isolate tan t from the x-equation: tan t = (x − h)/k.
  2. Rewrite the y-equation using sec²t = 1 + tan²t.
  3. Substitute tan²t = ((x − h)/k)² into y.
  4. Simplify to the requested Cartesian form (expanded polynomial or a(x+b)² + c).
  5. State the domain of x by tracking the range of tan t over the given t-interval, if required.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $x = 5 + 2\tan t$, $y = 8\sec^2 t$. Then $\tan t = \tfrac{x-5}{2}$, so $y = 8(1 + \tan^2 t) = 8\!\left(1 + \tfrac{(x-5)^2}{4}\right) = 8 + 2(x-5)^2$. Hence $y = 2(x-5)^2 + 8$, giving $a=2$, $b=-5$, $c=8$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

x = a + b sin t, y = c + d cos²t (or cos 2t)
Use sin²t + cos²t = 1 or cos 2t = 1 − 2sin²t to bridge directly.
x = a cos t, y = b sin t (ellipse)
Apply cos²t + sin²t = 1 after dividing: (x/a)² + (y/b)² = 1.
x = a + b cot t, y = c csc²t
Use 1 + cot²t = csc²t — the cot/csc Pythagorean partner.
Only sec t appears (not sec²t)
Square the y-equation first to obtain sec²t, then apply the identity.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to express both equations in terms of sin t and cos t separately instead of spotting the direct sec²–tan² bridge.
  • Reaching for cos 2t or double-angle identities when a single Pythagorean identity suffices.
  • Treating sec²t as 1/cos²t and chasing cos t from the x-equation — much messier.

Pitfalls

  • Squaring (x−5)/2 incorrectly as (x−5)²/2 instead of (x−5)²/4.
  • Sign error matching the form a(x+b)² + c: writing b = +5 instead of b = −5.
  • Forgetting to multiply the constant 1 by the outer coefficient (e.g., dropping the 8 in 8(1 + tan²t)).
  • Omitting domain restrictions on x when t is restricted (e.g., t ∈ (−π/2, π/2)).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure (parametric equations in P3/P4) using the Pythagorean identity 1 + tan²t = sec²t from C2/P2. No content beyond syllabus.
