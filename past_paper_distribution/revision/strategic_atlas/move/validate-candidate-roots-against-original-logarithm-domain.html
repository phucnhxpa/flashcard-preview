Algebra & Functions

Validate candidate roots against original logarithm domain

Family: Extraneous Solution Rejection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a logarithmic (or ln) equation has been converted into a polynomial equation via the power, product, or quotient laws and candidate roots have been obtained. Particularly urgent when the power law $\log_b(A^2) = 2\log_b A$ has been used, since squaring absorbs sign information. Also triggered when arguments of the original logs are linear (or otherwise sign-dependent) expressions in $x$. Any time the algebraic equation could have more roots than the log equation, this check is mandatory.

Motivation — Why this works

The domain of $\log_b(A)$ requires $A>0$, but the polynomial obtained after applying log laws does not encode this restriction. Steps such as $2\log_b(1-x) = \log_b(1-x)^2$ silently widen the solution set because $(1-x)^2 > 0$ even when $(1-x)<0$. Therefore every candidate root must be tested against the original arguments, not the simplified ones. At A-level, the final mark is often reserved for an explicit rejection statement.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that the equation originated as a log equation, so any candidate roots are provisional until domain-checked.
  2. Rules out trusting the polynomial alone, recognising that the power law $2\log A = \log A^2$ may have introduced sign-blind roots.
  3. Commits to listing the strict positivity conditions on each original log argument before substituting.
  4. Bridges to computation by writing out each inequality (e.g. $32-12x>0$ and $1-x>0$) and testing each candidate root in turn.
  5. Tactically states the rejection and reason in writing, then declares the surviving root as the final answer.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Return to the original equation and list every argument $A_i$ that appears inside a logarithm.
  2. Write the domain conditions $A_i > 0$ for all $i$.
  3. Substitute each candidate root from the polynomial into every $A_i$.
  4. Reject any root that makes some $A_i \le 0$, stating the reason explicitly.
  5. Accept the remaining root(s) and present them as the final solution.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\log_3(32-12x) = 2\log_3(1-x)$. Equating arguments gives $(1-x)^2 = 32-12x$, i.e. $x^2+10x - 31 + ... $, leading to candidates $x=5/3$ and $x=-1/9$. Domain: need $1-x>0$, i.e. $x<1$. Since $5/3>1$, reject $x=5/3$. Check $x=-1/9$: $1-(-1/9)=10/9>0$ and $32-12(-1/9)=100/3>0$. Hence $x=-1/9$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation in $\ln$ such as $\ln(2x-3)+\ln(x)=\ln 5$
Require each argument $2x-3>0$ and $x>0$ from the original; reject candidates failing either.
Quotient form $\log_b(f(x)) - \log_b(g(x)) = c$
Need both $f(x)>0$ and $g(x)>0$ separately, not merely $f(x)/g(x)>0$.
Power-law use $n\log_b(A) = \log_b(A^n)$ with even $n$
Be especially vigilant: even powers hide the sign of $A$, so test $A>0$ on the original.
Equations mixing logs and surds, e.g. $\log_b(\sqrt{x-1})=\dots$
Add the surd's non-negativity condition $x-1\ge 0$ alongside the log positivity.

Common Misreadings

  • Checking the simplified argument $(1-x)^2$ for positivity instead of the original $(1-x)$.
  • Believing that any algebraic root of the polynomial is automatically a solution of the log equation.
  • Testing only one log's argument and ignoring the others.

Pitfalls

  • Omitting the domain check entirely, costing the final accuracy mark.
  • Stating 'reject' without giving the reason (which arguments fail and why).
  • Using $\ge 0$ instead of $>0$ for log arguments, accidentally allowing $A=0$.
  • Arithmetic slip when substituting fractional candidates into the original arguments.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P2/P3 logarithm work: solving log equations via log laws and rejecting extraneous roots is an explicitly examined skill, with marks tied to a clear rejection statement.
