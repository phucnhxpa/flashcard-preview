Differentiation

Differentiate parametrically via chain rule to obtain dy/dx in the parameter

Family: Parametric Differentiation Frequency: 6 questions

Trigger — When to use

A point on a curve is given in parametric form (x(t), y(t)) — e.g. (3t², 6t), (at², 2at), (8cos θ, 6sin θ), (a cos θ, b sin θ), (ct, c/t), (a sec t, b tan t) — and the question asks for the gradient of the tangent or normal at that point, or to 'show that' a tangent/normal equation in the parameter holds. The cue is that the final required expression is in terms of the parameter, not in x and y. Equivalent phrasings: 'find the equation of the tangent at P(t)', 'show that the normal at P(θ) is …', 'find dy/dx at the point with parameter t'.

Motivation — Why this works

When x and y are both given as functions of a single parameter, the chain rule identity dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) yields the gradient directly in that parameter, with no need to eliminate the parameter or convert to Cartesian form. The alternative — converting to Cartesian and differentiating implicitly — typically produces a gradient in x and y that must then be re-substituted in terms of the parameter, doubling the algebra. Parametric differentiation preserves the parametric structure that downstream parts of the question (tangent equation, normal equation, intersection with axes) will need.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the point P is given parametrically as (x(t), y(t)) and the target expression (gradient, tangent equation, normal equation) is required in terms of the parameter.
  2. Consider the alternative: eliminate the parameter to get a Cartesian relation (e.g. y² = 12x or x²/a² + y²/b² = 1) and differentiate implicitly. This gives dy/dx in x and y, and a re-substitution of the parametric coordinates is then forced — strictly more work.
  3. Commit to parametric differentiation because dx/dt and dy/dt are usually one-term derivatives, and the quotient is immediate.
  4. Bridge to operation: write dx/dt and dy/dt separately, being careful with sign conventions (d/dθ of cos θ is −sin θ) and with constants (a, c are constants, not variables).
  5. Form dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt). If a normal is wanted, take the negative reciprocal m_normal = −(dx/dt)/(dy/dt).
  6. Sanity check: the resulting gradient is a function of the parameter only, ready to slot into y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) using the parametric (x₁, y₁).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify x = x(t) and y = y(t) from the given parametric point.
  2. Differentiate to obtain dx/dt, treating any letters like a, b, c as constants.
  3. Differentiate to obtain dy/dt similarly.
  4. Form dy/dx = (dy/dt) ÷ (dx/dt) and simplify.
  5. If the normal gradient is needed, compute m_normal = −1 / (dy/dx) = −(dx/dt)/(dy/dt).
  6. Check the parameter value where dx/dt = 0 (vertical tangent) is excluded.

Worked Micro-Example

Curve: $x = at^2,\; y = 2at$. Then $\dfrac{dx}{dt} = 2at$ and $\dfrac{dy}{dt} = 2a$, so $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{2a}{2at} = \dfrac{1}{t}$. The tangent at $P(at^2, 2at)$ is therefore $y - 2at = \tfrac{1}{t}(x - at^2)$, i.e. $ty = x + at^2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Parabola x = at², y = 2at (or x = 3t², y = 6t)
dy/dx = 2a/(2at) = 1/t — gradient is the reciprocal of the parameter.
Ellipse x = a cos θ, y = b sin θ
dx/dθ = −a sin θ, dy/dθ = b cos θ, so dy/dx = −(b cos θ)/(a sin θ). Mind the negative sign from differentiating cos θ.
Rectangular hyperbola x = ct, y = c/t
dx/dt = c, dy/dt = −c/t², so dy/dx = −1/t².
Hyperbola x = a sec t, y = b tan t
dx/dt = a sec t tan t, dy/dt = b sec² t, so dy/dx = b sec t/(a tan t) = b/(a sin t).
Normal rather than tangent required
After computing dy/dx, take the negative reciprocal m_normal = −(dx/dt)/(dy/dt) and use it in the point-gradient form.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the curve as Cartesian and differentiating implicitly, then forgetting to re-substitute the parameter — produces a gradient in x and y that doesn't match the required form.
  • Confusing the request for a normal with a tangent and using dy/dx directly without taking the negative reciprocal.
  • Treating the constants a, b, c as variables to be differentiated.

Pitfalls

  • Dividing the wrong way round: forming dx/dy = (dx/dt)/(dy/dt) instead of dy/dx.
  • Sign error from differentiating cos θ (gives −sin θ) — dropping the minus flips the gradient sign.
  • Forgetting that dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) is invalid where dx/dt = 0 (vertical tangents, e.g. t = 0 on a parabola).
  • Algebraic slip when simplifying the quotient, especially with trigonometric forms.

A-level Scope Notes

This is the standard parametric differentiation result in the Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics formula booklet (P3/P4 chain rule application). It stays within scope for tangent/normal questions on conics and parametrically defined curves; do not invoke higher-order parametric formulae (d²y/dx² for parametric curves) unless explicitly asked, as the formula is more delicate than a naive second derivative.
