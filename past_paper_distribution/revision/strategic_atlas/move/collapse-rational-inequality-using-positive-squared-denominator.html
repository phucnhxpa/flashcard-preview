Algebra & Functions

Collapse rational inequality using positive squared denominator

Family: Sign Reduction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a rational expression P(x)/Q(x) must be sign-analysed and Q(x) is a perfect even power, e.g. (x-a)^2, (x^2+1)^2, or v(x)^2 from a quotient-rule derivative. Cue phrases include 'find where f'(x) > 0', 'solve f(x) ≥ 0', or 'determine intervals of increase' when the denominator is visibly squared. Also fires when Q(x) = x^2 + c with c > 0 (always positive). The key recognition is that Q(x) cannot change sign on the domain.

Motivation — Why this works

If Q(x) > 0 on the entire domain, then P(x)/Q(x) and P(x) share the same sign everywhere, so the inequality P(x)/Q(x) > 0 is equivalent to P(x) > 0. This is just the rule 'dividing by a positive number preserves the inequality', applied pointwise. Exploiting it removes one factor from the sign analysis, eliminates the spurious critical point at the zero of Q, and avoids errors at vertical asymptotes.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the denominator is a perfect square (or even power), so it is ≥ 0 automatically.
  2. Rule out the zero of the denominator by checking it is excluded from the domain, confirming Q(x) > 0 strictly.
  3. Commit to dropping the denominator entirely: the inequality reduces to a polynomial sign problem on the numerator.
  4. Translate P(x)/Q(x) > 0 into P(x) > 0 (and analogously for ≥, <, ≤, remembering equality cases).
  5. Solve the resulting polynomial inequality by factor analysis or critical points, then re-attach the domain restriction x ≠ (zero of Q).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify Q(x) as an even power [g(x)]^{2n} or otherwise verify Q(x) > 0 on the domain.
  2. State explicitly: Q(x) > 0 for all x ≠ (its zeros), and confirm those zeros are excluded.
  3. Replace the rational inequality P(x)/Q(x) > 0 with the polynomial inequality P(x) > 0.
  4. Solve P(x) > 0 by factoring and a sign diagram or critical-points method.
  5. Write the final solution set, excluding any x where Q(x) = 0.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $f'(x) = \dfrac{(2x+5)(2x-17)}{(x-3)^2} > 0$. Since $(x-3)^2 > 0$ for all $x \neq 3$, the inequality reduces to $(2x+5)(2x-17) > 0$. Roots are $x = -\tfrac{5}{2}$ and $x = \tfrac{17}{2}$, so the solution is $x < -\tfrac{5}{2}$ or $x > \tfrac{17}{2}$ (both already satisfy $x \neq 3$).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quotient-rule derivative $f'(x) = \frac{u'v - uv'}{v^2}$
The denominator $v^2$ is automatically a square; reduce sign analysis to the numerator $u'v - uv'$.
Denominator is $x^2 + k$ with $k > 0$
Not a perfect square, but still strictly positive; same collapse to numerator-only analysis.
Inequality involves $\geq 0$ instead of $> 0$
Allow numerator equal to zero, but still exclude zeros of the denominator from the solution set.
Denominator is $[g(x)]^4$ or higher even power
Same argument: even power is non-negative, zero only at roots of $g$, which are excluded.
Stationary-point classification using $f''(x) = P(x)/[g(x)]^{2n}$
Sign of $f''$ at a stationary point equals sign of $P(x)$ there; denominator can be ignored.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $(x-3)^2$ as if it could be negative and including $x = 3$ as a sign-changing critical point.
  • Believing the rational inequality requires the same treatment as $P(x)Q(x) > 0$ (it doesn't, when Q is squared).
  • Confusing 'denominator is always positive' with 'denominator is always defined' — the zero of Q must still be excluded.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to exclude the zero of the denominator from the final solution set.
  • Multiplying both sides by $(x-3)^2$ without justification in cases where the denominator is not squared (e.g. $(x-3)$ alone), where this move would be invalid.
  • Dropping the denominator when it is an odd power, e.g. $(x-3)^3$, which does change sign.
  • Including $x = 3$ as a critical point in a sign table, producing spurious intervals.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics: appears routinely in P2/P3 work on increasing/decreasing functions, stationary points via the quotient rule, and rational inequalities. No content beyond standard A-level algebra and differentiation.
