Coordinate Geometry

Eliminate the parameter to obtain a Cartesian equation

Family: Parametric Elimination Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a curve or moving point is given in parametric form x = f(t), y = g(t) (or in θ) and the question asks to 'find the Cartesian equation', 'show that y = ...', 'find the locus of', or 'show that the point lies on the curve ...'. Visual cues: two equations, each containing the same parameter t or θ, and the answer demanded contains only x and y. Equivalent phrasings include 'eliminate the parameter', 'express y in terms of x', or 'show that Q lies on ...'.

Motivation — Why this works

A Cartesian equation requires a direct relationship between x and y with no parameter present. Two strategies achieve this: (i) algebraic — invert the simpler/linear parametric equation to express t in terms of one variable, then substitute into the other; (ii) identity-based — when the parametrisation involves trig or reciprocal forms, use a bridging identity (cos²θ + sin²θ = 1, sec²t − tan²t = 1, or multiplying ct · c/t = c²) to fuse the two equations. The choice depends on which parametric equation is easier to invert and whether a standard conic identity is hiding in the structure.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that both coordinates depend on the same parameter, and the target equation must contain only x and y — so the parameter must be removed.
  2. Inspect the two equations and rule out blunt approaches (equating x and y, or dividing them) — these don't isolate the parameter.
  3. Decide between the two elimination routes: if one equation is linear (or a simple reciprocal) in t, isolate t there; if both involve trig/secant/tangent, look for a Pythagorean-type identity instead.
  4. Commit to the route that minimises sign ambiguity and extra rearrangement — e.g. invert y = 4t (gives t = y/4, no ±) rather than x = 2t² (which forces ±√).
  5. Carry out isolation/substitution carefully, combining fractions over a common denominator before substituting to keep the algebra clean.
  6. Simplify to a recognisable Cartesian form (line, parabola y² = 4ax, ellipse x²/a² + y²/b² = 1, hyperbola, or rectangular hyperbola xy = c²) and sanity-check by testing a single parameter value.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify which parametric equation is easier to invert (linear > simple reciprocal > quadratic > trig).
  2. Algebraic route: solve that equation for t, e.g. from x = 3/(t−1) get t = 3/x + 1 = (3+x)/x; or from y = 4t get t = y/4.
  3. Substitute the expression for t into the other equation; combine compound fractions over a common denominator before inverting.
  4. Identity route: from x = a cos θ get cos θ = x/a, from y = b sin θ get sin θ = y/b, then apply cos²θ + sin²θ = 1 to get x²/a² + y²/b² = 1.
  5. For hyperbola parametrisations (a sec t, b tan t) use sec²t − tan²t = 1; for rectangular hyperbola (ct, c/t) simply multiply to get xy = c².
  6. Simplify the resulting equation to the form requested ('show that' target) and state any restrictions on x or y arising from the parameter's range.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $x = 2t^2$, $y = 4t$. From the linear equation $t = y/4$. Substitute: $x = 2(y/4)^2 = y^2/8$. Hence $y^2 = 8x$ — a parabola.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Both parametric equations are simple reciprocals, e.g. x = 3/(t−1), y = 1/(t+2)
Invert one equation for t, add/subtract the constant offset to get t, substitute into the other, then combine the resulting compound fraction over a single denominator.
Trig parametrisation of an ellipse: x = a cos θ, y = b sin θ
Isolate cos θ = x/a, sin θ = y/b, then use cos²θ + sin²θ = 1 to get x²/a² + y²/b² = 1 directly.
Hyperbola parametrisation: x = a sec t, y = b tan t
Use sec²t − tan²t = 1 to obtain x²/a² − y²/b² = 1.
Rectangular hyperbola: x = ct, y = c/t
Multiply the coordinates: xy = c² — no rearrangement needed.
Parabola: x = at², y = 2at
From the linear equation t = y/(2a); substitute to get y² = 4ax.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to equate x and y or divide one by the other without isolating t first.
  • Using brute-force algebra on a trig parametrisation when a Pythagorean identity would eliminate θ in one line.
  • Isolating t from the quadratic equation (introducing ±√) when a linear equation is available.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to square the substituted expression: writing x = 2(y/4) instead of x = 2(y/4)².
  • Failing to combine 3/x + 1 over a common denominator before substituting, leading to messy nested fractions.
  • Sign errors when offsets differ between the two equations (e.g. (t−1) vs (t+2)) — the +2 must use the full expression for t, not t−1.
  • Squaring to invoke an identity can introduce extraneous solutions; check the parameter's domain to restrict x and y appropriately.
  • Mixing up identities: cos²+sin²=1 for ellipse, sec²−tan²=1 for hyperbola.

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 (parametric equations) and FP1/FP3 (conics: parabola y²=4ax, ellipse, hyperbola, rectangular hyperbola xy=c²). Standard parametrisations and the Pythagorean identities are in the formula booklet; avoid out-of-scope general conic theory or projective methods.
