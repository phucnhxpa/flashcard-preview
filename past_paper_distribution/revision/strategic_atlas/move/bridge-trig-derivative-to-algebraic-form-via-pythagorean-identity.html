Trigonometry

Bridge trig derivative to algebraic form via Pythagorean identity

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when implicit or inverse-trig differentiation produces dy/dx in terms of cos y, sin y, or sec²y, but the required answer must be in terms of x only. Typical cues: the original substitution was x = a sin y, x = a tan y, or x = a sec y, and the mark scheme demands a surd form like 1/√(a²−x²). The trigonometric variable must be eliminated, and a Pythagorean identity is the natural bridge because sin y (or tan y, sec y) is already pinned to x.

Motivation — Why this works

The Pythagorean identities cos²y = 1 − sin²y, sec²y = 1 + tan²y, and cot²y = csc²y − 1 connect the trig function appearing in the derivative to the one already expressed in x. Substituting sin y = x/a (or tan y = x/a) directly converts cos y or sec²y into a surd in x. This produces the standard inverse-trig derivative forms and matches the algebraic target shape demanded by the question.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that dy/dx contains a trig function of y, but the answer must be purely in x.
  2. Rules out re-differentiating or solving for y explicitly (y = arcsin(x/2) just restates the problem).
  3. Commits to the Pythagorean identity because the original substitution already gives sin y in terms of x, so cos y is one identity-step away.
  4. Bridges by writing cos²y = 1 − sin²y and substituting sin y = x/2 to get cos²y in terms of x.
  5. Decides the sign of the square root using the principal-value range of y (e.g. y ∈ [−π/2, π/2] gives cos y ≥ 0, so the positive root is taken).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Read off sin y = x/2 (or analogous relation) from the original substitution.
  2. Apply the Pythagorean identity: cos²y = 1 − sin²y = 1 − x²/4 = (4 − x²)/4.
  3. Take the positive square root (justified by the range of y): cos y = √(4 − x²)/2.
  4. Substitute back into dy/dx = 1/(2 cos y) to get dy/dx = 1/√(4 − x²).
  5. Check the result matches the standard derivative of arcsin(x/2).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $x = 2\sin y$, differentiating gives $\frac{dx}{dy} = 2\cos y$, so $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{2\cos y}$. From $\sin y = x/2$: $\cos^2 y = 1 - x^2/4 = (4-x^2)/4$, so $\cos y = \tfrac{1}{2}\sqrt{4-x^2}$ (positive since $y \in [-\pi/2, \pi/2]$). Hence $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4-x^2}}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Inverse tangent: x = a tan y giving dy/dx = 1/(a sec²y)
Use sec²y = 1 + tan²y = 1 + x²/a² to obtain dy/dx = a/(a² + x²).
Inverse secant or x = a sec y type substitution
Use tan²y = sec²y − 1 = x²/a² − 1 to express tan y as √(x² − a²)/a.
Implicit differentiation of x² + y² = r²
Instead of a trig identity, use the circle equation itself to write y = ±√(r² − x²) and resolve sign from the branch.
Integration by trig substitution (reverse direction)
After substituting x = a sin θ, use cos θ = √(1 − sin²θ) = √(a² − x²)/a to simplify √(a² − x²) inside the integrand.

Common Misreadings

  • Thinking the answer can be left in terms of cos y because dy/dx has been 'found'.
  • Believing one must invert y = arcsin(x/2) and differentiate from scratch instead of using the identity bridge.
  • Confusing which Pythagorean identity to use — e.g. reaching for 1 + tan²y when the substitution was x = a sin y.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the factor of a in sin y = x/a, e.g. writing cos²y = 1 − x² instead of 1 − x²/4.
  • Mishandling the surd: √((4 − x²)/4) becomes √(4 − x²)/2, not √(4 − x²)/4.
  • Taking ± without justifying the positive root from the principal range of y.
  • Cancelling the 2 in 2·√(4−x²)/2 incorrectly and ending with 2/√(4−x²).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard technique in Edexcel IAL P3/P4 for deriving the derivatives of inverse trig functions and for trig substitution in integration; uses only the Pythagorean identities and principal-value conventions in the syllabus.
