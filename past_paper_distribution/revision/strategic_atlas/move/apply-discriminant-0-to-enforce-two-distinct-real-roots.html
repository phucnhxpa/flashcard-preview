Algebra & Functions

Apply discriminant > 0 to enforce two distinct real roots

Family: Discriminant Condition Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question hinges on a quadratic (already given, or formed by substituting a line into a curve) and demands 'two distinct real roots', 'two distinct points of intersection', 'the line meets the curve at two distinct points', or equivalent phrasing. The quadratic typically contains an unknown parameter (k, p, m, …) and the question asks you to derive an inequality in that parameter — often a 'show that' target. The cue is the combination of: (i) a quadratic structure ax² + bx + c = 0, and (ii) a root-counting requirement linked to a parameter. Variations of the same trigger include 'has real and distinct solutions', 'crosses the x-axis at two points', or 'cuts the curve twice'.

Motivation — Why this works

For ax² + bx + c = 0 the discriminant Δ = b² − 4ac classifies the roots: Δ > 0 ↔ two distinct real roots, Δ = 0 ↔ one repeated real root, Δ < 0 ↔ no real roots. Imposing Δ > 0 therefore converts the geometric/root-counting condition into a clean algebraic inequality in the parameter. This is almost always the fastest and most rigorous route — completing the square or graphical reasoning are slower and rarely produce the required 'show that' inequality directly.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the keyword 'distinct' (or 'two points of intersection') sitting beside a quadratic — this is the canonical discriminant trigger.
  2. Rule out alternatives: solving the quadratic explicitly is impossible while the parameter is unknown; completing the square or sketching is slower and less marks-friendly for a 'show that' proof.
  3. Commit to Δ = b² − 4ac > 0 because 'distinct' demands strict inequality (Δ ≥ 0 would wrongly include the repeated-root case).
  4. Carefully identify a, b, c from the quadratic in standard form ax² + bx + c = 0, paying attention to signs and to which terms depend on the parameter.
  5. Substitute into Δ > 0, then expand methodically — squaring b first, then computing 4ac with full attention to the factor of 4 and to negative terms inside c.
  6. Aim the simplification toward the target inequality printed in the question; if it is a 'show that', preserve every intermediate line so no marks are lost.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Put the equation in standard form ax² + bx + c = 0 and read off a, b, c.
  2. Write the condition: Δ = b² − 4ac > 0.
  3. Compute b² (watch sign: (k − 8)² = k² − 16k + 64, not k² + 64).
  4. Compute 4ac in full, including the leading 4 (e.g. 4·8·(12p + 15 − 4p²) = 32(12p + 15 − 4p²)).
  5. Subtract: b² − 4ac, distributing the minus sign over every term of 4ac.
  6. Collect like terms and, if needed, divide through by a positive common factor to reach the printed target inequality.

Worked Micro-Example

Line y = kx + 1 meets curve 4x² + y² = 2y at two distinct points → quadratic 4x² + (k−8)x + (1−2k) = 0. Apply Δ > 0: $(k-8)^2 - 4(4)(1-2k) > 0$ ⟹ $k^2 - 16k + 64 - 16 + 32k > 0$ ⟹ $k^2 + 16k + 48 > 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line is tangent to the curve (one point of contact)
Set Δ = 0 instead of Δ > 0; the rest of the algebra is identical.
Line does not meet the curve
Set Δ < 0.
Parameter sits in the leading coefficient or in trig form (e.g. a = sin θ)
Same Δ-logic; the resulting inequality may be trigonometric or require separate care that a ≠ 0 (so the equation is genuinely quadratic).
Quadratic given in non-standard order (e.g. constants on the right)
Rearrange to ax² + bx + c = 0 first before identifying a, b, c.

Common Misreadings

  • Using Δ ≥ 0 instead of Δ > 0 — 'distinct' strictly excludes the repeated-root case.
  • Trying to solve the quadratic for x explicitly while the parameter is still unknown.
  • Misidentifying a, b, c when the quadratic is not written in standard form, or when the parameter sits inside more than one coefficient.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors expanding (k − 8)² — students sometimes write k² + 64 (missing cross term) or +16k instead of −16k.
  • Dropping the factor of 4 in 4ac, e.g. writing 4·(1 − 2k) instead of 4·4·(1 − 2k).
  • Failing to distribute the minus sign across every term of 4ac, especially when c contains negatives like (12p + 15 − 4p²).
  • Skipping intermediate algebra in 'show that' questions and losing method marks even when the final inequality is correct.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 scope; the discriminant b² − 4ac is assumed knowledge and not in the formula booklet. Avoid invoking results about complex roots beyond 'no real roots' — Further Pure complex-root theory is not needed here.
