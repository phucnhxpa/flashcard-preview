Strategic Atlas / Integration / Self Referencing Ibp
Integration

Apply IBP twice to recover the original integral and solve algebraically

Family: Self Referencing Ibp Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integrand is a product of $e^x$ (or $e^{kx}$) with a trigonometric power $\sin^n x$ or $\cos^n x$, and a single IBP fails to close because the trig factor only loses one degree. A reduction formula linking $I_n$ to $I_{n-2}$ is requested, signalling that two IBP rounds are needed. The exponential's self-reproducing antiderivative is the key cue: after two rounds, $I_n$ must reappear on the right-hand side.

Motivation — Why this works

Each IBP applied with $dv = e^x\,dx$ preserves the $e^x$ factor while differentiating the trig power, so after two rounds the trig factor has been differentiated twice — producing both $\sin^{n-2}x$ (via $\cos^2 x = 1-\sin^2 x$) and $\sin^n x$ terms. The $\sin^n x$ piece is precisely $I_n$ again, so the integral equation becomes algebraic: $I_n = (\text{boundary}) + cI_{n-2} + dI_n$, which rearranges into a reduction formula. This 'loop closure' is the hallmark of self-referencing IBP.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the integrand is $e^x \sin^n x$ and the target is a reduction $I_n \to I_{n-2}$, so the power must drop by 2.
  2. I rule out a single IBP (drops the power by only 1) and the DI/tabular method (it only terminates for polynomial × exponential).
  3. I commit to double IBP because $e^x$ is its own antiderivative, guaranteeing the exponential survives both rounds and the trig factor is differentiated twice.
  4. First operation: take $u = \sin^n x$, $dv = e^x\,dx$, anticipating that the resulting $\int e^x \sin^{n-1}x \cos x\,dx$ will be tackled by a second IBP with the same $dv$ choice.
  5. Tactical decision: keep $dv = e^x\,dx$ both times (never swap roles), and after the second IBP collect $I_n$ terms on the left so that dividing by $(1+n^2)$ yields the reduction formula.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. First IBP with $u=\sin^n x$, $dv=e^x\,dx$: $I_n = e^x \sin^n x - n\int e^x \sin^{n-1}x \cos x\,dx$.
  2. Let $J = \int e^x \sin^{n-1}x \cos x\,dx$ and apply a second IBP with $u = \sin^{n-1}x\cos x$, $dv = e^x\,dx$.
  3. Differentiate via product rule: $du = [(n-1)\sin^{n-2}x\cos^2 x - \sin^n x]\,dx$.
  4. Obtain $J = e^x \sin^{n-1}x\cos x - \int e^x[(n-1)\sin^{n-2}x\cos^2 x - \sin^n x]\,dx$.
  5. Replace $\cos^2 x = 1 - \sin^2 x$ to express $J$ in terms of $I_n$ and $I_{n-2}$.
  6. Substitute $J$ back into $I_n$ and collect: $(1+n^2)I_n = e^x\sin^{n-1}x(\sin x - n\cos x) + n(n-1)I_{n-2}$.
  7. Divide through by $1+n^2$ to obtain the reduction formula.

Worked Micro-Example

For $I_1 = \int e^x \sin x\,dx$: first IBP gives $I_1 = e^x\sin x - \int e^x\cos x\,dx$. Second IBP on $\int e^x\cos x\,dx$ yields $e^x\cos x + \int e^x\sin x\,dx = e^x\cos x + I_1$. Substituting back: $I_1 = e^x\sin x - e^x\cos x - I_1$, so $2I_1 = e^x(\sin x - \cos x)$ and $I_1 = \tfrac{1}{2}e^x(\sin x - \cos x) + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$\int e^x \cos^n x\,dx$ reduction formula
Identical double-IBP structure; the product-rule derivative produces $\sin^2 x = 1-\cos^2 x$ instead, leading to $(1+n^2)I_n$ on the left.
$\int e^{kx}\sin x\,dx$ or $\int e^{kx}\cos x\,dx$ (definite or indefinite)
Same loop closes in exactly two IBPs; the algebraic factor becomes $(1+k^2)$ instead of $(1+n^2)$.
$\int \sin^n x\,dx$ reduction (no exponential)
Only one IBP is needed, combined with the Pythagorean identity $\cos^2 x = 1-\sin^2 x$ to regenerate $I_n$.
Definite integral $\int_0^{\pi/2} e^x \sin^n x\,dx$
Same algebra; evaluate boundary terms at the limits before dividing by $(1+n^2)$.

Common Misreadings

  • Attempting the tabular (DI) method — it only terminates for polynomial × exponential, not trig-power × exponential.
  • Trying a single IBP and expecting the $I_n \to I_{n-2}$ reduction to emerge in one step.
  • Swapping the roles of $u$ and $dv$ on the second IBP, which undoes the first IBP and returns $I_n$ trivially.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when substituting $J$ back, especially the double negative $-n \times (-I_n)$ that should produce $+n^2 I_n$ (not $-n^2 I_n$).
  • Forgetting to distribute the outer factor $-n$ through every term of $J$, including the boundary piece.
  • Mishandling $\cos^2 x = 1 - \sin^2 x$ and dropping the $I_{n-2}$ contribution.
  • Failing to collect $I_n$ on one side before dividing, leading to a circular non-formula.

A-level Scope Notes
