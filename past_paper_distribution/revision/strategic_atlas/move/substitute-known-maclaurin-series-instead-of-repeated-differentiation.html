Sequences & Series

Substitute known Maclaurin series instead of repeated differentiation

Family: Known Expansion Reuse Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The function to expand is a composition $f(g(x))$ where both $f$ and $g$ have standard Maclaurin series in the formula booklet — e.g. $e^{\sin x}$, $\ln(\cos x)$, $e^{x^2}$, $\sin(\ln(1+x))$. Also fires for products like $x^2\ln(1+x)$ or $e^x\cos x$ where multiplying series is cleaner than differentiating. The question typically asks for the expansion up to a specific power $x^n$.

Motivation — Why this works

Repeated differentiation of a composite function escalates rapidly: each derivative compounds chain, product, and quotient rules, and evaluating at $0$ becomes error-prone by the third or fourth derivative. Substituting $g$'s known polynomial series into $f$'s known series reduces the task to truncated polynomial arithmetic, which is mechanical and self-checking. Because both expansions converge to the same Maclaurin series of $f\circ g$, the coefficients agree, but the workload is a fraction of brute-force differentiation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the function is a composition (or product) of two elementary functions whose Maclaurin series I already know from the booklet.
  2. I rule out direct repeated differentiation: by the third derivative the chain/product rule expressions would be unwieldy, and I would still need to evaluate at $x=0$ cleanly.
  3. I commit to series substitution because it converts calculus into polynomial algebra, and I only need terms up to a fixed power $x^n$.
  4. I plan the truncation level: how many terms of the inner series do I need so that all contributions up to $x^n$ in the outer expansion are captured?
  5. I expand powers of the inner polynomial carefully, dropping any term of total degree $>n$ as soon as it appears, then collect like powers.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the required order $x^n$ from the question.
  2. Write the inner series $u=g(x)$ to sufficient order so that $u^k$ still contributes correctly up to $x^n$.
  3. Write the outer series $f(u)=\sum a_k u^k$ to enough terms (stop once $u^k$ would only produce powers exceeding $x^n$, since $u$ starts at $x^1$ or higher).
  4. Substitute and expand each $u^k$ as a polynomial, discarding terms of degree $>n$ at every stage.
  5. Collect like powers of $x$ and present the truncated Maclaurin series.

Worked Micro-Example

Expand $e^{\sin x}$ up to $x^4$. Use $\sin x = x - \tfrac{x^3}{6}+\cdots$ and $e^u = 1+u+\tfrac{u^2}{2}+\tfrac{u^3}{6}+\tfrac{u^4}{24}+\cdots$. Then $u^2 = x^2 - \tfrac{x^4}{3}+\cdots$, $u^3 = x^3+\cdots$, $u^4 = x^4+\cdots$. So $e^{\sin x} = 1 + \left(x-\tfrac{x^3}{6}\right) + \tfrac{1}{2}\left(x^2-\tfrac{x^4}{3}\right) + \tfrac{x^3}{6} + \tfrac{x^4}{24} = 1 + x + \tfrac{x^2}{2} - \tfrac{x^4}{8}+\cdots$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Product such as $x^2\ln(1+x)$
Take the known series for $\ln(1+x)$ and multiply each term by $x^2$; no substitution needed, just shift powers.
$\ln(\cos x)$ up to $x^4$
Let $u=\cos x - 1 = -\tfrac{x^2}{2}+\tfrac{x^4}{24}-\cdots$ and use $\ln(1+u)=u-\tfrac{u^2}{2}+\cdots$.
$e^{x^2}$
Substitute $u=x^2$ directly into $e^u$; only even powers appear, so half as many terms are needed.
$\tan x = \sin x/\cos x$
Use polynomial long division of the truncated $\sin$ series by the truncated $\cos$ series, or multiply $\sin x$ by the series for $\sec x = 1/\cos x$.
$\sqrt{1+\sin x}$
Use $(1+u)^{1/2} = 1+\tfrac{u}{2}-\tfrac{u^2}{8}+\cdots$ with $u=\sin x$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the problem as requiring repeated differentiation because the question says 'Maclaurin series' — students forget that any valid method giving the correct coefficients is acceptable.
  • Assuming the inner series only needs the first non-zero term, then missing higher contributions when $u^k$ is expanded.

Pitfalls

  • Truncating the inner series too early: e.g. for $e^{\sin x}$ to $x^4$, $\sin x$ must be taken to at least $x^3$ because the $-x^3/6$ term contributes at $x^3$.
  • Dropping cross-terms when squaring or cubing the inner polynomial: e.g. $(x-\tfrac{x^3}{6})^2$ has a $-\tfrac{x^4}{3}$ cross-term, not just $x^2$.
  • Forgetting that $\ln$ is expanded about $1$: writing $\ln(\cos x)$ as 'plug $\cos x$ into $\ln(1+u)$' requires $u=\cos x - 1$, not $u=\cos x$.
  • Including $u^k$ terms whose lowest power already exceeds $n$ — wasted work, but also a sign of not planning the truncation.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL FP2/FP3 Maclaurin series work; uses only standard expansions ($e^x$, $\sin x$, $\cos x$, $\ln(1+x)$, $(1+x)^n$) given in the formula booklet, applied via substitution and truncation.
