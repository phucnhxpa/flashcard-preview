Trigonometry

Apply a Pythagorean identity to homogenise into a single trig function

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an expression or equation contains TWO different trig ratios from the same Pythagorean pair — typically tan² alongside sec² (or cot² alongside cosec², or sin² alongside cos²) — and progress requires a single-variable form. Visual cues: a derivative with mixed sec^n x · tan² x products that you must differentiate again; an equation with tan θ on one side and sec² θ on the other; an integrand mixing tan and sec powers. In short: 'I see two related trig functions; I want one.' Recognise it as the right tool whenever further differentiation, factorisation, or root-finding would be hampered by the mixture but trivial in a single ratio.

Motivation — Why this works

The Pythagorean identities sin²+cos²=1, 1+tan²=sec², and 1+cot²=cosec² let you trade one squared ratio for the other, collapsing a mixed expression into a polynomial in just one trig function. This matters because polynomials in a single ratio behave predictably: powers of sec x differentiate by the chain rule cleanly (d/dx[sec^n x] = n sec^n x · tan x), and polynomials in tan θ become ordinary algebraic equations (often quadratic) solvable by standard methods. Choosing the identity that matches the function ALREADY dominating the problem minimises algebraic clutter and avoids the explosion you'd get from converting everything to sin/cos.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the mixture: the expression contains two different trig ratios (e.g. tan² alongside sec², or sec² alongside tan).
  2. Anticipate the next step (another differentiation, solving for θ, integrating). Ask: 'Will the mixture make that step messier?' If yes, homogenisation is needed.
  3. Rule out converting to sin/cos — that typically introduces rational expressions and products of sin and cos, making things worse.
  4. Decide WHICH ratio to keep: keep the one that already dominates the cleanest side (e.g. if LHS is already in tan θ, convert sec²θ → 1 + tan²θ; if you have sec^n x already, convert tan²x → sec²x − 1).
  5. Apply the correct signed identity (1 + tan² = sec², so tan² = sec² − 1; mind the sign), expand, and collect — the result should be a polynomial in one ratio.
  6. Confirm the payoff: the next operation (differentiation, factoring, quadratic formula) is now routine.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two competing ratios and pick the target ratio T (the one to keep).
  2. Select the matching identity: tan²x = sec²x − 1, sec²x = 1 + tan²x, cot²x = cosec²x − 1, cosec²x = 1 + cot²x, or sin²x = 1 − cos²x.
  3. Substitute the identity into every occurrence of the unwanted ratio.
  4. Expand brackets and collect like powers of T.
  5. Verify the expression is now a polynomial in T alone, then proceed with the intended operation (differentiate, solve, integrate).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $f''(x) = 2\sec^4 x + 4\tan^2 x \sec^2 x$, replace $\tan^2 x = \sec^2 x - 1$: $f''(x) = 2\sec^4 x + 4(\sec^2 x - 1)\sec^2 x = 6\sec^4 x - 4\sec^2 x$. Now $f'''(x) = 24\sec^3 x(\sec x \tan x) - 8\sec x(\sec x \tan x) = 24\sec^4 x \tan x - 8\sec^2 x \tan x$ — clean.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation mixing tan θ (linear) with sec²θ
Use sec²θ = 1 + tan²θ to obtain a quadratic in tan θ, then solve with the quadratic formula.
Differentiating expressions involving cot²x
Use cot²x = cosec²x − 1 to homogenise into cosec x; then d/dx[cosec^n x] = −n cosec^n x · cot x.
Integrating odd powers of tan x
Peel off tan²x = sec²x − 1 to set up a u = tan x or u = sec x substitution.
Equation mixing sin²θ and cos²θ
Use sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 to eliminate whichever ratio leaves the cleanest single-variable polynomial.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating sec²θ = 1/cos²θ and cross-multiplying — produces sin·cos products instead of a clean polynomial.
  • Trying to convert everything to sin/cos as a default, ignoring that a Pythagorean swap is a one-step homogenisation.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error: writing tan²x = sec²x + 1 or sec²x = 1 − tan²x. The correct forms are 1 + tan² = sec² and 1 + cot² = cosec².
  • Applying the identity once but leaving residual mixed terms — every occurrence of the unwanted ratio must be replaced.
  • Choosing the wrong target ratio (converting toward the harder side), making the algebra worse rather than better.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure scope (P2/P3/P4). The Pythagorean identities sin²θ + cos²θ = 1, 1 + tan²θ = sec²θ, 1 + cot²θ = cosec²θ are in the formula booklet. No exotic identities required.
