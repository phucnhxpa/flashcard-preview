Sequences & Series

Eliminate a via the simpler product equation into S∞

Family: Substitution Elimination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Two simultaneous equations in $a$ and $r$ are given for a geometric series — typically a term/product equation like $ar = k$ alongside a sum-to-infinity equation $a/(1-r) = S$. The question asks to 'show that' $r$ satisfies a specific quadratic, so $a$ must be eliminated. The cue is that one of the equations is a clean product (no fractions), making it the obvious source for an expression for $a$. Phrasings include 'the second term is...', 'the product of first two terms is...' combined with 'the sum to infinity is...'.

Motivation — Why this works

Substitution-elimination converts a 2-variable system into a 1-variable equation. Choosing the algebraically simpler equation to isolate $a$ minimises fraction-handling: $ar = k \Rightarrow a = k/r$ is a single division, whereas $a = S(1-r)$ also works but introduces a binomial factor. Substituting into the sum-to-infinity formula then yields a rational equation that clears to a quadratic in $r$, matching the required 'show that' target. Both substitution directions converge to the same quadratic, but starting from the product equation usually produces fewer sign and bracket errors.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice two equations in two unknowns and a target that is a quadratic in $r$ only — so $a$ must go.
  2. I rule out solving the system by elimination of $r$ (target is in $r$) and rule out trial values (a 'show that' demands algebra).
  3. I commit to substitution because the product equation gives $a$ in one step with no brackets.
  4. Bridge: isolate $a = k/r$ from the product equation, ready to drop into the $S_\infty$ formula.
  5. Inside the move, I clear the denominator by multiplying through by $r(1-r)$ in one go, then collect terms to match the required quadratic form (leading coefficient positive).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. From $ar = -6$, isolate $a = -6/r$.
  2. Substitute into $a/(1-r) = 25$: $(-6/r)/(1-r) = 25$.
  3. Combine the denominators: $-6/[r(1-r)] = 25$.
  4. Multiply both sides by $r(1-r)$: $-6 = 25r(1-r) = 25r - 25r^2$.
  5. Rearrange to standard form: $25r^2 - 25r - 6 = 0$.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $ar = -6$ and $\dfrac{a}{1-r} = 25$, write $a = -\dfrac{6}{r}$. Then $\dfrac{-6/r}{1-r} = 25 \Rightarrow \dfrac{-6}{r(1-r)} = 25$. Multiplying by $r(1-r)$ gives $-6 = 25r - 25r^2$, hence $25r^2 - 25r - 6 = 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Sum-to-infinity paired with a specific term, e.g. $u_2 = ar = c$ and $S_\infty = S$
Same move: isolate $a$ from the term equation and substitute into $S_\infty$.
Two terms given, e.g. $ar = p$ and $ar^3 = q$
Divide rather than substitute: $r^2 = q/p$ eliminates $a$ directly without fractions.
Arithmetic sequence with $a$ and $d$ from two conditions (e.g. a term and a sum)
Same pattern: isolate $a$ from the simpler linear equation and substitute into the $S_n$ formula to get an equation in $d$.
Reverse direction: substitute $a = S(1-r)$ from $S_\infty$ into $ar = k$
Yields $S(1-r)r = k$, expanding to the same quadratic; useful when the product equation is messier.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $ar = -6$ as $a + r = -6$ and adding equations instead of substituting.
  • Assuming the question wants numerical $a$ and $r$ immediately, then solving the quadratic before showing it — losing 'show that' marks.
  • Believing $S_\infty$ applies without checking $|r|<1$; for a 'show that' the convergence check is usually deferred but still expected when later identifying the valid root.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when substituting $a = -6/r$ — the negative is easy to drop.
  • Clearing only one factor of the denominator (e.g. multiplying by $r$ but not by $1-r$).
  • Expanding $25r(1-r)$ as $25r - r^2$ instead of $25r - 25r^2$.
  • Leaving the quadratic with a negative leading coefficient and not matching the printed form.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P1/P2 geometric series technique: combines $S_\infty = a/(1-r)$ (with $|r|<1$) and basic simultaneous-equation manipulation, all within the core specification.
