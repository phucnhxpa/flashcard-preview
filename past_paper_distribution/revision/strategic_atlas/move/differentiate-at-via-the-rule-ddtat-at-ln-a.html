Differentiation

Differentiate a^t via the rule d/dt(a^t) = a^t ln a

Family: Exponential Differentiation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a quantity modelled as A = p·q^t (or any constant-base exponential with base ≠ e) needs a rate of change, gradient, or instantaneous derivative. Linguistic cues include 'rate of increase/decrease', 'how fast is ... changing', 'find dA/dt at t = ...', or being asked for a tangent slope on an exponential growth/decay curve. Also fires when a model is given in the form a^{f(t)} with f linear, since the base is constant but not e.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity a^t = e^{t ln a} (valid for a > 0) lets you reduce any exponential to base e, where differentiation is immediate. Applying the chain rule to e^{t ln a} gives e^{t ln a} · ln a = a^t ln a. Constants such as p in A = pq^t pass through linearly, so dA/dt = p q^t ln q. The natural logarithm appears because ln is the unique log whose derivative produces no extra constant.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the phrase 'rate of …' and registers that differentiation with respect to t is required.
  2. Rules out the power rule because the variable t is in the exponent, not the base — q^t is exponential, not polynomial.
  3. Commits to the exponential rule d/dt(a^t) = a^t ln a, recognising q is a positive constant base ≠ e.
  4. Bridges to operation by writing dA/dt = p·q^t·ln q, keeping p as a passive multiplicative constant.
  5. Decides to substitute the specific t-value only after differentiating, and to keep ln q in exact form until the final numerical evaluation.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the base q and the constant multiplier p in A = p q^t.
  2. Apply the rule: dA/dt = p · q^t · ln q.
  3. If the exponent is kt rather than t, include the chain-rule factor k: dA/dt = pk q^{kt} ln q.
  4. Substitute the required value of t.
  5. Evaluate numerically using ln (natural log), keeping the sign of ln q in mind.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $A = 10^{0.32} \cdot 10^{0.03t}$. Then $\frac{dA}{dt} = 10^{0.32} \cdot 10^{0.03t} \cdot 0.03\ln 10$. At $t = 6$: $\frac{dA}{dt} = 0.03\ln 10 \cdot 10^{0.50} \approx 0.03 \times 2.3026 \times 3.1623 \approx 0.22$ m²/day.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Natural-base model A = p e^{kt}
Derivative simplifies to dA/dt = kp e^{kt}; the ln factor becomes k since ln e = 1.
Decay model A = p q^t with 0 < q < 1
Same rule applies, but ln q < 0, so dA/dt is negative — confirming decay.
Composite exponent A = p a^{f(t)}
Chain rule gives dA/dt = p a^{f(t)} · f'(t) · ln a.
Logarithmic differentiation of y = a^{g(t)}
Take ln both sides: ln y = g(t) ln a, then differentiate implicitly to recover the same formula.

Common Misreadings

  • Applying the power rule t·q^{t-1}, confusing exponent-variable with base-variable.
  • Treating q^t as if q itself were the variable, leading to spurious dq/dt terms.
  • Reading 'rate' as average rate (Δ A/Δ t) rather than instantaneous derivative.

Pitfalls

  • Using log₁₀ instead of ln — the differentiation rule requires the natural logarithm.
  • Omitting the ln q factor and writing dA/dt = p q^t.
  • Forgetting the inner-derivative factor k when the exponent is kt rather than t.
  • Substituting t before differentiating, which collapses the function to a constant.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P3/P4): exponential models, the derivative of a^x, and chain rule. No content beyond standard A-level differentiation is required.
