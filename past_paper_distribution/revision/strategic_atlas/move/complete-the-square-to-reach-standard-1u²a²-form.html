Integration

Complete the square to reach standard ∫1/(u²−a²) form

Family: Completing The Square Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an integrand has the form 1/(quadratic) dx where the quadratic is irreducible (no real linear factors in a friendly form) and the numerator is not a multiple of the derivative of the denominator. Typical cue: ∫1/(x²+bx+c) dx with b ≠ 0, leaving no obvious substitution. Also fires as a sub-step inside partial-fraction work when one residual piece has an irreducible quadratic denominator. The move is selected once you confirm there is no f'(x)/f(x) structure available.

Motivation — Why this works

Any quadratic x²+bx+c can be rewritten as (x+b/2)² + (c−b²/4), turning it into u²±a² after substituting u = x+b/2. This matches two formula-book standards: ∫1/(u²−a²) du = (1/2a) ln|(u−a)/(u+a)| and ∫1/(u²+a²) du = (1/a) arctan(u/a). The sign of the constant remainder c−b²/4 determines which standard form applies — negative gives the logarithmic (partial-fractions-style) result, positive gives arctan. This reduces an unfamiliar integral to a single line of formula-book recognition.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the denominator is an irreducible (or awkward) quadratic and the numerator is a constant — no f'/f shortcut, no obvious u-substitution.
  2. Rule out direct recognition (no logarithm pattern), partial fractions (denominator doesn't factor nicely over the rationals), and trig substitution (out of A-level scope here).
  3. Commit to completing the square because that is the only standard route from ∫1/(quadratic) dx to a formula-book integral.
  4. Bridge: compute (x+b/2)² + (c−b²/4), then read off whether the constant remainder is positive (→ arctan) or negative (→ log).
  5. Tactical: set u = x+b/2 and a = √|remainder|, apply the matching standard form, then back-substitute and (optionally) clear fractions inside the logarithm.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rewrite the quadratic: x²+bx+c = (x+b/2)² + (c−b²/4).
  2. Identify the sign of the remainder c−b²/4 to choose between u²−a² (log) and u²+a² (arctan).
  3. Set u = x+b/2 and a = √|c−b²/4|; note du = dx.
  4. Apply the formula-book standard: ∫1/(u²−a²) du = (1/2a) ln|(u−a)/(u+a)| or ∫1/(u²+a²) du = (1/a) arctan(u/a).
  5. Substitute back u = x+b/2 and simplify, optionally clearing halves by multiplying numerator and denominator inside the log by 2.
  6. Add the constant of integration.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int \frac{1}{x^2+5x+3}\,dx$. Complete the square: $x^2+5x+3 = (x+\tfrac{5}{2})^2 - \tfrac{13}{4}$. With $u=x+\tfrac{5}{2}$, $a=\tfrac{\sqrt{13}}{2}$, $\int \frac{du}{u^2-a^2} = \frac{1}{2a}\ln\left|\frac{u-a}{u+a}\right|+C = \frac{1}{\sqrt{13}}\ln\left|\frac{2x+5-\sqrt{13}}{2x+5+\sqrt{13}}\right|+C.$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Completed square gives (x+p)² + q with q > 0
Use ∫1/(u²+a²) du = (1/a) arctan(u/a); answer is (1/√q) arctan((x+p)/√q) + C.
Quadratic is a²−(x+p)² (e.g. integrand 1/(3−2x−x²))
Factor a leading −1 first to expose a²−u²; use ∫1/(a²−u²) du = (1/2a) ln|(a+u)/(a−u)| — note the swapped sign inside the log.
Numerator is linear, e.g. ∫(2x+7)/(x²+5x+3) dx
Split the numerator into a multiple of the derivative (2x+5) plus a constant remainder; the derivative part gives ln|x²+5x+3|, and the constant part feeds back into this completing-the-square move.
Leading coefficient ≠ 1, e.g. ∫1/(2x²+4x+5) dx
Factor the leading coefficient out first: 2(x²+2x+5/2) = 2((x+1)²+3/2), then apply the standard form with the 1/2 carried outside.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming every ∫1/(quadratic) dx gives arctan, ignoring the sign of the constant remainder after completing the square.
  • Treating an irreducible quadratic as if it factors and attempting partial fractions over the rationals.
  • Missing that a linear numerator first needs splitting into derivative-part + constant-part before completing the square is relevant.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips when halving an odd b (e.g. b=5 → 25/4) and combining with c.
  • Confusing ∫1/(u²−a²) du with ∫1/(a²−u²) du — the log argument flips sign.
  • Forgetting the 1/(2a) (or 1/a) prefactor from the standard form.
  • Dropping the modulus inside the logarithm or omitting +C.
  • When the integrand sits inside a partial-fraction decomposition, forgetting to multiply by the decomposition coefficient when assembling the final answer.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope: completing the square is C1/C2 algebra, and ∫1/(u²±a²) du and ∫1/(a²−u²) du are listed standard integrals in the FP formula book. No hyperbolic substitution required.
