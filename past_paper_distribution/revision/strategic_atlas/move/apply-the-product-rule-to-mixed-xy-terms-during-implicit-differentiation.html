Differentiation

Apply the product rule to mixed x–y terms during implicit differentiation

Family: Product Rule Selection Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a term in an implicit equation is a product of two factors that both depend on x — typically one explicit x-factor (like x, x², 4x, e^{2x}, sin x) multiplied by a y-factor (like y, y², sin y). Visual cues: 'xy', '4xy', 'x²y³', 'ye^{2x}', 'y sin x', 'x ln y' sitting as a single term in an equation F(x,y)=c. Equivalent phrasings of the trigger: 'I see two letters multiplied where one is y', or 'this is not a pure power of y nor a pure function of x — it is a genuine product of both'. If only one factor depends on x (the other being a constant), the product rule is unnecessary; constants pull out.

Motivation — Why this works

The product rule d/dx[uv] = u'v + uv' is the only correct way to differentiate a product of two x-dependent functions. In implicit differentiation y is treated as y(x), so any term mixing x-stuff and y-stuff is genuinely a product of two functions of x. Skipping the product rule (treating y as constant) discards the term containing dy/dx entirely, which is fatal because dy/dx is exactly what we are solving for. Combined with the chain rule on the y-factor, the product rule produces both a 'plain' term and a 'dy/dx' term, feeding the standard collect-and-isolate procedure.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Scan the equation term by term and ask of each: 'is this a product of two things that both depend on x?'
  2. For a term like xy, 4xy, ye^{2x}, or x²y³: the x-factor obviously depends on x, and the y-factor depends on x implicitly — so YES, two x-dependent factors.
  3. Rule out alternatives: a pure y^n term needs only the chain rule (one function composed, not two multiplied); a pure x^n term needs only the power rule; a constant·y term still needs chain rule but not product rule.
  4. Commit to the product rule because treating y as a constant would silently drop the x·(dy/dx) contribution — the very piece we need to solve for dy/dx.
  5. Assign u and v cleanly: let u be the x-only factor and v be the y-containing factor (this keeps u' easy and isolates the chain-rule action in v').
  6. Differentiate: u' is routine; v' invokes the chain rule, producing a dy/dx factor on the y-piece.
  7. Sort the two resulting terms: the one without dy/dx joins the 'plain' side; the one with dy/dx joins the collection that will be factored to isolate dy/dx.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the term as uv where u = u(x) (x-only) and v = v(y) or v = v(x,y) (contains y).
  2. Compute u' = du/dx by ordinary differentiation.
  3. Compute v' = dv/dx using the chain rule: if v = f(y), then dv/dx = f'(y)·dy/dx.
  4. Write d/dx[uv] = u'·v + u·v' = u'(x)·v(y) + u(x)·f'(y)·dy/dx.
  5. Place the u'v term on the non-dy/dx side and the uv' term in the dy/dx collection ready for factoring.

Worked Micro-Example

Differentiate the term $4xy$ implicitly. Set $u=4x$, $v=y$, so $u'=4$, $v'=\dfrac{dy}{dx}$. Then $\dfrac{d}{dx}(4xy) = 4\cdot y + 4x\cdot\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 4y + 4x\dfrac{dy}{dx}$. The $4y$ stays as a plain term; the $4x\,dy/dx$ joins the collection to be factored.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Term is x^a · y^b (e.g. x²y³)
Product rule plus chain rule on the y-power: d/dx(x²y³) = 2x·y³ + x²·3y²·(dy/dx).
Term is x · f(y) where f is a transcendental (e.g. x sin y, x ln y)
Same template: d/dx(x·sin y) = sin y + x·cos y·(dy/dx); the chain rule supplies cos y·(dy/dx) for v'.
Term is g(x) · y where g is transcendental (e.g. ye^{2x}, y sin x)
u = g(x), v = y, so u' = g'(x), v' = dy/dx: d/dx(ye^{2x}) = 2ye^{2x} + e^{2x}·(dy/dx).
Term is g(x) · f(y) (both transcendental, e.g. e^x sin y)
Full product+chain: d/dx(e^x sin y) = e^x sin y + e^x cos y·(dy/dx).

Common Misreadings

  • Mistaking xy for a single 'variable' and writing d/dx(xy) = 1 — ignoring that it is a product.
  • Seeing ye^{2x} and treating y as a constant coefficient, getting only 2ye^{2x}.
  • Confusing a product like x²y³ with a composite, and trying to apply only the chain rule.

Pitfalls

  • Treating y as a constant and losing the u·(dy/dx) term entirely — the most damaging error because it kills the unknown we are solving for.
  • Applying the product rule but forgetting the chain-rule factor dy/dx on v' (e.g. writing d/dx(xy²) = y² + x·2y instead of y² + 2xy·dy/dx).
  • Mis-assigning u and v so that v' becomes harder than necessary — always let u be the x-only factor when possible.
  • Sign or coefficient slips when the term carries a leading constant (e.g. forgetting to carry the 4 through both pieces of d/dx(4xy)).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P3/P4 implicit differentiation. Uses the product rule and chain rule, both listed in the formula booklet under 'Differentiation'. No need for partial derivatives or multivariable calculus — y is always treated as a single-variable function y(x).
