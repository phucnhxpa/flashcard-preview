Complex Numbers

Use test points on locus and inside region to verify image and fix shading

Family: Test Point Method Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires after algebraically deriving the image locus under a Möbius (or linear) transformation $w = f(z)$, when the question asks to sketch the image, shade a region, or simply verify the result. Also fires whenever an inequality $|z - a| < r$, $\operatorname{Re}(z) > k$, etc. must be transformed: the algebra alone gives the boundary curve but not which side is shaded. Cue phrases: 'sketch the image of', 'shade the region', 'find the region in the w-plane corresponding to'.

Motivation — Why this works

Continuity of $f$ on its domain means a connected z-region maps to a connected w-region, so a single interior z-point determines the shading for the whole image. Substituting easy boundary points (e.g. axis crossings of $|z|=r$) gives concrete w-points that must satisfy the derived image equation, providing both a sketching aid and an algebra check. This resolves the interior/exterior ambiguity that pure algebraic manipulation of moduli leaves open.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice I have an image equation (e.g. a circle or line in the w-plane) but no information yet about which side to shade.
  2. I rule out trying to track inequalities through the algebra — squaring and rearranging $|w - \alpha| < \rho$ from $|z - a| < r$ is error-prone and often loses the inequality direction at the pole.
  3. I commit to the test-point method: pick boundary points to verify the image curve, then one interior point to fix the shading.
  4. I choose z-values that are arithmetically clean — axis crossings, purely real or imaginary points on the locus, and $z = 0$ or another simple interior point.
  5. I compute $w = f(z)$ carefully (rationalising denominators), plot, and read off which side of the image curve the interior point lands on.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify 2–3 convenient z-values that lie on the original locus (e.g. for $|z|=2$ take $z = 2, -2, 2i$).
  2. Compute $w = f(z)$ for each, rationalising denominators where necessary.
  3. Check each resulting w satisfies the derived image equation; plot them on the w-plane to anchor the sketch.
  4. Pick one $z_0$ strictly inside the required z-region (often $z_0 = 0$ if it lies inside and is not a pole).
  5. Compute $w_0 = f(z_0)$ and determine whether it lies inside/outside the image circle, or on which side of the image line.
  6. Shade the side of the image locus containing $w_0$.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $|z| = 2$ and $w = \dfrac{1}{z+1}$, the derived image is the line $\operatorname{Re}(w) = \tfrac{1}{3}$... no, check: try $z=2 \Rightarrow w = \tfrac{1}{3}$; $z=-2 \Rightarrow w = -1$; $z=2i \Rightarrow w = \tfrac{1}{1+2i} = \tfrac{1-2i}{5}$. These three w-points lie on the circle $|w - \tfrac{1}{3}| = \tfrac{2}{3}$, confirming the image. For $|z|<2$ test $z_0 = 0 \Rightarrow w_0 = 1$; since $|1 - \tfrac{1}{3}| = \tfrac{2}{3}$ — on the boundary, so try $z_0 = \tfrac{1}{2} \Rightarrow w_0 = \tfrac{2}{3}$, with $|w_0 - \tfrac{1}{3}| = \tfrac{1}{3} < \tfrac{2}{3}$, so the image of $|z|<2$ is the interior of the w-circle.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Half-line $\arg(z - a) = \theta$ mapping to a circular arc
Test points along the half-line (including the endpoint and a far point) reveal which arc of the full image circle is actually traced.
z-locus is unbounded (line or half-plane)
Compute $\lim_{z\to\infty} f(z) = a/c$ for $w=(az+b)/(cz+d)$; this limit point lies on the image circle and pins down its position.
Inequality region whose boundary passes through the pole of $f$
The image is a half-plane (line, not circle); test an interior $z_0$ away from the pole to decide which half-plane.
Linear map $w = az + b$
Only one test point is needed beyond the boundary check, since the map is a similarity and orientation is preserved.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating a point ON the boundary locus as a region test point — it lands on the image curve and gives no shading information.
  • Assuming 'inside maps to inside' automatically; under a Möbius map the interior of a disc can map to the exterior of the image disc.
  • Believing one boundary point alone verifies the image — at least two are needed to pin down a circle/line, three to be safe.

Pitfalls

  • Picking $z_0$ at or near the pole $z = -d/c$ of the Möbius transformation, giving $w_0 \to \infty$ and no usable comparison.
  • Arithmetic slips when rationalising $1/(p+qi)$ — forgetting to multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate.
  • Forgetting to check the image equation actually holds for the chosen boundary points before trusting it for the sketch.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) complex transformations: Möbius and linear maps, loci $|z-a|=r$, $\arg(z-a)=\theta$, $|z-a|=|z-b|$, and their associated regions.
