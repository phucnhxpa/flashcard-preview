Factorise a quadratic by splitting the middle term (ac-method)

Family: Strategic Factorisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires on a quadratic ax² + bx + c with integer coefficients and a leading coefficient a ≠ 1, where ac has few factor pairs to scan. Especially attractive when the discriminant b² − 4ac is a perfect square, guaranteeing rational roots and hence an integer split. Typical surfaces: solving ax² + bx + c = 0, factorising before cancelling in a rational expression, or factorising a disguised quadratic in y = x², eˣ, sin x, etc.

Motivation — Why this works

If ax² + bx + c factorises over the integers as (px + q)(rx + s), then pr = a, qs = c, and the middle coefficient b = ps + qr; the products ps and qr multiply to (pr)(qs) = ac and sum to b. So finding two integers with product ac and sum b reconstructs the split that allows grouping. This is faster than the quadratic formula when the split is easy to spot, and yields the factorisation directly rather than as a by-product of the roots.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the quadratic has integer coefficients and a leading coefficient ≠ 1, so a clean factorisation (rather than the formula) might exist.
  2. Rule out trial-factor of (αy + β)(γy + δ) by inspection if a or c has many divisors — the ac-method systematises the search.
  3. Commit because ac is small (here 6), so the candidate factor pairs are quickly enumerated and the discriminant 25 is a perfect square.
  4. Search for two integers with product ac = 6 and sum b = −7; both must be negative since the product is positive and the sum is negative — try −1 and −6.
  5. Split −7y as −y − 6y, group into pairs, extract a common binomial, and read off the roots.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute ac and identify target sum b.
  2. List factor pairs of ac (with correct signs) and pick the pair summing to b.
  3. Rewrite the middle term bx as the sum of these two terms.
  4. Group the four-term expression into two pairs and factor each pair.
  5. Factor out the common binomial to obtain (·)(·).
  6. If solving = 0, set each factor to zero to obtain the roots.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $2y^2 - 7y + 3 = 0$. Here $ac = 6$, $b = -7$; the pair $-1, -6$ works. Rewrite: $2y^2 - y - 6y + 3 = 0$. Group: $y(2y-1) - 3(2y-1) = 0$, so $(2y-1)(y-3) = 0$. Hence $y = \tfrac{1}{2}$ or $y = 3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Disguised quadratic, e.g. $2e^{2x} - 7e^x + 3 = 0$
Substitute $u = e^x$, factorise $2u^2 - 7u + 3$ identically, then back-substitute and take logs (rejecting non-positive $u$).
Quadratic in $\sin x$ or $\cos x$, e.g. $2\sin^2 x - 7\sin x + 3 = 0$
Factorise as $(2\sin x - 1)(\sin x - 3) = 0$; reject $\sin x = 3$ as out of range.
Factorising before cancelling in a rational expression
Use the ac-method on numerator/denominator to expose a common linear factor for cancellation.
Leading coefficient $a = 1$
The ac-method reduces to the standard 'find two numbers multiplying to $c$ and summing to $b$'.
Discriminant not a perfect square
Abandon factorisation; switch to the quadratic formula or completing the square.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the method as guesswork rather than a systematic search keyed to ac and b.
  • Forgetting that both split numbers must share the sign pattern dictated by the signs of ac and b.
  • Assuming every integer-coefficient quadratic factors over the integers — it only does when the discriminant is a perfect square.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when splitting, particularly when both numbers are negative or have opposite signs.
  • Choosing a pair that multiplies to $c$ instead of $ac$ when $a \ne 1$.
  • Grouping that fails to produce a common binomial — usually a symptom of the wrong split or a sign slip.
  • Forgetting to divide through by the leading coefficient of the bracket when reading off roots, e.g. writing $y = 1$ instead of $y = \tfrac{1}{2}$ from $(2y-1)$.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard technique within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics for solving quadratics and factorising disguised quadratics in exponentials, logarithms, or trigonometric functions; no content beyond the IAL syllabus is required.
