Integration

Swap integration limits to absorb a leading negative sign

Family: Limit Manipulation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires immediately after a substitution (often u = a − x, u = cos x, or similar) leaves the integrand multiplied by −1 AND produces limits where the upper bound is numerically less than the lower bound. The two 'wrongs' — a leading minus and reversed limits — are precisely cancellable. Recognise the cue: a factor of −1 from du = −dx combined with limits in descending order. Equivalent phrasings: 'flip the limits to kill the minus', 'restore lower < upper'.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity ∫_a^b f(u) du = −∫_b^a f(u) du means swapping limits introduces exactly one sign change. So ∫_a^b [−f(u)] du = −∫_a^b f(u) du = ∫_b^a f(u) du. Performing this swap once at the start removes the negative sign before any partial fractions, evaluation, or subtraction is done, dramatically reducing sign-tracking risk in long calculations. It also restores the conventional 'lower < upper' presentation expected in solutions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice my post-substitution integral has both a leading minus sign AND limits in the 'wrong' order (upper < lower).
  2. I rule out carrying the −1 as a coefficient through partial fractions or evaluation, because each subsequent line is another chance to drop a sign.
  3. I commit to swapping the limits now, because the swap-identity costs zero algebra and removes the negative simultaneously.
  4. Concretely, I rewrite ∫_a^b (−g(u)) du as ∫_b^a g(u) du in a single step before doing anything else.
  5. Inside the move, I double-check that I have applied exactly one sign change — not zero (forgot to drop the minus) and not two (kept the minus and swapped).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the leading −1 (typically from du = −dx after a substitution like u = a − x).
  2. Identify that the current limits satisfy upper < lower.
  3. Apply ∫_a^b (−f(u)) du = ∫_b^a f(u) du in one rewrite — drop the minus and swap the limits simultaneously.
  4. Verify the new limits now satisfy lower < upper, and the integrand has no leading negative.
  5. Proceed with partial fractions / evaluation on the cleaner expression.

Worked Micro-Example

After substituting u = 1 − x in ∫_{−8}^{−13} \frac{-1}{u^3(1-u)}\,du, I have a leading minus and reversed limits. Swap once: ∫_{−8}^{−13} \frac{-1}{u^3(1-u)}\,du = ∫_{−13}^{−8} \frac{1}{u^3(1-u)}\,du. Now the integrand is positive in form and limits ascend, ready for partial fractions.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Substitution u = a − x in ∫_0^a f(x) dx
du = −dx introduces the minus; the limits become u = a down to u = 0; swap to ∫_0^a f(a−u) du.
Substitution u = cos x with x going from 0 to π/2
du = −sin x dx; u-limits run from 1 down to 0; swap once to restore 0 to 1 and remove the minus.
Trig substitution where the new variable decreases as x increases
Same recipe: one swap absorbs the −1 from the differential change.
Reduction-formula derivations using integration by parts with a u = a − x trick
After the substitution, swap limits immediately so the resulting integral matches the original orientation, enabling clean comparison.

Common Misreadings

  • Thinking the minus sign must be tracked separately because it 'belongs to the integrand' rather than recognising it as exchangeable with limit order.
  • Believing that limits with upper < lower are an error rather than a legitimate (if unconventional) form fixable by the swap identity.
  • Confusing this move with the symmetry rule ∫_0^a f(x) dx = ∫_0^a f(a−x) dx, which is a different (though related) identity.

Pitfalls

  • Swapping the limits but forgetting to drop the minus sign (net effect: introduced an extra sign error).
  • Dropping the minus but forgetting to swap the limits (same net error in the opposite direction).
  • Swapping limits before fully accounting for the du = −dx factor, leading to an inconsistent setup.
  • Forgetting that the substitution must also be applied to the integrand (1 − u vs 1 − x) before the swap is meaningful.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P3/P4) integration by substitution. The identity ∫_a^b f = −∫_b^a f is standard and assumed without proof at A-level.
