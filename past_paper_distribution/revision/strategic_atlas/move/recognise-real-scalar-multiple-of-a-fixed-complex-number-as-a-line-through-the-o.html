Complex Numbers

Recognise real-scalar multiple of a fixed complex number as a line through the origin

Family: Geometric Interpretation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

After simplifying or rationalising a locus/image expression, the result takes the form $w = k(a+bi)$ where $k$ is a real parameter and $a+bi$ is a fixed nonzero complex number. Equivalent phrasings include $w = \lambda z_0$ with $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$, or finding that $\arg w$ is constant (modulo $\pi$). This typically appears when a transformation $w = f(z)$ is applied to a real input set (e.g. $z$ purely real or purely imaginary) and the result has a single real degree of freedom multiplying a fixed direction.

Motivation — Why this works

A complex number $a+bi$ represents a fixed direction in the Argand diagram. Multiplying by any real $k$ scales along that direction (and reflects through the origin when $k<0$), so $\{k(a+bi) : k \in \mathbb{R}\}$ is precisely the line through the origin with gradient $b/a$. Reading off $u = ka$, $v = kb$ gives $v/u = b/a$ instantly, bypassing parametric elimination. This is the complex-plane analogue of recognising $t\mathbf{d}$ as a line through the origin in vectors.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that after simplification the expression has the shape (real expression) $\times$ (fixed complex number).
  2. Rule out treating it as a circle or general locus — there is only one real parameter, and the complex factor is constant in argument.
  3. Commit to the line-through-origin reading because $\arg w$ is fixed (or differs by $\pi$ when $k<0$).
  4. Bridge to mechanics: read the direction $a+bi$ to get gradient $b/a$, hence $v = (b/a)u$.
  5. Check the range of $k$ to decide whether the locus is the full line, a ray, or has excluded points (e.g. values of the original variable that were forbidden).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the simplified image as $w = k(a+bi)$ with $k$ real and $a+bi$ fixed.
  2. Identify real and imaginary parts: $u = ka$, $v = kb$.
  3. Eliminate $k$: $v = (b/a)u$, i.e. the line through the origin with gradient $b/a$.
  4. Examine the range of $k$ as the original parameter varies to determine which portion of the line is traced and whether any points must be excluded.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $w = \dfrac{y}{5(y+1)}(1+3i)$ for real $y \neq -1$. Setting $k = \dfrac{y}{5(y+1)}$, we have $w = k(1+3i)$, so $u = k$ and $v = 3k$. Eliminating $k$ gives the line $v = 3u$ through the origin in the Argand diagram. As $y$ ranges over $\mathbb{R}\setminus\{-1\}$, $k$ takes every real value except $\tfrac15$ (the limit as $y\to\infty$), so that single point is excluded.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$w = c + k(a+bi)$ with $c$ a fixed complex constant and $k$ real
The image is a line in direction $a+bi$ passing through $c$ (not through the origin); gradient is still $b/a$ but the intercept shifts.
$k$ ranges only over $k \geq 0$ (e.g. $k = |z|$ or $k = e^t$)
The locus is a ray from the origin in direction $a+bi$, not the full line; argument is exactly $\arg(a+bi)$, not $\arg(a+bi)+\pi$.
$w = k\,\mathrm{cis}\,\theta$ with $k$ real and $\theta$ fixed
Same line-through-origin recognition; gradient is $\tan\theta$.
Image of a real axis under a Möbius map $w = \frac{az+b}{cz+d}$ that happens to factorise as real$\times$constant
Apply the same recognition after rationalising; remember to exclude the image of any pole and to include/exclude the limit point.

Common Misreadings

  • Separating into $u$ and $v$ and attempting full parametric elimination, missing that one real parameter times a fixed complex number is automatically a line.
  • Reading the locus as a ray when $k$ actually takes both signs, or as a full line when $k$ is restricted to one sign.
  • Forgetting to identify and exclude limit values of $k$ that the original parameter cannot reach.

Pitfalls

  • Inverting the gradient (writing $u = (b/a)v$ instead of $v = (b/a)u$).
  • Omitting excluded points arising from forbidden values of the original variable (e.g. denominator zero).
  • Claiming the origin lies on the locus without checking that some allowed value of the parameter gives $k=0$.

A-level Scope Notes

This recognition uses only Argand-diagram geometry and basic algebra of $u+iv$, both squarely within Edexcel IAL Further Pure complex numbers (loci and transformations $w=f(z)$).
