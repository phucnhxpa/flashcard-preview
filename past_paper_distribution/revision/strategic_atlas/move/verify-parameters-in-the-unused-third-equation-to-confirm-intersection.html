Vectors

Verify parameters in the unused third equation to confirm intersection

Family: Consistency Check Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a 'show that' or 'prove that' question asks you to demonstrate two 3D lines intersect, after you have already solved two of the three component equations for $\lambda$ and $\mu$. Cue phrases include 'show the lines meet', 'find the point of intersection' (where prior justification is implicit), or any setting where three scalar equations arise from equating $\mathbf{r}_1 = \mathbf{r}_2$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$. The third, unused equation must be checked. If only two equations exist (e.g. 2D vectors), this move is not needed.

Motivation — Why this works

Equating two 3D vector lines yields three scalar equations in two unknowns — an overdetermined system. Solving any two equations always produces values of $\lambda, \mu$, but those values correspond to a genuine intersection point only if the third equation is also satisfied; otherwise the lines are skew. The third equation is the discriminating constraint that distinguishes intersecting from skew lines. Edexcel mark schemes routinely award an explicit B1 or A1 mark for this verification.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice I have three component equations but only two unknowns $\lambda, \mu$, so the system is overdetermined.
  2. I rule out declaring intersection from the two equations I solved — that only guarantees agreement in two coordinates, not all three.
  3. I commit to substituting the found values into the remaining (unused) equation, because skew lines would fail precisely here.
  4. I identify which equation was not used in solving (often the $\mathbf{i}$- or $\mathbf{k}$-component) and prepare to substitute.
  5. I write LHS and RHS separately so the examiner sees the equality explicitly, then state the conclusion.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Solve two of the three component equations simultaneously for $\lambda$ and $\mu$.
  2. Identify the third (unused) component equation.
  3. Substitute the values of $\lambda$ and $\mu$ into the LHS of that equation.
  4. Substitute into the RHS independently.
  5. State LHS = RHS explicitly and conclude that the lines intersect; otherwise conclude they are skew.

Worked Micro-Example

Lines $\mathbf{r}_1 = (1,2,0) + \lambda(2,1,1)$ and $\mathbf{r}_2 = (4,3,-1) + \mu(1,1,0)$. Solving the $\mathbf{j}$- and $\mathbf{k}$-components gives $\lambda = 1, \mu = -1$. Check the $\mathbf{i}$-component: LHS $= 1 + 2(1) = 3$, RHS $= 4 + (-1) = 3$. Since LHS $=$ RHS, the lines intersect at $(3,3,1)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Lines turn out to be skew
LHS $\neq$ RHS in the third equation; conclude the lines do not intersect (are skew, given they are also non-parallel).
Line meets a plane (Further Maths)
Only one parameter is involved, so there is no overdetermined system; this consistency check is replaced by substituting the parametric line into the plane equation.
Two lines in 2D
Only two scalar equations arise, exactly matching two unknowns; no redundant equation exists, so the check is not required (lines either meet or are parallel).
'Find the point of intersection' without the word 'show'
The verification is still mathematically required to justify that an intersection exists, though some mark schemes are lenient; best practice is to include it.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the system as two equations in two unknowns and ignoring the third component entirely.
  • Believing that finding consistent $\lambda, \mu$ from any two equations automatically proves 3D intersection.
  • Confusing this with the parallel/skew test using direction vectors — that is a separate prior check.

Pitfalls

  • Omitting the verification step in a 'show that' question and losing the dedicated mark.
  • Substituting into one of the two equations already used, which is circular and proves nothing.
  • Arithmetic slip when computing LHS or RHS, leading to a false 'skew' conclusion.
  • Forgetting to state the explicit conclusion 'therefore the lines intersect' after showing LHS = RHS.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (vector lines in 3D, P4) and Further Pure (FP3) scope. No matrix rank or linear-algebra machinery is invoked — the check is a direct substitution.
