Matrices

Apply the adjugate-over-determinant formula to invert a parametric 3×3 matrix

Family: Adjugate Inverse Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Part (b) of a multi-part question asks for M⁻¹ (or A⁻¹) where M is a 3×3 matrix whose entries depend on a parameter (k, p, λ, …), and part (a) has typically already produced det M as a linear/quadratic expression in that parameter. The structural cue is: 3×3 size + at least one symbolic entry + det M already in hand. Equivalent phrasings: 'find the inverse in terms of k', 'express M⁻¹ as a single matrix divided by …', 'hence find M⁻¹'. Whenever row-reducing [M | I] would force you to divide by an expression in the parameter, the adjugate route is the safer trigger.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity M⁻¹ = (1/det M)·adj(M) decomposes inversion into nine independent 2×2 cofactor determinants plus a transpose plus a single scalar division — a fully algorithmic procedure that keeps the parameter symbolic throughout. Gauss–Jordan on [M | I] with a parameter forces division by expressions in k at every pivot, branching into cases whenever such an expression could vanish. The adjugate method avoids all case-splitting (only the single condition det M ≠ 0 matters) and reuses the determinant already found in part (a), so roughly half the work is free. Errors stay local: a slip in one cofactor does not cascade through subsequent rows.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question asks for the inverse of a 3×3 matrix containing a symbolic parameter, and that part (a) has already produced det M as an expression in that parameter.
  2. Consider the two standard routes: (i) row-reduce the augmented matrix [M | I] to [I | M⁻¹], or (ii) apply M⁻¹ = (1/det M)·adj(M).
  3. Rule out row reduction: each pivot would require dividing by an expression in k, forcing case analysis on when those expressions vanish and producing tangled algebraic fractions mid-computation.
  4. Commit to the adjugate route because det M is already in hand, the nine cofactors are independent self-contained 2×2 determinants, and the only invertibility condition is the clean 'det M ≠ 0'.
  5. Plan the bookkeeping: compute the 3×3 matrix of minors row by row, overlay the checkerboard sign pattern (+ − + / − + − / + − +) to get the cofactor matrix C, then transpose to obtain adj(M).
  6. Finally divide by det M (kept as a single factored expression like 5(k−2) or −2(k−2)) so the answer presents as one matrix over one denominator, and state the value(s) of the parameter excluded by det M = 0.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. For each position (i,j), delete row i and column j of M and compute the resulting 2×2 minor M_{ij} = det of the remaining submatrix.
  2. Apply the checkerboard sign: cofactor C_{ij} = (−1)^{i+j} · M_{ij}. Negative signs sit at (1,2), (2,1), (2,3), (3,2).
  3. Assemble the nine cofactors into the cofactor matrix C.
  4. Transpose: adj(M) = Cᵀ. (Reflect across the main diagonal — this is the step most often forgotten.)
  5. Write M⁻¹ = (1/det M) · adj(M), using the factored form of det M from part (a), and state the parameter value(s) excluded for invertibility.

Worked Micro-Example

Let M have det M = 5k − 10 = 5(k−2). Suppose the (1,1) cofactor is C_{11} = \det\begin{pmatrix}k & 1\\ 2 & 3\end{pmatrix} = 3k-2 and the (2,1) cofactor is C_{21} = -\det\begin{pmatrix}1 & 0\\ 2 & 3\end{pmatrix} = -3. After computing all nine and transposing, adj(M) sits as a matrix in k, and M^{-1} = \dfrac{1}{5(k-2)}\,\text{adj}(M), valid for k \neq 2.

Variants — Same move, different surface

2×2 parametric matrix
Use the shortcut M⁻¹ = (1/det M)·[[d, −b],[−c, a]] — this is the adjugate formula in miniature, no cofactor grid needed.
Numerical (non-parametric) 3×3 matrix
Row reduction of [M | I] is equally valid and often faster, since no case-splitting risk arises.
Question asks 'hence solve Mx = b' after finding M⁻¹
Once M⁻¹ = (1/det M)·adj(M) is built, simply compute x = M⁻¹b; the scalar 1/det M can be applied at the end.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 'find the inverse' as a cue to row-reduce, even though the parameter makes pivots unsafe and would force case analysis.
  • Computing the matrix of minors and then transposing immediately, skipping the checkerboard sign step (so producing the transpose of the minor matrix instead of adj(M)).
  • Treating the cofactor matrix C itself as the adjugate without transposing.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to transpose the cofactor matrix — adj(M) = Cᵀ, not C. This is the single most common error.
  • Sign errors in the (−1)^{i+j} pattern, especially at C_{12}, C_{21}, C_{23}, C_{32} where the sign is negative.
  • Computing a minor from the wrong submatrix (deleting the wrong row or column), particularly for off-diagonal cofactors.
  • Arithmetic slips inside the nine 2×2 determinants — each should be checked independently since errors do not self-correct.
  • Failing to factor det M before dividing, leaving an unsimplified denominator and losing presentation marks.
  • Omitting the invertibility condition (e.g. 'k ≠ 2') in the final answer.

A-level Scope Notes

This sits squarely within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP1/FP2) on matrices: 3×3 inverse via adjugate/determinant is examinable. The cofactor expansion and adj(M) = Cᵀ identity are standard; no formula booklet entry is provided for the 3×3 inverse, so the method must be reproduced. Avoid invoking general n×n adjugate theory or Cramer's rule beyond 3×3.
