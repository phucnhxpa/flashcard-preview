Square-and-add then divide to decouple R and α

Trigger — When to use

Two simultaneous equations of the form R·cos α = a and R·sin α = b appear, with R > 0 and α in a specified range. Equivalent surfaces: matching coefficients after writing a cos x + b sin x ≡ R cos(x − α) (or R sin(x + α)), or converting Cartesian point (a, b) to polar form. Cue words: 'express in the form R cos(...)','find R and α', or any pair of equations sharing one angle locked between cos and sin.

Motivation — Why this works

Squaring both equations and adding invokes cos²α + sin²α = 1, killing α and leaving R² = a² + b². Dividing the sin-equation by the cos-equation invokes sin α / cos α = tan α, killing R and leaving tan α = b/a. The two Pythagorean-ratio identities are complementary: one isolates the magnitude, the other isolates the angle, so the system separates cleanly without substitution.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice two equations sharing the same α, with R appearing as a common multiplier — this is the signature of a harmonic-form identification problem.
  2. I rule out direct substitution (solving one equation for cos α and plugging in) because that introduces square roots and quadrant ambiguity.
  3. I commit to the square-add / divide pair because cos²α + sin²α = 1 and tan α = sin α / cos α are exactly the identities that decouple R from α.
  4. First operation: square each equation so the squared trig terms can combine via Pythagoras to give R².
  5. Tactically I take R > 0 (as stipulated), then use the signs of a and b to place α in the correct quadrant before applying arctan.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the two equations: R cos α = a, R sin α = b.
  2. Square both: R² cos²α = a², R² sin²α = b².
  3. Add and use cos²α + sin²α = 1: R² = a² + b², so R = √(a² + b²) (positive root).
  4. Divide the sin-equation by the cos-equation: tan α = b/a.
  5. Determine α from arctan(b/a), adjusting by ±π if the quadrant (set by signs of a and b) requires it; check α lies in the stated range.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $R\cos\alpha = \sqrt5$ and $R\sin\alpha = 2\sqrt5$ with $R>0,\;0<\alpha<\tfrac{\pi}{2}$: square and add to get $R^2 = 5 + 20 = 25$, so $R = 5$. Divide to get $\tan\alpha = \dfrac{2\sqrt5}{\sqrt5} = 2$, so $\alpha = \arctan 2 \approx 1.107$ rad. Hence $\sqrt5\cos x + 2\sqrt5\sin x \equiv 5\cos(x - 1.107)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Expressing a cos x + b sin x ≡ R sin(x + α)
Match to get R sin α = a (cos-coefficient) and R cos α = b (sin-coefficient); same square-add gives R, divide gives tan α = a/b.
Expressing a sin x − b cos x ≡ R sin(x − α)
Matching gives R cos α = a, R sin α = b; technique unchanged but mind the sign convention on α.
Cartesian to polar conversion (x, y) → (r, θ)
Use r = √(x² + y²) and θ = arctan(y/x), with quadrant correction based on signs of x and y.
Complex number a + bi to modulus-argument form
|z| = √(a² + b²) plays the role of R; arg z = arctan(b/a) (with quadrant adjustment) plays the role of α.
Solving a cos x + b sin x = c (after harmonic conversion)
First apply this move to obtain R and α, then solve R cos(x − α) = c.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the system as linear in cos α and sin α and trying to eliminate by row operations — misses the Pythagorean shortcut.
  • Reading R·cos α as a product of two unknowns to be solved separately, rather than as a coupled pair to be decoupled by identity.
  • Confusing which form is being targeted (R cos(x − α) vs R sin(x + α)) and matching coefficients to the wrong slot.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to take the positive root for R when the question stipulates R > 0.
  • Applying arctan without quadrant analysis: if a < 0 or b < 0, arctan(b/a) alone gives the wrong α.
  • Squaring loses sign information — recover sign from the original equations, not the squared ones.
  • Mixing up which equation is sin and which is cos when dividing, giving tan α = a/b instead of b/a.
  • Leaving α in degrees when the question (or range) is in radians, or vice versa.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P3 'harmonic form' technique (a cos x + b sin x ≡ R cos(x ∓ α) or R sin(x ± α)); also reused in FP1 polar/Cartesian conversion and complex-number modulus-argument work.
