Algebra & Functions

Decompose exponential term using index laws to reveal quadratic structure

Family: Structure Recognition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The equation mixes exponential terms whose exponents are linearly related — typically one exponent is twice another, e.g. $a^{2x}$ alongside $a^x$, or $a^{2x+1}$ alongside $a^x$, plus a constant. Cues include seeing $2x$ and $x$ in the same equation, or shifted exponents like $x+1$ that can absorb a constant factor. Any equation of the shape $\alpha\, a^{f(x)} + \beta\, a^{g(x)} + \gamma = 0$ where $f = 2g$ (up to additive constants) is a candidate. The presence of three terms — two exponentials and a constant — strongly hints at a hidden quadratic.

Motivation — Why this works

By the index laws $a^{m+n} = a^m \cdot a^n$ and $a^{mn} = (a^m)^n$, any exponential whose exponent is a linear function of $x$ can be expressed as a power of a single base expression $u = a^x$. Once every term is rewritten in $u$, the equation becomes a polynomial in $u$ — usually quadratic — which standard factorisation or the quadratic formula can solve. This converts a transcendental-looking problem into routine algebra, with the final step being to undo the substitution via logarithms.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice exponentials with exponents $2x+1$ and $x$ alongside a constant — the exponents are proportional, with $2x$ being double $x$.
  2. I rule out trying to take logs directly (the sum of exponentials prevents that) and rule out numerical methods since the structure looks algebraic.
  3. I commit to the substitution $u = 2^x$ once I see that every exponential term can be expressed as a power of $u$ after applying index laws.
  4. The bridge: split $2^{2x+1} = 2 \cdot 2^{2x} = 2u^2$ and rewrite $2^x = u$, turning the equation into a quadratic in $u$.
  5. Tactically, I keep the leading coefficient ($2$ from $2^1$) explicit, and I remember that any solution must satisfy $u > 0$ before recovering $x$ via $x = \log_2 u$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the smallest exponential expression $u = a^x$ that all other exponential terms can be written in terms of.
  2. Apply $a^{m+n} = a^m \cdot a^n$ to peel off any constant in the exponent (e.g. $2^{2x+1} = 2 \cdot 2^{2x}$).
  3. Apply $(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$ in reverse to express $a^{2x}$ as $(a^x)^2 = u^2$.
  4. Substitute to obtain a quadratic $Au^2 + Bu + C = 0$ in $u$.
  5. Solve for $u$, discard any non-positive roots, then recover $x$ using logarithms.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $2^{2x+1} - 7 \cdot 2^x + 3 = 0$. Write $2^{2x+1} = 2(2^x)^2$ and let $u = 2^x$, giving $2u^2 - 7u + 3 = 0$. Factorising: $(2u - 1)(u - 3) = 0$, so $u = \tfrac{1}{2}$ or $u = 3$. Hence $2^x = \tfrac{1}{2} \Rightarrow x = -1$, or $2^x = 3 \Rightarrow x = \log_2 3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation $3^{2x} - 4 \cdot 3^{x+1} + 27 = 0$
Rewrite $3^{x+1} = 3 \cdot 3^x$ and $3^{2x} = (3^x)^2$; substitute $u = 3^x$ to get $u^2 - 12u + 27 = 0$.
Equation in $e^{2x}$ and $e^x$, e.g. $e^{2x} - 5e^x + 6 = 0$
Substitute $u = e^x$ directly; recover $x = \ln u$.
Hidden quadratic in $a^{-x}$, e.g. $4^x + 4^{-x} = \tfrac{17}{4}$
Multiply through by $4^x$ first, then substitute $u = 4^x$ to get a quadratic.
Disguised version with different bases linked by $4^x = (2^x)^2$
Rewrite $4^x = (2^x)^2$ so both terms share base $2^x$, then substitute.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading $2^{2x+1}$ as $2^{2x} + 1$ instead of $2^{(2x+1)}$, losing the multiplicative decomposition.
  • Failing to notice that the exponent $2x$ is double $x$, so missing the quadratic pattern entirely.
  • Treating the equation as solvable by taking $\log$ of both sides directly, which fails because of the sum of exponentials.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the factor of $2$ from $2^1$ when splitting $2^{2x+1}$, producing $u^2 - 7u + 3 = 0$ instead of $2u^2 - 7u + 3 = 0$.
  • Accepting a negative or zero value of $u$ as a solution, when $u = a^x > 0$ is required.
  • Stopping at the values of $u$ and forgetting to convert back to $x$ via logarithms.
  • Using $\log_{10}$ when $\log_2$ (or $\ln$) gives a cleaner exact answer.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics — index laws and logarithms (P2), and quadratic equations (P1). The substitution-to-quadratic technique is a standard exam pattern.
