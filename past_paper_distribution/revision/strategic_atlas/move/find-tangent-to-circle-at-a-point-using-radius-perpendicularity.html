Coordinate Geometry

Find tangent to circle at a point using radius-perpendicularity

Family: Tangent Via Radius Perpendicularity Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks for the equation of the tangent to a circle at a specified point P that lies on the circle, with the centre C either given or readily extractable from the circle's equation. Typical phrasings include 'find the equation of the tangent at P', 'show that the tangent at P has equation...', or 'the line l touches the circle at P'. Both the centre and the point of contact must be known (or recoverable by completing the square).

Motivation — Why this works

By the circle theorem, the tangent at any point on a circle is perpendicular to the radius drawn to that point. Hence the tangent's gradient is the negative reciprocal of the gradient of CP. Combined with the known contact point P, the tangent equation follows immediately from the point-slope form $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$. This avoids the heavier discriminant-of-quadratic approach and uses only Pure 1 / Pure 2 coordinate geometry.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the contact point P lies on the circle and the centre C is known (or can be found by completing the square), so the radius CP is fully determined.
  2. Rule out substituting y = mx + c into the circle equation and forcing discriminant = 0: it is valid but algebraically heavy and unnecessary when C and P are both known.
  3. Commit to perpendicularity: tangent ⟂ radius is the defining property, so $m_{\text{tan}} = -1/m_{CP}$.
  4. Bridge: compute $m_{CP}$ from the two coordinate pairs, take its negative reciprocal, then plug into point-slope form at P.
  5. Tactical: clear fractions early to reach the requested form (often $ax + by + c = 0$ with integer coefficients), and keep careful track of signs in the final rearrangement.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the centre C (read off, or complete the square on the circle equation) and the point of tangency P(x_1, y_1).
  2. Compute the radius gradient $m_{CP} = (y_1 - y_C)/(x_1 - x_C)$.
  3. Take the negative reciprocal: $m_T = -1/m_{CP}$.
  4. Write the tangent in point-slope form: $y - y_1 = m_T(x - x_1)$.
  5. Multiply through to clear fractions and rearrange into the required form (e.g. $ax + by + c = 0$ with integer coefficients).
  6. Optional check: confirm P satisfies the tangent equation, and that the line meets the circle only at P.

Worked Micro-Example

Circle has centre $C(8, -6)$ and passes through $S(1, 18)$. Then $m_{CS} = (18-(-6))/(1-8) = 24/(-7) = -24/7$, so $m_T = 7/24$. Tangent at S: $y - 18 = \tfrac{7}{24}(x - 1)$. Multiplying by 24: $24y - 432 = 7x - 7$, giving $7x - 24y + 425 = 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Circle equation given in expanded form $x^2 + y^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$
Complete the square to extract centre $(-D/2, -E/2)$ before computing $m_{CP}$.
Circle centred at the origin, point $(x_1, y_1)$ on $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$
Tangent can be written directly as $x x_1 + y y_1 = r^2$, bypassing the gradient calculation.
Radius CP is horizontal ($y_C = y_1$)
$m_{CP} = 0$, so the tangent is vertical: $x = x_1$ (do not attempt the negative reciprocal).
Radius CP is vertical ($x_C = x_1$)
$m_{CP}$ is undefined, so the tangent is horizontal: $y = y_1$.
Circle given implicitly, contact point known
Implicit differentiation of the circle equation gives the same tangent gradient at $(x_1, y_1)$ — useful as a check.

Common Misreadings

  • Computing the gradient of a chord through P (e.g. a diameter QS) instead of the radius CP, especially when the chord was just used in a previous part.
  • Treating a point that is not actually on the circle as the point of tangency, leading to a 'tangent' that is really a secant or misses the circle.
  • Confusing 'tangent at P' with 'tangent from an external point', which requires a different method.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to take the negative reciprocal and using $m_{CP}$ itself as the tangent gradient.
  • Arithmetic slips when clearing fractions to obtain integer-coefficient form $ax + by + c = 0$.
  • Sign errors in the final rearrangement, producing the wrong constant term.
  • Failing to handle the special cases where CP is horizontal or vertical (vertical/horizontal tangent).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P2 coordinate geometry of the circle): uses only the centre–radius perpendicularity property, gradient of a line segment, negative reciprocals, and point-slope form. No calculus required, though implicit differentiation (P3/P4) gives an equivalent gradient as a cross-check.
