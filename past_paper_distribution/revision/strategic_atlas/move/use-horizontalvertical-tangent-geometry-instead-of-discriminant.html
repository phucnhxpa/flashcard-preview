Coordinate Geometry

Use horizontal/vertical tangent geometry instead of discriminant

Family: Geometric Shortcut Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a tangent line to a circle is horizontal (y = k) or vertical (x = k), and the circle's centre and radius are already known or easily found. Cues include phrases like 'find the values of k for which y = k is tangent to the circle' or 'horizontal line touches the circle'. The axis-aligned nature of the line removes the need for any algebraic manipulation.

Motivation — Why this works

A horizontal line y = k touches a circle exactly when it grazes the top or bottom of the circle, i.e. at y = (centre y) ± r. Equivalently, the perpendicular distance from the centre (a, b) to y = k is |b − k|, so tangency requires |b − k| = r. This bypasses the algebraic route of substituting y = k into the circle equation and forcing the resulting quadratic in x to have discriminant zero, which gives the same answer but with much more work.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the tangent line is axis-aligned (y = k or x = k), so it has no slope component to handle.
  2. Rule out the discriminant method as overkill — substitution would just rediscover the top/bottom of the circle algebraically.
  3. Commit to the geometric shortcut: extreme y-values of the circle are (centre y) ± radius.
  4. Bridge to operation: read off the centre (a, b) and radius r from the circle equation in completed-square form.
  5. Tactically write down both values k = b + r and k = b − r, ensuring the ± is preserved.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rewrite the circle in form (x − a)² + (y − b)² = r² to identify centre (a, b) and radius r.
  2. For a horizontal tangent y = k, set k = b ± r.
  3. For a vertical tangent x = k, set k = a ± r.
  4. State both values of k.

Worked Micro-Example

Circle: $(x+3)^2 + (y-2)^2 = 27$, so centre $(-3, 2)$ and radius $r = 3\sqrt{3}$. The horizontal line $y = k$ is tangent when $k = 2 \pm 3\sqrt{3}$. Hence $k = 2 + 3\sqrt{3}$ or $k = 2 - 3\sqrt{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Vertical tangent line x = k to a circle with centre (a, b)
Use k = a ± r instead of b ± r.
Tangent line with general slope, e.g. y = mx + c
Use the perpendicular distance formula d = |ma − b + c|/√(m² + 1) = r, since no axis-symmetry shortcut applies.
Tangent from an external point to a circle
Use the right-angle property: distance from point to centre, radius, and tangent length form a right triangle (Pythagoras).
Horizontal/vertical tangent to a circle given in expanded form x² + y² + Dx + Ey + F = 0
First complete the square to extract centre and radius, then apply the same ± shortcut.

Common Misreadings

  • Defaulting to the algebraic discriminant method (substitute y = k, set Δ = 0) when the geometry is immediate.
  • Treating y = k as a generic tangent and setting up the perpendicular distance formula in full when the simpler |b − k| = r suffices.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the ± and only reporting one value of k.
  • Using the x-coordinate of the centre instead of the y-coordinate (or vice versa for vertical tangents).
  • Misreading r² from the circle equation as r (e.g. taking r = 27 instead of r = 3√3).

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics coordinate geometry of the circle (P2): completing the square to find centre and radius, and tangency conditions. Uses no tools beyond the standard circle equation.
