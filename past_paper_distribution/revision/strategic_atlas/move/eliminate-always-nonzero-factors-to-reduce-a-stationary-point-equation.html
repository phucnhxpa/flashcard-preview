Strategic Atlas / Differentiation / Zero Product Elimination
Differentiation

Eliminate always-nonzero factors to reduce a stationary-point equation

Family: Zero Product Elimination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when f'(x)=0 has been written as a product of two or more factors and at least one factor is structurally incapable of being zero on the relevant domain. Typical signals: an exponential factor like $e^{kx}$, a reciprocal trig factor like $\sec(kx)$ or $\csc(kx)$, a positive constant, or a sum of squares appearing alongside a 'real' bracket. Phrased as 'show that the stationary points satisfy ...' or 'hence find x such that f'(x)=0'. The remaining bracket is usually a clean linear, quadratic, or single-trig equation.

Motivation — Why this works

By the zero-product property, a product equals zero iff at least one factor equals zero, so any factor that can never vanish contributes no solutions. $e^{kx}>0$ for all real $x$, $\sec$ and $\csc$ are never zero (they are either nonzero or undefined), and strictly positive expressions like $1+x^2$ likewise cannot be zero. Discarding these factors is logically equivalent to dividing both sides by a nonzero quantity, which preserves the solution set. The result is a single, much simpler equation in one variable.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that $f'(x)$ has been factorised as a product of two or more pieces set equal to zero.
  2. Scan each factor and rule out those that cannot be zero on the stated domain — exponentials, secants/cosecants, strictly positive expressions.
  3. Commit to the only factor that can vanish, since by the zero-product property it carries all the stationary-point information.
  4. Bridge to the concrete operation: write down the surviving equation, e.g. $3+2\tan(2x)=0$, and isolate the trig/algebraic term.
  5. Decide on the tactical follow-up — solve within the given interval, check domain endpoints, and confirm the discarded factors are genuinely defined throughout.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write $f'(x)$ in fully factorised form.
  2. For each factor, justify in one line why it is nonzero (or zero) on the given domain.
  3. Strike out the always-nonzero factors and equate the remaining factor(s) to zero.
  4. Solve the reduced equation by standard techniques (linear, quadratic, or trig).
  5. Check each solution lies in the stated domain.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $g'(x)=e^{3x}\sec(2x)\bigl(3+2\tan(2x)\bigr)=0$ on $-\tfrac{\pi}{4}0$ and $\sec(2x)\neq 0$ throughout this interval, the equation reduces to $3+2\tan(2x)=0$, i.e. $\tan(2x)=-\tfrac{3}{2}$. Hence $2x=\arctan(-\tfrac{3}{2})$, giving $x=\tfrac{1}{2}\arctan(-\tfrac{3}{2})$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$f'(x)=e^{x}(x^2-4)=0$
Discard $e^{x}$ since it is positive; solve $x^2-4=0$ to get $x=\pm 2$.
Product rule gives $f'(x)=\cos(x)\bigl(2\sin(x)-1\bigr)$ but only the bracket is required
Here $\cos(x)$ CAN be zero, so do not discard it — both factors yield solutions.
$f'(x)=\dfrac{2x-3}{1+x^2}=0$
Denominator $1+x^2>0$ is never zero, so the fraction is zero iff numerator $2x-3=0$.
$f'(x)=\csc(x)\bigl(1-2\cos x\bigr)=0$
$\csc(x)\neq 0$ where defined, so reduce to $\cos x=\tfrac12$.
Implicit differentiation yields $(x^2+1)\dfrac{dy}{dx}=h(x)$ for stationary points
$x^2+1>0$ allows division; stationary points come from $h(x)=0$ alone.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $\sec(2x)=0$ or $e^{3x}=0$ as legitimate cases and trying to solve them.
  • Confusing 'never zero' with 'always defined' — $\sec$ is never zero but can be undefined.
  • Forgetting the zero-product principle and instead expanding the factorised derivative back out.

Pitfalls

  • Discarding a factor without verifying it is nonzero on the entire stated domain (e.g. $\sec(2x)$ at $x=\pm\pi/4$).
  • Dividing by a factor that could be zero elsewhere, thereby losing valid stationary points.
  • Solving the reduced trig equation without restricting to the given interval, producing extraneous roots.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics: uses positivity of $e^{kx}$, the definition of $\sec/\csc$, and the zero-product property — all standard at P2/P3 level.
