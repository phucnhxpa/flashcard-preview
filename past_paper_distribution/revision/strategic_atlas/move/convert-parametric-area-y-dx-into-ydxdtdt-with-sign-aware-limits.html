Integration

Convert parametric area ∫y dx into ∫y(dx/dt)dt with sign-aware limits

Family: Parametric Area Conversion Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a curve is given in parametric form x = x(t), y = y(t) and the question asks for the area under the curve (between the curve and the x-axis), or an area bounded by such a curve and vertical lines. Visual cues: 'the curve C has parametric equations…', x-limits given as numbers (e.g. x = 0 and x = a) rather than t-limits, and y not expressible neatly as a function of x. Equivalent phrasings: 'find the area of region R bounded by C and the x-axis', or 'show that area = ∫₀^a … dt'.

Motivation — Why this works

Since y and x are both functions of t, the integral ∫y dx cannot be evaluated directly in x. The substitution dx = (dx/dt) dt converts everything into a single-variable integral in t, which is tractable. This is just the chain rule applied to the differential dx, and it is the standard parametric-area formula in the Edexcel formula book. The sign care (handling dx/dt < 0 or reversed limits) ensures the geometric area comes out positive.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the curve is parametric: y is given via t and x is given via t, so I cannot simply integrate y(x) dx in closed form.
  2. Rule out: solving x(t) for t and substituting into y(t) — usually messy or impossible (e.g. x = 2cos2t inverts non-uniquely); also rule out splitting into y = f(x) for direct integration.
  3. Commit to the parametric area formula A = ∫ y (dx/dt) dt, because it is exactly designed for this situation and keeps everything in t.
  4. Bridge to operation: differentiate x(t) to get dx/dt, then convert the given x-limits into t-limits by solving x(t) = (x-bound) for t.
  5. Tactical sign check: inspect the sign of dx/dt over the t-interval. If dx/dt < 0 (x decreasing as t increases), either insert a leading minus sign or swap the t-limits so that the integrand stays positive and the answer represents true (positive) area.
  6. Finally, simplify the resulting integrand in t and identify any constant K the question is asking you to confirm.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Differentiate x(t) to obtain dx/dt.
  2. Solve x(t) = x_lower and x(t) = x_upper to get the corresponding t-limits t₁, t₂.
  3. Substitute into A = ∫_{t₁}^{t₂} y(t) · (dx/dt) dt.
  4. Check sign: if t₁ > t₂ or dx/dt < 0, flip limits and/or attach a minus sign so the integrand is positive on the interval.
  5. Simplify the integrand to the form requested (e.g. match a target ∫₀^{a} f(t) dt to read off K).

Worked Micro-Example

Curve: $x = 9/4 - t$, $y = t^3/\sqrt{81-16t^2}$. Find area from $x=0$ to $x=9/4$. Then $dx/dt = -1$; limits: $x=0\Rightarrow t=9/4$, $x=9/4\Rightarrow t=0$. So $A = \int_{9/4}^{0} \frac{t^3}{\sqrt{81-16t^2}}(-1)\,dt = \int_{0}^{9/4} \frac{t^3}{\sqrt{81-16t^2}}\,dt$ (the two sign flips cancel).

Variants — Same move, different surface

x is a decreasing function of t on the integration interval (e.g. x = 2cos2t on [0, π/4])
Either prepend a minus sign: A = -∫ y(dx/dt)dt, or reverse the t-limits — both absorb the negative dx/dt to give positive area.
Parametric volume of revolution about the x-axis
Replace y dx with πy² dx, then use the same dx = (dx/dt) dt conversion: V = ∫ πy²(t)(dx/dt) dt.
x(t) is non-monotonic on the t-interval (e.g. closed loop / ellipse)
Split the t-interval at turning points where dx/dt = 0 and treat each monotonic piece separately, or use |dx/dt| with care about signed vs geometric area.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the given x-limits as t-limits and integrating ∫₀^{x_upper} y(t)(dx/dt) dt without conversion — produces a meaningless mixed-variable bound.
  • Using dy/dt instead of dx/dt in the substitution because of confusion about which differential is being replaced.
  • Trying to eliminate t to get y as a function of x when the question is clearly steering you to the parametric formula.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error: with both a negative dx/dt AND reversed limits, two negatives must cancel — forgetting one gives -A instead of A.
  • Plugging an x-bound into the y-equation (or vice versa) when finding t-limits — always solve x(t) = x_bound for t.
  • Forgetting to check that the t-interval lies within the parameter's natural domain (e.g. avoiding values where the integrand is undefined).

A-level Scope Notes

This is the standard parametric area result quoted (in essence) in the Edexcel IAL P3/P4 formula book entry for parametric integration: 'Area = ∫ y (dx/dt) dt'. Stay within polynomial, rational, trig, exponential and root integrands typical at IAL level; do not invoke Green's theorem or signed-area arguments beyond the syllabus.
