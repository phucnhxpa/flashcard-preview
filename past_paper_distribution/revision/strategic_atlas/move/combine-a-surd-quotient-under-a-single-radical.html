Combine a surd quotient under a single radical

Family: Surd Simplification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The expression is a quotient of two square roots, e.g. √a / √b, and you need a clean numerical value — typically in a 'show that' question demanding an integer or simple surd. The cue is strongest when a/b is recognisably a perfect square or has obvious cancellation. Phrasings include 'show that √180/√5 is an integer' or 'simplify √a/√b giving your answer in simplest form'.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity √a / √b = √(a/b) (for a ≥ 0, b > 0) merges two radicals into one, replacing a surd-division problem with a single arithmetic division inside a root. If a/b turns out to be a perfect square, the whole expression collapses to an integer in one line. This avoids rationalising denominators or factorising both surds separately, giving the most efficient route to a 'show that' answer.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the structure is √(numerator) over √(denominator) with both arguments numeric and small enough to divide mentally.
  2. Rules out rationalising the denominator (multiplying by √5/√5) as wasteful — it leads to 6√5·√5/5 = 30/5 = 6, but with extra steps that risk arithmetic slips.
  3. Commits to the single-radical identity because 180/5 is obviously 36, a perfect square, so √(180/5) lands on an integer immediately.
  4. Bridges to operation: write √180/√5 = √(180/5) on the next line, fully justified by the surd quotient law.
  5. Tactically evaluates 180/5 = 36, then √36 = 6, and explicitly states '6 is an integer' to secure the 'show that' mark.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Apply the identity √a / √b = √(a/b): √180 / √5 = √(180/5).
  2. Perform the division inside: 180 ÷ 5 = 36.
  3. Evaluate the resulting square root: √36 = 6.
  4. State the conclusion: 6 is an integer, as required.

Worked Micro-Example

Show that $\dfrac{\sqrt{180}}{\sqrt{5}}$ is an integer.\\ $\dfrac{\sqrt{180}}{\sqrt{5}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{180}{5}} = \sqrt{36} = 6$.\\ Since $6 \in \mathbb{Z}$, the expression is an integer.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Surd quotient that simplifies to a smaller surd, e.g. √75/√3
Same identity gives √25 = 5; here the quotient is a perfect square, but in √50/√2 = √25 = 5 you still apply it identically.
Surd product rather than quotient, e.g. √8 × √2
Use the partner identity √a × √b = √(ab) to get √16 = 4 — same merging idea applied to multiplication.
Algebraic surds, e.g. √(12x³)/√(3x)
Apply √a/√b = √(a/b) treating x symbolically: √(4x²) = 2x (with x ≥ 0 noted).
Quotient that doesn't reduce to a perfect square, e.g. √50/√5
Still combine: √(50/5) = √10, leaving the answer in surd form rather than as an integer.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating it as needing rationalisation (multiplying top and bottom by √5) when the direct identity is faster.
  • Trying to simplify √180 and √5 separately and cancel, which works but is longer and more error-prone.
  • Misreading 'show that it is an integer' as 'evaluate' and stopping at √36 without stating the integer value.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic error in 180 ÷ 5, e.g. writing 38 instead of 36.
  • Forgetting to state explicitly that the final value (6) is an integer — 'show that' requires the conclusion.
  • Applying the identity when the radicand could be negative; the rule needs a ≥ 0 and b > 0.
  • Writing √(a/b) = √a/√b without checking the quotient simplifies — sometimes the original form is already simpler.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths P1 (surds and indices). The identity √a/√b = √(a/b) for non-negative a and positive b is standard and routinely used in 'show that' surd questions worth 1–2 marks.
