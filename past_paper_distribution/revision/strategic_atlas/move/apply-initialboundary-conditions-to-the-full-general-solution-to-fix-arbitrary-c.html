Differential Equations

Apply initial/boundary conditions to the full general solution to fix arbitrary constants

Family: Initial Value Problem Frequency: 5 questions

Trigger — When to use

The general solution y = CF + PI (or V = ... + C for a first-order ODE) has been written down and contains arbitrary constants. The question supplies matching conditions: one condition like y(0) = y₀ for a first-order ODE, or two conditions such as y(0) = a and y'(0) = b for a second-order ODE. Phrasings include 'given that y = ... when x = 0', 'subject to the initial conditions', or 'the curve passes through ...'. The number of conditions always matches the number of arbitrary constants, signalling that a unique particular solution is required.

Motivation — Why this works

The general solution of an n-th order linear ODE is an n-parameter family of curves; supplying n independent conditions collapses the family to a single particular solution. Substituting each condition produces one linear equation in the unknown constants, giving a determined system. Crucially the conditions act on the FULL general solution (CF + PI together), since the particular integral also contributes a value at x = 0. This is the standard way to convert a 'general solution' answer into a 'particular solution' answer, and it is the final step of every IVP/BVP question.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the general solution still contains arbitrary constants (one C, or A and B), so the answer is not yet unique — the question must therefore provide enough conditions to determine them.
  2. Rule out alternatives: I am not being asked to verify a solution, find an integrating factor, or classify stability — the presence of explicit numerical conditions y(0)=…, y'(0)=… signals 'pin down the constants'.
  3. Commit to substitution: count the constants and count the conditions; they match, so a 1×1 or 2×2 linear system in A, B (or just C) will result.
  4. Plan the order: substitute the y-condition first (cleanest, no differentiation needed), then differentiate the FULL general solution (CF + PI together) before substituting the y'-condition. Differentiating only the CF is the classic error to avoid.
  5. Decide the algebra route: from equation (1) express A in terms of B (or vice versa), substitute into equation (2), solve for one constant, back-substitute for the other. Keep fractions over a common denominator throughout.
  6. Finally, write the particular solution by substituting the determined constants back into y = CF + PI.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the general solution y = CF + PI with arbitrary constants (e.g. A, B, or single C).
  2. Substitute the first condition (typically x = 0, y = y₀) directly into y to obtain equation (1) in the constants. Use e^0 = 1, sin 0 = 0, cos 0 = 1, ln(·) carefully.
  3. Differentiate the ENTIRE general solution (CF and PI) with respect to x to obtain y'.
  4. Substitute the second condition (e.g. x = 0, y' = y₁) into y' to obtain equation (2).
  5. Solve the resulting simultaneous equations: from (1) express one constant in terms of the other, substitute into (2), solve, and back-substitute.
  6. Write the final particular solution with the numerical values of A and B (or C) inserted.

Worked Micro-Example

General solution: $y = Ae^{-2x} + Be^{-3x} + \tfrac{1}{10}\cos x + \tfrac{1}{10}\sin x$, with $y(0)=0$, $y'(0)=0$.\n$y(0)=0 \Rightarrow A+B+\tfrac{1}{10}=0$ … (1).\n$y' = -2Ae^{-2x} - 3Be^{-3x} - \tfrac{1}{10}\sin x + \tfrac{1}{10}\cos x$, so $y'(0)=0 \Rightarrow -2A-3B+\tfrac{1}{10}=0$ … (2).\nSolving: $A=-\tfrac{2}{5}$, $B=\tfrac{3}{10}$, giving the particular solution.

Variants — Same move, different surface

First-order ODE with one condition (e.g. V = 0 when t = 0)
Only one constant C; substitute the single condition directly into the integrated general solution and solve for C — no differentiation needed.
Boundary conditions at two different x-values (e.g. y(0) = 1, y(1) = 0)
Same procedure but evaluate y at x = 0 and x = 1 separately; no derivative is needed, but exponential terms at x = 1 stay symbolic.
CF of repeated-root form (A + Bx)e^{mx}
When differentiating, apply the product rule carefully: y' = Be^{mx} + m(A+Bx)e^{mx}, then evaluate at x = 0.
Condition involves y''
Substitute into the original ODE to express y''(0) in terms of y(0) and y'(0), reducing to the standard two-condition case.

Common Misreadings

  • Applying the conditions to the CF only, ignoring the PI's contribution at x = 0.
  • Treating y'(0) = 0 as if it referred to the original ODE rather than the differentiated general solution.
  • Confusing 'general solution' with 'particular solution' and stopping before substituting the conditions.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to differentiate the PI when forming y' — the PI's derivative contributes to equation (2).
  • Sign errors evaluating sin 0 = 0, cos 0 = 1, and negative exponents at 0.
  • Fraction arithmetic slips when the PI introduces denominators like 16, 64, or 10 — keep a common denominator.
  • Product-rule errors when the CF has the form (A + Bx)e^{mx}.
  • Leaving '+ C' in the final answer because the initial condition step was skipped entirely.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL P3/FP2 (first-order separable ODEs and second-order linear ODEs with constant coefficients). Uses no formulae from the booklet beyond standard derivatives. Avoid invoking Laplace transforms, variation of parameters, or matrix exponentials — these are out of scope.
