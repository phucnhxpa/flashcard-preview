Coordinate Geometry

Clear fractions to present a line as ax + by + c = 0 with integer coefficients

Family: Standard Form Presentation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires after deriving a straight-line equation in point-gradient or slope-intercept form whose gradient (or intercept) is a fraction, e.g. y − 3 = −(5/8)(x − 2). Also triggers when the question explicitly asks for the answer in the form ax + by + c = 0 where a, b, c are integers, or says 'give your answer in the form...'. The cue is any non-integer coefficient appearing in an otherwise correct line equation. On Edexcel mark schemes the final accuracy mark is reserved for the tidy integer form.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying both sides of the equation by the lowest common denominator clears all fractions simultaneously, preserving the equality. Once fractions are gone, expanding brackets and collecting terms on one side gives the conventional Ax + By + C = 0 presentation. This is the standard mark-scheme form because it is unique up to an overall sign and avoids ambiguity in how the answer is written. Conventionally A is taken positive, which sometimes requires multiplying through by −1 at the end.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the current equation is correct but contains a fraction in the gradient or intercept, and that the question (or marking convention) demands integer coefficients.
  2. Rule out leaving the answer in point-gradient form or y = mx + c with a fractional m, since these will lose the final A-mark on Edexcel papers asking for ax + by + c = 0.
  3. Commit to multiplying the entire equation by the denominator (or LCM of denominators) to clear fractions in a single move before any rearrangement.
  4. Bridge: write the equation with brackets intact, multiply both sides by the denominator, then expand carefully watching signs of negative gradients.
  5. Tactically decide which side to collect terms on so that the leading coefficient A is positive, and double-check by substituting the original point to confirm the line still passes through it.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Start from point-gradient form, e.g. y − 3 = −(5/8)(x − 2).
  2. Multiply both sides by the denominator 8: 8(y − 3) = −5(x − 2).
  3. Expand both sides: 8y − 24 = −5x + 10.
  4. Move all terms to one side: 5x + 8y − 34 = 0.
  5. Check sign convention (A > 0) and verify by substituting a known point on the line.

Worked Micro-Example

Line through (2, 3) with gradient −5/8: y − 3 = −\tfrac{5}{8}(x − 2). Multiply by 8: 8(y − 3) = −5(x − 2). Expand: 8y − 24 = −5x + 10. Rearrange: 5x + 8y − 34 = 0. Check: 5(2) + 8(3) − 34 = 10 + 24 − 34 = 0. ✓

Variants — Same move, different surface

Gradient is a terminating decimal such as 0.75
Rewrite as 3/4, then multiply through by 4 to clear the denominator before rearranging.
Both endpoints give a gradient like 7/3 from two-point form
After substituting into y − y₁ = m(x − x₁), multiply by 3 to clear, then collect into ax + by + c = 0.
Perpendicular line whose gradient is the negative reciprocal, e.g. 8/5
Same strategy: multiply by 5, expand, and rearrange; ensure the leading x-coefficient is positive.
Question asks for y = mx + c form instead
Skip the clearing step and instead expand and isolate y, leaving m as a fraction if necessary.
Coefficients share a common factor after rearranging, e.g. 10x + 16y − 68 = 0
Divide through by the gcd to give the simplest integer form 5x + 8y − 34 = 0.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'find the equation of the line' as permitting fractional coefficients when the question explicitly demands integer form.
  • Thinking the answer 5x + 8y = 34 is wrong because it isn't '= 0'; it is equivalent but mark schemes targeting ax + by + c = 0 want everything on one side.
  • Believing only the gradient's denominator matters, when an intercept fraction can also require clearing.
  • Assuming the integer form is unique without a sign convention, leading to disagreement with the mark scheme over ±.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when expanding −5(x − 2): must give −5x + 10, not −5x − 10.
  • Forgetting to multiply every term on both sides by the denominator (e.g. multiplying only the right-hand side).
  • Leaving a common factor in the final answer, e.g. 10x + 16y − 34 = 0 instead of 5x + 8y − 17 = 0 if a factor of 2 was missed.
  • Presenting with negative leading coefficient when the convention (or mark scheme) wants A > 0.
  • Stopping at 8(y − 3) = −5(x − 2) without expanding and collecting terms.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics P1 coordinate geometry: equations of straight lines, gradients, and the standard ax + by + c = 0 presentation expected on mark schemes.
