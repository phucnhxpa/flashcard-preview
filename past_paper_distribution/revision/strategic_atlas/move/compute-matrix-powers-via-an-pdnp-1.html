Matrices

Compute matrix powers via A^n = PD^nP^{-1}

Family: Diagonalisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question asks for a power of a square matrix — either a specific numerical power like A^5 or A^{10}, or a general/symbolic A^n — and the matrix has already been diagonalised (or is clearly diagonalisable) as A = PDP^{-1}. Cues include phrasings such as 'find A^n', 'hence find A^k', 'deduce A^n in terms of n', or part (b)/(c) of a question whose earlier part produced eigenvalues, eigenvectors, P, and D. The move also fires when an iterative/transition matrix is raised to a power to model long-term behaviour.

Motivation — Why this works

Because (PDP^{-1})^n telescopes: every interior P^{-1}P collapses to the identity, leaving PD^nP^{-1}. Diagonal matrices commute with themselves and raise entrywise, so D^n = diag(λ_1^n, λ_2^n, …). This converts an otherwise intractable repeated multiplication (especially for symbolic n) into one diagonal exponentiation plus two ordinary matrix products, using P and P^{-1} that are already in hand.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question demands A^n for a specific or symbolic n, and direct repeated multiplication is impractical or impossible.
  2. Rule out brute-force multiplication and rule out trying to find a pattern by computing A^2, A^3 directly — both are slow and fragile for symbolic n.
  3. Commit to the diagonalisation route because A = PDP^{-1} is already established (or easy to establish) and the telescoping identity A^n = PD^nP^{-1} is exact.
  4. Bridge: write down D^n by raising each eigenvalue to the n-th power, keeping P and P^{-1} from the earlier diagonalisation work.
  5. Tactically, form PD^n first (this is just scaling the columns of P by λ_i^n), then right-multiply by P^{-1}; keep n symbolic until the final entries to get a clean closed form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. State A^n = PD^nP^{-1} with the P, D, P^{-1} already obtained.
  2. Write D^n = diag(λ_1^n, λ_2^n, …).
  3. Compute PD^n by scaling each column of P by the corresponding λ_i^n.
  4. Right-multiply by P^{-1} to obtain A^n.
  5. Collect entries as expressions in n; if n is specific, substitute and evaluate.

Worked Micro-Example

Let A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} with eigenvalues 3, 2 and P = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}, P^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}. Then D^n = \begin{pmatrix} 3^n & 0 \\ 0 & 2^n \end{pmatrix}, so PD^n = \begin{pmatrix} 3^n & 2^n \\ 0 & -2^n \end{pmatrix}, and A^n = PD^nP^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 3^n & 3^n - 2^n \\ 0 & 2^n \end{pmatrix}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Inverse of A (when all eigenvalues are non-zero)
Use A^{-1} = PD^{-1}P^{-1} with D^{-1} = diag(1/λ_1, 1/λ_2, …); this is just the n = -1 case of the same identity.
Negative or symbolic integer powers A^{-n}
Apply A^{-n} = PD^{-n}P^{-1} with D^{-n} = diag(λ_i^{-n}); requires every λ_i ≠ 0.
3×3 transition/stochastic matrix raised to a high power
Same procedure; the dominant eigenvalue (often 1) controls the long-term limit since other |λ_i|^n → 0 as n → ∞.
Polynomial in A, e.g. A^3 - 2A + I
Replace D^n by p(D) = diag(p(λ_i)), then sandwich: p(A) = P·p(D)·P^{-1}.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 'find A^n' as a request to spot a pattern from A, A^2, A^3 rather than to use the diagonalisation already established.
  • Treating PD^nP^{-1} as if D^n could be replaced by D itself, i.e. forgetting that the exponent transfers onto D.
  • Assuming the formula needs A to be symmetric — it only needs A to be diagonalisable.

Pitfalls

  • Writing the product in the wrong order: P^{-1}D^nP instead of PD^nP^{-1} (matrix multiplication is non-commutative).
  • Pairing eigenvalues with the wrong eigenvectors, so that D and P are inconsistent — the i-th column of P must correspond to the i-th diagonal entry of D.
  • Forgetting to right-multiply by P^{-1} and stopping at PD^n.
  • Arithmetic slips when raising negative or fractional eigenvalues to n, e.g. mishandling (-1)^n or (1/2)^n.
  • Using this when A is not diagonalisable (repeated eigenvalue with insufficient eigenvectors) — the method then fails.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope: applies to 2×2 and 3×3 diagonalisable matrices over ℝ with real eigenvalues, where P and P^{-1} are computed by hand. Matrix exponentials e^{At} are beyond IAL and are not expected.
