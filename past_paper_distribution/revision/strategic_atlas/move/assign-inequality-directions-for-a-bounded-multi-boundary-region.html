Coordinate Geometry

Assign inequality directions for a bounded multi-boundary region

Family: Region Inequality Assignment Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question says 'define the region $R$ bounded by' (or 'enclosed by') a list of curves and lines — typically a curve, a line, and one or both coordinate axes — and asks for a system of inequalities. Cue words: 'region', 'bounded', 'enclosed', 'shaded', combined with multiple named boundaries. Also fires when a sketch shows a closed region whose algebraic description is required.

Motivation — Why this works

Each boundary splits the plane into two half-regions, and the enclosed region is the intersection of the correct half-region for every boundary. Whether the region sits above or below a curve/line is a geometric fact about that specific enclosure, not a fixed rule, so the relative position of boundaries inside $R$ must be read off the picture. Once the position is read, $\le$ vs $\ge$ follows directly, and a single interior test point confirms all four directions at once.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the word 'bounded' with several listed boundaries — this is a region-as-intersection-of-half-planes problem, not a single-inequality problem.
  2. Rule out applying a memorised 'lower function $\le$' rule: the directions depend on where $R$ sits relative to each boundary, which I must read from a sketch.
  3. Commit once I have located $R$ on a sketch and identified, for each boundary, which side of it $R$ lies on.
  4. Bridge to operation: pick an obvious interior point of $R$ (e.g. a lattice point well inside) to act as a universal tester.
  5. For each boundary in turn, write the inequality that the test point satisfies; collect the four inequalities as the answer.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. List every boundary the question names (curve, line, $x$-axis, $y$-axis, etc.).
  2. Sketch the boundaries and shade the enclosed region $R$, marking key intersection points.
  3. Pick a clearly interior test point $(x_0, y_0)$ of $R$.
  4. For each boundary $f(x,y)=0$, evaluate $f(x_0,y_0)$ and record whether $R$ corresponds to $f \le 0$ or $f \ge 0$ (equivalently $y \le \ldots$ or $y \ge \ldots$).
  5. Write the four (non-strict) inequalities as the system defining $R$.
  6. Sanity-check by testing a point just outside $R$ across one boundary — at least one inequality should fail.

Worked Micro-Example

Region $R$ bounded by $y = 2x^2 - 12x + 14$, $y = -8x + 20$, the $x$-axis and $y$-axis. Sketch shows $R$ in the first quadrant, below the line and above the curve. Test point $(1,4)$: line gives $-8(1)+20=12$, so $y \le -8x+20$; curve gives $2-12+14=4$, so $y \ge 2x^2-12x+14$; clearly $x \ge 0$ and $y \ge 0$. Hence $R:\ y \le -8x+20,\ y \ge 2x^2-12x+14,\ x \ge 0,\ y \ge 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Region above a line and below a curve (e.g. line cuts beneath an upward-opening parabola)
Inequalities reverse to $y \ge mx+c$ and $y \le f(x)$; never apply 'curve $\le$' or 'line $\le$' as a fixed rule.
Region not in the first quadrant (e.g. bounded on the right by $x = a$ or below by $y = b$)
Replace $x \ge 0$ / $y \ge 0$ with $x \le a$, $y \ge b$, etc., chosen so the test point satisfies them.
Region bounded by two curves intersecting at two points (no axes involved)
Only two inequalities arise; identify which curve is the upper boundary on the relevant interval and assign $\le$/$\ge$ accordingly, often together with $a \le x \le b$ from the intersection $x$-coordinates.
Region bounded by a circle and a line (Further Pure / coordinate geometry)
Use $(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2 \le r^2$ for inside the circle, combined with the appropriate half-plane inequality from the line.

Common Misreadings

  • Reversing the curve/line inequalities by assuming the 'lower' function always gets $\le$, without checking which side $R$ is on.
  • Omitting $x \ge 0$ or $y \ge 0$ because the axes are seen as a frame rather than as boundaries.
  • Treating 'bounded by' as a hint that all inequalities go the same way (e.g. all $\le$).

Pitfalls

  • Guessing inequality directions without a sketch, especially when the curve dips below the $x$-axis so that $y \ge 0$ is an active constraint and not redundant.
  • Writing strict inequalities $<,>$ instead of $\le,\ge$; 'bounded by' includes the boundary itself.
  • Choosing a test point on a boundary (giving equality) and being unable to decide the direction — pick a point strictly inside $R$.
  • Forgetting to rearrange implicit boundaries into a clean $y \le \ldots$ or $y \ge \ldots$ form expected by the mark scheme.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure content: regions defined by lines, quadratics/cubics, and coordinate axes, described by systems of non-strict inequalities. No linear programming objective functions or vertex optimisation are required at this level.
