Algebra & Functions

Absorb leading coefficient into fractional factor to clear denominators before expanding

Family: Strategic Distribution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a factored expression has the shape A·(quadratic with fractional coefficients)·(linear or other factor), where A is an integer whose value matches the LCM of the denominators inside one factor. Typical context: after reconstructing a polynomial from its roots and a known leading coefficient (e.g. cubic from sum/product of roots), or after dividing through to make a factor monic and now needing to re-expand. Cue phrases: 'find the cubic with integer coefficients', 'expand and simplify', or seeing fractions like 3/4, 5/4 inside one factor and an integer 4 sitting outside.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplication is associative, so A·(B)·(C) = (A·B)·C. If A exactly clears the denominators of B, then A·B becomes an integer-coefficient polynomial of the same degree. This converts fraction arithmetic — which is slow and error-prone — into integer arithmetic, while leaving the final product unchanged. It exploits the identity that scaling one factor of a product by k scales the whole product by k, so the leading-coefficient slot is the natural place to deposit the cleared fractions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the form A·(quadratic with /4 coefficients)·(linear), with A=4 sitting outside ready to be distributed somewhere.
  2. Rule out expanding the two polynomial factors first and multiplying by 4 at the end — that drags fractions through every intermediate term.
  3. Commit to absorbing A into the fractional factor because 4 cleanly cancels the 4's in the denominators, yielding integer coefficients.
  4. Bridge: rewrite 4·(x² − 3x/4 + 5/4) as (4x² − 3x + 5) by distributing 4 term-by-term into that factor only.
  5. Tactical check: confirm A is distributed into exactly one factor (not both), then proceed with a clean integer × integer polynomial multiplication.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the integer leading coefficient A sitting outside a factor with fractional coefficients.
  2. Check that A is a multiple of the LCM of the denominators inside that factor.
  3. Distribute A into that factor only, multiplying every term (including the constant) by A.
  4. Replace the original A·(fractional factor) with the new integer-coefficient polynomial.
  5. Expand the remaining product using standard integer polynomial multiplication.

Worked Micro-Example

Expand $4\left(x^2 - \tfrac{3}{4}x + \tfrac{5}{4}\right)(x-4)$.\\ Absorb the 4: $4\left(x^2 - \tfrac{3}{4}x + \tfrac{5}{4}\right) = 4x^2 - 3x + 5$.\\ Now expand $(4x^2 - 3x + 5)(x-4) = 4x^3 - 16x^2 - 3x^2 + 12x + 5x - 20$\\ $= 4x^3 - 19x^2 + 17x - 20$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Reconstructing a cubic from roots whose sum/product give fractional coefficients in the monic form
Multiply the monic cubic by the required integer leading coefficient to clear denominators before any further manipulation.
Quadratic written as $a(x^2 + (b/a)x + c/a)$ after completing the square or applying Vieta's
Distribute $a$ back inside to recover the integer-coefficient quadratic $ax^2+bx+c$ before expanding products.
Partial fractions with a constant multiplier in front of a sum of fractions
Distribute the constant into each numerator first, then combine — same 'clear before expanding' philosophy.
Binomial expansion of $k(1 + x/n)^m$ where $k$ is an integer multiple of $n^m$
Pull $k$ inside via $(n+x)^m \cdot k/n^m$ trick to keep coefficients integral throughout.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the outer A as if it must multiply the entire expanded product at the end, missing that absorbing it earlier saves work.
  • Reading the expression as A·(B·C) and distributing A into both B and C, instead of into exactly one factor.
  • Assuming the fractions must be cleared by multiplying the whole equation through (as in solving), rather than just regrouping a product.

Pitfalls

  • Multiplying A into both factors, effectively introducing an extra factor of A.
  • Forgetting to multiply the constant term inside the bracket (e.g. leaving 5/4 instead of 5).
  • Sign slip when distributing across a negative fractional term such as $-3x/4$.
  • Distributing A into a factor whose denominators A does not actually clear, reintroducing fractions.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard manipulation within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1–P4): polynomial expansion, factor/remainder theorem reconstructions, and roots-of-polynomials work in Further Pure where Vieta-style relations naturally produce fractional coefficients.
