Vectors

Reuse part (a) angle in |a||b|sinθ for parallelogram area

Family: Area Via Sine Product Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A multi-part vectors question first asks for two side lengths and the included angle (often via the dot-product formula), then asks for the area of a parallelogram or triangle built on those sides. The two adjacent side vectors and their included angle are all already in hand. Phrases like 'Hence find the area of parallelogram ABCD' or 'Hence show that the area is...' strongly cue this chaining. Any time |a|, |b|, and ∠(a,b) have just been computed, the sine-product area formula is the path of least resistance.

Motivation — Why this works

A parallelogram on adjacent sides a and b has area |a||b|sinθ, which is exactly |a × b| — the geometric meaning of the cross-product magnitude. When part (a) has already produced the magnitudes and the included angle, substituting into |a||b|sinθ avoids redoing a 3×3 determinant. It is a deliberate reuse-of-results efficiency: in 'hence' parts the examiner is signalling that earlier work should feed the next step. It also keeps the computation in scalar arithmetic, reducing arithmetic error.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that part (a) has already delivered |BA|, |BC|, and ∠ABC — three of the three ingredients for |a||b|sinθ.
  2. Rule out recomputing |BA × BC| from components: it duplicates work and the word 'Hence' (or the structural cue) tells me to chain.
  3. Commit to Area = |BA||BC|sin(∠ABC) because |a||b|sinθ ≡ |a × b|, so the answer is identical with less algebra.
  4. Bridge: write the formula with the symbolic values, then substitute the stored (unrounded) angle to keep precision.
  5. Tactically, carry the exact cosθ from the dot-product step and use sinθ = √(1 − cos²θ) rather than retyping a rounded angle, so the final area lies inside mark-scheme tolerance.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. State Area(ABCD) = |→BA| · |→BC| · sin(∠ABC).
  2. Substitute the magnitudes from part (a) and the stored (unrounded) angle, e.g. Area = 7 · √93 · sin(112.651…°).
  3. Evaluate to the required precision, e.g. Area ≈ 62.3 (3 s.f.).
  4. Sanity check: sinθ > 0 for 0 < θ < 180°, so the area is positive; compare with a quick |a × b| spot-check if uncertain.

Worked Micro-Example

From part (a): $|\vec{BA}| = 7$, $|\vec{BC}| = \sqrt{93}$, $\cos(\angle ABC) = -\tfrac{15}{7\sqrt{93}}$. Then $\sin(\angle ABC) = \sqrt{1 - \tfrac{225}{49\cdot 93}} = \sqrt{\tfrac{4332}{4557}}$. Area $= 7\sqrt{93}\,\sin(\angle ABC) = \sqrt{49\cdot 93 - 225} = \sqrt{4332} \approx 62.3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Triangle ABC instead of parallelogram
Use Area = ½|a||b|sinθ; halve the parallelogram result.
No angle pre-computed (standalone area question)
Compute |a × b| directly via the 3×3 determinant — usually faster than finding θ first.
2D vectors a = (x₁,y₁), b = (x₂,y₂)
Use the scalar cross-product analogue: Area = |x₁y₂ − x₂y₁| (parallelogram) or ½|x₁y₂ − x₂y₁| (triangle).
Angle given via dot product cosθ only
Avoid converting to degrees; use sinθ = √(1 − cos²θ) to keep an exact surd answer.
Rhombus or square as a special parallelogram
Same formula; |a| = |b| simplifies to Area = |a|² sinθ.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating ABCD as a triangle and halving the area unnecessarily.
  • Trying to use base × perpendicular height without a way to find the height in 3D.
  • Misidentifying which angle is included — using ∠BAD instead of ∠ABC, or the angle between non-adjacent sides.

Pitfalls

  • Substituting a rounded angle (e.g. 112.65°) instead of the stored value, pushing the area outside mark-scheme tolerance.
  • Forgetting to take sin (leaving |a||b|cosθ from the dot-product step by mistake).
  • Sign confusion: writing Area = |a||b|sinθ with a negative cosθ and then negating the area — area is always positive since sinθ > 0 on (0°,180°).
  • Computing sin of the supplement and worrying — sin(180° − θ) = sinθ, so the value is unchanged, but students sometimes 'correct' it and break their answer.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure/Further Pure 3D vectors: dot product, magnitudes, and the parallelogram/triangle area formula |a||b|sinθ. The cross-product equivalence |a × b| = |a||b|sinθ is acknowledged in Further Pure but the scalar formula alone suffices here.
