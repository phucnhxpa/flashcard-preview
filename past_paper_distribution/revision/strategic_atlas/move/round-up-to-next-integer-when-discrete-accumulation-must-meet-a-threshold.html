Sequences & Series

Round up to next integer when discrete accumulation must meet a threshold

Family: Contextual Rounding Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever solving an inequality or equation in $n$ (number of terms, weeks, months, years) yields a non-integer value, and the context requires the cumulative quantity to reach or exceed a target. Cue phrases include 'enough money', 'at least £X', 'first time the total exceeds', 'minimum number of weeks/years to save/repay', or 'how many terms are needed so that $S_n \geq T$'. Applies to both arithmetic accumulation ($S_n$ for AP savings/instalments) and geometric accumulation (compound interest, depreciation thresholds). Distinct from 'round down' contexts where one asks how many complete units fit within a budget.

Motivation — Why this works

The variable $n$ counts discrete events (weeks, terms, payments) and must be a positive integer, but the algebraic equation $S_n = T$ generally has a non-integer root. Because $S_n$ is increasing in $n$, the smallest integer $n$ satisfying $S_n \geq T$ is $\lceil n^* \rceil$ where $n^*$ is the real root. Rounding down would give $S_n < T$, failing the modelling requirement. The rounding direction is dictated by the inequality's direction, not by 'nearest integer' conventions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the algebraic solution $n \approx 36.89$ is non-integer, but $n$ counts weeks so must be a positive integer.
  2. Rule out the negative root on physical grounds (cannot have negative weeks).
  3. Commit to rounding up rather than to nearest, because the question demands $S_n \geq 350$ (a threshold, not an approximation).
  4. Bridge to verification: compute $S_{36}$ and $S_{37}$ explicitly to confirm which integer first crosses the threshold.
  5. Tactical choice: present the verification ($S_{36} < 350 < S_{37}$) as the justification for $n=37$ — examiners reward this evidence over a bare 'round up'.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Solve the equation/inequality $S_n = T$ algebraically (quadratic for AP, logarithm for GP).
  2. Reject any negative or non-physical root with a brief reason.
  3. Identify the two integers bracketing the real root, $\lfloor n^* \rfloor$ and $\lceil n^* \rceil$.
  4. Evaluate $S_n$ at both bracketing integers to show $S_{\lfloor n^* \rfloor} < T$ and $S_{\lceil n^* \rceil} \geq T$.
  5. State the answer as $n = \lceil n^* \rceil$ with a one-line contextual conclusion (e.g. '37 weeks are needed').

Worked Micro-Example

AP savings: £10 in week 1, increasing by £0.25 each week. Find least $n$ with $S_n \geq 350$. Setting $\tfrac{n}{2}(20 + (n-1)(0.25)) \geq 350$ gives $n^2 + 79n - 2800 \geq 0$, so $n \geq \tfrac{-79+\sqrt{79^2+11200}}{2} \approx 36.89$. Check: $S_{36} = 18(18.75) = 337.50 < 350$ and $S_{37} = 18.5(19) = 351.50 \geq 350$. Therefore $n = 37$ weeks.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Geometric compound interest: 'least number of years for investment to first exceed £X'
Solve $a r^n \geq X$ via logs giving non-integer $n^*$; round up to $\lceil n^* \rceil$ and verify with the value at that integer.
Geometric series total exceeds a target (e.g. cumulative production)
Solve $\frac{a(r^n-1)}{r-1} \geq T$ for $n$; same round-up logic since $S_n$ is increasing.
Depreciation falling below a threshold: 'first year value < £X'
Sequence is decreasing, so still round UP the log solution — the smallest integer $n$ for which $ar^n < X$ exceeds the real root.
'How many complete weeks can she save before running out of £200 budget?'
Direction reverses: round DOWN, because the constraint is $S_n \leq 200$ and we want the largest integer below $n^*$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the rounding as 'nearest integer' rather than dictated by the inequality direction — coincidentally correct sometimes, but reasoning is flawed.
  • Reading 'enough money to buy' as a budget constraint (round down) rather than a target to reach (round up).
  • Believing the algebraic answer $n \approx 36.89$ is itself acceptable to leave un-rounded.

Pitfalls

  • Rounding down to 36 without checking $S_{36}$, missing that the target is unmet.
  • Failing to reject the negative root explicitly — examiners deduct for unstated rejection.
  • Not showing the verification $S_{n-1} < T \leq S_n$, which is the evidential basis for the final mark.
  • Arithmetic slip in $S_n$ formula when verifying — using $a + (n-1)d$ instead of $2a + (n-1)d$ inside the bracket.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P1/P2 sequences-and-series contextual modelling: applies to both arithmetic and geometric accumulation problems where $n$ is a positive integer count, with verification by direct substitution always in scope.
