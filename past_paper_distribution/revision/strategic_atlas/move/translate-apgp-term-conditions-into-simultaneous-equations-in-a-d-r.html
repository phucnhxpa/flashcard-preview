Sequences & Series

Translate AP/GP term conditions into simultaneous equations in a, d, r

Family: Modelling To Algebra Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question gives verbal conditions about specific terms (or sums) of an arithmetic and/or geometric sequence — typically with shared first term — and asks you to find a, d, or r. Cues include phrases like 'the 4th term of the AP equals...', 'the 3rd term of the GP is...', 'the nth term of the AP equals the mth term of the GP'. Any time a sequence problem reads as English sentences about indexed terms, this move converts them into algebra. It is the mandatory first step before elimination/substitution can begin.

Motivation — Why this works

The standard formulas u_n = a + (n-1)d and v_n = ar^{n-1} are the definitional bridge between sequence language and algebra. Each verbal condition becomes one equation in the unknowns a, d, r; if the AP and GP share a, the system is coupled and a finite simultaneous solve becomes possible. Counting equations against unknowns confirms determinacy before any algebra is attempted. Without this translation no further progress (elimination, quadratic in r, etc.) can occur.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question describes specific indexed terms of an AP and a GP, possibly with a shared first term a — this is a translation problem, not a manipulation one.
  2. I rule out trying to spot a clever shortcut or pattern: the conditions are independent statements that must each be written as an equation first.
  3. I commit to writing u_n = a + (n-1)d and v_n = ar^{n-1} and substituting the given indices, because this guarantees correctness of each equation.
  4. I count unknowns (a, d, r) versus equations produced; if they match, the system is determined and I proceed to eliminate.
  5. Tactically, I label the equations (Eq 1, Eq 2, Eq 3) and isolate the simplest one (often a linear AP equation) to substitute into the others.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the unknowns (typically a, d, r) and the index n attached to each stated term.
  2. Write each AP condition using a + (n-1)d, taking care with the (n-1) exponent on the index.
  3. Write each GP condition using ar^{n-1}, again with exponent n-1, not n.
  4. Write any cross-condition (e.g. kth AP term = mth GP term) as a single equation linking a, d, r.
  5. Label equations and verify: number of independent equations ≥ number of unknowns before solving.

Worked Micro-Example

AP and GP share first term $a$. The 4th AP term is 10, the 3rd GP term is 16, and the 2nd AP term equals the 2nd GP term. Translate: $a+3d=10$ (Eq 1), $ar^{2}=16$ (Eq 2), $a+d=ar$ (Eq 3). Three equations in $a,d,r$ — ready to eliminate (e.g. from Eq 3, $d=a(r-1)$, sub into Eq 1).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Conditions given as sums S_n rather than individual terms
Use $S_n = \tfrac{n}{2}(2a+(n-1)d)$ or $S_n = \tfrac{a(r^n-1)}{r-1}$ to translate each sum statement into an equation.
Recurrence relation $u_{n+1}=f(u_n)$ specifying an AP or GP
First identify the common difference/ratio from the recurrence, then translate term conditions using the explicit nth-term formula.
Three terms of one sequence given (no second sequence)
Same translation; AP gives linear equations, GP gives equations whose ratios eliminate $a$ to leave a single equation in $r$.
Three consecutive terms form an AP/GP
Use $2b = a+c$ (AP) or $b^2 = ac$ (GP) as the translated condition instead of full nth-term formulas.

Common Misreadings

  • Writing $ar^{3}$ instead of $ar^{2}$ for the 3rd GP term (using exponent $n$ rather than $n-1$).
  • Treating the AP and GP as having different first terms when the question states they share $a$.
  • Reading 'the nth term' as a sum, or 'the sum to n terms' as a single term.

Pitfalls

  • Applying the AP formula to a GP term or vice versa, especially under time pressure.
  • Off-by-one index errors in the $(n-1)$ exponent — the single most common source of wrong equations.
  • Failing to label equations, leading to substitution errors when eliminating variables.
  • Forgetting to check whether the resulting system is determined before solving.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 (arithmetic and geometric series): only the standard formulas $u_n=a+(n-1)d$, $v_n=ar^{n-1}$, and the corresponding sum formulas are used.
