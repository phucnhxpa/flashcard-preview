Factorise non-monic quadratic via ac-method and grouping

Family: Quadratic Factorisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a quadratic $ax^2+bx+c$ has leading coefficient $a\neq 1$ and the coefficients are small integers suggesting rational roots. Typical phrasings include 'solve', 'factorise', or 'find the roots of' such a quadratic, especially when it appears as a residual factor after polynomial division or a substitution. Also triggers when the discriminant $b^2-4ac$ is a perfect square, signalling rational roots. Prefer this over the quadratic formula when $|ac|$ is small and a factor pair is easy to spot.

Motivation — Why this works

If $ax^2+bx+c=(px+q)(rx+s)$ then $pr=a$, $qs=c$ and $ps+qr=b$, so the integers $ps$ and $qr$ multiply to $ac$ and sum to $b$. Finding such a pair therefore reconstructs the split of the middle term, after which grouping extracts the common binomial. This avoids surds from the quadratic formula and yields exact roots in factored form, which is faster and less error-prone for clean integer coefficients.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice $a\neq 1$, so simple monic inspection $(x+\alpha)(x+\beta)$ does not directly apply.
  2. Rule out the quadratic formula because $|ac|$ is small and the discriminant looks like a perfect square, so an integer factor pair should exist.
  3. Commit to ac-method: search for two integers with product $ac$ and sum $b$; if found quickly, factorisation is guaranteed clean.
  4. Bridge to mechanics by splitting the middle term using that pair, preparing the expression for grouping.
  5. Inside the move, choose the order of the split so the first pair shares an obvious factor with $a$, ensuring both groups expose the same binomial.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute $ac$.
  2. Find integers $m,n$ with $mn=ac$ and $m+n=b$; if none exist, abandon and use the formula.
  3. Rewrite $bx$ as $mx+nx$, giving $ax^2+mx+nx+c$.
  4. Group into two pairs and factor each: $x(\ldots)+\text{const}(\ldots)$, producing a common binomial.
  5. Extract the common binomial to get $(\text{linear})(\text{linear})=0$.
  6. Set each factor to zero and solve for $x$.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $2x^2-5x-3=0$. Here $ac=2\cdot(-3)=-6$ and we need a pair summing to $-5$: take $-6$ and $+1$. Split: $2x^2-6x+x-3=0$. Group: $2x(x-3)+1(x-3)=0$, so $(2x+1)(x-3)=0$. Hence $x=-\tfrac{1}{2}$ or $x=3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic $6x^2+x-2=0$ with larger $|a|$
$ac=-12$, factor pair $(4,-3)$; split to $6x^2+4x-3x-2$, group to $(2x-1)(3x+2)=0$.
Quadratic in a substitution, e.g. $2u^2-5u-3=0$ where $u=e^x$ or $u=\sqrt{x}$
Apply the same factorisation, then back-substitute and discard roots inadmissible for the substitution (e.g. $u=e^x>0$ rejects $u=-\tfrac12$).
Residual quadratic after factor-theorem division of a cubic
Use ac-method on the quotient quadratic to obtain the remaining two roots.
Disguised quadratic $2x^4-5x^2-3=0$
Let $y=x^2$, factorise $2y^2-5y-3=(2y+1)(y-3)$, then solve $y=x^2$ cases.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the quadratic as monic and trying $(x+\alpha)(x+\beta)$ form, ignoring the leading coefficient.
  • Searching for a factor pair of $c$ instead of $ac$.
  • Assuming integer roots must exist; if no integer pair multiplies to $ac$ and sums to $b$, the roots are irrational and the formula is required.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when $c<0$: forgetting one of the two integers must be negative.
  • Choosing a grouping order that does not yield a common binomial; swap the middle terms if the brackets disagree.
  • Reading the root from $(2x+1)$ as $x=1/2$ instead of $x=-1/2$.
  • Forgetting to set the factored expression equal to zero before declaring roots.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard technique in Edexcel IAL Pure (P1) for solving quadratic equations and factorising, and reused throughout P2–P4 whenever a non-monic quadratic appears as a residual factor or via substitution.
