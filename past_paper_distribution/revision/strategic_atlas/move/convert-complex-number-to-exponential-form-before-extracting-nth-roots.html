Complex Numbers

Convert complex number to exponential form before extracting nth roots

Family: Exponential Form Conversion Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

An equation of the form z^n = w (e.g. z^5 = 32i) asks for all nth roots of a complex number w given in Cartesian form a+bi. Root extraction is intractable in Cartesian form but trivial in modulus–argument form. The cue is any 'find all solutions' or 'find the nth roots of...' phrasing where the RHS is given as a real, imaginary, or general Cartesian complex number. Also fires when De Moivre's theorem is to be applied.

Motivation — Why this works

Exponential form re^{i\theta} separates a complex number into its modulus and argument, the two quantities De Moivre's theorem acts on independently. Taking an nth root then reduces to taking the real nth root of r and dividing \theta (plus 2\pi k) by n. This converts a non-elementary complex equation into two pieces of real arithmetic, and naturally generates all n distinct roots via k = 0,1,\dots,n-1.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the equation has the shape z^n = (complex constant), so this is an nth-roots problem.
  2. I rule out expanding z = x+iy and equating real/imaginary parts — that gives a messy polynomial system for n \ge 3.
  3. I commit to modulus–argument form because De Moivre's theorem makes z^n = w solvable in one line once w = re^{i\theta}.
  4. First concrete operation: read off |w| and \arg(w) from the Cartesian form, using the Argand diagram rather than blindly applying \arctan(b/a).
  5. Tactically I choose exponential form re^{i\theta} over polar r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta) for compactness, and I keep \theta in the principal range (-\pi,\pi] (or [0,2\pi) if the question prefers) before adding 2\pi k.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the RHS w = a + bi.
  2. Compute the modulus r = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}.
  3. Determine the argument \theta by locating w on the Argand diagram (axis cases by inspection; quadrant cases via \arctan adjusted).
  4. Write w = r e^{i\theta}.
  5. (Optional, for the next move) Replace \theta by \theta + 2\pi k to expose all n roots.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $z^5 = 32i$. Here $w = 32i$ lies on the positive imaginary axis, so $|w| = 32$ and $\arg(w) = \tfrac{\pi}{2}$. Hence $32i = 32 e^{i\pi/2} = 32 e^{i(\pi/2 + 2\pi k)}$. Taking fifth roots: $z = 32^{1/5} e^{i(\pi/2 + 2\pi k)/5} = 2 e^{i(\pi + 4\pi k)/10}$ for $k = 0,1,2,3,4$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

RHS is a negative real number, e.g. z^4 = -16
Use r = 16, \theta = \pi (negative real axis), giving -16 = 16 e^{i\pi}.
RHS is a general Cartesian complex number, e.g. z^3 = 1 + i
Compute r = \sqrt{2}, \theta = \pi/4 via \arctan(1/1) in the first quadrant, giving 1+i = \sqrt{2} e^{i\pi/4}.
RHS is in the second/third/fourth quadrant
Use \arctan(b/a) and adjust by \pm\pi to land in the principal range; signs of a and b dictate the quadrant.
Question requests answers in polar (trig) form
Write w = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta) instead and apply De Moivre directly.
Equation is z^n = 1 (roots of unity)
Use 1 = e^{i\cdot 0} = e^{2\pi i k}; the modulus step is trivial.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 32i as having argument 0 or \pi (confusing the imaginary axis with the real axis).
  • Writing \arg(32i) = -\pi/2 instead of \pi/2 (sign error / confusing with -32i).
  • Computing |a+bi| as a+b or as |a|+|b| instead of \sqrt{a^2+b^2}.
  • Forgetting to add the +2\pi k before dividing by n, and so producing only one root instead of n.

Pitfalls

  • Blindly applying \theta = \arctan(b/a) for pure imaginary inputs where a = 0 (division by zero) — must reason from the Argand diagram.
  • Using \arctan without adjusting for quadrant when a < 0, putting \theta in the wrong half-plane.
  • Mixing degrees and radians; A-level complex work uses radians throughout.
  • Leaving the argument outside the stated principal range required by the question.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2) treatment of complex numbers, exponential form, De Moivre's theorem, and nth roots. No use of complex logarithm or branch cuts beyond the principal argument is required.
