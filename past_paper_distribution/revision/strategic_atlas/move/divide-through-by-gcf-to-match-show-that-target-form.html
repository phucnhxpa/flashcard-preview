Algebra & Functions

Divide through by GCF to match show-that target form

Family: Goal Directed Simplification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in a 'show that' or 'prove that' question where your derived equation has the correct structure but inflated coefficients compared to the printed target. Cue: every coefficient (including the constant) shares a common integer factor, and dividing by it produces exactly the required equation. Typical phrasing: 'show that ... = 0' with a monic or low-coefficient target while your working yields a scaled-up version. Also fires when the target has leading coefficient 1 but yours is 2, 3, 4, etc.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying or dividing both sides of '... = 0' by a non-zero constant preserves the solution set, so any common factor across all terms can be cleared without changing the equation's content. In 'show that' questions the printed form is non-negotiable: examiners award the final mark only when the candidate reaches that exact expression. Recognising and removing the common factor is therefore the bridge between an algebraically correct but cosmetically wrong line and the demanded conclusion.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice my equation has the same shape as the target (same variable powers, same sign pattern) but every coefficient looks larger by a constant multiple.
  2. I rule out re-doing the algebra from scratch — the structure already matches, so the discrepancy must be a single scaling factor.
  3. I commit to checking the GCF of all coefficients including the constant; if it divides cleanly, one division finishes the proof.
  4. I compute the ratio of leading coefficients first (mine ÷ target) to conjecture the factor, then verify on the other terms.
  5. I divide every term — not just the visible ones — and write the result as a clean line so the examiner sees the target reproduced verbatim.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compare your equation term-by-term with the printed target to identify the suspected common factor k.
  2. Verify k divides each coefficient exactly (leading, middle, constant).
  3. Divide every term of the equation by k.
  4. Write the resulting equation on a fresh line, matching the target's layout, and state '= 0' on the right.

Worked Micro-Example

Derived: $4n^2 + 32n - 4292 = 0$. Target: $n^2 + 8n - 1073 = 0$.\ GCF of $4, 32, 4292$ is $4$; check $32 \div 4 = 8$ and $4292 \div 4 = 1073$.\ Divide through by $4$: $n^2 + 8n - 1073 = 0$, as required.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Target equation has leading coefficient 1 but your quadratic is $6x^2 + 18x - 24 = 0$.
Divide by 6 to obtain $x^2 + 3x - 4 = 0$ before any factorising.
Trigonometric 'show that' where you reach $2\sin\theta\cos\theta + 2\cos^2\theta = 2\cos\theta$ but target is $\sin\theta + \cos\theta = 1$ (with $\cos\theta \neq 0$).
Divide through by the common factor $2\cos\theta$, noting and stating the assumption $\cos\theta \neq 0$.
Series/sigma proof reaching $\tfrac{1}{2}n(n+1)(2n+2) = \text{target}$ where target is $n(n+1)^2$.
Recognise $2n+2 = 2(n+1)$ and cancel the factor of 2 with the $\tfrac{1}{2}$ to match the target form.
Polynomial identity giving $3x^3 - 6x^2 + 9x - 12 \equiv 3(x^3 - 2x^2 + 3x - 4)$ for matching to a printed cubic.
Factor out the constant 3 rather than dividing, when both sides are expressions (not equations).

Common Misreadings

  • Believing the derived and target equations are different equations rather than scalar multiples of one another.
  • Thinking the GCF must divide only the variable-coefficient terms, forgetting the constant term must also be divisible.
  • Assuming you may divide an expression (not equated to zero) by a constant without preserving it as a multiple.

Pitfalls

  • Dividing only some terms by the GCF and leaving others unchanged.
  • Arithmetic slip on the largest coefficient (e.g. $4292 \div 4 = 1073$) breaking the match.
  • Choosing a divisor that works for two coefficients but not the third, and not checking before committing.
  • Dividing by an expression that could be zero (e.g. $\cos\theta$) without stating the non-zero condition.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard manipulation across Edexcel IAL Pure (P1–P4), used in 'show that' quadratics, trigonometric identities, and proof-by-induction tidy-up steps; no content beyond A-level scope is invoked.
