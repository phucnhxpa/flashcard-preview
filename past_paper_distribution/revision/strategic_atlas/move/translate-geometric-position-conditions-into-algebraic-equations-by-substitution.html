Coordinate Geometry

Translate geometric position conditions into algebraic equations by substitution

Family: Geometric To Algebraic Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question describes intersections in geometric language — 'where the curve crosses the x-axis', 'a horizontal line through the y-intercept meets the curve again', 'the line and curve intersect at P and Q' — but gives no equation to solve directly. Look for phrases like 'crosses', 'meets', 'cuts', 'intersects', or a horizontal/vertical line tied to a known feature of the curve. The cue is that you have algebraic objects (a curve y = f(x), a line) but the relationship between them is stated only geometrically.

Motivation — Why this works

Every geometric incidence condition corresponds to an algebraic equation: points on the x-axis satisfy y = 0; points on the y-axis satisfy x = 0; a horizontal line through (0, f(0)) has equation y = f(0); a generic intersection of y = f(x) and y = g(x) satisfies f(x) = g(x). Substituting the geometric constraint into the curve equation produces a polynomial equation whose roots are exactly the x-coordinates of the intersection points. When the line passes through a known point (a, f(a)) on the curve, this also engineers x = a as a guaranteed root of f(x) − f(a) = 0, letting you factor out (x − a) and reduce the degree.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question uses geometric vocabulary ('crosses', 'meets', 'horizontal line through the y-intercept') rather than giving an explicit equation to solve.
  2. Rule out differentiation (which would find turning points, not crossings) and rule out trying to read coordinates off a sketch (which only gives approximate values).
  3. Commit to converting the geometric condition into an algebraic equation: identify the defining property of the geometric object (x-axis ⇒ y = 0; horizontal line through (0, c) ⇒ y = c; another curve ⇒ y = g(x)).
  4. Bridge to algebra by substituting that property into the curve equation, producing f(x) = (constant) or f(x) = g(x), then rearrange to f(x) − (RHS) = 0.
  5. Tactical check: if the line passes through a known point on the curve (e.g. the y-intercept), recognise that x = 0 (or x = a) must be a root by construction — the constant term of the rearranged polynomial will vanish, and you can factor it out without hunting via the factor theorem.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the geometric object and write its defining algebraic equation (y = 0 for x-axis, x = 0 for y-axis, y = f(0) for horizontal line through y-intercept, f(x) = g(x) for curve–curve intersection).
  2. Substitute into the curve equation y = f(x) to eliminate y (or x).
  3. Rearrange to standard form: f(x) − (RHS) = 0.
  4. If the line passes through a known point (a, f(a)) on the curve, observe that x = a is a guaranteed root and factor (x − a) out.
  5. Solve the resulting (possibly reduced) polynomial equation for the remaining x-coordinates.

Worked Micro-Example

Curve $y = x^3 - 3x^2 + 2$; line $l$ is horizontal through the y-intercept. Then $f(0) = 2$, so $l: y = 2$. Solve $x^3 - 3x^2 + 2 = 2 \Rightarrow x^3 - 3x^2 = 0 \Rightarrow x^2(x-3) = 0$, giving $x = 0$ (the y-intercept itself) and $x = 3$ (the second intersection).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Curve crosses the x-axis
Set y = 0 in the curve equation and solve f(x) = 0.
Curve crosses the y-axis
Set x = 0; the y-intercept is simply f(0), no equation to solve.
Two curves intersect
Set f(x) = g(x) and rearrange to f(x) − g(x) = 0; same translation principle.
Horizontal line through a known curve point (a, f(a))
Set f(x) = f(a); x = a is automatically a root, so factor (x − a) out to reduce the degree.
Enclosed-area / inequality follow-up
Use the intersection x-values found algebraically as integration limits and as the boundary of the inequality solution; verify against the sketch that upper/lower curves are correctly identified.

Common Misreadings

  • Setting x = 0 instead of y = 0 (or vice versa) when finding axis crossings.
  • Confusing 'crosses the x-axis' with 'turning point' and reaching for differentiation.
  • When a horizontal line passes through the y-intercept, hunting for a root via the factor theorem instead of recognising that x = 0 is forced by the construction.
  • Assuming the curve is always below (or above) the line without verifying, when later parts ask for the enclosed region.

Pitfalls

  • Mixing factored and expanded forms of f(x) carelessly when computing f(0) and then rearranging — pick one form and stay with it.
  • Forgetting to include the 'known' root (x = 0 or x = a) in the final list of intersection x-coordinates after factoring it out.
  • Shading the wrong region (outside the intersection points rather than between them) in a follow-up sketch part.
  • Dropping a sign when moving the constant term across the equation, especially when f(0) is negative.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1–P4): polynomial equations, intersections of curves and lines, factor theorem. No formula-booklet entry is required — this is a definitional translation. Do not generalise to implicit curves or simultaneous nonlinear systems beyond polynomial degree 3 unless the question explicitly supports it.
