Integration

Match integrand to a formula-booklet standard form for direct antidifferentiation

Family: Standard Form Recognition Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the integrand has a clean structural shape that matches a named entry in the Edexcel formula booklet: a constant over a linear expression k/(ax+b), an inverse-trig form like 1/√(a²−x²) or 1/(a²+x²), or a recognisable trigonometric product such as cosec x cot x, sec x tan x, sec²x, cosec²x. Visual cues include: a single linear factor in the denominator (suggests ln), a square root of (a² − x²) (suggests arcsin), a sum a² + x² without a root (suggests arctan), or a product of reciprocal trig functions matching a derivative table. Equivalent phrasings: 'this is just a standard integral', 'this is in the formula book', 'this is the derivative of … in reverse'. The trigger is essentially: before reaching for substitution, partial fractions, or parts, ask whether the integrand IS already a listed standard form (possibly up to a constant multiple).

Motivation — Why this works

Standard-form recognition short-circuits unnecessary technique. Each formula-book entry encodes a reverse chain rule already done for you: ∫1/(ax+b) dx = (1/a)ln|ax+b|+C bakes in the 1/a from u = ax+b; ∫1/√(a²−x²) dx = arcsin(x/a)+C bakes in the substitution x = a sin θ; ∫cosec x cot x dx = −cosec x +C is just d/dx(−cosec x) read backwards. Recognising the form turns a potentially multi-step problem into a one-line antiderivative plus an evaluation, which both saves time and removes opportunities for substitution-related sign and scaling errors.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the global shape of the integrand: how many factors, what is in the denominator, are there square roots, are there trig products?
  2. Rule out heavier machinery: is the numerator the derivative of the denominator (would be a direct ln)? Is partial fractions needed (denominator factorises further)? Is integration by parts indicated (product of unrelated functions)? If none, suspect a standard form.
  3. Match against the formula booklet: linear-denominator → log; √(a²−x²) → arcsin; a²+x² → arctan; cosec x cot x / sec x tan x / sec²x / cosec²x → direct trig antiderivative.
  4. Pull all constant multipliers outside the integral so what remains looks EXACTLY like the booklet entry — e.g. write 16/(9x−7) as 16·1/(9x−7), or 3/√(9−x²) as 3·1/√(3²−x²).
  5. Identify the parameter a (or a, b for linear) carefully: in arcsin(x/a) the a is the square root of the constant term; in (1/a)ln|ax+b| the a is the coefficient of x — do not confuse the two roles.
  6. Write the antiderivative directly, restore the constant multiplier, then evaluate at the bounds (upper minus lower) using exact values.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Simplify the integrand algebraically so any compound constants are visible (e.g. 3(3x − 7/3) = 9x − 7).
  2. Factor out all numerical constants: ∫ k·f(x) dx = k ∫ f(x) dx.
  3. Identify the matching standard form and read off a (and b if linear).
  4. Write the antiderivative F(x): (1/a)ln|ax+b|, or arcsin(x/a), or (1/a)arctan(x/a), or −cosec x, etc.
  5. Multiply by the extracted constant to get the full antiderivative.
  6. Evaluate [F(x)]_lower^upper = F(upper) − F(lower), using exact trig/log values, and simplify with log laws if applicable (e.g. ln A − ln B = ln(A/B)).

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_0^3 \frac{3}{\sqrt{9-x^2}}\,dx$. Recognise $\frac{1}{\sqrt{a^2-x^2}}$ form with $a=3$: antiderivative is $3\arcsin(x/3)$. Then $[3\arcsin(x/3)]_0^3 = 3(\pi/2) - 3(0) = \tfrac{3\pi}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Constant over linear: ∫ k/(ax+b) dx
Antiderivative is (k/a)ln|ax+b| + C — pick up a 1/a from the coefficient of x.
Numerator is derivative of denominator: ∫ f'(x)/f(x) dx
Direct ln|f(x)| + C — no 1/a factor needed because the chain has cancelled itself.
1/(a² + x²) instead of 1/√(a² − x²)
Antiderivative is (1/a)arctan(x/a) + C — different inverse trig, plus a 1/a factor.
Constant over linear SQUARED: ∫ k/(ax+b)² dx
This is power-rule, NOT log: result is −k/[a(ax+b)] + C.
Other trig standard forms: sec x tan x, sec²x, cosec²x
Antiderivatives are sec x, tan x, −cot x respectively — pattern-match to the formula booklet.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing ∫1/(ax+b) dx (logarithm) with ∫1/(ax+b)² dx (power rule, negative power result).
  • Confusing ∫1/√(a²−x²) dx (arcsin) with ∫1/(a²−x²) dx (no square root → partial fractions).
  • Confusing ∫1/√(a²−x²) dx (arcsin) with ∫1/√(x²−a²) dx or ∫1/√(x²+a²) dx (different inverse hyperbolic / log forms — outside core IAL P3 anyway).

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the 1/a factor that comes from the coefficient of x — the most common A-level integration error.
  • Dropping absolute value signs on the logarithm (harmless when the argument is positive throughout the interval, but mark-losing in indefinite integrals).
  • Sign error on the trig antiderivatives: ∫cosec x cot x dx = −cosec x (not +cosec x); ∫cosec²x dx = −cot x (not +cot x).
  • Wrong exact values: arcsin(1) = π/2 (not 1 or π); cosec(π/6) = 2 (not 1/√3); cosec(π/4) = √2.
  • Subtracting the bounds in the wrong order — always F(upper) − F(lower).
  • Arithmetic slips when a or b are fractions: simplify the linear expression first (e.g. 3(3x − 7/3) = 9x − 7) before integrating.

A-level Scope Notes

All listed antiderivatives appear in the Edexcel IAL formula booklet (P3 'Integrals' table for ln and trig forms; P3 inverse-trig integrals for arcsin and arctan). Stay within these listed forms — do NOT extend to ∫1/√(x²±a²) dx (inverse hyperbolic), which is out of scope for IAL Maths (and only partially covered in Further Pure).
