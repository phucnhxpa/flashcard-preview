Sequences & Series

Multiply standard Maclaurin series to bypass differentiation

Family: Series Composition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The target function is a product (or simple composition) of functions whose Maclaurin series appear in the IAL formula book — typically $e^x$, $\sin x$, $\cos x$, $\ln(1+x)$, or $(1+x)^n$. Phrasings include 'find the series expansion up to and including the term in $x^n$' for things like $e^x\sin x$, $e^{2x}\ln(1+x)$, or $(1+x)^{1/2}\cos x$. The cue is a product where direct differentiation would force repeated product rule, but each factor's series is already known.

Motivation — Why this works

If $g(x)=\sum a_k x^k$ and $h(x)=\sum b_k x^k$ converge near $0$, then $g(x)h(x)=\sum_k\bigl(\sum_{i+j=k} a_i b_j\bigr)x^k$ — the Cauchy product. Because Maclaurin coefficients are unique, the product of truncated series gives exactly the Maclaurin coefficients of $gh$ up to the truncation order. This bypasses the product/chain rule, which would require evaluating $f^{(k)}(0)$ for each $k$. It is faster, more systematic, and uses results the candidate is already permitted to quote.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice $f(x)$ is literally a product of two functions whose Maclaurin series I can quote from the formula book.
  2. I rule out repeated differentiation: applying the product rule four or five times to get $f^{(n)}(0)$ would be slow and error-prone.
  3. I commit to series multiplication because the Cauchy product reproduces the same Maclaurin coefficients with only algebra.
  4. I decide on a truncation order: I keep each factor at least up to $x^n$ (the target), and check whether any higher term in one factor could combine with a low term in the other to land at $x^n$.
  5. Inside the move I organise the multiplication by collecting like powers, working power-by-power from $x^0$ upward so I can stop cleanly at $x^n$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two factors and quote each standard series from the formula book.
  2. Truncate each series so that every cross-product reaching power $x^n$ is captured (usually keep terms up to $x^n$ in each factor).
  3. Multiply systematically: for each $k=0,1,\dots,n$, sum all $a_i b_j$ with $i+j=k$.
  4. Simplify each coefficient, combining fractions to a single form.
  5. State the final expansion up to and including the term in $x^n$.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the Maclaurin series of $e^x\sin x$ up to $x^4$. Use $e^x=1+x+\tfrac{x^2}{2}+\tfrac{x^3}{6}+\tfrac{x^4}{24}+\cdots$ and $\sin x = x-\tfrac{x^3}{6}+\cdots$. Multiplying and collecting: $x$-term: $1\cdot x = x$; $x^2$: $x\cdot x = x^2$; $x^3$: $\tfrac{x^2}{2}\cdot x + 1\cdot(-\tfrac{x^3}{6}) = \tfrac{x^3}{3}$; $x^4$: $\tfrac{x^3}{6}\cdot x + x\cdot(-\tfrac{x^3}{6}) = 0$. So $e^x\sin x = x + x^2 + \tfrac{x^3}{3} + 0\cdot x^4 + \cdots$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Composition such as $e^{\sin x}$ or $\ln(1+\sin x)$
Substitute the inner series (truncated) into the outer series, then expand and re-collect powers up to the target order.
Quotient such as $\dfrac{\sin x}{1+x}$
Replace the denominator by the geometric expansion $(1+x)^{-1}=1-x+x^2-\cdots$ and multiply as a product.
Three-factor product like $e^x\cos x\,(1+x)^{1/2}$
Multiply two factors first to a truncated polynomial, then multiply the result by the third series, truncating at each stage.
Product where one factor is a polynomial, e.g. $(1+2x)\ln(1+x)$
Treat the polynomial as an exact (non-truncated) factor and just distribute it across the standard series.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming the shortcut always applies — it fails when the function isn't a clean product/composition of formula-book series.
  • Treating the multiplication as if only matching-power terms combine, forgetting that cross-terms $a_i b_j$ with $i+j=k$ all contribute.
  • Believing one must expand each factor to the same number of terms; the requirement is that all $i+j\le n$ pairs are covered.

Pitfalls

  • Truncating a factor too early and missing a cross-term that contributes to $x^n$.
  • Forgetting the constant term $1$ in $e^x$ or $(1+x)^n$ when multiplying, dropping a whole column of contributions.
  • Misquoting a standard series — wrong sign in $\ln(1+x)=x-\tfrac{x^2}{2}+\tfrac{x^3}{3}-\cdots$ or missing the $\tfrac{1}{k!}$ in $e^x$.
  • Arithmetic slips when adding fractional coefficients; failing to put them over a common denominator.
  • Ignoring the validity range — e.g. $\ln(1+x)$ and $(1+x)^n$ require $|x|<1$, and the product inherits the stricter condition.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure: the Maclaurin series for $e^x$, $\sin x$, $\cos x$, $\ln(1+x)$, and $(1+x)^n$ are quotable from the formula book, and series multiplication up to a stated power is a standard exam task.
