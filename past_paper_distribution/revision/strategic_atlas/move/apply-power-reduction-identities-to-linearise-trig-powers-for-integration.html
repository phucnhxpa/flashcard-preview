Strategic Atlas / Integration / Power Reduction Linearisation
Integration

Apply power-reduction identities to linearise trig powers for integration

Family: Power Reduction Linearisation Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integrand contains a power of a trig function — typically sin²(kx), cos²(kx), sin³(kx) or cos³(kx) — which has no entry in the formula book and cannot be integrated directly. Visual cues: a squared bracket like (1 + cos θ)² that expands to give cos²θ; an explicit cos²(3x) or sin³x term; or a 'hence' instruction following a part (a) that proved a multiple-angle identity such as cos 2A = 2cos²A − 1 or sin 3x = 3 sin x − 4 sin³x. Whenever the student sees an even or odd integer power of sin or cos sitting inside a definite integral, the antiderivative does not exist in elementary closed form until the power is reduced to a linear combination of sin(kx) and cos(kx).

Motivation — Why this works

Linearisation rewrites sinⁿx or cosⁿx as a sum of constants and first-power sines/cosines of multiple angles, each of which integrates to ±(1/k)cos(kx) or ±(1/k)sin(kx) by reverse chain rule. The two workhorse identities are cos²A = (1 + cos 2A)/2 and sin²A = (1 − cos 2A)/2 (both rearrangements of cos 2A = 2cos²A − 1 = 1 − 2sin²A), plus the cubic identities sin 3x = 3 sin x − 4 sin³x and cos 3x = 4cos³x − 3cos x for odd cubes. The mathematical principle is the same in every case: trade a non-integrable power for a sum of integrable single-frequency harmonics.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand contains a power of a trig function (sin²θ, cos²(3x), sin³x, or a bracket like (1+cosθ)² that will expand into one). Standard antiderivative tables don't cover these directly.
  2. Rule out alternatives: u-substitution u = cos(kx) only works if a matching sin(kx) factor is present (it isn't, for even powers); integration by parts loops on cos²; the Weierstrass t = tan(θ/2) substitution is overkill for a single squared term.
  3. Look for a signpost: a 'hence' clause referencing part (a), or simply recognise that the formula book / standard identities give cos²A = (1+cos 2A)/2, sin²A = (1−cos 2A)/2, sin³x = ¼(3 sin x − sin 3x), cos³x = ¼(cos 3x + 3 cos x).
  4. Commit to power-reduction. Match the angle inside the power to the identity's variable: e.g. for cos²(3x), set A = 3x so the doubled angle becomes 6x (NOT 3x).
  5. Substitute the identity into the integrand, expand and collect: every term should now be a constant or a single sin(kx)/cos(kx). Confirm this before integrating.
  6. Integrate term-by-term using ∫cos(kx)dx = sin(kx)/k and ∫sin(kx)dx = −cos(kx)/k, then evaluate the limits carefully.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the offending power (e.g. cos²(3x), sin³x, or a squared bracket that will expand to one).
  2. Select the identity: cos²A = (1+cos 2A)/2, sin²A = (1−cos 2A)/2, sin³x = ¼(3 sin x − sin 3x), or cos³x = ¼(cos 3x + 3 cos x).
  3. Substitute with the correct inner angle: e.g. cos²(3x) = (1 + cos 6x)/2 — the angle doubles.
  4. Distribute any external coefficients and collect constants (e.g. 5 − 4·(1+cos 6x)/2 = 3 − 2cos 6x).
  5. Integrate term-by-term: ∫cos(kx)dx = (1/k)sin(kx), ∫sin(kx)dx = −(1/k)cos(kx), ∫c dx = cθ.
  6. Substitute the limits and simplify, taking care with surds and any external factor (e.g. the ½ in polar area).

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_0^{\pi/3} \sin^3 x\,dx$. Using $\sin 3x = 3\sin x - 4\sin^3 x$, rearrange to $\sin^3 x = \tfrac{1}{4}(3\sin x - \sin 3x)$. Then $\int \sin^3 x\,dx = \tfrac{1}{4}\bigl[-3\cos x + \tfrac{1}{3}\cos 3x\bigr]$. Evaluating from $0$ to $\pi/3$ gives $\tfrac{1}{4}\bigl[(-3\cdot\tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{3}(-1)) - (-3 + \tfrac{1}{3})\bigr] = \tfrac{5}{24}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Even power sin²(kx) in the integrand
Use sin²A = (1 − cos 2A)/2 with A = kx; the doubled angle becomes 2kx.
Even power cos²(kx) in the integrand
Use cos²A = (1 + cos 2A)/2 with A = kx; the doubled angle becomes 2kx.
Fourth power cos⁴x or sin⁴x
Apply the identity twice: first cos⁴x = ((1+cos 2x)/2)², then expand and reduce the resulting cos²(2x) using cos²A = (1+cos 4x)/2.
Odd cube sin³x or cos³x with a 'hence' from a triple-angle identity
Rearrange sin 3x = 3 sin x − 4 sin³x to sin³x = ¼(3 sin x − sin 3x), or cos 3x = 4cos³x − 3cos x to cos³x = ¼(cos 3x + 3 cos x).
Odd cube without a signposted identity
Strip one factor: sin³x = sin x(1 − cos²x), then substitute u = cos x. Power-reduction is not needed for odd powers when substitution is available.
Squared bracket like (1 + cos θ)² in a polar area integral
Expand first to 1 + 2cos θ + cos²θ, then apply cos²θ = (1+cos 2θ)/2 to the surviving square.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying u-substitution u = cos(kx) on cos²(kx) — fails because no compensating sin(kx)dx factor is present.
  • Attempting integration by parts on cos²x or sin²x — produces a circular equation rather than progress.
  • Pattern-matching cos²θ with cos 2θ and writing ∫cos²θ dθ = (sin 2θ)/2 — confusing the square with the double angle.
  • Ignoring a signposted part (a) identity and reaching for an independent (longer) method.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the angle doubles: writing cos²(3x) = (1 + cos 3x)/2 instead of (1 + cos 6x)/2.
  • Sign error when rearranging the cube identity: writing sin³x = ¼(sin 3x − 3 sin x) instead of ¼(3 sin x − sin 3x).
  • Forgetting the 1/k factor when integrating sin(kx) or cos(kx) (reverse chain rule), e.g. ∫sin 3x dx = −⅓cos 3x not −cos 3x.
  • Dropping or mishandling external coefficients when distributing, e.g. losing the factor of 2 in 4·(1+cos 6x)/2 = 2 + 2cos 6x.
  • Forgetting the leading ½ in the polar area formula A = ½∫r² dθ after evaluating the inner integral.
  • Arithmetic slips evaluating cos π, cos(π/3), sin(2π/3) at the bounds, particularly with surds.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 (Pure 3/4) and Further Pure. The double-angle identities cos 2A = 2cos²A − 1 = 1 − 2sin²A appear in the formula booklet; the rearranged power-reduction forms cos²A = (1+cos 2A)/2 and sin²A = (1−cos 2A)/2 are expected to be quoted from these. Triple-angle identities (sin 3x, cos 3x) are NOT in the booklet and must either be derived or supplied by an earlier part. Avoid invoking complex exponentials (e^{ikx}) or Fourier methods — out of scope.
