Algebra & Functions

Back-substitute into the algebraically simpler equation to recover the remaining unknown

Family: Strategic Substitution Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

After eliminating one variable from a pair (or system) of simultaneous equations and finding its value, you face a choice of which original equation to substitute back into. The trigger fires whenever two or more equations remain valid for back-substitution but they differ in computational complexity — e.g. one is linear in the unknown while the other involves a higher power, larger base, or more rearrangement. Visual cues: equations like 'ar = 12 and ar⁴ = 324', or 'y = 2^x and y = 4^{x−6}', where one side is structurally lighter. Equivalently phrased: 'pick the cheaper equation', 'substitute into the lower-power form', 'use the linear branch'.

Motivation — Why this works

Both equations must yield the same answer at the solution point, so the choice is purely about minimising arithmetic load and error risk. The simpler equation typically involves a lower power, a smaller base, or fewer operations, which means fewer chances to slip on mental arithmetic and a faster route to the answer under exam conditions. The underlying principle is that consistency of the system guarantees agreement, so we exploit this freedom to optimise. This is a micro-optimisation but compounds across a paper into significant time and accuracy gains.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I have just solved for one variable (say r or x) by elimination, and I now need the other.
  2. I notice that I can substitute back into either of the original equations — both must give the same answer by consistency.
  3. I rule out brute-forcing the harder equation: e.g. computing 3⁴ = 81 then dividing 324/81, or evaluating 4⁶ when 2¹² will do.
  4. I commit to the equation with the lowest power / simplest base / least rearrangement, because it gives the answer in one clean step.
  5. Tactically, I scan for the equation that is linear (or near-linear) in the remaining unknown, or whose constants I can recognise instantly (e.g. 2¹⁰ = 1024).
  6. I substitute, simplify, and write down the answer — then optionally verify by plugging into the other equation as a sanity check.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two (or more) candidate equations available for back-substitution.
  2. Compare their structural complexity: power of the unknown, size of base, amount of rearrangement needed.
  3. Substitute the known value into the simpler equation.
  4. Solve the resulting one-variable equation directly (often a single division or single power evaluation).
  5. (Optional) Verify by substituting both values into the other equation.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $ar = 12$ and $ar^4 = 324$, dividing yields $r^3 = 27$, so $r = 3$. To find $a$, I choose $ar = 12$ (linear in $a$) over $ar^4 = 324$ (which needs $3^4 = 81$). Then $3a = 12 \Rightarrow a = 4$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Intersection of two exponential curves, e.g. $y = 2^x$ and $y = 4^{x-6}$, after solving $x = 12$
Substitute into $y = 2^x$ to get $2^{12} = 4096$ rather than $4^6$, since powers of 2 are more readily recalled.
GP/AP simultaneous problems where one equation is linear and one is higher-degree
Always back-substitute into the linear equation to avoid evaluating high powers.
Logarithmic systems where one equation is already isolated (e.g. $y = \log_a x$) and the other is implicit
Substitute into the explicit form to bypass rearrangement.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the two equations as giving different answers, leading the student to 'check' both unnecessarily and waste time.
  • Assuming the harder-looking equation is 'more rigorous' and choosing it by default.

Pitfalls

  • Substituting into the wrong equation by accident — e.g. an AP equation instead of the GP equation in a mixed-sequence problem — which conflates the two systems.
  • Miscalculating a 'simple' power such as $2^{12}$ by confusing it with $2^{10} = 1024$ instead of $4096$.
  • Forgetting to check sign or domain constraints that one equation might enforce more strictly than the other.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1–P4) for simultaneous equations involving sequences, exponentials, and logarithms. No formula-booklet entry is needed — this is a procedural choice, not an identity. Avoid generalising to nonlinear systems with multiple solution branches without checking each candidate.
