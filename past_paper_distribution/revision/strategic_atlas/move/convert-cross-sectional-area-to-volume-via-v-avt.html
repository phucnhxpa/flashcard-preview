Integration

Convert cross-sectional area to volume via V = A·v·t

Family: Units Dimensional Modelling Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in physical modelling questions where a cross-sectional area (often estimated in a prior part using the trapezium rule or integration) is combined with a constant flow speed and a time interval. Cue phrases include 'water flows at ... m/s', 'volume passing through in ... minutes', or 'rate of flow through the cross-section'. Typically appears as a follow-on part after numerical integration of a river/channel/pipe profile.

Motivation — Why this works

A constant-speed flow through a fixed cross-section sweeps out a prism whose length in time t is d = v·t, so the volume is V = A·d = A·v·t. The identity is just 'volume of a prism = base area × length' applied to the column of fluid that has crossed the section. Dimensional analysis (m² × m/s × s = m³) confirms the structure, and reusing the previously computed A avoids redoing the integral.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that A has already been estimated in the earlier part — no new integration is required.
  2. Rule out re-integrating or modelling a varying speed: the question states the speed is constant.
  3. Commit to the prism model V = A·v·t once units are made consistent.
  4. Bridge to operations: spot the unit mismatch (speed in m/s, time in minutes) and convert time to seconds first.
  5. Carry the unrounded value of A from part (a) through the multiplication, then round only at the end to the requested significant figures.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify A (from previous part), v (flow speed), and t (time interval).
  2. Convert all quantities to consistent SI units (e.g. minutes → seconds).
  3. Compute the distance travelled by the fluid: d = v·t.
  4. Compute the volume: V = A·d = A·v·t.
  5. Round to the requested accuracy (s.f. or d.p.) only at the final step.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $A \approx 31.38\,\text{m}^2$, $v = 1.5\,\text{m/s}$, $t = 1\,\text{minute} = 60\,\text{s}$. Then $d = 1.5 \times 60 = 90\,\text{m}$, so $V = 31.38 \times 90 = 2824.2\,\text{m}^3 \approx 2800\,\text{m}^3$ (2 s.f.).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Flow-rate question asking for volume per second
Compute Q = A·v directly (m³/s), then multiply by t in seconds if total volume is needed.
Pipe or channel with area given in cm² and speed in m/s
Convert area to m² (divide by 10⁴) before multiplying, or convert speed to cm/s.
Mass flow through a section with fluid density ρ
Multiply the volume by ρ to obtain mass: m = ρ·A·v·t.
Mechanics displacement under constant velocity
Same multiplicative structure with A replaced by 1: s = v·t.

Common Misreadings

  • Forgetting to convert minutes to seconds, giving an answer 60 times too small.
  • Using the rounded value of A from part (a) instead of the unrounded value, losing accuracy.
  • Treating the flow as filling a fixed reservoir of volume A rather than a swept prism of length v·t.

Pitfalls

  • Rounding the area too early in part (a) and propagating that error into part (b).
  • Misreading '2 significant figures' as '2 decimal places'.
  • Mixing units (e.g. A in m², v in km/h, t in minutes) without harmonising before multiplying.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits comfortably within Edexcel IAL Pure/Mechanics modelling: the trapezium rule supplies A, and V = A·v·t is the standard prism-of-fluid model used in numerical integration questions.
