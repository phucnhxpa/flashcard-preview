Algebra & Functions

Multiply outer numerator and denominator by the inner denominator to clear a compound fraction

Family: Fraction Clearing Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a composition like f(f(x)) of a Möbius/linear-rational function produces a 'fraction within a fraction', where the SAME inner denominator (e.g. (x−4) or (x−1)) appears in both the outer numerator and the outer denominator. Visual cue: a big fraction bar with smaller fractions stacked above and below it, all sharing a common sub-denominator. Alternative phrasing: 'nested rational expression with a shared inner denominator', or 'compound fraction arising from self-composition of a rational function'. Also applies to simplifying short continued fractions.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying the outer numerator and outer denominator by the common inner denominator preserves the value of the fraction (multiplying top and bottom by the same non-zero quantity) while simultaneously cancelling every nested fraction in one stroke. This exploits the fact that any term not containing the inner fraction is simply scaled, while any term that does contain it has its denominator cancelled. The result collapses to a ratio of two linear (or polynomial) expressions, which is far easier to simplify, equate, or invert than the original layered form. It avoids the error-prone alternative of finding separate common denominators in the numerator and denominator and then dividing fractions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that after substituting f(x) into f, the resulting expression has small fractions stacked inside a larger fraction, and crucially the SAME expression (e.g. x−4) sits in the denominator of every inner fraction.
  2. Rule out brute-force approaches: separately combining the outer numerator over a common denominator, then the outer denominator over a common denominator, then dividing — this works but doubles the algebra and invites sign errors.
  3. Rule out expanding f∘f symbolically using a generic Möbius formula, since at A-level we want a transparent, checkable calculation.
  4. Commit to the clearing move: multiply the outer numerator AND outer denominator by the shared inner denominator. This is legal because it is multiplication by 1 in disguise (assuming the inner denominator is non-zero in the stated domain).
  5. Bridge to operation: identify the inner denominator D (e.g. x−4 or x−1), then distribute D across every term of the outer numerator and outer denominator, taking care that constant terms in the outer expression get multiplied by D too.
  6. Tactical care: track signs carefully when the outer expression contains subtractions like '− 3' or '− 1', since these become −3D or −D after multiplication.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write f(f(x)) explicitly as a single big fraction with the inner f(x) substituted in: [a·(px+q)/(rx+s) + b] / [c·(px+q)/(rx+s) + d].
  2. Identify the common inner denominator D = rx+s.
  3. Multiply the outer numerator and outer denominator by D, distributing across every term: numerator → a(px+q) + bD, denominator → c(px+q) + dD.
  4. Expand each bracket carefully, watching signs on any subtracted constants.
  5. Collect like terms to obtain a single rational expression of the form (Ax+B)/(Cx+E).

Worked Micro-Example

Let $f(x)=\frac{2x+3}{x-1}$. Then $f(f(x)) = \dfrac{2\cdot\frac{2x+3}{x-1} + 3}{\frac{2x+3}{x-1} - 1}$. Multiply top and bottom by $(x-1)$: numerator $= 2(2x+3)+3(x-1) = 7x+3$; denominator $= (2x+3)-(x-1) = x+4$. Hence $f(f(x)) = \dfrac{7x+3}{x+4}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Composition of two different Möbius functions g(f(x)) where f(x) = (px+q)/(rx+s)
Same move: multiply the outer numerator and denominator by (rx+s), the inner denominator of f.
Short continued fraction such as a + b/(c + d/(e+x))
Apply the clearing move from the innermost level outward, each time multiplying by the local inner denominator.
Solving f(f(x)) = x or finding a fixed-point equation
Clear the compound fraction first to get a linear/quadratic equation, then solve.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the expression as needing two separate common-denominator calculations (one for the outer numerator, one for the outer denominator) instead of clearing both simultaneously.
  • Trying to 'cancel' the inner denominator without multiplying — i.e. just deleting it — which is invalid.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when distributing a negative constant: e.g. −3(x−4) = −3x+12 (not −3x−12), and −(x−1) = −x+1 (not −x−1).
  • Forgetting to multiply EVERY term — especially the standalone constants like '+3' or '−1' — by the inner denominator.
  • Not stating the domain restriction (the inner denominator ≠ 0) when this is required for the multiplication to be valid.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P2/P3 functions and composition of functions). No formula booklet entry is invoked; this is a standard algebraic manipulation. Avoid generalising to formal Möbius-transformation theory or matrix representations, which are out of scope.
