Coordinate Geometry

Choose the algebraically simpler of two valid equations to recover the missing coordinate

Family: Equation Selection Frequency: 5 questions

Trigger — When to use

You have just found one coordinate (typically x) of a point that lies on TWO known curves/lines (e.g. a curve and its tangent/normal, a line meeting a hyperbola, a line meeting a parabola), and you now need the other coordinate. Both equations are valid at the point, so either could be substituted into. The cue is the phrase 'point of intersection' or 'where the line meets the curve', combined with two equations of differing algebraic complexity (e.g. one linear, one quadratic; or one rational like y = c²/x, one linear with fractional coefficients).

Motivation — Why this works

Because the point lies on both curves, substitution into either equation must yield the same y-value, so the student is free to pick. The simpler equation (usually the line y = mx + c, or the rational form y = c²/x for rectangular hyperbolas) requires fewer expansions, fewer fraction manipulations, and fewer sign-handling steps. This minimises arithmetic-error risk and produces an answer already in a clean form, which often matches the mark scheme presentation directly. The principle is just 'use the lighter computational path when two equivalent paths exist'.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the point P lies on two known equations and only the x-coordinate (say) has been pinned down — y is still needed.
  2. List both options mentally: substitute x into Equation 1 (e.g. the curve, often quadratic or rational), or into Equation 2 (e.g. the line, usually linear).
  3. Rule out the more complex substitution by anticipating the work: a quadratic curve will require expanding (1−m)² or similar, a fractional x-value into a linear-with-fractions formula will require careful LCM arithmetic.
  4. Commit to the equation that gives the answer in the fewest steps AND in the form most likely to match downstream needs (e.g. already factored, already simplified).
  5. Execute the single substitution and simplify; sanity-check by mentally computing the other equation's output to confirm agreement (cheap insurance against an x-coordinate error).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two equations satisfied by the point, e.g. y = mx and y = x − x².
  2. Compare their structures: count operations needed after substituting the known x.
  3. Substitute the known x into the simpler equation, e.g. y_P = m(1 − m).
  4. Simplify if needed and state the coordinates of the point.
  5. (Optional check) Substitute into the other equation and confirm the same y.

Worked Micro-Example

Curve $y = x - x^2$ meets line $y = mx$ at $P$ with $x_P = 1-m$. Using the line: $y_P = m(1-m)$ in one step. Using the curve: $y_P = (1-m) - (1-m)^2 = (1-m)[1-(1-m)] = m(1-m)$ — same answer, more work. So $P = (1-m,\; m(1-m))$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Rectangular hyperbola xy = c² met by a normal/tangent line
Once x is known, prefer y = c²/x (single division) over the line equation with fractional gradient — e.g. $y_Q = 36/(-81/4) = -16/9$.
Parabola y² = 4ax met by a chord/tangent
If x is known, y = ±√(4ax) introduces a sign ambiguity — usually safer to substitute into the line equation instead, where the sign is determined automatically.
Two linear equations (line–line intersection)
Pick the one with smaller coefficients or already solved for y.
Curve given parametrically
If you know the parameter t at the point, use the parametric formulae directly rather than back-substituting into the Cartesian equation.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming you 'must' use the curve equation because that is what the question is 'about', and ignoring the line equation entirely.
  • Thinking both equations will give the answer in the same form — often one is already factored/simplified while the other needs further work to match the mark scheme.

Pitfalls

  • If using the more complex equation, leaving the answer un-simplified and getting a different surface form from the mark scheme (presentation-mark loss).
  • Arithmetic errors with negative fractions when dividing into y = c²/x — watch the double negatives.
  • Forgetting to verify with the second equation when the x-coordinate itself was hard-won; an undetected slip in x propagates into y.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard technique within Edexcel IAL P3/P4/FP1 coordinate geometry (lines, parabolas y² = 4ax, rectangular hyperbolas xy = c², ellipses, hyperbolas). No formula-booklet entry is invoked — this is a strategic choice, not a formula. Stay with substitution into a single equation; do not attempt to 'average' or combine the two equations.
