Vectors

Split skew-lines proof into non-parallel and non-intersecting checks

Family: Proof By Definition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question asks to prove (or 'show that') two lines in 3D are skew, often with a parameter to determine. Cues include phrases like 'show that the lines are skew', 'prove L₁ and L₂ do not lie in the same plane', or being given two vector line equations r = a₁ + λd₁ and r = a₂ + μd₂ in ℝ³. Skew is defined by exactly two conditions, so the proof must address both.

Motivation — Why this works

By definition, two lines in 3D are skew iff they are neither parallel nor intersecting. Splitting the proof into these two sub-goals converts an abstract claim into two concrete algebraic checks: a proportionality test on direction vectors and a consistency test on a 3-equation, 2-unknown system. This structure also matches the mark scheme, which typically awards marks separately for each condition.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Student notices 'skew' is a defined term and recalls it requires two conditions, not one.
  2. They rule out checking only intersection, since parallel non-coincident lines also fail to intersect but are not skew; they also rule out d₁ · d₂ = 0 as that tests perpendicularity, not skewness.
  3. They commit to a two-part proof because each condition maps to a clean algebraic check and earns independent marks.
  4. They start with the parallelism check (cheap, just inspect direction vectors) before the longer intersection check.
  5. Inside the intersection check, they equate components, solve two equations for λ and μ, then test the third for inconsistency.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. State that skew requires (1) direction vectors not parallel and (2) lines do not intersect.
  2. Test (1): check whether d₂ = k d₁ for any scalar k; if not, lines are not parallel.
  3. Test (2): set a₁ + λd₁ = a₂ + μd₂, giving three component equations in λ and μ.
  4. Solve two of the equations simultaneously for λ and μ.
  5. Substitute into the third equation; if it is inconsistent, the lines do not intersect.
  6. Conclude explicitly: since the lines are not parallel and do not intersect, they are skew.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $L_1: \mathbf{r}=(1,0,2)+\lambda(1,1,0)$ and $L_2: \mathbf{r}=(0,1,3)+\mu(2,1,1)$. Direction vectors $(1,1,0)$ and $(2,1,1)$ are not proportional (1/2 ≠ 1/1), so $L_1$ and $L_2$ are not parallel. Equating: $1+\lambda=2\mu$, $\lambda=1+\mu$, $2=3+\mu$. From the third, $\mu=-1$, so $\lambda=0$; but then $1+0=1\ne 2(-1)=-2$, contradiction. Hence the lines do not intersect, and so $L_1$ and $L_2$ are skew.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Find the value of a parameter (e.g. b) for which the lines are NOT skew
Run the same two checks but treat b as unknown; the parallelism check usually rules out one value, and the intersection check yields the critical b for which the third equation becomes consistent.
Prove two lines are coplanar (i.e. not skew)
Show either that direction vectors are parallel, or that the system a₁ + λd₁ = a₂ + μd₂ has a solution; equivalently show the scalar triple product [d₁, d₂, a₂ − a₁] = 0 (FM only).
Lines given in Cartesian form (x−x₀)/a = (y−y₀)/b = (z−z₀)/c
First convert to vector form to extract direction vectors and a point on each line, then apply the same two-condition proof.
Further Maths: single-step skew test
Compute the scalar triple product (d₁ × d₂) · (a₂ − a₁); if non-zero, lines are skew. Still preferable to split for 'show that' style questions.

Common Misreadings

  • Believing 'no intersection' alone proves skew, forgetting parallel non-coincident lines also fail to intersect.
  • Confusing 'skew' with 'perpendicular' and trying to show d₁ · d₂ = 0.
  • Treating 'skew' as meaning the lines just look non-coplanar without invoking the formal definition.
  • Using a single parameter for both lines when equating position vectors, instead of distinct λ and μ.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to state the final combined conclusion that both conditions hold, hence the lines are skew.
  • Solving two of the three component equations and forgetting to verify (or contradict) the third.
  • Declaring 'not parallel' from a single non-proportional component pair without checking all three components.
  • Algebraic slip when substituting back to test the third equation, leading to a false 'intersection found'.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Core Pure / Further Pure vectors content; the parallelism and intersection checks are standard, and the scalar-triple-product variant is an FP-level shortcut.
