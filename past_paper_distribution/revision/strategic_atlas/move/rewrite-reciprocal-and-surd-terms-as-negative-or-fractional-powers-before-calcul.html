Algebra & Functions

Rewrite reciprocal and surd terms as negative or fractional powers before calculus

Family: Exponent Rewriting Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The expression contains terms with x in the denominator (like a/x^n) or under a root (like √x or ∛x), and you need to differentiate or integrate. The power rule d/dx(ax^n) = anx^{n−1} and ∫ax^n dx = ax^{n+1}/(n+1) + C both require each term to be in the explicit form ax^n. Cues: fractions with x in the denominator, surds in numerator or denominator, or mixed forms like x^2/√x that hide a single power.

Motivation — Why this works

The power rule operates only on terms of the form ax^n where n is a real constant. Reciprocals and surds are mathematically equivalent to negative or fractional powers via the index laws 1/x^n = x^{−n} and ⁿ√x = x^{1/n}. Rewriting first lets you apply the power rule term-by-term, avoiding the quotient rule or chain rule when they aren't needed and reducing algebraic friction and error risk.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the expression has a term that is not yet in ax^n form — there's an x in the denominator or under a root.
  2. I rule out the quotient rule (when the numerator is constant) and the chain rule (when the surd is just √x), since these would be heavier machinery for what is really a single power of x.
  3. I commit to rewriting because once every term is ax^n, the power rule applies directly and uniformly across the sum.
  4. I rewrite each offending term using index laws: 1/x^n → x^{−n}, √x → x^{1/2}, and combine coefficients cleanly.
  5. I double-check signs (a minus in front of a fraction stays with the coefficient) and that fractional/negative exponents are written as a single power before doing any calculus.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Scan each term and flag any that are not already in ax^n form (denominators with x, surds, mixed forms).
  2. Apply index laws: a/x^n = ax^{−n}; ⁿ√x = x^{1/n}; x^p/x^q = x^{p−q}.
  3. Carry any leading sign or coefficient through the rewrite (e.g. −5/x² becomes −5x^{−2}).
  4. Rewrite the full expression as a sum of ax^n terms.
  5. Verify each term has a single real exponent before differentiating or integrating.

Worked Micro-Example

Differentiate y = 4x^3 − 5/x^2 + 3√x.\\ Rewrite: y = 4x^3 − 5x^{−2} + 3x^{1/2}.\\ Then dy/dx = 12x^2 + 10x^{−3} + (3/2)x^{−1/2}.\\ Equivalently, dy/dx = 12x^2 + 10/x^3 + 3/(2√x).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Surd in numerator: y = 3√x
Rewrite as 3x^{1/2}, then power rule gives (3/2)x^{−1/2}.
Cube root: y = ∛x or 1/∛x
Rewrite as x^{1/3} or x^{−1/3} respectively.
Fraction with coefficient: 3/(2x^5)
Rewrite as (3/2)x^{−5}; keep the rational coefficient intact.
Mixed surd/power quotient: x^2/√x
Use index laws to combine into x^{2−1/2} = x^{3/2} before applying calculus.
Expanding before rewriting: y = (2x+1)/x^2
Split into 2x^{−1} + x^{−2} first, then differentiate or integrate term-by-term.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 5/x^2 as requiring the quotient rule, when it is just a constant times a negative power.
  • Treating √x as requiring the chain rule rather than seeing it as x^{1/2}.
  • Reading x^2/√x as needing the quotient rule instead of simplifying via index laws to x^{3/2}.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the negative sign on the coefficient: writing 5x^{−2} instead of −5x^{−2} when the original term is −5/x^2.
  • Dropping the negative on the exponent: writing 5x^2 instead of 5x^{−2}.
  • Errors with fractional exponents: writing √x as x^2 or x^{−1/2} instead of x^{1/2}.
  • Forgetting to combine x^p/x^q into a single power before differentiating, then misapplying the quotient rule.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1/P2) — index laws and the power rule for differentiation and integration of x^n for real n (excluding n = −1 for integration, which requires ln|x| at later stages).
