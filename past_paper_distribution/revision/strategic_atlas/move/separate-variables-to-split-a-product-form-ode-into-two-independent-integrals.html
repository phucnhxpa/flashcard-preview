Strategic Atlas / Differential Equations / Separation Of Variables
Differential Equations

Separate variables to split a product-form ODE into two independent integrals

Family: Separation Of Variables Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a first-order ODE is presented in the form dy/dx = f(y)·g(x), where the right-hand side visibly factorises into a part depending only on y and a part depending only on x. Typical surface cues: powers like √A·t^(-2/5), v²·(-2x), or rational structures like N(1−N)·k. Equivalent phrasings: 'the RHS is separable', 'y and x can be moved to opposite sides', or 'each variable can be paired with its own differential'. If you can write the RHS as (y-stuff) × (x-stuff) without any mixed terms, this move applies.

Motivation — Why this works

Separation works because dividing by f(y) and multiplying by dx legally rewrites the ODE as dy/f(y) = g(x) dx, where each side is a single-variable integrand. The underlying principle is the chain rule run in reverse: ∫(1/f(y)) dy/dx dx = ∫(1/f(y)) dy. This converts a differential equation into two ordinary integrals, which is the fastest route whenever the factorisation is already explicit — no integrating factor, substitution, or linearisation is needed.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the RHS of dy/dx is a clean product of a y-only factor and an x-only factor (e.g. √A · t^(-2/5), or v²·(-2x)) — no mixed terms like (x+y) or e^(xy).
  2. Rule out integrating factors (needed only if the ODE is linear in y but not separable) and substitution methods (needed for homogeneous or Bernoulli forms not already factored).
  3. Commit to separation because both resulting integrals look elementary (power rule, log, or partial fractions at worst).
  4. Bridge to operation: divide both sides by the y-factor (noting any y-values that make it zero — these may be lost solutions) and multiply both sides by dx to write the differential form g(y) dy = h(x) dx.
  5. Decide how to handle the constant of integration: usually combine into a single +C on one side, and choose its sign/position to simplify the algebra when applying an initial condition later.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the ODE as dy/dx = f(y)·g(x).
  2. Divide both sides by f(y) (recording f(y)=0 as possible singular solutions): (1/f(y)) dy/dx = g(x).
  3. Multiply by dx to obtain differential form: dy/f(y) = g(x) dx.
  4. Integrate both sides independently: ∫ dy/f(y) = ∫ g(x) dx + C.
  5. Solve algebraically for y (or leave implicit), then apply any initial/boundary condition to fix C.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\frac{dv}{dx} = -2x v^{2}$. Separate: $v^{-2}\,dv = -2x\,dx$. Integrate: $-v^{-1} = -x^{2} + C$, i.e. $\frac{1}{v} = x^{2} + c$. Hence $v = \frac{1}{x^{2}+c}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Square-root y-factor, e.g. dA/dt = (3/2)·A^{1/2}·t^{-2/5}
Divide by A^{1/2} to get A^{-1/2} dA = (3/2) t^{-2/5} dt; both sides integrate by the power rule, yielding 2A^{1/2} = (5/2)t^{3/5} + C.
Logistic-type y-factor, e.g. dN/dt = kN(1−N)
Separate as dN/[N(1−N)] = k dt, then apply partial fractions on the LHS — same separation principle, harder integration.
After a substitution reduces a harder ODE to separable form (e.g. y = vx in homogeneous ODEs)
Apply separation to the reduced equation in (v,x); remember to back-substitute at the end.

Common Misreadings

  • Misreading 3√A as 3A (missing the square root), which collapses the integral to ln A and prevents the expected squared form.
  • Treating an additive RHS like x + y as if it were separable — only multiplicative factorisation qualifies.
  • Mistaking a linear ODE dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x) with non-zero Q for separable; integrating factor is needed instead.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the constant of integration, especially when the initial condition is applied later — leads to a wrong particular solution.
  • Losing singular solutions like y = 0 (or v = 0) when dividing by f(y); always note where f(y) = 0.
  • Sign errors when rearranging, e.g. moving from -1/v = -x² + C to 1/v = x² + c — track the sign of C carefully (or just relabel as a new constant).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 (first-order separable ODEs) and FP-level extensions. No formula booklet entry is required; standard integrals (power rule, ln, partial fractions for FP) suffice. Avoid invoking exact equations or general integrating-factor theory beyond the linear case dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x), which is out of scope here.
