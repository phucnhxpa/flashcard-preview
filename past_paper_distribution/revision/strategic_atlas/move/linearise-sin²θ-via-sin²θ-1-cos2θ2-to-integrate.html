Strategic Atlas / Integration / Double Angle Linearisation
Integration

Linearise sin²θ via sin²θ = (1 − cos2θ)/2 to integrate

Family: Double Angle Linearisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an integrand contains sin²θ (or cos²θ) and no substitution simplifies it directly. Especially common after expanding squared polar integrands like (1 + sinθ)² inside ½∫r² dθ for polar area, or in arc-length/volume integrals where (a ± sinθ)² appears. Cue phrases: 'find the area enclosed by the polar curve', 'evaluate ∫sin²θ dθ between given bounds'. The presence of an even power of sine or cosine with no odd-power partner to absorb into du is the giveaway.

Motivation — Why this works

sin²θ has no elementary antiderivative as written, but the double-angle identity cos2θ = 1 − 2sin²θ rearranges to sin²θ = (1 − cos2θ)/2, expressing the squared term as a linear combination of a constant and cos2θ — both of which integrate immediately. This linearisation converts a non-trivial term into standard pieces, so the whole integrand becomes a sum of constants, sinθ, cosθ, sin2θ, cos2θ that integrate term by term. It is the standard A-level technique for any even power of sine or cosine.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that after expansion the integrand contains sin²θ (or cos²θ), which cannot be integrated directly as a power.
  2. Rule out u-substitution (no spare sinθ or cosθ factor to pair with dθ) and integration by parts (would loop).
  3. Commit to the double-angle identity because it is the canonical A-level linearisation for even trig powers.
  4. Replace sin²θ with (1 − cos2θ)/2 and simplify constants before integrating to avoid bookkeeping errors.
  5. When integrating cos2θ, remember the chain factor: ∫cos2θ dθ = (sin2θ)/2, then combine with any existing coefficient.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Expand any squared bracket fully, e.g. (1 + sinθ)² = 1 + 2sinθ + sin²θ.
  2. Replace sin²θ with (1 − cos2θ)/2 (or cos²θ with (1 + cos2θ)/2).
  3. Combine constant terms to simplify the integrand.
  4. Integrate term by term: ∫1 dθ = θ, ∫sinθ dθ = −cosθ, ∫cos2θ dθ = (sin2θ)/2.
  5. Substitute the upper and lower limits and subtract carefully.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_{\pi/6}^{\pi/2}(1+\sin\theta)^2\,d\theta$. Expand: $1+2\sin\theta+\sin^2\theta$. Linearise: $\sin^2\theta=\tfrac{1-\cos 2\theta}{2}$, giving $\tfrac{3}{2}+2\sin\theta-\tfrac{1}{2}\cos 2\theta$. Integrate: $\tfrac{3\theta}{2}-2\cos\theta-\tfrac{1}{4}\sin 2\theta$. Evaluate between the limits to obtain the area expression.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Integrand contains cos²θ
Use cos²θ = (1 + cos2θ)/2 instead; the rest of the procedure is identical.
Integrand contains sin⁴θ or cos⁴θ
Apply the identity twice: first to write sin⁴θ = ((1 − cos2θ)/2)², then linearise the resulting cos²2θ term using cos²2θ = (1 + cos4θ)/2.
Integrand sin²(kθ) with k ≠ 1
Use sin²(kθ) = (1 − cos(2kθ))/2 and remember the chain factor 1/(2k) when integrating cos(2kθ).
Mixed product sinθ cosθ in a polar/area context
Use sinθ cosθ = ½ sin2θ to linearise instead of the squared identity.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating sin²θ as (sinθ)² and trying to use the power rule directly.
  • Attempting u = sinθ even though no spare cosθ dθ is available.
  • Confusing the identities and writing sin²θ = (1 + cos2θ)/2.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the chain factor when integrating cos2θ → (sin2θ)/2, then mishandling the existing 1/2 coefficient (correct combined coefficient is 1/4).
  • Sign error from cos2θ = 1 − 2sin²θ versus cos2θ = 2cos²θ − 1 when rearranging.
  • Evaluating sin(2·π/6) = sin(π/3) = √3/2 incorrectly, or forgetting sin(2·π/2) = sinπ = 0.
  • Dropping the overall ½ from the polar area formula ½∫r² dθ after focusing on the linearisation.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL scope: double-angle identities are in Pure Maths (C3/P3) and the technique is standard for polar area in Further Pure (FP2/FP3).
