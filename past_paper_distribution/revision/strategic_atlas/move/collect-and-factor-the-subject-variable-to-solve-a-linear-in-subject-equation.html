Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever the variable you are trying to make the subject (call it y) appears in two or more separate terms on opposite sides of an equation, so direct division cannot isolate it. Typical surface cues: after clearing a fraction you see something like xy − x = 3y + 2; after applying integration by parts you see I_n appearing on both sides; when finding an inverse function of a Möbius form (ax+b)/(cx+d); or when eliminating a parameter t that appears in two terms. The key visual signal is: the subject variable appears to the first power in every term it occurs in (so the equation is LINEAR in the subject), but it is scattered across the equation.

Motivation — Why this works

Recognising that the equation is linear in the subject variable is the unlocking insight: a linear equation ay = b, however disguised, is solved by gathering all y-terms onto one side and factoring y out to give y·(expression) = (other expression), then dividing once. This converts a seemingly tangled algebraic equation — even a self-referential one like I_n = (n−1)(I_{n−2} − I_n) — into a one-step division. The underlying principle is simply the distributive law in reverse: if y appears additively in several terms, it can always be factored.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the subject variable appears in two (or more) separate terms on different sides of the equation, so I cannot just divide both sides by a single quantity to isolate it.
  2. Rule out cross-multiplying further or introducing a quadratic — checking each term, the subject appears only to the first power, so the equation is linear in the subject and a quadratic approach would be wasteful or wrong.
  3. Rule out 'this is circular' panic when the subject appears on both sides (e.g. I_n on both sides of a reduction relation): self-reference at degree 1 is just a linear equation in the subject, not a definitional loop.
  4. Commit to the standard linear-rearrangement template: move every term containing the subject to one side, every other term to the other side, then factor the subject out.
  5. Execute the move: factor to get (subject)·(bracket) = (rest), then divide by the bracket once to read off the closed form.
  6. Record any restriction created by the division — e.g. the bracketed factor must be non-zero — since this often forms the next part of an exam question (domain/range, validity of n).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify every term containing the subject variable y.
  2. Add/subtract terms across the equals sign to gather all y-terms on one side and all non-y terms on the other side, watching signs carefully.
  3. Factor y out of the gathered side: y·(bracket) = (other side).
  4. Divide both sides by the bracket to obtain y = (other side)/(bracket).
  5. State the restriction that the bracket ≠ 0 (gives the excluded value of the parameter).

Worked Micro-Example

Make $y$ the subject of $xy - x = 3y + 2$. Gather $y$-terms: $xy - 3y = x + 2$. Factor: $y(x-3) = x+2$. Divide: $y = \dfrac{x+2}{x-3}$, valid for $x \neq 3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Self-referential reduction formula, e.g. $I_n = (n-1)(I_{n-2} - I_n)$ from integration by parts.
Treat $I_n$ as the subject: expand to $I_n + (n-1)I_n = (n-1)I_{n-2}$, factor to $nI_n = (n-1)I_{n-2}$, divide to get $I_n = \tfrac{n-1}{n}I_{n-2}$.
Finding the inverse of a Möbius function $f(x) = (ax+b)/(cx+d)$.
Set $y = f(x)$, clear the fraction, collect $x$-terms, factor $x$, divide — same template with $x$ as subject.
Parameter elimination where $t$ appears in two terms of the parametric equations.
Rearrange one equation to collect $t$-terms, factor, divide, then substitute the resulting expression for $t$ into the other equation.
Surd or complex denominators where the subject is hidden inside a conjugate combination.
Multiply through to clear the denominator first, then apply the same collect-and-factor template.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the equation as quadratic in the subject and trying the quadratic formula, when in fact every occurrence of the subject is to the first power.
  • Seeing the subject on both sides and concluding the relation is circular or ill-defined, instead of recognising it as a linear equation to solve.
  • Attempting to 'cross-multiply' a second time or introducing extra fractions instead of just collecting like terms.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when moving terms across the equals sign — particularly with negative coefficients of the subject.
  • Forgetting to factor cleanly: leaving the answer as $xy - 3y = x+2$ without progressing to $y(x-3) = x+2$.
  • Dividing by the bracket without recording the restriction that it must be non-zero (e.g. $x \neq 3$), which is usually needed for the domain of an inverse or the validity of a reduction formula.
  • Forgetting to include all subject-terms when collecting — easy to miss one when the subject is buried inside a product.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (changing the subject, inverse functions in P2/P3) and Further Pure (reduction formulae via integration by parts in FP2/FP3). No formula booklet entry is required — this is a core algebraic manipulation. Avoid generalising to non-linear cases (where the subject appears squared or inside a transcendental function) without further technique.
