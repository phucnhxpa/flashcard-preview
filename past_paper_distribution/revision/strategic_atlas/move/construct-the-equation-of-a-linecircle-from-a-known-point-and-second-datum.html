Coordinate Geometry

Construct the equation of a line/circle from a known point and second datum

Family: Line Equation Construction Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever the question asks for the equation of a straight line or circle and the work so far has produced (i) a specific point that lies on the locus and (ii) a second datum that pins it down — a gradient for a line, or a centre/radius-determining feature for a circle. Typical cues: 'Find the equation of the tangent/normal at P', 'Write down the equation of the circle with centre N passing through P', or having just computed dy/dx at a stated x-value. Equivalently: you have all the ingredients of a standard form and only the assembly step remains.

Motivation — Why this works

Standard forms y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) for a line and (x − a)² + (y − b)² = r² for a circle are designed precisely to be filled in once a point and one further datum are known. Choosing the form that matches your data avoids unnecessary algebra (e.g. solving for c in y = mx + c, or expanding and comparing coefficients in a circle). The underlying principle is that two pieces of information uniquely determine the locus, so direct substitution into the right canonical form is the shortest route.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question wants an equation of a line or circle — so I need a canonical form to fill in.
  2. Inventory what is already pinned down: do I have a point on the locus? Do I have a gradient (line) or a centre (circle)? If yes to both, assembly is immediate.
  3. Rule out detours: y = mx + c would force me to solve for c; expanding (x−a)² + (y−b)² = r² and matching x² + y² + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0 coefficients is longer. The point-gradient / centre-radius forms use the data directly.
  4. Commit to the canonical form that matches the data: y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) for a line, (x − a)² + (y − b)² = r² for a circle.
  5. If a radius isn't given, derive it from the geometry: e.g. r = distance from centre N to a known point P on the circle (r² = (x_P − a)² + (y_P − b)²), or r = perpendicular distance from N to a tangent line.
  6. Substitute carefully, watching signs (a centre at (−1, 2) gives (x + 1)² + (y − 2)²) and squaring r when needed; finally tidy to whatever form the mark scheme expects (y = mx + c, ax + by + c = 0, or expanded circle form).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the canonical form: line → y − y₁ = m(x − x₁); circle → (x − a)² + (y − b)² = r².
  2. Substitute the known point (x₁, y₁) or centre (a, b) directly into the form.
  3. Substitute the second datum: gradient m for the line, or r² (computed via distance formula or perpendicular-distance formula) for the circle.
  4. Simplify: clear brackets/fractions, e.g. y − 1 = (3/5)x ⇒ y = (3/5)x + 1, or 3x − 5y + 5 = 0 if integer-coefficient form is required.
  5. Sanity-check: the point should satisfy the final equation; the gradient/radius should reappear when read off.

Worked Micro-Example

Tangent at $(0,1)$ with gradient $\tfrac{3}{5}$: $y - 1 = \tfrac{3}{5}(x - 0) \;\Rightarrow\; y = \tfrac{3}{5}x + 1$. Circle with centre $N(-1,2)$ through $P(0,0)$: $r^2 = (0-(-1))^2 + (0-2)^2 = 5$, so $(x+1)^2 + (y-2)^2 = 5$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Normal line instead of tangent
Replace the gradient by −1/m before substituting into y − y₁ = m(x − x₁).
Circle where only a tangent line and centre are known (no point on the circle given)
Compute r as the perpendicular distance from the centre to the tangent: r = |ax₁ + by₁ + c| / √(a² + b²).
Mark scheme demands ax + by + c = 0 or expanded circle form x² + y² + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0
Multiply through to clear fractions / expand the standard form at the end.

Common Misreadings

  • Mistaking a diameter endpoint pair for a point-and-radius pair (so NP gets used as diameter rather than radius, or vice versa).
  • Treating the gradient of the curve at a different x-value as the gradient at the point of interest.

Pitfalls

  • Sign slips in (x − x₁), (y − y₁), or (x − a)² when the coordinate is negative — e.g. writing (x − 1)² when the centre's x-coordinate is −1.
  • Forgetting to square the radius: writing (x+1)² + (y−2)² = √5 instead of = 5.
  • Leaving the line in unsimplified form when y = mx + c or integer coefficients are required.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL P1/P2 coordinate geometry: straight-line forms and the standard circle equation. The point-gradient form and (x−a)² + (y−b)² = r² are assumed knowledge (not explicitly in the formula booklet); the perpendicular-distance formula is a P3-level tool. No need to invoke parametric or polar forms.
