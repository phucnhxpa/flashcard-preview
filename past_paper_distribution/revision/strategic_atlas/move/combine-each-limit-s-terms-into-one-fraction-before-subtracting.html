Trigger — When to use

Fires after integrating when the antiderivative has three or more terms with different denominators (e.g. 3, 5, 2, 1) and you must evaluate F(b) − F(a). Cue phrases: 'lots of fractions to subtract', 'mixed denominators at both limits', 'large numerical coefficients from polynomial expansion'. Also applies after partial-fraction integration where logs/powers leave several rational terms. If subtracting term-by-term would generate 6+ separate fractions, this move applies.

Motivation — Why this works

Subtracting F(b) − F(a) term-by-term across many mismatched denominators multiplies the number of fractional manipulations and the chances of sign or LCD errors. By collapsing F(b) into one fraction and F(a) into one fraction first, you isolate the arithmetic at each limit (where the numbers are independent) and reduce the final step to a single two-fraction subtraction. This is purely an error-management discipline — the answer is unchanged — but it makes mistakes locatable and the working easier to mark.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the antiderivative has 3+ different denominators, so direct term-by-term subtraction will produce many fractions to combine at the end.
  2. Rules out term-by-term subtraction (too many simultaneous fractions) and rules out converting everything to a single global LCD immediately (the LCDs at the two limits may differ, and forcing one LCD inflates the numbers).
  3. Commits to evaluating F(b) and F(a) independently as single fractions, then performing one final subtraction with a fresh LCD.
  4. Bridges to the first operation: identify the LCD of the antiderivative's denominators (this is fixed, independent of the limit), substitute x = b, and combine.
  5. Tactical decisions: keep numerators unsimplified until inside the single fraction; track signs carefully when the lower-limit value is negative; only at the very end take the LCD of the two resulting fractions.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the LCD of the antiderivative's denominators (e.g. 3, 5, 2, 1 → LCD 30, but you may use a smaller LCD per limit if some terms vanish).
  2. Substitute x = b into each term, convert to the common denominator, and combine the numerators into a single fraction p/q.
  3. Repeat with x = a to obtain a single fraction r/s.
  4. Compute p/q − r/s using LCD = lcm(q, s); be explicit with the sign if r/s is negative.
  5. Simplify the resulting single fraction.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_2^3 \!(8x^5 - 36x^4 + 54x^3 - 27x^2)\,dx$. Antiderivative: $F(x) = \tfrac{4x^6}{3} - \tfrac{36x^5}{5} + \tfrac{27x^4}{2} - 9x^3$. At $x=3$: combine over 10 → $F(3) = \tfrac{9720 - 17496 + 10935 - 2430}{10} = \tfrac{729}{10}$. At $x=2$: combine over 15 → $F(2) = \tfrac{1280 - 3456 + 2160}{15} = -\tfrac{16}{15}$. Then $F(3) - F(2) = \tfrac{729}{10} + \tfrac{16}{15} = \tfrac{2187 + 32}{30} = \tfrac{2219}{30}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Definite integral after partial fractions producing several log and power terms
Combine the rational (non-log) terms into one fraction at each limit; keep the log terms as a separate combined logarithm using log laws.
Volume of revolution with $\pi\int y^2\,dx$ leaving polynomial in x with mixed denominators
Factor the $\pi$ outside, then apply the same single-fraction-per-limit discipline to the bracketed value.
Integration by parts giving a boundary term $[uv]_a^b$ plus an integral, each producing fractions
Treat $[uv]_a^b$ and the remaining integral separately; collapse each to a single fraction before adding.
Mean value of a function $\tfrac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$
Combine F(b) and F(a) into single fractions first, then divide once by $(b-a)$ at the end.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the move as mathematically required rather than as error-management — leading to forced use even when only two simple terms appear.
  • Subtracting F(b) − F(a) term-by-term across mismatched denominators and losing track of signs when F(a) is negative.
  • Picking a single global LCD up-front and inflating numerators unnecessarily.

Pitfalls

  • Choosing an incorrect LCD (e.g. forgetting the integer term has denominator 1).
  • Sign error when F(a) < 0: writing F(b) − F(a) but failing to flip the sign of F(a)'s numerator.
  • Arithmetic slips when scaling large integers like $3^6$ or $3^5$ to a common denominator — keep the conversion factor visible.
  • Forgetting to simplify the final fraction (e.g. cancelling a common factor with 30).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL definite-integral practice (P2/P3/FP-level). Uses only polynomial integration, basic log/exp antiderivatives, and elementary fraction arithmetic — no techniques outside the syllabus.
