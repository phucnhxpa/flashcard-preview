Trigonometry

Apply Pythagorean/reciprocal identity to homogenize equation into one trig function

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 5 questions

Trigger — When to use

The equation contains two different trig functions (e.g. cos θ with sin²θ, or cos θ with cosec θ) and you need to reduce it to a single variable to solve. Look specifically for a mismatch where one function appears at degree 1 while another appears squared, OR where a reciprocal function (cosec, sec, cot) sits opposite a primary function (sin, cos). The visual cue is: 'two trig families present, one equation' — and standard solution methods (factoring, quadratic formula, inverse trig) require a single function.

Motivation — Why this works

Substituting via the Pythagorean identity sin²θ = 1 − cos²θ (or its mirror) collapses a two-function equation into a polynomial in one trig function — typically a quadratic — which is then solvable by factoring or the quadratic formula. Substituting reciprocal identities (cosec θ = 1/sin θ, sec θ = 1/cos θ, cot θ = cos θ/sin θ) removes reciprocal forms so that double-angle identities like sin 2θ = 2 sin θ cos θ become applicable. The choice of which direction to substitute is forced by the powers present: replace the squared function so the surviving function stays at its original degree, avoiding square roots and preserving polynomial structure.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation mixes two trig functions — list them and their powers (e.g. cos θ at degree 1, sin²θ at degree 2; or cos θ alongside cosec θ).
  2. Rule out direct factoring or inverse-trig because the two functions block a single-variable solve; rule out Pythagorean identities involving squares (1 + cot²θ = cosec²θ) when no squared term is present.
  3. Decide the substitution direction by checking powers: if one function appears squared and the other at degree 1, replace the squared one (so the linear function survives without introducing a √). If a reciprocal function appears, replace it with its sin/cos form to unify families.
  4. Commit to the identity: sin²θ = 1 − cos²θ to get a quadratic in cos θ; or cosec θ = 1/sin θ to expose sin θ alongside cos θ for a double-angle setup.
  5. Execute the substitution, expand carefully (watch the sign on −2(1 − cos²θ)), and rearrange to standard quadratic form a·cos²θ + b·cos θ + c = 0.
  6. Note any domain restrictions introduced (e.g. sin θ ≠ 0 when dividing) and confirm solutions lie in [−1, 1] and the prescribed range.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the squared trig function (or the reciprocal function) to be eliminated.
  2. Substitute the appropriate identity: sin²θ → 1 − cos²θ, cos²θ → 1 − sin²θ, cosec θ → 1/sin θ, sec θ → 1/cos θ, or cot θ → cos θ/sin θ.
  3. Expand and clear fractions (cross-multiply if a reciprocal was introduced).
  4. Rearrange into standard quadratic form, e.g. a·cos²θ + b·cos θ + c = 0.
  5. Solve the quadratic (factorise or use the quadratic formula); discard any root outside [−1, 1].
  6. Apply inverse trig and use symmetries (CAST or graph) to find all θ in the required range.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $3\cos\theta - 2\sin^2\theta = 0$. Substitute $\sin^2\theta = 1 - \cos^2\theta$: $3\cos\theta - 2(1 - \cos^2\theta) = 0 \Rightarrow 2\cos^2\theta + 3\cos\theta - 2 = 0$. Factorise: $(2\cos\theta - 1)(\cos\theta + 2) = 0$, so $\cos\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}$ (rejecting $\cos\theta = -2$).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation has cos²θ and sin θ at first power
Substitute cos²θ = 1 − sin²θ to obtain a quadratic in sin θ — same logic, mirror direction.
Equation contains cosec θ alongside cos θ with a target involving sin 2θ
Replace cosec θ = 1/sin θ; then multiply through by sin θ so the equation features sin θ cos θ, ready for sin 2θ = 2 sin θ cos θ.
Double-angle equation involving cos 2θ with sin²θ or cos²θ already present
Choose the matching form: cos 2θ = 1 − 2sin²θ if sin appears, or cos 2θ = 2cos²θ − 1 if cos appears — to keep a single trig family.
Rational/sum-to-infinity equation with sin²θ in numerator and cos θ in denominator
Substitute sin²θ = 1 − cos²θ, then cross-multiply to obtain a quadratic in cos θ; check for factor cancellation (1 − cos θ)(1 + cos θ) before expanding.
cot θ appears with sin θ or cos θ
Replace cot θ = cos θ/sin θ to unify into sin/cos form.

Common Misreadings

  • Seeing 'cosec' and immediately reaching for 1 + cot²θ = cosec²θ even when no squared term is in play — that identity has no entry point here.
  • Trying cos θ = ±√(1 − sin²θ), introducing surds and squaring later, when a clean substitution in the other direction was available.
  • Treating sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 as the goal of the substitution rather than as a tool — students sometimes 'add 1' instead of substituting.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when expanding −2(1 − cos²θ): writing −2 − 2cos²θ instead of −2 + 2cos²θ.
  • Forgetting that reciprocal substitutions implicitly require sin θ ≠ 0 (or cos θ ≠ 0) — these excluded values must be checked or noted.
  • After solving the quadratic, failing to reject roots outside [−1, 1] (e.g. cos θ = −2).
  • In ranges like π < α < 2π, forgetting that cos can be positive or negative — both quadratic roots may need testing against the domain.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P2/P3): uses the Pythagorean identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 and reciprocal definitions (cosec, sec, cot), all in the formula booklet. Avoid invoking sum-to-product or Weierstrass t-substitution — these are out of scope and unnecessary here.
