Trigger — When to use

Fires when an expression — typically under a square root in an arc-length or surface-area integral — contains a sum like sin⁴θ/cos²θ + sin²θ, or more generally two trig terms that share a sin²θ (or cos²θ) factor once placed over a common denominator. Cue words: 'simplify before integrating', 'show the integrand reduces to tan θ / sec θ / a single trig function'. Also fires whenever a radicand is a sum of two trig terms and one term is a quotient with cos²θ (or sin²θ) in the denominator.

Motivation — Why this works

Putting both terms over cos²θ produces a numerator of the form sin²θ(sin²θ + cos²θ), and the Pythagorean identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 collapses the bracket. What was a two-term sum becomes the single quotient sin²θ/cos²θ = tan²θ, and the square root then reduces to |tan θ|. This converts an apparently intractable radical into an elementary integrand, which is exactly the design of such exam questions.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice both terms in the radicand carry a sin²θ — there is an obvious common factor waiting to be exposed.
  2. Rule out attacking the root directly (no obvious substitution) and rule out expanding into a quartic in sin θ — neither leads anywhere.
  3. Commit to forcing a common denominator of cos²θ, because that is what will surface the Pythagorean identity inside a bracket.
  4. Rewrite sin²θ as sin²θ·cos²θ/cos²θ so the two terms can be added as a single fraction.
  5. Once sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 has been used, take the square root carefully and check the sign on the given θ-interval before dropping the modulus.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write sin⁴θ/cos²θ + sin²θ = (sin⁴θ + sin²θ cos²θ)/cos²θ.
  2. Factor the numerator: sin²θ(sin²θ + cos²θ).
  3. Apply sin²θ + cos²θ = 1, leaving sin²θ/cos²θ = tan²θ.
  4. Take the square root: √(tan²θ) = |tan θ|.
  5. Resolve the modulus using the stated range of θ (e.g. on [0, π/4], tan θ ≥ 0, so √(tan²θ) = tan θ).

Worked Micro-Example

Simplify $\sqrt{\tfrac{\sin^4\theta}{\cos^2\theta} + \sin^2\theta}$ for $\theta\in[0,\tfrac{\pi}{4}]$. Combine: $\tfrac{\sin^4\theta + \sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta}{\cos^2\theta} = \tfrac{\sin^2\theta(\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta)}{\cos^2\theta} = \tfrac{\sin^2\theta}{\cos^2\theta} = \tan^2\theta$. Hence the radical equals $|\tan\theta| = \tan\theta$ on the given interval, so $\int \sqrt{\cdots}\,d\theta = -\ln|\cos\theta| + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Arc-length integrand √((dx/dt)² + (dy/dt)²) with x, y given by trig functions
Expand the squares, then factor a common sin²t or cos²t so that sin²t + cos²t = 1 collapses the bracket to leave a perfect square under the root.
Surface-area integral 2π y √(1 + (dy/dx)²) with dy/dx in trig form
Place 1 and (dy/dx)² over a common denominator (often cos²θ) so the numerator factors and reduces via the Pythagorean identity to sec²θ or tan²θ.
Radicand of the form cos⁴θ/sin²θ + cos²θ
Mirror the move with sin²θ as common denominator: factor cos²θ, apply the identity, obtain cot²θ; resolve √(cot²θ) using the sign of cot θ on the interval.
FP-style reduction-formula setups requiring a single trig power before integration
Use the same factor-and-identity manoeuvre to rewrite sums of trig powers as a single power before applying the reduction formula.

Common Misreadings

  • Failing to spot the common sin²θ factor because the second term is left as sin²θ rather than rewritten over cos²θ.
  • Trying to apply 1 − cos²θ = sin²θ term-by-term instead of first creating a single fraction, which scrambles the algebra.
  • Treating sin⁴θ/cos²θ + sin²θ as already simplified and attempting a substitution on the unsimplified radicand.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the cos²θ denominator when factoring the numerator.
  • Writing √(tan²θ) = tan θ without checking the sign of tan θ on the given interval (it should be |tan θ|).
  • Algebraic slip when forming the common denominator: forgetting to multiply sin²θ by cos²θ in the numerator.
  • Mis-applying the identity as sin²θ − cos²θ = 1 or sin²θ + cos²θ = sin 2θ under exam pressure.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the Pythagorean identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 and basic fraction manipulation, both core to Edexcel IAL P2/P3; the resulting integral ∫tan θ dθ = −ln|cos θ| + C is standard P3/FP-level content.
