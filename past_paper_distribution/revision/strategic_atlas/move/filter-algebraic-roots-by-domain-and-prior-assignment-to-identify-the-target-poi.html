Coordinate Geometry

Filter algebraic roots by domain and prior assignment to identify the target point

Family: Domain Filtering Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an intersection or simultaneous-equation step produces more roots than the geometric configuration admits — typically a cubic or quartic with three or four solutions when only one new point is sought. Cues include an explicit domain restriction (e.g. $x \geq 0$, $x > 0$, or an interval from a sketch), a labelled point already known to sit at one of the roots, or a diagram showing the intersection lies in a specific region. Also triggers when 'show that' or 'find the coordinates of B' follows after A has been given.

Motivation — Why this works

Polynomial elimination between two curves produces every algebraic intersection, including those outside the stated domain or already named in the problem. The original geometric setup imposes constraints (sign of $x$, quadrant, branch of a curve) that the algebra cannot see. Discarding extraneous roots restores the bijection between algebraic solutions and geometric points, so the remaining root is forced to be the requested point.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the elimination has produced more roots than the figure can accommodate — a cubic gave three $x$-values but only two intersection points are visible.
  2. Rule out any root that violates the stated domain (e.g. $x \geq 0$ kills $x = -5$).
  3. Rule out roots already consumed by previously named points (e.g. $x = 1$ is point $A$), committing to the unique survivor as the target.
  4. Substitute the surviving $x$-value into one original curve equation to recover $y$, giving the full coordinates.
  5. Verify by substituting into the second curve equation; matching $y$-values confirms the point lies on both curves and the elimination was sound.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. List all roots produced by the eliminated polynomial.
  2. Discard roots violating the explicit domain constraint (e.g. $x \geq 0$).
  3. Discard roots already assigned to known points in the question.
  4. Take the unique remaining root as the $x$-coordinate of the target point.
  5. Substitute into the simpler of the two curve equations to find $y$.
  6. Cross-check by substituting into the other curve equation; values must agree.

Worked Micro-Example

Curves $C_1: y = x^3 - 3x^2 + 9$ and $C_2: y = -x^3 + 7x - 1$ meet where $2x^3 - 3x^2 - 7x + 10 = 0$, giving $x = 1, -5, 2$. With $x \geq 0$ imposed and $A$ at $x = 1$, only $x = 2$ remains. Then $y = 2^3 - 3(2)^2 + 9 = 5$, and check $-2^3 + 7(2) - 1 = 5$. Hence $B = (2, 5)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Trigonometric equation on a restricted interval
Reject any solutions outside $0 \leq \theta < 2\pi$ (or the stated range) after solving the general equation.
Line meets curve in a 'show that' context with one tangent point given
After forming the quadratic/cubic, discard the $x$-value of the known tangency to isolate the second intersection.
Logarithmic or surd equations
Reject roots that make arguments of $\ln$ negative or radicands negative, then keep only roots in the natural domain.
Geometry problems with length or area
Reject negative roots when the unknown represents a length, radius, or area.
Parametric intersection with $t \geq 0$ time-like parameter
Discard negative parameter values not realised on the given branch.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating all algebraic roots as valid intersection points and listing them all as answers.
  • Confusing the domain restriction on $x$ with a restriction on $y$, filtering the wrong coordinate.
  • Assuming the largest or positive root is automatically the answer without checking against the named points.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to apply the stated domain constraint and reporting an extraneous negative root.
  • Failing to subtract out the known point's root, leading to two candidates for $B$.
  • Arithmetic slip when substituting back, with no verification against the second curve.
  • Substituting into a rearranged form of the equation and propagating a sign error.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P1–P4 technique: solve simultaneous polynomial equations, then filter using domain restrictions or given points. No content beyond A-level algebra and coordinate geometry is required.
