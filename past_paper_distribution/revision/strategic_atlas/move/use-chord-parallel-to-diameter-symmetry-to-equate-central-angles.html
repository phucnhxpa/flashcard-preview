Coordinate Geometry

Use chord-parallel-to-diameter symmetry to equate central angles

Family: Symmetry Argument Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a circle/semicircle problem states a chord is parallel to a diameter (or to another chord through the centre), and asks for an angle, arc, or sector quantity. Cues include phrases like 'BD is parallel to AE', 'AE is a diameter', or a diagram showing two parallel chords with O on one of them. Most reliable when one of the two parallel segments passes through the centre, so a perpendicular from O bisects the other.

Motivation — Why this works

A chord parallel to a diameter is symmetric about the perpendicular from the centre to that chord, so the two arcs cut off between the chord's endpoints and the diameter's endpoints are congruent. Equal arcs subtend equal central angles, giving an instant pairing of angles without any trigonometry. This collapses the unknown angle into a simple subtraction from π (or 2π) along the straight diameter.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice a chord is parallel to a diameter and the centre O sits on the diameter — that screams reflective symmetry.
  2. I rule out doing alternate-angle chasing with a transversal or setting up coordinates; both work but are heavier than needed.
  3. I commit to the symmetry route because the perpendicular from O to the parallel chord is an axis of symmetry of the whole figure.
  4. Bridging to operations: identify the two 'outer' central angles either side of the chord and assert they are equal because the symmetry swaps them.
  5. Tactically, I write ∠AOE = π for the diameter, set the two equal outer angles to the given value, and subtract to get the middle angle.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the diameter (e.g. AE) and the parallel chord (e.g. BD); note O lies on the diameter.
  2. State ∠AOE = π because AOE is a straight line.
  3. Invoke the parallel condition: BD ∥ AE implies arc AB = arc ED, hence ∠BOA = ∠DOE.
  4. Substitute the given angle (e.g. ∠BOA = 0.7) for both equal angles.
  5. Compute the required central angle as π minus the sum of the two equal angles, e.g. ∠BOD = π − 2(0.7) = π − 1.4.

Worked Micro-Example

Semicircle on diameter $AE$, centre $O$, with $B,D$ on the arc and $BD \parallel AE$. Given $\angle BOA = 0.7$ rad, find $\angle BOD$. By symmetry about the perpendicular from $O$ to $BD$, $\angle DOE = \angle BOA = 0.7$. Since $\angle AOE = \pi$, $\angle BOD = \pi - 0.7 - 0.7 = \pi - 1.4 \approx 1.742$ rad (4 s.f.).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two parallel chords (neither a diameter) in a full circle
The symmetry axis is still the perpendicular from O to the chords; equal arcs lie between the chords' endpoints on each side, giving paired equal central angles.
Chord parallel to a tangent at a point P
OP is perpendicular to the tangent and hence to the chord, so OP bisects the chord and the arc, again pairing equal arcs/angles either side.
Sector area or arc length question with the same parallel setup
Use the deduced equal central angles in $s = r\theta$ or $A = \tfrac12 r^2\theta$ rather than computing the angle from coordinates.
Isosceles trapezium inscribed in a circle (parallel sides are chords)
Same symmetry gives equal non-parallel sides and equal base angles; central angles over the two legs are equal.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating BD ∥ AE as merely giving alternate angles with a transversal, then doing a longer angle-chase instead of using arc equality.
  • Assuming ∠BOD equals ∠BOA (confusing the 'middle' angle with an 'outer' one).
  • Thinking the symmetry needs B and D to be reflections in the y-axis of a coordinate system rather than in the perpendicular from O to BD.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to justify ∠DOE = ∠BOA explicitly via the parallel condition in a 'show that' question.
  • Mixing radians and degrees — if the given angle is in radians, keep π (not 180°) for the straight line.
  • Subtracting only one copy of the equal angle from π instead of both.
  • Using the parallel condition without checking that one of the lines truly passes through O (the argument can fail for two generic parallel chords if you forget the symmetry is about the perpendicular from O).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 circular measure: uses only 'angles on a straight line', the circle theorem that equal arcs subtend equal central angles, and basic symmetry — no advanced geometry required.
