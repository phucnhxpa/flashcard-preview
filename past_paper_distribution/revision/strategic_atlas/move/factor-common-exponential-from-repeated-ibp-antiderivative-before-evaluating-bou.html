Factor common exponential from repeated-IBP antiderivative before evaluating bounds

Family: Strategic Factorisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires immediately after completing integration by parts (often twice) on an integrand of the form $x^n e^{ax}$, leaving an antiderivative as a sum of terms each containing $e^{ax}$. Recognise the cue: every term shares the same exponential factor (and possibly a sign). Also fires whenever a definite integral is about to be evaluated and the antiderivative is a sum of products sharing a transcendental factor. Phrasings: 'collect terms', 'tidy before substituting bounds', 'write as single exponential times polynomial'.

Motivation — Why this works

Each bound substitution into a sum of $k$ terms requires $k$ multiplications and $k$ exponential evaluations, multiplying arithmetic-error risk. Factoring to $e^{ax}\,P(x)$ reduces this to one exponential and one polynomial evaluation per bound. The factored form also exposes structural features — e.g. that $e^{-10}$ is negligible, or that a bound makes the polynomial vanish — which the expanded form hides. Algebraically it is just the distributive law in reverse, so no information is lost.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that after IBP the antiderivative reads $-x^2 e^{-x} - 2x e^{-x} - 2e^{-x}$, with $e^{-x}$ in every term.
  2. Rules out evaluating term-by-term: that would mean six separate substitutions and six chances to mis-sign.
  3. Commits to factoring because the shared factor is obvious and pulling it out leaves a clean polynomial $x^2 + 2x + 2$.
  4. Bridges to operation: write $-e^{-x}(x^2 + 2x + 2)$ and place inside the bound brackets $[\,\cdot\,]_0^{10}$.
  5. Tactical decision: factor out the sign too (i.e. $-e^{-x}$ rather than $e^{-x}$) so the polynomial has positive coefficients, minimising sign errors at the bounds.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the common transcendental factor (here $-e^{-x}$) shared by every term of the antiderivative.
  2. Factor it out: $-x^2 e^{-x} - 2x e^{-x} - 2e^{-x} = -e^{-x}(x^2 + 2x + 2)$.
  3. Rewrite the definite integral as $\bigl[-e^{-x}(x^2 + 2x + 2)\bigr]_0^{10}$ (carrying any outer constant such as $100\pi$).
  4. Substitute the upper bound: $-e^{-10}(100 + 20 + 2) = -122 e^{-10}$.
  5. Substitute the lower bound: $-e^{0}(0 + 0 + 2) = -2$.
  6. Subtract upper $-$ lower: $-122 e^{-10} - (-2) = 2 - 122 e^{-10}$.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_0^{10} x^2 e^{-x}\,dx$. Two IBPs give the antiderivative $-x^2 e^{-x} - 2x e^{-x} - 2 e^{-x}$. Factor: $-e^{-x}(x^2 + 2x + 2)$. Then $\bigl[-e^{-x}(x^2 + 2x + 2)\bigr]_0^{10} = -122 e^{-10} - (-2) = 2 - 122 e^{-10} \approx 1.9945$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Repeated IBP on $\int x^n e^{ax}\,dx$ for any $n \in \mathbb{N}$
Antiderivative is always $e^{ax} Q_n(x)$ for some degree-$n$ polynomial $Q_n$; keep factored throughout, don't re-expand.
IBP producing $\int x^n \sin(ax)\,dx$ or $\int x^n \cos(ax)\,dx$
Group into $\sin(ax)\,P(x) + \cos(ax)\,R(x)$ — two factored chunks rather than one — before substituting bounds.
Volume of revolution $\pi\int y^2\,dx$ with $y = x e^{-x/2}$ or similar
Carry the outer $\pi$ (or $100\pi$) as a constant multiplier; factor inside the brackets first, multiply by the constant only at the final numerical step.
Reduction-formula outputs in Further Pure
Each $I_n$ in the recursion already appears as $e^{ax}$ times a polynomial; preserve that factored form rather than expanding to combine $I_n$ values.
Antiderivatives of the form $e^{ax}(A\cos bx + B\sin bx)$
Factor $e^{ax}$ once, evaluate the bracketed trig combination at each bound separately.

Common Misreadings

  • Leaving the antiderivative expanded and substituting each of three terms at each bound — six substitutions, high error rate.
  • Treating the factored polynomial as if it must itself be factorised over $\mathbb{R}$ (it need not be — $x^2+2x+2$ has no real roots and that's fine).
  • Thinking the factoring changes the value of the integral; it is purely cosmetic algebra.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when extracting the negative: writing $+e^{-x}(x^2 + 2x + 2)$ instead of $-e^{-x}(x^2 + 2x + 2)$.
  • Evaluating $e^{0}$ as $0$ rather than $1$ at the lower bound.
  • Forgetting the outer constant (e.g. $100\pi$ in a volume problem) when stating the final answer.
  • Mis-subtracting: computing lower $-$ upper instead of upper $-$ lower.
  • Dropping the negligible $e^{-10}$ term too early and losing exact-form marks; keep $2 - 122e^{-10}$ as the exact answer, then approximate.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure (integration by parts in P3/P4) and Further Pure (reduction formulae); the move is just distributive-law factoring applied to a sum of products and requires no tools beyond the specification.
