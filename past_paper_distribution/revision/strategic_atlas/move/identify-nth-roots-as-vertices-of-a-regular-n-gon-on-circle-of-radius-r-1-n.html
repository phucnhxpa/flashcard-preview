Trigger — When to use

Fires when solving z^n = w and the question asks for an Argand diagram, or to prove/describe a geometric property of the roots (e.g. 'show the roots form a square/regular hexagon', 'find the area of the polygon whose vertices are the roots', 'find the perimeter'). Also triggers when the n roots have already been found in modulus–argument form with a common modulus and arguments in arithmetic progression. Phrases like 'show on a single diagram' or 'hence find the exact area' are strong cues.

Motivation — Why this works

All solutions of z^n = w share modulus |w|^{1/n}, so they lie on a circle of that radius centred at the origin. Their arguments form an arithmetic progression with common difference 2π/n, giving equal angular spacing. Equal modulus plus equal angular spacing is exactly the definition of vertices of a regular n-gon inscribed in that circle. This lets distance, perimeter and area questions be answered using circle/polygon geometry instead of further algebra.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice every root I found has the same modulus r^{1/n} — so they all lie on one circle centred at 0.
  2. I rule out treating them as scattered points or pairing only by conjugates; the structure is rotational, not reflective.
  3. I commit to the regular polygon interpretation because the arguments differ by exactly 2π/n, which is the defining symmetry of a regular n-gon.
  4. Bridge: I sketch the circle of radius r^{1/n}, mark one root from its argument, then rotate by 2π/n to place the rest.
  5. Tactically, for area I use A = (1/2) n R² sin(2π/n) with R = r^{1/n}; for a side length I use 2R sin(π/n) rather than the distance formula in cartesians.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Solve z^n = w to get roots z_k = r^{1/n} e^{i(θ + 2πk/n)/?} in mod-arg form (k = 0,1,...,n−1).
  2. State that |z_k| = r^{1/n} for all k, so the roots lie on a circle of radius r^{1/n} centred at O.
  3. State that arg(z_{k+1}) − arg(z_k) = 2π/n, so the roots are equally spaced around that circle.
  4. Conclude: the roots are the vertices of a regular n-gon inscribed in the circle |z| = r^{1/n}.
  5. If a side length is required, use s = 2 r^{1/n} sin(π/n).
  6. If an area is required, use A = (1/2) n (r^{1/n})² sin(2π/n) = (1/2) n r^{2/n} sin(2π/n).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve z^4 = -4 and find the area of the polygon with the roots as vertices. Here w = -4 = 4 e^{iπ}, so z_k = 4^{1/4} e^{i(π + 2πk)/4} = √2 e^{i(π/4 + kπ/2)}, k=0,1,2,3. All four roots have modulus √2 and arguments π/4, 3π/4, 5π/4, 7π/4, equally spaced by π/2, so they form a square inscribed in |z| = √2. Area = (1/2)(4)(√2)² sin(π/2) = 4.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation of the form (z − z₀)^n = w
The regular n-gon is centred at z₀ instead of the origin; circumradius is still |w|^{1/n}.
nth roots of unity, z^n = 1
Polygon is inscribed in the unit circle with one vertex at z = 1; arguments are 2πk/n.
z^n = r (positive real)
One vertex lies on the positive real axis at r^{1/n}; remaining vertices obtained by rotating by 2π/n.
z^n = w with w purely imaginary or complex
The polygon is rotated by (arg w)/n from the standard orientation; structure and circumradius unchanged.
Only some roots requested (e.g. roots in upper half-plane)
Still identify all n vertices first, then select those in the required region by argument.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the roots as merely conjugate pairs and missing the full rotational symmetry.
  • Assuming the polygon is always centred at the origin even when the equation is (z − z₀)^n = w.
  • Reading 'regular polygon' as 'any polygon' and trying to compute area by shoelace on cartesian coordinates instead of using the circumradius formula.
  • Confusing the circumradius r^{1/n} with r itself.

Pitfalls

  • Claiming a regular polygon without explicitly justifying both equal modulus AND equal angular spacing.
  • Using side length 2R sin(2π/n) instead of the correct 2R sin(π/n).
  • Forgetting the factor 1/2 in the area formula A = (1/2) n R² sin(2π/n).
  • Mislabelling vertices on the Argand diagram (wrong starting argument, or going clockwise instead of anticlockwise).
  • If loci (e.g. |z − a| = b or arg(z − a) = α) are overlaid, drawing them inconsistently with the marked roots.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3): de Moivre's theorem, nth roots of a complex number, Argand diagrams, and standard polygon area via (1/2) n R² sin(2π/n). No tools beyond mod-arg form and basic trigonometry are required.
