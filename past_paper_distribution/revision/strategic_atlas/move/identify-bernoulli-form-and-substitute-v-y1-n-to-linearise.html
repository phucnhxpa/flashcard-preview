Differential Equations

Identify Bernoulli form and substitute v = y^(1-n) to linearise

Family: Bernoulli Substitution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The ODE has the shape dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x)y^n with n ≠ 0, 1, so it is not linear in y but becomes linear after a power substitution. Typical surface cues: a y^n term (commonly y^2, y^3, or y^{-1}) multiplying a function of x on one side, with a linear-looking y term on the other. The question often hands you the substitution v = y^{1-n} explicitly, confirming the Bernoulli route. If integrating-factor methods fail because of the nonlinear y^n, this template is the standard rescue.

Motivation — Why this works

Dividing through by y^n gives y^{-n} y' + P(x) y^{1-n} = Q(x), and the combination y^{-n} y' is exactly (1/(1-n)) d/dx(y^{1-n}). So setting v = y^{1-n} converts the nonlinear ODE into dv/dx + (1-n)P(x) v = (1-n)Q(x), a first-order linear ODE solvable by integrating factor. The exponent 1-n is forced by the chain rule: it is the unique power that makes dv/dx absorb the y^{-n} factor.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the ODE has a nonlinear y^n term on the right (here y^3), so it is not linear in y.
  2. Rule out separation (x and y not cleanly separable) and direct integrating factor (requires linearity in y).
  3. Commit to the Bernoulli template once n is read off cleanly from the y^n factor.
  4. Bridge: write down v = y^{1-n} and compute dv/dx via chain rule to plan the substitution.
  5. Tactically, divide the ODE through by y^n first so the substitution slots in without algebraic clutter.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Read off n from the y^n factor (e.g. n = 3 in dy/dx + (6/x)y = (e^x/x)y^3).
  2. Define v = y^{1-n} (here v = y^{-2}).
  3. Differentiate: dv/dx = (1-n) y^{-n} dy/dx, so y^{-n} dy/dx = (1/(1-n)) dv/dx.
  4. Divide the original ODE by y^n and substitute to obtain dv/dx + (1-n)P(x) v = (1-n) Q(x).
  5. Solve the resulting linear ODE in v by integrating factor, then back-substitute y = v^{1/(1-n)}.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{6}{x}y = \frac{e^x}{x}y^3$. Here $n=3$, so set $v = y^{-2}$, giving $\frac{dv}{dx} = -2y^{-3}\frac{dy}{dx}$. Divide the ODE by $y^3$ and multiply by $-2$ to obtain $\frac{dv}{dx} - \frac{12}{x}v = -\frac{2e^x}{x}$, a linear ODE in $v$ ready for the integrating factor $x^{-12}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

ODE of form y' + P(x)y = Q(x)y^2 (n = 2)
Substitute v = y^{-1}; then dv/dx = -y^{-2} y', giving dv/dx - P(x)v = -Q(x).
ODE with y^{-1} on the right (n = -1)
Substitute v = y^2; then dv/dx = 2y y', so multiply through by 2y before substituting.
ODE of form y' + P(x)y = Q(x)\sqrt{y} (n = 1/2)
Substitute v = y^{1/2}; chain rule gives dv/dx = (1/2)y^{-1/2} y'.
Bernoulli with n = 0 or n = 1
Do not use this move — n = 0 is already linear, n = 1 is separable/linear after collecting y terms.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the ODE as linear and applying an integrating factor directly, ignoring the y^n nonlinearity.
  • Using v = y^n instead of v = y^{1-n}, which fails to cancel the y^{-n} factor.
  • Misreading n: e.g. taking n = 2 when the right-hand side is actually y^3 because of an exponent typo.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when differentiating v = y^{-2}: forgetting the negative in dv/dx = -2y^{-3} dy/dx.
  • Omitting the chain-rule factor dy/dx when differentiating v with respect to x.
  • Forgetting to multiply through by (1-n) after dividing by y^n, leaving incorrect coefficients in the linear ODE.
  • Failing to back-substitute to recover y from v at the end.

A-level Scope Notes

Bernoulli reduction with a given substitution v = y^{1-n} is in scope for Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) first-order ODE topics; the question typically supplies the substitution explicitly.
