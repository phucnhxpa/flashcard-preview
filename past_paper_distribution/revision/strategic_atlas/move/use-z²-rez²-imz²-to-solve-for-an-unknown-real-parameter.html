Complex Numbers

Use |z|² = Re(z)² + Im(z)² to solve for an unknown real parameter

Family: Modulus Equation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a complex number z is given in Cartesian form with one real or imaginary component containing an unknown real parameter, and |z| (or |z|²) is specified. Typical phrasings: 'Given |z| = r and z = a + bi where b (or a) involves λ, find λ', or 'z = (p+λ) + qi with |z| known'. The signal is one numerical equation (the modulus) and one unknown inside Re(z) or Im(z).

Motivation — Why this works

Because |z|² = Re(z)² + Im(z)² is a polynomial identity in the real components, squaring the modulus equation converts a square-root condition into a clean quadratic in the unknown. This avoids manipulating √ expressions and produces λ² directly. Any sign ambiguity from λ² = k is then resolved by the stated domain constraint (positive real, integer, etc.).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that |z| is given numerically and z is in Cartesian form with exactly one unknown sitting inside its real or imaginary part.
  2. Rule out polar/argument methods — argument is not given, and squaring the modulus is the most direct route.
  3. Commit to |z|² = Re(z)² + Im(z)² because it produces one equation in one unknown with no square roots.
  4. Square both components carefully (in particular (−λ)² = λ²) and equate to |z|², giving a quadratic in λ.
  5. Solve λ² = k, then apply the stated sign/domain constraint to pick the correct root, and substitute back to fix z for the rest of the problem.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify Re(z) and Im(z) from the given Cartesian form.
  2. Write |z|² = Re(z)² + Im(z)² and equate to the given value squared.
  3. Simplify to obtain a quadratic in the unknown parameter.
  4. Solve for the unknown, taking ± from the square root.
  5. Apply the stated constraint (e.g. λ > 0) to select the valid root.
  6. Substitute back to obtain the explicit value of z.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $|z| = 5$ and $z = -\lambda + 3i$ with $\lambda > 0$. Then $|z|^2 = (-\lambda)^2 + 3^2 = \lambda^2 + 9 = 25$, so $\lambda^2 = 16$ and $\lambda = \pm 4$. Since $\lambda$ is a positive real constant, $\lambda = 4$, giving $z = -4 + 3i$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Unknown sits in the imaginary part: z = a + (b+λ)i with |z| given
Equation becomes a² + (b+λ)² = |z|², a quadratic in λ rather than λ²; both roots may be admissible unless constrained.
|z − z₀| = r given with z containing an unknown
Apply modulus-squared to z − z₀: (Re(z)−Re(z₀))² + (Im(z)−Im(z₀))² = r², yielding a circle-type equation in the unknown.
|z|² appears via z·z̄ in a Further Maths context
Use z z̄ = |z|² instead of expanding components — same identity, different presentation.
Two unknowns with two modulus conditions
Each |z| equation contributes one quadratic relation; solve simultaneously.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating |z| = 5 as Re(z) + Im(z) = 5 instead of squaring components.
  • Reading −λ as a negative quantity and assuming λ < 0 must follow.
  • Confusing |z| with |z|² and equating λ² + 9 to 5 instead of 25.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error: writing (−λ)² as −λ² instead of λ².
  • Forgetting to apply the 'λ positive' constraint and leaving λ = ±4 as final answer.
  • Failing to substitute λ back into z, so later parts of the question are attempted with λ still symbolic.
  • Squaring only one side or mishandling the constant term when |z| is irrational.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Core/Further Pure complex numbers: uses only Cartesian form, modulus definition |z|² = Re(z)² + Im(z)², and basic quadratic solving.
