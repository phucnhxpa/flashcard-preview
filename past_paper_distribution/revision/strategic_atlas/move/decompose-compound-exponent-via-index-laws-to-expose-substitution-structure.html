Algebra & Functions

Decompose compound exponent via index laws to expose substitution structure

Family: Index Law Decomposition Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an exponential equation contains a term with a compound exponent — that is, an exponent built from a constant added to (or subtracted from) a multiple of the variable, such as 2^{2x+2}, 3^{1-x}, 5^{2x+1}, or 2^{3+2x}. Other terms in the equation are typically simple powers like a^x or constants, hinting that the equation is secretly a polynomial (often quadratic) in p = a^x. The visual cue is a 'mixed' exponent of the form mx ± c that blocks the direct substitution p = a^x. Equivalently: you spot two or more powers of the same base a but with exponents that don't all match the pattern needed for a clean substitution.

Motivation — Why this works

The two index laws a^{m+n} = a^m · a^n and (a^m)^n = a^{mn} let you peel a compound exponent into a constant factor times a clean power of a^x. The constant part of the exponent becomes a numerical coefficient (e.g. 2^2 = 4, 3^1 = 3), and the variable part becomes either p, p^2, or 1/p after substituting p = a^x. This converts an apparently transcendental equation into a polynomial (usually quadratic) in p, which can then be solved by factorisation or the quadratic formula. Without this decomposition, no single substitution simplifies the whole equation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice an exponential term whose exponent is not just x or 2x but a compound expression such as 2x+2 or 1−x; this blocks a direct substitution p = a^x.
  2. I rule out logging both sides (the equation has multiple exponential terms, so logs won't simplify it) and rule out trial-and-error roots (I want an exact algebraic method).
  3. I commit to the substitution route: if every term can be written as a constant times p^k where p = a^x, the equation becomes a polynomial in p — most likely a quadratic.
  4. To bridge to that, I split the compound exponent using a^{m+n} = a^m · a^n, peeling the constant part out as a numerical factor.
  5. If a doubled variable exponent like 2x remains, I apply (a^m)^n = a^{mn} to write a^{2x} = (a^x)^2 = p^2; if a negative variable exponent like −x remains, I write a^{-x} = 1/p.
  6. Finally I check that every term of the original equation is now expressed as a constant, p, or p^2 — confirming a clean quadratic in p has emerged.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify each term with a compound exponent of the form mx + c (or c − x, etc.).
  2. Apply a^{m+n} = a^m · a^n to split: e.g. 2^{2x+2} = 2^{2x} · 2^2, or 3^{1−x} = 3^1 · 3^{−x}.
  3. Evaluate the constant power numerically: 2^2 = 4, 3^1 = 3, etc.
  4. Apply (a^m)^n = a^{mn} to convert any a^{kx} into (a^x)^k = p^k; convert any a^{−x} into 1/p.
  5. Substitute p = a^x throughout to obtain a polynomial equation (typically quadratic) in p.
  6. Solve the polynomial in p, then back-substitute and take logs to recover x.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $4 \cdot 2^{2x+2} - 33 \cdot 2^x + 8 = 0$. Split: $2^{2x+2} = 2^{2x} \cdot 2^2 = 4(2^x)^2$. Let $p = 2^x$, giving $16p^2 - 33p + 8 = 0$. Factorise: $(16p - 1)(p - 2) = 0$, so $p = 1/16$ or $p = 2$, hence $x = -4$ or $x = 1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Exponent has the form 2x + c (constant added to doubled variable), e.g. 3^{2x+1}
Split as 3^{2x+1} = 3 · (3^x)^2 = 3p^2 — same two index laws applied in sequence.
Exponent has the form c − x (negative variable part), e.g. 3^{1−x}
Split as 3^{1−x} = 3 · 3^{−x} = 3/p — the negative exponent becomes a reciprocal in p.
Different base that hides the same base, e.g. 5·4^x − 3·2^x + 1 = 0
Rewrite 4^x = (2^2)^x = (2^x)^2 = p^2 — power-of-a-power applied to unify bases before substituting.
Mixed compound exponent, e.g. 2^{3+2x}
Split as 2^3 · 2^{2x} = 8(2^x)^2 = 8p^2 — same two-step decomposition.

Common Misreadings

  • Misreading 2^{2x+2} as (2^{2x}) + 2 or as 2^{2(x+2)}, leading to wrong decompositions.
  • Treating 3^{1−x} as 3 − 3^x by confusing the internal subtraction in the exponent with subtraction of powers.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the constant coefficient: writing 2^{2x+2} as (2^x)^2 + 4 instead of 4·(2^x)^2.
  • Misapplying (a^m)^n: writing 2^{2x} = 2·(2^x) instead of (2^x)^2.
  • Sign errors with negative exponents: writing 3^{1−x} = 3·3^x instead of 3·3^{−x} = 3/p.
  • Forgetting to back-substitute and solve for x via logarithms after finding p.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 exponentials and logarithms. Uses only the standard index laws a^{m+n} = a^m · a^n and (a^m)^n = a^{mn} (not in the formula booklet — assumed knowledge from P1). Avoid generalising to non-integer or complex exponents beyond the syllabus.
