Binomial Expansion

Factor out leading constant to reach (1+X)^n standard form

Family: Binomial Standardisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a binomial of the form (a+bx)^n must be expanded with n fractional or negative, and a ≠ 1. Cues include expressions like (4−5x)^{−1/2}, (9+2x)^{1/2}, (8−x)^{−1/3}, or any bracket whose first term is not 1. Also fires when an expansion is requested 'in ascending powers of x' for such a bracket. The generalised binomial series is only stated for (1+X)^n, so the leading constant must be extracted before applying the formula.

Motivation — Why this works

The generalised binomial theorem (1+X)^n = 1 + nX + n(n−1)/2! X^2 + … is valid only when the bracket begins with 1 and |X|<1. Factoring (a+bx) = a(1 + bx/a) and distributing the exponent via (a·u)^n = a^n · u^n converts the problem into a^n times a standard expansion. The constant a^n becomes a clean scalar multiplier, while the bracket (1+bx/a)^n is in canonical form with X = bx/a, ready for term-by-term expansion.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the bracket does not begin with 1, so the series formula in the formula booklet cannot be applied as-is.
  2. Rules out direct Pascal-style expansion (only valid for positive integer n) and rules out a u-substitution (which fails to deliver ascending powers of x).
  3. Commits to factoring the constant out of the bracket because this is the unique standardisation that preserves the variable x while normalising the leading term to 1.
  4. Bridges to operation: rewrite (a+bx)^n as [a(1 + bx/a)]^n, then split as a^n · (1 + bx/a)^n.
  5. Tactical decisions: evaluate a^n explicitly (e.g. 4^{−1/2} = 1/2), identify X = bx/a (preserving its sign), and note the validity condition |bx/a| < 1 for later.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the leading constant a inside the bracket (a+bx).
  2. Rewrite (a+bx) = a(1 + bx/a).
  3. Apply (a·u)^n = a^n · u^n to obtain a^n · (1 + bx/a)^n.
  4. Evaluate a^n as a numerical scalar.
  5. Set X = bx/a and expand (1+X)^n using the generalised binomial series.
  6. Multiply every term of the expansion by the scalar a^n.
  7. State the validity range |bx/a| < 1, i.e. |x| < |a/b|.

Worked Micro-Example

Expand $(4-5x)^{-1/2}$ in ascending powers of $x$.\\ $(4-5x)^{-1/2} = [4(1-\tfrac{5x}{4})]^{-1/2} = 4^{-1/2}(1-\tfrac{5x}{4})^{-1/2} = \tfrac{1}{2}(1-\tfrac{5x}{4})^{-1/2}$.\\ With $X=-\tfrac{5x}{4}$, $n=-\tfrac12$: $(1+X)^n \approx 1 + \tfrac{5x}{8} + \tfrac{75x^2}{128}+\dots$\\ Hence $(4-5x)^{-1/2} \approx \tfrac12 + \tfrac{5x}{16} + \tfrac{75x^2}{256} + \dots$, valid for $|x|<\tfrac{4}{5}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

(9+2x)^{1/2} with a perfect-square constant
Factor as 9^{1/2}(1+2x/9)^{1/2} = 3(1+2x/9)^{1/2}; the scalar is a clean integer.
(8−x)^{−1/3} with a perfect-cube constant
Factor as 8^{−1/3}(1−x/8)^{−1/3} = (1/2)(1−x/8)^{−1/3}.
(2+3x)^{−2} with negative integer n
Same procedure: 2^{−2}(1+3x/2)^{−2} = (1/4)(1+3x/2)^{−2}.
Expression of form (a+bx)^n /(c+dx)^m as a product
Standardise each bracket separately, then multiply the two expansions and collect terms up to required order.
Validity range required
After factoring, the condition becomes |bx/a|<1, giving |x|<|a/b| — different from the naïve |bx|<1.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 4 as the 'first term a' in an (a+b)^n Pascal expansion — valid only for positive integer n, not for fractional/negative exponents.
  • Believing the formula (1+X)^n applies with X = 4−5x − 1 = 3−5x; the factorisation must produce a literal 1, not just shift constants.
  • Reading 4^{−1/2} as −2 or as 4·(−1/2); confusing negative-fractional indices with multiplication.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to raise the extracted constant to the power n — writing 4·(1−5x/4)^{−1/2} instead of 4^{−1/2}·(1−5x/4)^{−1/2}.
  • Sign error in X: with (a−bx) the value is X = −bx/a, and the minus sign must propagate into every X^k term.
  • Dropping the scalar a^n when stating the final series — every coefficient must be multiplied by it.
  • Stating validity as |bx|<1 instead of |bx/a|<1, giving the wrong x-range.
  • Arithmetic slips when simplifying a^n for fractional n (e.g. 9^{1/2}=3, 8^{−1/3}=1/2).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P3/P4 technique for generalised binomial expansions with fractional or negative index; the factor-out step and the resulting validity condition |x|<|a/b| are explicitly examinable.
