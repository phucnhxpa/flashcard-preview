Algebra & Functions

Analyse end-behaviour by identifying asymptotes and dominant terms

Family: Asymptotic Analysis Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a function involves division by a variable expression (rational form $p(x)/q(x)$, reciprocal-power $k/x^n$, or exponential ratio $A e^{kt}/(B + Ce^{kt})$) AND the question asks about long-term behaviour, sketching, horizontal/vertical asymptotes, maximum/limiting values, or 'as $t \to \infty$'. Visual cues: a variable in a denominator, an exponential appearing in both numerator and denominator, or wording like 'the value approaches', 'the maximum possible', 'long-term', 'sketch the graph'. Equivalent phrasings: 'find the limit as $x \to \infty$', 'state the equation of the horizontal asymptote', 'what value does $A$ approach'.

Motivation — Why this works

Asymptotic behaviour is determined entirely by which term dominates and which term vanishes. For reciprocal curves $y = k/x^n$, setting the denominator to zero locates the vertical asymptote, and letting $|x| \to \infty$ kills the reciprocal, yielding $y = 0$ as a horizontal asymptote — combined with sign and symmetry, this fully fixes the sketch. For ratios involving a growing exponential or polynomial, dividing numerator and denominator by the dominant term converts an indeterminate $\infty/\infty$ into a transparent limit, where decaying terms $e^{-kt}$ or $1/x^n$ visibly tend to zero. The underlying principle is the same: isolate the dominant term so that subordinate terms reveal themselves as negligible.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structural feature: a variable in a denominator, or the same exponential/polynomial appearing in both numerator and denominator — flagging an asymptote or limit question.
  2. Check what 'fails' in the expression: where does the denominator vanish (vertical asymptote), and what happens as $|x| \to \infty$ or $t \to \infty$ (horizontal asymptote / limiting value)?
  3. Rule out heavier machinery: L'Hôpital is unnecessary at A-level, and informal 'the constant becomes negligible' is not rigorous enough for an examiner — commit instead to the algebraic 'divide by the dominant term' manipulation, or to the geometric 'asymptote + sign + symmetry' framework.
  4. Bridge to action: for a sketch, list (i) vertical asymptote from denominator $=0$, (ii) horizontal asymptote from the limit, (iii) sign of $y$, (iv) symmetry $f(-x)$ vs $f(x)$. For a limit, divide numerator and denominator by the dominant term so every remaining variable piece decays to $0$.
  5. Tactical decision: choose the dominant term carefully — for $k/x^2$ it is $x^2$ in the denominator; for $80e^{0.15t}/(p + e^{0.15t})$ it is $e^{0.15t}$. Dividing by this term makes the surviving constants the answer.
  6. State the vanishing terms explicitly (e.g. 'as $t \to \infty$, $e^{-0.15t} \to 0$') so the limit is justified, not asserted.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Locate vertical asymptotes: set the denominator equal to $0$ and solve.
  2. For a horizontal asymptote / limiting value, identify the dominant term in the expression as the variable tends to $\pm\infty$.
  3. Divide every term in numerator and denominator by this dominant term so that subordinate terms become reciprocals (e.g. $1/x^n$ or $e^{-kt}$).
  4. Apply the limit: each subordinate term $\to 0$, leaving a ratio of constants — this is the horizontal asymptote / limiting value.
  5. For sketching, additionally check sign ($y > 0$ or $y < 0$ on each interval) and symmetry ($f(-x)=f(x)$ even, $f(-x)=-f(x)$ odd), then draw branches respecting both asymptotes.
  6. State the asymptote equations and limit value explicitly in the conclusion.

Worked Micro-Example

Find $\lim_{t\to\infty} \dfrac{80 e^{0.15t}}{p + e^{0.15t}}$. Divide top and bottom by $e^{0.15t}$: $\dfrac{80}{p e^{-0.15t} + 1}$. As $t\to\infty$, $e^{-0.15t}\to 0$, so the limit is $\dfrac{80}{0+1} = 80$. Hence $A=80$ is the horizontal asymptote (limiting surface area).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Reciprocal power curve $y = k/x^n$ with $k>0$
Vertical asymptote $x=0$, horizontal asymptote $y=0$; even $n$ gives y-axis symmetry (Q1 & Q2), odd $n$ gives origin symmetry (Q1 & Q3). Negative $k$ flips below the x-axis.
Rational function $p(x)/q(x)$ as $x\to\infty$
Divide numerator and denominator by the highest power of $x$ appearing — same dominant-term logic. Limit equals ratio of leading coefficients (or $0$ / $\infty$ if degrees differ).
Logistic-type model $L/(1 + Ce^{-kt})$
No algebra needed: $e^{-kt}\to 0$ directly, so limit is $L$. This is the same dominant-term reasoning already pre-arranged.
Decaying exponential ratio $Ae^{-kt}/(B+e^{-kt})$
Here $e^{-kt}$ is the vanishing term, so the limit is $0/B = 0$ — no division step required.

Common Misreadings

  • Drawing a reciprocal curve crossing one of its asymptotes — the curve approaches but never reaches them.
  • Claiming an exponential ratio diverges to $\infty$ because the numerator grows, missing that the denominator grows at the same rate.
  • Reaching for L'Hôpital's rule when a single division by the dominant term would settle the limit cleanly.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to divide every term in the denominator by the dominant term, leaving a stray exponential or power.
  • Stating the limit without explicitly noting that the decaying term tends to $0$ — examiners want this justification visible.
  • Forgetting that $(-x)^2 = x^2$, then drawing asymmetric branches of $y = k/x^2$.
  • Confusing even and odd reciprocal powers, placing branches in the wrong quadrants.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL P2/P3/P4 work on graphs of $y=k/x^n$, exponential models, and rational-function asymptotes. The 'divide by dominant term' technique is standard A-level practice; L'Hôpital's rule is NOT in scope and should be avoided. No formula booklet entry is invoked.
