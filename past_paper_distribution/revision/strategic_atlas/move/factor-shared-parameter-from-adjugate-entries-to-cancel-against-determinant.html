Matrices

Factor shared parameter from adjugate entries to cancel against determinant

Family: Common Factor Cancellation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when computing A⁻¹ = (1/det A)·adj(A) for a parametric matrix, and several adjugate entries visibly share a linear factor (e.g. (k−2), (k+1)) with det(A). Cues: det(A) factors as a constant times a linear expression in the parameter, and cofactor entries like 4−2k, 2k−4, 2k−2 appear. Equivalent phrasings: 'simplify the parametric inverse', 'cancel common factor before dividing'. Particularly valuable when the question asks to express A⁻¹ in simplest form or to evaluate at a specific k.

Motivation — Why this works

The adjugate and determinant are polynomial in the matrix entries, so any linear factor of det(A) coming from a row/column structure of A often appears in cofactors too. Cancelling it produces entries that are either constants or simple rational functions of the parameter, which is the expected 'clean' form of A⁻¹. Failing to cancel leaves the answer as an algebraically ugly matrix of fractions and obscures structural features (such as a fully parameter-free row). The cancellation also acts as a self-check: if a row entirely loses the parameter, this corroborates correct cofactor signs.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that det(A) has a linear factor in k (e.g. −2(k−2)) and that several adj(A) entries contain the same factor (e.g. 4−2k, 2k−4).
  2. Rule out leaving the answer as a single fraction (1/det A)·adj(A) — the question wants simplest form, and unfactored entries hide cancellations.
  3. Commit to factoring every adjugate entry over det(A) explicitly, because cofactor-by-cofactor simplification is reliable and exposes which entries become parameter-free.
  4. Begin by rewriting each adj(A) entry to display the (k−2) factor, with care over signs (e.g. 4−2k = −2(k−2)).
  5. Divide entry-by-entry: entries containing (k−2) reduce to constants; entries without it remain as fractions of the form (something)/(−2(k−2)). Record the implicit assumption k ≠ 2 from non-singularity.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute det(A) and factor it as c·(k−a) for constant c and root a.
  2. Compute adj(A); rewrite every entry to expose any factor of (k−a), tracking signs carefully.
  3. Divide each entry of adj(A) by det(A); cancel (k−a) wherever it appears.
  4. Simplify constants and present A⁻¹ as a matrix whose entries are either numbers or simple rational functions of k.
  5. State the validity condition k ≠ a inherited from det(A) ≠ 0.

Worked Micro-Example

Let \(\det A = -2(k-2)\) and the second row of \(\operatorname{adj}(A)\) be \((4-2k,\ 2k-4,\ 0) = (-2(k-2),\ 2(k-2),\ 0)\). Dividing by \(-2(k-2)\) gives \((1,\ -1,\ 0)\), free of \(k\). Other rows, e.g. entry \(2k-2 = 2(k-1)\), become \(\dfrac{2(k-1)}{-2(k-2)} = -\dfrac{k-1}{k-2}\), valid for \(k\neq 2\).

Variants — Same move, different surface

2×2 parametric inverse where det = (k−a)(k−b) and one off-diagonal entry equals (k−a)
Cancel the shared (k−a) so that entry simplifies to 1/(k−b); state k ≠ a, b.
3×3 inverse with det = c·(k+1) and several cofactors divisible by (k+1)
Same procedure with factor (k+1); the simplified row/column highlights structural symmetry of A.
Solving A x = b parametrically via x = A⁻¹ b
Factor (k−a) inside adj(A)·b before dividing by det(A); often x simplifies to a vector with no parameter in some components.
Showing A⁻¹ tends to a finite limit as k → some value ≠ a
After cancellation, substitute k directly into the simplified entries rather than into the unsimplified fraction.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating det(A) as a single number to divide at the end, instead of factoring it to expose cancellations.
  • Believing that because adj(A) entries look messy, the inverse must remain a messy fraction — missing the structural cancellation.
  • Confusing 'cancel (k−2)' with 'set k = 2', which would make A singular.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when factoring linear expressions, e.g. writing 4−2k = 2(k−2) instead of −2(k−2).
  • Cancelling (k−2) without explicitly recording k ≠ 2 from the non-singularity condition.
  • Forgetting cofactor sign pattern, leading to an adj(A) that does not factor cleanly and masking the cancellation.
  • Dividing only some entries by det(A) and leaving others in fractional form inconsistently.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope: 3×3 matrix inverses via adjugate/determinant, with parameter-dependent entries and the standard non-singularity condition det(A) ≠ 0.
