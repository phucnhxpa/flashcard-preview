Strategic Atlas / Proof / Recurrence Substitution
Proof

Drive the inductive step through the recurrence, not the closed form

Family: Recurrence Substitution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in induction proofs where a sequence is defined by a recurrence (e.g. u_{k+2} = 5u_{k+1} − 6u_k) and a closed form is to be verified. The cue is that P(k+2) (or P(k+1)) involves a term whose only definition comes from the recurrence. Equivalent phrasings: 'use the defining relation to introduce u_{k+2}', 'substitute the hypothesis into the recurrence'. Applies for both ordinary induction (one previous term) and strong induction (two or more previous terms).

Motivation — Why this works

The recurrence is the only equation in the problem that links the new term u_{k+2} to terms already covered by the hypothesis. Working backwards from the closed form at k+2 is unmotivated and circular, since the closed form is what we are trying to verify. Substituting the assumed closed forms into the RHS of the recurrence produces a purely exponential (or polynomial) expression that can be reshaped into the target. This is essentially the only forward route, and it succeeds because linear recurrences with constant coefficients are designed so that the conjectured closed form is closed under the recurrence operation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that the goal P(k+2) involves u_{k+2}, a quantity the hypothesis says nothing about directly.
  2. Rules out manipulating 3·2^{k+2} − 2·3^{k+2} backwards into 5u_{k+1} − 6u_k, because that assumes the conclusion.
  3. Commits to the recurrence as the bridge: it is the unique equation that expresses u_{k+2} via u_{k+1} and u_k, both covered by the strong hypothesis.
  4. Bridge to first operation: write u_{k+2} = 5u_{k+1} − 6u_k and prepare to substitute the assumed closed forms.
  5. Tactical choice: keep exponents as 2^{k+1}, 2^k (rather than expanding to 2·2^k) until after collecting like terms, to make the factor-out step cleanest.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. State the inductive goal: u_{k+2} = 3·2^{k+2} − 2·3^{k+2}.
  2. Invoke the recurrence: u_{k+2} = 5u_{k+1} − 6u_k.
  3. Substitute the inductive hypotheses u_{k+1} = 3·2^{k+1} − 2·3^{k+1} and u_k = 3·2^k − 2·3^k.
  4. Expand: 5(3·2^{k+1} − 2·3^{k+1}) − 6(3·2^k − 2·3^k).
  5. Group 2-powers and 3-powers separately and factor: 2^k(15·2 − 18) and 3^k(−10·3 + 18) — or analogous regrouping.
  6. Simplify each coefficient and re-express as 3·2^{k+2} − 2·3^{k+2}, matching the target.

Worked Micro-Example

Assume u_k = 3·2^k − 2·3^k and u_{k+1} = 3·2^{k+1} − 2·3^{k+1}. Then u_{k+2} = 5u_{k+1} − 6u_k = 5(3·2^{k+1} − 2·3^{k+1}) − 6(3·2^k − 2·3^k) = 30·2^k − 30·3^k − 18·2^k + 18·3^k = 12·2^k − 12·3^k = 3·2^{k+2} − 2·3^{k+2}, as required.

Variants — Same move, different surface

First-order recurrence u_{k+1} = a·u_k + b with conjectured closed form
Use ordinary induction; substitute u_k once into the recurrence, then simplify to match P(k+1).
Recurrence involving a non-homogeneous term, e.g. u_{k+1} = 2u_k + 3^k
After substituting the hypothesis, combine the inhomogeneous term with the exponential parts before matching the target.
Matrix/power induction M^{k+1} = M·M^k in Further Pure
The recurrence is M^{k+1} = M · M^k; substitute the hypothesis for M^k and perform a single matrix multiplication.
Divisibility induction where u_{k+1} is defined recursively
Use the recurrence to express u_{k+1}, then isolate a multiple of the divisor using the hypothesis.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the closed form as the starting point and trying to derive the recurrence from it.
  • Believing only u_k (not u_{k+1}) is needed, missing that strong induction supplies both.
  • Reading the goal as 'verify P(k+2) numerically' rather than 'derive it algebraically from the hypothesis'.

Pitfalls

  • Substituting into only one of u_{k+1} or u_k and leaving the other symbolic.
  • Sign errors when distributing −6 across the bracket (especially on the −2·3^k term).
  • Failing to convert 30·2^k into 3·2^{k+2} (i.e. not recognising 30 = 3·4·... vs 12 = 3·2^2) at the final matching stage.
  • Forgetting to state the base cases needed for strong induction (both u_1 and u_2).

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL Further Pure proof-by-induction for sequences and recurrences; uses only index laws and routine algebra, no characteristic-equation theory.
