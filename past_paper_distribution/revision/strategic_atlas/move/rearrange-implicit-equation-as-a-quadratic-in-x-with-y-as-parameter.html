Rearrange implicit equation as a quadratic in x with y as parameter

Family: Variable Role Assignment Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an implicit curve equation contains x² and x terms (and possibly y-terms) and the question asks about horizontal tangents, range of x for given y, or extreme values where y is treated as fixed. Cues include phrases like 'find the greatest/least value of y', 'show the curve has no points with y > k', or 'find horizontal tangents'. The presence of x at degree at most 2 alongside y-terms signals a quadratic-in-x reframe is available.

Motivation — Why this works

The discriminant condition B² − 4AC ≥ 0 only applies to a genuine quadratic in a single unknown. By treating y as a parameter and collecting in powers of x, the equation becomes Ax² + Bx + C(y) = 0, where A, B, C(y) are constants/functions of y. Real x then exists iff B² − 4AC(y) ≥ 0, which converts a 2-variable geometric condition into a 1-variable inequality in y. This is the engine behind range-of-y and horizontal-tangent arguments without calculus.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation is implicit and that x appears to degree at most 2, while y appears in a more complex (possibly higher-degree) way.
  2. Rule out rearranging as a quadratic in y: that would suit vertical tangents or range-of-x questions, not the horizontal/range-of-y question being asked.
  3. Commit to x as the unknown because the question fixes y (either at a tangent height or as a parameter whose feasible range is sought).
  4. Bridge to operation: rewrite the equation by grouping x²-terms, x-terms, and y-only terms, treating y as a symbolic constant.
  5. Tactically watch for cross-terms like xy or x·f(y), which belong inside coefficient B, making B itself a function of y.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the highest power of x in the equation; verify it is at most 2.
  2. Collect all x²-terms; their net coefficient is A (often a constant, possibly a function of y if x²y appears).
  3. Collect all x-terms (including xy, x·y², etc.); their net coefficient is B(y).
  4. Collect every remaining term — those with no x — as C(y).
  5. Write the equation in standard form A x² + B(y) x + C(y) = 0, ready for the discriminant step.

Worked Micro-Example

Given x² + xy + y² + 15x − 3y = 0, treat y as a parameter. Group: A = 1, B(y) = y + 15, C(y) = y² − 3y. Standard form: x² + (y+15)x + (y² − 3y) = 0. The discriminant in x is (y+15)² − 4(y² − 3y), and requiring this ≥ 0 yields the range of y for which real x (and hence points on the curve) exist.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Vertical tangents or range of x sought
Reverse roles: rearrange as a quadratic in y with x as parameter, then apply discriminant in y.
Parametric curve given as x = f(t), y = g(t)
Eliminate t first to obtain an implicit relation, then rearrange as quadratic in the appropriate variable.
Equation containing x² but also x³ or higher
Quadratic-in-x reframe fails; consider differentiation or substitution instead.
Line meets curve, find condition for tangency
Substitute the line into the curve to obtain a quadratic in x, then set discriminant = 0.

Common Misreadings

  • Choosing the wrong variable as parameter: rearranging in y when horizontal tangents (fixed y) are sought, which solves the dual problem.
  • Treating B as a constant when an xy term forces B to depend on y.
  • Forgetting that A may itself depend on y if a term like x²y is present, which changes the discriminant structure.

Pitfalls

  • Mis-sorting cross terms: dropping the y inside (y+15)x and writing B = 15.
  • Sign errors when moving y-terms to the C(y) bucket.
  • Assuming A ≠ 0 without checking; if A depends on y, the case A = 0 reduces the equation to linear in x and must be handled separately.
  • Writing C as a number rather than a function of y, then producing a meaningless discriminant.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard technique within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (used in line-meets-curve discriminant problems) and extended in Further Pure for implicit curves and range-of-values arguments; uses only quadratic theory and the discriminant.
