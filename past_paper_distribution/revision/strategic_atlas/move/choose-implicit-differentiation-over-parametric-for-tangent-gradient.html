Differentiation

Choose implicit differentiation over parametric for tangent gradient

Family: Differentiation Pathway Choice Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A tangent or gradient is required at a point on a curve given by a Cartesian implicit equation (e.g. $4x^2 - y^2 = 4$), but the point itself is supplied in parametric form (e.g. $P(\sec\theta, 2\tan\theta)$). Both implicit differentiation on the Cartesian equation and parametric differentiation via $(dy/d\theta)/(dx/d\theta)$ are available. The cue is that the implicit equation differentiates to a short rational expression in $x$ and $y$, into which the parametric coordinates substitute cleanly.

Motivation — Why this works

Implicit differentiation of a low-degree polynomial relation produces $dy/dx$ as a single ratio of $x$ and $y$ in one line. Substituting the parametric coordinates then reduces to a trig simplification, avoiding the extra work of computing two separate $\theta$-derivatives and forming their quotient. Because the parametric point automatically satisfies the Cartesian equation, the two routes must agree, so picking the shorter one is purely a pathway-choice optimisation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the curve is presented as a Cartesian implicit equation while the point of interest is parametric — two valid differentiation routes exist.
  2. Rule out parametric differentiation here because it needs $dx/d\theta$, $dy/d\theta$ and a ratio simplification, which is longer than one implicit step.
  3. Commit to implicit differentiation: the equation is polynomial in $x,y$, so $dy/dx$ emerges as a clean rational expression.
  4. Plan the bridge: differentiate, isolate $dy/dx$, then substitute $x=\sec\theta$, $y=2\tan\theta$.
  5. Tactically, simplify using $\sec\theta/\tan\theta = 1/\sin\theta$ to land on a tidy closed form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Differentiate $4x^2 - y^2 = 4$ implicitly: $8x - 2y\,\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 0$.
  2. Rearrange to $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{4x}{y}$.
  3. Substitute the parametric point $x=\sec\theta$, $y=2\tan\theta$: $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{4\sec\theta}{2\tan\theta} = \dfrac{2\sec\theta}{\tan\theta}$.
  4. Simplify using $\dfrac{\sec\theta}{\tan\theta} = \dfrac{1}{\sin\theta}$ to obtain $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{2}{\sin\theta}$.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the gradient of $4x^2 - y^2 = 4$ at $P(\sec\theta, 2\tan\theta)$. Implicit: $8x - 2y\,y' = 0 \Rightarrow y' = \dfrac{4x}{y}$. At $P$: $y' = \dfrac{4\sec\theta}{2\tan\theta} = \dfrac{2}{\sin\theta}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Ellipse $\dfrac{x^2}{a^2} + \dfrac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$ with point $(a\cos\theta, b\sin\theta)$.
Implicit gives $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = -\dfrac{b^2 x}{a^2 y}$; substitute to get $-\dfrac{b\cos\theta}{a\sin\theta}$.
Hyperbola $\dfrac{x^2}{a^2} - \dfrac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$ with point $(a\sec\theta, b\tan\theta)$.
Implicit yields $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{b^2 x}{a^2 y} = \dfrac{b\sec\theta}{a\tan\theta} = \dfrac{b}{a\sin\theta}$.
Circle $x^2+y^2 = r^2$ with point $(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta)$.
Implicit gives $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = -\dfrac{x}{y} = -\cot\theta$ in one step.
Mixed-term curve such as $xy = c^2$ with point $(ct, c/t)$.
Implicit (product rule) gives $y + x\,y' = 0 \Rightarrow y' = -y/x = -1/t^2$.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming a parametric point forces parametric differentiation, ignoring that the underlying Cartesian relation is also available.
  • Thinking the two methods could give different answers and so both must be computed as a check.
  • Treating $4x^2 - y^2 = 4$ as if $y$ were an explicit function and trying to solve for $y$ first.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the chain rule on $y^2$, writing $-2y$ instead of $-2y\,\dfrac{dy}{dx}$.
  • Sign errors from the $-y^2$ term when rearranging for $dy/dx$.
  • Failing to simplify $\sec\theta/\tan\theta$ to $1/\sin\theta$ and leaving an unsimplified gradient.
  • Substituting the parametric coordinates before isolating $dy/dx$, leading to algebraic clutter.

A-level Scope Notes

Implicit differentiation of polynomial relations and parametric differentiation are both standard in Edexcel IAL Pure (P3/P4); recognising which is shorter is a routine exam-skill choice. All trig simplifications used ($\sec\theta/\tan\theta = 1/\sin\theta$) are within the IAL identity toolkit.
