Differentiation

Apply the power rule directly to fractional and negative exponents

Family: Power Rule Extension Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a term of the form $ax^n$ appears with $n$ a fraction (e.g. $x^{5/2}$, $x^{1/2}$, $x^{-3/2}$) or a surd/reciprocal that can be rewritten as $x^n$. Typical cues: 'find $f'(x)$' or '$\frac{dy}{dx}$' for an expression mixing integer and non-integer powers, or terms like $\sqrt{x}$, $1/\sqrt{x}$, $x\sqrt{x}$ already pre-rewritten in index form. Also fires on second-derivative requests where the first derivative still carries fractional powers.

Motivation — Why this works

The power rule $\frac{d}{dx}(ax^n) = nax^{n-1}$ holds for every real exponent $n$, not just positive integers. So fractional and negative powers can be differentiated in a single line without rewriting them as products of square roots or invoking chain/product rules. This keeps every term in clean $ax^n$ form, which is essential because successive differentiations (and later integrations) only stay tractable if each term remains a single power of $x$.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the exponent $5/2$ is non-integer but the term is still in pure $ax^n$ form.
  2. I rule out rewriting $x^{5/2}$ as $x^2\sqrt{x}$ and using the product rule — that would be unnecessary work and risk errors.
  3. I commit to the power rule because it is valid for all real $n$, so fractional exponents are no obstacle.
  4. Concretely, I will multiply the coefficient by the exponent and subtract 1 from the exponent, term-by-term.
  5. I also plan ahead: after one differentiation the exponent is still fractional ($3/2$), so I will reapply the same rule for $f''(x)$ rather than switching method.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Ensure every term is written as $ax^n$ (rewrite surds/reciprocals as fractional/negative powers if needed).
  2. For each term $ax^n$: new coefficient $= a \cdot n$, new exponent $= n-1$.
  3. Constants differentiate to $0$; linear terms $bx$ differentiate to $b$.
  4. Simplify each coefficient (e.g. $2 \cdot \tfrac{5}{2} = 5$) and each exponent (e.g. $\tfrac{5}{2}-1 = \tfrac{3}{2}$).
  5. If $f''(x)$ is required, repeat the rule on the result, keeping fractional exponents intact.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $f(x) = 2x^{5/2} - 40x + 8$.\n$f'(x) = 2 \cdot \tfrac{5}{2} x^{5/2 - 1} - 40 = 5x^{3/2} - 40$.\n$f''(x) = 5 \cdot \tfrac{3}{2} x^{3/2 - 1} = \tfrac{15}{2} x^{1/2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Negative fractional exponent, e.g. $g(x) = 3x^{-1/2}$
Apply the same rule: $g'(x) = 3 \cdot (-\tfrac{1}{2}) x^{-3/2} = -\tfrac{3}{2} x^{-3/2}$.
Surd form $\sqrt{x}$ or $x\sqrt{x}$
First rewrite as $x^{1/2}$ or $x^{3/2}$, then apply the power rule directly.
Reciprocal form $\dfrac{1}{x^3}$ or $\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{x}}$
Rewrite as $x^{-3}$ or $x^{-1/2}$ before differentiating.
Reverse operation: integrating $x^{n}$ for fractional $n \neq -1$
Add 1 to the exponent and divide by the new exponent: $\int x^{5/2}\,dx = \tfrac{2}{7}x^{7/2}+c$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $x^{5/2}$ as needing the chain rule with $u = x^{1/2}$, when the power rule applies directly.
  • Carrying the constant $8$ through to $f'(x)$ instead of dropping it.
  • Believing the power rule only works for positive integer $n$, and so rewriting fractional powers as products.

Pitfalls

  • Coefficient slip: $2 \cdot \tfrac{5}{2}$ should give $5$, not $\tfrac{5}{2}$ or $\tfrac{10}{2}$ left unsimplified.
  • Exponent slip: $\tfrac{5}{2} - 1 = \tfrac{3}{2}$, not $\tfrac{5}{2} - \tfrac{1}{2} = 2$.
  • Sign error with negative exponents: $-\tfrac{1}{2} - 1 = -\tfrac{3}{2}$, not $-\tfrac{1}{2} + 1$.
  • Forgetting to rewrite $\sqrt{x}$ or $1/x^k$ in index form before applying the rule.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics: the power rule for all rational $n$ is standard in P1/P2 differentiation, and reappears throughout C12/C34-style problems on tangents, normals, and stationary points.
