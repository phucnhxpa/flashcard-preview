Sequences & Series

Justify sum to infinity by stating |r|<1 and substituting r

Family: Condition Verification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an Edexcel question says 'explain why', 'show that', or 'state the condition for' the sum to infinity of a geometric series to exist. Typically appears as a 1-mark follow-up after r has been found in an earlier part. Also triggered by phrasing like 'why does $S_\infty$ exist?' or 'give a reason why the series converges'. The presence of a computed common ratio together with an explanatory verb is the key cue.

Motivation — Why this works

A geometric series $\sum ar^{n-1}$ converges iff $|r|<1$, because $r^n\to 0$ only under this condition, giving $S_\infty = a/(1-r)$. Edexcel explain-marks require both the general criterion and the specific numerical verification; either alone is incomplete. The move makes the logical structure (general rule → instance → conclusion) explicit so the examiner can tick the single available mark.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the verb 'explain why' attached to a sum-to-infinity claim — this is a justification, not a calculation.
  2. Rule out simply quoting the formula $S_\infty = a/(1-r)$; the formula is not the reason for convergence.
  3. Commit to the two-part template: cite |r|<1, then plug in the r found earlier.
  4. Bridge: locate r from the previous part (or compute $r = u_2/u_1$) and prepare to evaluate $|r|$.
  5. Tactically write the inequality with absolute-value bars, give the numerical value, and finish with a one-line conclusion.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. State the general criterion: a geometric series has a sum to infinity iff $|r|<1$.
  2. Substitute the value of r found previously and compute $|r|$.
  3. Compare $|r|$ to 1 explicitly (e.g. $|1/4| = 1/4 < 1$).
  4. Conclude: hence $S_\infty$ exists / the series converges.

Worked Micro-Example

Given a GP with $r = \tfrac{1}{4}$, explain why $S_\infty$ exists. \\ A GP converges to a sum to infinity iff $|r|<1$. \\ Here $|r| = \left|\tfrac{1}{4}\right| = \tfrac{1}{4} < 1$. \\ Therefore $S_\infty$ exists.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Binomial expansion validity: 'state the range of x for which the expansion of $(1+ax)^n$ is valid'
Use $|ax|<1$ and rearrange to $|x|<1/|a|$; same convergence principle, but answer is a range for x rather than a yes/no.
Negative common ratio, e.g. $r=-2/3$
Take absolute value: $|-2/3| = 2/3 < 1$, so still converges; emphasise the bars to handle the sign cleanly.
r given as an algebraic expression in a parameter k
Solve $|r(k)|<1$ as an inequality in k to give the set of values for which $S_\infty$ exists.
'Show that the series diverges' with $|r|\ge 1$
Mirror the move: state criterion, compute $|r|$, note $|r|\ge 1$, conclude no sum to infinity.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the question as a calculation and computing $S_\infty$ instead of justifying its existence.
  • Writing $-1<r<1$ without absolute-value notation when the question explicitly asks for a condition on $|r|$.
  • Citing $r<1$ alone, forgetting the lower bound and so admitting divergent cases like $r=-2$.

Pitfalls

  • Stating the criterion $|r|<1$ but not substituting the actual r (or vice versa) — examiners require both for the single mark.
  • Computing $|r|$ but failing to write the comparison with 1 or the closing conclusion.
  • Confusing the condition with $|a|<1$ or $|ar|<1$ instead of $|r|<1$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully inside Edexcel IAL P2 (Geometric series, sum to infinity) and reused in C12/P3-style binomial expansion validity questions; no limits beyond the standard $|r|<1$ criterion are required.
