Multiply by the conjugate surd to rationalise a binomial denominator

Family: Rationalisation Frequency: 8 questions

Trigger — When to use

The denominator of a fraction is a binomial of the form a ± b√c (e.g. 3 + √17, 4 − 2√2, 3 − √5, 3 + 2√2), so it contains an irrational surd term. The question typically demands an exact answer in a specific form such as p + q√c, a + b√c with rational/integer coefficients, or simply 'simplify' / 'rationalise the denominator'. Equivalent surface phrasings: 'express in the form a + b√n', 'give your answer in simplest surd form', 'write with a rational denominator'. Whenever a surd sits in a binomial denominator and an exact simplified form is required, this move fires.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying numerator and denominator by the conjugate (a ∓ b√c) exploits the difference-of-squares identity (X − Y)(X + Y) = X² − Y². Since (b√c)² = b²c is rational, the product (a + b√c)(a − b√c) = a² − b²c is a rational number, which clears the surd from the denominator. The expression's value is preserved because we are multiplying by conjugate/conjugate = 1. Once the denominator is rational, each numerator term can be divided through to read off the required p + q√c form.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the denominator is a binomial containing a surd (e.g. a + b√c), so the expression is not yet in the requested 'rational + surd' form — the surd must be removed from the denominator.
  2. Rule out alternatives: multiplying only by √c/√c does not clear a binomial denominator; squaring the denominator makes things worse; factoring usually fails because a and b√c share no rational factor that helps.
  3. Commit to conjugate multiplication: the conjugate of a + b√c is a − b√c, and their product a² − b²c is guaranteed rational by difference of squares.
  4. Bridge to operation: write the fraction × (conjugate)/(conjugate), which equals 1, then expand top and bottom separately.
  5. Tactical check: anticipate the value of a² − b²c before committing — if it is small (or even ±1), the arithmetic will be especially clean. Track signs carefully if the denominator goes negative.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the conjugate of the denominator: if denominator is a + b√c, conjugate is a − b√c (and vice versa).
  2. Multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate: (numerator)(a ∓ b√c) / [(a ± b√c)(a ∓ b√c)].
  3. Expand the denominator using difference of squares: (a)² − (b√c)² = a² − b²c (a rational number).
  4. Expand the numerator using FOIL, simplifying (√c)(√c) = c wherever it occurs.
  5. Collect rational and surd terms in the numerator separately.
  6. Divide every numerator term by the rational denominator to obtain the form p + q√c.
  7. Read off the required coefficients.

Worked Micro-Example

Simplify $\dfrac{24}{3+\sqrt{17}}$. Multiply by conjugate: $\dfrac{24}{3+\sqrt{17}}\cdot\dfrac{3-\sqrt{17}}{3-\sqrt{17}} = \dfrac{24(3-\sqrt{17})}{9-17} = \dfrac{24(3-\sqrt{17})}{-8} = -3(3-\sqrt{17}) = 3(\sqrt{17}-3)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Denominator of the form √a − √b (two surd terms, no rational part)
Multiply by (√a + √b)/(√a + √b); denominator becomes a − b — same difference-of-squares logic.
Denominator a − b√c with sign reversed
Use conjugate a + b√c; identity is identical, only the sign of the surd flips.
Complex-number denominator a + bi (Further Maths)
Multiply by complex conjugate a − bi; denominator becomes a² + b² (note the +, since i² = −1) — same algebraic skeleton.
Conjugate product equals 1 (e.g. (3+2√2)(3−2√2) = 9−8 = 1)
No final division needed — the numerator after expansion IS the answer.

Common Misreadings

  • Multiplying by the SAME binomial again (squaring the denominator) instead of the conjugate, which worsens the surd.
  • Multiplying only by √c/√c, which fails to clear a binomial denominator.
  • Multiplying only the numerator by the conjugate while leaving the denominator alone, which changes the value of the expression.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to square the entire surd term: writing (√c)² = c is correct, but (b√c)² = b²c, not bc — common slip when b ≠ 1 (e.g. (2√2)² = 8, not 2 or 4).
  • Sign errors during FOIL expansion of the numerator, especially with negative terms.
  • Sign error when the rationalised denominator is negative (e.g. 9 − 17 = −8) — must propagate the minus sign through the whole numerator.
  • Failing to divide ALL numerator terms by the denominator (e.g. writing −1 + (5/4)√5 instead of −1/4 + (5/4)√5).
  • Arithmetic slip when collecting like surd terms (e.g. 12√5 − 7√5 = 5√5, not 4√5).
  • Forgetting to simplify the final fraction (e.g. 24/8 = 3).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics P1 (surds) and reused throughout P2–P4 and FP1 (where the same conjugate technique handles complex denominators). No formula-booklet entry is required; the difference-of-squares identity is assumed known. Avoid generalising to nested surds (e.g. √(a+√b)) or higher-order radicals (cube roots), which fall outside the IAL syllabus.
