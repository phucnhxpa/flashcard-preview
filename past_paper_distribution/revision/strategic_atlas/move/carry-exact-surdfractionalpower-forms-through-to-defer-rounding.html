Algebra & Functions

Carry exact surd/fractional/power forms through to defer rounding

Family: Exact Value Propagation Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an intermediate result is an irrational surd (e.g. r = 10/√3), a fractional power (e.g. r = (17/15)^{1/3}), or a recognisable exact power of an integer (e.g. y = 4 = 2², y = 8 = 2³), AND that result is reused in later parts or fed into a sensitive operation (logarithm, cosine rule, integer threshold, squaring). Visual cues: a surd or cube root popping out of part (a), a multi-part question where part (a)'s answer recurs in (b)/(c)/(d), an integer-valued answer obtained from a logarithmic inequality, or quadratic roots that happen to be small powers of the exponential base. Equivalent phrasings: 'don't round yet', 'keep it exact', 'recognise the exact power', 'avoid premature rounding'.

Motivation — Why this works

Rounding an intermediate value introduces error that compounds when the value is squared, divided into a small logarithm, or used to decide an integer boundary — the final answer can then drift outside the tolerance or shift by a whole integer. Carrying exact form (surds, fractional powers, log identities such as ln(a^{1/n}) = (1/n)ln a, or recognised equalities like 2^x = 2^k ⇒ x = k) preserves full accuracy at no algebraic cost. The underlying principle is that exact arithmetic commutes cleanly with the operations used downstream, whereas decimal approximation does not. Edexcel mark schemes explicitly penalise premature rounding, especially in logarithmic-threshold and multi-step geometry problems.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the value coming out of this step is exact: a surd like 10/√3, a fractional power like (17/15)^{1/3}, or a clean power match like 2^x = 8 = 2³.
  2. I rule out immediately reaching for the calculator — converting to a decimal would mean later squaring, logging, or comparing to an integer boundary on a corrupted value.
  3. I rule out logarithms when an exact power match is visible: 2^x = 2^k gives x = k directly with no log error.
  4. I commit to keeping the exact form because (i) the question asks for a final accuracy (3 s.f. or integer N) that is sensitive to drift, and (ii) the exact form is no harder to substitute than the decimal.
  5. Tactical choice: I write the exact value once at the top of my working and only substitute decimals in the very last line of each part. For logs, I rewrite ln((a/b)^{1/n}) as (1/n)ln(a/b) so the log is evaluated on a clean rational.
  6. Final check: I confirm the question's wording ('where appropriate', '3 s.f.', 'smallest integer N') signals that exactness should be preserved as long as possible.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Record the exact value from the prior step in symbolic form (e.g. r = 10/√3, r = (17/15)^{1/3}, or x = 2 from 2^x = 4).
  2. When substituting into a later formula, substitute the symbolic form, not the decimal: e.g. ½ · r · r · sin C with r² = 100/3.
  3. If a logarithm of a fractional power appears, apply ln(a^{p/q}) = (p/q) ln a before evaluating.
  4. If an exponential equation b^x = N reduces to b^x = b^k, equate exponents: x = k, no logs.
  5. Only convert to decimals at the final line, then round to the required s.f. or apply ceiling/floor for integer thresholds.
  6. Sanity-check by recomputing with a high-precision unrounded decimal to confirm the rounded answer is stable.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose part (a) gives $r = (17/15)^{1/3}$ and part (b) requires the smallest integer $N$ with $r^N > 2.5$. Then $N \ln r > \ln 2.5$, i.e. $N \cdot \tfrac{1}{3}\ln(17/15) > \ln 2.5$, so $N > \dfrac{3\ln 2.5}{\ln(17/15)} \approx 21.97$, giving $N = 22$. Using $r \approx 1.0426$ instead would yield $\approx 21.95$ or $\approx 22.05$ depending on rounding, risking $N = 21$ or $23$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Surd radius/length feeding into area or cosine rule
Keep r = 10/√3 (or rationalised 10√3/3) and substitute symbolically into ½ab sin C and into a² + b² − 2ab cos C; only decimalise at the end.
Fractional-power common ratio in geometric/log inequality
Apply ln(a^{1/n}) = (1/n) ln a so the log acts on a rational; this prevents the small denominator from amplifying rounding error.
Exponential equation where RHS is a recognisable power of the base
Equate exponents directly (b^x = b^k ⇒ x = k); skip logs entirely.
Quadratic root in surd form feeding into integration limits or coordinate geometry
Keep the surd root unsimplified; substitute symbolically into the integral or distance formula.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'give answer to 3 s.f.' as permission to round every intermediate to 3 s.f. — it applies only to the FINAL answer.
  • Reaching for logarithms on 2^x = 8 instead of recognising 8 = 2³.
  • Reconstructing an earlier exact value from its already-rounded decimal printout instead of returning to the exact symbolic form.

Pitfalls

  • Rounding r to 5.77 and then squaring it inside the cosine rule — error doubles and the area/length can be off by 0.1+ units.
  • Using a rounded base r ≈ 1.0426 in a logarithmic inequality — the small ln r amplifies error and shifts N by ±1.
  • Mis-keying a rationalised surd like 10√3/3 into the calculator (forgetting brackets around the denominator).
  • Forgetting to check both quadratic roots when each generates its own exponential equation — only reporting one x value.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P2/P3): laws of logarithms (ln(a^k) = k ln a), exponential equations, sector/triangle geometry (½r²sin θ, cosine rule from the formula booklet). No appeal to numerical analysis or error-propagation theory is needed — the move is purely about deferring decimal evaluation.
