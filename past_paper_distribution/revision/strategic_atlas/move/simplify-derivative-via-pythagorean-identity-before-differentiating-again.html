Differentiation

Simplify derivative via Pythagorean identity before differentiating again

Family: Intermediate Simplification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in repeated-differentiation problems (finding f''(x), f'''(x), f⁽⁴⁾(x)) where the previous derivative is a trigonometric quotient. After applying the quotient rule, the expanded numerator contains both sin²x and cos²x terms, signalling a hidden Pythagorean identity. Also fires when the denominator is a perfect square like (1 + sin x)² or (1 - cos x)², because partial cancellation is likely after simplification. Cue phrases: 'find the first four derivatives', 'show that f''(x) = …', or any chain where naïve differentiation would explode in length.

Motivation — Why this works

In iterated differentiation, expression complexity compounds at each stage; without simplification, f⁽⁴⁾(x) becomes intractable by hand. The identity sin²x + cos²x = 1 collapses two-term polynomials in sin and cos into a constant or linear expression, often producing a factor matching the denominator. The resulting cancellation reduces a quotient with squared denominator to a simple reciprocal, which differentiates cleanly via the chain rule. This is essentially algebraic hygiene: each derivative should be in its simplest form before the next differentiation, otherwise errors and bulk multiply.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the quotient-rule numerator, after expansion, contains both sin²x and cos²x — a strong cue that an identity is lurking.
  2. Rule out brute-force differentiation of the messy form: the next derivative would require product/quotient/chain rules on a four-term numerator.
  3. Commit to simplifying first because the denominator is (1 + sin x)², so a factor of (1 + sin x) in the numerator would cancel cleanly.
  4. Bridge: expand the numerator carefully, replace sin²x + cos²x by 1, then factor out −1 to expose the (1 + sin x) factor.
  5. Tactical decision: record the simplified result as f''(x) = −(1 + sin x)⁻¹ in index form so the next differentiation is a one-line chain rule rather than another quotient rule.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Apply quotient rule and write the numerator without simplifying prematurely: −sin x(1 + sin x) − cos²x.
  2. Expand: −sin x − sin²x − cos²x.
  3. Apply sin²x + cos²x = 1 to combine the squared terms: −sin x − 1.
  4. Factor: −(1 + sin x).
  5. Cancel one factor of (1 + sin x) against the denominator (1 + sin x)² to get −1/(1 + sin x).
  6. Rewrite as −(1 + sin x)⁻¹ in preparation for the next differentiation by chain rule.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $f'(x) = \dfrac{\cos x}{1 + \sin x}$, quotient rule gives $f''(x) = \dfrac{-\sin x(1+\sin x) - \cos^2 x}{(1+\sin x)^2}$. Expanding the numerator: $-\sin x - \sin^2 x - \cos^2 x = -\sin x - 1 = -(1+\sin x)$. Hence $f''(x) = \dfrac{-(1+\sin x)}{(1+\sin x)^2} = -\dfrac{1}{1+\sin x}$. Then $f'''(x) = \dfrac{\cos x}{(1+\sin x)^2}$ by chain rule — a one-line step.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Repeated differentiation of tan x = sin x / cos x
After each quotient rule step, sin²x + cos²x = 1 in the numerator collapses to give the standard sec²x form, keeping subsequent derivatives compact.
Derivatives involving 1/(1 − cos x) or cos x/(1 − cos x)
Same Pythagorean identity simplification applies; expect cancellation with the denominator after factoring.
Implicit differentiation finding d²y/dx²
Simplify dy/dx (often using the original implicit equation to eliminate variables) before differentiating again, rather than differentiating a bulky quotient directly.
Maclaurin series construction requiring f⁽ⁿ⁾(0)
Simplifying each derivative to lowest terms before evaluating at 0 prevents propagation of avoidable algebraic complexity.

Common Misreadings

  • Failing to spot sin²x + cos²x hidden inside an expanded numerator and proceeding to differentiate the messy unsimplified expression.
  • Treating −sin²x − cos²x as if it simplifies to −1·(sin²x − cos²x) or some other non-identity, missing the sign structure.
  • Believing simplification is optional polish rather than essential preparation for the next differentiation.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error in expanding −sin x(1 + sin x): writing −sin x + sin²x instead of −sin x − sin²x.
  • Cancelling (1 + sin x) factors when the numerator power is lower than the denominator power, leaving the wrong residual power.
  • Forgetting to record the simplified derivative in index form, leading to an unnecessary quotient rule on the next step.
  • Dropping the overall minus sign during the factor −(1 + sin x), giving the wrong sign for f''(x).

A-level Scope Notes

All techniques used — quotient rule, chain rule, and the Pythagorean identity sin²x + cos²x = 1 — are standard Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics content (P3/P4), and repeated differentiation appears in Maclaurin series questions in Further Pure.
