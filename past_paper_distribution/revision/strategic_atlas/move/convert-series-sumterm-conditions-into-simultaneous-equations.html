Sequences & Series

Convert series sum/term conditions into simultaneous equations

Family: Equation Formulation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a problem states numerical values for sums or specific terms of an arithmetic and/or geometric series and asks for the underlying parameters $a$, $d$, or $r$. Typical cues: 'the sum of the first $n$ terms is...', 'the sum to infinity is...', 'the $k$th term equals...'. Often two series share a common element (e.g. same first term) so the conditions interlock. The student must recognise that each numerical condition is one equation waiting to be written.

Motivation — Why this works

Every standard sum/term formula ($u_n$, $S_n$, $S_\infty$) is an identity linking the parameters to a numerical output. Substituting the given value turns each verbal condition into a clean algebraic equation. Collecting these equations produces a simultaneous system whose size matches the number of unknowns, reducing a word problem to routine algebra. This is the universal bridge between sequence vocabulary and algebraic technique.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the problem gives numerical results (sums or specific terms) for one or more series — these are equations in disguise.
  2. I rule out trying to compute terms one by one or guessing values; the formulas $S_n=\tfrac{n}{2}(2a+(n-1)d)$ and $S_\infty=\tfrac{a}{1-r}$ are the right tools.
  3. I commit to writing one equation per numerical condition before attempting to solve, so I see the full system at once.
  4. I substitute the given values into the matching formula and simplify to a tidy form (e.g. $a+2d=7$) ready for elimination/substitution.
  5. If unknowns outnumber equations, I look for a linking phrase ('same first term', 'common ratio equals...') to supply the missing relation, and I flag any convergence requirement $|r|<1$ to check later.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify which formula matches each given condition: $u_n=a+(n-1)d$, $S_n=\tfrac{n}{2}(2a+(n-1)d)$, $u_n=ar^{n-1}$, $S_n=\tfrac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r}$, or $S_\infty=\tfrac{a}{1-r}$.
  2. Substitute the numerical condition into the formula to produce one equation per condition.
  3. Simplify each equation (clear fractions, divide by common factors) into a clean algebraic form.
  4. Use any linking statement between series to equate parameters and reduce the number of unknowns.
  5. Solve the resulting simultaneous system, then verify domain conditions (e.g. $|r|<1$ for $S_\infty$).

Worked Micro-Example

An AS and GS share first term $a$. Given $S_5=35$ for the AS and $S_\infty=32$ for the GS:\n$S_5=\tfrac{5}{2}(2a+4d)=35 \Rightarrow a+2d=7$ (Eq 1).\n$S_\infty=\tfrac{a}{1-r}=32 \Rightarrow a=32(1-r)$ (Eq 2).\nA further link (e.g. $d=ar$) then closes the system, with $|r|<1$ checked at the end.

Variants — Same move, different surface

nth-term condition for an AS
Use $u_n=a+(n-1)d$; e.g. '7th term is 20' becomes $a+6d=20$.
nth-term condition for a GS
Use $u_n=ar^{n-1}$; e.g. '4th term is 54' becomes $ar^3=54$.
Finite GS sum given
Use $S_n=\tfrac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r}$ instead of $S_\infty$; no convergence check needed but $r\ne 1$.
Sum of an AS via first and last term
If the last term $l$ is known, use $S_n=\tfrac{n}{2}(a+l)$ to avoid introducing $d$.
Ratio of two terms in a GS
Dividing two term equations $ar^m$ and $ar^k$ eliminates $a$ and yields a power equation in $r$.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing $S_n$ (sum) with $u_n$ (single term) and applying the wrong formula.
  • Using $S_n=\tfrac{n}{2}(a+l)$ when the last term is not actually given.
  • Treating 'sum to infinity' as a finite sum and using $S_n$ with a large $n$ instead of $S_\infty$.
  • Assuming the two series share more than they do (e.g. equal common difference and ratio) without textual support.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the convergence requirement $|r|<1$ when invoking $S_\infty=\tfrac{a}{1-r}$.
  • Arithmetic slips when clearing the $\tfrac{n}{2}$ factor in the AS sum formula.
  • Dropping a sign when rearranging $a=32(1-r)$ or similar.
  • Solving the system but failing to back-substitute to verify each original condition holds.

A-level Scope Notes

All formulas used ($u_n$, $S_n$ for AS and GS, and $S_\infty$ with $|r|<1$) are standard in Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1/P2); no techniques beyond simultaneous equations are required.
