Algebra & Functions

Recognise 1 + (dy/dx)² as a perfect square to collapse the arc-length radical

Family: Perfect Square Recognition Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

This move fires whenever you are setting up an arc-length or surface-area integral and have just formed 1 + (dy/dx)² (or the parametric/polar analogues (dx/dt)² + (dy/dt)², r² + (dr/dθ)²) which sits under a square root. The structural cue is a three-term expression resembling a² ± 2ab + b² — typically with one cross-term whose sign has flipped because of the '+1'. Other surface cues: the original y has the form x^n ± x^(−n), or contains (½)·x² with a (½)ln x term, or a difference (1 − x²)² appears in the numerator after squaring. In short: any time an FP3-style arc-length problem produces a quartic, or a sum of x² and 1/x² type terms, suspect a perfect square.

Motivation — Why this works

FP3 arc-length and surface-area curves are deliberately engineered so that 1 + (dy/dx)² is a perfect square (g(x))²; the radical then collapses to |g(x)| = g(x) on the relevant interval, converting an intractable surd integrand into a polynomial or rational one that standard A-level techniques can integrate. The underlying identity is a² − 2ab + b² + (something making the cross-term flip) = a² + 2ab + b² = (a + b)². Concretely, if dy/dx = A − B with 2AB = 1, then (dy/dx)² + 1 = (A + B)². Without spotting this collapse, the integral is essentially unmanageable at A-level — recognition of the perfect square is the central algebraic step of the entire question.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question is arc length / surface area, so I will inevitably need √(1 + (dy/dx)²) — examiners design these to simplify, so I expect a perfect square.
  2. Rule out brute-force substitution or trig substitution on the raw radical: at A-level these radicals are never integrated directly; if I cannot simplify, I am missing the trick.
  3. Commit to the perfect-square hypothesis as soon as I see the 'difference structure' in dy/dx (e.g. x/2 − 1/(2x), or x^n − x^(−n)) or a quartic with symmetric coefficients (1, 2, 1).
  4. Square dy/dx carefully, watching the cross-term sign: (A − B)² = A² − 2AB + B², a NEGATIVE middle term.
  5. Add 1 and check whether 1 = 2·2AB (i.e. whether the +1 exactly flips the sign of the cross-term to give A² + 2AB + B²); if fractions are present, place over a common denominator first so the structure is visible.
  6. Confirm by writing the candidate (A + B)² and expanding back; then take the positive root, justified by the geometry of the interval.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Differentiate to get dy/dx in simplest form (look for a difference A − B).
  2. Square: (dy/dx)² = A² − 2AB + B², expanded fully.
  3. Add 1, combining over a common denominator if necessary, to obtain a single rational expression.
  4. Inspect the three-term result and factor as a perfect square: 1 + (dy/dx)² = (A + B)².
  5. Take the square root: √(1 + (dy/dx)²) = A + B (positive on the interval).
  6. Substitute into ∫√(1 + (dy/dx)²) dx and proceed with standard integration.

Worked Micro-Example

For $y = \tfrac{x^2}{4} - \tfrac{1}{2}\ln x$: $\tfrac{dy}{dx} = \tfrac{x}{2} - \tfrac{1}{2x}$. Then $\left(\tfrac{dy}{dx}\right)^2 = \tfrac{x^2}{4} - \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{4x^2}$, so $1 + \left(\tfrac{dy}{dx}\right)^2 = \tfrac{x^2}{4} + \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{4x^2} = \left(\tfrac{x}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2x}\right)^2$. Hence $\sqrt{1 + (dy/dx)^2} = \tfrac{x}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2x}$, integrable directly.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quartic numerator under a root, e.g. (1 − x²)² + 4x² over (1 − x²)²
Expand to x⁴ + 2x² + 1 and factor as (1 + x²)²; the radical reduces to (1 + x²)/|1 − x²|, a rational function.
Hyperbolic curves such as y = a cosh(x/a)
Use the identity 1 + sinh²(x/a) = cosh²(x/a) — the perfect square comes from a hyperbolic identity rather than algebraic factorisation.
Parametric arc length
Apply the same logic to (dx/dt)² + (dy/dt)²; the curve is engineered so this collapses to a perfect square in t.
Polar arc length
r² + (dr/dθ)² is similarly designed to be a perfect square in θ — same recognition step.

Common Misreadings

  • Attempting to integrate the unsimplified surd directly (trig or hyperbolic substitution on the raw radical) instead of first simplifying.
  • Trying to 'complete the square' as if the expression were a quadratic to be manipulated, rather than recognising an existing perfect square.
  • Missing that a 'difference' in dy/dx becomes a 'sum' inside the root once 1 is added.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when squaring dy/dx: getting +2AB instead of −2AB destroys the flip and the perfect-square structure disappears.
  • Forgetting the '+1' when forming 1 + (dy/dx)² — the 1 is precisely what flips the cross-term sign.
  • Arithmetic slip when squaring fractional terms: e.g. 2·(x/2)·(1/(2x)) = 1/2, not 1 — getting this constant wrong breaks the identification.
  • When fractions are involved, failing to use a common denominator before pattern-matching, hiding the perfect square.
  • Taking |g(x)| without checking the sign of g(x) on the interval — at A-level it is almost always positive, but state this.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP3) arc length and surface area of revolution. The arc-length formula ∫√(1 + (dy/dx)²) dx and its parametric/polar/surface-area counterparts are in the FP3 formula booklet. Do not invoke general results about quartic factorisation or non-elementary integrals — at this level the perfect-square collapse is always intended.
