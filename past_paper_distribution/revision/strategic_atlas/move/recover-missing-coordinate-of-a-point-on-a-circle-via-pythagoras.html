Coordinate Geometry

Recover missing coordinate of a point on a circle via Pythagoras

Family: Pythagoras Coordinate Recovery Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A point lies on a circle of known centre and radius, and exactly one of its coordinates is given (often by a vertical/horizontal line, a tangent foot, or a shared coordinate with another labelled point). The other coordinate is required to compute a length, area, or perimeter in a composite figure. Cue phrases include 'B lies on the circle', 'the line x = k meets the circle', or a diagram showing a chord/segment whose endpoint sits on the arc.

Motivation — Why this works

Any point (x, y) on a circle satisfies its Cartesian equation, so once one coordinate is fixed the other is forced (up to sign) by the equation. For a circle centred at the origin this reduces to x² + y² = r², a direct Pythagorean relation between the radius and the coordinates. Solving for the unknown coordinate is therefore a one-line algebraic step that unlocks every downstream length calculation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that the point in question lies on a circle whose equation (or centre and radius) is known, and that only one coordinate is missing.
  2. Rules out trigonometric parameterisation or simultaneous-equation methods because a single coordinate is already pinned down — substitution is faster.
  3. Commits to substituting the known coordinate into the circle equation, recognising this as the Pythagorean identity for points on a circle.
  4. Bridges to computation by writing the circle equation explicitly and isolating the unknown squared term.
  5. Tactically chooses the correct sign of the square root using the diagram, quadrant, or contextual cues (e.g. 'above the x-axis').

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the circle equation: x² + y² = r² (or (x − a)² + (y − b)² = r² if not centred at origin).
  2. Substitute the known coordinate to obtain a single equation in the unknown.
  3. Rearrange to unknown² = r² − (known)² and take the square root.
  4. Select the sign consistent with the diagram or stated region.
  5. Record the full coordinate of the point and feed it into the subsequent length/area calculation.

Worked Micro-Example

Circle $x^2 + y^2 = 16$, point $B$ on the circle with $x_B = 1.5$. Then $y_B^2 = 16 - 1.5^2 = 13.75$, so $y_B = \sqrt{13.75} \approx 3.708$ (positive root chosen as $B$ lies above the $x$-axis). Hence $B = (1.5,\ \sqrt{13.75})$, and a vertical segment to $C(1.5, 6)$ has length $CB = 6 - \sqrt{13.75}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Circle not centred at the origin
Use $(x-a)^2 + (y-b)^2 = r^2$; substitute the known coordinate and solve the resulting quadratic in the other.
Known y-coordinate, missing x
Symmetric: solve $x^2 = r^2 - y^2$ and pick the sign from the diagram (left/right of centre).
Point on a circle given in expanded form $x^2+y^2+Dx+Ey+F=0$
Either complete the square first to identify centre/radius, or substitute the known coordinate directly to get a quadratic in the unknown.
Intersection of a vertical/horizontal line with a circle
Same substitution gives two solutions; both intersection points are recovered, then context selects which is needed.
Endpoint of a chord with one coordinate shared with another labelled point
Use the shared coordinate as the 'known' value and apply the same Pythagorean substitution.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the given coordinate as a length from the centre rather than as an actual coordinate value (matters when centre is not at origin).
  • Assuming the point is on the axis or on a tangent line rather than on the circle itself.
  • Confusing the radius with the diameter when reading the figure.

Pitfalls

  • Taking the wrong sign of the square root when the quadrant or diagram dictates a specific one.
  • Computing $\sqrt{r^2 + x^2}$ instead of $\sqrt{r^2 - x^2}$.
  • Forgetting to shift coordinates when the centre is not at the origin, leading to an incorrect substitution.
  • Rounding $\sqrt{r^2 - x^2}$ too early and propagating error into perimeter/area answers — keep surd form until the final step.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the Cartesian equation of a circle and Pythagoras, both core to Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P2 coordinate geometry of the circle). No trigonometric parameterisation or vector methods required.
