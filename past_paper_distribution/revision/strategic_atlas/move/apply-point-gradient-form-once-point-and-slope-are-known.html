Coordinate Geometry

Apply point-gradient form once point and slope are known

Family: Point Gradient Form Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever you have (or can immediately compute) a specific point (x₁, y₁) on a line together with its gradient m. Typical surface cues: the question asks for 'the equation of the line/tangent/normal through P', a gradient has just been found (from two points, perpendicularity, or by evaluating dy/dx at a given x), and a coordinate of the point is explicitly stated. The same trigger applies whether the line is a chord, a tangent to a curve, a normal, or a perpendicular bisector — the unifying signal is 'one point + one slope, find the line'.

Motivation — Why this works

The point-gradient form y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) is the identity that directly encodes a point and a slope into a single linear equation, with no unknown constant left to solve for. It saves a step compared to y = mx + c, where you would have to substitute the point afterwards to find c. Mathematically it is just the definition of slope (rise/run) rearranged, so it is always available and never requires an auxiliary calculation. The output can then be tidied into y = mx + c or ax + by + c = 0 as the question demands.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the data on hand: a concrete point P(x₁, y₁) and a numerical gradient m (either given, computed from two points, derived via perpendicularity m₂ = −1/m₁, or obtained by substituting coordinates into dy/dx).
  2. Consider y = mx + c as an alternative — it works, but forces a second substitution to determine c, which is wasted effort and an extra place for sign errors.
  3. Commit to point-gradient form because both required inputs are already in hand and the equation drops out in one line.
  4. Bridge to action: write down y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) literally, being careful with signs when y₁ or x₁ is negative (so y − (−3) becomes y + 3).
  5. Decide on the final presentation form: leave as y = mx + c if the question wants slope-intercept, or clear fractions and rearrange to ax + by + c = 0 if integer coefficients are required.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify (x₁, y₁) and m explicitly; if m is not yet numerical (e.g. given as dy/dx in terms of x, y), substitute the point's coordinates first to get a number.
  2. Write y − y₁ = m(x − x₁), substituting carefully with brackets around negative coordinates.
  3. Expand the right-hand side: y − y₁ = mx − mx₁.
  4. Rearrange to the form requested — either y = mx + (y₁ − mx₁ + … wait, simply isolate y) for slope-intercept, or clear denominators and collect to one side for ax + by + c = 0.
  5. Sanity-check by substituting the original point back into the final equation.

Worked Micro-Example

Point P(2, −3), gradient m = −2. Apply y − y_1 = m(x − x_1): \; y − (−3) = −2(x − 2) \Rightarrow y + 3 = −2x + 4 \Rightarrow y = −2x + 1. Check: at x=2, y = −2(2)+1 = −3. ✓

Variants — Same move, different surface

Tangent to a curve at a given point, with dy/dx as a function of x (and possibly y)
First evaluate m = dy/dx at the given coordinates to get a numerical slope, then apply y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) exactly as usual.
Normal to a curve at a given point
Replace m with −1/m (where m is the tangent gradient there), then apply the same point-gradient template.
Two points known but no gradient given
Compute m = (y₂ − y₁)/(x₂ − x₁) first, then apply point-gradient form with either point.
Parametric curve
Find dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt), evaluate at the given t-value, read off (x, y) at that t, then apply point-gradient form.
Tangent to a circle
Find the radius gradient from centre to point, take its negative reciprocal as the tangent gradient, then apply point-gradient form.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating dy/dx as the gradient without first substituting the specific point — leaving the slope as an expression in x, y rather than a number.
  • Reading 'normal' as 'tangent' (or vice versa) and using the wrong gradient in an otherwise correct point-gradient set-up.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors with negative coordinates: writing y − 3 instead of y − (−3) = y + 3.
  • Sign errors when the gradient is a ratio of two negatives, e.g. −5/−7 = +5/7, not −5/7.
  • Forgetting to rearrange to the form the question demands (slope-intercept vs. integer-coefficient general form), losing presentation marks.
  • Substituting into a pre-differentiated implicit equation rather than the isolated dy/dx expression.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1 straight lines; P3/P4 tangents and normals via implicit and parametric differentiation). The point-gradient form is not in the formula booklet — it must be memorised. Avoid conflating with vector line equations r = a + λd, which is a separate (FP) representation.
