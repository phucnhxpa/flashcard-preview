Integration

Repeat integration by parts n times to eliminate a degree-n polynomial factor

Family: Repeated Parts Reduction Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

After a first application of integration by parts to an integral of the form ∫x^n e^{ax} dx (or x^n sin(ax), x^n cos(ax)), the remaining integral still has the same structural shape — a polynomial times an exponential/trig function — but with the polynomial degree dropped by one. The visual cue is: 'I applied IBP once, but the leftover integral isn't elementary yet — it's another polynomial × exponential.' Equivalently: the integrand still resists direct integration, and substitution is unhelpful because the polynomial factor remains. This signals that IBP must be cascaded until the polynomial is fully differentiated away.

Motivation — Why this works

Each round of integration by parts with u = (polynomial) differentiates that polynomial, lowering its degree by exactly one, while the exponential/trig partner is integrated (preserving its form up to a constant). Therefore a degree-n polynomial factor demands exactly n IBP applications before it becomes a constant and disappears, leaving a pure exponential or trig integral that is elementary. This guarantees termination, and recognising the count n upfront lets the student plan the layers of nested expressions and avoid losing coefficients during back-substitution. The underlying identity is the product-rule reversal ∫u dv = uv − ∫v du applied repeatedly.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the original integral has the form (polynomial of degree n) × (exponential or sine/cosine), e.g. ∫x²e^{2x} dx — a classic IBP candidate by LIATE (Algebraic before Exponential).
  2. I rule out u-substitution: there is no inner-function/derivative pairing, and the polynomial factor will not collapse under any substitution.
  3. I rule out 'one-shot' IBP: after one round, the leftover integral ∫xe^{2x} dx still has the same algebraic-times-exponential form, just with degree reduced by 1 — so a single IBP cannot finish the job.
  4. I commit to cascading IBP because each round strictly reduces the polynomial degree by 1; with degree-n polynomial, exactly n rounds will leave only ∫e^{ax} dx, which integrates trivially.
  5. Tactical decision: I keep the LIATE assignment consistent across every round (u = polynomial factor, dv = exponential/trig factor) so the polynomial is always the one being differentiated.
  6. I plan the back-substitution carefully: the result of the inner IBP will be subtracted (often multiplied by an outer fractional coefficient), so I will bracket it and distribute the minus sign at the very end, adding +C only once.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. First IBP on ∫x^n e^{ax} dx: set u = x^n, du = n x^{n-1} dx; dv = e^{ax} dx, v = (1/a)e^{ax}. Result: (x^n/a)e^{ax} − (n/a)∫x^{n-1} e^{ax} dx.
  2. Recognise the leftover ∫x^{n-1} e^{ax} dx has the same form. Apply IBP again with u = x^{n-1}, dv = e^{ax} dx, v = (1/a)e^{ax}.
  3. Continue cascading: after k rounds the polynomial factor has degree n−k. Stop when n−k = 0, leaving ∫e^{ax} dx = (1/a)e^{ax}.
  4. Back-substitute outward, layer by layer, keeping each intermediate result in brackets so the alternating signs and accumulated coefficients (n/a, n(n−1)/a², …) are preserved.
  5. Distribute all minus signs and collect like terms; append a single constant of integration +C at the very end.

Worked Micro-Example

Compute $\int x^2 e^{2x}\,dx$. First IBP ($u=x^2$, $dv=e^{2x}dx$): $=\tfrac12 x^2 e^{2x}-\int x e^{2x}\,dx$. Second IBP ($u=x$, $dv=e^{2x}dx$): $\int x e^{2x}\,dx=\tfrac12 x e^{2x}-\tfrac14 e^{2x}$. Substitute back: $\int x^2 e^{2x}\,dx=\tfrac12 x^2 e^{2x}-\tfrac12 x e^{2x}+\tfrac14 e^{2x}+C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Higher-degree polynomial, e.g. ∫x³e^{x} dx or ∫x³e^{2x} dx
Apply IBP three times by the same logic — the polynomial degree dictates the count.
Polynomial × trigonometric, e.g. ∫x² sin(x) dx
Same cascade applies; the partner factor cycles sin → −cos → −sin → cos under repeated integration, so track signs carefully.
Tabular / DI method
Systematise the cascade as a sign-alternating table of derivatives of the polynomial column and integrals of the exponential column — purely a bookkeeping device for the same technique.

Common Misreadings

  • Stopping after one IBP and leaving ∫xe^{ax} dx unresolved, treating it as a final answer.
  • Attempting a substitution like u = ax on the leftover integral instead of recognising the recurring structural pattern that calls for another IBP.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error during back-substitution: the entire inner IBP result is subtracted, so the minus sign must be distributed across every term in the bracket.
  • Losing the outer fractional coefficient (e.g. the −n/a multiplier) when substituting the second-round result into the first — a frequent source of fraction errors.
  • Switching the LIATE assignment mid-cascade (e.g. letting u = e^{ax} on the second round) — this undoes the previous round and traps the student in a circular calculation.
  • Adding +C after every intermediate IBP rather than once at the very end of the indefinite integral.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics P3 (Integration by Parts), formula booklet entry ∫u(dv/dx)dx = uv − ∫v(du/dx)dx. Reduction-formula derivations (∫x^n e^{ax} dx expressed recursively in n) belong to Further Pure and should not be invoked unless the question explicitly asks for them.
