Complex Numbers

Use conjugate-pair vertical symmetry to compute Argand triangle area

Family: Argand Geometry Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Three roots of a polynomial (typically a real cubic) are plotted on an Argand diagram and form a triangle. Two of the roots are a complex conjugate pair $a \pm bi$, and the third is real. Cues include: 'find the area of the triangle formed by the roots', 'show on an Argand diagram', or a cubic with real coefficients whose roots have already been found. The conjugate pair sits symmetrically across the real axis, instantly giving a vertical edge.

Motivation — Why this works

Because the polynomial has real coefficients, non-real roots come in conjugate pairs $a+bi$ and $a-bi$, sharing real part $a$. In the Argand plane these are vertically aligned, so the segment joining them is vertical with length $2b$. The third (real) root lies on the real axis, so its perpendicular distance to the vertical line $\operatorname{Re}(z)=a$ is just $|a-x_3|$. The triangle therefore has a built-in right angle between base and height, reducing the area to $\tfrac{1}{2}\times 2b\times|a-x_3|$ with no shoelace or determinant work.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that two of the three plotted points share the same x-coordinate (real part) because they are a conjugate pair — the segment between them is vertical.
  2. Rules out the shoelace formula and Heron's formula as overkill; also rules out side-length computations since they are not needed.
  3. Commits to base × height because the vertical conjugate segment is automatically perpendicular to the horizontal distance from the real root.
  4. Bridges to operation: take the conjugate pair as the base of length $2|\operatorname{Im}(z)|$ and the real root's horizontal offset as the height.
  5. Tactically chooses which pair to call 'base' (the vertical one is easiest), and is careful to subtract real parts (not imaginary parts) for the height.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Read off coordinates: conjugate pair at $(a, b)$ and $(a, -b)$; real root at $(x_3, 0)$.
  2. Base = vertical segment between the conjugates, length $= 2|b|$.
  3. Height = perpendicular distance from $(x_3,0)$ to the line $\operatorname{Re}(z)=a$, i.e. $|a-x_3|$.
  4. Area $= \tfrac{1}{2}\times 2|b|\times|a-x_3| = |b|\,|a-x_3|$.
  5. Sanity-check by confirming the configuration is isoceles (the real root is equidistant from the two conjugates).

Worked Micro-Example

The cubic $2z^3-21z^2+82z-41=0$ has roots $z=\tfrac12,\;5+4i,\;5-4i$. Plot $A(5,4)$, $B(5,-4)$, $C(\tfrac12,0)$. Base $AB$ is vertical of length $4-(-4)=8$. Height from $C$ to the line $x=5$ is $|5-\tfrac12|=\tfrac92$. Area $=\tfrac12\times 8\times\tfrac92=18$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quartic with two conjugate pairs $a\pm bi$ and $c\pm di$ forming a quadrilateral
Both diagonals (or one pair of opposite sides) are vertical; split into two triangles sharing a vertical edge, or use the trapezium formula $\tfrac12(2b+2d)|a-c|$ if the pairs sit on parallel vertical lines.
Three roots all non-real (e.g. cube roots of a complex number)
No automatic vertical edge; use $\tfrac12|\operatorname{Im}(\overline{z_1}z_2 + \overline{z_2}z_3 + \overline{z_3}z_1)|$ or the shoelace formula on $(\operatorname{Re},\operatorname{Im})$ coordinates.
Roots of unity or $z^n=k$ giving a regular polygon
Use rotational symmetry: area $=\tfrac{n}{2}r^2\sin(2\pi/n)$ rather than base × height.
Triangle with one vertex at the origin and two conjugates
Origin lies on the real axis, so the same vertical-base / horizontal-height shortcut applies with $x_3=0$, giving area $=|a|\,|b|$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the Argand point $a+bi$ as having coordinates $(b,a)$ instead of $(a,b)$, swapping real and imaginary axes.
  • Computing all three side lengths and applying Heron's formula — correct but slow and error-prone.
  • Assuming the triangle is equilateral because it 'looks symmetric', and using $\tfrac{\sqrt3}{4}s^2$.

Pitfalls

  • Placing the real root $z=\tfrac12$ at $(0,\tfrac12)$ instead of $(\tfrac12,0)$.
  • Taking the base as $|b|$ instead of $2|b|$ (forgetting both endpoints contribute).
  • Computing height as $|a|+|x_3|$ when both have the same sign, instead of $|a-x_3|$.
  • Forgetting the leading factor $\tfrac12$ in the area formula.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard FP1/FP2 Argand-diagram material: conjugate-root theorem for real-coefficient polynomials and elementary triangle area. No vectors, determinants beyond $2\times2$, or complex-number area identities beyond what is implicit in $\tfrac12\times\text{base}\times\text{height}$ are required.
