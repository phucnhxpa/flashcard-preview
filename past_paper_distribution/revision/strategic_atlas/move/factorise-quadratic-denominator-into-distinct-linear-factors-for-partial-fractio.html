Factorise quadratic denominator into distinct linear factors for partial fractions

Family: Factorisation For Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a rational expression has a quadratic denominator and the next step (partial fractions, integration, or series expansion) requires linear-factor form. Typical cues: target form shown as A/(x+a) + B/(x+b), or an integral of the form ∫(px+q)/(x²+bx+c) dx where the quadratic factors over ℝ. Also fires after polynomial division has reduced a top-heavy fraction to a proper remainder fraction needing decomposition.

Motivation — Why this works

Partial fraction decomposition theorems require the denominator written as a product of irreducible factors over ℝ. A quadratic with positive discriminant splits as (x+a)(x+b), and the decomposition theorem then guarantees a unique representation A/(x+a) + B/(x+b). Each piece is now a standard form that can be integrated as a logarithm, expanded by the binomial series, or summed by partial fractions telescoping.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the denominator is a quadratic and the surrounding task (integrate / expand / sum) needs simpler pieces.
  2. Rule out leaving it as a quadratic — that would force an (Ax+B)/(quadratic) form, which is not what the target structure shows.
  3. Commit to factoring once the discriminant b²−4c ≥ 0 and integer factor pairs of the constant term suggest a clean split.
  4. Bridge: search factor pairs of the constant term whose sum equals the middle coefficient.
  5. Tactically verify by expansion before substituting into the partial-fraction template, so any sign error is caught early.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Check the discriminant (or inspect for an obvious factor pair) to confirm the quadratic factors over ℝ.
  2. Find integers (or rationals) p, q with p·q = constant term and p+q = coefficient of x.
  3. Write the quadratic as (x+p)(x+q) and expand mentally to verify.
  4. Substitute the factored form into the partial fraction template A/(x+p) + B/(x+q).

Worked Micro-Example

Decompose $\dfrac{5}{x^2+3x+2}$. Factor pair of $2$ summing to $3$: $1$ and $2$, so $x^2+3x+2=(x+1)(x+2)$. Write $\dfrac{5}{(x+1)(x+2)}=\dfrac{A}{x+1}+\dfrac{B}{x+2}$. Cover-up: $A=\dfrac{5}{(-1)+2}=5$, $B=\dfrac{5}{(-2)+1}=-5$, giving $\dfrac{5}{x+1}-\dfrac{5}{x+2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Leading coefficient ≠ 1, e.g. $2x^2+5x+3$
Use the AC-method or factor as $(2x+3)(x+1)$; the partial fraction template becomes $A/(2x+3)+B/(x+1)$.
Difference of squares denominator $x^2-a^2$
Factor immediately as $(x-a)(x+a)$ — no search needed.
Repeated linear factor, e.g. $(x+1)^2$ appearing after factoring
Use template $A/(x+1)+B/(x+1)^2$ instead of two distinct linear terms.
Irreducible quadratic factor (negative discriminant)
Do not force a real factorisation; keep as $(Ax+B)/(x^2+bx+c)$ in the decomposition.
Cubic denominator with one obvious root
Use the factor theorem to extract a linear factor first, then factor the resulting quadratic by this same move.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the quadratic as irreducible and jumping to an (Ax+B)/quadratic form when it actually factors.
  • Confusing the factor-pair signs when the constant term is positive but the middle coefficient is negative (both roots negative vs. both positive).
  • Reading the coefficient of x as the product instead of the sum of the required pair.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors: e.g. writing $(x-1)(x-2)$ for $x^2+3x+2$ instead of $(x+1)(x+2)$.
  • Forgetting to factor out a leading coefficient before applying the simple sum/product search.
  • Skipping the verification expansion and propagating a wrong factorisation into the decomposition.
  • Not checking the discriminant first and wasting time hunting for integer roots that don't exist.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL scope: partial fractions with distinct linear factors and repeated linear factors are examinable in Pure (C3/C4 and P3) for use in integration, binomial expansion, and series summation.
