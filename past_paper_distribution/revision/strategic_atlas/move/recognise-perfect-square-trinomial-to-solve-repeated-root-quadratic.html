Recognise perfect square trinomial to solve repeated-root quadratic

Family: Pattern Recognition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a quadratic $ax^2+bx+c=0$ has discriminant zero (i.e. $b^2=4ac$) and the coefficients match $(\sqrt{a}\,x \pm \sqrt{c})^2$. Typical cues: leading and constant terms are both perfect squares, and the middle coefficient equals $\pm 2\sqrt{ac}$. Common in 'hence find $r$' parts of geometric series problems, in 'show that' identities, and whenever a quadratic in $r$, $x$, or a trig expression collapses to a single value. Phrases like 'find the exact value of $r$' (singular) often hint at a repeated root.

Motivation — Why this works

A perfect square trinomial $(px+q)^2$ equals zero exactly when $px+q=0$, giving one root $x=-q/p$ directly without the quadratic formula. This is faster, avoids arithmetic with zero discriminants, and signals to the examiner that the structure has been understood. Recognising the pattern also explains why the problem has a unique solution rather than two — a fact the student must justify when checking validity (e.g. $|r|<1$).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the first and last coefficients of the quadratic are perfect squares (e.g. $4r^2$ and $1$).
  2. Rule out trying generic two-distinct-factor factorisation when the middle term equals $\pm 2\sqrt{ac}$, which signals a repeated root rather than two factors.
  3. Commit to the perfect-square form once the check $b^2-4ac=0$ confirms a single repeated root.
  4. Write the quadratic as $(\sqrt{a}\,x \pm \sqrt{c})^2 = 0$ and read off the unique solution.
  5. Validate the solution against any domain constraint (e.g. $|r|<1$ for GP convergence) and state that the repeated root means no second case to reject.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Check the discriminant: confirm $b^2-4ac=0$.
  2. Identify $\sqrt{a}$ and $\sqrt{c}$ and the sign from the middle term.
  3. Write the quadratic as $(\sqrt{a}\,x \pm \sqrt{c})^2 = 0$.
  4. Set the linear bracket to zero and solve for the variable.
  5. Verify the solution against any contextual constraint and state uniqueness.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $4r^2-4r+1=0$ for $|r|<1$. Discriminant: $(-4)^2-4(4)(1)=0$, so a repeated root. Recognise $4r^2-4r+1=(2r-1)^2$. Then $(2r-1)^2=0 \Rightarrow 2r-1=0 \Rightarrow r=\tfrac{1}{2}$. Since $|\tfrac{1}{2}|<1$, the solution is valid.

Variants — Same move, different surface

GP sum-to-infinity equations giving $9r^2-6r+1=0$
Factor as $(3r-1)^2=0$ to get $r=\tfrac{1}{3}$.
Quadratic in $\cos\theta$ such as $4\cos^2\theta-4\cos\theta+1=0$
Factor as $(2\cos\theta-1)^2=0$, then solve $\cos\theta=\tfrac{1}{2}$ in the given range.
'Show that' problems where a known value must be verified as a root
Substitute the value and demonstrate the trinomial equals $(\text{linear})^2=0$, confirming a repeated root.
Hidden quadratic via substitution, e.g. $u=e^x$ giving $u^2-2u+1=0$
Factor as $(u-1)^2=0$, then back-substitute to solve $e^x=1$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the quadratic as having two distinct factors and attempting trial-and-error factorisation that fails.
  • Applying the quadratic formula and mishandling $\sqrt{0}$, sometimes producing two 'different' roots by sign error.
  • Assuming a repeated root means 'two solutions' and listing $r=\tfrac{1}{2}$ twice as separate cases.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to check the contextual constraint (e.g. $|r|<1$) even though only one root exists.
  • Sign errors when matching the middle term: $(2r-1)^2$ vs $(2r+1)^2$ — verify by expanding.
  • Stating the discriminant is zero but failing to write the explicit factorisation, losing method marks.
  • If the quadratic had distinct roots, students sometimes carry over the 'unique solution' assumption from this pattern.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1/P2 quadratics, P2 geometric series, P3 trig equations). No content beyond standard factorisation and discriminant analysis is required.
