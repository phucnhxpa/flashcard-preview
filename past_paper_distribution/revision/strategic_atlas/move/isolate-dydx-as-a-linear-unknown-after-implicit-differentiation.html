Algebra & Functions

Isolate dy/dx as a linear unknown after implicit differentiation

Family: Linear Solve For Derivative Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires immediately after implicit differentiation produces an equation containing dy/dx in multiple terms but only to the first power. Cues: dy/dx appears as a multiplier of polynomial/exponential/trigonometric expressions in x and y, never squared or inside another function. The task asks for dy/dx explicitly, or for a gradient at a point. Phrasings include 'find dy/dx', 'show that dy/dx = ...', or 'find the gradient at (a, b)'.

Motivation — Why this works

The chain rule introduces dy/dx as a first-power factor whenever it differentiates a function of y, so any equation arising from implicit differentiation is automatically linear in dy/dx. This means dy/dx behaves exactly like an unknown x in ax + b = c: collecting it on one side, factoring it out, and dividing yields an explicit closed form. The procedure is mechanical and always succeeds, which is why every implicit differentiation question in A-level reduces to this algebraic step.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice dy/dx appears in two (or more) terms but only to the first power — no (dy/dx)^2, no dy/dx inside a function.
  2. I rule out separating variables or treating it as a differential equation: this is a finished derivative equation, not an ODE to solve.
  3. I commit to the linear-unknown view because the structure A·(dy/dx) + B·(dy/dx) + (other) = 0 is identical to ax + bx = c.
  4. First concrete operation: move every term WITHOUT dy/dx to the opposite side, keeping dy/dx terms together.
  5. Tactically, I factor dy/dx from the grouped side, divide by the bracket coefficient, then simplify (cancel common numerical factors, tidy signs).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify all terms containing dy/dx and all terms not containing it.
  2. Move non-dy/dx terms to the RHS (watch signs).
  3. Factor dy/dx out of the LHS to get (coefficient)·(dy/dx) = RHS.
  4. Divide both sides by the bracketed coefficient.
  5. Simplify: cancel common factors in numerator and denominator, tidy signs.

Worked Micro-Example

From $4x^2 + 2xy - y^2 + 10x = 0$, implicit differentiation gives $8x + 2y + 2x\tfrac{dy}{dx} - 2y\tfrac{dy}{dx} + 10 = 0$. Group: $(2x - 2y)\tfrac{dy}{dx} = -8x - 2y - 10$. Divide and simplify by 2: $\tfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{-4x - y - 5}{x - y}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Implicit equations involving e^y or sin(y)
After differentiation, terms like e^y·(dy/dx) or cos(y)·(dy/dx) appear; treat e^y or cos(y) as the coefficient of dy/dx and factor it out exactly the same way.
Parametric or related-rates equations linear in an unknown rate
Replace dy/dx with the unknown rate (e.g. dV/dt or dr/dt); collect, factor, divide identically.
Logarithmic differentiation of y = f(x)^{g(x)}
After differentiating ln y = g(x) ln f(x), the equation (1/y)·(dy/dx) = ... is already solved by multiplying through by y; same linear-isolation logic.
Inverse function derivatives via x = f(y)
Differentiating gives 1 = f'(y)·(dy/dx), a one-term linear equation; divide directly.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the equation as a differential equation to be 'solved' rather than rearranged for dy/dx.
  • Splitting the resulting rational expression into separate fractions before substituting a point, increasing algebraic clutter.
  • Believing dy/dx must be on the left from the start and trying to divide before collecting all dy/dx terms together.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when moving terms across the equation, especially negative dy/dx coefficients like -2y·(dy/dx).
  • Failing to factor dy/dx cleanly when its coefficient spans several terms.
  • Dividing by the bracket without checking it is non-zero (relevant if a point is later substituted).
  • Leaving the answer unsimplified, which is accepted but causes downstream arithmetic mistakes when evaluating gradients.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P3/P4 implicit differentiation technique; the linearity of dy/dx in the differentiated equation is guaranteed by the chain rule and is a routine post-differentiation algebraic step.
