Algebra & Functions

Substitute the derived linear relation back into the original curve equation

Family: Simultaneous Substitution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a process (setting dy/dx = 0, imposing perpendicularity, equating two curves, applying a tangency condition) yields a relationship between x and y but not the coordinates themselves. The relation is necessary but not sufficient — it describes a locus of candidates, not the actual points on the curve. Look for phrases like 'stationary points on the curve', 'where the normal meets the curve again', or 'points of intersection'. The cue is: one equation in two unknowns has been derived, and a second equation (the original curve) is still available to pin down specific coordinates.

Motivation — Why this works

A single equation in x and y defines a curve or line of possibilities; two simultaneous equations are needed to isolate discrete points. The derived relation (e.g. y = −x) and the original curve equation form exactly such a system, so their intersection gives the required points. Substituting the linear relation into the (typically quadratic or higher) curve equation collapses it to a single-variable polynomial that can be solved by standard techniques. This is the canonical method for solving a linear–nonlinear system in IAL algebra.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the derived relation y = −x alone has infinitely many solutions — it is a line, not a set of points.
  2. Rule out treating the gradient condition as the final answer, and rule out re-using the differentiated equation (which holds identically on the curve and adds no information).
  3. Commit to the original curve equation as the second, independent constraint that must also be satisfied.
  4. Bridge: since y = −x is linear, direct substitution into the curve equation is the cleanest route — it produces a single equation in x.
  5. Tactically, after solving for x, recover y from the linear relation rather than from the curve equation, to avoid spurious roots and extra work.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two equations: the original curve equation and the derived relation (here y = −x).
  2. Substitute the linear relation into every occurrence of y in the curve equation.
  3. Expand carefully, paying attention to signs (e.g. (−x)² = x², not −x²).
  4. Collect like terms to obtain a single-variable polynomial equation in x.
  5. Solve for x by factorising, square-rooting, or using the quadratic formula.
  6. Substitute each x-value back into y = −x (the simpler equation) to obtain the corresponding y.
  7. State the resulting coordinate pairs explicitly.

Worked Micro-Example

Stationary points of $x^2 + 2xy - 3y^2 + 16 = 0$ satisfy $y = -x$. Substitute: $x^2 + 2x(-x) - 3(-x)^2 + 16 = 0 \Rightarrow x^2 - 2x^2 - 3x^2 + 16 = 0 \Rightarrow -4x^2 + 16 = 0$. Hence $x^2 = 4$, so $x = \pm 2$. Using $y = -x$: stationary points are $(2,-2)$ and $(-2,2)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Normal meets curve again
The normal's equation y = mx + c plays the role of the linear relation; substitute into the curve equation and discard the known root corresponding to the original point.
Intersection of a line and a curve
Rearrange the line for y (or x) and substitute into the curve; identical mechanics, no derivative step needed.
Tangency condition
Substitute the line into the curve, then impose discriminant = 0 on the resulting quadratic instead of solving outright.
Implicit differentiation giving y = f(x)
Same template — the relation derived from setting dy/dx = 0 (or any prescribed gradient) is fed back into the original implicit equation.
Parametric curves with a Cartesian constraint
Substitute the constraint into x(t), y(t) to get an equation in t, then back-substitute for coordinates.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the derived relation y = −x as the answer, giving a line instead of points.
  • Believing the differentiated equation is a second independent equation — it is satisfied by every point on the curve and adds no new constraint.
  • Thinking substitution must go into the differentiated form rather than the original curve.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when squaring negatives: writing (−x)² as −x² instead of x².
  • Substituting into the wrong equation (the derivative relation rather than the original curve).
  • Recovering y from the curve equation rather than the linear relation, introducing extra cases or spurious pairings.
  • Failing to pair x and y values correctly when listing the final coordinates.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure scope: solving a linear–quadratic simultaneous system arises in C12 (lines meeting curves) and C34 (implicit differentiation, normals, stationary points on implicit curves).
