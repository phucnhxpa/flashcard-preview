Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a quadratic expression in one or two variables appears with linear terms still present and a structural feature (vertex, minimum/maximum, circle centre/radius) needs to be read off explicitly. Visual cues: (i) a single-variable quadratic ax² + bx + c whose vertex/turning point is requested; (ii) a two-variable equation x² + y² + Dx + Ey + F = 0 with equal coefficients on x² and y² and no xy cross-term — a circle in general form; (iii) any rearrangement that has been divided through by a leading coefficient leaving x² + 2x + … or similar. Alternative phrasings of the trigger: 'find the minimum value', 'state the centre and radius', 'write in the form (x−h)² + k', 'show that the locus is a circle'.

Motivation — Why this works

Completing the square exploits the identity x² + 2hx = (x+h)² − h² to convert an implicit/general-form quadratic into explicit vertex form a(x−h)² + k or standard circle form (x−h)² + (y−k)² = r². Once in that form, the geometric features (vertex, minimum value, centre, radius) can be read off directly with no calculus. The underlying principle is that any monic quadratic is a perfect square shifted by a constant, so the minimum (or maximum) is achieved exactly when the squared bracket vanishes. This is the standard structural rewrite that exam mark schemes reward.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the surface form: a quadratic with linear terms that obscure the structural information the question needs (vertex, centre, radius).
  2. Rule out alternatives: differentiation (dy/dx = 0) gives the vertex but is heavier and may be out of scope or unrewarded; the formula x = −b/(2a) needs a back-substitution and hides structure; factorising won't expose a vertex or radius.
  3. Commit to completing the square because it produces vertex/standard form in one algebraic move and matches what mark schemes typically demand.
  4. Bridge to operation: first normalise — if a ≠ 1 (single variable) or if x² and y² have a common coefficient ≠ 1, divide through so the squared terms are monic.
  5. Tactical decision: take half the coefficient of each linear term, square it, add-and-subtract that constant; for two-variable circles, do this independently for x and y, then collect constants on the right-hand side.
  6. Read off the answer: vertex (h, k) from a(x−h)² + k; centre (h, k) and radius √(RHS) from (x−h)² + (y−k)² = r².

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Normalise: if necessary, divide the whole equation by the leading coefficient so x² (and y²) are monic.
  2. For each variable with a linear term coefficient b, write x² + bx = (x + b/2)² − (b/2)².
  3. Substitute back and simplify the leftover constants.
  4. Rearrange into standard form: y = a(x − h)² + k, or (x − h)² + (y − k)² = r².
  5. Read off vertex (h, k), or centre (h, k) and radius r = √(RHS).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $3x^2 + 6x + 3y^2 = 9$: divide by 3 to get $x^2 + 2x + y^2 = 3$. Complete the square in $x$: $(x+1)^2 - 1 + y^2 = 3$, so $(x+1)^2 + y^2 = 4$. Centre $(-1, 0)$, radius $2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Single-variable quadratic with a ≠ 1, e.g. y = 2x² − 8x + 5
Factor a out of the x-terms first: y = 2(x² − 4x) + 5 = 2[(x−2)² − 4] + 5 = 2(x−2)² − 3. Vertex (2, −3).
Circle in general form with both x and y linear terms, e.g. x² + y² + Dx + Ey + F = 0
Complete the square in x and y independently: (x + D/2)² + (y + E/2)² = D²/4 + E²/4 − F.
Möbius/locus problem giving u² + v² + (linear in u,v) = 0
Same procedure; equal coefficients on u² and v² are guaranteed because Möbius maps send circles/lines to circles/lines.
Maximum point (a < 0)
Same completed-square form a(x−h)² + k applies; since a < 0 the squared term is subtracted, so (h, k) is a maximum.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the equation as needing factorisation rather than rewriting — students try to factor x² + 2x + y² − 3, which doesn't expose centre/radius.
  • Jumping to differentiation when the mark scheme explicitly demands the completed-square form, losing method marks.
  • Mistaking a non-monic equation (e.g. 3x² + 3y² + …) for a circle in standard form without first dividing through.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to divide by the leading coefficient before completing the square, giving an incorrect radius or vertex constant.
  • Sign error on the centre/vertex coordinate: (x + 5/2)² has h = −5/2, not +5/2.
  • Forgetting to subtract the (b/2)² that was added inside the bracket, producing a wrong constant term k.
  • Failing to simplify surd radii, e.g. leaving √(20/9) instead of (2√5)/3.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure 1 (quadratics, vertex form) and Pure 2 (circle geometry). No formula booklet entry is required — completing the square is an expected algebraic technique. Avoid generalising to non-circular conics (ellipses/hyperbolas with unequal x²,y² coefficients) unless the question is in Further Pure.
