Algebra & Functions

Rearrange to one side and factor out the common variable instead of dividing

Family: Preserve Solutions Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an equation has the form $A(x)\cdot f(x) = A(x)\cdot g(x)$ where $A(x)$ is a non-constant expression (typically $x$, $\sin x$, $(x-a)$, etc.) appearing as a factor on both sides. Common surface cues: 'find the points of intersection of $y_1$ and $y_2$', 'solve $x\,p(x) = x\,q(x)$', or any equation where cancelling looks tempting. The student should hear an internal alarm whenever they are about to divide both sides by something that could be zero.

Motivation — Why this works

Dividing both sides of an equation by an expression $A(x)$ silently assumes $A(x)\neq 0$, so any root of $A(x)=0$ is discarded. Moving everything to one side and factoring keeps the equation as a product equal to zero, which by the zero-product principle yields all roots — those from $A(x)=0$ and those from the remaining factor. This matters especially in intersection problems, where $A(x)=0$ usually corresponds to a genuine intersection point (e.g. the origin) that must be reported.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the same factor (here $x$) multiplying both sides of the equation.
  2. Rules out dividing by that factor, recognising it could equal zero and that root would be lost.
  3. Commits to the 'all-to-one-side, then factor' route because it preserves every solution and matches the standard zero-product workflow.
  4. Bridges by subtracting one side from the other to form $\text{LHS}-\text{RHS}=0$, keeping the common factor visible.
  5. Tactically decides to factor the common variable out first, then expand and simplify only inside the bracket, leaving a quadratic (or lower) to solve.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Set the two expressions equal: $x(3-x) = x(x-2)(5-x)$.
  2. Move all terms to one side: $x(3-x) - x(x-2)(5-x) = 0$.
  3. Factor out the common $x$: $x\big[(3-x) - (x-2)(5-x)\big] = 0$.
  4. Expand the inner product: $(x-2)(5-x) = -x^2 + 7x - 10$.
  5. Simplify the bracket: $(3-x) - (-x^2 + 7x - 10) = x^2 - 8x + 13$.
  6. Write final factored form: $x(x^2 - 8x + 13) = 0$, then solve each factor.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $x(x+1) = x(2x-3)$. Rearrange: $x(x+1) - x(2x-3) = 0$, so $x\big[(x+1)-(2x-3)\big] = 0$, giving $x(4-x)=0$. Hence $x=0$ or $x=4$. Note that dividing by $x$ at the start would have lost the $x=0$ root.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Trigonometric equation $\sin x \cdot f(x) = \sin x \cdot g(x)$ on a given interval.
Rearrange to $\sin x\,[f(x)-g(x)] = 0$; solve $\sin x = 0$ and $f(x)=g(x)$ separately, keeping all roots in the interval.
Equation with a linear common factor, e.g. $(x-2)p(x) = (x-2)q(x)$.
Move to one side and factor: $(x-2)[p(x)-q(x)] = 0$, retaining $x=2$ as a solution.
Exponential common factor, e.g. $e^{x}f(x)=e^{x}g(x)$.
Here $e^x$ is never zero, so dividing is safe — but the equivalent rearrangement $e^{x}[f(x)-g(x)]=0$ still works and reduces directly to $f(x)=g(x)$.
Intersection of a curve with the x-axis written as $x\cdot h(x) = 0$ form already.
No rearrangement needed — read off $x=0$ and the roots of $h(x)=0$ immediately.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'cancel the common $x$' as algebraically harmless, the same way one cancels a non-zero constant.
  • Believing the lost root $x=0$ is 'trivial' and not worth reporting, when it is in fact a required intersection point.
  • Confusing this with cancelling a factor in an identity (where the factor is genuinely non-zero), and assuming the same licence in an equation.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when distributing the minus across an expanded product, e.g. $-(-x^2+7x-10)$ becoming $-x^2-7x+10$.
  • Forgetting to list $x=0$ (or whichever root the common factor gives) in the final answer after correctly factoring.
  • Expanding the whole equation before factoring, which buries the common $x$ and loses the structural shortcut.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths: applies to polynomial intersection problems (P1–P3) and to trigonometric equations in P2/P3 where common factors like $\sin x$ or $\cos x$ appear. No content beyond standard A-level algebra is required.
