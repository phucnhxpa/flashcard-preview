Trigger — When to use

Fires when a region bounded by two concentric (or co-centred) circular arcs and connecting straight edges is presented — e.g. annular sectors, fan blades, petal/segment arrangements, or 'sections cut from a ring'. Cues include phrases like 'find the perimeter of the shaded region', a diagram showing inner and outer arcs of differing radii, and explicit mention of multiple identical sections. Also triggers when the region is the difference of two sectors sharing a vertex/centre, where the perimeter is not the boundary of a single sector.

Motivation — Why this works

The perimeter of a closed planar region is the sum of the lengths of all distinct boundary pieces, traversed once. For an annular-sector-type region the boundary naturally splits into circular pieces (arcs of radius r) and rectilinear pieces (radial line segments of length r_outer − r_inner). Decomposing forces a complete inventory and prevents either missing the radial 'connectors' or double-counting shared edges between adjacent sections.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the boundary is piecewise: some pieces are curved (arcs of two different radii) and some are straight (radial segments).
  2. Rule out treating the figure as a single sector or using a closed-form 'annular sector' formula, since there may be several disjoint sections each contributing their own boundary.
  3. Commit to a parts-list approach: count inner arcs, outer arcs, and radial edges separately, multiplying by the number of identical sections.
  4. Bridge to computation by writing arc lengths via s = rθ (θ in radians) and each radial edge as r_outer − r_inner.
  5. Inside the move, decide whether neighbouring sections share radial edges (merge them) or are separated (each section contributes 2 radial edges); for n disjoint sections expect 2n radial edges.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify n = number of identical sections, r_inner, r_outer, and angle θ (radians) of each section.
  2. Compute inner arc length per section: L_in = r_inner · θ; outer arc: L_out = r_outer · θ.
  3. Compute radial edge length: d = r_outer − r_inner.
  4. Count edges: n inner arcs + n outer arcs + 2n radial segments (if sections are disjoint).
  5. Sum: P = n·L_in + n·L_out + 2n·d, keeping arc lengths in exact form (multiples of π) until the final step.

Worked Micro-Example

Three identical fan sections share a centre, with r_inner = 9, r_outer = 84, and each section subtending θ such that L_in = 15 and L_out ≈ 35.93. Then d = 84 − 9 = 75. Perimeter P = 3(15) + 3(35.93) + 6(75) = 45 + 107.79 + 450 ≈ 602.79, so P ≈ 603 cm.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Single annular sector (one section between two radii)
Use n = 1: P = r_inner θ + r_outer θ + 2(r_outer − r_inner).
Adjacent sections sharing radial edges (e.g. ring divided into n contiguous wedges)
Shared radial edges are interior, not boundary; count only the outer-rim radials, or none if the ring is complete.
Sector minus an inscribed triangle or chord region
Replace the inner arc with the chord length 2r sin(θ/2); the radial edges remain r.
Region bounded by an arc and a chord (circular segment)
Perimeter = rθ + 2r sin(θ/2); no radial edges, no inner arc.
Composite shape with arcs of different angles on inner vs outer boundary
Compute each arc with its own (r, θ); radial connectors may not be purely radial — check geometry before using r_outer − r_inner.

Common Misreadings

  • Using the full radius (r_outer or r_inner) for the straight edges rather than the difference r_outer − r_inner.
  • Forgetting the radial segments entirely and summing only arc lengths.
  • Treating the boundary as that of a full sector of radius r_outer, ignoring the inner cut-out.
  • Counting only n radial edges instead of 2n when the n sections are disjoint.

Pitfalls

  • Miscounting radial segments: for n disjoint sections there are 2n, not n.
  • Using θ in degrees in s = rθ; the formula requires radians.
  • Rounding arc lengths early (e.g. 35.9 instead of 35.929…) and propagating an error of 1 cm into the final answer.
  • Double-counting an edge that is shared between two adjacent sections.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the IAL P2/P3 arc-length formula s = rθ with θ in radians and elementary perimeter addition; no integration or vector calculus is required, so this remains within Edexcel IAL Maths/Further Maths scope.
