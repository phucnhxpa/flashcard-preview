Algebra & Functions

Clear denominators by multiplying through by the LCM

Family: Scaling For Integrality Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation (or inequality) carries fractional coefficients but the question demands integer coefficients, or asks for the 'simplest' form, or wants a polynomial 'with integer coefficients' for which a given value is a root. Common phrasings include 'find integers a, b, c such that ax² + bx + c = 0', 'write in the form …' with integer slots, or 'show that x satisfies …'. The cue is visual: at least one term has a denominator other than 1.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying every term of an equation by a fixed nonzero constant k preserves the solution set, since p(x)=0 ⇔ k·p(x)=0. Choosing k as the LCM of all denominators eliminates every fraction in a single step, producing the unique smallest integer-coefficient equivalent (up to sign). This is preferred to multiplying by an arbitrary common multiple because it yields the simplest, lowest-magnitude integer coefficients required by the mark scheme.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation has fractional coefficients and the question stipulates integer coefficients.
  2. Rule out rearranging or factoring first — fractions block clean integer factorisation and the constraint is purely cosmetic, not structural.
  3. Commit to scaling the whole equation by a constant, since this preserves roots and is the standard route to integerise.
  4. Identify the LCM of all denominators as the minimal positive multiplier (here all denominators are 3, so k = 3).
  5. Apply k to every term — including the constant and the right-hand side — and check the result has gcd 1 across coefficients so no further simplification is possible.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. List all denominators appearing in the equation.
  2. Compute their LCM; call it k.
  3. Multiply every term on both sides of the equation by k.
  4. Simplify each term to remove the denominators.
  5. Check the resulting integer coefficients share no common factor (otherwise divide through).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $x^2 - \tfrac{4}{3}x - \tfrac{32}{3} = 0$, all denominators are $3$, so multiply through by $3$: $$3x^2 - 4x - 32 = 0.$$ The coefficients $3, -4, -32$ share no common factor, so this is the required integer-coefficient form.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Mixed denominators, e.g. $\tfrac{1}{2}x^2 + \tfrac{1}{3}x - \tfrac{1}{6} = 0$
Use the LCM of $\{2,3,6\}$, which is $6$, giving $3x^2 + 2x - 1 = 0$.
Equation arises from substitution $y = ax + b$ producing fractions
After substituting and expanding, scale by the LCM of denominators introduced by $a$.
Forming a polynomial with integer coefficients having a given rational root $p/q$
Start from $(qx - p)$ as the factor rather than $(x - p/q)$; this avoids fractions altogether.
Inequality with fractional coefficients
Same multiplier rule applies, but ensure $k > 0$ so the inequality direction is preserved.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the integer-coefficient demand as optional and leaving fractional answers.
  • Believing scaling changes the roots and so refusing to multiply through.
  • Confusing 'integer coefficients' with 'integer roots' and trying to factor prematurely.

Pitfalls

  • Multiplying only the fractional terms and leaving integer terms unscaled, breaking equality.
  • Forgetting to multiply the right-hand side (especially when it is $0$ this is fine, but nonzero RHSs are often overlooked).
  • Using an unnecessarily large multiplier (e.g. $9$ instead of $3$) and producing a non-simplest form.
  • Sign errors when distributing the multiplier across negative fractional terms.

A-level Scope Notes

Routine in Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1–P4) and Further Pure when forming quadratics/polynomials with integer coefficients, especially after using sum/product of roots or substitution methods.
