Integration

Spot [f(t)]^n · f'(t) and integrate by reverse chain rule

Family: Substitution By Recognition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integrand contains a power of a function multiplied by that function's derivative — most commonly sin^n t · cos t, cos^n t · sin t, tan^n t · sec^2 t, or (ax+b)^n · a. The cue is seeing one trig (or simple) function raised to a power sitting next to its exact derivative, possibly with a constant scalar out front. Phrased differently: the integrand factors as g(f(t)) · f'(t) where g is a pure power. No partial fractions, no parts, no identity rewriting is needed.

Motivation — Why this works

If u = f(t), then du = f'(t) dt, so ∫[f(t)]^n f'(t) dt = ∫u^n du = u^(n+1)/(n+1) + C. This is just the chain rule applied in reverse, and once recognised it bypasses a formal written substitution. For sin^2 t · cos t the antiderivative is therefore sin^3 t / 3, obtained instantly by the power rule on u = sin t.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice a power of sin t (here sin^2 t) sitting next to a single cos t — the derivative of sin t.
  2. I rule out trig identities (no need to convert sin^2 t = (1−cos 2t)/2) and rule out integration by parts — the structure is simpler than that.
  3. I commit because the integrand exactly matches the [f(t)]^n · f'(t) template with f(t) = sin t, n = 2.
  4. Bridge: mentally set u = sin t, du = cos t dt, so the integral becomes ∫u^2 du = u^3/3.
  5. Tactically, I keep the constant 32 outside, write the antiderivative as (32/3) sin^3 t, then substitute the limits, taking care to cube sin(π/4) correctly.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify f(t) = sin t and confirm f'(t) = cos t appears as a factor.
  2. Apply reverse chain rule: ∫sin^2 t · cos t dt = sin^3 t / 3 (+C).
  3. Reattach the constant: 32 · sin^3 t / 3 evaluated between the given limits.
  4. Substitute t = π/4: sin(π/4) = √2/2, so sin^3(π/4) = (√2/2)^3 = 2√2/8 = √2/4.
  5. Compute (32/3)(√2/4) − 0 = 8√2/3.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_0^{\pi/4} 32\sin^2 t\cos t\,dt$. Recognise $\sin^2 t\cos t = u^2\,du$ with $u=\sin t$. So $\int 32\sin^2 t\cos t\,dt = \tfrac{32}{3}\sin^3 t$. Then $\tfrac{32}{3}\big[\sin^3 t\big]_0^{\pi/4} = \tfrac{32}{3}\cdot\tfrac{\sqrt{2}}{4} = \tfrac{8\sqrt{2}}{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

∫cos^n t · sin t dt
Use u = cos t; du = −sin t dt introduces a minus sign, giving −cos^(n+1) t/(n+1).
∫tan^n t · sec^2 t dt
Use u = tan t; du = sec^2 t dt, giving tan^(n+1) t/(n+1).
∫sec^n t · sec t tan t dt
Use u = sec t; du = sec t tan t dt, giving sec^(n+1) t/(n+1).
∫(ax+b)^n dx
Linear inner function: divide by extra factor a, giving (ax+b)^(n+1)/[a(n+1)].
∫[f(x)]^n f'(x) dx in general
Same template; the trig case is just the most common A-level surface.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating sin^2 t cos t as needing the double-angle identity sin^2 t = (1−cos 2t)/2 — overcomplicates a direct pattern.
  • Mistaking it for an integration-by-parts integrand because two trig factors are present.
  • Reading the power as applying to the whole product rather than to sin t alone.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to cube sin(π/4): computing (√2/2)^2 = 1/2 instead of (√2/2)^3 = √2/4.
  • Mishandling (√2)^3: writing it as 2 or √8 rather than 2√2.
  • Dropping the 1/(n+1) factor — writing sin^3 t instead of sin^3 t / 3.
  • Sign error in the cos^n t · sin t variant from overlooking du = −sin t dt.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (C4/P3 integration techniques): reverse chain rule on standard trig forms and on linear inner functions, with no need for formal u-substitution writeup.
