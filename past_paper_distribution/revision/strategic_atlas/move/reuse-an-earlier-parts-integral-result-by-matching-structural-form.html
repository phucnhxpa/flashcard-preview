Integration

Reuse an earlier part's integral result by matching structural form

Family: Cross Part Linkage Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

After setting up an integral in a later part of a multi-part question (e.g. from separation of variables, an arc-length formula, or a surface-area formula), you notice the resulting integrand matches — up to a change of dummy variable, a substitution, or a numerical index — an integral already evaluated or a reduction formula already established in part (a) or (b). Visual cues include: identical functional form (e.g. $x^n\cos x$ becoming $t^n\cos t$), matching limits, or an integrand that simplifies to $I_n$ for a specific $n$. Equivalent phrasings: 'this integral is part (a) in disguise', 'the examiner has scaffolded this result for me', 'the dummy letter has changed but the antiderivative is the same'.

Motivation — Why this works

Multi-part exam questions are deliberately scaffolded so that earlier results feed forward into later geometric or differential-equation contexts. Recognising that the new integral is structurally identical to the earlier one means you can quote the antiderivative or numerical value directly, saving time, avoiding fresh integration-by-parts errors, and confirming you are on the intended solution pathway. The underlying principle is that a definite or indefinite integral depends only on the integrand and limits, not on the name of the dummy variable — so $\int f(x)\,dx$ and $\int f(t)\,dt$ have the same antiderivative form.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that after the algebraic setup (separation of variables, arc-length simplification, or surface-area formula), the integral on the page has the same shape as the integral from part (a) or (b).
  2. Rule out starting integration by parts or a reduction-formula derivation from scratch — that would duplicate work the examiner has already credited in an earlier part.
  3. Commit to the cross-part link: the dummy variable might be different ($t$ vs $x$), or a substitution might be needed first, but the antiderivative structure is identical.
  4. Bridge to operation: write the earlier result with the new variable name substituted in, or quote the numerical $I_n$ value, treating it as a known quantity.
  5. Tactically decide whether you need the indefinite form (with $+C$) or a definite numerical value, and carry through any constant prefactors (e.g. $2\pi$ for surface area, factor of $\tfrac12$ from $y^2/2$).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write out the new integral explicitly, e.g. $\int t^2\cos t\,dt$ or $2\pi\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$.
  2. Identify the matching earlier result: e.g. part (a) gave $\int x^2\cos x\,dx = x^2\sin x + 2x\cos x - 2\sin x + C$, or part (b) gave $I_n = \text{value}$.
  3. Substitute the dummy variable (or apply any required substitution first) so the form matches exactly.
  4. Quote the earlier result directly, transcribing carefully to avoid sign errors.
  5. Multiply through by any outstanding constant factor (e.g. the $2$ from $y^2/2$, or the $2\pi$ from a surface-area formula) and add the new constant of integration $A$ if indefinite.

Worked Micro-Example

Part (a) established $\int x^2\cos x\,dx = x^2\sin x + 2x\cos x - 2\sin x + C$. In part (b), separation of variables gives $\tfrac{1}{2}y^2 = \int t^2\cos t\,dt$. Recognising the same form, write $\tfrac{1}{2}y^2 = t^2\sin t + 2t\cos t - 2\sin t + C$, then $y^2 = 2t^2\sin t + 4t\cos t - 4\sin t + A$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Differential-equation context after separation of variables
The earlier integral appears with a renamed dummy variable; substitute the new letter into the antiderivative and absorb the factor of $\tfrac12$ (or similar) by multiplying through.
Arc-length or surface-area integral matching a reduction-formula value $I_n$
After simplifying the integrand (often via a perfect-square step), substitute the numerical $I_n$ from part (b) and remember the geometric prefactor (e.g. $2\pi$).
Connection visible only after a substitution
Apply a preliminary substitution (e.g. $u = \pi/2 - x$) to expose the matching form before quoting the earlier result.
Surface-area integral splitting into two terms
Each term matches a different $I_n$; substitute both values and combine.

Common Misreadings

  • Failing to spot that the dummy variable change is cosmetic, and redoing full integration by parts from scratch.
  • Misidentifying which $n$ in the reduction formula is needed because a power shifted during simplification.
  • Treating the new integral as unrelated because the surrounding context (geometry vs ODE) looks different.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when transcribing the earlier antiderivative into the new context — always re-check each term.
  • Forgetting outstanding constant prefactors such as $2\pi$, $\tfrac12$, or coefficients from arc-length simplification.
  • Applying the reduction formula at the wrong index $n$ if the integrand's power changed during the setup.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) and Pure (P3) scope: integration by parts, reduction formulae, separation of variables, and arc-length/surface-area formulae are all in the formula booklet. Do not generalise to non-elementary integrals or to changes of variable that require Jacobians beyond a single substitution.
