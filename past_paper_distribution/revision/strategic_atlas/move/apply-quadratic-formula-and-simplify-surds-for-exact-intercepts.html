Coordinate Geometry

Apply quadratic formula and simplify surds for exact intercepts

Family: Quadratic Formula Surds Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when substituting x=0 (or y=0) into a curve/circle equation produces a quadratic in one variable whose discriminant is positive but not a perfect square, and the question demands exact coordinates. Cues include phrases like 'find the exact coordinates', 'give your answer in the form a ± √b', or a circle equation with non-integer-friendly constants. Also triggered when factoring is attempted and fails (no integer pair of factors works). The exact-form requirement rules out decimal approximations.

Motivation — Why this works

A quadratic with non-square discriminant has irrational roots that must be expressed using surds to remain exact. The quadratic formula y = (−b ± √(b²−4ac))/(2a) delivers these roots directly, and simplifying √(b²−4ac) by extracting square factors often allows cancellation with the denominator 2a, producing a clean form a ± √b. This matches the standard exact-answer presentation expected in Edexcel mark schemes.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the quadratic in y (after setting x=0) does not factor over the integers — no integer pair multiplies to c and sums to b.
  2. Rules out numerical/decimal solving because the question specifies 'exact' coordinates.
  3. Commits to the quadratic formula (or completing the square) as the route to surd-form roots.
  4. Computes the discriminant carefully and checks whether the surd simplifies via a square factor (e.g. 28 = 4·7).
  5. Cancels the common factor between numerator and denominator 2a to land on the tidy form a ± √b, then writes the two intercept points.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Substitute x = 0 (or y = 0) to reduce the curve equation to a quadratic in one variable.
  2. Check factorability quickly; if it fails, apply the quadratic formula y = (−b ± √(b²−4ac))/(2a).
  3. Compute the discriminant b² − 4ac.
  4. Simplify the surd by extracting the largest square factor (e.g. √28 = 2√7).
  5. Cancel any common numerical factor with the denominator 2a.
  6. State the intercept points as ordered pairs with the fixed coordinate (0 or 0 in the other slot).

Worked Micro-Example

Find where the circle $x^2 + y^2 + 8y + 9 = 0$ meets the y-axis. Set $x=0$: $y^2 + 8y + 9 = 0$. Then $y = \dfrac{-8 \pm \sqrt{64-36}}{2} = \dfrac{-8 \pm \sqrt{28}}{2} = \dfrac{-8 \pm 2\sqrt{7}}{2} = -4 \pm \sqrt{7}$. So the intercepts are $S(0,\,-4+\sqrt{7})$ and $T(0,\,-4-\sqrt{7})$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

x-intercepts of a circle or parabola
Set y = 0 instead and solve the resulting quadratic in x by the same formula-and-surd procedure.
Intersection of a line y = mx + c with a circle
Substitute to get a quadratic in x; if the discriminant is non-square, apply the formula and simplify surds to give exact intersection coordinates.
Roots of a quadratic with leading coefficient ≠ 1
The denominator becomes 2a; ensure the surd's extracted factor cancels with 2a (e.g. √20/4 = 2√5/4 = √5/2).
Completing the square as alternative
Write $(y+4)^2 = 7$, giving $y = -4 \pm \sqrt{7}$ — equivalent route, often faster when b is even.
Tangency check / single intercept
If discriminant = 0, there is one repeated root and one intercept point (curve touches the axis).

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'exact' as 'to 3 s.f.' and giving decimal approximations.
  • Thinking the quadratic must factor and abandoning the problem when it doesn't.
  • Confusing the discriminant condition: assuming non-square discriminant means no real roots.

Pitfalls

  • Computing the discriminant as b² − 4c instead of b² − 4ac when a = 1 is implicit but mis-handled with other coefficients.
  • Failing to simplify √28 to 2√7 before dividing, leaving the answer as (−8 ± √28)/2.
  • Cancelling only one term of the numerator with the denominator (e.g. writing −4 ± 2√7 instead of −4 ± √7).
  • Sign error on −b when b is negative.
  • Swapping coordinates: writing (−4+√7, 0) for a y-intercept.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 scope: quadratic formula, surd simplification, and circle/line intersection problems all appear in the Pure Mathematics specification.
