Algebra & Functions

Isolate the variable from the simpler equation and substitute to eliminate it

Family: Elimination By Substitution Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

Two simultaneous equations in two unknowns where the target/answer involves only one of the variables, so the other must be eliminated. One equation is structurally simpler in a chosen variable — typically linear or with the variable as an isolated term — while the other is higher degree (quadratic, cubic) or has the variable embedded in a product. Surface cues include: a linear-quadratic pair (line meets circle), a perimeter/area pair, an arithmetic/geometric series pair giving na = k and n(n−1)a² = m, or any system where rearranging one equation for a variable takes a single clean step. Equivalently: 'one equation gives y = (clean polynomial/rational expression) in one move'.

Motivation — Why this works

Substitution converts a 2-variable system into a single-variable equation that standard tools (factorising, quadratic formula, polynomial roots) can finish. Choosing the simpler equation to rearrange minimises algebraic mess: a linear equation gives a polynomial expression, whereas isolating from a quadratic introduces square roots or rational expressions with hidden domain restrictions (e.g. dividing by x assumes x ≠ 0). The principle is that adding/subtracting equations cannot eliminate a variable when the equations have different degrees in that variable, so substitution is the natural — often the only clean — route.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the goal equation or required answer involves only one variable, so the other must be eliminated entirely.
  2. Rule out adding/subtracting the equations: the variable to be eliminated appears at different degrees (e.g. linear in one, quadratic or inside a product in the other), so direct combination won't cancel it cleanly.
  3. Compare the two equations side-by-side: which one isolates a variable in a single, clean step? Linear terms beat quadratic terms; isolated terms beat embedded products; polynomial expressions beat surd or rational expressions.
  4. Commit to rearranging the simpler equation. Verify the substitution will not introduce surds, rational expressions with the eliminated variable in the denominator, or assumptions like x ≠ 0.
  5. Substitute into EVERY occurrence of the eliminated variable in the other equation — including coefficients of squared terms — then expand carefully and collect like terms to obtain a single-variable equation.
  6. Solve the resulting single-variable equation, then back-substitute each solution into the rearranged equation to recover the corresponding values of the eliminated variable.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the simpler equation (linear / variable-as-isolated-term) and rearrange it to give variable = expression, e.g. y = f(x) or a = −6/n.
  2. Substitute this expression into the other equation, replacing every occurrence of the eliminated variable.
  3. Expand brackets carefully — squaring and distributing — and combine like terms to reach a polynomial equation in one variable.
  4. Solve that single-variable equation by factorising, quadratic formula, or polynomial root methods.
  5. Back-substitute each solution into the rearranged simple equation to obtain the matching values of the other variable.
  6. Check the solution pairs satisfy the ORIGINAL equations (catches sign and arithmetic slips).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $y - 3x = 4$ and $x^2 + y^2 = 20$.\n Step 1: Rearrange the linear equation: $y = 3x + 4$.\n Step 2: Substitute: $x^2 + (3x+4)^2 = 20 \Rightarrow x^2 + 9x^2 + 24x + 16 = 20$.\n Step 3: Simplify: $10x^2 + 24x - 4 = 0 \Rightarrow 5x^2 + 12x - 2 = 0$.\n Step 4: Solve for $x$, then $y = 3x+4$ gives the matching values.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Linear-quadratic system (line meets circle/parabola)
Rearrange the linear equation for whichever variable has coefficient ±1; substitute into the quadratic to obtain a quadratic in the remaining variable.
Two quadratic equations (e.g. two circles)
First subtract one equation from the other to eliminate the squared terms, producing a linear equation; then apply this substitution move as usual.
Geometry-based pair such as perimeter/area of a sector: $r(\theta+2)=P$ and $r^2\theta=A$
Isolate $r$ from the linear (perimeter) equation as $r = P/(\theta+2)$ to avoid a square root, then substitute into the area equation.
Series coefficient pair $na = k,\; n(n-1)a^2 = m$ (binomial expansion problems)
Isolate $a = k/n$ from the linear relation and substitute; the $n^2$ in $a^2$ cancels with the $n$ factor, giving a linear equation in $n$.
Linear-cubic system
Same substitution strategy; the resulting single-variable equation is a cubic, solved by factor theorem or rational root search.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to rearrange the harder equation for a variable, producing rational/surd expressions and tangled algebra (e.g. solving $2xy - 3x^2 = 50$ for $y$ to get $(50+3x^2)/(2x)$).
  • Attempting to add/subtract equations of different degree, which cannot eliminate a variable.
  • Treating a circle-and-line problem geometrically (centre/radius reasoning) when exact coordinates are required — valid but less efficient.
  • Dividing one equation by the other to cancel a variable; works occasionally but invites slips with the resulting compound fraction.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to substitute into EVERY occurrence of the eliminated variable (e.g. missing a $-4y$ term).
  • Sign and squaring errors when expanding $(3x+4)^2$ or $\left(\tfrac{10}{\theta+2}\right)^2$ — both numerator and denominator must be squared.
  • Distributive errors such as multiplying $2x \cdot (-6x)$ to give $-6x^2$ instead of $-12x^2$.
  • When rearranging by dividing, forgetting to record the assumption that the divisor is non-zero (e.g. $x \neq 0$) and losing a potential solution.
  • Producing $x$-values but forgetting to back-substitute to find matching $y$-values, or pairing them up incorrectly.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure (P1–P4) technique for simultaneous equations; no formula booklet entry is invoked. Stays well within scope for line-circle intersections (P1/P2), tangent/normal problems, sector mensuration (P2), and binomial-series coefficient problems (P2). Avoid generalising to systems requiring resultants or Gröbner bases — these are out of scope.
