Trigger — When to use

A later part of a question gives a transformed curve such as $y = af(x-b)+c$ or $y = af(bx)$ and asks for its stationary points (or roots, turning points, asymptotes), after an earlier part already located these features on $y=f(x)$. Cues include phrases like 'hence find', 'state the coordinates of the stationary points of', or 'using your answer to part (b)'. The original $f(x)$ is typically messy (product, quotient, exponential, logarithmic) so re-differentiating would be costly. This is the signal to use transformation geometry rather than calculus a second time.

Motivation — Why this works

Stationary points are specific $(x,y)$ pairs whose location moves predictably under graph transformations: replacing $x$ by $x-b$ shifts $x$-coordinates by $+b$, multiplying the output by $a$ scales $y$-coordinates by $a$, and these operations preserve the stationary property because $\frac{d}{dx}[af(x-b)] = af'(x-b)$ vanishes exactly where $f'$ vanished. The chain factor is a non-zero constant, so no new stationary points appear and none disappear. This converts a calculus problem into pure coordinate arithmetic, saving time and reducing algebraic error.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the new curve is written explicitly as a transformation of $f$, and the previous part already gave stationary points of $y=f(x)$.
  2. Rule out re-differentiating: substituting and applying product/chain rule to a messy $f$ would duplicate work already done.
  3. Commit to the transformation route once you confirm the outer constant is non-zero (so stationary points are preserved, not destroyed) and the inner change of variable is linear.
  4. Bridge: decode each piece of the expression as a single geometric operation — inner $x\mapsto x-b$ is a horizontal translation, outer $\times a$ is a vertical stretch.
  5. Tactically, apply horizontal changes to $x$-coordinates only and vertical changes to $y$-coordinates only; check any cancellations (e.g. an outer $8$ against a denominator $8$) as a self-verification.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Decode the transformation: identify the horizontal shift/stretch from the inner argument and the vertical shift/stretch from the outer operations.
  2. For each known stationary point $(x_0,y_0)$, compute the new $x$-coordinate by applying only the horizontal transformation.
  3. Compute the new $y$-coordinate by applying only the vertical transformation to $y_0$.
  4. Simplify and check that any obvious cancellations occur as expected.
  5. State the transformed coordinates as the stationary points of the new curve.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose $y=f(x)$ has stationary points $(-\tfrac12,0)$ and $(\tfrac14,\tfrac{27}{8e})$. For $y=8f(x-2)$: shift $x$ by $+2$ and multiply $y$ by $8$. First point: $(-\tfrac12+2,\,8\cdot 0)=(\tfrac32,0)$. Second point: $(\tfrac14+2,\,8\cdot\tfrac{27}{8e})=(\tfrac94,\tfrac{27}{e})$, with the $8$ cancelling cleanly.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$y = f(x+b)+c$
Shift $x$-coordinates by $-b$ and add $c$ to $y$-coordinates.
$y = f(kx)$ with $k>0$
Divide $x$-coordinates by $k$ (horizontal stretch by factor $1/k$); leave $y$-coordinates unchanged.
$y = -f(x)$ or $y = f(-x)$
Negate $y$-coordinates, or negate $x$-coordinates, respectively; stationary points stay stationary.
Roots/asymptotes/intercepts of a transformed curve
Same coordinate-mapping idea — transform the known feature points directly rather than re-solving.
Combined $y = af(x-b)+c$
Apply horizontal shift to $x$, then scale $y$ by $a$, then add $c$ — vertical operations are not independent of order when both stretch and shift are present.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading $f(x-2)$ as a shift LEFT by 2 instead of RIGHT by 2.
  • Treating the outer constant as if it shifted the curve vertically rather than stretching it.
  • Believing the stretch alters the $x$-coordinate of stationary points (it does not).
  • Confusing the order of horizontal and vertical operations when shifts and stretches are combined.

Pitfalls

  • Multiplying the $x$-coordinate by the outer constant by mistake.
  • Subtracting instead of adding $2$ to $x$ for $f(x-2)$.
  • Forgetting to also scale a non-zero $y$-coordinate (leaving it equal to the original).
  • Applying $y=f(kx)$ as if $x$-coordinates were multiplied by $k$ rather than divided.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure scope: graph transformations $y=af(x)$, $y=f(x)+a$, $y=f(x+a)$, $y=f(ax)$ and their stationary-point implications are standard P2/P3 content and frequently linked to differentiation questions in P3/P4.
