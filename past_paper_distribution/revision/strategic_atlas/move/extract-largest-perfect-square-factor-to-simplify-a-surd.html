Extract largest perfect-square factor to simplify a surd

Family: Surd Simplification Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a calculation produces $\sqrt{N}$ for a non-square integer $N$ that has obvious composite structure — typically as the output of a quadratic formula discriminant, a Pythagorean length, or a distance/magnitude computation. Visual cues: $N$ is moderately large (often 50–200 000), $N$ is divisible by 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, etc., and the surrounding expression has a denominator or coefficient that may cancel with the extracted square root. Equivalent phrasings: 'put the surd in simplest form', 'write the answer as $a + b\sqrt{c}$ with $c$ square-free', 'give the exact value'. The move is mandatory whenever the question demands an exact answer or the surd will feed into further exact algebra.

Motivation — Why this works

The move uses the multiplicative identity $\sqrt{ab} = \sqrt{a}\sqrt{b}$ in reverse: pulling the largest perfect square $k^2$ out of $N = k^2 m$ gives $\sqrt{N} = k\sqrt{m}$ with $m$ square-free. This is the unique simplest surd form, and it almost always reveals a hidden common factor with surrounding rational coefficients (e.g. $\frac{384 \pm 16\sqrt{571}}{640}$ collapses to $\frac{24 \pm \sqrt{571}}{40}$). Without this step, downstream operations — Sine Rule fractions, equation solving, comparing roots — become numerically clumsy and prone to error. Keeping exact form also preserves precision for any final answer.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the calculation has produced $\sqrt{N}$ where $N$ is not a perfect square (check by bracketing: find consecutive integers $k$ with $k^2 < N < (k+1)^2$).
  2. Rule out leaving the surd unsimplified — the question asks for exact form, and a non-simplified surd will lose marks and obstruct any subsequent cancellation.
  3. Rule out decimal approximation — that destroys the exactness the question demands and propagates rounding error.
  4. Commit to factoring $N$ as $k^2 \cdot m$ with $k^2$ as large as possible. Strategy: peel off small square factors repeatedly (divide by 4, then by 4 again, then by 9, by 25...) until the residue is square-free.
  5. Tactical check on the residue $m$: confirm it is not divisible by $4, 9, 25, 49, \ldots$ so that $\sqrt{m}$ truly cannot be simplified further.
  6. After writing $\sqrt{N} = k\sqrt{m}$, scan the full surrounding expression for a common factor between $k$ and the rational parts (numerator constant and denominator), and cancel to reach the cleanest form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify $\sqrt{N}$ in the working.
  2. Find the largest $k^2$ dividing $N$ — either by repeated division by $4, 9, 25, \ldots$ or by prime-factorising $N$ and pairing primes.
  3. Write $N = k^2 m$ and apply $\sqrt{N} = k\sqrt{m}$.
  4. Verify $m$ is square-free (not divisible by any $p^2$ for primes $p$).
  5. Substitute back into the expression and cancel any common factor between $k$ and the rational coefficients/denominator.

Worked Micro-Example

Quadratic formula gives $p = \frac{384 \pm \sqrt{146176}}{640}$. Factor: $146176 = 4 \cdot 36544 = 16 \cdot 9136 = 64 \cdot 2284 = 256 \cdot 571$, and $571$ is square-free. So $\sqrt{146176} = 16\sqrt{571}$, giving $p = \frac{384 \pm 16\sqrt{571}}{640} = \frac{24 \pm \sqrt{571}}{40}$ after dividing through by $16$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Surd appears in a length/magnitude, e.g. $AB = \sqrt{108}$ feeding into the Sine Rule.
Same factoring: $108 = 36 \cdot 3$ so $AB = 6\sqrt{3}$. The factor of $6$ then typically cancels inside the trig fraction.
Surd in a denominator, e.g. $\frac{1}{\sqrt{50}}$.
First simplify to $\frac{1}{5\sqrt{2}}$, then rationalise to $\frac{\sqrt{2}}{10}$.
Discriminant is a very large number with no obvious small square factor.
Prime-factorise systematically; if no prime appears to an even power $\geq 2$, the surd is already in simplest form.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming the discriminant 'should' be a perfect square and wasting time hunting for an integer factorisation of the quadratic.
  • Treating the surd as a signal that the working must be wrong, and restarting unnecessarily.

Pitfalls

  • Stopping at a non-largest square factor, e.g. writing $\sqrt{108} = 2\sqrt{27}$ instead of $6\sqrt{3}$.
  • Failing to simplify at all — leaving $\frac{384 \pm \sqrt{146176}}{640}$ as a final answer loses the simplification mark.
  • Cancelling only part of the numerator: e.g. dividing $384$ by $16$ but forgetting to divide the $16\sqrt{571}$ term, or dividing the surd term but not the constant.
  • Arithmetic slips computing the discriminant ($384^2 = 147456$, not $147546$) — a single digit error invalidates the whole surd extraction.
  • Approximating to decimals mid-calculation, destroying the exact form.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1 surds; P1/P2 quadratic formula). The quadratic formula is in the formula booklet; surd manipulation rules ($\sqrt{ab}=\sqrt{a}\sqrt{b}$) are assumed knowledge, not booklet entries. Do not generalise to $n$-th roots or to surds with negative radicands (complex numbers) unless the question is explicitly in FP1/FP2.
