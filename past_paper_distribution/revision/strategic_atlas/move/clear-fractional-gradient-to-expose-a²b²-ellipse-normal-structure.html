Coordinate Geometry

Clear fractional gradient to expose a²−b² ellipse-normal structure

Family: Clearing Denominators Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a parametric normal/tangent line has been written in point-slope form with a fractional gradient like (b sinθ)/(a cosθ) or (a sinθ)/(b cosθ), and the target equation has integer coefficients with a right-hand side proportional to sinθ cosθ. A strong cue is seeing the constant a²−b² (e.g. 25−16=9) appear in the target. Equivalent phrasings: 'show that the normal at P has equation …', 'show the line meets the axes at …'. The move applies whenever clearing one trigonometric denominator yields cancellation between a² and b² terms.

Motivation — Why this works

The standard normal to an ellipse x²/a²+y²/b²=1 at (a cosθ, b sinθ) has the canonical form ax sinθ − by cosθ = (a²−b²) sinθ cosθ. Multiplying through by the denominator of the gradient symmetrises the two sides and lets the a² cosθ sinθ and b² cosθ sinθ terms combine via the difference a²−b². This is faster and cleaner than converting to Cartesian or rationalising separately, and it produces exactly the structure the question is steering toward.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the point-slope equation contains a fraction (a sinθ)/(b cosθ) or (b sinθ)/(a cosθ), and the target equation is fraction-free.
  2. Rule out converting to Cartesian via cos²θ+sin²θ=1 — that destroys the parametric symmetry the next part of the question will need.
  3. Commit to multiplying both sides by the single denominator (b cosθ or a cosθ): one stroke clears the fraction and keeps θ explicit.
  4. Anticipate that expanding will give an a² sinθ cosθ term on one side and a b² sinθ cosθ term on the other, which combine into (a²−b²) sinθ cosθ — matching the target constant.
  5. Tactically rearrange so that the x and y terms sit on the left in the order matching the printed answer, then fold the two sinθ cosθ terms together on the right.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the denominator of the gradient (here 4cosθ) and multiply both sides of the point-slope form by it.
  2. Expand each side fully, keeping sinθ cosθ products unsimplified.
  3. Move all x- and y-terms to one side, leaving the sinθ cosθ terms on the other.
  4. Combine the sinθ cosθ terms: a² sinθ cosθ − b² sinθ cosθ = (a²−b²) sinθ cosθ.
  5. Read off the printed target form and check coefficients of x, y and the constant a²−b² match.

Worked Micro-Example

Ellipse x²/25 + y²/16 = 1 at P(5cosθ, 4sinθ). Normal gradient = 5sinθ/(4cosθ), so y − 4sinθ = (5sinθ/(4cosθ))(x − 5cosθ). Multiply by 4cosθ: 4y cosθ − 16 sinθ cosθ = 5x sinθ − 25 sinθ cosθ. Rearranging: 5x sinθ − 4y cosθ = (25 − 16) sinθ cosθ = 9 sinθ cosθ.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Tangent to the same ellipse at P(a cosθ, b sinθ)
Gradient is −(b cosθ)/(a sinθ); multiply by a sinθ to obtain bx cosθ + ay sinθ = ab, with no a²−b² since the b² and a² terms now both reduce to ab.
Normal to a hyperbola x²/a² − y²/b² = 1 at (a secθ, b tanθ)
Clearing denominators yields ax sinθ + by = (a²+b²) tanθ; the sign in a²+b² replaces a²−b² because of the hyperbola's defining minus.
Normal to a parabola y² = 4ax at (at², 2at)
Gradient is −t; no fraction to clear, but the analogous tidy-up is grouping to get y + tx = 2at + at³.
Chord joining P(a cosα, b sinα) and Q(a cosβ, b sinβ) on the ellipse
After point-slope, multiply by the trig denominator and use sum-to-product identities so that (α+β)/2 and (α−β)/2 appear; the a²−b² collapse is replaced by a clean half-angle form.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to eliminate θ to get a Cartesian equation, which discards the structure later parts rely on.
  • Treating the fractional gradient as if both numerator and denominator must be cleared separately (multiplying by 4cosθ·5sinθ), introducing a spurious sinθ factor.
  • Mistaking the target a²−b² for a numerical coincidence rather than the ellipse-normal identity, and so failing to verify the coefficient.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when transposing the −16 sinθ cosθ and −25 sinθ cosθ terms across the equals sign.
  • Forgetting to multiply every term on both sides by the denominator, especially the constant 4sinθ on the LHS.
  • Writing 4y cosθ − 5x sinθ = −9 sinθ cosθ and not flipping signs to match the printed form 5x sinθ − 4y cosθ = 9 sinθ cosθ.
  • Cancelling sinθ cosθ at the end, which silently excludes the axis points where θ = 0, π/2, etc.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure coordinate-geometry technique for parametric ellipse/hyperbola normals and tangents; uses only basic algebra and the identity a²−b² without invoking calculus or implicit differentiation beyond the syllabus.
