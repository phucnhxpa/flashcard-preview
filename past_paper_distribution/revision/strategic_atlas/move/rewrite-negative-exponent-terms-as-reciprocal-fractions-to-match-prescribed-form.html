Algebra & Functions

Rewrite negative-exponent terms as reciprocal fractions to match prescribed form

Family: Coefficient Matching Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a final answer is demanded in a specific algebraic form (e.g. T = a\ln(b(e+1)/e)) but the working has produced a sum like 1 + e^{-1} or 1 - e^{-k}. Cue words: 'give your answer in the form...', 'where a and b are constants to be found'. Also fires when a logarithm's argument needs to be a single fraction p/q rather than a sum involving negative powers.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity e^{-1} = 1/e lets 1 + e^{-1} be combined over a common denominator as (e+1)/e, turning a sum into a single fraction. Single fractions inside ln(...) can be inverted, split via ln(p/q) = ln p - ln q, or matched coefficient-by-coefficient against a template. Without this rewrite, the structural match to the required form is invisible even though the value is already correct.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the answer template specifies a single fraction (e+1)/e inside the logarithm, but the current expression contains the sum 1 + e^{-1}.
  2. Rule out leaving the negative exponent: it is numerically equal but doesn't fit the prescribed surface form, so marks for 'correct form' would be lost.
  3. Commit to the rewrite e^{-1} = 1/e because the target shape (e+1)/e is exactly what 1 + 1/e produces over a common denominator.
  4. First concrete operation: replace e^{-1} with 1/e and combine: 1 + 1/e = (e+1)/e.
  5. Tactically, decide whether to invert (for ln of a reciprocal, picking up a minus sign) before or after the combination — usually combine first, then invert.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify every e^{-k} (or 2^{-n}, etc.) in the expression that obstructs the target form.
  2. Replace e^{-k} with 1/e^k.
  3. Combine over a common denominator: 1 + 1/e^k = (e^k + 1)/e^k.
  4. If the expression sits inside ln(...) and the template uses the reciprocal, invert and apply ln(1/x) = -ln x.
  5. Read off the constants a, b by comparing coefficient-by-coefficient with the prescribed form.

Worked Micro-Example

Given e^{-0.2T} = 1/[2(1+e^{-1})], express T in the form a\ln(b(e+1)/e). Rewrite: 1+e^{-1} = (e+1)/e, so e^{-0.2T} = e/[2(e+1)]. Take logs: -0.2T = \ln(e/[2(e+1)]) = -\ln(2(e+1)/e). Hence T = 5\ln(2(e+1)/e), giving a=5, b=2.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Expression contains 1 - e^{-k} and answer wants (e^k - 1)/e^k
Same move with a minus sign: 1 - e^{-k} = (e^k - 1)/e^k.
Geometric series with ratio written as 2^{-n}
Rewrite 2^{-n} = 1/2^n to expose r = 1/2 in the standard sum formula.
Logarithm answer required as ln a - ln b
After forming the single fraction, split using ln(p/q) = ln p - ln q rather than leaving as one log.
Surd form with x^{-1/2} terms
Rewrite x^{-1/2} = 1/\sqrt{x} and combine over \sqrt{x} to match a prescribed surd shape.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'find T' as purely numerical and leaving T = 5\ln(2 + 2e^{-1}), losing the form mark.
  • Believing e^{-1} and 1/e are different objects requiring different manipulations.
  • Reading the template (e+1)/e as e+1/e (missing brackets) and combining incorrectly.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when combining: writing (e-1)/e instead of (e+1)/e.
  • Forgetting the minus sign when inverting inside a log: \ln(e/[2(e+1)]) = -\ln(2(e+1)/e), not +\ln(2(e+1)/e).
  • Mis-identifying a and b after the rewrite by not matching the exact coefficient positions in the template.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the index law e^{-1} = 1/e and basic log laws \ln(p/q) = \ln p - \ln q, both core to Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P2/P3). The 'answer in the form...' demand is a standard Edexcel mark-scheme device.
