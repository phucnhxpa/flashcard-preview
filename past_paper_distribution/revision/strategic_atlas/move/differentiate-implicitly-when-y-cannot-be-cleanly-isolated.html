Differentiation

Differentiate implicitly when y cannot be cleanly isolated

Family: Implicit Differentiation Frequency: 13 questions

Trigger — When to use

The curve equation mixes x and y inside multiple terms — typically a cross-product like kxy alongside a y² (or higher y power) term, e.g. x² + 4xy − 3y² + c = 0 or 2x − 4y² + 3x²y = 4x² + 8. Solving such an equation for y as an explicit function of x would require the quadratic formula (or worse), producing ± branches and surds that are painful to differentiate. Equivalently, the question may explicitly say 'find dy/dx in terms of x and y', which is the strongest verbal cue. Whenever y appears in more than one term and cannot be cleanly separated onto one side, implicit differentiation is the correct tool.

Motivation — Why this works

Implicit differentiation treats y as an unspecified differentiable function of x and differentiates every term of F(x, y) = 0 with respect to x. By the chain rule, each y-term picks up a factor of dy/dx, and because the original equation is polynomial in y, the derivative equation is LINEAR in dy/dx. We can then collect dy/dx terms, factor, and divide to obtain dy/dx as an expression in x and y. This bypasses the algebraically hopeless task of isolating y and gives a formula valid at every point on the curve.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that y appears in multiple terms — for instance a cross-term like 4xy AND a y² term — so the equation is genuinely a quadratic (or higher) in y.
  2. Rule out explicit differentiation: solving for y would require the quadratic formula, producing y = (… ± √(…))/(…), and differentiating that surd-laden expression would be ugly and error-prone.
  3. Rule out parametric methods: there is no parameter t in the equation; this is a Cartesian implicit relation.
  4. Commit to implicit differentiation: differentiate the whole equation term-by-term with respect to x, attaching dy/dx via the chain rule wherever y appears, and use the product rule on any xy cross-terms.
  5. Plan the algebra in advance: after differentiating, gather all dy/dx terms on one side, factor dy/dx out, and divide to isolate it — the result will be a rational expression in x and y.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Differentiate every term of the equation with respect to x.
  2. Pure-x terms differentiate normally: d/dx(x²) = 2x, d/dx(constant) = 0.
  3. Pure-y terms get a chain-rule factor: d/dx(y²) = 2y·(dy/dx), d/dx(y³) = 3y²·(dy/dx).
  4. Mixed terms like kxy use the product rule: d/dx(kxy) = ky + kx·(dy/dx); for x²y: d/dx(x²y) = 2xy + x²·(dy/dx).
  5. Collect all terms containing dy/dx on one side and all other terms on the other side.
  6. Factor dy/dx out: (…)·dy/dx = (…), then divide to obtain dy/dx = (…)/(…).
  7. Simplify by cancelling any common factor (e.g. a factor of 2).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $x^2 + 4xy - 3y^2 + 21 = 0$, differentiate term-by-term: $2x + (4y + 4x\tfrac{dy}{dx}) - 6y\tfrac{dy}{dx} = 0$. Collect: $(4x - 6y)\tfrac{dy}{dx} = -2x - 4y$. Hence $\tfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{-2x - 4y}{4x - 6y} = \dfrac{-(x + 2y)}{2x - 3y}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equations with trig/exp functions of y, e.g. sin(xy) = x or e^y + xy = 1
Same framework, but apply chain rule with the outer function: d/dx(sin(xy)) = cos(xy)·(y + x·dy/dx); d/dx(e^y) = e^y·dy/dx.
Parametric curves x = f(t), y = g(t)
Don't use implicit differentiation — instead use dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt).
Higher y-powers like y³ or y⁴
Chain rule still applies: d/dx(y^n) = n y^(n-1)·dy/dx.
Question asks for second derivative d²y/dx²
Differentiate the implicit dy/dx expression again, treating dy/dx itself as a function of x, then substitute the first-derivative expression back in.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to rearrange to y = f(x) using the quadratic formula, leading to ± surd expressions that waste time and create sign errors.
  • Missing the verbal cue 'in terms of x and y', which is the examiner's hint that dy/dx will involve both variables.
  • Confusing this with logarithmic differentiation — implicit differentiation does not require taking logs.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to attach dy/dx when differentiating a y-term — e.g. writing d/dx(3y²) = 6y instead of 6y·dy/dx (the single most common error).
  • Forgetting the product rule on mixed terms like 4xy, writing d/dx(4xy) = 4y instead of 4y + 4x·dy/dx.
  • Sign errors when transposing terms before factoring out dy/dx.
  • Treating y as a constant in any one term — every y-term contributes a dy/dx.

A-level Scope Notes

Implicit differentiation is core Edexcel IAL P3/P4 (and reappears in FP for higher derivatives). No formula-booklet entry is required — it is a direct application of chain and product rules. Avoid generalising to partial differentiation or to differentiation of multi-valued complex relations; stay with real, single-valued branches of the curve.
