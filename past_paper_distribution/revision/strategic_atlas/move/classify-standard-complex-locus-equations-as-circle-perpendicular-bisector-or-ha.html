Complex Numbers

Classify standard complex locus equations as circle, perpendicular bisector, or half-line

Family: Locus Recognition Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a sub-part presents a condition on z of the form |z − z₁| = r, |z − z₁| = |z − z₂|, or arg(z − z₁) = α and asks the student to describe, sketch, or find the Cartesian equation of the locus. Equivalent phrasings include 'sketch the locus of points satisfying...', 'describe the set of complex numbers z such that...', or 'find the Cartesian equation of the curve given by...'. Also fires when a region is defined by an inequality version of one of these (e.g. |z − z₁| ≤ r) or when two such loci must be intersected. The cue is the appearance of |z − constant| or arg(z − constant) on its own side of an equation.

Motivation — Why this works

Each of the three standard forms corresponds directly to a geometric object on the Argand diagram: |z − z₁| = r is the set of points at distance r from z₁ (a circle), |z − z₁| = |z − z₂| is the set equidistant from z₁ and z₂ (the perpendicular bisector of z₁z₂), and arg(z − z₁) = α is the set of points whose displacement from z₁ has argument α (a half-line from z₁). Recognising the template instantly converts an algebraic complex condition into a known geometric object, bypassing the slower route of substituting z = x + iy and expanding. This pattern-matching is the foundation of every FP2 locus question and is essential before any intersection, region, or extremal-value work begins.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the condition isolates either a single modulus, two equal moduli, or a single argument expression involving z minus a fixed complex number.
  2. Rule out brute-force algebra (z = x + iy expansion) as the first step — it is slower, error-prone, and obscures the geometry; reserve it for when a Cartesian equation is explicitly demanded.
  3. Match the equation to one of three templates: |z − z₁| = r ⇒ circle; |z − z₁| = |z − z₂| ⇒ perpendicular bisector; arg(z − z₁) = α ⇒ half-line. Commit to the geometric description.
  4. Extract the parameters carefully: for a circle read off the centre z₁ and radius r (watch the sign — |z − (3 + 2i)| has centre (3, 2), not (−3, −2)); for a perpendicular bisector identify the two fixed points and their midpoint; for a half-line identify the start point z₁ and the angle α measured anticlockwise from the positive real direction.
  5. Decide whether the answer required is a sketch, a verbal description, or a Cartesian equation. If Cartesian, substitute z = x + iy and simplify; if a sketch, mark centre/radius, midpoint and bisector, or start-point-with-open-circle and angle α.
  6. Check edge conventions: half-line excludes its start point (open circle), and the half-line points in only one direction — not both.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Parse the equation and identify the template: |z − z₁| = r, |z − z₁| = |z − z₂|, or arg(z − z₁) = α.
  2. For |z − z₁| = r: state 'circle, centre z₁, radius r'. On the diagram, plot z₁ and draw a circle of radius r.
  3. For |z − z₁| = |z − z₂|: compute midpoint M = (z₁ + z₂)/2 and the gradient of the segment z₁z₂; the locus is the line through M perpendicular to z₁z₂.
  4. For arg(z − z₁) = α: plot z₁ with an open circle, and draw a ray from z₁ in the direction making angle α anticlockwise with the positive real axis.
  5. If a Cartesian equation is required, set z = x + iy, substitute, square moduli where needed, and simplify to standard form (e.g. (x − a)² + (y − b)² = r² or y = mx + c).

Worked Micro-Example

Sketch the locus of $|z - (3 + 2i)| = 5$. Recognise the form $|z - z_1| = r$ with $z_1 = 3 + 2i$ and $r = 5$. Therefore the locus is a circle, centre $(3, 2)$, radius $5$. Cartesian form: $(x-3)^2 + (y-2)^2 = 25$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Inequality version, e.g. |z − z₁| < r or |z − z₁| ≥ r
Same centre and radius, but shade the interior (open disk) or exterior of the circle; use a dashed boundary for strict inequality.
Apollonius form |z − a| = k|z − b| with k ≠ 1
Square both sides, substitute z = x + iy, expand, and complete the square to reveal a circle with non-obvious centre and radius.
arg((z − z₁)/(z − z₂)) = α
The locus is an arc of a circle through z₁ and z₂ subtending angle α; not a half-line. Use the inscribed angle theorem to construct it.
Combined locus, e.g. |z − z₁| = r AND arg(z − z₂) = α
Sketch each locus separately and identify the intersection point(s) geometrically or algebraically.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing |z − z₁| = |z − z₂| (perpendicular bisector — a line) with |z − z₁| + |z − z₂| = k (an ellipse, out of FP2 scope in this form).
  • Reading |z − (a + bi)| = r as having centre (−a, −b) instead of (a, b) due to the minus sign confusion.
  • Treating arg(z − z₁) = α as a full line through z₁ rather than a single half-line ray.

Pitfalls

  • Drawing a full line instead of a half-line for arg conditions — the ray extends in one direction only.
  • Forgetting to exclude the start point z₁ from a half-line locus (it should be marked with an open circle since arg(0) is undefined).
  • Measuring the argument from the wrong reference (e.g. from the y-axis or clockwise) — it must be measured anticlockwise from the positive real direction at z₁.
  • On a perpendicular bisector, forgetting that the gradient is the negative reciprocal of the segment's gradient.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure 2 (FP2) Argand-diagram loci. The three templates (circle, perpendicular bisector, half-line) and the Apollonius extension are all in scope. Avoid invoking ellipses (|z − z₁| + |z − z₂| = k) or hyperbolas as loci — these are not part of the FP2 complex-locus syllabus.
