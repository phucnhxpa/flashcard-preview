Differentiation

Substitute point coordinates into implicit derivative to find tangent gradient

Family: Evaluate Implicit Derivative Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when implicit differentiation has produced dy/dx as an expression in both x and y, and the question asks for the gradient (or tangent/normal equation) at a specific point P(a, b) on the curve. Typical phrasings: 'find the equation of the tangent at P', 'show that the tangent at P is horizontal', or 'find the gradient of C at the point (a, b)'. The two-variable form of dy/dx is the key cue — unlike explicit y = f(x), a single x-value alone is insufficient.

Motivation — Why this works

Implicit differentiation produces dy/dx as a rational expression in x and y because y has not been isolated. This expression gives the gradient at any point on the curve, so to extract the gradient at a specific point both coordinates must be substituted. This sidesteps the often impossible task of solving the implicit equation for y(x), which is the whole point of implicit differentiation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that dy/dx contains both x and y, so a single coordinate is not enough — both values from P are needed.
  2. Rules out resolving the curve equation for y explicitly (typically not possible or wasteful) and rules out re-differentiating.
  3. Commits to direct substitution because P is given as lying on the curve, so the derivative formula is valid there.
  4. Plans to substitute x = a and y = b carefully into numerator and denominator separately, then simplify.
  5. Checks that the denominator is non-zero (otherwise tangent is vertical), and interprets a zero numerator as a horizontal tangent.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write dy/dx in the form N(x,y)/D(x,y) obtained from implicit differentiation.
  2. Substitute the given coordinates x = a, y = b into the numerator N.
  3. Substitute x = a, y = b into the denominator D.
  4. Check D(a,b) ≠ 0; if D = 0 and N ≠ 0 the tangent is vertical, if both vanish further analysis is needed.
  5. Compute m = N(a,b)/D(a,b); use m in y − b = m(x − a) for the tangent equation if required.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $x^2 + 2xy - 3y^2 + 21 = 0$, implicit differentiation gives $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{x + 2y}{3y - 2x}$. At $P(2, -1)$: numerator $= 2 + 2(-1) = 0$, denominator $= 3(-1) - 2(2) = -7$. So $m = 0/(-7) = 0$, and the tangent at $P$ is the horizontal line $y = -1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Parametric curve with x = f(t), y = g(t)
Substitute the parameter value t = t₀ into dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) rather than coordinates.
Normal rather than tangent requested
Compute m as above, then use gradient −1/m in the point-slope form (handle m = 0 as a vertical normal).
Denominator vanishes at P
If D(a,b) = 0 and N(a,b) ≠ 0, conclude the tangent is vertical: x = a.
Second derivative needed at P
After finding dy/dx at P, substitute both (a,b) and the value of dy/dx into the expression for d²y/dx².

Common Misreadings

  • Treating dy/dx as a function of x alone and substituting only the x-coordinate.
  • Assuming a gradient of 0 means an error rather than a horizontal tangent.
  • Forgetting to verify P lies on the curve when the question does not state it explicitly.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when substituting negative coordinates, especially in the denominator.
  • Swapping x and y coordinates in the substitution.
  • Simplifying dy/dx algebraically before substitution and introducing cancellation errors.
  • Reporting m = 0/0 without recognising it requires a separate limiting argument.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P3/P4 implicit differentiation: tangent and normal problems on curves like conics or polynomial implicit relations. Vertical-tangent and 0/0 cases lie at the edge of scope and are usually flagged explicitly.
