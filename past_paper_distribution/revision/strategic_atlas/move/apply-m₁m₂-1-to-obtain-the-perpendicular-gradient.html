Coordinate Geometry

Apply m₁m₂ = −1 to obtain the perpendicular gradient

Family: Perpendicularity Condition Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question explicitly states that one line (or segment) is perpendicular to another and the gradient of one of them is already known or readily computable. Surface cues include the words 'perpendicular to', 'normal to', a right angle marked at an intersection, or a problem asking for the equation of a line through a point at right angles to a given line. Equivalent phrasings to watch for: 'at right angles to L₁', 'forms a 90° angle with', or 'the normal to the curve at P' (where the tangent gradient is known).

Motivation — Why this works

Two non-vertical lines are perpendicular if and only if the product of their gradients is −1. This identity converts the geometric constraint of perpendicularity into a one-line algebraic equation, immediately yielding m₂ = −1/m₁ — the negative reciprocal of the known gradient. It works because perpendicular direction vectors (1, m₁) and (1, m₂) must satisfy 1 + m₁m₂ = 0 (zero dot product), which rearranges to m₁m₂ = −1. This is the standard and only A-level tool for switching between a line's gradient and that of any line perpendicular to it.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the keyword 'perpendicular' (or a right-angle symbol) linking two lines — this is the structural cue that perpendicularity, not parallelism or general intersection, governs the relationship.
  2. Rule out alternatives: parallel condition m₁ = m₂ doesn't apply; rotating a direction vector by 90° gives the same answer but is heavier machinery than needed at A-level; using vectors/dot product is overkill when gradients are already in hand.
  3. Commit to m₁m₂ = −1 because it is the cleanest algebraic encoding of the geometric fact and directly delivers m₂ once m₁ is known.
  4. Bridge to operation: write the gradient of L₁ explicitly as a fraction, substitute into m₁ × m₂ = −1, and solve for m₂.
  5. Tactical check: confirm the result is the negative reciprocal — numerator and denominator have swapped AND the sign has flipped — and verify the product equals −1 before proceeding to use m₂ in a point-gradient equation.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify and write down m₁ as a fraction (e.g. m₁ = 3/2).
  2. State the perpendicularity condition: m₁ × m₂ = −1.
  3. Substitute: (3/2) × m₂ = −1.
  4. Solve by multiplying both sides by the reciprocal of m₁: m₂ = −1 ÷ (3/2) = −1 × (2/3) = −2/3.
  5. Sanity-check: (3/2) × (−2/3) = −1 ✓.

Worked Micro-Example

Given L₁ has gradient $m_1 = \tfrac{3}{2}$, find the gradient of any line $L_2 \perp L_1$. Apply $m_1 m_2 = -1$: $\tfrac{3}{2} \cdot m_2 = -1 \implies m_2 = -\tfrac{2}{3}$. Check: $\tfrac{3}{2} \times -\tfrac{2}{3} = -1$ ✓.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Parallel lines instead of perpendicular
Replace the condition with m₁ = m₂ — the same trigger-recognition step but a simpler equation (no reciprocation, no sign change).
Gradient given in surd form (e.g. m₁ = √2)
Same negative-reciprocal logic: m₂ = −1/√2, then rationalise to m₂ = −√2/2 if required.
Gradient contains a parameter (e.g. m₁ = k or m₁ = 2t)
m₁m₂ = −1 becomes an equation in the parameter: m₂ = −1/k or solve for the parameter value that makes lines perpendicular.
Normal to a curve at a point
First compute the tangent gradient via dy/dx at the point, then apply m₁m₂ = −1 to get the normal's gradient.
One line is vertical (m₁ undefined)
The product rule fails; instead state directly that the perpendicular line is horizontal (m₂ = 0), and vice versa.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing 'perpendicular' with 'parallel' and setting m₂ = m₁ instead of m₂ = −1/m₁.
  • Misreading the additive condition m₁ + m₂ = −1 (incorrect) for the multiplicative one m₁m₂ = −1.
  • Treating 'normal' as if it meant 'tangent' and using the same gradient instead of the negative reciprocal.

Pitfalls

  • Negating without reciprocating: writing m₂ = −3/2 instead of −2/3.
  • Reciprocating without negating: writing m₂ = 2/3 instead of −2/3.
  • Arithmetic slip when dividing by a fraction — forgetting that −1 ÷ (a/b) = −b/a.
  • Forgetting to handle the vertical/horizontal edge case where one gradient is undefined.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1 coordinate geometry). The condition m₁m₂ = −1 is a standard result, not in the formula booklet — it must be memorised. Avoid invoking dot-product or rotation-matrix justifications in a Pure paper unless the question is set in a vector context (FP).
