Integration

Kill sin(kπ) terms when polar/integration limits are multiples of π

Family: Limit Simplification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a definite integral has been integrated to an antiderivative containing terms like sin(kθ) and the limits are integer multiples of π (most often 0 and π, or 0 and 2π). Typical in polar area problems ½∫r² dθ where r = 0 at θ = 0 and θ = π. Cue phrases: 'find the area enclosed by the polar curve', 'between θ = 0 and θ = π'. Also fires whenever a Fourier-style expansion in cosines is integrated over [0, π] or [0, 2π].

Motivation — Why this works

sin(kπ) = 0 for every integer k, so every sin(kθ) contribution to the antiderivative collapses at both ends. The only surviving pieces are the non-oscillatory terms — typically a constant multiple of θ that came from integrating cos²(mθ) or similar via the double-angle identity. Spotting this up front turns a messy substitution into a one-line evaluation and avoids fractional arithmetic with sin(2π/3), sin(π/2) etc.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the antiderivative is a sum of a linear-in-θ term plus several sin(kθ) terms with integer k.
  2. I check the limits: they are 0 and an integer multiple of π, so I rule out the worry that any sin(kθ) might survive.
  3. I commit to ignoring every sin(kθ) bracket because sin(0) = sin(kπ) = 0 — no exceptions for integer k.
  4. I therefore only need to evaluate the θ-linear (and any constant) part at the upper minus lower limit.
  5. I stay alert: if a cos(kθ) term were present I would NOT discard it, since cos(kπ) = (−1)^k ≠ 0.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Confirm both limits α and β are integer multiples of π.
  2. Confirm every oscillatory term in the antiderivative is of the form A sin(kθ) with k ∈ ℤ.
  3. Set each sin(kα) and sin(kβ) to 0 and strike those terms.
  4. Evaluate the surviving polynomial-in-θ part at β minus at α.
  5. Multiply by any outer constant (e.g. the ½ in polar area) to finish.

Worked Micro-Example

Compute $A = \tfrac12\int_0^{\pi} (1+\cos 2\theta)\,d\theta$. Antiderivative: $\tfrac12\big[\theta + \tfrac12\sin 2\theta\big]_0^{\pi}$. Since $\sin 2\pi = \sin 0 = 0$, the sine bracket dies, leaving $\tfrac12(\pi - 0) = \tfrac{\pi}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Polar area on [0, 2π] with r² containing cos²(2θ)
Use cos²(2θ) = ½(1+cos 4θ); after integration sin(4θ) vanishes at both 0 and 2π, leaving only the θ-term.
Limits are 0 and π/2
sin(2kθ) still vanishes at both ends, but sin((2k+1)θ) does NOT — evaluate those carefully (e.g. sin(π/2) = 1).
Antiderivative contains cos(kθ) instead of sin(kθ)
Do not discard: cos(kπ) = (−1)^k. Compute (−1)^k − 1, which is 0 for even k and −2 for odd k.
Limits are −π and π (symmetric)
sin(kθ) still vanishes at ±π for integer k, and by oddness sin(kπ) − sin(−kπ) = 0 anyway.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating sin(kπ/2) as 0 — it is only 0 for even k.
  • Assuming any term with a sine in it must vanish, even when k is non-integer (e.g. sin(θ/2) at θ = π gives 1, not 0).
  • Confusing the rule with cosines and zeroing cos(kπ) by mistake.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the outer ½ factor in polar area after the sines drop out.
  • Dropping a sin term whose argument is kθ with k non-integer (e.g. after a substitution that scaled θ).
  • Sign slips at the lower limit when α ≠ 0 (e.g. α = −π) — still subtract the lower value.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (polar area via ½∫r² dθ) and Core integration of cos²/sin² via double-angle identities; no results outside the syllabus are invoked.
