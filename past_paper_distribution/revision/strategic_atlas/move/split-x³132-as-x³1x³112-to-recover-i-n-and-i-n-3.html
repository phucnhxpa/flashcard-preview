Integration

Split (x³+1)^{3/2} as (x³+1)·(x³+1)^{1/2} to recover I_n and I_{n-3}

Family: Reduction Self Reference Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires during a reduction-formula derivation when integration by parts on I_n = ∫ x^{n-3}·x²·√(x³+1) dx (or similar) leaves a remainder integral involving (x³+1)^{3/2} instead of the expected (x³+1)^{1/2}. The exponent mismatch — a half-power higher than the I_k family — is the key cue. Equivalently, any time IBP on a square-root integrand produces a (·)^{3/2} factor that must be flattened back to (·)^{1/2} to match the reduction target.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity (x³+1)^{3/2} = (x³+1)·(x³+1)^{1/2} is purely algebraic (½+1 = 3/2) and costs nothing, yet it splits the inflated power into the original √(x³+1) factor multiplied by a polynomial. Distributing the polynomial against x^{n-3} produces x^n√(x³+1) + x^{n-3}√(x³+1), which are precisely the integrands of I_n and I_{n-3}. This manufactures the self-referential equation I_n appears on both sides — the structural mechanism that makes a reduction formula possible.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the remainder integral has (x³+1)^{3/2}, but my I_k family is defined with (x³+1)^{1/2}, so the powers don't line up.
  2. I rule out a second IBP: differentiating (x³+1)^{3/2} would raise the polynomial degree further and never close back on I_n.
  3. I commit to algebraic factorisation because ½ + 1 = 3/2 is the unique split that returns √(x³+1) (matching the I_k family) while leaving a clean polynomial factor (x³+1).
  4. Concretely, I write (x³+1)^{3/2} = (x³+1)·(x³+1)^{1/2} and prepare to distribute against x^{n-3}.
  5. I expect to see x^n·√(x³+1) + x^{n-3}·√(x³+1), i.e. I_n + I_{n-3}, then move the I_n term to the LHS to solve for I_n.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write (x³+1)^{3/2} = (x³+1)(x³+1)^{1/2}.
  2. Distribute: x^{n-3}(x³+1)(x³+1)^{1/2} = x^n(x³+1)^{1/2} + x^{n-3}(x³+1)^{1/2}.
  3. Identify the resulting integral as I_n + I_{n-3}.
  4. Substitute back into the IBP equation, collect I_n on one side, and solve for I_n in terms of I_{n-3} and the boundary term.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose IBP gives I_n = [boundary] − k∫ x^{n-3}(x³+1)^{3/2} dx. Factor: (x³+1)^{3/2} = (x³+1)√(x³+1), so x^{n-3}(x³+1)^{3/2} = x^n√(x³+1) + x^{n-3}√(x³+1). Hence ∫ x^{n-3}(x³+1)^{3/2} dx = I_n + I_{n-3}, giving I_n = [boundary] − k(I_n + I_{n-3}), which solves to (1+k)I_n = [boundary] − k I_{n-3}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

∫ x^n (1−x²)^{k+1/2} dx reduction
Factor (1−x²)^{k+1/2} = (1−x²)·(1−x²)^{k−1/2} to expose the lower I_k integrand.
∫ sin^n x dx reduction
Use sin²x = 1 − cos²x after IBP to split into I_{n-2} and a term involving I_n.
∫ x^n √(a²−x²) dx
Factor (a²−x²)^{3/2} = (a²−x²)·√(a²−x²) to recover a polynomial multiple of √(a²−x²).
∫ x^n (x²+1)^{1/2} dx
After IBP yielding (x²+1)^{3/2}, write (x²+1)^{3/2} = (x²+1)√(x²+1) to recover I_n + I_{n-2}.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating (x³+1)^{3/2} as needing a substitution u = x³+1, which loses the x^{n-3} structure needed for self-reference.
  • Attempting a second integration by parts on (x³+1)^{3/2}, which inflates rather than reduces the integral.
  • Assuming the factorisation produces only I_n (forgetting the I_{n-3} term from the +1 in (x³+1)).

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting that distributing (x³+1) yields TWO integrals (I_n and I_{n-3}), not just one.
  • Sign errors when the factor involves subtraction, e.g. (1−x²) instead of (x³+1).
  • Failing to move the I_n term to the LHS, leaving an implicit equation rather than an explicit reduction formula.
  • Mismatching the index shift (n vs n−3) when the original I_n was defined with a different exponent on x.

A-level Scope Notes
