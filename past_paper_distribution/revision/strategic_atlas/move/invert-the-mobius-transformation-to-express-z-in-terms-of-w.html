Trigger — When to use

The question gives a Möbius transformation w = (az+b)/(cz+d) together with a locus condition on z (typically |z|=r, |z−α|=r, or arg(z−α)=θ), and asks for the image locus in the w-plane. The cue is the mismatch: the constraint is in z but the answer must describe w. Equivalent phrasings: 'find the locus of w when z lies on …', 'show the image of the circle … is …', 'sketch the image in the Argand diagram of w'. Whenever you see a z-locus that needs to be pushed through a fractional linear map, this move fires.

Motivation — Why this works

The locus equation is written purely in z, so to get a condition purely in w we must eliminate z by substituting z = g(w). Because w = f(z) is Möbius, its inverse g is also Möbius, so the rearrangement stays a clean rational expression and substitution into |z−α|=r or arg-conditions remains tractable. This is far cleaner than parametrising z = re^{iθ} (which gives a messy parametric curve) or splitting into real/imaginary parts simultaneously in z and w (which doubles the algebra). The underlying principle is that Möbius maps form a group under composition, so inversion preserves the linear-fractional structure and circles/lines map to circles/lines.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the locus is a constraint on z (e.g. |z|=2), but I'm asked for a curve in the w-plane — the variables don't match, so I must eliminate one.
  2. I consider parametrising z = re^{iθ} and pushing through w = f(z); but identifying the resulting parametric curve as a circle/line is awkward.
  3. I also consider writing z = x+iy, w = u+iv and equating real/imaginary parts in both the locus and the map — this is doable but algebraically heavy.
  4. I commit to inversion: rearrange w = (az+b)/(cz+d) for z, because the inverse of a Möbius map is again Möbius, so the substitution into the z-locus stays clean.
  5. Concretely: cross-multiply to clear the denominator, expand, gather all z-terms on one side, factor out z, and divide. Then substitute z = g(w) directly into the locus equation and simplify.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Start from w = (az + b)/(cz + d).
  2. Cross-multiply: w(cz + d) = az + b.
  3. Expand: cwz + dw = az + b.
  4. Collect z-terms on one side: z(cw − a) = b − dw.
  5. Isolate: z = (b − dw)/(cw − a).
  6. Substitute this expression into the z-locus, e.g. |z − α| = r becomes |(b − dw)/(cw − a) − α| = r, then simplify (often by multiplying through by |cw − a|) to recognise a circle or line in w.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $w = \dfrac{z+2}{z-i}$ with $|z|=2$. Cross-multiply: $w(z-i) = z+2$, so $wz - wi = z + 2$, giving $z(w-1) = 2 + wi$, hence $z = \dfrac{2+wi}{w-1}$. Substituting into $|z|=2$ yields $|2+wi| = 2|w-1|$, which simplifies to a circle in the $w$-plane.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Simple reciprocal map w = 1/z
Inversion is immediate: z = 1/w; substitute directly into the locus.
General Möbius w = (az+b)/(cz+d)
Inverse is z = (dw − b)/(−cw + a) = (b − dw)/(cw − a) — itself Möbius.
Locus is an arg condition rather than a modulus
After substituting z = g(w), use arg(P/Q) = arg P − arg Q to convert to an arg-condition on a linear expression in w (typically a half-line or arc).
Non-Möbius map such as w = z²
Inversion gives z = ±√w (multi-valued); proceed but be alert to branch issues — generally beyond IAL scope.

Common Misreadings

  • Substituting w = f(z) into the z-locus directly — this does not eliminate z and leaves a mixed equation.
  • Trying to parametrise z = re^{iθ} and identify the resulting parametric (u(θ), v(θ)) curve as a circle by inspection — usually unworkable.
  • Assuming the image must be a circle; if z is on a circle through the pole z = −d/c, the image is a straight line.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when collecting z-terms: writing (cw + a) instead of (cw − a) in the denominator.
  • Dropping the factor i when handling the wi term, or mis-conjugating when later applying |·|² = (·)(·̄).
  • Forgetting that the pole z = −d/c maps to ∞: if the z-locus passes through this point, the w-image is a line, not a circle.
  • Not multiplying through by |cw − a| to clear the fraction before squaring, leading to algebraic tangles.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) on transformations of the complex plane. No formula-booklet entry is needed; rely on standard Möbius algebra. Stay with linear fractional maps — inversion of w = z² or higher-degree maps is out of scope and introduces multi-valued issues that should be avoided.
