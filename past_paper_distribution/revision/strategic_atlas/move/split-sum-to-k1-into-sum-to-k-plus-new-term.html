Proof

Split sum to k+1 into sum to k plus new term

Family: Induction Separation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A 'prove by induction' question asks you to verify a closed form for ∑_{r=1}^{n} f(r). You have already established the base case and assumed P(k): ∑_{r=1}^{k} f(r) = g(k). The inductive step requires you to evaluate ∑_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r) and show it equals g(k+1). Phrasings include 'prove that ... for all positive integers n' or 'hence prove by induction'.

Motivation — Why this works

The inductive hypothesis is only useful if a sum to k literally appears in your working, so you must engineer that sum by separating off the final term. The identity ∑_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r) = ∑_{r=1}^{k} f(r) + f(k+1) is the canonical bridge between P(k) and P(k+1). Once the hypothesis is substituted, the remaining task is purely algebraic: combine g(k) + f(k+1) and factor it into the form g(k+1). This converts a statement about sums into a polynomial identity that A-level algebra can handle.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the target is a summation identity and the question demands induction, so P(k) ⇒ P(k+1) must be shown.
  2. Rules out direct proof of P(k+1) from scratch and rules out working backwards from the goal — both lose marks for logical direction.
  3. Commits to peeling because the inductive hypothesis only earns its keep if a ∑_{r=1}^{k} appears explicitly in the working.
  4. Bridges by writing ∑_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r) = ∑_{r=1}^{k} f(r) + f(k+1), creating a substitution slot.
  5. Inside the move, decides whether to factor out (k+1) early or expand fully — usually factoring (k+1) immediately after substitution leads cleanly to g(k+1).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. State the inductive hypothesis P(k) explicitly: assume ∑_{r=1}^{k} f(r) = g(k).
  2. Write ∑_{r=1}^{k+1} f(r) = ∑_{r=1}^{k} f(r) + f(k+1).
  3. Substitute g(k) for ∑_{r=1}^{k} f(r) using the hypothesis.
  4. Factor out a common factor (typically (k+1)) and simplify the remaining bracket.
  5. Rearrange to match g(k+1) — the target closed form with k replaced by k+1.
  6. State the conclusion: since P(1) is true and P(k) ⇒ P(k+1), by induction P(n) holds for all n ∈ ℤ⁺.

Worked Micro-Example

Prove $\sum_{r=1}^{n} r(r+1) = \tfrac{1}{3}n(n+1)(n+2)$. Assume $\sum_{r=1}^{k} r(r+1) = \tfrac{1}{3}k(k+1)(k+2)$. Then $\sum_{r=1}^{k+1} r(r+1) = \tfrac{1}{3}k(k+1)(k+2) + (k+1)(k+2) = \tfrac{1}{3}(k+1)(k+2)\bigl[k+3\bigr] = \tfrac{1}{3}(k+1)(k+2)(k+3)$, which is the target with $n=k+1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Product induction (e.g. ∏ identities)
Write ∏_{r=1}^{k+1} = (∏_{r=1}^{k}) · (k+1)th factor, then substitute the hypothesis.
Matrix power induction (FP1)
Write M^{k+1} = M^k · M, substitute the assumed closed form for M^k, and multiply.
Divisibility induction
Express f(k+1) as f(k) + (extra term) where f(k) is divisible by hypothesis, leaving only the extra term to verify.
Recurrence-defined sequence
Use the recurrence u_{k+1} = h(u_k) and substitute the hypothesised form of u_k.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating P(k+1) as a fresh statement to prove directly, never invoking the hypothesis.
  • Starting from the desired P(k+1) and working backwards to a true statement — loses marks at Edexcel for incorrect logical flow.
  • Confusing the inductive hypothesis (an assumption) with something already proved for all n.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to state the inductive hypothesis explicitly before using it.
  • Algebra slip when combining g(k) + f(k+1) — failing to extract the common factor (k+1).
  • Omitting the final structured conclusion that references the base case, the implication, and 'therefore true for all n ∈ ℤ⁺'.
  • Failing to verify the base case at all, or verifying it for n=0 when the statement starts at n=1.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope (FP1 induction): summation, divisibility, matrix powers, and recurrence inductions all use this peel-off mechanic with the standard four-step write-up (base, assume, step, conclude).
