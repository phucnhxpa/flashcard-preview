Trigger — When to use

Fires when an expression contains two or more surds with large radicands (e.g. √675, √48, √200, √72) that must combine into a single 'exact form'. Cue phrases: 'give your answer in exact form', 'in the form a√b', or a definite integral evaluating to a difference of awkward roots. Also fires whenever surds are added/subtracted and the radicands are not equal but share a square-free kernel.

Motivation — Why this works

Every positive integer n factors uniquely as n = k²·m with m square-free, giving √n = k√m. Two surds √n₁ and √n₂ can be combined linearly iff their square-free kernels match. Factoring each radicand exposes this kernel, turning √675 − √48 into 15√3 − 4√3 = 11√3. Without this reduction the answer is not in 'simplest exact form' and loses the final accuracy mark.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the answer contains √675 and √48 — both large, neither obviously a clean multiple of a small surd.
  2. I rule out leaving them as-is (not 'exact form') and rule out decimal approximation (the question demands exact).
  3. I commit to factoring each radicand into (largest perfect square) × (square-free remainder), expecting a common kernel to emerge.
  4. First operation: strip out perfect-square factors — 675 = 225·3 and 48 = 16·3, so the kernel √3 is shared.
  5. Tactical: pull constants outside, collect like surds (15√3 − 4√3 = 11√3), then carry through any outer factor (here, the 1/2).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. For each surd √n, find the largest perfect square k² dividing n; write n = k²·m.
  2. Rewrite √n = k√m.
  3. Check that the square-free parts m agree across all surds in the expression.
  4. Combine coefficients of the common surd: a√m ± b√m = (a±b)√m.
  5. Multiply through by any outer constants and present in the form (a√m)/c or a√m as required.

Worked Micro-Example

Simplify $\tfrac{1}{2}(\sqrt{675}-\sqrt{48})$. \\ $675 = 225\times 3 \Rightarrow \sqrt{675}=15\sqrt{3}$. \\ $48 = 16\times 3 \Rightarrow \sqrt{48}=4\sqrt{3}$. \\ $\tfrac{1}{2}(15\sqrt{3}-4\sqrt{3}) = \tfrac{1}{2}(11\sqrt{3}) = \tfrac{11\sqrt{3}}{2}.$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Definite integral of a power function evaluating to a difference of surds, e.g. $\int_a^b x^{1/2}\,dx$.
After applying the power rule, factor each surd and combine on a common kernel before stating the exact answer.
Pythagoras / distance problems giving lengths like $\sqrt{200}+\sqrt{72}$.
Reduce to $10\sqrt{2}+6\sqrt{2}=16\sqrt{2}$; same kernel-extraction logic.
Rationalising denominators where numerator surds must first be simplified, e.g. $\dfrac{\sqrt{50}}{\sqrt{2}}$.
Simplify $\sqrt{50}=5\sqrt{2}$ first, then cancel: result is $5$.
Quadratic formula yielding $\sqrt{discriminant}$ with a non-prime radicand.
Factor the discriminant to extract square factors so the roots appear in lowest surd form.
Vector magnitudes producing $\sqrt{n}$ where n has square factors.
Reduce $|\mathbf{v}|=\sqrt{n}$ to $k\sqrt{m}$ before reporting.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating √675 − √48 as if it equalled √(675−48) = √627 — surds do not distribute over subtraction.
  • Believing the radicands must be equal to combine, missing that only the square-free kernels need to match.
  • Thinking 'exact form' allows √675 unsimplified because no decimal was used.

Pitfalls

  • Incomplete factorisation, e.g. stopping at √675 = 5√27 instead of continuing to 15√3.
  • Arithmetic slips when factoring large numbers (missing that 225 | 675).
  • Forgetting to multiply the simplified surd by any outer constant or fractional coefficient.
  • Sign errors when subtracting coefficients of the common surd.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure Maths surd manipulation (P1/C1 level), routinely required when questions ask for answers in 'exact form' or 'in the form a√b' across algebra, integration, and geometry contexts.
