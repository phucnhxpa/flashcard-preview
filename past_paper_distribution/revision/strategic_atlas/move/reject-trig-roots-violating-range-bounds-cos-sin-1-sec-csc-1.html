Trigonometry

Reject trig roots violating range bounds (|cos|, |sin| ≤ 1; |sec|, |csc| ≥ 1)

Family: Domain Validation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires after solving a polynomial in secθ, cosθ, sinθ, or cscθ (often a quadratic produced via an identity substitution like tan²θ = sec²θ − 1) and obtaining two or more candidate values. Before converting to angles, inspect each candidate against the bounded range of the underlying primary ratio. Cues: secθ or cscθ with absolute value < 1, or cosθ or sinθ with absolute value > 1. Whenever a 'reciprocal' ratio appears, silently rewrite it as cosθ or sinθ and apply the −1 ≤ … ≤ 1 test.

Motivation — Why this works

Algebraic manipulation can produce roots that no real angle satisfies, because cosθ and sinθ are bounded in [−1, 1] and consequently |secθ|, |cscθ| ≥ 1. The identity substitution itself does not introduce extraneous roots, but the algebra has no memory of the bounded range, so the constraint must be re-imposed at the end. Skipping this filter generates phantom angles and costs the validation mark. The principle is simply: the equation lives on the unit circle, so any candidate outside it is geometrically meaningless.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the quadratic has produced two roots in sec/cosec/cos/sin and that one of them looks suspiciously small (for sec/cosec) or large (for cos/sin).
  2. Rule out the offending root by converting to the primary ratio: secθ = 2/3 ⇒ cosθ = 3/2, which violates |cosθ| ≤ 1.
  3. Commit to the remaining root because it sits inside the legal range and will produce genuine angles in the given interval.
  4. Bridge to the next step: take the surviving value (e.g. cosθ = −1/3) into the angle-finding stage using arccos plus symmetry.
  5. Tactically, write a one-line justification ('reject sec θ = 2/3 since |cos θ| ≤ 1') so the examiner sees the validation explicitly.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. List all algebraic roots of the trig polynomial.
  2. For each root, rewrite in terms of cosθ or sinθ.
  3. Check whether |cosθ| ≤ 1 (or |sinθ| ≤ 1); equivalently |secθ| ≥ 1 or |cscθ| ≥ 1.
  4. Discard any root failing the bound, with a brief written reason.
  5. Carry only the surviving root(s) into the angle-extraction stage.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $2\sec^2\theta + 5\sec\theta - 3 = 0$ for $\theta \in [0°, 360°)$. Factorise: $(2\sec\theta - 1)(\sec\theta + 3) = 0$, so $\sec\theta = \tfrac12$ or $\sec\theta = -3$. The first gives $\cos\theta = 2$, impossible since $|\cos\theta|\le 1$, so reject. Keep $\cos\theta = -\tfrac13$, then $\theta = \arccos(-\tfrac13) \approx 109.47°$ or $250.53°$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic in sinθ or cosθ directly
Reject any root with absolute value > 1 (e.g. sinθ = 1.2).
Quadratic in cscθ
Convert to sinθ = 1/cscθ and reject if |cscθ| < 1, i.e. |sinθ| > 1.
Equations involving tanθ or cotθ
No range bound to apply (tan, cot are unbounded), but still exclude θ where the ratio is undefined, e.g. cosθ = 0 for tanθ.
After squaring both sides of a trig equation
Substitute each candidate angle back into the original equation to discard extraneous roots introduced by squaring.

Common Misreadings

  • Thinking secθ must be positive and rejecting the negative root instead.
  • Believing the substitution tan²θ = sec²θ − 1 itself introduces extraneous roots and over-rejecting.
  • Confusing the bound for sec/csc (|·| ≥ 1) with that for cos/sin (|·| ≤ 1).

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the range check entirely and reporting four angles instead of two.
  • Rejecting the wrong root by misapplying the inequality direction.
  • Stating 'reject' without justification, losing the validation/communication mark.
  • Computing arccos of an out-of-range value on the calculator and being misled by a 'math error' rather than recognising the cause.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure (P2/P3) trig-equation work: quadratics in a single trig ratio, identities sec²θ = 1 + tan²θ and csc²θ = 1 + cot²θ, and solutions in a given degree or radian interval.
