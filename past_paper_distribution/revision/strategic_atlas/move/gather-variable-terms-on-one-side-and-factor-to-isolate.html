Algebra & Functions

Gather variable terms on one side and factor to isolate

Family: Gather And Factor Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever the variable you are trying to make the subject appears in two or more additive terms, typically on opposite sides of the equation. Common cues: after cross-multiplying a rational equation such as $y(x-1) = 3x+2$, after expanding brackets in a 'make $x$ the subject' problem, or after differentiating implicitly so that $\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x$ appears in several terms. A single rearrangement step cannot isolate the variable; you need to collect-then-factor.

Motivation — Why this works

If $x$ appears linearly in several additive terms, the equation has the form $Ax + B = Cx + D$, which can always be rewritten as $x(A-C) = D-B$. Factoring converts multiple occurrences of $x$ into a single occurrence multiplying a coefficient, after which one division finishes the job. This is the only clean algebraic route — you cannot 'cancel' $x$ from a sum, because $x$ is not a common factor of the whole side.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the target variable $x$ appears on both sides of the expanded equation, so no single inverse operation will isolate it.
  2. I rule out dividing through by $x$ (it is not a factor of every term) and rule out treating it as quadratic (no $x^2$ appears — it is linear in $x$).
  3. I commit to the gather-and-factor pattern because the equation is linear in $x$ of the form $Ax+B=Cx+D$, which always admits this resolution.
  4. First concrete operation: move every term containing $x$ to one side and every term free of $x$ to the other, watching signs carefully.
  5. Inside the move I factor $x$ out of the collected side, then divide by the resulting bracket, noting any value that would make that bracket zero as an excluded case.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Clear fractions if present (cross-multiply) and expand all brackets so every term is visible.
  2. Identify every term that contains the target variable.
  3. Add/subtract to move all such terms to one side and all other terms to the other side.
  4. Factor the target variable out of the collected side as a common factor.
  5. Divide both sides by the remaining bracket to isolate the variable, recording the restriction that the bracket $\neq 0$.

Worked Micro-Example

Make $x$ the subject of $y = \dfrac{3x+2}{x-1}$. Cross-multiply: $y(x-1) = 3x+2$, so $yx - y = 3x + 2$. Gather $x$-terms: $yx - 3x = y + 2$. Factor: $x(y-3) = y+2$. Divide: $x = \dfrac{y+2}{y-3}$, with $y \neq 3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Making $r$ the subject of $A = \dfrac{P(1+r)}{1-r}$
After cross-multiplying $A(1-r) = P(1+r)$, expand to get $r$-terms on both sides, then collect and factor $r$ exactly as above.
Implicit differentiation, e.g. $x^2 + xy + y^2 = 1$ differentiated to $2x + y + x\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x} + 2y\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x} = 0$
Treat $\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x$ as the target variable: collect its terms on one side and factor it out before dividing.
Solving a linear equation with the unknown on both sides, e.g. $5x - 7 = 2x + 8$
No factoring of a coefficient bracket is needed because the unknown's coefficient becomes a number; otherwise the logic is identical.
Rearranging a logarithmic or exponential formula after substitution reduces it to linear form, e.g. solving for $x$ in $kx = a + bx$
Apply the same collect-and-factor step on $x$; the coefficient bracket $(k-b)$ must be non-zero.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the equation as quadratic and trying to use the quadratic formula when it is actually linear in the target variable.
  • Trying to 'cancel' $x$ by dividing both sides by $x$ even though $x$ is not a factor of every term.
  • Believing that the target variable cannot be isolated because it appears more than once, and giving up or leaving the answer implicit.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when moving terms across the equals sign (e.g. writing $yx + 3x$ instead of $yx - 3x$).
  • Forgetting to factor the variable out cleanly, leaving an expression like $yx - 3x$ instead of $x(y-3)$.
  • Omitting the restriction that the factored bracket cannot equal zero (e.g. $y \neq 3$), which often corresponds to a horizontal asymptote or domain exclusion of the inverse function.
  • Dividing only part of the right-hand side by the bracket rather than the whole expression.

A-level Scope Notes

This move is core to IAL P1–P4 'change of subject' questions, inverse-function problems, and Further Pure implicit differentiation; no techniques beyond linear algebra and basic factorisation are required.
