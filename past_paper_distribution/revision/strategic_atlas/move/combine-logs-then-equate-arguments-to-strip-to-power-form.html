Algebra & Functions

Combine logs then equate arguments to strip to power form

Family: Log Stripping Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation has been manipulated into the shape $\log_{10} M = (\text{sum of }\log_{10}\text{ terms})$, or more generally $\log_a A = \log_a B$. Typical cues: a model question asking for $M = pr^q$ (or $y = ax^n$, $y = ab^x$) after working from a linearised log–log or log–linear relationship. Also fires when a constant on one side has been rewritten as $\log_{10}(10^c)$ to match the log on the other side. The target form is explicitly logarithm-free.

Motivation — Why this works

The function $\log_a$ is one-to-one on $(0,\infty)$, so $\log_a A = \log_a B \Rightarrow A = B$. The addition law $\log_a x + \log_a y = \log_a(xy)$ lets a sum of logs be written as a single log, after which both sides have the structure $\log_a(\cdot)$ and the logs can be removed. This converts an additive log-linear relationship into a multiplicative power-law form, which is exactly the shape $M = pr^q$ required.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice both sides are (or can be made into) a single $\log_{10}$ of an expression — the LHS is already $\log_{10} M$ and the RHS is a sum of $\log_{10}$ terms.
  2. I rule out exponentiating each term separately or applying $10^{(\cdot)}$ to a sum of logs as written — that would invite errors; cleaner to consolidate the RHS first.
  3. I commit to using the addition rule to merge the RHS into one logarithm, because then injectivity of $\log_{10}$ lets me strip logs cleanly.
  4. Concrete first operation: rewrite any constant $c$ as $\log_{10}(10^c)$ if needed, then combine $\log_{10}(r^q) + \log_{10}(10^c) = \log_{10}(10^c r^q)$.
  5. Tactically, I keep $p = 10^c$ symbolic until the end, then evaluate to the precision the question asks for (often 3 s.f.), and read off $q$ directly as the exponent of $r$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Ensure each side is a single $\log_{10}$: rewrite any constant $c$ as $\log_{10}(10^c)$.
  2. Apply the addition rule: $\log_{10}(r^q) + \log_{10}(10^c) = \log_{10}(10^c \cdot r^q)$.
  3. Equation now reads $\log_{10} M = \log_{10}(10^c \cdot r^q)$.
  4. Use injectivity of $\log_{10}$ to equate arguments: $M = 10^c \cdot r^q$.
  5. Identify $p = 10^c$ and $q$ from the exponent of $r$; evaluate $p$ numerically to required s.f.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\log_{10} M = 1.93\log_{10} r + 0.684$. Rewrite: $\log_{10} M = \log_{10}(r^{1.93}) + \log_{10}(10^{0.684})$. Combine: $\log_{10} M = \log_{10}(10^{0.684}\, r^{1.93})$. Strip logs: $M = 10^{0.684} r^{1.93} \approx 4.83\, r^{1.93}$, so $p \approx 4.83$, $q = 1.93$ (3 s.f.).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Natural log relationship $\ln y = a\ln x + b$
Rewrite $b = \ln(e^b)$, combine to $\ln y = \ln(e^b x^a)$, strip to $y = e^b x^a$.
Exponential model $\log_{10} y = (\log_{10} b) x + \log_{10} a$
Combine RHS as $\log_{10}(a b^x)$, strip to $y = a b^x$ (linearised exponential, not power, law).
Reverse direction (linearising for a straight-line graph)
Start from $M = pr^q$ and take $\log_{10}$ of both sides to obtain $\log_{10} M = q \log_{10} r + \log_{10} p$, identifying gradient and intercept.
General base $\log_a A = \log_a B$
Same injectivity argument gives $A = B$ provided $A,B>0$ and $a>0$, $a\neq 1$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $1.93 \log_{10} r + 0.684$ as already a single log and stripping prematurely to get $M = 1.93 r + 0.684$.
  • Reading the constant $0.684$ as $\log_{10}(0.684)$ rather than as a value that must be rewritten as $\log_{10}(10^{0.684})$.
  • Believing the relationship is exponential ($M = pq^r$) when the coefficient sits in front of $\log_{10} r$, which signals a power law $M = pr^q$.

Pitfalls

  • Misapplying the addition rule as $\log a + \log b = \log(a+b)$ instead of $\log(ab)$.
  • Stripping logs when only one side is a single log and the other is still a sum of logs plus a constant.
  • Leaving the answer as $p = 10^{0.684}$ when a numerical value to a stated accuracy is required.
  • Rounding $10^{0.684}$ too early in multi-part problems where $p$ is reused.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 logarithm content and the standard exponential-model/linearisation questions; uses only the laws of logarithms and injectivity of $\log_a$ for $a>0$, $a\neq 1$.
