Integration

Algebraically pre-process the integrand into a sum of standard x^n or e^{kx} terms

Family: Algebraic Preparation Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integrand is presented in a compact form that hides its true structure: a single fraction p(x)/(ax^k) with a polynomial numerator over a monomial denominator, or an expression like (f(x))^2 where f contains an exponential e^{g(x)}. Visual cues include: (i) one fraction bar with multiple numerator terms over a single power of x, (ii) a squared bracket inside ∫y² dx (volume of revolution) where y itself is a product or exponential, (iii) fractional exponents like e^{-x/2} that will simplify under squaring. Equivalently: 'before integrating, can I split or expand this into separate terms each matching a standard rule?'

Motivation — Why this works

The power rule ∫x^n dx = x^{n+1}/(n+1) (n ≠ −1) and ∫e^{kx} dx = e^{kx}/k only apply to clean monomial or exponential forms — not to fractions or squared products as written. Splitting a fraction term-by-term, or applying the index law (e^a)^2 = e^{2a}, converts the integrand into a SUM of such standard forms, each integrable directly or via a tractable IBP. This is almost always the cheapest first step: it sidesteps substitution and partial fractions entirely, or it cleans up an integrand so the heavier technique (IBP, reduction) becomes routine.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Look at the integrand and ask: does it match a standard form (x^n, e^{kx}, sin/cos, 1/x) directly? If not, can simple algebra get it there?
  2. Rule out heavier tools: substitution is unwarranted if there is no inner-function/derivative pairing; partial fractions need a factored polynomial denominator (not a single x^k); trig identities are irrelevant here.
  3. Spot the structural giveaway — either a single denominator dividing every numerator term, or a squared expression that can be expanded using index laws like (e^{a})^2 = e^{2a} and (cx)^2 = c²x².
  4. Commit to the move because it strictly simplifies and never introduces new variables or constants of integration to track.
  5. Execute the algebra carefully: divide each numerator term separately, OR square each factor separately, watching signs and constants.
  6. Re-read the new integrand and confirm each term is now a standard form before reaching for the integration rule.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the algebraic obstacle: a fraction p(x)/(ax^k), or a power (cf(x))^n where f involves e^{g(x)}.
  2. If a fraction: split as Σ (numerator term)/(ax^k), then simplify each quotient using x^a/x^b = x^{a−b} to obtain a sum of αx^n terms.
  3. If a squared exponential product (cx·e^{g(x)})^2: apply (cx)^2 = c²x² and (e^{g(x)})^2 = e^{2g(x)} separately.
  4. Pull any numeric constants outside the integral sign.
  5. Integrate each resulting standard term using ∫x^n dx = x^{n+1}/(n+1) (n ≠ −1), ∫x^{−1} dx = ln|x|, or ∫e^{kx} dx = e^{kx}/k (or set up IBP if a polynomial × exponential remains).
  6. Recombine and add +C (or evaluate between limits).

Worked Micro-Example

Compute $\int \frac{8 - 5x^2}{2x^2}\,dx$. Split: $\frac{8}{2x^2} - \frac{5x^2}{2x^2} = 4x^{-2} - \tfrac{5}{2}$. Integrate: $4 \cdot \frac{x^{-1}}{-1} - \tfrac{5}{2}x + C = -\frac{4}{x} - \tfrac{5}{2}x + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Polynomial numerator over x^k where one quotient gives x^{−1}
After splitting, the x^{−1} term integrates to ln|x|, not via the power rule — e.g. (3x³ + 2x − 1)/x² → 3x + 2x^{−1} − x^{−2}.
Volume of revolution V = π∫y² dx with y = cx·e^{g(x)}
Square component-wise: (cx)² = c²x² and (e^{g(x)})² = e^{2g(x)}. Factor out c²π, then apply IBP to x²·e^{2g(x)}.
Squared bracket of a sum, e.g. (x + e^{-x})²
Expand as x² + 2xe^{-x} + e^{-2x} — a sum of three standard-form terms, each integrable separately.

Common Misreadings

  • Attempting u-substitution on the whole fraction (e.g. u = 2x²) when no chain-rule pairing exists.
  • Believing ∫(f/g) dx = (∫f dx)/(∫g dx) and integrating numerator and denominator separately — this identity is false.
  • Confusing (e^a)² with e^{a²}: squaring the EXPONENT instead of doubling it.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to apply the negative exponent rule correctly: ∫x^{−2} dx = x^{−1}/(−1) = −1/x, NOT +1/x.
  • Sign errors when splitting a fraction — losing the minus sign on a subtracted numerator term.
  • Forgetting to square the numeric coefficient when squaring a product (e.g. writing 10 instead of 100 from (10x)²).
  • Halving the exponent instead of doubling when squaring e^{−x/2} — writing e^{−x/4} instead of e^{−x}.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure scope: power rule and ∫e^{kx} dx are standard (formula booklet lists ∫e^{kx} dx = e^{kx}/k). Volume of revolution V = π∫y² dx is a P3/P4 staple. Avoid generalising to ∫(polynomial)/(general polynomial) — that requires partial fractions, a separate technique.
