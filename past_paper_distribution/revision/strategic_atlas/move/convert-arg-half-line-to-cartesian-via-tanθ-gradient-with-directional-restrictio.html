Complex Numbers

Convert arg half-line to Cartesian via tan(θ) gradient with directional restriction

Family: Arg To Cartesian Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a locus is given in the form arg(z − z₀) = θ (a half-line from z₀ at angle θ to the positive real direction) and you need to intersect it with another locus (typically a circle |z − a| = r) given or convertible to Cartesian form. Also fires when asked for the Cartesian equation of an arg-type locus, or when finding points where a half-line meets axes/lines. Recognise it whenever 'arg(...) = constant' appears alongside an algebraic substitution need.

Motivation — Why this works

arg(z − z₀) = θ describes the set of points whose displacement from z₀ has fixed direction θ, i.e. a ray (half-line) from z₀ with gradient tan(θ). So the underlying full line is y − y₀ = tan(θ)(x − x₀), but only the half on the side where the displacement vector (cos θ, sin θ) points is valid. Converting to Cartesian unlocks substitution into circle/line equations; the directional constraint then filters spurious intersections from the opposite ray.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice arg(z − z₀) = θ — this is a half-line from z₀, not a full line, and z₀ itself is excluded.
  2. I rule out treating it as a full line: any algebraic intersection must be filtered by the direction of the ray.
  3. I commit to gradient form because tan(θ) is defined and finite (θ ≠ ±π/2); otherwise I'd switch to a vertical-line or parametric description.
  4. I write the full line y − y₀ = tan(θ)(x − x₀), then encode the direction by an inequality on x − x₀ (sign matches cos θ) or on y − y₀ (sign matches sin θ).
  5. On substitution into the second locus, I solve algebraically and discard any roots violating the directional inequality.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify z₀ = x₀ + y₀ i and the fixed angle θ.
  2. Check θ ≠ ±π/2 (else use x = x₀ with a y-inequality instead).
  3. Compute m = tan(θ) and write y − y₀ = m(x − x₀).
  4. Record direction: x − x₀ has the sign of cos θ; y − y₀ has the sign of sin θ.
  5. Substitute into the partner equation, solve, then reject roots failing the directional inequality.
  6. Also exclude z = z₀ itself if it appears as a root.

Worked Micro-Example

Locus: $\arg(w - 8 - 6i) = -\tfrac{3\pi}{4}$. Then $m = \tan(-3\pi/4) = 1$, giving full line $y - 6 = 1\cdot(x - 8)$, i.e. $y = x - 2$. Since $\cos(-3\pi/4) < 0$ and $\sin(-3\pi/4) < 0$, the half-line is $\{(x,y): y = x-2,\ x < 8\}$. Intersecting with $(x-8)^2+(y-6)^2 = 10$ gives $2(x-8)^2 = 10$, so $x = 8 \pm \sqrt{5}$; only $x = 8 - \sqrt{5}$ is valid.

Variants — Same move, different surface

arg(z − a) = π/2 or −π/2
Gradient is undefined; use the vertical line x = Re(a) restricted by y > Im(a) (for π/2) or y < Im(a) (for −π/2).
arg(z − a) = 0 or π
Horizontal half-line y = Im(a); restrict by x > Re(a) for θ = 0, or x < Re(a) for θ = π.
arg(z + a) = θ rewritten as arg(z − (−a)) = θ
Identify z₀ = −a before applying the gradient; sign errors are common here.
arg((z − a)/(z − b)) = θ
Different locus type — this is an arc of a circle, not a half-line; do not apply this move.
Half-line meeting a perpendicular bisector |z − a| = |z − b|
Convert both to Cartesian (line + line) and solve simultaneously, still filtering by the half-line's direction.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating arg(z − z₀) = θ as a full line and accepting both intersection points with a circle.
  • Confusing θ with θ + π — these give the same gradient but opposite rays.
  • Reading arg(z + z₀) = θ as a ray from z₀ instead of from −z₀.
  • Assuming the starting point z₀ lies on the locus (it does not; arg is undefined there).

Pitfalls

  • Sign of tan: e.g. tan(−3π/4) = +1 (third-quadrant reference), not −1.
  • Forgetting to translate the directional constraint into an inequality before substitution, leading to extraneous roots.
  • Using cos θ / sin θ signs incorrectly when θ is negative or exceeds π in absolute value — reduce to standard interval first.
  • Including z₀ as a solution when it accidentally satisfies the Cartesian line equation.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL FP2/FP3 Argand-diagram material: half-line loci arg(z − z₀) = θ, intersection with circles |z − a| = r and perpendicular bisectors. No use of complex inequalities or regions beyond the syllabus.
