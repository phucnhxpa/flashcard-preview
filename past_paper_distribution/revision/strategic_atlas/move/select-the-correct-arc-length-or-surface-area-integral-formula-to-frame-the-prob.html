Integration

Select the correct arc length or surface area integral formula to frame the problem

Family: Formula Selection Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question asks for the 'length of a curve', 'exact length', 'arc length', 'surface area generated by rotating', or 'area of the surface of revolution' — standard FP3 Chapter 4 applications of integration. Visual cues include a curve given as y = f(x), x = g(y), parametric pair (x(t), y(t)), or polar r(θ), accompanied by definite limits. The phrase 'using calculus' alongside a geometric measurement of a curve is a strong signal. Distinguish from area-under-curve or volume-of-revolution questions, which use different formulas.

Motivation — Why this works

Arc length and surface area of revolution each have a specific integral formula derived from the line element ds = √(1 + (dy/dx)²) dx (or its parametric/polar equivalents). Choosing the correct formula is the essential first commitment that frames every subsequent step — differentiate, square, add 1, simplify the radical, integrate. A wrong formula choice (e.g. omitting the 2πy factor for surface area, or rotating about the wrong axis) invalidates all downstream work. The underlying principle is Pythagoras applied to an infinitesimal arc, scaled by 2π·radius for surfaces.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Read the question carefully: identify whether it asks for 'length' (arc length) or 'surface area' (surface of revolution); these are distinct formulas.
  2. Rule out other integration applications: this is not area under a curve (∫y dx) nor volume of revolution (π∫y² dx), so those formulas do not apply.
  3. Identify the form of the curve: explicit Cartesian y = f(x), inverse Cartesian x = g(y), parametric (x(t), y(t)), or polar r(θ) — this dictates which form of ds to use.
  4. If surface area, identify the axis of revolution: rotation about the x-axis uses radius y (factor 2πy), rotation about the y-axis uses radius x (factor 2πx).
  5. Commit to the formula and write it down with the correct limits before differentiating — this anchors the rest of the solution.
  6. Anticipate that 1 + (dy/dx)² will simplify to a perfect square (FP3 questions are designed this way), so the integrand becomes tractable.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. For Cartesian arc length: write L = ∫_a^b √(1 + (dy/dx)²) dx.
  2. For Cartesian arc length with x = g(y): write L = ∫_c^d √(1 + (dx/dy)²) dy.
  3. For surface area about the x-axis: write S = 2π ∫_a^b y √(1 + (dy/dx)²) dx.
  4. For surface area about the y-axis: write S = 2π ∫_a^b x √(1 + (dy/dx)²) dx (or use dy with dx/dy).
  5. For parametric curves: replace ds with √((dx/dt)² + (dy/dt)²) dt and convert limits to parameter values.
  6. Identify and substitute the limits from the question, then proceed to compute the derivative(s).

Worked Micro-Example

Curve y = \tfrac{2}{3}x^{3/2}, find arc length on [0, 3]. Recognise 'length' → choose L = ∫_0^3 √(1 + (dy/dx)²) dx. Then dy/dx = x^{1/2}, so 1 + (dy/dx)² = 1 + x, giving L = ∫_0^3 √(1+x)\,dx = \tfrac{2}{3}[(1+x)^{3/2}]_0^3 = \tfrac{2}{3}(8 - 1) = \tfrac{14}{3}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Curve given parametrically as x(t), y(t)
Use L = ∫ √((dx/dt)² + (dy/dt)²) dt; for surface area about x-axis, S = 2π ∫ y(t) √((dx/dt)² + (dy/dt)²) dt. Convert limits to t-values.
Curve given in polar form r = f(θ)
Use L = ∫ √(r² + (dr/dθ)²) dθ — same Pythagorean structure adapted to polar coordinates.
Curve given as x = g(y) with y-limits
Swap roles: L = ∫_c^d √(1 + (dx/dy)²) dy; for surface about y-axis, S = 2π ∫ x √(1 + (dx/dy)²) dy.
Surface of revolution about the y-axis instead of x-axis
Replace the radius factor: use 2πx instead of 2πy in the integrand, since the radius of each circular slice is the x-coordinate.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing arc length with surface area — omitting the 2πy factor when surface area is asked, or including a spurious 2πy when only length is asked.
  • Mistaking the question for volume of revolution (π∫y² dx) and using the wrong formula entirely.
  • Rotating about the y-axis but reflexively using 2πy (the x-axis form) instead of 2πx.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the 2π factor in surface area problems.
  • Failing to simplify 1 + (dy/dx)² before integrating, leading to an apparently intractable square root.
  • Forgetting to convert limits when switching from Cartesian to parametric form.
  • Using the wrong variable of integration when the curve is given as x = g(y) — must use dy and dx/dy.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays strictly within Edexcel IAL Further Maths FP3 Chapter 4. The arc length and surface area formulas appear in the IAL Further Maths formula booklet under 'Further calculus'. Avoid generalisations to surfaces parameterised by two variables (double integrals) — these are out of scope.
