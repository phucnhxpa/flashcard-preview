Strategic Atlas / Integration / Polar Area Formula
Integration

Apply the polar area formula ½∫r²dθ with correct limits

Family: Polar Area Formula Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question asks for the area of a region enclosed by a polar curve r = f(θ), or bounded by a polar curve and one or two radial lines (θ = α, θ = β). Phrases like 'area enclosed by the curve', 'area of the region', 'area swept out', or 'area of one loop/petal' are direct cues. Also fires when comparing/subtracting regions described in polar form.

Motivation — Why this works

An infinitesimal sector of radius r and angle dθ has area ½r²dθ, so summing these sectors gives A = ½∫_α^β r² dθ. This is the natural polar analogue of ∫y dx and is exact because each thin wedge is well approximated by a circular sector. The limits α, β must correspond to the angular sweep that traces the boundary of the desired region exactly once.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the boundary is given as r = f(θ) (or two such curves), so Cartesian ∫y dx is awkward.
  2. Rule out converting to Cartesian: it usually destroys the clean trig structure.
  3. Commit to A = ½∫r² dθ because the polar element matches the geometry of the region.
  4. Bridge: determine the θ-range that traces the boundary once — use symmetry, zeros of r, or where curves intersect.
  5. Tactical: square r, expand using identities like cos²θ = ½(1+cos2θ), and integrate term-by-term.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Sketch the curve(s) and identify the region; mark the angular limits α and β.
  2. Check whether symmetry can simplify (e.g. integrate over half the range and double).
  3. Write A = ½∫_α^β r² dθ (or ½∫(r_outer² − r_inner²) dθ for a region between curves).
  4. Expand r² and rewrite using power-reduction identities (cos²θ, sin²θ, cosθcos2θ, etc.).
  5. Integrate and substitute the limits, simplifying surd/π terms.

Worked Micro-Example

For the cardioid r = 1 + cosθ: A = ½∫_0^{2π}(1+cosθ)² dθ = ½∫_0^{2π}(1 + 2cosθ + cos²θ) dθ = ½∫_0^{2π}(3/2 + 2cosθ + ½cos2θ) dθ = ½·(3π) = 3π/2.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Area between two polar curves r₁ (outer) and r₂ (inner)
Use A = ½∫_α^β (r₁² − r₂²) dθ where α, β are the θ-values at intersections.
Area of one petal/loop of r = a sin(nθ) or r = a cos(nθ)
Take limits as the consecutive zeros of r, e.g. 0 to π/n, so the petal is traced exactly once.
Region bounded by curve and a half-line θ = α
Use the half-line as one of the limits in ½∫r² dθ.
Curve with symmetry about the initial line
Compute ½∫_0^{π} r² dθ and double, after verifying the symmetry r(θ) = r(−θ).

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the integrand as r instead of r² (confusing with arc length).
  • Dropping the ½ factor.
  • Reading 'area' as a Cartesian ∫y dx problem and converting prematurely.

Pitfalls

  • Choosing limits that trace the curve twice (e.g. 0 to 2π for r = cos2θ), doubling the true area.
  • Forgetting to square cross-terms when expanding (1+cosθ)² etc.
  • Mis-handling intervals where r < 0, which can trace a reflected loop and inflate the area.
  • Using wrong intersection angles when subtracting two curves (failing to solve r₁ = r₂ correctly).

A-level Scope Notes

Polar coordinates and the area formula A = ½∫r² dθ are in Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3); curves are typically cardioids, limaçons, circles, or roses with standard trig identities sufficient for integration.
