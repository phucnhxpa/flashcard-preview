Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever solving for the common ratio r of a geometric series produces multiple algebraically valid candidates (typically two roots from a quadratic, or potentially ± from a square root, or multiple roots from a higher-power equation) AND the question states or implies that a sum to infinity S∞ exists. Look for phrases such as 'the series has a sum to infinity', 'the series is convergent', 'find S∞', or a given numerical value of S∞. The cue is essentially: 'I have more than one candidate for r, and the problem context guarantees convergence' — the convergence condition becomes the discriminator.

Motivation — Why this works

A geometric series ∑ar^(n−1) converges (has a sum to infinity) if and only if |r| < 1. This is a hard mathematical constraint that eliminates any candidate root lying outside the open interval (−1, 1). When the algebra yields multiple roots, the question is deliberately engineered so that only one root satisfies |r| < 1 — the convergence condition supplies the extra information needed to pin down the unique physical solution. Stating this condition explicitly is also required for full marks on 'show that' or 'justify' style questions.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that solving the algebraic equation in r (quadratic, square root, cube root, etc.) has yielded more than one candidate value, or could in principle yield more.
  2. Rule out picking arbitrarily: both/all candidates satisfy the algebra, so I cannot decide on algebraic grounds alone.
  3. Re-read the question: it mentions 'sum to infinity' or 'convergent' — this is the external constraint I need.
  4. Commit to the convergence test: |r| < 1 must hold for S∞ to exist; this is the discriminator.
  5. Compute |r| for each candidate; reject any with |r| ≥ 1, accept the unique one with |r| < 1.
  6. Write the rejection reason explicitly (e.g. 'r = 2 rejected since |r| ≥ 1, so S∞ would not exist') — this is required for the justification mark.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Solve the equation in r to obtain all candidate roots (factorise quadratic, take real n-th root, etc.).
  2. State the convergence requirement: 'For S∞ to exist, |r| < 1.'
  3. Compute |r| for each candidate.
  4. Reject any candidate with |r| ≥ 1, giving an explicit reason.
  5. Conclude with the unique accepted value of r and (if needed) substitute it back into S∞ = a/(1−r).

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose 3r^2 − 4r − 4 = 0, given that S∞ exists. Factorising: (3r+2)(r−2)=0, so r = −2/3 or r = 2. Since S∞ exists, |r|<1 is required. |2| = 2 ≥ 1, so reject r = 2. |−2/3| = 2/3 < 1, so r = −2/3 is the valid common ratio.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic in r yields two real roots
Reject the root with |r| ≥ 1; explicitly state the convergence condition.
Equation r^2 = k with k > 0 yields ±√k
Both signs may satisfy |r| < 1; use additional info (e.g. 'all terms positive' ⇒ r > 0, or sign of S∞ relative to a) to pick the sign.
Equation r^3 = k (e.g. r^3 = 1/64)
Take the unique real cube root (r = ∛k); cube roots of positives are uniquely positive in ℝ, so no sign ambiguity — just confirm |r| < 1 for consistency.
Question says 'series is convergent' (no explicit S∞ value)
Same |r| < 1 condition applies; the wording is equivalent.
Question states terms are positive and decreasing
Strengthen the constraint to 0 < r < 1 (rules out negative roots as well).

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming r must be positive because the given terms are positive, and rejecting a valid negative root like r = −2/3 instead of the truly invalid r = 2.
  • Treating the cube root r^3 = 1/64 as having a ± solution by analogy with square roots, and considering r = −1/4 (which actually cubes to −1/64).
  • Forgetting that |r| < 1 is needed and reporting both roots as answers.

Pitfalls

  • Writing r < 1 instead of |r| < 1 — this incorrectly admits large negative values such as r = −5.
  • Not stating the convergence condition explicitly — examiners require the reasoning to be visible for the justification mark.
  • Confusing cube roots with square roots: ∛(positive) is unique and positive, unlike √ which gives ± in selection problems.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays inside Edexcel IAL P-series scope (geometric series, S∞ = a/(1−r) for |r|<1, formula booklet entry under 'Series'). Do not invoke complex cube roots or radius of convergence — these are out of scope; the common ratio is always real at IAL.
