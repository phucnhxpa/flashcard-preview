Integration

Integrate polynomial times exponential using integration by parts

Family: Integration By Parts Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integrand is a product of a polynomial in x and an exponential e^{kx} (or e^{-kx}) that cannot be handled by inspection or substitution. Typical surfaces: ∫xe^{kx} dx, ∫(ax+b)e^{kx} dx, ∫x²e^{kx} dx, or such an integral arising on the RHS after a prior IBP step or differential-equation manipulation. The polynomial factor signals that one (or more) applications of IBP will reduce its degree to zero.

Motivation — Why this works

Integration by parts ∫u dv = uv − ∫v du works because differentiating a polynomial lowers its degree while integrating e^{kx} reproduces an exponential (up to a 1/k factor). Choosing u as the polynomial therefore strictly simplifies the residual integral with each application. After deg(polynomial) rounds, the remaining integrand is a pure exponential which integrates immediately. This is the standard LIATE-guided choice for polynomial × exponential products.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand factors as (polynomial in x) × (exponential e^{kx}); no obvious substitution collapses it.
  2. Rule out u-substitution (no inner-function/derivative pairing) and standard antiderivatives (the product is not in any table).
  3. Commit to IBP because differentiating the polynomial reduces its degree while the exponential is preserved on integration — the residual integral must be simpler.
  4. Bridge: assign u = polynomial factor, dv = e^{kx} dx, so du is a lower-degree polynomial and v = (1/k)e^{kx}.
  5. Tactically track the 1/k factor and signs carefully; if deg ≥ 2, plan to repeat IBP (or set out a tabular layout) until the polynomial vanishes.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the polynomial factor P(x) and exponential factor e^{kx}.
  2. Set u = P(x), dv = e^{kx} dx ⇒ du = P'(x) dx, v = (1/k)e^{kx}.
  3. Write ∫P(x)e^{kx} dx = (1/k)P(x)e^{kx} − (1/k)∫P'(x)e^{kx} dx.
  4. If P'(x) is still non-constant, repeat IBP on the residual integral; otherwise integrate directly.
  5. Simplify, factor out e^{kx} where neat, and add the constant of integration C.

Worked Micro-Example

Compute ∫ −6x e^{−4x} dx. Take u = −6x, dv = e^{−4x} dx, so du = −6 dx, v = −¼e^{−4x}. Then ∫ = (−6x)(−¼e^{−4x}) − ∫(−¼e^{−4x})(−6) dx = (3x/2)e^{−4x} − (3/2)∫e^{−4x} dx = (3x/2)e^{−4x} + (3/8)e^{−4x} + C.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Degree-2 polynomial × exponential, e.g. ∫x²e^{3x} dx
Apply IBP twice (or use the tabular/DI method) so that P(x) is differentiated down to a constant before the final exponential integration.
Linear × exponential with negative exponent, e.g. ∫(2x+1)e^{−x} dx
Same u-choice; v = −e^{−x}; track the cascading minus signs carefully.
Definite integral ∫_a^b xe^{kx} dx
Apply the same IBP, then evaluate [uv]_a^b − ∫_a^b v du with the limits substituted at the end.
Polynomial × sin/cos (analogue family)
Same u = polynomial choice; for degree ≥ 1 with trig, repeated IBP is straightforward since trig also reproduces under integration (no simultaneous-equation trick needed unless both factors are exponential/trig).
RHS of a separable/integrating-factor ODE producing ∫xe^{kx} dx
Recognise the embedded polynomial × exponential and apply IBP as a sub-step before resolving the ODE.

Common Misreadings

  • Choosing u = e^{kx} and dv = P(x) dx — this raises the polynomial degree on the residual integral, making the problem worse.
  • Treating ∫xe^{kx} dx as if it equals (1/k)·(½x²)e^{kx} by integrating each factor separately — a misuse of linearity.
  • Attempting a u-substitution like u = e^{kx} and forcing the polynomial into terms of u, which does not separate cleanly.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when k < 0 so v carries a negative coefficient and the −∫v du contributes another sign flip.
  • Forgetting the 1/k factor when integrating e^{kx}, leading to an answer off by a constant multiple.
  • Stopping after one IBP for a degree-2 polynomial, leaving an unfinished xe^{kx} term in the residual.
  • Omitting +C, or failing to factor out e^{kx} cleanly when the question expects a tidy closed form.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P3 integration by parts) and recurs in Further Pure differential equations. The tabular shortcut is acceptable as scratch work but final solutions should display the standard ∫u dv = uv − ∫v du layout.
