Coordinate Geometry

Derive conic tangent via point-slope then collapse to standard form

Family: Standard Tangent Formula Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger â€” When to use

Fires when a tangent to an ellipse, hyperbola, or parabola at a parametric point is required, especially under 'show that' phrasing where quoting the formula book is unsafe. Cues include the parametric point already given (e.g. $(a\cos\theta, b\sin\theta)$), the gradient $\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x$ obtainable by parametric differentiation, and a follow-up part needing axis intercepts, locus, or chord-of-contact work. Also fires when later parts ask for tangentâ€“normal geometry where the symmetric form is decisively easier than $y=mx+c$.

Motivation â€” Why this works

Starting from point-slope $y-y_1=m(x-x_1)$ with the parametric gradient guarantees the derivation is from first principles, securing method marks in 'show that' questions. The Pythagorean identity $\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta=1$ (or $\sec^2\theta-\tan^2\theta=1$) collapses the algebra into the symmetric standard form. This symmetric form exposes intercepts on the axes immediately ($a/\cos\theta$ and $b/\sin\theta$), making locus elimination and area calculations far cleaner than dealing with a gradient-intercept expression.

Thought Process â€” The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question asks for the tangent at a parametric point and the target form is the symmetric standard tangent â€” quoting alone is risky if 'show that' appears.
  2. Rule out implicit differentiation of $x^2/a^2+y^2/b^2=1$ as messier here; the parametric gradient $-\tfrac{b\cos\theta}{a\sin\theta}$ is already cheap to obtain.
  3. Commit to point-slope because it is the most defensible launchpad and lines up perfectly with a 'clear the fraction, then group' rearrangement.
  4. Bridge: multiply through by $\sin\theta$ (or $a\sin\theta$) to eliminate the denominator before grouping $x$ and $y$ terms on one side.
  5. Tactically watch for $\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta$ to appear on the constant side â€” that is the signal to invoke the identity and divide through to obtain the symmetric form.

Mechanic â€” Concrete operations

  1. Compute gradient parametrically: $\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}x}=\dfrac{\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}\theta}{\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}\theta}=-\dfrac{b\cos\theta}{a\sin\theta}$.
  2. Write $y-b\sin\theta = -\dfrac{b\cos\theta}{a\sin\theta}(x-a\cos\theta)$.
  3. Multiply by $a\sin\theta$: $a\sin\theta\,y - ab\sin^2\theta = -b\cos\theta\,x + ab\cos^2\theta$.
  4. Collect: $b\cos\theta\,x + a\sin\theta\,y = ab(\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta) = ab$.
  5. Divide by $ab$: $\dfrac{x\cos\theta}{a}+\dfrac{y\sin\theta}{b}=1$.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the tangent to $\tfrac{x^2}{9}+\tfrac{y^2}{4}=1$ at $P(3\cos\theta,2\sin\theta)$. Gradient $=-\tfrac{2\cos\theta}{3\sin\theta}$. Point-slope: $y-2\sin\theta=-\tfrac{2\cos\theta}{3\sin\theta}(x-3\cos\theta)$. Multiply by $3\sin\theta$ and collect: $2\cos\theta\,x+3\sin\theta\,y=6(\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta)=6$. Divide by $6$: $\tfrac{x\cos\theta}{3}+\tfrac{y\sin\theta}{2}=1$.

Variants â€” Same move, different surface

Hyperbola $\tfrac{x^2}{a^2}-\tfrac{y^2}{b^2}=1$ at $(a\sec\theta,b\tan\theta)$
Use gradient $\tfrac{b\sec\theta}{a\tan\theta}$ and identity $\sec^2\theta-\tan^2\theta=1$ to obtain $\tfrac{x\sec\theta}{a}-\tfrac{y\tan\theta}{b}=1$.
Parabola $y^2=4ax$ at $(at^2,2at)$
Gradient $1/t$; point-slope rearranges to $ty=x+at^2$ â€” no Pythagorean identity needed, just polynomial collection.
Rectangular hyperbola $xy=c^2$ at $(ct,c/t)$
Gradient $-1/t^2$; point-slope rearranges to $x+t^2 y=2ct$.
Tangent to ellipse at a non-parametric point $(x_1,y_1)$ on the curve
Use implicit differentiation to get $m=-\tfrac{b^2 x_1}{a^2 y_1}$, then identical rearrangement yields $\tfrac{xx_1}{a^2}+\tfrac{yy_1}{b^2}=1$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating a 'show that' as a 'state' question and quoting the standard tangent formula directly without derivation, losing method marks.
  • Reading the parametric point off the wrong curve (e.g. using $(a\sec\theta,b\tan\theta)$ on an ellipse) because the standard forms look superficially similar.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to multiply through by $\sin\theta$ (or $a\sin\theta$) early, leaving nested fractions that obscure the identity step.
  • Not invoking $\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta=1$ at the collection stage and leaving the constant side as $b\sin^2\theta+b\cos^2\theta$.
  • Sign slip on the hyperbola variant: forgetting that $\tfrac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}\theta}\sec\theta=\sec\theta\tan\theta$ and $\tfrac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}\theta}\tan\theta=\sec^2\theta$ produce a positive gradient, hence a minus sign in the final symmetric form.
