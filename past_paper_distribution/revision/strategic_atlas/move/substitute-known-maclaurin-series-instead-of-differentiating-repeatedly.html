Sequences & Series

Substitute known Maclaurin series instead of differentiating repeatedly

Family: Standard Series Substitution Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The function whose Maclaurin/Taylor series is required is built from elementary blocks — e^x, sin x, cos x, ln(1+x), (1+x)^n — either by composition (e.g. ln(1+sin x), e^{sin x}), by argument-substitution (e.g. e^{2x}, sin 3x, ln(1+2x)), or by product/sum (e.g. e^x cos x). Equivalently: the question asks for an expansion up to a fixed power x^n and the function visibly decomposes into pieces that appear in the formula booklet. A reliable verbal cue is 'find the series expansion of f(x) up to and including the term in x^n' where f is not itself a primitive elementary function but is assembled from them.

Motivation — Why this works

Differentiating a composite or product four or more times at x=0 explodes via the chain and product rules — each derivative spawns more terms and more sign-tracking. Quoting the booklet series for each building block and then doing algebraic series arithmetic (substitute, multiply, add, truncate) is strictly faster and less error-prone. The underlying principle is uniqueness of Maclaurin coefficients: any legal manipulation of convergent power series produces the correct expansion, so we are free to swap brutal calculus for clean algebra. As a bonus, this method offers an independent cross-check on a direct-differentiation answer.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that f(x) is not elementary itself but is a composition / product / argument-substitution of functions whose series are in the formula booklet (e^x, sin x, cos x, ln(1+x), (1+x)^n).
  2. Consider the differentiate-and-evaluate route: estimate how messy f''''(0) would be via product/chain rule — usually clearly worse, with many terms to combine.
  3. Commit to series substitution because every component has a memorised/booklet expansion, and the required order x^n is small enough that truncation keeps the algebra finite.
  4. Decide the working precision: since the answer is needed to order x^n, expand each inner piece to order x^n and keep only terms ≤ x^n at every multiplication/power step.
  5. Choose the assembly rule: argument substitution (replace x by 2x, −x, sin x, …), term-by-term multiplication (for products), or substitute the inner series into the outer series (for compositions).
  6. Execute the algebra, then collect like powers of x and present coefficients in simplest form; optionally verify one coefficient by a single derivative at 0.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the standard series needed and write each one truncated to the required order, e.g. e^x = 1 + x + x^2/2! + x^3/3! + ⋯, sin x = x − x^3/3! + ⋯, ln(1+u) = u − u^2/2 + u^3/3 − u^4/4 + ⋯.
  2. If the argument is modified, substitute it everywhere: e^{2x} = 1 + (2x) + (2x)^2/2! + ⋯; sin(3x) = 3x − (3x)^3/3! + ⋯; ln(1+2x) by replacing x with 2x (and noting validity |2x|<1).
  3. For a composition g(h(x)), let u = h(x) truncated to order x^n; compute u^2, u^3, … keeping only terms up to x^n.
  4. Substitute these powers into the outer series g(u) = a_0 + a_1 u + a_2 u^2 + ⋯, again truncating beyond x^n.
  5. For a product f(x)g(x), multiply the two truncated series term by term, discarding any product whose total degree exceeds n.
  6. Collect coefficients of like powers of x and simplify; state the answer up to and including the x^n term.

Worked Micro-Example

Expand $\ln(1+\sin x)$ up to $x^4$. Let $u=\sin x = x - x^3/6 + \cdots$, so $u^2 = x^2 - x^4/3 + \cdots$, $u^3 = x^3 + \cdots$, $u^4 = x^4 + \cdots$. Then $\ln(1+u) = u - u^2/2 + u^3/3 - u^4/4 = (x - x^3/6) - \tfrac12(x^2 - x^4/3) + \tfrac13 x^3 - \tfrac14 x^4$. Collecting: $x - \tfrac{x^2}{2} + \tfrac{x^3}{6} - \tfrac{x^4}{12}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Modified argument inside a single standard series, e.g. e^{−x}, sin(3x), ln(1+2x)
Substitute the new argument throughout the booklet series, e.g. e^{−x} = 1 − x + x^2/2! − x^3/3! + ⋯; for ln(1+2x) note the validity becomes |2x|<1, i.e. |x|<1/2.
Product of two standard series, e.g. e^x cos x or e^x sin x
Write both series truncated to order x^n and multiply term by term, dropping all products of total degree > n.
Composition g(h(x)), e.g. e^{sin x}, ln(1+sin x), cos(ln(1+x))
Truncate h(x) to order x^n, compute the needed powers h^k truncated to order x^n, and substitute into the outer series.
Verification of a derivative-based answer
Run the substitution method as an independent route; the coefficients must match those obtained from f^{(k)}(0)/k!.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 'find the Maclaurin series' as a mandate to differentiate from scratch, even when the function is clearly an assembly of standard blocks.
  • Confusing ln(1+u) with a non-existent series for ln(u) about 0, or quoting (1+x)^n's series when n is not the booklet's rational/integer case.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to adjust the argument: writing e^{2x} = 1 + x + x^2/2 + ⋯ instead of 1 + 2x + (2x)^2/2 + ⋯.
  • Failing to track which terms in u^2, u^3, … contribute up to the required power x^n — leading to missing or extra terms.
  • Sign errors when squaring/cubing a series with alternating signs (e.g. (x − x^3/6)^2).
  • Quoting the wrong sign pattern, e.g. alternating signs for e^x (which has all positive coefficients).
  • Ignoring the validity interval of ln(1+u): the substituted series only converges where |u|<1.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses the Maclaurin/Taylor series listed in the Edexcel IAL Mathematics/Further Mathematics formula booklet (P3/P4/FP2 depending on specification): e^x, sin x, cos x, ln(1+x), and (1+x)^n. Avoid invoking radius-of-convergence arguments beyond stating the simple validity (e.g. |x|<1 for ln(1+x)); do not appeal to general analytic-function theorems.
