Integration

Evaluate boundary brackets immediately after each IBP round

Family: Early Evaluation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever integration by parts is applied to a definite integral and produces a [uv]ₐᵇ term alongside a new integral. The cue is the appearance of a fully determined bracket whose value depends only on the limits, not on any remaining integration. Especially relevant when a second or third IBP round is anticipated, since unevaluated brackets accumulate and clutter the working.

Motivation — Why this works

A definite-integral IBP step yields [uv]ₐᵇ − ∫ₐᵇ v du'; the bracket is already a number once the limits are substituted. Reducing it immediately collapses symbolic clutter into a single constant, so subsequent rounds operate on a simpler equation like I = c − kJ. This lowers transcription risk and makes sign tracking cleaner when multiple constants are combined at the end.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice that the IBP output contains a bracket [uv]ₐᵇ that involves no remaining integral — it is a closed numerical expression.
  2. I rule out leaving it symbolic 'for neatness later'; experience shows mixed bracket-and-integral lines invite copy errors.
  3. I commit to evaluating now because the bracket value is independent of any further work on the residual integral.
  4. Bridge: substitute the upper and lower limits into uv, subtract, simplify to a single constant before rewriting the equation.
  5. Tactically, I keep e, ln 2, π/2 etc. in exact form, and watch for terms that vanish (e.g. x² = 0 at x = 0) so I can drop them outright.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. After IBP, isolate the [uv]ₐᵇ bracket from the residual integral.
  2. Substitute x = b and x = a into uv; compute uv(b) − uv(a) exactly.
  3. Simplify any zero factors or known constants (e⁰ = 1, sin 0 = 0, ln 1 = 0).
  4. Rewrite the equation with the bracket replaced by its numerical value before starting the next IBP.
  5. Repeat after each subsequent IBP round, then combine all constants at the very end.

Worked Micro-Example

For $I = \int_0^1 x^2 e^x\,dx$, IBP with $u = x^2$, $dv = e^x dx$ gives $I = [x^2 e^x]_0^1 - 2\int_0^1 x e^x\,dx$. Evaluate the bracket immediately: $[x^2 e^x]_0^1 = 1\cdot e - 0\cdot 1 = e$. So $I = e - 2J$, and a second IBP on $J$ yields $J = [xe^x]_0^1 - [e^x]_0^1 = e - (e-1) = 1$, hence $I = e - 2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Definite integration by substitution
Change the limits at the moment of substitution rather than reverting to x; evaluate the new definite integral directly in u.
Partial fractions with a definite integral
After integrating each fraction, substitute limits into each ln/arctan piece immediately and combine numerically rather than carrying symbolic logs.
Reduction formula applied to a definite integral
Each application produces a boundary term; evaluate it numerically before recursing so the recursion carries constants, not unevaluated brackets.
Volume of revolution involving IBP
Evaluate the [uv]ₐᵇ portion at once; this often produces clean multiples of π that simplify subsequent algebra.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating [uv]ₐᵇ as something that must be retained until the very last line, mirroring indefinite-integral habits.
  • Believing that 'evaluating early' means evaluating the full integral early, rather than just the boundary bracket.
  • Confusing the bracket with the residual integrand and substituting limits into the wrong expression.

Pitfalls

  • Mishandling zero factors, e.g. writing 0² · e⁰ = 1 because e⁰ = 1, instead of recognising the product is 0.
  • Forgetting e⁰ = 1, ln 1 = 0, or cos 0 = 1 when simplifying the bracket.
  • Sign error when subtracting uv(a) from uv(b), particularly when uv(a) is itself negative.
  • Mixing the evaluated constant back into the integrand by accident when rewriting the equation.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard for Edexcel IAL P3/P4 definite integration by parts and reduction-formula questions in Further Pure; uses only limit substitution into elementary functions within the syllabus.
