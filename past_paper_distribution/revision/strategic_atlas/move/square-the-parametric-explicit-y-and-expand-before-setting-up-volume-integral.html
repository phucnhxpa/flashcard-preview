Audit note

The atlas has 'integrand-preparation' for (e^a + b)² binomials and 'exponential-simplification' for e^{x/2}·e^{x/2}=e^x, but neither covers the common volume pattern of squaring (polynomial·exponential) as polynomial²·e^{2kx} then expanding the polynomial² before integrating.

Evidence: 6::Oct_2020::Q3, 6::Jan_2022::Q7, 6::Jun_2022::Q8, 6::Oct_2022::Q5

Trigger — When to use

Fires in any volume-of-revolution about the x-axis where $y$ is a product or sum involving an exponential, e.g. $y=e^{0.5x}-2$ or $y=\tfrac{1}{4}(4-x)e^{x/2}$. Before you can integrate $V=\pi\int y^2\,dx$ you must square $y$ and turn it into a sum of terms you can integrate term-by-term.

Motivation — Why this works

$V=\pi\int y^2\,dx$ needs a $y^2$ that is a sum of standard integrable pieces. Squaring a (polynomial)$\times e^{kx}$ gives (polynomial)$^2\,e^{2kx}$ via $e^{kx}\cdot e^{kx}=e^{2kx}$, and expanding the polynomial square produces clean monomial-times-exponential terms each handled by a known rule.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Recognise 'rotate about the x-axis' so the formula is $V=\pi\int y^2\,dx$ — the integrand is $y^2$, never $y$.
  2. Find the limits: one is usually $x=0$ (the y-axis); the other is the x-intercept from setting $y=0$ and solving (often a $\ln$ value).
  3. Square $y$ exactly: treat it as a binomial/product, never $\,(A-B)^2=A^2-B^2$.
  4. Collapse the exponential factor first: $(e^{kx})^2=e^{2kx}$ and $e^{x/2}\cdot e^{x/2}=e^x$ — do NOT write $e^{x^2/4}$.
  5. Expand the polynomial part of the square so every term is $a x^m e^{2kx}$ or a pure exponential/constant.
  6. Decide the route for each term: pure $e^{2kx}$ and constants integrate directly; $x^m e^{2kx}$ terms flag integration by parts later.
  7. Only then attach $\pi$ and the limits and integrate term-by-term.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write $V=\pi\int_{a}^{b} y^2\,dx$ and substitute the given $y$.
  2. Square: e.g. $(e^{0.5x}-2)^2=(e^{0.5x})^2-2\cdot 2\,e^{0.5x}+4=e^{x}-4e^{0.5x}+4$.
  3. For a product, pull out constants and use $e^{kx}\cdot e^{kx}=e^{2kx}$: $\left[\tfrac{1}{4}(4-x)e^{x/2}\right]^2=\tfrac{1}{16}(4-x)^2 e^{x}$.
  4. Expand the bracket square: $(4-x)^2=16-8x+x^2$, giving $\tfrac{1}{16}(16-8x+x^2)e^{x}$.
  5. Keep the constant factor (here $\tfrac{1}{16}$) explicit so it is not lost in the final answer.

Worked Micro-Example

For $y=e^{0.5x}-2$ rotated about the x-axis from $x=0$ to the intercept, set $V=\pi\int y^2\,dx$. Squaring gives $y^2=(e^{0.5x})^2-4e^{0.5x}+4=e^{x}-4e^{0.5x}+4$, so $V=\pi\int_0^{2\ln 2}(e^{x}-4e^{0.5x}+4)\,dx$, which integrates term-by-term to $\pi[e^{x}-8e^{0.5x}+4x]_0^{2\ln 2}=\pi(8\ln 2-5)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Binomial $y=e^{kx}+c$ (exponential plus constant), as in $y=e^{0.5x}-2$.
Expand as $A^2+2Ac+c^2$ giving $e^{2kx}$, a single-exponential middle term, and a constant — all integrate directly, no IBP.
Polynomial $\times$ exponential, e.g. $y=\tfrac{1}{4}(4-x)e^{x/2}$ or $y=10xe^{-x/2}$.
Square to (polynomial)$^2\,e^{2kx}$, expand the polynomial, then each $x^m e^{2kx}$ term needs repeated integration by parts.
Rational $y$ such as $y=\dfrac{12\sqrt{x}}{2x^2+3}$ giving $y^2=\dfrac{144x}{(2x^2+3)^2}$.
Squaring is mechanical; recognise the result is a $\dfrac{f'}{f^2}$-type integrand and integrate by the substitution $u=2x^2+3$ rather than expanding.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating it as $\int y\,dx$ (an area) and forgetting to square — volumes use $y^2$.
  • Thinking the squaring is trivial and skipping the polynomial expansion, then trying to integrate $(4-x)^2 e^{x}$ directly.
  • Assuming the squared exponential needs a substitution when in fact $(e^{kx})^2=e^{2kx}$ is a one-line index law.

Pitfalls

  • Writing $(A-B)^2=A^2-B^2$ and dropping the cross term $-2AB$.
  • Squaring the exponent instead of the function: $(e^{x/2})^2=e^{x}$, NOT $e^{x^2/4}$.
  • Losing a constant coefficient (e.g. the $\tfrac{1}{16}$, or squaring the $10$ in $10x$ to get $100$ not $10$) when assembling the final volume.
  • Forgetting the factor $\pi$, or integrating $e^{kx}$ without dividing by $k$.
