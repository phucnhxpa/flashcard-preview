Trigonometry

Enumerate general solutions then match to diagram position

Family: Solution Enumeration Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A trig equation like $\cos(kx) = 0$ or $\sin(kx) = c$ has infinitely many solutions, but the diagram labels a specific point (e.g. R, P, Q) as one particular intercept or extremum. Cues include a labelled point on a sketched curve where the value is asked for, and a multiple-angle argument $kx$ that compresses the period. The phrase 'find the x-coordinate of R' combined with a figure showing R after several oscillations signals this move.

Motivation — Why this works

The general solution of $\cos\theta = 0$ is $\theta = 90° + 180°n$, producing a discrete arithmetic sequence of $x$-values once divided by $k$. Listing these in order and cross-referencing with the figure pins down the specific solution corresponding to the labelled point. This is more reliable than estimating coordinates from the diagram's scale, which is rarely drawn accurately.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the labelled point is a zero (or extremum) of a transformed trig function, so the equation $\cos(kx) = 0$ governs its $x$-coordinate.
  2. Rule out reading the value directly off the diagram — figures are not to scale, and the multiple-angle factor means intercepts are closer together than they look.
  3. Commit to enumerating solutions in order because the sequence is arithmetic and the diagram tells us *which term* of the sequence to pick.
  4. Bridge: solve $\cos\theta = 0$ generally, then divide each $\theta$ by $k$ to list candidate $x$-values.
  5. Tactically count intercepts on the diagram from the origin (or from the leftmost feature) up to the labelled point to choose the right index.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the general solution: $\cos\theta = 0 \Rightarrow \theta = 90° + 180°n$.
  2. Substitute $\theta = kx$ and solve: $x = \dfrac{90° + 180°n}{k}$ for $n = 0, 1, 2, \dots$.
  3. List the first several positive values of $x$ in order.
  4. Read the diagram to determine which intercept (1st, 2nd, 3rd, …) is labelled.
  5. Quote the matching $x$-value as the answer.

Worked Micro-Example

Find $k$ where $R(k,0)$ lies on $y = \cos(2x°)$ and $R$ is the 3rd positive $x$-intercept.\\ $\cos(2x°) = 0 \Rightarrow 2x = 90, 270, 450, \dots$\\ $\Rightarrow x = 45, 135, 225, \dots$\\ The 3rd intercept gives $k = 225$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$\sin(kx°) = 0$ with a labelled intercept
Use $kx = 180°n$, so $x = 0, \tfrac{180}{k}, \tfrac{360}{k}, \dots$; include $x=0$ in the count.
Labelled maximum/minimum of $y = a\cos(kx)$
Solve $kx = 180°n$ for maxima (cos) or $kx = 90° + 180°n$ for extrema of sin; enumerate and match.
Equation $\cos(kx) = c$ with $|c|<1$
Two families of solutions per period: $kx = \pm\alpha + 360°n$; interleave both lists in increasing order before counting.
Phase-shifted curve $y = \cos(kx - \phi)$
Set $kx - \phi = 90° + 180°n$ and solve for $x$; sequence still arithmetic but offset.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the diagram as to scale and guessing the $x$-coordinate visually.
  • Assuming $R$ is the first intercept simply because it is the only one labelled.
  • Confusing intercepts of $\cos$ (starts at 1) with intercepts of $\sin$ (starts at 0), miscounting the first crossing.

Pitfalls

  • Stopping at the first solution ($x = 45$) instead of enumerating to the indexed intercept.
  • Forgetting to divide by $k$ after solving for $\theta = kx$.
  • Including negative-$x$ solutions when the diagram only shows the positive branch, shifting the count.
  • Counting $x = 0$ as a zero of cosine when $\cos(0)=1\ne 0$.

A-level Scope Notes
