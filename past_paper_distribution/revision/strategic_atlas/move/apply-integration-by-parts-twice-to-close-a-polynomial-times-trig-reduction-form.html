Integration

Apply integration by parts twice to close a polynomial-times-trig reduction formula

Family: Reduction Formula Ibp Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when establishing a reduction formula for I_n = ∫ x^n · trig(x) dx (or trig of a composite like x²) where a single IBP leaves the 'wrong' trig function (sin became cos, or cos became sin) so the integral is not yet a member of the I_k family. The visual cue is a polynomial × trig integrand, an index-gap of 2 (or 4 for composite arguments like cos(x²)) between I_n and the target I_{n-2} or I_{n-4}, and the failure of one IBP to return to the original trig factor. Equivalently: 'after one IBP I do not recognise the resulting integral as any I_k', or 'the trig factor has flipped and the polynomial degree has only dropped by 1 (or 2)'.

Motivation — Why this works

One IBP swaps sin↔cos via the antiderivative and lowers the polynomial power by 1 (or by 2 when peeling an x into a chain-rule factor like d/dx[sin(x²)] = 2x cos(x²)). To return to the original trig factor — and thus to a genuine I_k — a second IBP is required, which lowers the power by another 1 (or 2). The two-step descent is the reverse of differentiating a product twice: it is the natural mechanism for reduction formulae of the form I_n = f(n) − n(n−1) I_{n−2} (simple argument) or I_n = g(n) + c·I_{n−4} (composite argument x²). This is exactly the product-rule-reversed pattern applied in cycle.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand has the form polynomial × trig (possibly with composite argument), so IBP is the natural tool with u = polynomial, dv = trig dx.
  2. Carry out one IBP and inspect the residual integral: the trig factor has flipped (sin↔cos) and the polynomial degree has dropped by 1 (or 2 if a chain-rule x was absorbed).
  3. Rule out alternatives: a Pythagorean identity won't help here (no sin² or cos² to convert), and substitution won't reduce the polynomial degree, so a single IBP cannot close the loop.
  4. Commit to a second IBP because it will (a) flip the trig factor back to the original and (b) drop the polynomial degree by another 1 (or 2), landing precisely on I_{n−2} (or I_{n−4}).
  5. Choose the second IBP's u and dv consistently: u must be the new polynomial factor (e.g. x^{n−1} or x^{n−3}), dv the new trig factor — never differentiate the trig back, or you'll undo the first IBP.
  6. Track the constant from the first IBP (the n, or the −½) carefully so the final coefficient n(n−1) — or the analogous coefficient for the composite case — is correct.
  7. Recognise that a step-2 (or step-4) recurrence requires two base cases (one for each parity class of n).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. First IBP on I_n = ∫ x^n · trig(x) dx: take u = x^n, dv = trig(x) dx; obtain boundary term plus n ∫ x^{n−1} · (other trig)(x) dx.
  2. (For composite argument cos(x²): split x^n = x^{n−1} · x and take dv = x·cos(x²) dx so v = ½ sin(x²); then the residual is ∫ x^{n−2} sin(x²) dx with degree dropped by 2.)
  3. Second IBP on the residual: take u = x^{n−1} (or x^{n−3} in the composite case), dv = (the flipped trig) dx, giving v = the original trig back (with appropriate sign).
  4. Evaluate boundary terms at the limits (e.g. 0 and π/2) using sin 0 = 0, cos(π/2) = 0 to simplify.
  5. Identify the resulting integral as I_{n−2} (or I_{n−4} in the composite case).
  6. Substitute back into the expression from the first IBP and collect to obtain a closed reduction formula such as I_n = n(π/2)^{n−1} − n(n−1) I_{n−2}.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $I_n = \int_0^{\pi/2} x^n \sin x\, dx$. First IBP ($u=x^n$, $dv=\sin x\, dx$): $I_n = [-x^n \cos x]_0^{\pi/2} + n\int_0^{\pi/2} x^{n-1}\cos x\, dx = n\int_0^{\pi/2} x^{n-1}\cos x\, dx$. Second IBP ($u=x^{n-1}$, $dv=\cos x\, dx$): $= n\bigl[x^{n-1}\sin x\bigr]_0^{\pi/2} - n(n-1)\int_0^{\pi/2} x^{n-2}\sin x\, dx$. Hence $I_n = n(\pi/2)^{n-1} - n(n-1)\,I_{n-2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Composite trig argument, e.g. I_n = ∫ x^n cos(x²) dx
Each IBP peels one x into the chain rule (using d/dx[sin(x²)] = 2x cos(x²)), so the index drops by 2 per IBP; two IBPs give a step-4 reduction I_n → I_{n−4}.
Pure trig powers, e.g. I_n = ∫ sin^n x dx or ∫ cos^n x dx
One IBP plus the Pythagorean identity sin² + cos² = 1 closes the loop to I_{n−2}; the second 'descent' is achieved by the identity rather than a second IBP.
Polynomial × exponential, e.g. I_n = ∫ x^n e^{ax} dx or ∫ x^n e^{x²} dx
Same double-IBP scaffolding, but no trig flip to track — the recurrence usually closes after one IBP for e^{ax}, or after two with the chain-rule peel for e^{x²}.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating ∫ x^{n−1} cos x dx (after one IBP on a sin integrand) as I_{n−1} of the same family, when in fact the trig factor has changed — leading to two coupled recurrences rather than one clean formula.
  • Trying to convert sin(x²) to cos(x²) via an identity after the first IBP, instead of recognising that a second IBP is the correct manoeuvre.
  • Stopping after one IBP and declaring the recurrence done because the polynomial degree has dropped, missing that the trig factor must also be restored.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors: the second IBP often introduces a double negative (e.g. −∫(−sin x)) that must be carefully bookkept.
  • Forgetting to carry the constant from the first IBP (the leading n, or the −½ from v = −½ cos(x²)) through the second application — this kills the final coefficient.
  • Choosing u and dv in the second IBP so as to undo the first: always differentiate the polynomial and integrate the trig, never the reverse.
  • Using the wrong polynomial power in round two (e.g. x^{n−2} instead of x^{n−3} in the composite-argument case), giving an integrand that is not I_{n−4}.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure / FP material on reduction formulae via integration by parts; uses the IBP formula from the formula booklet (∫ u dv = uv − ∫ v du). Stays within real-variable definite/indefinite integration — do not invoke complex exponentials (e^{ix}) or Gamma-function identities, even though they give the same answer.
