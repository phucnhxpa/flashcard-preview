Split modulus equation at interior's sign-change point

Family: Modulus Case Analysis Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation contains |ax+b| (or |linear|) equated to another expression and the modulus must be removed to solve. Cues: a single |·| appearing in an equation, or phrasing like 'solve f(x) = |2x−1|'. The sign of the interior is unknown, so the equation cannot be manipulated until cases are taken. Most common in IAL P2/P3 modulus questions.

Motivation — Why this works

|ax+b| is piecewise: it equals (ax+b) where ax+b ≥ 0 and −(ax+b) where ax+b < 0. Splitting at the critical point ax+b = 0 turns the modulus equation into two ordinary equations, each valid only on its half-line. Each candidate solution must lie inside its case's interval (and respect any original domain restriction) to count as a genuine root.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the modulus bars around a linear (or simple) expression — the equation cannot be solved until they are removed.
  2. Rule out squaring both sides as a first move here, since the other side is rational and squaring would multiply the algebra; case-splitting is cleaner.
  3. Commit to splitting at the interior's zero (x = 1/2 for |2x−1|) because that is the only point where the algebraic form of |2x−1| changes.
  4. Bridge: write Case 1 (interior ≥ 0) replacing |2x−1| with 2x−1, and Case 2 (interior < 0) replacing it with 1−2x.
  5. Tactically, carry forward all domain restrictions (e.g. x ≠ 1, x > 0), solve each resulting equation, and reject roots that fall outside their case's interval.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Locate the critical point where the interior of the modulus is zero: solve ax+b = 0.
  2. Form Case 1 on the interval where interior ≥ 0: replace |ax+b| with (ax+b) and solve.
  3. Form Case 2 on the interval where interior < 0: replace |ax+b| with −(ax+b) and solve.
  4. For each candidate root, verify it lies in that case's interval and satisfies any original domain conditions.
  5. Combine the validated roots from both cases as the full solution set.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation of the form |f(x)| = |g(x)|
Either square both sides (since both are non-negative) to get f(x)² = g(x)², or split into f(x) = g(x) and f(x) = −g(x); no domain restriction is induced by the modulus itself.
Inequality |ax+b| < g(x) or > g(x)
Same split at ax+b = 0, but each case's solution must be intersected with that case's interval rather than just checked for membership.
Two modulus terms |ax+b| and |cx+d| in the same equation
Use both critical points to partition the real line into three subintervals, and treat each subinterval as its own case with appropriate signs.
Graphical verification
Sketch y = f(x) and y = |ax+b| (a V-shape with vertex at the critical point) and count intersections to corroborate the algebraic root count.

Common Misreadings

  • Writing |2x−1| = ±(2x+3)/(x−1), placing the ± on the wrong side; the ± belongs to whatever the modulus is replaced by, not the other side.
  • Treating the case condition x ≥ 1/2 as optional rather than as a constraint that solutions must satisfy.
  • Confusing the critical point of the modulus (x = 1/2) with the domain exclusion of the rational expression (x = 1) and merging them into one condition.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to test each candidate root against its case's interval, accepting (5−√41)/4 ≈ −0.35 even though it violates x ≥ 1/2.
  • Dropping the original domain restriction (e.g. x ≠ 1 or x > 0) when stating the final solution set.
  • When cross-multiplying by (x−1), failing to note that x = 1 must remain excluded even if it appears as a root.
  • Squaring both sides as a shortcut and then forgetting to check that resulting roots actually satisfy the original signed equation.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P2/P3 modulus technique; uses only piecewise definition of |·|, linear/quadratic algebra, and basic domain checks — no material beyond IAL Pure scope.
