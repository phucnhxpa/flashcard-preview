Algebra & Functions

Propagate known range through chained operations including reciprocal

Family: Range Tracking Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a target function $g(\theta)$ is built from a function $f$ whose range is already known (typically from an $R\cos(\theta+\alpha)$ or $R\sin(\theta+\alpha)$ form), via a chain of algebraic operations such as scaling, translation, and reciprocation. Cues include phrasings like 'find the range of $g$' where $g = a/(b+cf(\theta))$ or $g = p + qf(\theta)$, immediately after a part establishing the harmonic form of $f$. Also fires when the question explicitly says 'hence' to signal reuse of the prior range.

Motivation — Why this works

Since $R\cos(\theta+\alpha)$ attains every value in $[-R,R]$, any monotone-on-the-relevant-interval operation applied to it transforms the bounds in a predictable way. Affine maps $x\mapsto px+q$ preserve order when $p>0$ and reverse it when $p<0$; the reciprocal $x\mapsto 1/x$ is strictly decreasing on each of $(-\infty,0)$ and $(0,\infty)$, so it reverses inequalities provided the interval does not cross zero. Tracking bounds through each layer is faster and safer than solving $g(\theta)=k$ directly.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that $f(\theta)$ has a known closed range $[-R,R]$ from the harmonic form found earlier, and that $g$ is a finite composition of $f$ with affine and reciprocal operations.
  2. Rules out calculus (differentiating $g$) and rules out solving $g(\theta)=k$ for $\theta$, since the bounds-propagation route is shorter and the operations are individually monotonic.
  3. Commits to the interval-tracking method once it is verified that the denominator never vanishes, so the reciprocal step is well-defined and monotone on the whole interval.
  4. Bridges to the first operation by writing the inequality $-R\le f(\theta)\le R$ explicitly, then applying each algebraic layer in the order they appear in $g$.
  5. Inside the move, decides at each step whether the operation preserves or reverses the inequality (positive vs negative multiplier, reciprocal on a positive interval), and keeps bounds in exact surd form rather than decimal.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the known range of $f$: $-R\le f(\theta)\le R$ (here $R=\sqrt{41}$).
  2. Apply each affine operation in order, preserving inequality direction for positive multipliers and reversing it for negative ones.
  3. Before reciprocating, check that the resulting interval lies entirely on one side of $0$; here $90-4\sqrt{41}\approx 64.4>0$.
  4. Take reciprocals and reverse the inequality: if $0<a\le u\le b$ then $1/b\le 1/u\le 1/a$.
  5. Apply the final scalar multiplier (positive constant preserves direction).
  6. State the range as a closed interval, since the endpoints of $\cos$ are attained: $\dfrac{2}{90+4\sqrt{41}}\le g(\theta)\le \dfrac{2}{90-4\sqrt{41}}$.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $f(\theta)=\sqrt{41}\cos(\theta+0.675)$, so $-\sqrt{41}\le f(\theta)\le \sqrt{41}$. Then $-4\sqrt{41}\le 4f(\theta)\le 4\sqrt{41}$, hence $90-4\sqrt{41}\le 90+4f(\theta)\le 90+4\sqrt{41}$. Since $90-4\sqrt{41}>0$, reciprocating reverses: $\dfrac{1}{90+4\sqrt{41}}\le \dfrac{1}{90+4f(\theta)}\le \dfrac{1}{90-4\sqrt{41}}$. Multiplying by $2$ gives $\dfrac{2}{90+4\sqrt{41}}\le g(\theta)\le \dfrac{2}{90-4\sqrt{41}}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Affine-only chain $g(\theta)=p+qf(\theta)$ with $q<0$
Inequality reverses once at the negative-multiplier step; no reciprocal check needed.
Squared trig term, e.g. $g(\theta)=a+b\cos^2\theta$
Use $0\le \cos^2\theta\le 1$ rather than $[-1,1]$, since squaring is non-monotone on $[-1,1]$.
Denominator that can vanish, e.g. $g(\theta)=1/(2+3\cos\theta)$ where $2-3<0$
Range becomes a union of two unbounded intervals; bounds-propagation fails and one must analyse each branch separately.
Logarithm or exponential applied to $f$
Both $\ln$ and $e^x$ are strictly increasing, so inequalities are preserved; check positivity of argument before $\ln$.
Using $R\sin(\theta+\alpha)$ form instead of cosine
Identical method; range of inner function is still $[-R,R]$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $1/x$ as order-preserving and so writing the reciprocated bounds in the same order as the originals.
  • Assuming $\min g$ occurs at $\min f$, ignoring that the reciprocal swaps which endpoint of $f$ produces the max/min of $g$.
  • Mistaking the harmonic amplitude $R$ for the range of $g$ itself rather than of $f$.

Pitfalls

  • Skipping the positivity check on the denominator before reciprocating.
  • Using strict inequalities and reporting an open interval, although $\cos$ attains $\pm 1$ so the bounds are achieved.
  • Converting $90\pm 4\sqrt{41}$ to decimals and losing the exact form required for full marks.
  • Forgetting to apply the final outer constant (the factor of $2$) after reciprocating.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure scope: uses only the $R\cos(\theta+\alpha)$ form from P3 trigonometry and elementary inequality manipulation; no calculus or analysis beyond monotonicity of affine and reciprocal maps is required.
