Sequences & Series

Pair consecutive alternating terms and use parity to find residual

Family: Alternating Cancellation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a finite sum contains a pure alternating component such as $\sum_{n=1}^{N}(-1)^n$, or any sum reducible to $\pm 1$ alternating values. Cue words: 'evaluate', 'find the exact value', combined with $(-1)^n$, $\cos(n\pi)$, or $(-1)^{n+1}$ inside a finite sum. Especially useful when the upper limit $N$ is a specific small-to-moderate integer rather than a symbolic limit. Also fires inside compound sums like $\sum (a + b(-1)^n)$ where the alternating part can be split off.

Motivation — Why this works

Consecutive terms $(-1)^k + (-1)^{k+1} = 0$, so adjacent pairing annihilates terms exactly. The entire sum therefore depends only on the parity of $N$: even $N$ gives 0, odd $N$ leaves one residual term equal to $(-1)^N$. This bypasses the geometric series formula and reduces evaluation to a parity check, which is faster and less error-prone with signs. The same identity $(-1)^k(1 + (-1)) = 0$ underlies the geometric formula's collapse when $r=-1$.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the summand is $(-1)^n$ (or equivalent), producing a strict $-1, +1, -1, +1, \ldots$ pattern.
  2. Rule out applying the geometric formula directly — it works but invites sign slips with $r=-1$, and rule out treating the sum as 0 by reflex.
  3. Commit to pairing because consecutive terms cancel exactly and only the parity of $N$ governs the result.
  4. Write out the first few terms to fix the starting sign and confirm whether the final index is odd or even.
  5. Decide: if $N$ even, answer is 0; if $N$ odd, answer is the unpaired final term $(-1)^N$, then re-apply any factored-out constants.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the alternating component and the index range, e.g. $\sum_{n=1}^{N}(-1)^n$.
  2. Expand the first few and last terms to confirm the sign pattern and parity of $N$.
  3. Group consecutive terms into pairs $((-1)^{2k-1}+(-1)^{2k}) = 0$.
  4. Count pairs: $\lfloor N/2 \rfloor$ pairs; if $N$ is odd, one term remains, namely $(-1)^N$.
  5. Sum: 0 if $N$ even, $(-1)^N$ if $N$ odd (which equals $-1$ for $N$ odd starting at $n=1$).
  6. Re-multiply by any constants factored out before pairing.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $3\sum_{n=1}^{59}(-1)^n$. Write the inner sum as $(-1)+1+(-1)+1+\cdots+(-1)$ with 59 terms. Pair terms 1–2, 3–4, …, 57–58 to get 29 pairs each summing to 0, leaving $(-1)^{59}=-1$. Hence $\sum_{n=1}^{59}(-1)^n=-1$ and the answer is $3\times(-1)=-3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Upper limit even, e.g. $\sum_{n=1}^{60}(-1)^n$
All terms pair completely; sum is 0.
Sum starts at $n=0$, e.g. $\sum_{n=0}^{N}(-1)^n$
First term is $+1$; pairing now leaves residual $+1$ if $N$ is even, $0$ if $N$ is odd.
Summand is $(-1)^{n+1}$ or $\cos(n\pi)$
Same pairing logic; just track the starting sign — $(-1)^{n+1}$ starts $+1$, $\cos(n\pi)=(-1)^n$.
Compound sum $\sum_{n=1}^{N}(a+b(-1)^n)$
Split into $aN + b\sum(-1)^n$; apply pairing to the second piece only.
Geometric formula route
Use $S_N = a(1-r^N)/(1-r)$ with $a=r=-1$: $S_N = -1\cdot(1-(-1)^N)/2$, equivalent to the parity result.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming any alternating sum equals 0 without checking the parity of the number of terms.
  • Confusing the lower limit ($n=0$ vs $n=1$), which flips the sign of the unpaired residual.
  • Misreading $(-1)^{n+1}$ as $(-1)^n$ and getting the overall sign wrong.
  • Treating $\cos(n\pi)$ as oscillating but not recognising it equals $(-1)^n$ exactly.

Pitfalls

  • Miscounting the number of terms: $\sum_{n=1}^{N}$ has $N$ terms, not $N-1$.
  • Evaluating $(-1)^N$ for odd $N$ as $+1$ instead of $-1$.
  • Forgetting to re-multiply by constants factored out before applying pairing.
  • Pairing from the wrong end (e.g. pairing terms 2–3, 4–5, …) and miscounting the leftover term.
