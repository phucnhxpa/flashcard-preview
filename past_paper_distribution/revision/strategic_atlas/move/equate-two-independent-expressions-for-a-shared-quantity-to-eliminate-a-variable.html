Algebra & Functions

Equate two independent expressions for a shared quantity to eliminate a variable

Family: Bridge Equation Elimination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Two earlier parts of a problem each produce an equation of the form Q = f(k) and Q = g(k), where Q is a shared quantity (e.g. 5r², the gradient, a distance squared) and the remaining unknown is the same parameter k. The cue is recognising that both conditions must hold simultaneously, so the two right-hand sides must be equal. This is especially common in coordinate-geometry circle problems where tangency and chord-length conditions each yield an expression for r².

Motivation — Why this works

If Q = f(k) and Q = g(k), then f(k) = g(k) by transitivity, immediately eliminating Q (and hence the auxiliary unknown r) without substitution or surds. The move works because both constraints come from the same geometric configuration, so they must be consistent. Equating bypasses the messier route of solving one equation for r and substituting, keeping the algebra polynomial and clean.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that parts (b) and (c) have each produced an expression equal to the same quantity 5r², written purely in k.
  2. Rule out solving one equation for r and substituting — that introduces square roots and sign cases unnecessarily.
  3. Commit to equating the two right-hand sides because both must hold simultaneously and r is not actually needed to find k.
  4. Bridge: write (k+1)² = k² − 10k + 34 as a single equation in k alone.
  5. Tactically expand the squared bracket first so the k² terms cancel, leaving a clean linear equation in k.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write both expressions explicitly: 5r² = (k + 1)² and 5r² = k² − 10k + 34.
  2. Equate the right-hand sides: (k + 1)² = k² − 10k + 34.
  3. Expand: k² + 2k + 1 = k² − 10k + 34.
  4. Cancel the k² terms on both sides: 2k + 1 = −10k + 34.
  5. Collect: 12k = 33, hence k = 11/4.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose tangency gives $5r^2 = (k+1)^2$ and a chord condition gives $5r^2 = k^2 - 10k + 34$. Equating, $(k+1)^2 = k^2 - 10k + 34$, so $k^2 + 2k + 1 = k^2 - 10k + 34$. The $k^2$ cancels: $12k = 33$, giving $k = \tfrac{11}{4}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two tangent-line conditions to a curve giving two expressions for the gradient m at a shared point
Equate the two gradient expressions m = f(x) = g(x) to solve for the x-coordinate of the contact point.
Two distance conditions in coordinate geometry both yielding expressions for d² in terms of a parameter
Equate the d² expressions directly; do not take square roots until after k is found.
Simultaneous parametric equations where t² (or another auxiliary) is expressed two ways
Equate the two expressions for t² to eliminate t and solve for the remaining parameter.
Binomial/series problems where a coefficient is computed two ways (e.g. from two expansions)
Equate the coefficient expressions to solve for the unknown constant.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to solve for r from one equation and substitute into the other, introducing unnecessary surds and sign cases.
  • Treating the two equations as independent simultaneous equations in r and k requiring a 2×2 system, when in fact r is already eliminated.
  • Square-rooting both sides of (k+1)² = k² − 10k + 34 prematurely, before recognising the right-hand side is not a perfect square in k.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to expand (k + 1)² before equating, leading to attempts to square-root both sides which can lose solutions or introduce sign errors.
  • Sign error when moving −10k across: 2k − (−10k) = 12k, not 8k or −8k.
  • Failing to verify that the resulting k is consistent with r² > 0 (geometric admissibility) when the problem demands a real circle.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1–P2 coordinate geometry of circles and standard simultaneous-equation technique; no methods beyond linear/quadratic manipulation are required.
