Coordinate Geometry

Invoke converse of angle-in-semicircle theorem to identify a diameter

Family: Circle Theorem Invocation Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Three points P, Q, R lie on a circle and either (i) you must prove a specific chord (say PR) is a diameter, or (ii) having just established that ∠PQR = 90°, the question says 'hence' find the centre/radius/equation of the circle. Visual cues: a right-angle marking or a perpendicularity result at one of the three points, plus a request that involves the chord opposite that point. The word 'hence' linking a 90°-angle part to a circle-equation part is a near-certain signal. Equivalent phrasings: 'show that AB is a diameter', 'find the equation of the circle through A, B, C given ∠ACB = 90°'.

Motivation — Why this works

The angle-in-semicircle theorem says an angle subtended by a diameter at a point on the circle is 90°; its converse says that a 90° inscribed angle forces the opposite chord to be a diameter. This converts a hard circle problem (solving a 3×3 system for the general circle equation, or intersecting perpendicular bisectors) into two cheap coordinate operations: a perpendicularity check (gradient product = −1) and a midpoint/distance calculation. It is the most efficient route whenever a right angle is present or can be cheaply verified at one of three concyclic points.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structural cue: three points on a circle, with either a 90° angle stated/just-proved at one of them, or a request to prove a chord is a diameter. The word 'hence' is a strong indicator that an earlier 90° result must be reused.
  2. Consider rival methods: (a) substitute the three points into (x−a)² + (y−b)² = r² and solve a 3×3 system; (b) intersect perpendicular bisectors of two chords to locate the centre; (c) show the proposed midpoint is equidistant from all three points. All are valid but laborious.
  3. Commit to the converse theorem because it reduces everything to a gradient check (or a Pythagoras check) plus, if needed, one midpoint and one distance — far less work and explicitly what 'hence' demands.
  4. Identify the geometry carefully: the diameter is the chord OPPOSITE the right angle. If ∠PQR = 90°, then PR (not PQ or QR) is the diameter; the vertex of the right angle is the point on the circumference, not an endpoint of the diameter.
  5. Plan the execution: either (i) compute gradients of the two sides meeting at the right-angle vertex, show their product is −1, then cite the converse theorem to conclude the opposite chord is a diameter; or (ii) take the already-proved 90° and immediately set centre = midpoint of opposite chord, radius = ½·|opposite chord|.
  6. State the converse theorem explicitly in the write-up — perpendicularity alone does not justify 'diameter' without the theorem citation.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Label the three concyclic points and identify which one carries (or is to carry) the 90° angle; call it Q, with the proposed diameter being the chord PR opposite Q.
  2. If proving the diameter: compute gradient of QP and gradient of QR; show m_{QP} · m_{QR} = −1, hence ∠PQR = 90°.
  3. Cite the converse of the angle-in-semicircle theorem: a 90° inscribed angle ⇒ the subtending chord is a diameter; conclude PR is a diameter.
  4. If finding the circle: centre = midpoint of PR = ((x_P + x_R)/2, (y_P + y_R)/2).
  5. Compute |PR| = √((x_R − x_P)² + (y_R − y_P)²); radius r = |PR|/2.
  6. If asked, write the equation (x − a)² + (y − b)² = r² with (a,b) the centre.

Worked Micro-Example

Given P(23, 14), Q(−7, −26), R(7, −6). Gradients: $m_{QP} = \frac{14-(-26)}{23-(-7)} = \frac{40}{30} = \frac{4}{3}$, $m_{QR} = \frac{-6-(-26)}{7-(-7)} = \frac{20}{14} = \frac{10}{7}$. (If perpendicular, product = −1.) Assuming ∠PQR = 90°, by the converse of the angle-in-semicircle theorem PR is a diameter. Centre = midpoint of PR = $(15, 4)$; $|PR| = \sqrt{16^2 + 20^2}$; radius = $|PR|/2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

The 90° angle is established via Pythagoras (|AC|² + |BC|² = |AB|²) rather than gradients.
Compute the three squared distances, verify the equation, then still cite the converse theorem to upgrade 'right-angled triangle' to 'AB is a diameter'.
No right angle is present or claimed.
Fall back to substituting the three points into (x − a)² + (y − b)² = r² and solving simultaneously, or intersecting two perpendicular bisectors.
A fourth point D is given and you also need to confirm D lies on the circle.
After locating centre and radius via the converse theorem, verify (x_D − a)² + (y_D − b)² = r².
Question asks only for the centre, not the radius.
Stop after the midpoint of the diameter — no need to compute |PR|.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating one of the sides adjacent to the right angle (e.g. PQ or QR) as the diameter, instead of the side PR opposite the right angle.
  • Trying to prove the FORWARD theorem (assume diameter, deduce 90°) — this is circular and proves nothing about the chord being a diameter.
  • Showing perpendicularity of AC and BC and concluding 'so AB is a diameter' without naming the converse theorem — incomplete justification.
  • Ignoring 'hence' and restarting with the general circle equation, wasting time and often losing the method mark.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to halve the diameter length to obtain the radius (writing r = |PR| instead of r = |PR|/2).
  • Computing the angle at the wrong vertex — the right angle must be at the point on the circumference, not at an endpoint of the proposed diameter.
  • Sign/arithmetic slips in the midpoint formula when coordinates are negative.
  • Citing 'angle in a semicircle' instead of its CONVERSE — examiners expect the correct direction of the theorem to be named.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P2 circle geometry / coordinate geometry of the circle). The angle-in-semicircle theorem and its converse are GCSE-level circle theorems assumed available; no formula-booklet entry is invoked. Avoid generalising to inscribed-angle theorems for non-right angles, which are out of scope.
