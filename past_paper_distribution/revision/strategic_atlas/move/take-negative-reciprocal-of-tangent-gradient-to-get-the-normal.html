Coordinate Geometry

Take negative reciprocal of tangent gradient to get the normal

Family: Normal Gradient Rule Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question asks for the equation of the normal to a curve at a stated point, or for a line perpendicular to the tangent there. Cue phrases include 'normal at the point', 'perpendicular to the curve', or 'line through P perpendicular to the tangent'. Often paired with prior differentiation that has already produced a tangent gradient $m_t$. Also triggers when a follow-up part asks where the normal meets an axis or another curve.

Motivation — Why this works

Two non-vertical perpendicular lines satisfy $m_1 m_2 = -1$, so the normal gradient is $m_n = -1/m_t$. This single algebraic flip converts the tangent-gradient information already obtained from $\frac{dy}{dx}$ into the gradient of the perpendicular line. Once $m_n$ is known, the point-slope form $y - y_1 = m_n(x - x_1)$ delivers the normal equation immediately. The rule is exact and avoids any need to recompute derivatives or use vector methods.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the word 'normal' (not 'tangent') in the question stem, so the line wanted is perpendicular to the curve at the point.
  2. Rule out reusing $m_t$ directly as the line's gradient, and rule out re-differentiating — the perpendicularity rule handles this in one step.
  3. Commit to $m_n = -1/m_t$ because the tangent gradient is already in hand and neither line is vertical (check $m_t \neq 0$).
  4. Bridge to operation: keep the contact point $(x_1,y_1)$ from the tangent step and substitute into point-slope form.
  5. Tactically keep $m_n$ as an exact fraction (e.g. $-1/4$) rather than a decimal, and distribute carefully when the $x_1$-coordinate is itself a fraction or surd.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Confirm the tangent gradient $m_t$ from $\frac{dy}{dx}$ evaluated at the given point.
  2. Check $m_t \neq 0$; then compute $m_n = -1/m_t$.
  3. Substitute $(x_1,y_1)$ and $m_n$ into $y - y_1 = m_n(x - x_1)$.
  4. Expand and simplify to the form requested (e.g. $y = mx + c$ or $ax + by + c = 0$).

Worked Micro-Example

At $x = \pi/12$ on a curve, $\frac{dy}{dx} = 4$ and $y_1 = 2\sqrt{3}$. Then $m_n = -\tfrac{1}{4}$, so the normal is $y - 2\sqrt{3} = -\tfrac{1}{4}\!\left(x - \tfrac{\pi}{12}\right)$, giving $y = -\tfrac{x}{4} + \tfrac{\pi}{48} + 2\sqrt{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Horizontal tangent ($m_t = 0$)
Negative reciprocal is undefined; the normal is the vertical line $x = x_1$.
Vertical tangent (e.g. from implicit differentiation where $\frac{dy}{dx} \to \infty$)
The normal is horizontal: $y = y_1$.
Parametric curve with $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}$
Compute $m_t$ via the chain rule first, then apply $m_n = -1/m_t$ unchanged.
Implicit curve $F(x,y)=0$
Get $m_t$ from implicit differentiation at $(x_1,y_1)$, then take $-1/m_t$.
Normal meets axis or curve again
After forming the normal, set $x=0$, $y=0$, or substitute back into the curve to find the intersection.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 'normal' as 'tangent' and giving $y - y_1 = m_t(x - x_1)$.
  • Interpreting 'perpendicular to the x-axis at P' as the normal to the curve, rather than perpendicular to the tangent.
  • Assuming the negative reciprocal rule applies when one line is vertical, where it breaks down.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the negative sign and writing $1/m_t$ instead of $-1/m_t$.
  • Inverting without negating, or negating without inverting.
  • Using the tangent gradient inside the normal equation by accident.
  • Arithmetic slips when distributing $m_n$ over a bracket containing fractions or surds (e.g. $\pi/12$).
  • Failing to keep exact form, replacing $-1/4$ by $-0.25$ and losing marks for accuracy.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure scope (P1–P4): perpendicular gradient rule from coordinate geometry combined with differentiation of standard, parametric, or implicit functions.
