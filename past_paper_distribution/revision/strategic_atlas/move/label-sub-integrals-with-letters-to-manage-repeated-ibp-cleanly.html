Integration

Label sub-integrals with letters to manage repeated IBP cleanly

Family: Structural Labelling Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when integration by parts must be applied two or more times, producing a nested integral inside the boundary expression. Typical cues: integrands of the form x^n e^x, x^n sin x, x^n cos x with n ≥ 2, or any IBP that leaves another non-trivial integral on the right. Also fires in cyclic IBP (e.g. ∫ e^x sin x dx) where the original integral reappears, and in reduction formula questions where I_n recurs in terms of I_{n-2}. The signal is: 'I am about to write a long bracketed expression with another ∫ inside it.'

Motivation — Why this works

Naming the outer integral I and the inner integral J turns a tangled one-line computation into a small system of equations, each line of which can be checked in isolation. This mirrors the structural identity ∫ u dv = uv − ∫ v du: the residual ∫ v du is itself an integral worthy of its own symbol. Modularising prevents sign-flip and coefficient errors and makes the final back-substitution a single arithmetic step. It also generalises directly to cyclic IBP (where I appears on both sides) and to reduction formulae (where I_n is defined recursively).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the first IBP leaves another integral on the RHS, e.g. I = e − 2∫₀¹ xe^x dx, which still needs work.
  2. Rule out inlining the second IBP: brackets-inside-brackets on one line is the dominant source of exam errors here.
  3. Commit to introducing a fresh symbol J for the inner integral, treating it as a self-contained sub-problem.
  4. Bridge: write the clean equation I = e − 2J, then move to a separate working block to evaluate J.
  5. Tactically, evaluate J completely (including limits) before substituting its numerical value back into I, and double-check the coefficient (here 2) is preserved.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Apply IBP once to the original integral I and identify the residual integral.
  2. Define J := (residual integral) and rewrite the result as I = (boundary terms) − k·J for some constant k.
  3. Evaluate J in a separate working block using a second IBP (or direct integration).
  4. Substitute the numerical value of J back into the expression for I, preserving the coefficient k.
  5. Simplify to a single closed form and sanity-check signs and limits.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $I = \int_0^1 x^2 e^x\,dx$. IBP with $u=x^2$, $dv=e^x dx$: $I = [x^2 e^x]_0^1 - 2\int_0^1 x e^x\,dx = e - 2J$. Now $J = \int_0^1 x e^x\,dx = [xe^x]_0^1 - \int_0^1 e^x\,dx = e - (e-1) = 1$. Hence $I = e - 2(1) = e - 2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cyclic IBP, e.g. ∫ e^x sin x dx
Label the original integral I; after two IBPs, I reappears on the RHS, giving an algebraic equation I = (terms) − I to solve for I.
Reduction formulae, e.g. I_n = ∫₀^{π/2} sin^n x dx
Use indexed labels I_n, I_{n-2}; one IBP produces a recurrence I_n = f(n)·I_{n-2}, then iterate down to a base case.
Polynomial × trig integrand, e.g. ∫ x^2 cos x dx
Same pattern: I = (boundary) − 2J with J = ∫ x sin x dx; evaluate J independently and back-substitute.
Definite integral within a larger expression (e.g. volume of revolution)
Compute the labelled integral as a stand-alone numerical value before inserting it into the surrounding π or area formula.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the inner integral as if it were already evaluated and writing a bare number where an integral still sits.
  • Trying to chain everything in one long line of equalities, losing track of which level of IBP each term belongs to.
  • Confusing J (the new sub-integral) with I (the original) in cyclic problems, leading to a tautology 0 = 0.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping or mis-multiplying the coefficient k when substituting J back into I (e.g. writing I = e − J instead of e − 2J).
  • Carrying the wrong limits into J, especially after a substitution change of variable inside the sub-problem.
  • Sign errors at the boundary term [uv]_a^b when uv vanishes at one endpoint — students sometimes drop the bracket entirely.
  • Forgetting to evaluate J at the limits (leaving it as an indefinite expression) before substituting back.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (Core/P-series IBP) and Further Pure (reduction formulae). All examples use standard A-level integrands: polynomial × exponential, polynomial × trig, or cyclic e^x·trig combinations.
