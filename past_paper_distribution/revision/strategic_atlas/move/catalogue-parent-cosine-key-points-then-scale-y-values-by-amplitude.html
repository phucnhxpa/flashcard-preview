Trigonometry

Catalogue parent cosine key points then scale y-values by amplitude

Family: Graph Transformation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The equation has the form y = A cos x° (or y = A sin x°) with no phase shift, frequency change, or vertical translation, and the question asks for coordinates of a labelled point P on the curve — typically a maximum, minimum, or x-intercept. Diagram-driven questions where P is described positionally (e.g. 'the first minimum to the right of the y-axis') are a strong cue. Phrasings like 'state the coordinates of', 'write down the value of', or 'the curve has equation y = 4 cos x°' alongside a sketch fire this move.

Motivation — Why this works

A vertical stretch y = A·f(x) multiplies every y-coordinate by A while leaving x-coordinates fixed, so the locations of maxima, minima, and zeros of cos x° transfer directly to y = A cos x°. Because the key points of the parent cosine are memorised (max at multiples of 360°, min at odd multiples of 180°, zeros at 90° + 180°k), the student can read off P's x-coordinate from the parent and only rescale the y-coordinate. This avoids redundant equation-solving and exploits the structural simplicity of pure amplitude scaling.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the equation is y = 4 cos x° — pure amplitude scaling, no Bx, no +c, no x − a inside.
  2. Rules out solving 4 cos x° = 0 or differentiating to find turning points: the parent's key points are already known and amplitude scaling preserves their x-coordinates.
  3. Commits to the strategy 'inherit x from parent cosine, multiply y by 4' because the transformation is purely vertical.
  4. Bridges to the diagram: identifies whether P is a max, min, or zero, and which member of the relevant family (which integer k) matches its position.
  5. Tactically selects positive vs negative x members of the key-point family to match the picture, then writes down (x, 4·y_parent).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the transformation type: y = A cos x° is a vertical stretch by factor A only.
  2. Recall parent key-point families: maxima at x = 360°k with y = 1; minima at x = 180° + 360°k with y = −1; zeros at x = 90° + 180°k.
  3. Classify P from the diagram (max / min / zero) and pick the integer k that matches its position.
  4. Keep the x-coordinate from the parent; multiply the parent y-value by A to obtain the scaled y.
  5. State P as an ordered pair (x, A·y_parent).

Worked Micro-Example

For $y = 4\cos x°$, find the first minimum P for $x > 0$. Parent $\cos x°$ has its first positive minimum at $x = 180°$ with $y = -1$. Scaling y by 4 gives $P = (180°,\,-4)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

y = A sin x°
Use sine's key-point families: max at 90° + 360°k, min at 270° + 360°k, zeros at 180°k; then scale y by A.
y = A cos(Bx°)
First compute period 360°/B and divide all parent x-coordinates by B; then scale y by A.
y = A cos x° + c
Scale y by A first, then translate every y-coordinate up by c; zeros of the parent are no longer zeros of the new curve.
y = −A cos x°
Maxima and minima swap x-locations because of the reflection in the x-axis; |A| still gives the magnitude of the extreme y-values.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 4 cos x° as cos 4x° and mistakenly changing the period to 90°.
  • Treating amplitude scaling as if it shifted zeros or extrema along the x-axis.
  • Confusing amplitude with vertical translation, e.g. thinking the maximum becomes y = 1 + 4 instead of y = 4.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to include negative-x members of the key-point family when P lies to the left of the y-axis.
  • Picking the wrong member of the family (e.g. stating 360° instead of 0° for the nearest maximum).
  • Writing the y-coordinate as ±1 from the parent instead of ±A after scaling.
  • Omitting the degree symbol when the equation is given in degrees.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths graph-transformation content (P2): amplitude scaling of sin/cos and identification of key points from sketches, with no calculus or radian-measure subtleties required.
