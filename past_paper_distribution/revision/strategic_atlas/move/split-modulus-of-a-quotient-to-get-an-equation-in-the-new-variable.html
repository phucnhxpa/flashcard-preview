Complex Numbers

Split modulus of a quotient to get an equation in the new variable

Family: Modulus Transfer Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires after a transformation w = f(z) has been inverted to give z as a quotient of linear expressions in w, and the original locus is given as |z| = k (or more generally |z - a| = k). The single condition is a modulus of a quotient in w, e.g. |z| = |(2+wi)/(w-1)| = 2. The cue words are 'find the locus of w' or 'show that w lies on a circle/line'. This is the standard bridge step before squaring to Cartesian form.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity |A/B| = |A|/|B| (valid whenever B ≠ 0) lets us clear the quotient without expanding it into real/imaginary parts. Multiplying through by |B| converts a single modulus condition into the form |A(w)| = k|B(w)|, which is an Apollonius condition: the locus of points whose distances from two fixed points are in a fixed ratio. For ratio ≠ 1 this is a circle; for ratio = 1 it is the perpendicular bisector. This is consistent with Möbius maps sending circles/lines to circles/lines.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice z is expressed as a quotient in w, and the locus condition is a modulus equation |z| = k.
  2. I rule out expanding numerator and denominator into u + iv at this stage — that would create a messy fraction with a complex denominator before I have simplified the structure.
  3. I commit to the modulus quotient rule because it cleanly separates the two linear factors and exposes the Apollonius structure.
  4. Concretely, I rewrite |z| = |A(w)|/|B(w)| = k as |A(w)| = k|B(w)|, with no denominators left.
  5. I check whether k = 1 (line) or k ≠ 1 (circle) so I anticipate the form of the final answer before squaring.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write |z| = |numerator(w)| / |denominator(w)|.
  2. Multiply both sides by |denominator(w)| to clear the fraction.
  3. Set the result equal to k·|denominator(w)|, where k is the value of |z| from the locus.
  4. Note whether k = 1 (expect a line) or k ≠ 1 (expect a circle) to anticipate the final form.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $z = \dfrac{2+wi}{w-1}$ and $|z| = 2$, write $\left|\dfrac{2+wi}{w-1}\right| = \dfrac{|2+wi|}{|w-1|} = 2$. Clearing the denominator gives $|2+wi| = 2|w-1|$. Since the ratio is $2 \ne 1$, the locus of $w$ will be a circle (Apollonius).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Locus condition is |z - a| = k instead of |z| = k
Substitute z = N(w)/D(w) into z - a, combine over the common denominator D(w), then apply |A/B| = |A|/|B| as before.
The modulus value k equals 1
The equation becomes |A(w)| = |B(w)|, an equidistance condition — the locus is the perpendicular bisector of the two associated points (a line, not a circle).
Locus given as arg(z) = θ rather than |z| = k
Do not use this move; instead use arg of a quotient = arg(num) - arg(denom), which is a different transfer rule.
Original variable mapping is w = (az+b)/(cz+d) and we want the locus of z
Same move applied without inversion: take moduli on both sides of the constraint on w directly.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading |2+wi| as |2+w|·i or as 2 + |w|i — the i is part of the complex number being moduled, not an external multiplier.
  • Treating |A/B| = |A|/|B| as if it required A and B to be real, when it holds for all complex A, B with B ≠ 0.
  • Believing the result must be a circle without checking whether the ratio k equals 1.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to multiply the RHS by k when clearing |denominator|; e.g. writing |2+wi| = |w-1| instead of |2+wi| = 2|w-1|.
  • Dropping the modulus bars on one side after clearing, leaving a complex equation rather than a real one.
  • Not stating the restriction that the denominator is non-zero (w ≠ 1 in the example), which excludes a point from the locus.

A-level Scope Notes

This sits squarely in Edexcel IAL Further Pure (transformations of the complex plane and loci). Only the standard identity |A/B| = |A|/|B| and recognition of Apollonius circles/perpendicular bisectors are required.
