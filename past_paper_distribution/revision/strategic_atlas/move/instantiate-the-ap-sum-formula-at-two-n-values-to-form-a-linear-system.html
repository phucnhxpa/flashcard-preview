Sequences & Series

Instantiate the AP sum formula at two n-values to form a linear system

Family: Formula Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an arithmetic progression problem gives two independent pieces of sum information (e.g. $S_4 = 50$ and $S_8 = 196$) and asks for the first term $a$ and/or common difference $d$. Recognise it whenever you see two sum conditions, or one sum plus one term, on the same AP with $a$ and $d$ unknown. Phrasings include 'the sum of the first $p$ terms is...' stated twice, or 'the sum of the first $p$ terms equals the sum of the first $q$ terms plus a constant'. The cue is: two unknowns, two sum facts, one formula to deploy twice.

Motivation — Why this works

An AP is fully determined by $a$ and $d$, so two independent linear conditions suffice to pin it down. The formula $S_n = \tfrac{n}{2}(2a + (n-1)d)$ is linear in $a$ and $d$ for any fixed $n$, so substituting two distinct values of $n$ yields a $2\times 2$ linear system guaranteed to have a unique solution (provided the conditions are independent). This is the most direct route — alternatives like writing every term out or guessing are slower and error-prone.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice: two sum conditions are given on the same AP, and the unknowns are $a$ and $d$ — exactly two unknowns.
  2. Rule out the $n$th-term formula $u_n = a + (n-1)d$: the data are sums, not individual terms, so $S_n$ is the right tool.
  3. Commit: apply $S_n = \tfrac{n}{2}(2a + (n-1)d)$ at each given $n$ to get two linear equations in $a,d$.
  4. Bridge: substitute the numerical $n$ first, then simplify the constant factor (divide through by $n/2$) before recording the equation.
  5. Tactical: label the simplified equations (1) and (2), keep them in the form '... = 2a + kd' so subtraction eliminates $a$ cleanly.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the AP sum formula $S_n = \tfrac{n}{2}(2a + (n-1)d)$.
  2. Substitute the first given $n$ and its sum value; simplify by dividing through by the numerical prefactor.
  3. Substitute the second given $n$ and its sum value; simplify the same way.
  4. Label the two resulting linear equations and proceed to solve simultaneously (typically by subtraction to eliminate $a$).
  5. Back-substitute the recovered $d$ into either equation to find $a$.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $S_4 = 50$ and $S_8 = 196$ for an AP. Using $S_n = \tfrac{n}{2}(2a+(n-1)d)$: \\ $n=4$: $50 = 2(2a+3d) \Rightarrow 2a+3d = 25$ \quad (1) \\ $n=8$: $196 = 4(2a+7d) \Rightarrow 2a+7d = 49$ \quad (2) \\ (2)$-$(1): $4d = 24$, so $d = 6$ and then $a = 7/2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two individual terms given (e.g. $u_3 = 11$, $u_7 = 27$) for an AP
Use $u_n = a + (n-1)d$ twice instead of $S_n$; same elimination strategy.
One sum and one term given (e.g. $S_{10} = 100$ and $u_5 = 8$)
Mix: one equation from $S_n$ formula, one from $u_n$ formula; still a $2\times 2$ linear system in $a,d$.
Geometric progression with two sum conditions
Use $S_n = \tfrac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r}$ twice; dividing the equations often eliminates $a$ but leaves a non-linear equation in $r$.
Condition stated as a relation between sums (e.g. $S_{2n} = 3 S_n$)
Expand both sides via the $S_n$ formula to get a single equation; combine with another given fact for the second equation.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $S_n$ values as $u_n$ values and substituting into the $n$th-term formula.
  • Reading 'sum of terms from the 4th to the 8th' as $S_8$ alone, instead of $S_8 - S_3$.
  • Assuming the two sum equations must be solved by substitution rather than noticing the $2a$ structure invites elimination.

Pitfalls

  • Writing $nd$ instead of $(n-1)d$ inside the bracket.
  • Forgetting the factor $\tfrac{n}{2}$ outside the bracket.
  • Not simplifying early — large coefficients make subtraction error-prone.
  • Arithmetic slip when dividing both sides by the numerical prefactor (e.g. dividing only one side).

A-level Scope Notes
