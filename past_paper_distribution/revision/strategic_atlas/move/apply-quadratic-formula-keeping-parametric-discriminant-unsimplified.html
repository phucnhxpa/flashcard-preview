Complex Numbers

Apply quadratic formula keeping parametric discriminant unsimplified

Family: Parametric Discriminant Analysis Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a quadratic equation has at least one coefficient depending on a parameter (e.g. d, k, λ) and the question asks for roots 'in terms of' that parameter, or asks about the nature of the roots. Typical surface: x² − 10x + d² = 0, or kx² + 2kx + 3 = 0. Also fires inside a part-(a)/part-(b) structure where part (b) later substitutes a specific value of the parameter — signalling that the general form must be preserved.

Motivation — Why this works

The quadratic formula x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac))/(2a) is valid regardless of the sign of the discriminant, so leaving √(b² − 4ac) intact gives a single expression that handles real, repeated, and complex-conjugate cases simultaneously. Premature evaluation (e.g. assuming 25 − d² ≥ 0) loses generality and is wrong for some values of the parameter. Keeping the radical symbolic also makes part (b) trivial: just substitute the given value of the parameter and read off whether the radicand is positive, zero, or negative.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the quadratic has a parameter inside c (or b), so the discriminant is itself a function of that parameter.
  2. Rule out factorising by inspection — the parameter prevents a clean integer factorisation.
  3. Commit to the quadratic formula because it is the only tool that handles all sign cases of Δ uniformly.
  4. Bridge: substitute a, b, c into x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac))/(2a) without committing to a sign for Δ.
  5. Tactically pull a perfect-square factor out of the radical (e.g. √(4(25 − d²)) = 2√(25 − d²)) so the 2 cancels with the denominator, but leave the parameter-dependent part untouched.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify coefficients a, b, c, noting which contain the parameter.
  2. Write Δ = b² − 4ac symbolically (here Δ = 100 − 4d²).
  3. Extract the largest perfect-square numerical factor: √(4(25 − d²)) = 2√(25 − d²).
  4. Substitute into x = (−b ± √Δ)/(2a) and cancel common factors: x = 5 ± √(25 − d²).
  5. Stop — do not assume a sign for 25 − d². Record this as the parametric root form.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $x^2 - 10x + d^2 = 0$ in terms of $d$.\\ Here $a=1,\ b=-10,\ c=d^2$, so $\Delta = 100 - 4d^2 = 4(25 - d^2)$.\\ Then $x = \dfrac{10 \pm \sqrt{4(25 - d^2)}}{2} = \dfrac{10 \pm 2\sqrt{25 - d^2}}{2} = 5 \pm \sqrt{25 - d^2}.$\\ For $|d|<5$ the roots are real; for $|d|=5$ repeated; for $|d|>5$ they are complex conjugates $5 \pm i\sqrt{d^2 - 25}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic with parameter in the leading coefficient, e.g. $kx^2 + 2kx + 3 = 0$.
Discriminant becomes $4k^2 - 12k = 4k(k-3)$; keep $\sqrt{k(k-3)}$ symbolic and use sign of $k(k-3)$ to classify roots.
Question asks only for the nature of the roots in terms of the parameter.
Skip the formula and analyse the sign of Δ directly via inequality on the parameter.
Complex-coefficient quadratic appearing later, e.g. $z^2 - 2z + (1+\lambda^2) = 0$.
Same procedure; the radicand becomes $-4\lambda^2$, so write roots as $1 \pm i\lambda$ directly.
Part (b) supplies a specific value (e.g. $d = 13$).
Substitute into $5 \pm \sqrt{25 - d^2}$; if radicand negative, rewrite as $5 \pm i\sqrt{d^2 - 25}$.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming the radicand is positive and writing real roots without justification.
  • Treating $d$ as a known number and trying to factorise numerically.
  • Confusing the parametric discriminant with a discriminant condition for tangency or shared roots.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error with $b = -10$: computing $-b$ as $-10$ instead of $+10$.
  • Failing to extract the factor 4 from under the radical, leaving an unsimplified fraction $(10 \pm \sqrt{100 - 4d^2})/2$.
  • Writing $\sqrt{25 - d^2} = 5 - d$, which is false in general.
  • Prematurely converting to $i\sqrt{d^2 - 25}$ before knowing the sign of $25 - d^2$.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL Core Pure / FP1 territory: quadratic formula, discriminant analysis, and complex roots of real-coefficient quadratics. No Galois or general-degree theory required.
