Algebra & Functions

Assign independent decay clocks to each superimposed dose

Family: Modelling Setup Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Two or more quantities follow the same exponential decay law $x = De^{-kt}$ but are introduced at different absolute times, and you are asked for the total at some later instant. Wording cues include 'a second dose is given $5$ hours after the first', 'find the total amount $T$ hours after the second dose', or any phrasing that mixes a global observation time with locally-reset decay models. The model's $t$ variable explicitly means 'time since this particular dose was administered', not absolute clock time. Fires whenever the same decay/growth law must be applied with different starting epochs and the contributions are then summed.

Motivation — Why this works

Each dose decays independently from the instant it enters the body, so its contribution at any later time depends only on its own elapsed time, not on when other doses were given. Because the differential equation $\frac{dx}{dt} = -kx$ is linear, the principle of superposition applies: the total amount is the sum of the individual decaying contributions. Assigning a separate elapsed-time variable to each dose turns a confusing two-clock problem into a clean sum of exponentials. Once elapsed times are correctly identified, every term is a direct substitution into the given model.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the model $x = De^{-0.2t}$ uses $t$ as 'time since administration', so each dose carries its own private clock starting at zero.
  2. Rule out using a single shared $t$ for both doses — that would ignore the offset between administration times and double-count the decay.
  3. Commit to introducing distinct elapsed-time expressions for each dose, measured from its own start, evaluated at the same absolute observation moment.
  4. Bridge: pick an absolute time axis, mark each administration time on it, mark the observation time, and read off each elapsed time as (observation time) − (administration time).
  5. Tactically, write the total as a sum of exponential terms with the same $D$ and $k$ but different exponents, then simplify only after the structure is clearly visible.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Set an absolute time axis and record each dose's administration time, e.g. dose 1 at $t_{\text{abs}} = 0$, dose 2 at $t_{\text{abs}} = 5$.
  2. Identify the absolute observation time, e.g. $t_{\text{abs}} = 5 + T$.
  3. For each dose, compute elapsed time = observation time − administration time: dose 1 gives $5 + T$, dose 2 gives $T$.
  4. Substitute each elapsed time into the decay model: $15e^{-0.2(5+T)}$ and $15e^{-0.2T}$.
  5. Sum the contributions: $x = 15e^{-0.2(5+T)} + 15e^{-0.2T}$, then simplify (e.g. factor $15e^{-0.2T}$) only if useful.

Worked Micro-Example

A $15$ mg dose is given at $t_{\text{abs}} = 0$ and another at $t_{\text{abs}} = 5$, each decaying as $x = 15e^{-0.2t}$ where $t$ is hours since that dose. Total amount $T$ hours after the second dose (i.e. at $t_{\text{abs}} = 5+T$): dose 1 has decayed for $5+T$ hours, dose 2 for $T$ hours, so $x = 15e^{-0.2(5+T)} + 15e^{-0.2T} = 15e^{-0.2T}\!\left(e^{-1} + 1\right)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Three or more repeated doses in pharmacokinetics
Sum one exponential term per dose, each with its own elapsed time measured from its own administration moment.
Radioactive samples mixed at different times
Replace 'doses' with 'sources'; same superposition $x = \sum A_i e^{-k(t - t_i)}$ for $t \ge t_i$.
Newton's law of cooling with two objects placed in the room at different times
Each temperature excess decays from its own placement time; total/average uses individual elapsed times.
Compound interest deposits made on different dates (growth instead of decay)
Use $A_i e^{r(t - t_i)}$ with positive exponent; otherwise identical structure.

Common Misreadings

  • Using a single $t$ for both doses, e.g. writing $15e^{-0.2T} + 15e^{-0.2T} = 30e^{-0.2T}$, which ignores the $5$-hour offset.
  • Treating $t$ in the model as absolute clock time rather than 'time since this dose'.
  • Measuring both elapsed times from the first dose but forgetting to subtract $5$ in the second dose's exponent.

Pitfalls

  • Sign/offset error: writing $5 - T$ instead of $5 + T$ for the first dose's elapsed time.
  • Combining exponentials incorrectly, e.g. $e^{-0.2(5+T)} + e^{-0.2T} = e^{-0.2(5+2T)}$ (exponentials do not add that way).
  • Forgetting that the result is only valid for $T \ge 0$ (after the second dose has been given).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure/Statistics exponential modelling: uses only $e^{kt}$ manipulation and summation, no differential equations or convolution machinery beyond linear superposition stated in words.
