Binomial Expansion

Match expression to standard binomial series form (1+u)^n

Family: Template Matching Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when expanding $(1+\text{something})^n$ where $n$ is a fraction or negative number, so the finite binomial theorem cannot be used. The bracket already has constant term $1$, meaning no preliminary factoring is needed. Typical cues: prompts asking for an expansion 'in ascending powers of $x$', expressions like $(1+2x)^{-1/2}$, $(1-3x)^{2/3}$, or $\sqrt{1+x}$. Also fires when a validity range $|u|<1$ is requested, since this only makes sense for the series form.

Motivation — Why this works

The infinite binomial series $(1+u)^n = 1 + nu + \tfrac{n(n-1)}{2!}u^2 + \dots$ is stated for a base of the form $(1+u)^n$ with $|u|<1$. Recognising the expression as already matching this template lets the student substitute $u$ and $n$ directly without algebraic preprocessing. If the constant term were not $1$, the identity would not apply until the constant is factored out. Reading off $u$ as the entire non-constant part of the bracket — including any coefficient and sign — is the key act that makes every subsequent term correct.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the bracket has the shape $(1+\text{stuff})^n$ with $n=-\tfrac{1}{2}$, a non-positive-integer exponent.
  2. Rules out the finite binomial theorem $(a+b)^n = \sum \binom{n}{k}a^{n-k}b^k$, since $n$ is not a positive integer.
  3. Commits to the infinite series form because the constant term is already $1$, so no factoring is required and the template matches directly.
  4. Bridges to action by identifying $n=-\tfrac{1}{2}$ and $u=2x$ — taking the whole non-constant part, coefficient included, as $u$.
  5. Decides how many terms to compute (usually up to $x^3$ or $x^4$) and prepares to track $u^k = (2x)^k = 2^k x^k$ carefully.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Confirm the bracket is in the form $(1+u)^n$ with constant term $1$.
  2. Read off $n$ (the exponent) and $u$ (the entire non-constant part of the bracket, including sign and coefficient).
  3. Write the template: $(1+u)^n = 1 + nu + \tfrac{n(n-1)}{2!}u^2 + \tfrac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}u^3 + \dots$
  4. Substitute the identified $n$ and $u$ into each term, then simplify $u^k$ as $(\text{coef})^k x^k$.
  5. State the validity condition $|u|<1$, e.g. $|2x|<1 \Rightarrow |x|<\tfrac{1}{2}$.

Worked Micro-Example

Expand $(1+2x)^{-1/2}$ up to $x^2$. Identify $n=-\tfrac{1}{2}$, $u=2x$. Then $(1+2x)^{-1/2} = 1 + (-\tfrac{1}{2})(2x) + \tfrac{(-1/2)(-3/2)}{2}(2x)^2 + \dots = 1 - x + \tfrac{3}{8}\cdot 4x^2 + \dots = 1 - x + \tfrac{3}{2}x^2 + \dots$, valid for $|2x|<1$, i.e. $|x|<\tfrac{1}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$(4+2x)^{-1/2}$ — constant term is not $1$
Factor out the constant first: $4^{-1/2}(1+\tfrac{x}{2})^{-1/2} = \tfrac{1}{2}(1+\tfrac{x}{2})^{-1/2}$, then apply the template with $u=\tfrac{x}{2}$.
$(1-3x)^{2/3}$ — negative coefficient inside
Take $u=-3x$; track signs through each $u^k=(-3)^k x^k$ so odd-power terms become negative.
$\sqrt{1+x} = (1+x)^{1/2}$
Same template with $n=\tfrac{1}{2}$, $u=x$; validity $|x|<1$.
$\dfrac{1}{(1+x)^2} = (1+x)^{-2}$
Rewrite reciprocal as a negative power before applying the template with $n=-2$, $u=x$.

Common Misreadings

  • Identifying $u$ as $x$ instead of $2x$, losing the factor $2^k$ in every term.
  • Forgetting the sign when $u$ is negative (e.g. taking $u=3x$ instead of $u=-3x$ in $(1-3x)^n$).
  • Trying to use the choose-notation form $\binom{n}{k}$ as if $n$ were a positive integer.
  • Applying the series directly to $(a+bx)^n$ without first factoring out $a^n$.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to factor out the constant when the bracket is not of the form $(1+u)^n$.
  • Computing $(2x)^k$ as $2x^k$ instead of $2^k x^k$.
  • Omitting or misstating the validity condition $|u|<1$ in terms of $x$.
  • Arithmetic slips in the coefficients $\tfrac{n(n-1)\cdots(n-k+1)}{k!}$, especially with fractional $n$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (binomial expansion for rational/negative $n$, with $|u|<1$ validity). No content beyond standard A-level scope is invoked.
