Sequences & Series

Encode AP/GP conditions as simultaneous equations to extract a and d (or r)

Family: Parameter Extraction Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question about an arithmetic or geometric progression supplies two distinct numerical facts (e.g. a specific term value and a partial sum, two term values, or two sums) while leaving the defining parameters (first term a and common difference d, or common ratio r) unknown. Visual cues: phrases like 'the 4th term is...', 'the sum of the first 10 terms is...', 'in week 1... in week 12...', combined with a request to find a, d, r, or any further term/sum. Equivalently: 'two pieces of data + two unknown parameters' is the signature. The structural cue is that you must determine the sequence completely before answering anything else.

Motivation — Why this works

Every AP fact (a term u_n or a partial sum S_n) is a linear expression in a and d via u_n = a + (n−1)d and S_n = (n/2)(2a + (n−1)d). Two independent linear equations in two unknowns guarantee a unique solution, reducing the problem to standard simultaneous-equation work. For GPs the analogous extraction uses u_n = ar^{n−1}, often yielding r directly when a is known or via division to eliminate a. This step unlocks every downstream calculation in the question.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the question asks for a, d (or r), so I have exactly two unknowns and need two independent equations.
  2. Identify the two given facts and classify each as either a term value or a partial sum — this dictates which standard formula to invoke.
  3. Rule out shortcut moves like S_n = (n/2)(a + l), since the last term l is rarely known; commit instead to the (n/2)(2a + (n−1)d) form which is explicitly linear in a and d.
  4. Be careful with the index: the kth term uses (k−1), not k — week 1 means n=1 (offset 0), week 12 means n=12 (offset 11).
  5. Translate each fact into an equation, simplify each one (divide through by common factors) before solving, to keep arithmetic clean.
  6. Solve the 2×2 linear system by elimination or substitution; for GPs, divide one equation by the other to eliminate a and isolate r.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. For each given fact, write down the appropriate formula: u_n = a + (n−1)d for a term, S_n = (n/2)(2a + (n−1)d) for a sum.
  2. Substitute the given n and value to obtain Equation (1) and Equation (2), each linear in a and d.
  3. Simplify each equation by expanding and dividing through by any common numerical factor.
  4. Solve simultaneously (subtract Equation (1) from Equation (2), or eliminate a) to find d, then back-substitute to find a.
  5. For GP analogues: form ar^{m−1} = A and ar^{n−1} = B, divide to get r^{n−m} = B/A, then solve for r and back-substitute for a.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $u_4 = 18$ and $S_8 = 154$ for an AP. Equation (1): $a + 3d = 18$. Equation (2): $\tfrac{8}{2}(2a + 7d) = 154 \Rightarrow 2a + 7d = 38.5$. Doubling (1): $2a + 6d = 36$. Subtracting: $d = 2.5$, hence $a = 18 - 3(2.5) = 10.5$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two term values given (e.g. u_3 and u_8)
Both equations come from u_n = a + (n−1)d; subtract them to eliminate a immediately.
Two partial sums given (e.g. S_5 and S_12)
Both equations come from S_n = (n/2)(2a + (n−1)d); same extraction logic, different surface.
First and last term given (endpoint conditions)
If a is given as the first term, then a + (n−1)d = (last term) is a single equation in d alone — directly solvable.
Geometric progression analogue
Use u_n = ar^{n−1}; divide one equation by another to eliminate a and isolate r^{n−m} = ratio. The system is non-linear but reducible.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing u_n (the nth term) with S_n (the sum of the first n terms) — applying the wrong formula to a given fact.
  • Off-by-one on the index: writing u_5 = a + 5d instead of a + 4d, or ar^{12} instead of ar^{11} for the 12th term.
  • Treating 'week 1' as index 0 instead of n=1, shifting all subsequent offsets.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to simplify the sum equation (e.g. not dividing 5(2a + 9d) = 280 down to 2a + 9d = 56) leaves messy coefficients that invite arithmetic errors.
  • Using S_n = (n/2)(a + l) and incorrectly assuming the last term l is known when only a partial sum is given.
  • When solving the GP system by division, forgetting to consider whether r could be negative (only relevant if the problem allows it).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 (arithmetic series) and P2 (geometric series). The formulae u_n = a + (n−1)d, S_n = (n/2)(2a + (n−1)d), u_n = ar^{n−1}, and S_n = a(1−r^n)/(1−r) are all in the IAL formula booklet. Avoid invoking generating functions or closed forms for non-standard recurrences — those are out of scope.
