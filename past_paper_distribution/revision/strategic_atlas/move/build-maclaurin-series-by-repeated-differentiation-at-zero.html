Sequences & Series

Build Maclaurin series by repeated differentiation at zero

Family: Maclaurin Direct Differentiation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an FP2 question explicitly asks for a Maclaurin expansion of a composite function such as $\ln(1+\sin x)$, $e^{\sin x}$, or $\sec x$ up to a stated power of $x$. Phrasings include 'use the definition of the Maclaurin series', 'show that the expansion is...', or 'find the series up to and including the term in $x^n$'. Mark allocations of 7+ and the absence of a hint to use standard series strongly signal that derivatives must be shown explicitly.

Motivation — Why this works

The Maclaurin formula $f(x)=\sum_{n\ge 0} \tfrac{f^{(n)}(0)}{n!}x^n$ recovers a polynomial approximation directly from successive derivatives at the origin. Each derivative contributes one coefficient, so computing them in turn produces the series term by term with no reliance on memorised expansions. In 'show that' questions this is the safest route because every method mark is tied to a visible derivative evaluated at $0$.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the command word ('use the definition' / 'show that') and the high mark tariff, which signal that an explicit derivative-by-derivative build-up is expected.
  2. Rule out shortcut composition (substituting one standard series into another) because it can lose method marks when the question demands the formal Maclaurin process.
  3. Commit to the formula $f(x)=f(0)+f'(0)x+\tfrac{f''(0)}{2!}x^2+\cdots$ and plan to compute each $f^{(k)}(0)$ up to the required order.
  4. Bridge: evaluate $f(0)$ first to anchor the constant term, then differentiate once and substitute $x=0$.
  5. Tactically simplify each derivative before differentiating again — e.g. rewrite $\tfrac{\cos x}{1+\sin x}$ cleanly — to control sign and chain-rule errors at higher orders.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the template $f(x)=f(0)+f'(0)x+\tfrac{f''(0)}{2!}x^2+\tfrac{f'''(0)}{3!}x^3+\tfrac{f^{(4)}(0)}{4!}x^4+\cdots$ to the required order.
  2. Evaluate $f(0)$ directly.
  3. Differentiate to obtain $f'(x)$, simplify, then substitute $x=0$.
  4. Repeat: compute $f''(x), f'''(x), \ldots$, evaluating each at $0$ and tabulating the values.
  5. Substitute the values into the template and divide by the appropriate factorial in each term.
  6. Collect terms and present the final polynomial up to the requested power.

Worked Micro-Example

For $f(x)=\ln(1+\sin x)$: $f(0)=0$. $f'(x)=\tfrac{\cos x}{1+\sin x}$, so $f'(0)=1$. Differentiating gives $f''(x)=\tfrac{-1}{1+\sin x}$, so $f''(0)=-1$. Then $f'''(x)=\tfrac{\cos x}{(1+\sin x)^2}$, so $f'''(0)=1$. Hence $\ln(1+\sin x)=x-\tfrac{x^2}{2}+\tfrac{x^3}{6}-\cdots$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Taylor series about $x=a$ (e.g. expand $\ln x$ about $x=1$)
Evaluate every derivative at $a$ and use $(x-a)^n$ powers in the template.
Function defined implicitly by a differential equation, e.g. $y'=x+y^2$, $y(0)=1$
Read $f(0)$ from the initial condition and obtain successive $f^{(k)}(0)$ by differentiating the DE and substituting $x=0$ each time.
Function defined implicitly by an equation in $x$ and $y$
Differentiate implicitly to express $y',y'',\ldots$ in terms of $x,y$, then evaluate at $x=0$ using the given $y(0)$.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing Maclaurin (centre $0$) with Taylor (centre $a$) and evaluating derivatives at the wrong point.
  • Quoting a standard series for $\ln(1+u)$ or $\sin x$ when the question requires the definition-based derivation.
  • Treating 'up to the term in $x^4$' as needing only four derivatives rather than including $f^{(4)}(0)$.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the $n!$ denominator — writing $f''(0)x^2$ instead of $\tfrac{f''(0)}{2!}x^2$.
  • Sign errors propagating through repeated quotient or chain rule applications.
  • Failing to simplify a derivative before differentiating again, leading to algebra that is hard to evaluate at $0$.
  • Stopping one term short of the required order, or including an extra unwanted term.

A-level Scope Notes

Maclaurin and Taylor series by repeated differentiation are core FP2 content in Edexcel IAL Further Pure Mathematics; convergence is not assessed beyond stating validity for standard expansions.
