Differentiation

Rewrite constant-over-linear as negative power before differentiating

Family: Algebraic Preprocessing Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger ŌĆö When to use

Fires when y is a constant (or simple expression) divided by a linear factor ŌĆö or by a power/root of a linear factor ŌĆö such as y = 16/(3(3xŌłÆk)), y = 5/(2x+1)^3, or y = 1/ŌłÜ(4xŌłÆ7). The numerator's derivative is zero or trivial, so the quotient rule would carry dead weight. Cue phrases: 'differentiate y = A/(ax+b)^n', 'find dy/dx where the variable only appears in the denominator'. Especially attractive when the denominator is itself wrapped in another constant factor that can be pulled out front.

Motivation ŌĆö Why this works

Any expression A/(ax+b)^n equals A(ax+b)^(ŌłÆn), reducing differentiation to one application of the chain rule on a power: bring the exponent down, reduce by one, multiply by the inner derivative. The quotient rule produces the same answer but with an f'g ŌłÆ fg' template where f' = 0, wasting steps and inviting sign errors. Extracting any pure constants first keeps the remaining composite as clean as possible, so only one piece ŌĆö the bracket raised to a power ŌĆö actually needs differentiating.

Thought Process ŌĆö The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the variable lives entirely in the denominator, with the numerator a pure constant ŌĆö no genuine quotient structure.
  2. Rule out the quotient rule: it would produce a zero from f' and is overkill for a constant-over-power.
  3. Commit to rewriting as constant ├Ś (linear)^(negative power), turning the problem into a standard chain-rule-on-a-power.
  4. Pull every constant factor (including any constant inside the denominator like the 3 in 3(3xŌłÆk)) out front before differentiating, so only the bracketed power remains.
  5. Apply n┬Ę(inner)^(nŌłÆ1)┬Ę(inner)' carefully, watching the sign from the negative exponent and remembering to multiply by the inner derivative.

Mechanic ŌĆö Concrete operations

  1. Identify the structure y = A/[c┬Ę(ax+b)^n] and pull the constant c into the front: y = (A/c)(ax+b)^(ŌłÆn).
  2. Differentiate using the chain rule on a power: dy/dx = (A/c)┬Ę(ŌłÆn)(ax+b)^(ŌłÆnŌłÆ1)┬Ęa.
  3. Multiply numerical constants together cleanly: combine A/c, ŌłÆn, and the inner derivative a into a single coefficient.
  4. If a positive-denominator form is required, rewrite the negative power back as a fraction for the final answer.

Worked Micro-Example

Differentiate $y = \dfrac{16}{3(3x-k)}$. Rewrite: $y = \tfrac{16}{3}(3x-k)^{-1}$. Then $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \tfrac{16}{3}\cdot(-1)(3x-k)^{-2}\cdot 3 = -\dfrac{16}{(3x-k)^2}$.

Variants ŌĆö Same move, different surface

Denominator is a power of a linear factor, e.g. y = 5/(2x+1)^3
Rewrite as 5(2x+1)^(ŌłÆ3); chain rule gives dy/dx = 5┬Ę(ŌłÆ3)(2x+1)^(ŌłÆ4)┬Ę2 = ŌłÆ30/(2x+1)^4.
Denominator is a root, e.g. y = 1/ŌłÜ(4xŌłÆ7)

Sum of such terms, e.g. y = 3/x ŌłÆ 4/x^2

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 3(3xŌłÆk) as a single bracket and forgetting to extract the leading 3, losing a factor of 1/3 in the final answer.
  • Reading the move as needing the quotient rule because the expression 'looks like a fraction', when in fact the numerator is constant.
  • Confusing the constant denominator factor with part of the chain ŌĆö only ax+b is the inner function, not c(ax+b).

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to multiply by the inner derivative (the 'a' from ax+b) ŌĆö the most common chain rule omission.
  • Sign error: the negative exponent contributes a minus sign that must persist into the simplified form.
  • Miscombining the constants: e.g. (16/3)┬Ę(ŌłÆ1)┬Ę3 should give ŌłÆ16, not ŌłÆ16/3.
  • Reducing the exponent incorrectly: ŌłÆ1 reduces to ŌłÆ2, not 0; ŌłÆ1/2 reduces to ŌłÆ3/2, not 1/2.
