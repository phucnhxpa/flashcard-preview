Algebra & Functions

Reverse substitution and clear compound fractions by scaling

Family: Substitution Reversal Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires at the final stage of a substitution-based solution (Bernoulli ODE, homogeneous ODE, reciprocal/power substitution) when the working variable has been solved but the original variable is still required. The cue is a general solution like z = ax/b + c/d + Ce^{kx} that must be inverted via y^n = 1/z or y = xv, producing a compound fraction with mixed denominators. Also triggers whenever an arbitrary constant is multiplied by an awkward numerical factor that can be absorbed.

Motivation — Why this works

Examiners reward a tidy closed form with integer coefficients in the denominator, not a stack of fractions. Multiplying numerator and denominator of a single fraction by the LCM of inner denominators is an identity-preserving operation. Because C (or any +C) is arbitrary, any nonzero constant multiple kC is again an arbitrary constant and may be relabelled A — this is a standard, legitimate re-parameterisation of the family of solutions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that the answer is required in y, not the auxiliary z (or v), so the substitution must be reversed.
  2. Rules out leaving fractions inside fractions: a denominator like 3x/2 + 3/8 + Ce^{4x} is messy and loses presentation marks.
  3. Commits to scaling top and bottom by the LCM of the inner denominators (here 8), since C has not been pinned down by an initial condition.
  4. Bridges to operation: writes y^2 = 1/z explicitly, then multiplies the single fraction by 8/8.
  5. Inside the move, decides to absorb 8C into a fresh arbitrary constant A, and double-checks that the squared variable y^2 (not y) is what was isolated.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the reverse-substitution identity, e.g. y^2 = 1/z.
  2. Substitute the explicit z to get y^2 = 1/(3x/2 + 3/8 + Ce^{4x}).
  3. Identify the LCM of inner denominators (here 8) and multiply numerator and denominator by it.
  4. Simplify each term: 8(3x/2) = 12x, 8(3/8) = 3, 8Ce^{4x} = 8Ce^{4x}.
  5. Relabel 8C as a new arbitrary constant A, giving y^2 = 8/(12x + 3 + Ae^{4x}).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve a Bernoulli ODE giving z = 3x/2 + 3/8 + Ce^{4x} where z = 1/y^2. Then y^2 = 1/(3x/2 + 3/8 + Ce^{4x}). Multiply top and bottom by 8: y^2 = 8/(12x + 3 + 8Ce^{4x}). Letting A = 8C, the tidy final form is y^2 = 8/(12x + 3 + Ae^{4x}).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Homogeneous ODE solved via v = y/x
After finding v(x), reverse with y = xv and expand; clear any fractions in v(x) before multiplying through by x.
Bernoulli with z = y^{1-n}
Reverse via y = z^{1/(1-n)}; if z is a sum of fractions, scale inside the bracket before taking the root.
Partial-fraction integration giving ln|y| = ln|f(x)| + C
Exponentiate and absorb e^C into a new arbitrary constant A, then clear inner fractions in f(x).
Integrating-factor solution y·μ(x) = ∫... + C
After dividing by μ(x), multiply top and bottom by a common factor to remove fractional coefficients, relabelling C if it picks up a constant multiple.

Common Misreadings

  • Leaving the answer as z = ... and forgetting to convert back to y entirely.
  • Believing the scaling changes the solution rather than its presentation.
  • Treating 8C and C as different constants in the answer scheme rather than recognising the relabel.

Pitfalls

  • Relabelling 8C as A is valid only because C is arbitrary — if an initial condition has already fixed C, the numerical factor must be kept.
  • Writing y = ... instead of y^2 = ..., or taking a square root without ± and without checking the sign permitted by the initial condition.
  • Multiplying only part of the denominator by 8 (forgetting the Ce^{4x} term) or forgetting to multiply the numerator.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard finishing step in Edexcel IAL Further Pure first-order ODE questions (Bernoulli, homogeneous, integrating factor); the arbitrary-constant relabel is explicitly accepted in mark schemes.
