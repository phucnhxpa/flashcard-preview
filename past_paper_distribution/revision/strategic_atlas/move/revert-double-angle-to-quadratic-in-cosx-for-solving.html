Trigonometry

Revert double-angle to quadratic in cosx for solving

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation mixes cos2x (or sin2x, cos4x etc.) with cosx (or sinx) at different 'frequencies' so it cannot be solved directly. Often appears in part (b) of a question whose part (a) was a 'show that' identity producing the mixed form. Cue phrases: 'hence solve', 'use part (a) to solve', or simply spotting both cos2x and cosx in the same equation. The presence of two different arguments is the key signal that one variable must be eliminated.

Motivation — Why this works

An equation in a single trig ratio is solvable; one mixing arguments is not. The double-angle identity cos2x = 2cos²x − 1 (or 1 − 2sin²x, depending on which single ratio also appears) collapses both terms onto one variable, producing a polynomial equation that factors or uses the quadratic formula. This exploits a deliberate two-way use of the identity — forward to manufacture the cos2x form for a 'show that', backward to reduce for solving.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the equation contains both cos2x and cosx — two different arguments, so direct inverse-trig won't work.
  2. I rule out treating cos2x as an independent unknown; that would give arccos branches that don't combine cleanly with the cosx term.
  3. I commit to substituting cos2x = 2cos²x − 1 because cosx is the ratio already present, making the substitution land in a single variable.
  4. First concrete operation: replace cos2x, expand, and collect into a quadratic in cosx.
  5. Tactically I let y = cosx, factor or use the formula, then reject any root outside [−1, 1] and finally solve the surviving cosx = k within the stated range, remembering cosine's even symmetry.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the mixed arguments (cos2x and cosx) and choose the identity that lands on cosx: cos2x = 2cos²x − 1.
  2. Substitute and expand to clear the double-angle term.
  3. Collect like terms and divide through by any common factor to simplify.
  4. Let y = cosx and write the resulting quadratic 3y² − 7y + 2 = 0 (or analogous).
  5. Factor or apply the quadratic formula to obtain candidate values of cosx.
  6. Reject any root outside [−1, 1].
  7. Solve cosx = k in the given range, using cosine's symmetry to capture all roots.
  8. Combine with any solutions from a separate factor (e.g. sinx = 0 if previously factored out).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $3\cos 2x - 14\cos x + 7 = 0$ for $-\tfrac{\pi}{2} \le x \le \tfrac{\pi}{2}$.\\ Substitute $\cos 2x = 2\cos^2 x - 1$: $6\cos^2 x - 3 - 14\cos x + 7 = 0 \Rightarrow 3\cos^2 x - 7\cos x + 2 = 0$.\\ Factor: $(3\cos x - 1)(\cos x - 2) = 0$, so $\cos x = \tfrac{1}{3}$ (reject $\cos x = 2$).\\ Hence $x = \pm \arccos\tfrac{1}{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation mixes cos2x with sinx (e.g. cos2x + 3sinx = 2)
Use cos2x = 1 − 2sin²x instead, to land on a quadratic in sinx.
Equation mixes sin2x with sinx or cosx (e.g. sin2x = sinx)
Use sin2x = 2sinxcosx and factor out the common ratio rather than form a quadratic; gives sinx(2cosx − 1) = 0.
'Hence solve' after a part (a) identity such as 2cos²x − 1 ≡ cos2x
Use the identity in the reverse direction from the way it was proven, substituting back into the equation.
Equation involving cos4x and cos2x
Apply cos4x = 2cos²2x − 1 to reduce to a quadratic in cos2x, then solve cos2x = k and divide the resulting angles by 2 (taking care with range).
Equation in tan2x and tanx
Use tan2x = 2tanx/(1 − tan²x), clear denominators, and form a polynomial in tanx.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating cos2x and cosx as independent quantities and applying arccos directly to the cos2x term.
  • Trying R-formula techniques (only valid for a·cosx + b·sinx with the same argument) on a mixed-argument equation.
  • Choosing cos2x = 1 − 2sin²x when the other term is cosx, introducing both sinx and cosx and complicating the equation.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to reject roots outside [−1, 1]; the quadratic typically gives one valid and one extraneous root.
  • On a symmetric range like −π/2 ≤ x ≤ π/2, giving only the positive arccos value and missing the negative counterpart due to cosine's even symmetry.
  • Losing solutions from a previously factored-out term (e.g. sinx = 0) when stitching the final solution set together.
  • Arithmetic slips when expanding 3(2cos²x − 1) and combining the constant with the +7.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the standard double-angle identity cos2x = 2cos²x − 1 and quadratic factoring/inverse trig within a stated range — fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P3 trigonometry).
