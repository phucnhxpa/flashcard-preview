Algebra & Functions

Back-substitute solved variable into earlier elimination relation

Family: Back Substitution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires at the end of a simultaneous-equations problem once one unknown has been found (e.g. via a cubic, quadratic, or linear elimination) and a clean linear relation linking the remaining unknowns to that variable was produced earlier. Cues: the question explicitly asks for both/all unknowns; an expression of the form 'a = ... in terms of r' (or 'd = ... in terms of a') sits in the working from the elimination step. Phrasings include 'find a and r', 'state the values of...', or 'hence find...'.

Motivation — Why this works

During elimination, one equation is typically rearranged to express one unknown in terms of another before substituting into the second equation. That rearrangement is itself a valid identity for the solution, so once the second unknown is pinned down, plugging it back recovers the first in one line. This avoids re-solving the original (often nonlinear) system and is the canonical closing step of the elimination method.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the question asked for two unknowns but only one (here r) has been numerically determined.
  2. Rule out returning to the original equations or re-solving the system — the linear bridge a = 7 − 3r is already on the page from the elimination step.
  3. Commit to back-substitution because that bridge equation is linear in a and holds for the valid root.
  4. Bridge to operation: substitute r = −2 into a = 7 − 3r, taking care with the sign of the negative root.
  5. Tactically, finish by sanity-checking both sequences (AP sum, GP terms) so any arithmetic slip surfaces before the answer is stated.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the linear relation from the elimination stage: a = 7 − 3r.
  2. Substitute the found root r = −2: a = 7 − 3(−2) = 7 + 6 = 13.
  3. Verify against the original conditions (e.g. check AP and GP terms agree).
  4. State both values: a = 13, r = −2.

Worked Micro-Example

From elimination: $a = 7 - 3r$, and the resulting cubic gave $r = -2$ as the only valid root. Substituting: $a = 7 - 3(-2) = 13$. Check: AP first four terms $13, 9, 5, 1$ (common difference $-4$); GP terms $13, -26, 52$ (ratio $-2$). Hence $a = 13,\ r = -2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Simultaneous linear-quadratic system (e.g. line meets curve)
After solving the quadratic for x, substitute each x-value into the linear equation y = mx + c to recover y; report coordinate pairs.
AP/GP problem where d (not a) is expressed in terms of r
Use d = f(r) from the elimination step and substitute the found r to obtain d, then a if required.
Polynomial division / factor theorem context
Once a root α is found, substitute into the relation linking remaining coefficients (e.g. from comparing coefficients) to recover them.
Parametric or vector simultaneous equations
Solve for one parameter, then substitute into the position-vector expression to obtain the point of intersection.
Logarithmic/exponential systems reduced by substitution u = a^x
After finding u, back-substitute u = a^x and solve for x using logs.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the problem as finished once the cubic/quadratic is solved, missing that the question asks for both unknowns.
  • Believing one must re-substitute into the original nonlinear equation rather than the cleaner linear bridge.
  • Substituting a rejected root (e.g. an extraneous r) instead of the validated one.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when the found root is negative, e.g. computing 7 − 3(−2) as 1 instead of 13.
  • Forgetting to recover the second unknown after solving for the first.
  • Skipping the consistency check, allowing arithmetic slips to propagate to the final answer.
  • Using an unsimplified or mis-rearranged version of the bridge equation.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard closing step for simultaneous-equation problems across Edexcel IAL Pure (P1–P4) and Further Pure, including AP/GP setups, line-curve intersections, and factor-theorem follow-ups.
