Proof

Encode divisibility hypothesis as an algebraic equation with integer parameter

Family: Induction Hypothesis Encoding Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in proof-by-induction problems whose statement P(n) is a divisibility claim such as 'd divides f(n)' rather than an explicit closed-form equation. Cue phrases include 'show that 21 divides 4^{n+2}+5^{2n+1}', 'prove that f(n) is a multiple of d', or 'is divisible by'. The hypothesis P(k) needs an algebraic handle before any inductive-step manipulation can begin. Use this move immediately after writing 'Assume true for n=k'.

Motivation — Why this works

A verbal statement 'd divides X' is logically equivalent to 'X = d·m for some integer m', and only the equation form supports substitution and rearrangement. Introducing the integer parameter m converts the hypothesis into a usable equation that can be solved for one term and slotted into the expression for P(k+1). The integrality of m is precisely what later allows the conclusion 'therefore divisible by d'. Without this encoding, the inductive step has nothing algebraic to lean on.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice P(n) is a divisibility claim, not an equation, so I cannot directly manipulate it.
  2. I rule out trying to 'see' divisibility at the end of the inductive step — that route tends to circular reasoning.
  3. I commit to rewriting 'd divides f(k)' as 'f(k) = d·m, where m ∈ ℤ' so the hypothesis becomes an equation.
  4. I label this Equation (1) so I can cite it explicitly when manipulating f(k+1).
  5. I anticipate I may need to solve (1) for one of the terms in f(k), e.g. isolate 4^{k+2} = 21m − 5^{2k+1}, ready for substitution.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write: 'Assume P(k) is true, i.e. f(k) = d·m for some integer m.'
  2. Label this as Equation (1) for later reference.
  3. If useful, rearrange (1) to isolate one term of f(k): e.g. 4^{k+2} = 21m − 5^{2k+1}.
  4. Keep m flagged as an integer so its role survives into the conclusion.

Worked Micro-Example

Claim: $21 \mid 4^{n+2}+5^{2n+1}$ for all $n\in\mathbb{N}$.\\ Inductive hypothesis: assume $4^{k+2}+5^{2k+1}=21m$ for some $m\in\mathbb{Z}$. \quad (1)\\ Rearranging (1): $4^{k+2}=21m-5^{2k+1}$, ready to substitute into $4^{(k+1)+2}+5^{2(k+1)+1}$ during the inductive step.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Divisibility induction like '6 divides 7^n − 1'
Write 7^k − 1 = 6m, m ∈ ℤ; rearrange to 7^k = 6m + 1 for substitution.
Divisibility of a sum of three exponentials
Still one equation suffices: f(k) = d·m; isolate whichever term cancels cleanly in f(k+1).
Matrix/expression divisibility (Further Pure)
Same encoding: write the matrix entry or expression equal to d·M (or d·m), keep integrality explicit.
Statement 'f(n) is a multiple of d'
Identical move — 'multiple of d' is read as '= d·m, m ∈ ℤ'.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the hypothesis as merely a phrase to quote later, instead of an equation to substitute.
  • Confusing this with the explicit-formula induction template, where P(k) is already an equation and no encoding is needed.
  • Thinking m must be a specific value rather than an unspecified integer.

Pitfalls

  • Omitting 'where m is an integer' — without integrality the final divisibility conclusion is unjustified.
  • Reusing the letter m for a different integer at the n=k+1 stage; introduce a new symbol (e.g. M) for the new multiple.
  • Rearranging incorrectly when isolating one exponential term, e.g. sign errors in 4^{k+2}=21m−5^{2k+1}.
  • Forgetting to reference Equation (1) explicitly when the substitution is made, weakening the logical chain.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard technique for proof-by-induction divisibility questions in Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP1); uses only integer arithmetic and basic algebra, well within A-level scope.
