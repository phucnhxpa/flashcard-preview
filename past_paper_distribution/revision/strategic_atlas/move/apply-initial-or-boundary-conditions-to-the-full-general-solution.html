Differential Equations

Apply initial or boundary conditions to the full general solution

Family: General To Particular Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires once the general solution $y = \text{CF} + \text{PI}$ has been assembled and the question supplies specific numerical values such as $y(x_0) = y_0$ and $y'(x_0) = y_1$, or two boundary values like $y(0) = a$ and $y(1) = b$. Cues include phrases 'given that... when $x = 0$', 'find the particular solution', or 'solve the differential equation subject to'. Two conditions are required because the CF of a second-order linear ODE carries two arbitrary constants $A$ and $B$.

Motivation — Why this works

The general solution describes an infinite family of curves; conditions select the unique member passing through the prescribed state. Each condition produces one linear equation in $A$ and $B$, and two independent conditions determine them uniquely. The conditions must be applied to $y = \text{CF} + \text{PI}$ (and its derivative) — never to the CF alone — because the PI contributes to both the value and slope of $y$ at $x_0$.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the general solution $y = \text{CF} + \text{PI}$ is fully assembled with exactly two unknown constants $A$ and $B$.
  2. Rule out re-deriving anything: the ODE work is done; the remaining task is purely algebraic substitution and solving a 2×2 system.
  3. Commit to the strategy because the count matches — two conditions for two constants — guaranteeing a unique determination.
  4. Bridge to action by first differentiating $y$ to obtain $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$, since one condition typically involves $y'$; product/chain rule is needed if the CF contains $xe^{mx}$ or $e^{px}\cos qx$ terms.
  5. Tactically substitute the simpler condition first (often $x = 0$, where exponentials and trig collapse to $1$ or $0$), then the second; solve the simultaneous equations and write out the particular solution explicitly.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the general solution $y = \text{CF} + \text{PI}$ explicitly with arbitrary constants $A$ and $B$.
  2. Differentiate to obtain $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$, applying product and chain rules as needed.
  3. Substitute the first condition into $y$ to get Equation (1) in $A$ and $B$.
  4. Substitute the second condition into $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ (or into $y$ at the second $x$-value) to get Equation (2) in $A$ and $B$.
  5. Solve the simultaneous equations for $A$ and $B$.
  6. State the particular solution with numerical values substituted.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose $y = Ae^{x} + Be^{-x} + 2x$ with $y(0) = 3$ and $y'(0) = 1$. Then $y'= Ae^{x} - Be^{-x} + 2$. At $x=0$: $A + B = 3$ and $A - B + 2 = 1 \Rightarrow A - B = -1$. Solving gives $A = 1$, $B = 2$, so the particular solution is $y = e^{x} + 2e^{-x} + 2x$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Boundary conditions at two different $x$-values, e.g. $y(0) = 1$ and $y(\pi/2) = 0$.
Substitute each condition into $y$ alone (no derivative needed); the resulting 2×2 system may have trig/exponential coefficients but is solved identically.
CF of repeated-root form $(A + Bx)e^{mx}$.
When differentiating, apply the product rule carefully: $y' = Be^{mx} + m(A + Bx)e^{mx}$ before substituting.
CF of complex-root form $e^{px}(A\cos qx + B\sin qx)$.
Differentiation requires both product and chain rules; evaluating at $x = 0$ usefully kills the $\sin$ terms in $y$ and isolates $A$ quickly.
Conditions given on $y$ and $y''$ (rare but possible after a substitution reduction).
Compute $y''$ from the general solution and substitute analogously; the system in $A$ and $B$ is still linear.
Coupled system reduced to a single second-order ODE in one variable.
After finding the particular solution for one variable, back-substitute into the original system to recover the other; conditions on the second variable may pin down constants instead.

Common Misreadings

  • Applying the conditions to the CF only and ignoring the PI's contribution to $y$ and $y'$ at $x_0$.
  • Differentiating the PI incorrectly or omitting it from $y'$ before substituting.
  • Treating a single condition as sufficient and leaving one constant arbitrary in the final answer.
  • Confusing initial conditions (same $x$) with boundary conditions (different $x$-values) and substituting both into $y'$.

Pitfalls

  • Missing the product rule when the CF contains $xe^{mx}$ or $e^{px}\cos qx$ / $e^{px}\sin qx$.
  • Sign errors from $e^{-x}$ differentiating to $-e^{-x}$ or from derivatives of $\cos$ and $\sin$.
  • Arithmetic slips when evaluating exponentials/trig at non-zero $x$-values such as $\pi/4$ or $\ln 2$.
  • Solving the 2×2 system incorrectly when coefficients involve surds, fractions, or transcendental values.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope: second-order linear ODEs with constant coefficients, standard PI forms (polynomial, exponential, trig), and at most two conditions yielding a linear 2×2 system in $A$ and $B$.
