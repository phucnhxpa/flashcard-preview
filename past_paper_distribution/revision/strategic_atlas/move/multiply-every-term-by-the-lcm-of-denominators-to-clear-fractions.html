Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an equation contains one or more fractional coefficients or rational terms (numeric denominators like 3, 7, 2, or algebraic denominators like x or g(x)) AND the target form requires integer coefficients or a polynomial. Visual cues: a 'show that ax² + bx + c = 0' style instruction with integer constants, a coordinate-geometry answer demanded in 'ax + by + c = 0' form, or a rational equation that must be reduced to a polynomial for discriminant/factorisation work. Equivalently phrased: 'rewrite the equation without fractions', 'in the form ... where a, b, c are integers', or 'hence show that ...'. Whenever you see denominators on one side and clean integers on the other, this move is the bridge.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying every term by the lowest common multiple of all denominators eliminates fractions in a single, symmetric operation, replacing brittle fractional arithmetic with clean integer (or polynomial) arithmetic. It exploits the basic equivalence principle: multiplying both sides of an equation by a non-zero quantity preserves its solution set. This is more efficient than clearing one fraction at a time and avoids accumulating intermediate non-integer expressions where sign or arithmetic errors creep in. For 'show that' questions it gives a clean checkpoint matching the printed target; for discriminant work it produces the required polynomial form.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the equation contains fractions — either numeric denominators (e.g. 3, 7, 217, 2) or an algebraic denominator like 1/x — while the form I'm asked to reach has integer coefficients or is a polynomial.
  2. I rule out expanding first and clearing fractions term-by-term: that path multiplies arithmetic mistakes and tends to produce ugly intermediate fractions. I also rule out leaving fractions in place, since the target form forbids them.
  3. I commit to a single multiply-through step. I list every denominator appearing in the equation and compute their LCM (for coprime denominators like 3 and 217 this is just their product; for shared factors like 7 and 2 it is 14).
  4. Before multiplying, I sanity-check the multiplier: a quick mental product against one or two known target coefficients confirms the LCM is correct (e.g. 14·(6/7) = 12 matches the target's leading coefficient).
  5. If the multiplier is an algebraic expression (like x or g(x)), I check that it is non-zero on the relevant domain so I don't introduce spurious roots — e.g. x = 0 is already excluded by a 1/x term.
  6. I then multiply EVERY term on both sides by the LCM, simplify each product separately, and rearrange to the requested standard form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify all denominators D₁, D₂, ..., Dₙ appearing in the equation (numeric or algebraic).
  2. Compute L = lcm(D₁, ..., Dₙ); for pairwise coprime numeric denominators, L is their product.
  3. If L is algebraic, note any domain restriction (values where L = 0 must already be excluded).
  4. Multiply every term on both sides of the equation by L, simplifying L/Dᵢ against each fractional term.
  5. Expand any brackets that remain, then collect like terms and rearrange to the target form (e.g. ax + by + c = 0 or ax² + bx + c = 0).
  6. Cross-check by substituting one coefficient against the printed target to confirm no term was missed.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $y - \tfrac{11}{3} = -\tfrac{4}{217}(x-4)$, denominators are 3 and 217 (coprime), so $L = 651$. Multiply through: $651y - 217\cdot 11 = -3\cdot 4(x-4)$, i.e. $651y - 2387 = -12x + 48$. Rearranging gives $12x + 651y - 2435 = 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Single fractional gradient in point-slope form, e.g. y - y₀ = (p/q)(x - x₀)
Multiply through by q only — same principle with one denominator.
Rational equation with x in the denominator (e.g. -k/x + k = 3x + 4)
Multiply every term by x to obtain a quadratic; verify x = 0 is already excluded so no spurious root is introduced.
Simultaneous equations both containing fractions
Clear each equation independently by its own LCM before applying elimination/substitution.
Surd denominators like 1/(√a − √b)
Different family (rationalising) — multiply by the conjugate, not the LCM.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the multiplier as applying only to the LHS, leaving the RHS fraction (e.g. -1/7) untouched.
  • Mistaking a rational equation for one already in polynomial form and trying to apply the discriminant directly.
  • Reading 'show that' as permission to skip the clearing step and just quote the target.

Pitfalls

  • Miscomputing the LCM when denominators share a factor (e.g. lcm(6, 4) = 12, not 24).
  • Forgetting to multiply the constant term or RHS by the LCM, producing an incorrect constant.
  • Sign errors when bringing terms across after clearing (e.g. -35 = -2 becoming -33, not -37).
  • Arithmetic slips with large products like 217 × 11 = 2387.
  • Multiplying by an algebraic expression that can be zero in the relevant domain, introducing spurious solutions.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1–P4) — no formula booklet entry is invoked; this is foundational algebraic manipulation used across coordinate geometry, quadratics, and 'show that' derivations. Avoid generalising to clearing fractions inside inequalities without checking the sign of the multiplier (out of typical scope and a common error source).
