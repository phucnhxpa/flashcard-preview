Integration

Substitute u = inner function to absorb its derivative from the numerator

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the integrand has the form f(g(x))·g'(x), i.e. a composite expression whose inner function's derivative appears (up to a constant) as a separate factor. Classic surface: a denominator like (3 + sin x)² with cos x in the numerator, since d/dx(3 + sin x) = cos x. Also triggered when a substitution u = ... is explicitly suggested in the question. Look for 'inner function + its derivative sitting alongside it' as the key cue.

Motivation — Why this works

By the chain rule in reverse, ∫ f(g(x))·g'(x) dx = ∫ f(u) du where u = g(x). The substitution converts the composite (3 + sin x)² into u² and exactly consumes cos x dx as du, leaving a pure power integrand 1/u² that integrates by the reverse power rule. No residual x remains because every x-dependent piece is either inside g(x) or inside g'(x) dx. This is the cleanest possible substitution: a one-to-one absorption with no leftover constants to balance.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand is cos x / (3 + sin x)²: a composite (3 + sin x) raised to a power, multiplied by something that looks like its derivative.
  2. Rule out expanding the bracket (creates sin²x mess) and integration by parts (no product of two genuinely different factor types — the cos x is a derivative, not an independent factor).
  3. Commit to substitution because the derivative of the inner function (cos x) is sitting right there in the numerator, ready to be absorbed.
  4. Bridge: set u = 3 + sin x, compute du = cos x dx, and rewrite the integral entirely in u.
  5. Tactically check the constant: here du = cos x dx exactly (no factor needed); if it were, say, ½ cos x dx, pull the constant outside before integrating u⁻².

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Let u = 3 + sin x.
  2. Differentiate: du/dx = cos x, so du = cos x dx.
  3. Replace cos x dx by du in the numerator.
  4. Replace (3 + sin x)² by u² in the denominator.
  5. Integral becomes ∫ u⁻² du = −u⁻¹ + C.
  6. Back-substitute: −1/(3 + sin x) + C.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int \dfrac{\cos x}{(3+\sin x)^2}\,dx$. Let $u = 3+\sin x$, so $du = \cos x\,dx$. Then $\int u^{-2}\,du = -u^{-1}+C = -\dfrac{1}{3+\sin x}+C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

∫ 2x(x²+1)⁵ dx
Let u = x²+1; du = 2x dx absorbs the 2x factor exactly, giving ∫ u⁵ du.
∫ eˣ/(1+eˣ) dx
Let u = 1+eˣ; du = eˣ dx, giving ∫ 1/u du = ln|1+eˣ| + C.
∫ sin x · cos⁴x dx
Let u = cos x; du = −sin x dx, so absorb with a sign flip: −∫ u⁴ du.
∫ x/(x²+4)³ dx
Let u = x²+4; du = 2x dx, so x dx = ½ du, pull out the ½ before integrating u⁻³.
∫ (ln x)/x dx
Let u = ln x; du = (1/x) dx absorbs the 1/x factor, giving ∫ u du = ½(ln x)² + C.

Common Misreadings

  • Expanding (3 + sin x)² to get 9 + 6 sin x + sin²x and integrating term-by-term — far harder and unnecessary.
  • Reaching for integration by parts because two factors are visible, missing that one factor is the derivative of the other's inner function.
  • Trying a trig identity on cos x / (3 + sin x)² when no identity simplifies it.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error: writing du = −cos x dx (confusing with d/dx(cos x) = −sin x).
  • Forgetting to back-substitute u = 3 + sin x at the end and leaving the answer in terms of u.
  • Missing a constant factor adjustment when du differs from the numerator by a constant (e.g. needing ½ du).
  • Forgetting + C in an indefinite integral.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL C34/P3 substitution technique; the inner function and its derivative pairing is the textbook cue. All variants listed remain within IAL Pure scope.
