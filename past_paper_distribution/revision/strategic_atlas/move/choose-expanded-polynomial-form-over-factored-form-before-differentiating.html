Differentiation

Choose expanded polynomial form over factored form before differentiating

Family: Implicit Differentiation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A subsequent part of a question asks for f'(x), a tangent/normal gradient, or a stationary point, and the same function is available in two forms: a factored form like 2(x+1)(x-3)^2 and an expanded polynomial like 2x^3-10x^2+6x+18 (often produced in an earlier 'show that' or expansion part). The trigger is recognising that the earlier expansion was placed there deliberately to streamline the differentiation. Also fires whenever any polynomial is presented as a product of two or more factors but can be cheaply expanded.

Motivation — Why this works

Differentiating ax^n term by term via the power rule is purely mechanical, with no rule-interaction. Differentiating a product of two or more factors requires the product rule (often combined with the chain rule), which introduces more places for sign and coefficient errors. When both forms are available, the expanded form gives the same f'(x) faster and with fewer error opportunities, which is why exam papers frequently scaffold an expansion step before asking for derivatives.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that f'(x) is needed for the next step (tangent gradient, stationary point, or rate).
  2. Notice the function is available in two equivalent forms: a factored product and an expanded polynomial from a previous part.
  3. Rule out differentiating the factored form: it would need the product rule (and chain rule on the squared factor), introducing avoidable complexity.
  4. Commit to the expanded form because power-rule differentiation is term-by-term and error-resistant.
  5. Plan: write f(x) as a sum of monomials, differentiate each using d/dx(x^n)=nx^{n-1}, then simplify.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the expanded polynomial form f(x) = 2x^3 - 10x^2 + 6x + 18.
  2. Apply the power rule term by term: d/dx(2x^3)=6x^2, d/dx(-10x^2)=-20x, d/dx(6x)=6, d/dx(18)=0.
  3. Combine: f'(x) = 6x^2 - 20x + 6.
  4. Substitute the required x-value into f'(x) to obtain the gradient at that point.

Worked Micro-Example

Given f(x) = 2(x+1)(x-3)^2 = 2x^3 - 10x^2 + 6x + 18, find the gradient at x = 1. Differentiating the expanded form: f'(x) = 6x^2 - 20x + 6. Then f'(1) = 6 - 20 + 6 = -8, so the tangent at x=1 has gradient -8.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Function given as a product (x+a)(x+b)(x+c) with no expansion supplied
Expand first into a cubic, then differentiate by power rule, rather than invoking a triple product rule.
Function given as (px+q)^n for small integer n
Either expand by binomial theorem and differentiate term by term, or use the chain rule directly — choose whichever is cleaner for the n at hand.
Rational function f(x) = polynomial / x^k
Split into a sum of powers of x (including negative powers) before differentiating, instead of using the quotient rule.
Function given as a sum involving sqrt(x) terms, e.g. x(2 - sqrt(x))
Expand into x - x^{3/2} and apply the power rule to each term rather than using the product rule.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the factored form as 'simpler' because it is shorter, and attempting product rule unnecessarily.
  • Forgetting the chain rule on (x-3)^2 when insisting on the factored form, producing an incorrect derivative.
  • Failing to notice that the earlier 'expand and simplify' part was scaffolding for the differentiation.

Pitfalls

  • Sign or coefficient slips when copying the expanded polynomial from the previous part.
  • Dropping the constant term incorrectly (e.g. writing +18 in f'(x) instead of 0).
  • Arithmetic errors when evaluating f'(x) at the required x-value, especially with fractional inputs like x = 1/3.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1/P2): power-rule differentiation of polynomials and the strategic preference for expanded forms before applying tangent/normal techniques.
