Differentiation

Differentiate f to prepare the Newton-Raphson denominator

Family: Derivative As Tool Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question instructs you to apply the Newton-Raphson formula $x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}$ to refine a root estimate. Cues include phrases like 'use the Newton-Raphson procedure', 'taking $x_0 = \dots$ as a first approximation', or 'find $x_1$ (and $x_2$) to a stated accuracy'. Before any iteration can occur, $f'(x)$ must exist and be computable. The move also fires implicitly whenever a tangent-based root-finding scheme is invoked.

Motivation — Why this works

Newton-Raphson improves a root estimate by linearising $f$ at $(x_n, f(x_n))$: the tangent has gradient $f'(x_n)$, and its $x$-intercept gives the next estimate $x_{n+1}$. The derivative is therefore not optional decoration — it is the denominator of the update rule. Without $f'(x)$, the iteration cannot start. A non-zero $f'(x_n)$ also guarantees the tangent is non-horizontal so the intercept exists.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question demands the Newton-Raphson formula, which has $f'(x_n)$ in its denominator.
  2. I rule out any iteration attempt before $f'(x)$ is in hand — using $f$ alone would be a structural error.
  3. I commit to differentiating $f$ now, once, as a clean preparatory step so each iteration only needs evaluation.
  4. I apply the standard rules (power rule for polynomials; chain/product/exponential rules for transcendentals) to obtain $f'(x)$ in simplified form.
  5. I check $f'(x_0) \neq 0$ to confirm the formula is well-defined before substituting.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify $f(x)$ from the problem statement.
  2. Differentiate term-by-term using the appropriate rules to obtain $f'(x)$.
  3. Simplify $f'(x)$ so substitution is clean.
  4. Evaluate $f'(x_0)$ at the given starting estimate.
  5. Verify $f'(x_0) \neq 0$ before using it as the denominator in the Newton-Raphson update.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $f(x) = x^3 - 3x^2 - 1$ with $x_0 = 3.5$. Differentiate: $f'(x) = 3x^2 - 6x$. Evaluate: $f'(3.5) = 3(12.25) - 6(3.5) = 36.75 - 21 = 15.75 \neq 0$. The Newton-Raphson step is therefore well-defined and ready to apply.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Transcendental $f$, e.g. $f(x) = e^x - 3x$
Use $\frac{d}{dx}(e^x) = e^x$ to get $f'(x) = e^x - 3$; the structural role is identical.
Trigonometric $f$, e.g. $f(x) = \cos x - x$
Differentiate to $f'(x) = -\sin x - 1$; ensure the calculator is in radians when evaluating.
Logarithmic $f$, e.g. $f(x) = \ln x - x + 2$
Use $\frac{d}{dx}(\ln x) = \frac{1}{x}$ to get $f'(x) = \frac{1}{x} - 1$; check the domain $x > 0$.
Product or quotient form, e.g. $f(x) = x e^{-x} - k$
Apply the product rule to get $f'(x) = e^{-x} - x e^{-x} = (1-x)e^{-x}$ before iterating.
$f'(x_n)$ very small (near-zero)
Iteration may overshoot; flag this as a strategic warning even though it rarely blocks the standard A-level computation.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing $f(x_n)$ and $f'(x_n)$ and putting the derivative in the numerator.
  • Treating Newton-Raphson as a fixed-point iteration $x_{n+1} = g(x_n)$ and skipping differentiation entirely.
  • Differentiating only after substituting $x_0$ numerically, producing a constant 'derivative' of $0$.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when differentiating $-3x^2$, writing $+6x$ instead of $-6x$.
  • Dropping the constant term correctly but mis-applying the power rule (e.g. $\frac{d}{dx}(x^3) = 2x^2$).
  • Arithmetic slips evaluating squares such as $(3.5)^2 = 12.25$.
  • For transcendentals, forgetting the chain rule (e.g. $\frac{d}{dx}(e^{2x}) = 2e^{2x}$, not $e^{2x}$).
  • Failing to check $f'(x_0) \neq 0$ before dividing.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL scope: Newton-Raphson appears in Pure/Core numerical methods, and the differentiation rules required (power, chain, product, quotient, $e^x$, $\ln x$, $\sin/\cos$) are all standard A-level content.
