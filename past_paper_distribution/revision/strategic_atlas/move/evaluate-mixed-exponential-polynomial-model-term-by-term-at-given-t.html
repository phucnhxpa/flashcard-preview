Algebra & Functions

Evaluate mixed exponential-polynomial model term-by-term at given t

Family: Substitution Evaluation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a model P(t) is defined by a sum of structurally different terms — typically a constant, exponential terms like 2^{t-a} or 2^{a-t}, and a polynomial like (t-a)^2 — and you must produce numerical values at specific t (often to fill a table for the trapezium rule). Watch for negative or fractional exponents created by substitution, and squared brackets where the inside is negative. Also fires when 'complete the table to 3 d.p.' or similar precision instructions appear.

Motivation — Why this works

Each term type obeys a different rule: exponentials need index laws (a^{-n}=1/a^n, a^{1/2}=√a), while squared brackets need (negative)^2 = positive. Treating the formula as one big chunk invites sign and exponent errors that cascade. Term-by-term evaluation isolates each computation, lets you check each value independently, and preserves the precision needed for the downstream numerical method.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the formula is a sum of heterogeneous terms (constant + exponentials + polynomial), and that substituting t produces a negative exponent in one exponential and a positive one in the other.
  2. Rule out expanding (t-a)^2 algebraically or combining exponentials before substituting — there is no algebraic simplification that reduces work, since t is numerical.
  3. Commit to substituting t directly into each term, evaluating each as a standalone number, then summing at the end.
  4. Bridge: write each term on its own line with the substituted value, converting fractional exponents to surds (2^{1/2}=√2) and negative exponents to reciprocals (2^{-1/2}=1/√2).
  5. Tactical decision: keep at least 4 decimal places in intermediate exponentials so the final answer rounds cleanly to the required precision (usually 3 d.p.).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Substitute the given t into each term separately, leaving the structure visible (e.g. 2^{t-12} → 2^{-2}).
  2. Convert any negative integer exponent to a reciprocal: 2^{-n} = 1/2^n.
  3. Convert any fractional exponent to a surd: 2^{1/2} = √2, 2^{-1/2} = 1/√2.
  4. Evaluate (t-a)^2 by computing (t-a) first, then squaring — the result is non-negative.
  5. Apply the leading sign of each term (especially the minus in front of (t-a)^2) when summing.
  6. Sum all terms, keeping ≥4 d.p. in intermediates, then round only the final answer.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $P = 0.95 + 2^{t-12} + 2^{12-t} - (t-12)^2$, find $P$ at $t=11.5$.\ Exponents become $-0.5$ and $+0.5$: $2^{-0.5} = 1/\sqrt{2} \approx 0.7071$, $2^{0.5} = \sqrt{2} \approx 1.4142$.\ Square term: $(11.5-12)^2 = (-0.5)^2 = 0.25$.\ Sum: $P = 0.95 + 0.7071 + 1.4142 - 0.25 \approx 2.821$ (3 d.p.).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Model uses base e instead of base 2, e.g. P = A + e^{t-a} + e^{a-t} - (t-a)^2
Use e^{-x}=1/e^x and a calculator value for e^{1/2}; the term-by-term structure is identical.
Polynomial term is cubic or higher, e.g. (t-a)^3
Sign of (t-a)^n now depends on parity of n; do not assume the term is non-negative.
Trapezium-rule table requires several t-values
Tabulate each term in a column; reuse symmetry like 2^{t-a}·2^{a-t}=1 to cross-check.
Fractional exponent has denominator other than 2, e.g. 2^{1/3}
Rewrite as a cube root $\sqrt[3]{2}$ or evaluate directly on the calculator; same conversion logic.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating (t-12)^2 as t^2 - 12^2 instead of (t-12)(t-12).
  • Evaluating 2^{-0.5} as -2^{0.5}, or as 2 × (-0.5) = -1.
  • Reading 2^{12-t} as 2^{12} - 2^t (separating the exponent over subtraction).
  • Dropping the leading minus on the (t-a)^2 term after squaring.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting that (t-a)^2 is always non-negative, so the subtracted term always reduces P.
  • Losing the minus sign in front of (t-a)^2 after evaluating the square.
  • Rounding 2^{-0.5} or 2^{0.5} to 2 d.p. too early, causing the final P to drift outside mark-scheme tolerance.
  • Mixing up which exponential has the negative exponent when t < a versus t > a.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only index laws (P1) and direct substitution; the resulting values typically feed the trapezium rule (P2/C2) or a modelling question. Entirely within Edexcel IAL Maths scope.
