Algebra & Functions

Collect and factor dy/dx to isolate it linearly

Family: Linear Isolation Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires immediately after implicit differentiation, when dy/dx appears in two or more separate additive terms (each multiplied by some function of x and/or y) and the question asks for dy/dx (or an equivalent gradient/related-rate quantity). Visual cues: the differentiated equation contains repeated 'dy/dx' tokens scattered across both sides, but never squared or inside another function — i.e. it is linear in dy/dx. Equivalent phrasings: 'solve for dy/dx', 'make dy/dx the subject', 'express dy/dx as a single fraction'. The same cue applies for dx/dy or dθ/dt in related-rates work.

Motivation — Why this works

Implicit differentiation, via the chain rule, contributes exactly one factor of dy/dx per y-term, so the resulting equation is always linear in dy/dx — no (dy/dx)² or trig-of-dy/dx ever appears. This means dy/dx can be treated as a single algebraic unknown in a linear equation A·(dy/dx) = B, solvable in one division. The underlying principle is just 'solve a linear equation in one unknown' applied to dy/dx as the variable. This is the universal algebraic endgame of every implicit-differentiation problem.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that after differentiating, dy/dx appears in several terms — e.g. cos y·dy/dx on one side and 2xy·dy/dx on the other — each multiplied by some coefficient in x and y.
  2. Rule out alternative tactics: there is no need to differentiate again, no need to substitute, and no quadratic-in-dy/dx so the quadratic formula is irrelevant. The equation is linear in dy/dx.
  3. Commit to treating dy/dx as the single unknown of a linear equation: the solution will be (dy/dx) = (stuff with no dy/dx) / (stuff with no dy/dx).
  4. Bridge to action: move every term containing dy/dx to one side of the equation (changing sign as it crosses) and every term without dy/dx to the other side.
  5. Tactical decision: factor dy/dx out as a common factor on its side, leaving a bracket (the coefficient) multiplying it; then divide by that bracket.
  6. Final tidying: simplify the resulting fraction (cancel common numerical factors, factor out −1 if the denominator looks cleaner that way) so the answer is in its simplest form for any subsequent part of the question.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Starting from the differentiated equation, identify every term containing dy/dx.
  2. Rearrange so all dy/dx terms are on the LHS and all other terms are on the RHS, flipping signs of any term that crosses '='.
  3. Factor dy/dx out of the LHS: (coefficient bracket)·dy/dx = RHS.
  4. Divide both sides by the coefficient bracket: dy/dx = RHS / (coefficient bracket).
  5. Simplify the fraction (cancel common factors, factor numerator/denominator if useful).

Worked Micro-Example

From $\sin y + e^{2x} = xy^2$, implicit differentiation gives $\cos y\,\dfrac{dy}{dx} + 2e^{2x} = y^2 + 2xy\,\dfrac{dy}{dx}$. Collect: $\cos y\,\dfrac{dy}{dx} - 2xy\,\dfrac{dy}{dx} = y^2 - 2e^{2x}$. Factor: $(\cos y - 2xy)\dfrac{dy}{dx} = y^2 - 2e^{2x}$. Divide: $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{y^2 - 2e^{2x}}{\cos y - 2xy}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Finding dx/dy instead of dy/dx in an implicit equation
Identical strategy: collect all dx/dy terms, factor, divide. The roles of x and y as 'independent/dependent' are simply swapped.
Related-rates problems (e.g. solving for dθ/dt or dV/dt)
After differentiating with respect to t, the target rate appears linearly across multiple terms. Same collect-factor-divide procedure, with t-derivatives playing the role of dy/dx.
Equation with a product rule term such as d/dx(xy) = y + x·dy/dx
Expand the product rule first, then collect — the x·dy/dx piece joins the other dy/dx terms before factoring.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to divide through by individual coefficients before grouping, which fragments the dy/dx terms and prevents clean isolation.
  • Mistaking a y' inside, say, sin(dy/dx) (which would be non-linear) — but in A-level scope this never occurs after implicit differentiation, so the linearity is guaranteed.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when moving terms across '=' — especially forgetting to negate a term like −6y·dy/dx or −2xy·dy/dx as it crosses.
  • Factoring incorrectly: e.g. writing (cos y + 2xy) instead of (cos y − 2xy) due to a sign slip when collecting.
  • Leaving dy/dx on both sides of the final equation (incomplete isolation).
  • Not simplifying the final fraction — while rarely penalised, an unsimplified form often causes arithmetic slips when substituting coordinates in a follow-up part.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P3/P4 implicit differentiation) and reused in Further Pure related-rates contexts. No formula-booklet entry is required — this is pure algebraic manipulation. Avoid generalising to non-linear-in-dy/dx situations (which do not arise in IAL).
