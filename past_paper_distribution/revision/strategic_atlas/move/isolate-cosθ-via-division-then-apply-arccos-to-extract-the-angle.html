Vectors

Isolate cosθ via division then apply arccos to extract the angle

Family: Inverse Trig Extraction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires once a·b, |a|, and |b| have all been computed and the unknown angle θ is still locked inside a·b = |a||b|cosθ. Cue phrases include 'find the angle between the vectors', 'the acute angle between AB and CD', or 'show that θ = ...'. The move is the final algebraic step that converts numerical data into an angle in degrees or radians.

Motivation — Why this works

Dividing both sides of a·b = |a||b|cosθ by the (non-zero) product |a||b| isolates cosθ as a single ratio in [−1, 1]. The principal arccos branch maps [−1, 1] bijectively to [0°, 180°], which exactly matches the conventional range for the angle between two vectors, so no quadrant analysis is needed. Arcsin or arctan would either lose sign information or fail to cover obtuse angles, making arccos the natural inverse here.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Student notices that all three ingredients a·b, |a|, |b| are now numerical, so θ is the only remaining unknown.
  2. Rules out arcsin/arctan: the dot-product formula delivers cosθ directly, and arccos uniquely covers [0°, 180°] without sign ambiguity.
  3. Commits to dividing through by |a||b|, knowing this is safe because non-zero vectors guarantee |a||b| ≠ 0.
  4. Bridges to computation by writing cosθ = (a·b)/(|a||b|) as one clean fraction, often with surds in the denominator.
  5. Tactically keeps the exact surd form until the final arccos step to avoid rounding error, and checks calculator mode (degrees vs radians) before evaluating.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the formula: cosθ = (a·b)/(|a||b|).
  2. Substitute the computed scalar and magnitudes, e.g. cosθ = −3/(√14 · √6) = −3/√84.
  3. Optionally rationalise/simplify: √84 = 2√21, so cosθ = −3/(2√21).
  4. Apply arccos to the exact value: θ = arccos(−3/√84).
  5. Evaluate in the required mode (degrees or radians) to required accuracy, e.g. θ ≈ 109.1° (1 d.p.).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=-3$, $|\mathbf{a}|=\sqrt{14}$, $|\mathbf{b}|=\sqrt{6}$: $\cos\theta = \dfrac{-3}{\sqrt{14}\,\sqrt{6}} = \dfrac{-3}{\sqrt{84}} = \dfrac{-3}{2\sqrt{21}}.$ Then $\theta = \arccos\!\left(\dfrac{-3}{2\sqrt{21}}\right) \approx 109.1^{\circ}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Acute angle between two lines (rather than vectors)
Use cosθ = |a·b|/(|a||b|); the absolute value forces θ ∈ [0°, 90°].
2D vectors in column form
Same rearrangement, with dot product and magnitudes computed from two components.
Direction vectors of two 3D lines
Extract direction vectors from r = a + λd form, then apply the same arccos step (with absolute value if 'acute angle' is requested).
Answer required in radians
Switch calculator to radian mode; arccos still returns a value in [0, π].
Exact-value angle (e.g. cosθ = 1/2)
Recognise standard value and write θ = π/3 or 60° directly without a calculator.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating a negative cosθ as an error and flipping its sign, instead of correctly reporting an obtuse angle.
  • Taking |a·b| automatically when the question asks for the angle between vectors (not the acute angle between lines).
  • Confusing the formula with sinθ = (a·b)/(|a||b|) and applying arcsin.
  • Reporting θ as the angle whose cosine appears, e.g. quoting −3/√84 as the answer instead of arccos(−3/√84).

Pitfalls

  • Calculator left in radian mode when degrees are required (≈ 1.904 rad vs ≈ 109.1°), or vice versa.
  • Premature rounding of cosθ before applying arccos, distorting the final angle.
  • Multiplying surds incorrectly, e.g. √14·√6 written as √20 or √80 instead of √84.
  • Forgetting to check |a||b| ≠ 0 in a question where one vector could be zero.
  • Stating θ outside [0°, 180°] by adding/subtracting 360° unnecessarily.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Core Pure / FP scope: dot product, magnitudes, and arccos on [−1, 1] → [0°, 180°] are standard, with no need for general inverse-trig branches.
