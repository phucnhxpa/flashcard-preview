Strategic Atlas / Differential Equations / Coefficient Comparison
Differential Equations

Equate coefficients of linearly independent basis functions to solve for PI constants

Family: Coefficient Comparison Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires immediately after substituting a trial particular integral (with undetermined constants) and its derivatives into a linear ODE. The LHS has been expanded into a linear combination of basis functions — typically powers of x (x², x, 1), or trig pairs (cos ωx, sin ωx), or exponentials e^{αx} — and must equal an analogous expression on the RHS for all x. Visual cue: you see an equation like (5a+5b)cos x + (−5a+5b)sin x = cos x, or 8ax² + (−12a+8b)x + (2a−6b+8c) = x² + 2x. Equivalent phrasings: 'matching coefficients', 'comparing like terms', 'identity must hold for all x'.

Motivation — Why this works

Functions like {1, x, x², …}, {cos ωx, sin ωx}, and {e^{αx}} are linearly independent over the reals, so an identity in them holds for all x if and only if the coefficient of each basis function matches on both sides. This converts a single functional equation into a finite system of algebraic equations in the undetermined constants — one equation per basis function. The system is exactly determined when the trial PI was chosen with the right number of unknowns, and is often triangular (for polynomial RHS) or a small simultaneous pair (for trig RHS), both of which are routine to solve.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice that after substituting y_p, y_p', y_p'' into the ODE and simplifying, both sides are expressed in the same basis (powers of x, or cos x and sin x, or e^{αx}).
  2. I rule out trying to solve by picking specific x-values: although that works, it's ad hoc and easy to get wrong; coefficient-matching is systematic and gives exactly the right number of equations.
  3. I commit to coefficient comparison because the basis functions are linearly independent — there is no way for, say, a non-zero cos x term to be cancelled by a sin x term for all x.
  4. I group the LHS carefully by basis function, writing each coefficient as a linear expression in the unknowns (a, b, c, …).
  5. I read off the RHS coefficients — being careful to record a 0 when a basis function is absent on the RHS (e.g. no sin x means coefficient = 0; no constant term means c-equation = 0).
  6. If the system is triangular (polynomial case), I solve top-down starting from the highest power; if it is genuinely simultaneous (trig case), I solve the 2×2 system by elimination or substitution.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Substitute y_p, y_p', y_p'' into the ODE and expand fully.
  2. Collect terms by basis function: e.g. (coeff of cos x)·cos x + (coeff of sin x)·sin x, or (coeff of x²)·x² + (coeff of x)·x + (constant).
  3. Set each LHS coefficient equal to the corresponding RHS coefficient, including 0 where a basis function is absent on the RHS.
  4. Solve the resulting algebraic system: triangular descent for polynomial cases, or simultaneous elimination for trig/exponential pairs.
  5. Substitute the solved constants back into the trial form to write the final particular integral y_p.

Worked Micro-Example

Trial $y_p = a\cos x + b\sin x$ in $y'' + 5y' + 6y = \cos x$. Substituting gives $(5a+5b)\cos x + (-5a+5b)\sin x = \cos x$. Equate: $5a+5b=1$ and $-5a+5b=0$. Solve: $a=b=\tfrac{1}{10}$, so $y_p = \tfrac{1}{10}(\cos x + \sin x)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

RHS is a polynomial in x (e.g. x² + 2x)
Match coefficients of each power x^k separately; system is triangular — solve from highest power down, cascading values.
RHS is a sum of cos ωx and sin ωx
Match cos and sin coefficients separately, giving a 2×2 simultaneous system in a and b.
RHS is e^{αx} (or a polynomial × e^{αx})
Factor out e^{αx} from both sides and match coefficients of the polynomial factor.
Mixed RHS (e.g. cos x + x)
Trial PI is a sum of the two trial forms; match coefficients within each independent family separately.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating cos x and sin x as if they could cancel each other, leading the student to set only one combined equation instead of two.
  • Forgetting that an absent term on the RHS imposes a coefficient = 0 equation rather than no equation at all.
  • Trying to substitute a specific x-value (e.g. x = 0) instead of matching coefficients, which gives only one equation per value and is unreliable.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic/sign errors when collecting terms — especially the −ω² from differentiating cos ωx twice, or chained sign flips from y'' and 5y'.
  • In the polynomial case, errors in early coefficients cascade into all subsequent ones, so verify each step before proceeding.
  • Fraction arithmetic mistakes when solving (e.g. mishandling 7/2 ÷ 8 = 7/16).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2) second-order linear ODEs with constant coefficients. The trial PI forms (polynomial, e^{αx}, cos/sin, products thereof) are standard FP2 content. Avoid generalising to variable-coefficient ODEs or to method of variation of parameters, which are out of scope.
