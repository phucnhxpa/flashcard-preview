Complex Numbers

Enumerate nth roots via (φ + 2kπ)/n argument shifts

Family: Roots Of Unity Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The problem asks for all solutions of z^n = w for some fixed complex number w, or equivalently the nth roots of w. Phrasings include 'find the cube roots of...', 'solve z^4 = ...', or 'find the fifth roots of unity'. The cue is the explicit demand for n distinct complex solutions, not just one. Periodicity of arg means a single equation hides n branches.

Motivation — Why this works

Because cos and sin have period 2π, the polar equation z^n = R(cos φ + i sin φ) is satisfied by any z whose argument θ obeys nθ ≡ φ (mod 2π). Dividing by n produces angles (φ + 2kπ)/n that are genuinely distinct for k = 0, 1, …, n − 1 before they cycle. The modulus condition |z|^n = R forces |z| = R^(1/n) uniquely (positive real). Together this gives exactly n roots, equally spaced by 2π/n on a circle of radius R^(1/n) — a regular n-gon centred at the origin.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Student notices z^n = w is an inverse de Moivre problem and that w must be expressed in polar (mod-arg) form before extracting roots.
  2. They rule out simply writing z = w^(1/n) as a single value: the nth root function is multi-valued on ℂ, so one answer cannot be correct.
  3. They commit to the (φ + 2kπ)/n template because it is the standard, exhaustive enumeration guaranteed to deliver n distinct roots with no repeats.
  4. Bridge: convert w to R(cos φ + i sin φ), take R^(1/n) for the modulus, and prepare to substitute k = 0, 1, …, n − 1.
  5. Tactical choices inside the move: pick k-values so arguments lie in the requested range (often −π < θ ≤ π), and decide whether the final answer needs polar, exponential, or Cartesian form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write w in polar form: w = R(cos φ + i sin φ), with R = |w| and φ = arg(w).
  2. State the general root: z_k = R^(1/n)[cos((φ + 2kπ)/n) + i sin((φ + 2kπ)/n)].
  3. Substitute k = 0, 1, 2, …, n − 1 to list all n distinct roots.
  4. Adjust k where needed so each argument lies in the principal range −π < θ ≤ π (or whatever range the question prescribes).
  5. If required, convert to Cartesian a + bi or mark the roots on an Argand diagram, noting they form a regular n-gon of radius R^(1/n).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve z^3 = 8i. Polar form: 8i = 8(cos(π/2) + i sin(π/2)), so R^(1/3) = 2 and arguments are (π/2 + 2kπ)/3 for k = 0, 1, 2. This gives θ = π/6, 5π/6, −π/2, hence z = 2(cos(π/6) + i sin(π/6)) = √3 + i, z = 2(cos(5π/6) + i sin(5π/6)) = −√3 + i, and z = 2(cos(−π/2) + i sin(−π/2)) = −2i.

Variants — Same move, different surface

nth roots of unity (w = 1, so R = 1, φ = 0)
Formula collapses to z_k = cos(2kπ/n) + i sin(2kπ/n) = e^(2kπi/n); roots lie on the unit circle and include 1.
Roots of a real negative number, e.g. z^n = −a with a > 0
Take φ = π so arguments become (π + 2kπ)/n = (2k+1)π/n; roots come in conjugate pairs symmetric about the real axis.
Equation written in exponential form z^n = Re^(iφ)
Use z_k = R^(1/n) e^{i(φ + 2kπ)/n}; identical structure, just exponential notation.
Question demands roots with arguments in −π < θ ≤ π
Choose k-values (possibly negative) such that each (φ + 2kπ)/n lies in the principal range, rather than mechanically using k = 0, …, n − 1.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating z^n = w as a single-valued equation and reporting only one root.
  • Confusing nth roots of unity with nth roots of a general complex number and dropping the base argument φ.
  • Mistaking 'fifth roots of 32' for 'fifth roots of unity scaled' without re-deriving the modulus 32^(1/5) = 2.

Pitfalls

  • Writing 2kπ/n instead of (φ + 2kπ)/n — omitting the base argument of w.
  • Forgetting to take the nth root of the modulus, leaving R rather than R^(1/n).
  • Listing arguments outside the principal range when the question specifies −π < θ ≤ π.
  • Continuing past k = n − 1, generating duplicates of earlier roots.

A-level Scope Notes

This is the standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure technique for solving z^n = w via de Moivre's theorem; mod-arg and exponential forms are both within scope, and answers are typically required in exact polar, exponential, or Cartesian form.
