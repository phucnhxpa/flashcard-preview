Algebra & Functions

Back-substitute exponential/log relation to express derivative in required variable

Family: Variable Conversion Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a derivative (or other expression) has been obtained in terms of the 'wrong' variable — typically dy/dx in terms of y after implicit or logarithmic differentiation — but the question demands the answer in terms of x (or vice versa). Cues include phrases like 'give your answer in terms of x', 'hence show that dy/dx = …', or a part (a) result of the form 10^y = f(x), e^y = f(x), or y = ln(f(x)) that is begging to be reused. Also fires in parametric questions when a Cartesian form is requested.

Motivation — Why this works

The original function is defined as y(x), so the derivative is properly a function of x. Any earlier-established identity such as 10^y = 2x+1 is an exact algebraic equality, so substituting it eliminates y without approximation or loss of information. Reusing the part (a) relation is intended by the examiner — it produces a clean closed form and avoids re-deriving y explicitly via logs.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice my current dy/dx still contains y (here as 10^y), but the question's independent variable is x.
  2. I rule out converting via y = log₁₀(2x+1) and resubstituting, since this is a longer route to the same place.
  3. I commit to using the identity 10^y = 2x+1 from part (a) — it is exact and immediate.
  4. The bridge is to locate every occurrence of the y-bearing term and replace it with its x-equivalent.
  5. Tactically, I keep the constant ln 10 untouched and simplify the denominator to (2x+1) ln 10.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the y-bearing term in the current expression (here 10^y in dy/dx = 2/(10^y · ln 10)).
  2. Recall or re-derive the exact x–y relation from earlier work (10^y = 2x + 1).
  3. Substitute the relation directly into the expression, replacing 10^y by 2x + 1.
  4. Simplify and present the final answer in the requested variable: dy/dx = 2/((2x + 1) ln 10).

Worked Micro-Example

Given y = log₁₀(2x+1), part (a) gives 10^y = 2x+1. Differentiating implicitly: 10^y · ln 10 · dy/dx = 2, so dy/dx = 2/(10^y · ln 10). Back-substituting 10^y = 2x+1 yields dy/dx = 2/((2x+1) ln 10).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Implicit differentiation giving dy/dx in mixed x,y form
Use the original equation to solve for the y-term and substitute, leaving the answer purely in x (or purely in y) as required.
Logarithmic differentiation of y = f(x)^{g(x)}
After obtaining (1/y) dy/dx = …, multiply by y and immediately replace y with f(x)^{g(x)}.
Parametric differentiation with Cartesian answer requested
After computing dy/dx in terms of t, invert x = f(t) (or use the given relation) to write t in terms of x and substitute.
Exponential form e^y = f(x)
Replace e^y by f(x) directly; do not waste time writing y = ln f(x) unless y itself is needed.

Common Misreadings

  • Leaving the answer in terms of y, not noticing the question specifies 'in terms of x'.
  • Thinking a fresh derivation from y = log₁₀(2x+1) is required, missing that part (a) already supplies the substitution.
  • Confusing 10^y with 10y or with e^y when substituting.

Pitfalls

  • Mis-stating the part (a) relation, e.g. using 10^y = 2x instead of 2x + 1.
  • Substituting into only one of several occurrences of the y-term.
  • Cancelling ln 10 against unrelated factors or dropping it during simplification.
  • Sign errors when the relation involves a subtraction, e.g. e^y = 1 − x.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P3/P4 technique for implicit and logarithmic differentiation; the substitution uses only exact algebraic identities established earlier in the same question.
