Trigonometry

Apply power-reduction identity to linearise sin²θ or cos²θ before integrating

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integrand contains an even power of a single trig function — most commonly sin²(nθ) or cos²(nθ) — sitting inside a definite or indefinite integral. Visual cues: a squared sine/cosine appears after expanding (1 + cosθ)², after squaring r in a polar area formula ½∫r²dθ, after squaring a parametric speed expression, or after a question explicitly asks you to write the integrand in the form p(1 ± cos2θ). The squared trig term has no elementary antiderivative in its raw form, so it must be linearised first. Equivalent phrasings: 'I see cos²θ / sin²θ inside an integral', 'the integrand has an even power of sine or cosine', 'I need to convert a squared trig into a form I can integrate term-by-term'.

Motivation — Why this works

Even powers of sine and cosine are not directly integrable using the basic formula book entries — the power rule for polynomials does NOT apply to trig functions. The double-angle identity cos2θ = 1 − 2sin²θ = 2cos²θ − 1 rearranges to give sin²θ = (1 − cos2θ)/2 and cos²θ = (1 + cos2θ)/2, replacing the squared term with a constant plus a single cosine of double the angle — both of which integrate immediately to θ and ½sin2θ/n respectively. This is the standard, essentially unique A-level technique for integrating even powers of sine or cosine, and it also produces the canonical 'p(1 ± cos2θ)' form that many Edexcel questions require you to write down.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the integrand contains sin²θ or cos²θ (possibly after expanding a bracket like (1 + cosθ)² or squaring r in a polar area integral).
  2. I rule out direct integration: there is no formula-book entry for ∫sin²θ dθ or ∫cos²θ dθ in raw form, and treating it as a power (e.g. cos³θ/3) would be wrong — that's the polynomial power rule, not valid for trig.
  3. I rule out integration by parts: it would loop back on itself and is unnecessary work.
  4. I commit to the power-reduction identity because it converts the squared term into a linear combination of a constant and cos(2θ), each of which integrates in one line.
  5. I choose the correct sign carefully: sin²θ = (1 − cos2θ)/2 (minus sign) versus cos²θ = (1 + cos2θ)/2 (plus sign). I double the argument, not halve it.
  6. If the question specifies a target form like p·π(1 − cos2θ), I keep the identity in that exact shape so I can read off the constant p directly by comparing coefficients.
  7. If a fourth power appears (e.g. cos⁴θ), I plan to apply the identity twice: first to get a cos²(2θ) term, then linearise that as well.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Fully expand r² or any bracketed expression so each term is a single trig power.
  2. Identify each occurrence of sin²(nθ) or cos²(nθ).
  3. Substitute sin²(nθ) = (1 − cos(2nθ))/2 and cos²(nθ) = (1 + cos(2nθ))/2.
  4. Combine constants with any external coefficient (e.g. 16π · ½ = 8π) to simplify.
  5. If the question asks for a specific form p(1 ± cos2θ), compare and read off p.
  6. Integrate term by term: ∫1 dθ = θ, ∫cos(nθ) dθ = sin(nθ)/n, ∫sin(nθ) dθ = −cos(nθ)/n.

Worked Micro-Example

Integrate $\int 16\pi \sin^2\theta\,d\theta$. Apply $\sin^2\theta = \tfrac{1-\cos 2\theta}{2}$: integrand becomes $16\pi \cdot \tfrac{1-\cos 2\theta}{2} = 8\pi(1 - \cos 2\theta)$. Then $\int 8\pi(1-\cos 2\theta)\,d\theta = 8\pi\theta - 4\pi\sin 2\theta + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

For cos²θ instead of sin²θ
Use cos²θ = (1 + cos2θ)/2 — same logic, plus sign instead of minus.
For sin⁴θ or cos⁴θ (higher even power)
Apply the identity twice: write cos⁴θ = ((1 + cos2θ)/2)², expand to get a cos²(2θ) term, then apply the identity again with argument 2θ → 4θ.
For products like sinθ cosθ
Don't use power-reduction; use the product-to-sum identity sin2θ = 2sinθ cosθ to get sinθ cosθ = ½sin2θ.
Polar area ½∫r²dθ where r involves (1 + cosθ) or similar
Expand r² first, then apply power-reduction to the resulting cos²θ term; combine the ½ from the area formula with any ½ from the identity.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating cos²θ as a polynomial and 'integrating' it to cos³θ/3 — confusing the polynomial power rule with trig integration.
  • Mistaking the trigger for an integration-by-parts problem and setting up u, dv unnecessarily.
  • Confusing power-reduction with the Pythagorean identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1, which rearranges but does not linearise.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error: writing sin²θ = (1 + cos2θ)/2 (that's the cos² version) or cos²θ = (1 − cos2θ)/2.
  • Dropping the factor of ½ from the identity, leaving e.g. 16π(1 − cos2θ) instead of 8π(1 − cos2θ).
  • Forgetting to double the argument: writing cos²θ = (1 + cosθ)/2 instead of (1 + cos2θ)/2.
  • When integrating cos(2θ), forgetting the 1/2 factor: ∫cos2θ dθ = ½sin2θ, not sin2θ.
  • When the area formula already has a ½ outside, double-counting or losing track of the combined constant.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 (integration of trig functions) and FP2 (polar area). The identities cos2θ = 1 − 2sin²θ = 2cos²θ − 1 are in the Edexcel formula booklet under 'Trigonometric identities'; the rearranged power-reduction forms are not listed explicitly and must be derived. Avoid invoking complex-exponential derivations (Euler's formula) — out of scope for IAL Maths.
