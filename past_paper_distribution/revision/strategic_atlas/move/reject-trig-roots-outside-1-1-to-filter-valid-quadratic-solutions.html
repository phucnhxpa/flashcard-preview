Trigonometry

Reject trig roots outside [−1, 1] to filter valid quadratic solutions

Family: Domain Filtering Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

After substituting u = sin θ, cos θ (or sin(θ+α), cos(θ+α), etc.) to convert a trig equation into a quadratic, you obtain two algebraic roots — and at least one of them lies outside [−1, 1]. Typical cues: factorisation gives something like (2cos θ − 1)(cos θ + 2) = 0, or the quadratic formula produces a value such as sin θ ≈ −1.93 or cos θ = −2. Any root u with |u| > 1 cannot equal a sine or cosine of a real angle and must be discarded before angle-finding. The same filter applies when the substituted variable is sin(θ+43°), cos(2θ), etc.

Motivation — Why this works

The substitution u = sin θ (or cos θ) temporarily forgets the bound −1 ≤ u ≤ 1 that the trig function imposes; the quadratic, viewed purely algebraically, can return any real number. Re-imposing |u| ≤ 1 is therefore a validity filter built into the codomain of sine/cosine, not an optional step. Discarding out-of-range roots prevents the nonsensical attempt to compute arcsin(−1.93) or arccos(−2), which is undefined over the reals. On Edexcel IAL papers, an explicit rejection statement is typically a mark-bearing step.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that after solving the quadratic in u, two roots emerge — and one of them looks 'too big' in magnitude (e.g. u = −2 or u ≈ −1.93).
  2. Rule out treating both roots equally: I considered just plugging both into arcsin/arccos, but the calculator will error and no real angle exists for |u| > 1.
  3. Commit to the domain-filtering move because u represents sin θ or cos θ, whose range is exactly [−1, 1]; any u outside this interval corresponds to no real θ.
  4. Bridge to action: compare each root to the interval [−1, 1] and write an explicit one-line justification for the rejection (e.g. 'no solutions since −1 ≤ sin θ ≤ 1').
  5. Tactical check: be careful NOT to reject negative roots that lie inside [−1, 1] — values like sin θ = −0.8 or cos θ = −1/2 are perfectly valid; only |u| > 1 fails.
  6. Proceed to solve only with the surviving root(s) when finding angles in the required interval.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Solve the quadratic in u (where u = sin θ, cos θ, sin(θ+α), …) by factorisation or formula to get two roots u₁, u₂.
  2. For each root, test whether −1 ≤ uᵢ ≤ 1.
  3. For any root failing the test, write explicitly: 'uᵢ = … has no solutions since −1 ≤ sin θ ≤ 1' (or cos θ as appropriate).
  4. Retain only the in-range root(s) and proceed to find θ in the specified interval using arcsin/arccos plus symmetry/periodicity.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $2\cos^2\theta + 3\cos\theta - 2 = 0$ for $0° \le \theta < 360°$. Let $c = \cos\theta$: $(2c-1)(c+2)=0$, giving $c = \tfrac12$ or $c = -2$. Reject $c=-2$ since $-1 \le \cos\theta \le 1$. From $\cos\theta = \tfrac12$: $\theta = 60°, 300°$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic in sin(θ+α) or cos(2θ) rather than in sin θ directly
The bound [−1, 1] still applies — the argument of sine/cosine is irrelevant; reject any root with |u| > 1 and then solve the resulting trig equation in the shifted/scaled variable.
Quadratic in sec θ or cosec θ
Range flips to (−∞, −1] ∪ [1, ∞); reject any root strictly between −1 and 1 instead.
Quadratic in tan θ
No range filter — tan θ takes all real values, so every algebraic root is admissible.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating an out-of-range root as merely 'a hard case' and attempting arcsin(−1.93) on the calculator, then panicking at the error.
  • Confusing the range of sin/cos (which is [−1,1]) with the domain of θ specified in the question.

Pitfalls

  • Wrongly rejecting negative roots inside [−1, 1] — e.g. discarding cos θ = −1/2 or sin θ = −0.8, which ARE valid.
  • Silently dropping the invalid root without stating the reason — examiners typically reserve a mark for the explicit rejection statement.
  • Forgetting to filter at all and writing 'θ = arcsin(−1.93)' as a final answer.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 trigonometry. Relies only on the standard ranges sin θ, cos θ ∈ [−1, 1] (no formula booklet entry needed). Do not generalise to complex arcsine/arccosine, which is out of scope.
