Differentiation

Rewrite into sum-of-simple-terms form before differentiating

Family: Pre Differentiation Simplification Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

The expression to be differentiated is NOT already a clean sum of ax^n (or basic ln/exp) terms. Visual cues include: a product of factors like h²(h+4); a fraction with x or √x in the denominator (e.g. 8/x², 24/x, (x²+2x)/√x); a power inside a logarithm such as ln((2x+1)²) or ln(√(3x+2)); a surd like √x or 1/√x. Equivalently: 'the function is dressed up in a form that would force product/quotient/chain rule, but a simple algebraic or log-law identity strips it back to power-rule food.'

Motivation — Why this works

The power rule d/dx(ax^n) = anx^{n−1} and the standard derivatives of ln x, e^x apply DIRECTLY only to individual terms in ax^n (or basic transcendental) form. By using log laws (ln(a^n)=n·ln a, ln(ab)=ln a+ln b), index laws (1/x^n = x^{−n}, √x = x^{1/2}), or simple polynomial expansion, we convert the expression into a sum of such terms. This dodges the product/quotient/chain rule (often out of P1 scope anyway) and replaces them with term-by-term differentiation that is faster, cleaner, and far less error-prone.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I look at the function and ask: is every term already in the form ax^n (or a basic ln/exp)? If yes, differentiate immediately; if no, this move fires.
  2. I identify the obstruction: is it a fraction (1/x^n), a surd (√x), a product of factors, or a power inside a log? Each has a standard rewrite.
  3. I consider the alternative — product rule, quotient rule, or chain rule directly — and rule it out because (a) at P1 the quotient rule isn't even available, and (b) even when available, the rewrite is shorter and reduces algebraic error.
  4. I commit to rewriting first: apply the relevant identity (log law, index law, or expansion) to convert the expression into a sum of ax^n terms.
  5. I tactically choose the simplest rewrite: e.g. ln(A²) → 2 ln A rather than chain rule on (ln A)·(ln A); h²(h+4) → h³+4h² rather than product rule.
  6. After rewriting, I differentiate term by term using the power rule, keeping fractional/negative exponents intact and only converting back to surd/fraction form at the end if the question demands it.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify each 'non-standard' term: fraction c/x^n, surd √x or 1/√x, product (f)(g), or log of a power ln(A^k).
  2. Apply the matching identity: c/x^n = c·x^{−n}; √x = x^{1/2}; 1/√x = x^{−1/2}; (f)(g) → expand; ln(A^k) = k·ln A; ln(AB) = ln A + ln B.
  3. Distribute any overall constant carefully across all rewritten terms (e.g. (1/3)(h³ + 4h²) = (1/3)h³ + (4/3)h²).
  4. Write the function as an explicit sum: y = Σ aᵢ x^{nᵢ} (+ optional ln/exp pieces).
  5. Differentiate term by term with the power rule: d/dx(ax^n) = an·x^{n−1}; d/dx(k ln x) = k/x.
  6. Simplify exponents (remember n − 1 on a fraction: 5/2 − 1 = 3/2, not 2) and present in the form requested (surd, fraction, or x^n).

Worked Micro-Example

Differentiate $y = \dfrac{x^2 + 2x}{\sqrt{x}}$.\n\nRewrite: $y = x^{2}\cdot x^{-1/2} + 2x\cdot x^{-1/2} = x^{3/2} + 2x^{1/2}$.\n\nDifferentiate term-by-term: $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \tfrac{3}{2}x^{1/2} + 2\cdot\tfrac{1}{2}x^{-1/2} = \tfrac{3}{2}\sqrt{x} + \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{x}}.$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Power inside a logarithm, e.g. y = ln((2x+1)²)
Use ln(A^n) = n·ln A: rewrite as y = 2 ln(2x+1), then chain rule once gives dy/dx = 4/(2x+1).
Surd inside a logarithm, e.g. y = ln(√(3x+2))
Rewrite as y = ½·ln(3x+2) before differentiating.
Product of low-degree polynomial factors, e.g. V = (1/3)h²(h+4)
Expand to (1/3)h³ + (4/3)h², then power rule term-by-term — avoids product rule entirely.
Fraction with x^n denominator, e.g. y = (1/3)x³ − 8/x² + 24/x
Rewrite as (1/3)x³ − 8x^{−2} + 24x^{−1}; differentiate using power rule with negative exponents.
Polynomial divided by a surd, e.g. y = (x³ + 2x²)/(3√x)
Split the fraction and use x^a/x^{1/2} = x^{a−1/2}: rewrite as (1/3)x^{5/2} + (2/3)x^{3/2} before applying power rule.
Higher-degree or trig/exp factored products where expansion is unwieldy
Prefer product rule directly — the rewrite move stops paying off when expansion creates more mess than it removes.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading ln((2x+1)²) as (ln(2x+1))² — these are different functions; the log power rule applies only to the former.
  • Reading 8/x² as 8x^{−1/2} (confusing reciprocal of x² with reciprocal-square-root).
  • Skipping part (a) of a structured 'show that … hence differentiate' question and trying to differentiate the original messy form.
  • Treating (x²+2x)/√x as needing the quotient rule rather than splitting and using index laws.

Pitfalls

  • Subtracting 1 from a fractional exponent incorrectly (5/2 − 1 must be 3/2, not 2).
  • Forgetting to distribute an overall constant to every term after expanding (e.g. (1/3)(h³+4h²) ≠ (1/3)h³ + 4h²).
  • Losing a minus sign when rewriting −c/x^n as −c·x^{−n}.
  • Applying ln(A^n) = n·ln A without checking A > 0 (technically ln(A²) = 2 ln|A|; in A-level questions the domain usually justifies dropping the absolute value, but state it if asked).
  • Failing to convert the final answer back to the form requested by the mark scheme (e.g. surd vs x^{1/2}, or a/(2x+1) form).

A-level Scope Notes

Stays fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths P1–P3. Uses only the power rule (P1), index laws (P1), log laws (P2), and at most a single chain-rule step (P3) after the rewrite. The quotient rule is not on P1, so this move is often the INTENDED route for early-paper differentiation of fractions/surds. Avoid invoking the general identity ln(A²)=2ln|A| in formal proofs unless the domain is genuinely ambiguous; the formula booklet entries used are the standard log laws and d/dx(x^n)=nx^{n−1}.
