Algebra & Functions

Combine transformed-root expressions over αβ to decompose into known power sums

Family: Symmetric Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a new quadratic must be formed whose roots are compound rational expressions in α and β (e.g. (α²+1)/β and (β²+1)/α), and direct substitution of individual roots is impractical. Cue phrases include 'find a quadratic whose roots are…' followed by fractions with α or β in the denominator. The common denominator αβ is itself a known symmetric quantity, signalling that regrouping over αβ will expose familiar power sums. Also fires whenever the two new roots are 'mirror images' under α↔β swap.

Motivation — Why this works

Placing the sum over the common denominator αβ converts the numerator into α(α²+1) + β(β²+1) = (α³+β³) + (α+β), which decomposes cleanly into power sums already available from Vieta's relations. The product's numerator (α²+1)(β²+1) expands to (αβ)² + (α²+β²) + 1, again reusing standard symmetric identities. The principle is that any symmetric polynomial in α, β can be written in terms of α+β and αβ, so the goal is always to reduce expressions to these elementary blocks rather than solve for the roots themselves.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the new roots are fractions with α and β in the denominators, so individual evaluation is messy — but the expression is symmetric under α↔β.
  2. Rule out solving the original quadratic for α, β explicitly: even if real, this wastes the symmetric structure; if complex, it is actively unhelpful.
  3. Commit to the symmetric-decomposition route because αβ is known and the numerators, once combined, are themselves symmetric polynomials.
  4. Bridge: write S = [α(α²+1) + β(β²+1)]/(αβ) and P = [(α²+1)(β²+1)]/(αβ), then expand each numerator into recognisable power sums.
  5. Tactically reuse α+β, αβ, α²+β² = (α+β)²−2αβ, and α³+β³ = (α+β)³−3αβ(α+β); substitute numerical values only at the final stage to avoid arithmetic errors.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify α+β and αβ from the original quadratic via Vieta.
  2. Pre-compute the power sums needed: α²+β² = (α+β)²−2αβ and α³+β³ = (α+β)³−3αβ(α+β).
  3. Sum of new roots: S = [(α³+β³) + (α+β)] / (αβ).
  4. Product of new roots: P = [(αβ)² + (α²+β²) + 1] / (αβ).
  5. Substitute the numerical values of α+β, αβ, α²+β², α³+β³ into S and P, simplifying fractions carefully.
  6. Form the required quadratic x² − Sx + P = 0, clearing denominators to obtain integer coefficients if needed.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\alpha+\beta = 5/2$, $\alpha\beta = 7/2$, so $\alpha^2+\beta^2 = 25/4 - 7 = -3/4$ and $\alpha^3+\beta^3 = 125/8 - 3(7/2)(5/2) = 125/8 - 105/4 = -85/8$. Then $S = \dfrac{-85/8 + 5/2}{7/2} = \dfrac{-65/8}{7/2} = -\dfrac{65}{28}$ and $P = \dfrac{(7/2)^2 + (-3/4) + 1}{7/2} = \dfrac{49/4 + 1/4}{7/2} = \dfrac{50/4}{7/2} = \dfrac{25}{7}$. The new quadratic is $x^2 + \tfrac{65}{28}x + \tfrac{25}{7} = 0$, i.e. $28x^2 + 65x + 100 = 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

New roots α + 1/β and β + 1/α
Combine each over its own denominator: sum = (α+β) + (α+β)/(αβ) = (α+β)(1 + 1/(αβ)); product expands to αβ + 1 + 1 + 1/(αβ).
New roots α²+β and β²+α
No common denominator needed; sum = (α²+β²) + (α+β) directly, product expands as (α²+β)(β²+α) = α²β² + α³ + β³ + αβ.
New roots (α+k)/β and (β+k)/α for constant k
Sum over αβ gives [α²+β² + k(α+β)]/(αβ); product gives [(α+k)(β+k)]/(αβ) = [αβ + k(α+β) + k²]/(αβ).
New roots α/β and β/α
Sum = (α²+β²)/(αβ); product = 1 — a degenerate but instructive case of the same regrouping.

Common Misreadings

  • Attempting to solve the original quadratic for α and β explicitly and substituting — algebraically valid but error-prone, especially when the roots are complex or surd-heavy.
  • Treating the new roots' sum as α²/β + 1/β + β²/α + 1/α and trying to simplify term-by-term without first imposing the common denominator αβ.
  • Confusing α²+β² with (α+β)² and forgetting the −2αβ correction.

Pitfalls

  • Writing α²β² rather than (αβ)² — equivalent, but the latter form makes immediate substitution of αβ cleaner.
  • Fraction arithmetic slips, e.g. −85/8 + 5/2 must be converted to −85/8 + 20/8 = −65/8 before dividing.
  • Forgetting to clear the denominator αβ when forming the final quadratic, producing a quadratic with fractional coefficients when integer form is requested.
  • Sign error in α³+β³ = (α+β)³ − 3αβ(α+β) — the −3αβ(α+β) term is often misremembered.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure 1/2 'roots of polynomials' technique: form a new quadratic from symmetric functions of α, β using only Vieta's relations and the identities α²+β² = (α+β)²−2αβ and α³+β³ = (α+β)³−3αβ(α+β).
