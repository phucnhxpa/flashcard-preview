Vectors

Apply sin formula with direction and normal to get line-plane angle directly

Family: Angle Formula Selection Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question explicitly asks for the acute angle between a line and a plane (not between a line and the normal, and not between two planes or two lines). You have, or can extract, the line's direction vector d and the plane's normal vector n (typically from a Cartesian equation ax+by+cz=k giving n=(a,b,c)). Phrasings include 'angle the line makes with the plane', 'acute angle between l and Π', or a diagram showing a line piercing a plane. The cue is: ONE direction vector + ONE normal vector.

Motivation — Why this works

The dot product naturally yields the angle α between two vectors, so d·n = |d||n|cos α gives the angle between the line's direction and the plane's normal — not the plane itself. The angle θ between the line and the plane is the complement: θ = 90° − α, so sin θ = cos α. Using sin θ = |d·n|/(|d||n|) folds the complementary relationship into a single step, eliminating the most common error of reporting α instead of θ. The absolute value enforces the acute angle regardless of how d or n are oriented.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice one direction vector (from the line) and one normal vector (from the plane's Cartesian form) — this is the line-plane configuration, not line-line or plane-plane.
  2. I rule out cos θ = |d·n|/(|d||n|) because that would give the angle between d and n, i.e. the angle to the normal, which is the complement of what is asked.
  3. I rule out computing α first and then doing 90° − α: it works but introduces an extra step where errors creep in.
  4. I commit to sin θ = |d·n|/(|d||n|) because sin θ = cos α encodes the complement directly, giving the line-plane angle in one shot.
  5. I include absolute value bars: the sign of d·n depends on arbitrary choices of direction for d and orientation for n, but the acute angle must be non-negative.
  6. Finally I take arcsin of the result and present the answer in the form requested (degrees to a stated accuracy, or exact form).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify d = direction vector of the line and n = (a,b,c) from the plane ax+by+cz=k.
  2. Compute the scalar product d·n.
  3. Compute the magnitudes |d| = √(d·d) and |n| = √(n·n).
  4. Form sin θ = |d·n| / (|d| |n|), with absolute value to guarantee an acute angle.
  5. Evaluate θ = arcsin(|d·n| / (|d| |n|)) and present to required accuracy.

Worked Micro-Example

Line direction $\mathbf{d}=(4,-2,7)$, plane normal $\mathbf{n}=(2,4,-1)$. Then $\mathbf{d}\cdot\mathbf{n}=8-8-7=-7$, $|\mathbf{d}|=\sqrt{69}$, $|\mathbf{n}|=\sqrt{21}$. So $\sin\theta=\dfrac{|-7|}{\sqrt{69}\cdot\sqrt{21}}=\dfrac{7}{\sqrt{1449}}$, giving $\theta\approx 10.6^\circ$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Angle between two planes (dihedral angle)
Use cos θ = |n₁·n₂|/(|n₁||n₂|) with the two normals — both objects are planes so the angle between normals equals the dihedral angle; no complement needed.
Angle between two lines
Use cos θ = |d₁·d₂|/(|d₁||d₂|) with the two direction vectors — both are directions so cos applies directly.
Plane given in vector form r·n = p
n is already the normal — read it off directly; no need to extract from Cartesian coefficients.
Plane given by three points or two direction vectors in the plane
First compute n = d_a × d_b via the cross product, then apply sin θ = |d·n|/(|d||n|) as usual.

Common Misreadings

  • Using cos θ = |d·n|/(|d||n|) and reporting that angle — this gives the angle between the line and the normal, off by 90° from the required answer.
  • Treating the plane's normal as if it were a direction in the plane, leading to use of the wrong formula.
  • Reading 'angle between line and plane' as 'angle between line and normal' when a diagram shows the normal drawn in.

Pitfalls

  • Omitting the absolute value when d·n < 0, which produces a negative sine and an invalid (or obtuse) angle.
  • Arithmetic slips in |d||n| — e.g. computing √69·√21 as √(69·21) = √1449 incorrectly (note 69×21 = 1449).
  • Forgetting to convert calculator output between radians and degrees as required by the question.
  • Using cos correctly to find α and then forgetting the final 90° − α subtraction (the sin formula sidesteps this entirely).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP3) result: sin θ = |d·n|/(|d||n|) appears in the formula booklet under vectors. Stay within 3D Cartesian/vector geometry; do not generalise to higher dimensions or to angles between curves and surfaces (out of scope).
