Trigonometry

Divide quadratic in sin by sin² to convert to quadratic in cosec

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation is a quadratic in $\sin k\theta$ (e.g. $a\sin^2 k\theta + b\sin k\theta + c = 0$) but the question demands a quadratic in $\operatorname{cosec} k\theta$. The give-away is a 'show that' instruction whose target form has $\operatorname{cosec}^2 k\theta$ where the original has a constant, and a constant where the original has $\sin^2 k\theta$. Equivalent phrasings include 'hence show' or 'deduce' immediately after producing the $\sin$-quadratic. The move is also signalled when $\sin k\theta \neq 0$ is already guaranteed (e.g. $\cot k\theta$ or $\operatorname{cosec} k\theta$ appears elsewhere in the problem).

Motivation — Why this works

Because $\operatorname{cosec} k\theta = 1/\sin k\theta$, dividing every term of a $\sin k\theta$-quadratic by $\sin^2 k\theta$ shifts every power down by 2: $\sin^2 \to 1$, $\sin \to \operatorname{cosec}$, $1 \to \operatorname{cosec}^2$. This is algebraically the reciprocal substitution $u \mapsto 1/u$ executed in place, without renaming the variable. It works precisely because the equation equals zero, so dividing by a nonzero quantity preserves the solution set. It is faster and cleaner than re-deriving the equation from the original trig identity in terms of $\operatorname{cosec}$.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the current equation is a quadratic in $\sin 2\theta$ but the target uses $\operatorname{cosec} 2\theta$, and the coefficients $10, 13, -3$ reappear (reordered/reflected) as $-3, 13, 10$ in the target.
  2. Rules out solving the $\sin$-quadratic first and then taking reciprocals — the question asks to 'show' the $\operatorname{cosec}$ form, not to find solutions yet.
  3. Commits to dividing through by $\sin^2 2\theta$ once they confirm $\sin 2\theta \neq 0$ (already implicit because $\operatorname{cosec} 2\theta$ must exist).
  4. Bridges to the operation by writing each term's reciprocal: $\sin^2 \to 1$, $\sin \to \operatorname{cosec}$, constant $\to \operatorname{cosec}^2$.
  5. Tactically multiplies through by $-1$ at the end to match the leading-positive sign convention of the target.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Check $\sin k\theta \neq 0$ on the relevant interval (usually automatic if $\operatorname{cosec} k\theta$ appears).
  2. Divide every term of $a\sin^2 k\theta + b\sin k\theta + c = 0$ by $\sin^2 k\theta$.
  3. Simplify: $a + b\,\operatorname{cosec} k\theta + c\,\operatorname{cosec}^2 k\theta = 0$.
  4. Multiply by $-1$ or rearrange to match the exact form requested.
  5. State the resulting quadratic in $\operatorname{cosec} k\theta$.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $10\sin^2 2\theta + 13\sin 2\theta - 3 = 0$, divide by $\sin^2 2\theta$ (valid since $\sin 2\theta \neq 0$): $10 + 13\,\operatorname{cosec} 2\theta - 3\,\operatorname{cosec}^2 2\theta = 0$. Multiplying by $-1$ gives $3\,\operatorname{cosec}^2 2\theta - 13\,\operatorname{cosec} 2\theta - 10 = 0$, as required.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic in $\cos x$ where the answer is wanted in $\sec x$
Divide through by $\cos^2 x$; coefficients reverse order around the $\sec x$ term.
Equation $a\sin^2 x + b\sin x\cos x + c\cos^2 x = 0$ to be converted to a quadratic in $\tan x$
Divide through by $\cos^2 x$ instead, using $\sin x/\cos x = \tan x$ and $\sin^2 x/\cos^2 x = \tan^2 x$.
Quadratic in $\tan x$ to be re-expressed in $\cot x$
Divide through by $\tan^2 x$ (assuming $\tan x \neq 0$); coefficients again reverse.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the request as a fresh substitution problem and re-introducing $u = \operatorname{cosec} k\theta$ from the original identity, doubling the work.
  • Believing the move requires a Pythagorean identity like $1 + \cot^2 = \operatorname{cosec}^2$ — it does not; it is purely algebraic division.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the final $\times(-1)$ so the printed form does not match the 'show that' target exactly, costing the mark.
  • Dividing only some terms (e.g. forgetting the constant term becomes $\operatorname{cosec}^2 k\theta$).
  • Failing to justify $\sin k\theta \neq 0$ when the examiner expects a brief reason.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 trigonometry: uses only the reciprocal definition $\operatorname{cosec}\theta = 1/\sin\theta$ and basic equation manipulation. Common in 'show that' parts that bridge a $\sin$-quadratic into a $\operatorname{cosec}$-quadratic before solving.
