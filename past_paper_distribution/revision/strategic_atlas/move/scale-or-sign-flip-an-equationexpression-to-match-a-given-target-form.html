Algebra & Functions

Scale or sign-flip an equation/expression to match a given target form

Family: Show That Simplification Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in any 'show that' or 'hence show' question where you have derived a correct intermediate equation, inequality, or expression but its coefficients or signs do not literally match the printed target. Typical visual cues: (a) all coefficients of your equation share a common numerical factor while the target's leading coefficient is 1 or smaller (e.g. 144p² − 384p − 480 > 0 vs target 3p² − 10p − 8 > 0); (b) every term in your numerator and denominator has the opposite sign to the target (e.g. (−2ye^{−2x} − 3)/(2y − e^{−2x}) vs (2ye^{−2x} + 3)/(e^{−2x} − 2y)); (c) the structure already matches but the surface presentation does not. Equivalent phrasings: 'tidy to match the printed answer', 'reconcile derived form with target form'.

Motivation — Why this works

The move works because multiplying or dividing every term of an equation/inequality by a non-zero constant k preserves its solution set, and multiplying both numerator and denominator of a fraction by the same non-zero k preserves its value (since k/k = 1). When k > 0 the inequality direction is preserved; when k < 0 it flips. In a 'show that' the endpoint is fixed by the question, so this transformation is the standard 'last-mile' technique: it converts a structurally correct but cosmetically mismatched result into the exact printed form, securing the final mark. The underlying principle is the multiplicative identity / invariance of equations under non-zero scaling.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the derived result is structurally the same shape as the target (same powers, same variables in the same positions), but the numerical coefficients or signs differ.
  2. Compare term-by-term ratios: divide a coefficient in your line by the corresponding coefficient in the target. If the same ratio k appears for every term, this is a pure scaling mismatch.
  3. Rule out re-doing the algebra from scratch (a common panic response) and rule out factorising or completing the square — those would change structure, not just scale.
  4. Commit to scaling: decide whether to divide by the GCF (to shrink coefficients), multiply through (to clear fractions), or multiply numerator and denominator by −1 (to flip signs).
  5. Check the sign of the scaling factor: if k > 0 the inequality/equation sign is preserved; if k < 0 an inequality must be flipped. For a fraction, ensure BOTH numerator and denominator are multiplied by the same factor.
  6. Execute the division/multiplication on every term, then verify each transformed term matches the target exactly before writing 'as required'.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the scaling factor k: compute gcd of the coefficients (for an equation), or inspect the sign pattern (for a fraction).
  2. Verify divisibility/consistency: every term must transform to an integer (or the target value) under the same k.
  3. Apply the operation uniformly: divide every term of the equation by k, OR multiply numerator and denominator of the fraction by −1.
  4. If k < 0 and the relation is an inequality, flip the inequality sign.
  5. Write the simplified form and state explicitly that it matches the required result (e.g. 'as required' or 'QED').

Worked Micro-Example

Derived: $144p^2 - 384p - 480 > 0$. Target: $3p^2 - 10p - 8 > 0$. Compute $\gcd(144,384,480) = 48 > 0$. Divide every term by 48: $\frac{144}{48}p^2 - \frac{384}{48}p - \frac{480}{48} > 0$, giving $3p^2 - 10p - 8 > 0$ as required (inequality preserved since $48 > 0$).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Derived equation has integer leading coefficient but target is monic (e.g. 15N² − 975N + 15540 = 0 vs N² − 65N + 1036 = 0).
Divide every term by the leading coefficient; verify each constant term divides cleanly (15540/15 = 1036).
Derived fraction has every sign opposite to target (e.g. implicit differentiation result).
Multiply numerator AND denominator by −1; the fraction's value is unchanged because (−1)/(−1) = 1.
Partial fractions yield −A/(b − x) but mark scheme prints A/(x − b).
Apply the same −1/−1 trick to flip the denominator's order.
'Show that' produces an inequality with negative leading coefficient and target has positive leading coefficient.
Multiply through by −1 AND flip the inequality sign.
Trig 'show that' where target has factored form and derived form has a common trig factor.
Divide by the common trig factor (e.g. cos²θ), but only after confirming it is non-zero on the relevant domain.

Common Misreadings

  • Panicking that 'my answer doesn't match' and restarting the whole derivation, when only a cosmetic scaling/sign step remains.
  • Trying to factorise a large-coefficient quadratic directly instead of first dividing by the obvious GCF.
  • Believing the derived form is wrong because the printed target has a different sign convention.

Pitfalls

  • Dividing by a negative common factor without flipping the inequality sign.
  • Multiplying only the numerator (or only the denominator) by −1, which changes the fraction's value.
  • Arithmetic slip when dividing the constant term (e.g. 15540 ÷ 15) — always verify by multiplying back.
  • Cancelling a factor that could be zero on the relevant domain (especially in trig contexts) without justification.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1–P4) and Further Pure scope; no formula-booklet entry is invoked — this is a manipulation principle. Avoid generalising to dividing by expressions that may equal zero (e.g. dividing by (x − a) without case-splitting), which is out of scope at this level of rigour.
