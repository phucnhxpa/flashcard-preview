Trigonometry

Use equal sides of isosceles triangle to deduce base angles

Family: Isosceles Property Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a triangle in the figure has two sides marked or computed to be equal (e.g. $BC = BD$, or two radii of the same circle, or two equal slant edges of a pyramid). Look for repeated lengths in the question stem, equal radii, or sides shown to be equal by an earlier calculation. Especially useful when only one angle of the triangle is known and the Sine/Cosine Rule cannot yet be applied. Often paired with a supplementary-angle deduction that gives one base angle.

Motivation — Why this works

In any triangle, equal sides force the angles opposite them to be equal (converse of the base-angles-of-isosceles theorem). So spotting one equality of sides instantly hands you an equality of angles, halving the unknowns. Combined with the angle-sum $180^\circ$, two base angles plus the apex are fully determined from a single known angle. This converts an under-determined triangle into a fully solvable one for the Sine/Cosine Rule.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices two sides of the triangle carry the same length label (e.g. $BC = BD = 4.7$).
  2. Rules out jumping straight to the Cosine Rule — only one angle is known, and not the included one, so more angle information is needed first.
  3. Commits to the isosceles route because equal sides ⇒ equal opposite angles is a one-line deduction with no computation cost.
  4. Bridges by identifying which angles are opposite the equal sides: $\angle BDC$ opposite $BC$, $\angle BCD$ opposite $BD$, hence these two are equal.
  5. Tactically uses the known angle (often obtained via supplementary/co-interior reasoning) as one base angle, mirrors it, then subtracts $2\times$ that from $180^\circ$ to get the apex, leaving the triangle fully determined for the Sine Rule.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two equal sides in the triangle (e.g. $BC = BD$).
  2. Mark the angles opposite those sides as equal: $\angle BCD = \angle BDC$.
  3. Substitute the known base angle into both equal-angle slots.
  4. Compute the apex angle as $180^\circ - 2 \times (\text{base angle})$.
  5. Apply the Sine Rule (or Cosine Rule) with the now-complete angle set to find the remaining side.

Worked Micro-Example

In $\triangle BCD$, $BC = BD = 4.7$ and $\angle BCD = 52.5^\circ$. Since $BC = BD$, $\angle BDC = \angle BCD = 52.5^\circ$. Then $\angle CBD = 180^\circ - 2(52.5^\circ) = 75^\circ$. By the Sine Rule, $\dfrac{CD}{\sin 75^\circ} = \dfrac{4.7}{\sin 52.5^\circ}$, giving $CD = \dfrac{4.7\sin 75^\circ}{\sin 52.5^\circ} \approx 5.72$ m.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two radii of the same circle form a triangle with a chord
The radii are automatically equal, so the triangle is isosceles by construction; base angles at the chord's endpoints are equal.
Pyramid with equal slant edges from apex to a base vertex
Each triangular face containing two equal slant edges is isosceles; use it to find apex/base angles in 3D problems.
Equilateral triangle (all three sides equal)
All three angles equal $60^\circ$ immediately — no further deduction needed.
Equal angles given instead of equal sides (converse)
Use the converse: equal base angles ⇒ equal opposite sides, useful for length deductions.

Common Misreadings

  • Failing to notice the equality because the two equal lengths are stated in different sentences or labelled on different parts of the diagram.
  • Treating the triangle as scalene and trying the Cosine Rule with insufficient information.
  • Confusing 'angles opposite equal sides' with 'angles between equal sides'.

Pitfalls

  • Pairing the equal angles with the wrong sides — equal angles are opposite the equal sides, not adjacent to them.
  • Forgetting the supplementary/co-interior step that produced the known base angle, and using a wrong angle value.
  • Arithmetic slip in $180^\circ - 2\theta$ for the apex angle.
  • Applying the Sine Rule with a side and a non-opposite angle paired together.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the base-angles-of-isosceles result and the Sine/Cosine Rule, both standard in Edexcel IAL Pure (P2 trigonometry). No results beyond A-level scope are required.
