Take logarithms of both sides to linearise mismatched-base exponents

Family: Logarithmic Linearisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the unknown x sits in the exponent on both sides of an equation and the bases cannot be rewritten as powers of a common base (e.g. 3^(2x-1) = 4^(x+2)). Also fires when one side is exponential and the other is a constant that is not a clean power of the base (e.g. 2^x = 5). A reliable cue is the phrase 'give your answer in terms of ln' or 'in exact form involving logarithms'.

Motivation — Why this works

The power rule ln(a^b) = b·ln(a) is the only standard tool that pulls a variable out of an exponent. Applying ln (or any log) to both sides preserves equality because ln is one-to-one on positive reals, and the resulting equation is linear in x with constant coefficients ln(a), ln(b). This converts an otherwise intractable exponential equation into routine linear algebra.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice x appears in the exponents on both sides, so direct rearrangement cannot isolate it.
  2. I rule out matching bases: 3 and 4 share no rational common base, so equating exponents is impossible; substitution u = 3^x fails because the other side is in base 4.
  3. I commit to taking ln of both sides because ln is monotonic (preserves the equation) and the power rule will drop both exponents into linear factors.
  4. Concretely I write ln(LHS) = ln(RHS), then immediately apply the power rule, keeping the entire exponent in brackets.
  5. I treat ln(3) and ln(4) as numerical constants and collect x-terms on one side to solve a linear equation; I keep the answer in exact ln-form unless a decimal is requested.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Apply ln to both sides: ln(a^{f(x)}) = ln(b^{g(x)}).
  2. Use the power rule, bracketing exponents: f(x)·ln(a) = g(x)·ln(b).
  3. Expand and collect all x-terms on one side, constants on the other.
  4. Factor x and divide by its coefficient to get x in exact log form.
  5. Optionally simplify using log laws (e.g. ln(4) = 2 ln(2)) or evaluate numerically.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $3^{2x-1} = 4^{x+2}$.\\ Take ln: $(2x-1)\ln 3 = (x+2)\ln 4$.\\ Expand: $2x\ln 3 - \ln 3 = x\ln 4 + 2\ln 4$.\\ Collect: $x(2\ln 3 - \ln 4) = 2\ln 4 + \ln 3$.\\ Hence $x = \dfrac{2\ln 4 + \ln 3}{2\ln 3 - \ln 4}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Single exponential equals a constant, e.g. $2^x = 5$.
Take ln once; only one side needs the power rule, giving $x = \ln 5/\ln 2$.
Base e on one side, e.g. $e^{kx} = A$.
Use ln directly as the inverse of e; no power rule needed: $kx = \ln A$.
Constant on one side and structured exponent on the other, e.g. $5^{3x-1} = 7$.
Take ln; power rule applies only to the LHS, giving $(3x-1)\ln 5 = \ln 7$.
Equation of the form $a \cdot b^x = c \cdot d^x$.
Divide first to get $(b/d)^x = c/a$, then take ln once to solve for x.
Disguised quadratic-in-exponential like $2^{2x} - 5\cdot 2^x + 6 = 0$.
Do NOT take logs first; substitute $u = 2^x$, solve the quadratic, then take ln to recover x.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating mismatched bases as if they could be matched, e.g. forcing 4 = 3^{log_3 4} and trying to equate exponents — technically valid but circular and messy.
  • Splitting exponents into products like $3^{2x-1} = 3^{2x}\cdot 3^{-1}$ and attempting substitution, producing two unknowns linked across different bases.
  • Assuming logs are needed for every exponential equation, including ones where the bases already match (e.g. $2^{x+1} = 8$), where equating exponents is faster.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping brackets when applying the power rule, writing $2x-1\cdot \ln 3$ instead of $(2x-1)\ln 3$ and losing the $-1$ term.
  • Misapplying the rule as $\ln(3^{2x-1}) = (\ln 3)^{2x-1}$, confusing power-of-log with log-of-power.
  • Cancelling $\ln 3$ and $\ln 4$ as if they were equal, or writing $\ln 3/\ln 4 = \ln(3/4)$ — both invalid.
  • Sign errors when collecting x-terms, especially when $\ln 4 > \ln 3$ but the x-coefficient is $2\ln 3 - \ln 4$ (still positive, but easy to flip).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard P2/P3 technique in Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics; uses only the power law of logarithms and linear rearrangement, and answers are typically left in exact ln form.
