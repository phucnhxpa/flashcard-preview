Integration

Apply iterated IBP to reduce powers of ln x against polynomial

Family: Iterated Ibp Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the integrand has the form x^n (ln x)^k with k ≥ 2, typically arising in volumes of revolution where y = x^a ln x gets squared. Cue phrases: 'find the volume generated', 'evaluate ∫x^n(ln x)^2 dx', or a part (b) following a part (a) that handled ∫x^m ln x dx. The squared (or higher) logarithm signals that one IBP is not enough — commit to k rounds.

Motivation — Why this works

Differentiating (ln x)^k gives k(ln x)^{k−1}/x, so choosing u = (ln x)^k in IBP strictly lowers the logarithmic power by one each round while dv = x^n dx integrates cleanly to x^{n+1}/(n+1). After k rounds the logarithm is gone and only a polynomial integral remains. This is the standard reduction template for ∫x^n (ln x)^k dx within A-level scope.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand contains (ln x)^2 multiplied by a polynomial x^n — substitution and standard formulae do not apply directly.
  2. Rule out u = x^n (which would make du polynomial but force ∫(ln x)^2 · polynomial dx, no simpler) and rule out trying to use a previous part's result, since ∫x ln x dx ≠ ∫x^2(ln x)^2 dx.
  3. Commit to IBP with u = (ln x)^2 because differentiating the log lowers its power, the key reduction principle.
  4. First IBP produces a residual ∫x^n ln x dx, which is itself a standard IBP — possibly the very integral computed in part (a).
  5. Plan exactly two rounds, keep dv = x^n dx throughout so v stacks as x^{n+1}/(n+1), and track the multiplicative constants (e.g. the 2/3) that propagate between rounds.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Round 1: set u = (ln x)^2, dv = x^n dx; then du = (2 ln x / x) dx, v = x^{n+1}/(n+1).
  2. Write ∫x^n(ln x)^2 dx = x^{n+1}(ln x)^2/(n+1) − (2/(n+1)) ∫x^n ln x dx.
  3. Round 2 on the residual: u = ln x, dv = x^n dx; then du = (1/x) dx, v = x^{n+1}/(n+1).
  4. Get ∫x^n ln x dx = x^{n+1} ln x/(n+1) − x^{n+1}/(n+1)^2.
  5. Substitute back, distributing the constant from Round 1 carefully through the bracket.
  6. Simplify and, for definite integrals/volumes, evaluate the boundary term uv at both limits before integrating away.

Worked Micro-Example

Compute ∫x^2(ln x)^2 dx. Round 1 (u=(ln x)^2, dv=x^2 dx): = (x^3/3)(ln x)^2 − (2/3)∫x^2 ln x dx. Round 2 (u=ln x, dv=x^2 dx): ∫x^2 ln x dx = (x^3/3) ln x − x^3/9. Combine: ∫x^2(ln x)^2 dx = (x^3/3)(ln x)^2 − (2x^3/9) ln x + 2x^3/27 + C.

Variants — Same move, different surface

∫x^n (ln x)^k dx with general k
Apply IBP exactly k times, always taking u = (ln x)^j (current log power) and dv = x^n dx; constants 2/(n+1), 2/(n+1)^2, … cascade.
Volume of revolution V = π∫y^2 dx with y = x^a ln x
Squaring y produces x^{2a}(ln x)^2; apply the two-round IBP to the integrand, then evaluate between the given x-limits and multiply by π.
∫(ln x)^2 dx (no polynomial factor, n = 0)
Take dv = dx so v = x; same two-round structure produces x(ln x)^2 − 2x ln x + 2x + C.
Integrand x^n (ln x)(something else)
If the 'something else' is also polynomial, expand first; if it is e^{ax}, IBP cycles differently and this template does not apply directly.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to reuse a part (a) answer like ∫x ln x dx as if it equalled ∫x^2(ln x)^2 dx — the integrals are genuinely different.
  • Reading (ln x)^2 as ln(x^2) and using log laws to pull out a factor of 2 — these are not equal.
  • Choosing u = x^n (the polynomial) instead of the logarithm, which fails because (ln x)^2 has no elementary antiderivative against dv.

Pitfalls

  • Losing the propagated coefficient (e.g. the 2/3 from the first round) when substituting the second-round result back.
  • Sign errors when distributing the minus sign from uv − ∫v du across the nested bracket.
  • For definite integrals/volumes, forgetting to evaluate the boundary term uv at both limits at each stage, especially at x = 1 where ln 1 = 0 kills several terms.
  • Algebra slips simplifying x^{n+1}/(n+1) coefficients across two rounds.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P3/P4) integration by parts and applications to volumes of revolution; no reduction-formula notation or beyond-syllabus techniques are required.
