Rewrite decimal coefficients as fractions for exact form

Family: Exact Form Conversion Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question demands an 'exact' answer (length, area, perimeter, coordinate, value of a constant) and the working has produced a coefficient that is a terminating decimal multiplying a surd, $\pi$, $e$, or $\ln$ term — e.g. $6.5\sqrt{6}$, $0.5\pi$, $1.25\ln 2$. Cue phrases include 'give your answer in exact form', 'leave your answer in surd form', or 'in the form $a\sqrt{b}$ where $a$ and $b$ are integers/rationals'. The move also fires when the requested form specifies a fractional structure (e.g. $\frac{p}{q}\sqrt{n}$) that a decimal cannot satisfy.

Motivation — Why this works

At A-level, 'exact' conventionally excludes decimals: mark schemes expect fractions, surds, $\pi$, $e$, or logarithms. A terminating decimal like $6.5$ is technically exact, but it does not match the expected presentation and risks losing the final accuracy/form mark. Converting $6.5$ to $\tfrac{13}{2}$ is a one-step rewrite using $a.b = \tfrac{10a+b}{10}$ then simplifying, and it removes any ambiguity about whether the candidate has rounded.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question wording 'exact' (or a prescribed form $\tfrac{p}{q}\sqrt{n}$) and scan the current answer for any decimal coefficients.
  2. Rule out leaving the decimal as-is: even though $6.5$ terminates, mark schemes for exact-form questions reward fractional presentation.
  3. Commit to converting because the cost is one line of arithmetic and the benefit is matching the expected canonical form.
  4. Bridge: identify the decimal, write it as a fraction over a power of 10, then cancel to lowest terms.
  5. Tactical choice: decide between $\tfrac{13}{2}\sqrt{6}$ and $\tfrac{13\sqrt{6}}{2}$ — both are accepted, pick whichever matches the form requested in the question.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the decimal coefficient in the answer (e.g. $6.5$ in $6.5\sqrt{6}$).
  2. Express it as a fraction over a power of 10: $6.5 = \tfrac{65}{10}$.
  3. Cancel common factors: $\tfrac{65}{10} = \tfrac{13}{2}$.
  4. Reattach the surd/irrational factor: $\tfrac{13}{2}\sqrt{6}$ or equivalently $\tfrac{13\sqrt{6}}{2}$.
  5. Sense-check numerically: $\tfrac{13}{2}\sqrt{6} \approx 6.5 \times 2.449 \approx 15.92$, matching the original.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose a perimeter calculation yields $P = 6.5\sqrt{6}$ and the question asks for the exact perimeter. Write $6.5 = \tfrac{65}{10} = \tfrac{13}{2}$, so $P = \tfrac{13}{2}\sqrt{6} = \tfrac{13\sqrt{6}}{2}$. Quick check: $\tfrac{13\sqrt{6}}{2} \approx 15.92$, agreeing with $6.5 \times \sqrt{6}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Decimal multiple of $\pi$ from a sector/arc problem, e.g. $0.75\pi$
Rewrite $0.75 = \tfrac{3}{4}$ to give $\tfrac{3\pi}{4}$.
Decimal angle in radians, e.g. $\theta = 1.5$ rad used inside $\tfrac{1}{2}r^2\theta$
Convert $1.5 = \tfrac{3}{2}$ before substituting so the area emerges as a clean fraction.
Decimal coefficient on a logarithm, e.g. $1.25\ln 2$
Rewrite as $\tfrac{5}{4}\ln 2$, or use log laws to write $\ln 2^{5/4}$ if a single-log form is requested.
Question prescribes $a + b\sqrt{c}$ with $a,b$ rational, but working gives $2.5 + 1.5\sqrt{3}$
Convert each decimal: $\tfrac{5}{2} + \tfrac{3}{2}\sqrt{3}$, optionally combine over a common denominator: $\tfrac{5+3\sqrt{3}}{2}$.
Coordinates of a stationary point come out as $(0.5, -2.25)$ in an exact-form question
Present as $\left(\tfrac{1}{2}, -\tfrac{9}{4}\right)$.

Common Misreadings

  • Believing $6.5\sqrt{6}$ is not exact because it 'contains a decimal' — it is exact, just not in conventional form.
  • Thinking the conversion changes the value; it is purely a presentational rewrite.
  • Assuming only non-terminating decimals need converting — examiners expect fractions even for terminating decimals in exact-form answers.

Pitfalls

  • Evaluating $\sqrt{6}$ as a decimal and giving $\approx 15.92$, which is not exact and loses the final mark.
  • Writing $13\sqrt{6}/2$ ambiguously so it could be read as $13\sqrt{6/2} = 13\sqrt{3}$ — use clear fraction notation $\tfrac{13\sqrt{6}}{2}$ or $\tfrac{13}{2}\sqrt{6}$.
  • Failing to cancel: leaving $\tfrac{65}{10}\sqrt{6}$ instead of $\tfrac{13}{2}\sqrt{6}$ (lowest-terms is expected).
  • Mismatching the prescribed form: if the question asks for $\tfrac{p}{q}\sqrt{n}$ with $p,q$ integers, leaving $6.5\sqrt{6}$ does not satisfy the stated form.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths conventions for exact-form answers (P1–P4), and applies identically in Further Pure where surd/$\pi$/$\ln$ coefficients arise. No content beyond standard rational-fraction manipulation is required.
