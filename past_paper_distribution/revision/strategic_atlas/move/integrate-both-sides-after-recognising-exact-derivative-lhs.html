Integration

Integrate both sides after recognising exact-derivative LHS

Family: Exact Derivative Integration Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires once a first-order linear ODE has been multiplied by its integrating factor μ(x) and the LHS has been written (or is recognised) as d/dx[μ(x)·y]. The RHS is now a function of x alone, μ(x)·Q(x). Equivalent phrasings include 'the LHS is now an exact derivative', 'collapse the product rule', or simply 'integrate both sides'. Any time you see d/dx[F(x,y)] = g(x), this move is the natural next step.

Motivation — Why this works

By the fundamental theorem of calculus, integrating d/dx[μ(x)·y] with respect to x recovers μ(x)·y up to a constant. This converts the ODE into a single antiderivative problem on the RHS, after which only algebraic isolation of y remains. The integrating factor was constructed precisely to make this collapse possible — without it, the LHS would not be a perfect derivative and this step would be invalid.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the LHS is in the form d/dx[μ(x)·y] = μ(x)·Q(x), i.e. an exact derivative equal to a pure function of x.
  2. Rules out trying to integrate the original LHS term-by-term (μ·dy/dx and μPy separately), since that discards the product-rule structure that was just engineered.
  3. Commits to integrating both sides because the LHS antiderivative is immediate (μ(x)·y) and only the RHS integral requires technique.
  4. Bridges to operation: writes μ(x)·y = ∫ μ(x)Q(x) dx + C, then classifies the RHS integral (direct, substitution, parts, or partial fractions).
  5. Tactically chooses the integration technique (e.g. LIATE for parts), keeps the constant C explicit, and finally divides through by μ(x) to isolate y.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Confirm the LHS equals d/dx[μ(x)·y] by a quick product-rule check.
  2. Integrate both sides w.r.t. x: μ(x)·y = ∫ μ(x)·Q(x) dx + C.
  3. Evaluate the RHS integral using the appropriate technique (direct, substitution, by parts, partial fractions).
  4. Add the constant of integration C explicitly.
  5. Divide every term on the RHS by μ(x) to obtain y = [∫ μ(x)Q(x) dx + C] / μ(x).
  6. If a boundary condition is given, substitute to determine C.

Worked Micro-Example

Given dy/dx + y = x with μ = e^x, the equation becomes d/dx[e^x · y] = x e^x. Integrating both sides: e^x · y = ∫ x e^x dx + C = (x-1)e^x + C (by parts with u=x, dv=e^x dx). Dividing by e^x: y = (x-1) + C e^{-x}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

μ(x)Q(x) is a polynomial × exponential, e.g. x e^{2x}
Use integration by parts (sometimes twice) with LIATE: take u = polynomial, dv = exponential dx.
μ(x)Q(x) simplifies to a pure polynomial or power of x
Integrate term-by-term directly using the power rule; no parts needed.
μ(x)Q(x) involves e^{ax} sin(bx) or e^{ax} cos(bx)
Apply integration by parts twice and solve the resulting cyclic equation for the integral.
μ(x)Q(x) is a rational function of x
Decompose into partial fractions before integrating to obtain logarithms and/or arctan terms.
μ(x)Q(x) matches a standard chain-rule pattern, e.g. f'(x)/f(x) or f'(x)·f(x)^n
Use direct recognition (ln|f(x)| or f(x)^{n+1}/(n+1)) instead of formal substitution.
Definite-integral form with boundary condition at x = a
Integrate from a to x to absorb the constant: μ(x)y - μ(a)y(a) = ∫_a^x μ(t)Q(t) dt.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to integrate the original LHS μ·dy/dx + μPy term-by-term instead of recognising it as one exact derivative.
  • Reading μ(x)·y on the LHS as needing integration too (it does not — it is already the antiderivative).
  • Mistaking the RHS μ(x)Q(x) for something that always integrates by parts when in fact direct integration may suffice.

Pitfalls

  • Omitting the constant of integration C, which makes any boundary-condition step impossible.
  • Sign errors in repeated integration by parts, especially when the integrand cycles (e.g. e^x sin x).
  • Forgetting to divide the constant C by μ(x) when isolating y, leaving a wrong general solution.
  • Dropping absolute-value bars in ln|·| terms when integrating 1/x or f'/f, which can matter for domain considerations.
  • Algebraic slips when simplifying [∫μQ dx + C]/μ(x), particularly when μ(x) is exponential.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope: first-order linear ODEs solved via integrating factor, with RHS integrals drawn from standard A-level techniques (direct, substitution, by parts, partial fractions).
