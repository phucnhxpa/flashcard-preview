Clear a compound fraction by multiplying top and bottom by the inner LCD

Family: Compound Fraction Clearance Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an expression has fractions nested inside a larger fraction — typically after substituting x = 1/a, k/x, or similar reciprocal inputs into a rational function f(x) = (x+b)/(cx+d). Concretely, you see something like (1/a + 5)/(3/a − 2) where both numerator and denominator contain sub-fractions sharing a common inner denominator. Also triggers when simplifying difference quotients, partial fraction setups, or rational equations before cross-multiplying. The cue word is 'fraction inside a fraction'.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying the numerator and denominator of a single fraction by the same non-zero quantity leaves its value unchanged (it is multiplication by 1 in disguise: k/k). Choosing k to be the LCD of the inner fractions kills every sub-denominator in one stroke, converting the compound fraction into a clean linear-over-linear (or polynomial-over-polynomial) form. This is faster and less error-prone than separately combining the numerator's and denominator's sub-fractions and then dividing (flip-and-multiply). The result is immediately ready for cross-multiplication or equation-solving.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the structure: the main fraction has 1/a appearing in both its numerator and denominator — a compound fraction.
  2. Rules out combining each level separately ((1+5a)/a divided by (3−2a)/a) because that requires two common-denominator steps plus a reciprocal flip — more places to slip.
  3. Commits to the single-shot method: multiply top and bottom of the outer fraction by a, the LCD of the inner fractions.
  4. Bridges to operation: writes a·(1/a + 5) over a·(3/a − 2), distributing a into each term so every 1/a cancels.
  5. Tactical check: confirms a ≠ 0 is implicit from the substitution x = 1/a, so the multiplier is legitimate; no sign-flip concerns since this is multiplication within one fraction, not across an (in)equality.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the inner LCD (here, a) common to all sub-fractions.
  2. Write f(1/a) = (1/a + 5)/(3/a − 2).
  3. Multiply the numerator by a, distributing: a·(1/a) + a·5 = 1 + 5a.
  4. Multiply the denominator by a, distributing: a·(3/a) − a·2 = 3 − 2a.
  5. Record the simplified form: f(1/a) = (1 + 5a)/(3 − 2a), now ready to equate or manipulate.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $f(x) = \dfrac{x+5}{3x-2}$, find a simplified form for $f(1/a)$. \\ Substitute: $f(1/a) = \dfrac{1/a + 5}{3/a - 2}$. \\ Multiply top and bottom by $a$: $f(1/a) = \dfrac{a(1/a + 5)}{a(3/a - 2)} = \dfrac{1 + 5a}{3 - 2a}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Substitution f(k/x) for any constant k
Inner LCD is still x; multiplying top and bottom by x clears every k/x term simultaneously.
Difference quotient (f(x+h) − f(x))/h with f rational
After expanding f at x+h and x, multiply numerator and denominator of the compound fraction by the product of the two sub-denominators to clear them in one step.
Compound fractions with surds, e.g. (1 + 1/√2)/(1 − 1/√2)
Multiply top and bottom by √2 to clear; follow with rationalisation if needed.
Partial-fractions resolution where coefficients appear as 1/(a−b)
Multiply through by the common factor to clear denominators before equating coefficients.
Integration of expressions like (e^{-x} + 1)/(e^{-x} − 2)
Multiply numerator and denominator by e^{x} to remove the negative exponentials before integrating.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the compound fraction as if it were two separate fractions to be added, rather than one fraction to simplify.
  • Multiplying only the numerator (or only the denominator) by a, which changes the value of the expression.
  • Confusing this with cross-multiplication across an equation, and prematurely 'moving' the denominator to the other side.
  • Reading the expression as f(1/a) = (1/a + 5) ÷ (3/a) − 2 due to misparsing the main fraction bar.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to distribute a to every term in the numerator/denominator (e.g. writing a·(1/a + 5) = 1 + 5 instead of 1 + 5a).
  • Forgetting the implicit assumption a ≠ 0 (and also 3 − 2a ≠ 0 for the new fraction to be defined).
  • Sign errors when the inner fractions involve subtraction, e.g. mishandling a·(3/a − 2) as 3 + 2a.
  • Over-applying the move to ordinary single-level fractions where there is nothing to clear.

A-level Scope Notes
