Coordinate Geometry

Decompose 'perpendicular bisector' into midpoint plus perpendicular gradient

Family: Problem Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question asks for the equation of the 'perpendicular bisector' of a segment $AB$, or equivalently 'the line equidistant from $A$ and $B$' phrased as a straight line. Also fires for sub-parts that ask for the locus of a circle's centre given two points on its circumference, since that centre lies on such a bisector. Cue words: 'perpendicular bisector', 'mediator', 'equidistant from two fixed points'.

Motivation — Why this works

A straight line in the plane is uniquely determined by one point and one gradient. The compound term 'perpendicular bisector' supplies exactly these two pieces: 'bisector' pins down a point (the midpoint of $AB$), and 'perpendicular' pins down the gradient (negative reciprocal of $m_{AB}$). Splitting the phrase into its two defining constraints turns a geometric description into two routine algebraic computations that can be assembled with $y - y_0 = m(x - x_0)$.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Student notices the phrase 'perpendicular bisector' and parses it as two independent line-defining constraints rather than a single mysterious object.
  2. They rule out the locus approach $|PA|^2 = |PB|^2$ as overkill for a straight line, and rule out guessing intercepts or using only the midpoint without the gradient.
  3. They commit because 'point + gradient' is the minimal data for a line, and both pieces are directly readable from the wording.
  4. Bridge to first operation: compute the midpoint $M$ of $AB$ via the average formula, then compute $m_{AB}$.
  5. Tactical decision: take the negative reciprocal $m_\perp = -1/m_{AB}$, then substitute $M$ and $m_\perp$ into point-gradient form and tidy to the form requested (often $ax+by+c=0$).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Sub-task 1: compute midpoint $M = \left(\tfrac{x_1+x_2}{2}, \tfrac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)$.
  2. Sub-task 2: compute $m_{AB} = \tfrac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}$.
  3. Sub-task 3: take perpendicular gradient $m_\perp = -\tfrac{1}{m_{AB}}$ (handle vertical/horizontal $AB$ separately).
  4. Assembly: write $y - y_M = m_\perp(x - x_M)$ and rearrange to the requested form.

Worked Micro-Example

For $A(2,-1)$ and $B(8,5)$: midpoint $M = (5,2)$; gradient $m_{AB} = \tfrac{5-(-1)}{8-2} = 1$; so $m_\perp = -1$. The perpendicular bisector is $y - 2 = -1(x-5)$, i.e. $y = -x + 7$ or $x + y - 7 = 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Perpendicular bisector of a chord of a circle
Same decomposition, but the resulting line must pass through the centre — use this as a cross-check or to locate an unknown centre.
Locus of points equidistant from $A$ and $B$
Set $|PA|^2 = |PB|^2$, expand and simplify; this bypasses gradients and is useful when $AB$ is vertical/horizontal or when the perpendicular gradient is awkward.
Circumcentre of a triangle
Apply the move to two sides separately and intersect the two perpendicular bisectors.
$AB$ is horizontal ($y_1 = y_2$)
Perpendicular bisector is the vertical line $x = \tfrac{x_1+x_2}{2}$; do not invoke negative reciprocal.
$AB$ is vertical ($x_1 = x_2$)
Perpendicular bisector is the horizontal line $y = \tfrac{y_1+y_2}{2}$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'perpendicular bisector' as a line through the midpoint parallel to $AB$, dropping the perpendicularity condition.
  • Computing the midpoint correctly but using $m_{AB}$ instead of $-1/m_{AB}$ as the gradient.
  • Using one of the endpoints $A$ or $B$ instead of the midpoint as the point on the line.
  • Confusing 'perpendicular bisector of $AB$' with 'perpendicular from a point to $AB$', which is a different construction.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when forming the negative reciprocal, e.g. writing $1/m$ instead of $-1/m$.
  • Arithmetic slip in the midpoint, especially with negative coordinates: $\tfrac{x_1+x_2}{2}$, not $\tfrac{x_2-x_1}{2}$.
  • Failing to handle the vertical/horizontal $AB$ edge cases where the gradient is $0$ or undefined.
  • Leaving the answer in point-gradient form when the question demands $ax+by+c=0$ with integer coefficients.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics P1 (coordinate geometry of straight lines): midpoint formula, gradient, perpendicular gradients via $m_1 m_2 = -1$, and point-gradient form. No vectors or calculus required.
