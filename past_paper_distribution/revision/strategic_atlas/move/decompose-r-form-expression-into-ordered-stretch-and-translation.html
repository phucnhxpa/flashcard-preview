Trigonometry

Decompose R-form expression into ordered stretch and translation

Family: Transformation Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question, having produced an R-form such as y = R\cos(\theta + \alpha) or y = R\sin(\theta - \alpha), asks to describe the geometric transformations mapping y = \cos\theta (or \sin\theta) onto it. Cues include phrases like 'describe two transformations', 'state the sequence of transformations', or specifying that one transformation is applied before the other. Also fires whenever a function is presented as a constant multiplier outside times a trig function with a shifted argument.

Motivation — Why this works

The R-form y = R\cos(\theta + \alpha) differs from y = \cos\theta by exactly two independent geometric operations: a vertical scaling by R (amplitude) and a horizontal shift by -\alpha (phase). Because vertical and horizontal transformations act on perpendicular axes, they commute, but the question's specified order dictates how the intermediate function is described. The sign convention \theta + \alpha \Rightarrow shift of -\alpha along the \theta-axis follows from replacing \theta by \theta + \alpha in f(\theta), which slides the graph leftwards.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the target function is a constant R outside multiplied by a trig of a linearly shifted argument — exactly two ingredients beyond the parent curve.
  2. Rules out reflections, horizontal stretches (no coefficient on \theta), and vertical translations (no constant added outside).
  3. Commits to 'vertical stretch + horizontal translation' as the only transformations needed, noting they commute so the question's prescribed order is purely descriptive.
  4. Bridges to the first operation: apply the outside factor R as a stretch parallel to the y-axis with scale factor R, producing the intermediate y = R\cos\theta.
  5. Reads the inside shift carefully: +\alpha inside means translation by vector \begin{pmatrix}-\alpha\\0\end{pmatrix}, and writes it in vector form for full marks.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify R (the outside multiplier) and \alpha (the inside shift) from the R-form.
  2. Transformation (i): y = \cos\theta \to y = R\cos\theta is a stretch parallel to the y-axis, scale factor R.
  3. Transformation (ii): y = R\cos\theta \to y = R\cos(\theta + \alpha) is a translation by vector \begin{pmatrix}-\alpha\\0\end{pmatrix}.
  4. State each transformation precisely: name (stretch/translation), axis or direction, and exact magnitude (scale factor or vector).

Worked Micro-Example

Map y = \cos\theta to y = \sqrt{41}\cos(\theta + 0.675), stretch first.\n(i) Stretch parallel to the y-axis, scale factor \sqrt{41}, giving y = \sqrt{41}\cos\theta.\n(ii) Translation by \begin{pmatrix}-0.675\\0\end{pmatrix}, giving y = \sqrt{41}\cos(\theta + 0.675).

Variants — Same move, different surface

R-form with frequency, e.g. y = R\cos(n\theta + \alpha)
Insert a horizontal stretch parallel to the \theta-axis with scale factor 1/n; if applied before the translation the shift is -\alpha/n, if after, the shift is -\alpha.
R\sin(\theta - \alpha) form
Translation vector becomes \begin{pmatrix}+\alpha\\0\end{pmatrix} (rightward), parent curve is y = \sin\theta.
Order reversed: translate first, then stretch
Intermediate function is y = \cos(\theta + \alpha); the vertical stretch is unchanged because it acts only on the y-coordinate.
Negative R produced (e.g. -R\cos(\theta + \alpha))
Add a reflection in the \theta-axis, or absorb the sign by adjusting \alpha by \pi to keep R positive.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading +\alpha inside the bracket as a rightward shift of +\alpha instead of a leftward shift of -\alpha.
  • Stating the stretch factor as R^2 (e.g. 41) instead of R (e.g. \sqrt{41}), confusing the constant with the squared amplitude.
  • Treating the outside R as a horizontal stretch rather than a vertical one.
  • Calling the move a 'translation followed by stretch' regardless of what the question prescribes.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to specify the direction of the stretch ('parallel to the y-axis').
  • Writing the translation in words ('left by 0.675') without a column vector when the question demands precise notation.
  • Forgetting to give the intermediate function when the question asks for it explicitly.
  • Mixing up degrees and radians for \alpha when stating the translation vector.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P3): R-form for a\cos\theta \pm b\sin\theta and graph transformations (stretches and translations) of standard trigonometric functions.
