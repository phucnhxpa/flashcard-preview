Trigonometry

Apply cos² power-reduction identity to linearize squared cosine

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

An integrand or expression contains cos²(kθ) (or appears after expanding a bracket like (a − b cos kθ)²). The squared cosine cannot be integrated directly as a standard form. Cues include even powers of sine/cosine, definite integrals over a full period, or expansions of squared trig brackets. Whenever you see cos²(kθ) sitting inside an integral, this identity is the default first move.

Motivation — Why this works

The double-angle identity cos 2α = 2cos²α − 1 rearranges to cos²α = (1 + cos 2α)/2, replacing a non-elementary squared term with a constant plus a single cosine of doubled argument — both elementary to integrate. The cosine term integrates to a bounded oscillation, often vanishing over full periods, which collapses squared contributions to a clean constant. Avoiding this identity forces awkward substitutions or repeated integration by parts for the same result.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice cos²(kθ) sitting in the integrand — a squared trig term that has no direct antiderivative formula.
  2. Rule out u-substitution (du = −k sin kθ dθ has no matching sine factor) and integration by parts (would loop).
  3. Commit to power-reduction because cos² is the canonical trigger for the half-angle identity.
  4. Write cos²(kθ) = (1 + cos 2kθ)/2, being careful to double the argument from kθ to 2kθ.
  5. Distribute any leading coefficient through the ½, then integrate term-by-term as constant + cos(2kθ).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the squared cosine: cos²(kθ).
  2. Apply identity: cos²(kθ) = ½ + ½cos(2kθ).
  3. Multiply through by any external coefficient (e.g. 9cos²6θ = 9/2 + (9/2)cos 12θ).
  4. Substitute back into the full integrand and combine constants.
  5. Integrate: constant term gives linear-in-θ piece; cos(2kθ) integrates to sin(2kθ)/(2k).

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_0^{2\pi} 9\cos^2 6\theta \,d\theta$. Replace $\cos^2 6\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}(1 + \cos 12\theta)$, giving $\int_0^{2\pi} \tfrac{9}{2} + \tfrac{9}{2}\cos 12\theta \,d\theta = \tfrac{9}{2}(2\pi) + \tfrac{9}{2}\left[\tfrac{\sin 12\theta}{12}\right]_0^{2\pi} = 9\pi + 0 = 9\pi$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Squared sine term sin²(kθ)
Use sin²α = (1 − cos 2α)/2 — sign on the cosine flips.
Higher even powers cos⁴(kθ) or cos⁶(kθ)
Apply power-reduction iteratively, or expand via binomial after writing cos²(kθ) in linearized form.
Mixed product sin²(kθ)cos²(kθ)
Linearize each factor separately, then expand and use product-to-sum, or rewrite as ¼sin²(2kθ) and apply power-reduction once.
Polar-area integrals ½∫r² dθ where r contains cos kθ
Squaring r produces cos²(kθ); apply power-reduction before integrating over the angular range.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating cos²(kθ) as cos(k²θ²) or trying chain-rule substitution u = cos kθ.
  • Confusing the identity with cos²α + sin²α = 1 and trying to swap to 1 − sin²(kθ), which doesn't simplify.
  • Believing the identity changes a definite integral's bounds — only the integrand transforms.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to double the argument: cos²6θ becomes cos 12θ, not cos 6θ.
  • Dropping the factor of ½ in front of (1 + cos 2kθ).
  • When integrating cos(2kθ), forgetting the 1/(2k) chain factor.
  • Sign error when applying the sin² version (writing + cos 2α instead of − cos 2α).

A-level Scope Notes

The identity cos²α = (1 + cos 2α)/2 is a direct rearrangement of the double-angle formula in Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P3/P4) and is routinely required for trig integration in C34/P4.
