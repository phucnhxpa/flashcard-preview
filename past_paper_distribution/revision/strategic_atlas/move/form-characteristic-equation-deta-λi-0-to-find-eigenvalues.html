Matrices

Form characteristic equation det(A − λI) = 0 to find eigenvalues

Family: Characteristic Equation Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a square matrix A is given and the question asks for its eigenvalues, or asks for diagonalisation/orthogonal diagonalisation (where eigenvalues are the prerequisite). Also fires when asked to find values of λ for which Ax = λx has non-trivial solutions, or for which (A − λI) is singular. Visual cues: a 2×2 or 3×3 numerical matrix together with the words 'eigenvalues', 'diagonalise', 'characteristic equation', or 'spectrum'.

Motivation — Why this works

Eigenvalues are defined by Ax = λx with x ≠ 0, which rearranges to (A − λI)x = 0. A homogeneous system has non-trivial solutions iff its coefficient matrix is singular, i.e. det(A − λI) = 0. This converts a matrix-vector problem into a polynomial root-finding problem (a quadratic for 2×2, a cubic for 3×3), which is solvable by standard algebraic factorisation. The roots of this characteristic polynomial are precisely the eigenvalues.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question asks for eigenvalues (or sets up diagonalisation), so I recall that eigenvalues are scalars λ with Ax = λx for some non-zero x.
  2. I rule out guessing eigenvectors by inspection — unreliable for a general 3×3 — and rule out reading off the diagonal (only valid for triangular matrices).
  3. I commit to the characteristic equation route: (A − λI)x = 0 has non-trivial solutions iff det(A − λI) = 0, so this determinant equation captures every eigenvalue.
  4. Bridge to action: I subtract λ from each diagonal entry of A to write down A − λI explicitly.
  5. Tactical choice: I expand the determinant along the row or column with the most zeros (or simplest entries) to minimise arithmetic and reduce sign-error risk.
  6. After expansion I collect terms into a polynomial in λ and prepare to factorise — for a cubic I test small integer divisors of the constant term using the factor theorem to find a first root.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Form the matrix A − λI by subtracting λ from every diagonal entry of A (off-diagonal entries unchanged).
  2. Compute det(A − λI) by cofactor expansion along the row/column with most zeros, respecting the alternating +, −, + sign pattern.
  3. Collect terms to obtain the characteristic polynomial p(λ); for a 3×3 this has the form −λ³ + tr(A)λ² − (sum of 2×2 principal minors)λ + det(A).
  4. Set p(λ) = 0.
  5. Find one root by inspection / factor theorem (test λ = ±1, ±2, … dividing the constant term), then factorise the remaining quadratic to obtain all eigenvalues λ₁, λ₂, λ₃.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$. Then $A - \lambda I = \begin{pmatrix} 2-\lambda & 1 \\ 1 & 2-\lambda \end{pmatrix}$, so $\det(A-\lambda I) = (2-\lambda)^2 - 1 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = 0$. Factorising: $(\lambda - 1)(\lambda - 3) = 0$, giving eigenvalues $\lambda = 1, 3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

2×2 matrix
Use the direct formula det(A − λI) = λ² − tr(A)λ + det(A) = 0 — no cofactor expansion needed.
Triangular or diagonal matrix
Eigenvalues are simply the diagonal entries; the determinant of a triangular (A − λI) is the product of (a_ii − λ), so no expansion is needed.
Matrix with a known/given eigenvector
Compute Av directly to read off λ from Av = λv, bypassing the determinant — but for the full spectrum still form det(A − λI) = 0.

Common Misreadings

  • Forming det(A + λI) = 0 instead of det(A − λI) = 0, flipping signs throughout the polynomial.
  • Computing det(A) − λ instead of det(A − λI) — subtracting λ from the determinant rather than from the diagonal entries before taking the determinant.
  • Forgetting to subtract λ from ALL diagonal entries (missing one, especially in a 3×3).
  • Reading eigenvalues off the diagonal of a non-triangular matrix.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors in 3×3 cofactor expansion — the alternating +, −, + pattern is easy to mishandle.
  • Arithmetic slips when collecting λ², λ and constant terms; a single error cascades into wrong eigenvalues.
  • Failing to spot a small integer root (often λ = 0, ±1, ±2) which makes factoring the cubic dramatically easier.
  • Forgetting the leading −λ³ sign when expanding; this can mislead factorisation.

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope for 2×2 and 3×3 real matrices; the characteristic equation is standard FP content. The formula booklet does not give the cubic expansion explicitly — students must expand det(A − λI) themselves. Avoid invoking Cayley–Hamilton or general n×n spectral theory beyond the syllabus.
