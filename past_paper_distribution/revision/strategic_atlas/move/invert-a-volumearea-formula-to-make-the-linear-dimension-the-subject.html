Invert a volume/area formula to make the linear dimension the subject

Family: Formula Inversion Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A geometric formula is stated with volume, area, or surface area as the subject (e.g. $V=\tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^{3}$, $A=\pi r^{2}$, $S=4\pi r^{2}$), but the question supplies a numerical value of that quantity and asks for the underlying linear dimension (radius, side, height). This often appears mid-question, where a previous part has produced $V$ from a model and a later part needs $r$ to substitute into a derivative such as $\frac{dV}{dr}$. Watch for phrasing like 'find the radius when...' or 'hence find $r$' immediately after a value of $V$ has been obtained.

Motivation — Why this works

The formula $V=\tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^{3}$ defines $V$ as a one-to-one increasing function of $r$ for $r>0$, so it can be inverted uniquely. Isolating $r^{3}$ and taking the (real) cube root gives $r=\left(\tfrac{3V}{4\pi}\right)^{1/3}$. This converts the known output back into the input, which is exactly what is needed when a subsequent step (often a related-rates or chain-rule calculation) requires the value of $r$ rather than $V$.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the formula has $V$ as subject but the question is asking for $r$.
  2. Rule out plugging $V$ directly into $\frac{dV}{dr}$ — that derivative is a function of $r$, not $V$.
  3. Commit to algebraically inverting the formula because $V\mapsto r$ is well-defined for $r>0$.
  4. Begin by isolating the power of $r$: divide both sides by the constant $\tfrac{4}{3}\pi$ to get $r^{3}$ alone.
  5. Decide to keep $V$ in exact form (e.g. $\tfrac{900}{11}$) through the cube root and only round at the very end to avoid error propagation into later parts.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Substitute the given $t$ (or other input) into the model to obtain $V$ exactly, e.g. $V=\tfrac{300(3)}{2+9}=\tfrac{900}{11}$.
  2. Starting from $V=\tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^{3}$, multiply both sides by $\tfrac{3}{4\pi}$ to isolate $r^{3}=\tfrac{3V}{4\pi}$.
  3. Take the cube root: $r=\left(\tfrac{3V}{4\pi}\right)^{1/3}$.
  4. Substitute the exact $V$ to get $r=\left(\tfrac{675}{11\pi}\right)^{1/3}\approx 2.68$ cm.
  5. Carry the exact (or high-precision) value of $r$ forward to any subsequent part.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $V=\tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^{3}$ and $V=\tfrac{900}{11}$:\\ $r^{3}=\dfrac{3V}{4\pi}=\dfrac{3\cdot 900/11}{4\pi}=\dfrac{675}{11\pi}$.\\ $r=\left(\dfrac{675}{11\pi}\right)^{1/3}\approx 2.68$ cm.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Circle area $A=\pi r^{2}$ given, find $r$.
Isolate $r^{2}=A/\pi$ and take a square root (positive root only).
Sphere surface area $S=4\pi r^{2}$ given, find $r$.
Divide by $4\pi$ then take the positive square root: $r=\sqrt{S/(4\pi)}$.
Cube volume $V=x^{3}$ given, find side $x$.
Take the cube root directly: $x=V^{1/3}$.
Cylinder volume $V=\pi r^{2}h$ with $h$ fixed, find $r$.
Isolate $r^{2}=V/(\pi h)$ then take the positive square root.
Cone volume $V=\tfrac{1}{3}\pi r^{2}h$ with $h$ a known multiple of $r$.
Substitute the relation between $h$ and $r$ first to get $V$ as a single power of $r$, then invert.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the request for $r$ as a request for $\frac{dV}{dr}$ evaluated at the given $V$.
  • Reading $V=\tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^{3}$ as $V=\tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^{2}$ and taking a square root instead.
  • Assuming $r$ must be an integer and rounding to a 'nice' value before checking.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the cube root and taking a square root (or vice versa for area formulas).
  • Dropping the factor $\tfrac{3}{4\pi}$ when isolating $r^{3}$.
  • Rounding $r$ prematurely so that later substitution into $\frac{dV}{dr}=4\pi r^{2}$ propagates error into $\frac{dr}{dt}$.
  • Including a negative root for a physical length when only the positive root is meaningful.
