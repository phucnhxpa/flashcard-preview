Algebra & Functions

Clear fractional powers by multiplying through by the LCD factor

Family: Equation Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation (often from setting f'(x)=0) contains a mix of fractional powers such as $x^{-1/2}$ and $x^{1/2}$, or $x^{1/2}$ and $x^{3/2}$, that block further simplification. Common cues: a product-rule derivative on $\sqrt{x}\cdot f(x)$, a stated target form free of surds (e.g. $\tan 4x + kx = 0$), or fractional coefficients like $\tfrac12 x^{-1/2}$. The move also fires when negative powers of $x$ obscure a trigonometric or polynomial structure.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying by the lowest-power $x^{p}$ that clears all negative/fractional exponents converts the equation into one with integer (or zero) powers of $x$, exposing the underlying structure. Choosing the multiplier to also absorb numerical denominators (e.g. $2x^{1/2}$ instead of just $x^{1/2}$) avoids leftover fractional coefficients. The operation is reversible provided the multiplier is non-zero on the relevant domain, so no solutions are gained or lost.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation mixes $x^{-1/2}$ and $x^{1/2}$ terms with a fractional coefficient $\tfrac12$.
  2. Rule out factoring out $x^{-1/2}$ alone — that leaves a surd-laden bracket and doesn't match the target integer-power form.
  3. Commit to multiplying through, since the target form has no fractional powers and the domain guarantees $x>0$.
  4. Pick the multiplier $2x^{1/2}$: the $x^{1/2}$ kills the $x^{-1/2}$, and the $2$ kills the $\tfrac12$.
  5. Verify $x\neq 0$ at the stationary point of interest, then distribute term-by-term and confirm only integer powers of $x$ remain.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the most negative fractional power and the fractional coefficients present.
  2. Choose multiplier $c\cdot x^{p}$ where $p$ cancels the negative power and $c$ clears numerical fractions.
  3. Check the multiplier is non-zero on the relevant domain (e.g. $x>0$).
  4. Multiply each term separately, simplifying exponents via $x^{a}\cdot x^{b}=x^{a+b}$.
  5. Collect terms; verify the resulting equation matches the required target form.

Worked Micro-Example

From $g'(x)=\tfrac12 x^{-1/2}\sin 4x + 4x^{1/2}\cos 4x = 0$ on $(0,\pi/4)$, multiply by $2x^{1/2}$: $$2x^{1/2}\cdot\tfrac12 x^{-1/2}\sin 4x + 2x^{1/2}\cdot 4x^{1/2}\cos 4x = 0$$ $$\sin 4x + 8x\cos 4x = 0.$$ Dividing by $\cos 4x$ gives $\tan 4x + 8x = 0$, the required form.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation $3x^{1/2} - 5x^{-1/2} = 2$
Multiply by $x^{1/2}$ to obtain $3x - 5 = 2x^{1/2}$, then square (tracking extraneous roots).
Rational equation $\tfrac{A}{x} + Bx = C$
Multiply by $x$ (with $x\neq 0$) to clear the reciprocal and reach a quadratic.
Derivative of $x^{n}\ln x$ set to zero giving $x^{n-1}(n\ln x + 1)=0$
Divide (not multiply) by $x^{n-1}$ since $x>0$, isolating the bracket.
Mixed-power equation $\tfrac14 x^{-3/2} + x^{1/2} = 0$
Multiply by $4x^{3/2}$ to clear both the $\tfrac14$ and the $x^{-3/2}$, yielding $1 + 4x^{2} = 0$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the move as 'rationalising' and trying to multiply by a conjugate instead.
  • Believing the multiplier must be the simplest power $x^{1/2}$, missing that scaling by $2x^{1/2}$ also removes the $\tfrac12$ coefficient.
  • Thinking division by $\cos 4x$ is the first step — that's the next move, not this one.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to justify $x>0$ (or $x\neq 0$); multiplying by a possibly-zero quantity can introduce or lose roots.
  • Using $x^{1/2}$ alone and leaving a $\tfrac12$ coefficient that must then be cleared separately.
  • Mis-adding exponents: $x^{1/2}\cdot x^{-1/2}=x^{0}=1$, not $x^{1}$.
  • Distributing the multiplier to only one side of the equation.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure technique: combines index laws (P1) with stationary-point analysis (P2/P3) and is regularly required to reach a 'show that' target form in C34/P3 calculus questions.
