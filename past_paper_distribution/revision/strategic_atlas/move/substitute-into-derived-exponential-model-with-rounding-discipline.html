Substitute into derived exponential model with rounding discipline

Family: Model Evaluation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in part (b)/(c) of an exponential or power modelling question after a model of the form $N = ab^t$ or $y = ax^n$ has been established in part (a). The question asks for a prediction at a specific value (often large t), with phrasing like 'use the model to estimate', 'predict the value when t = ...', or 'find N when t = 30'. The cue is a direct substitution task following a fitted model, where exponent size means rounding choices matter.

Motivation — Why this works

Substitution is mechanical, but exponential models amplify any rounding in the base $b$ across many iterations, so $(1.16)^{30}$ and $(1.1604)^{30}$ can disagree by hundreds. The mark scheme typically allows a tolerance band covering both fully-rounded and unrounded inputs, so the student should keep parameters at the precision given in part (a), evaluate, then round only the final answer to the requested accuracy. This balances 'use the model' (use the stated a, b) against numerical fidelity.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that part (a)'s model is now fixed and the task is pure evaluation at a given input.
  2. Rule out re-deriving from the log equation or refitting — the model is given.
  3. Commit to substituting the rounded a and b from part (a) since the question says 'use the model', accepting that the mark scheme tolerance covers minor rounding drift.
  4. Bridge to computation: write $N = a \times b^t$ with the given numbers, evaluate $b^t$ first on the calculator, then multiply.
  5. Tactically: keep full calculator precision through the computation, round only at the final step, and attach correct units and the requested significant figures.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the model with the part (a) values, e.g. $N = 30 \times (1.16)^t$.
  2. Substitute the required input, e.g. $t = 30$: $N = 30 \times (1.16)^{30}$.
  3. Evaluate the exponential factor on the calculator: $(1.16)^{30} \approx 85.85$.
  4. Multiply by the coefficient: $N \approx 30 \times 85.85 \approx 2576$.
  5. Round to the specified accuracy (e.g. 2 s.f.) and attach units: $N \approx 2600 \text{ mm}^2$.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $N = 30 \times (1.16)^t$, predict $N$ at $t = 30$. $N = 30 \times (1.16)^{30} \approx 30 \times 85.85 \approx 2576$. To 2 s.f., $N \approx 2600 \text{ mm}^2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Model in form $N = A e^{kt}$ from a $\ln N$ vs $t$ linearisation
Substitute $t$ into $e^{kt}$ using the unrounded $k$ where possible, then multiply by $A$; same final-rounding discipline.
Power model $y = a x^n$ from $\log y$ vs $\log x$ linearisation
Compute $x^n$ first, then multiply by $a$; large $x$ amplifies error in $n$, so retain decimals of $n$.
Inverse query: solve for $t$ given $N$
Rearrange to $t = \log_b(N/a)$ or $t = \tfrac{1}{k}\ln(N/A)$; rounding discipline still applies to the final $t$.
Comment on validity of the prediction
After evaluating, add a one-line remark on extrapolation risk if $t$ lies outside the data range.

Common Misreadings

  • Plugging $t$ into the original log equation $\log_{10} N = mt + c$ and reporting $\log_{10} N$ instead of $N$.
  • Treating 'use the model' as licence to refit or to use raw data rather than the established $a, b$.
  • Confusing the requested accuracy (s.f. vs d.p.) with the precision of the inputs.

Pitfalls

  • Premature rounding of $b$ (e.g. $1.16 \to 1.2$) causing huge error when raised to a large power.
  • Rounding intermediate values like $b^t$ to too few s.f. before multiplying by $a$.
  • Omitting units or giving the wrong number of significant figures.
  • Calculator misuse: typing $30 \times 1.16 \wedge 30$ without brackets where needed, or evaluating $(30 \times 1.16)^{30}$ by mistake.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure exponential/log modelling task (P3/P4 style); requires only calculator evaluation of $b^t$ or $e^{kt}$ and basic rounding conventions.
