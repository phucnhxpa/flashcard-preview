Complex Numbers

Substitute inverse map into modulus locus and apply quotient rule

Family: Modulus Algebra Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a transformation w = f(z) has been inverted to z = g(w) and this must be substituted into a given modulus locus in z (e.g. |z+a| = k or |z-a| = |z-b|) to find the image locus in w. Typical cue: the inverted z is a Möbius/rational expression in w, and substitution produces |rational(w)| = constant. Also fires whenever a modulus equation contains a sum like |fraction + constant|, signalling that combining over a common denominator then applying |a/b| = |a|/|b| will expose a clean locus.

Motivation — Why this works

The modulus quotient identity |a/b| = |a|/|b| (valid for b ≠ 0) lets us split a modulus of a rational expression into numerator and denominator moduli, each of which is a simple linear modulus describing a distance. Combining additive terms over a common denominator first is essential because |x + y| ≠ |x| + |y| in general. Once split, the equation typically reduces to |linear in w| = k·|linear in w|, which is a standard circle or perpendicular bisector locus.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the substituted equation has the form |g(w) + c| = k where g(w) is a fraction, so I cannot apply modulus rules termwise.
  2. I rule out expanding into real and imaginary parts with w = u + iv — that is heavier and obscures the geometric locus.
  3. I commit to combining the additive terms over the common denominator first, because then the whole expression is a single quotient and |a/b| = |a|/|b| applies cleanly.
  4. Bridge: rewrite the constant as (denominator)/(denominator) so it merges with the fraction, then simplify the numerator algebraically.
  5. Tactical: pull out any constant factor from the numerator using |λa| = |λ||a|, then cancel matching constants on both sides to read off a recognisable locus (circle, line, or perpendicular bisector).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Substitute z = g(w) into the modulus equation, e.g. |z+1| = 2 becomes |(w+1)/(w-1) + 1| = 2.
  2. Write the constant term over the common denominator: |(w+1)/(w-1) + (w-1)/(w-1)|.
  3. Combine and simplify the numerator: |(2w)/(w-1)| = 2.
  4. Apply |a/b| = |a|/|b|: 2|w|/|w-1| = 2.
  5. Cancel constants and identify the locus: |w| = |w-1|, the perpendicular bisector of 0 and 1, i.e. Re(w) = 1/2.

Worked Micro-Example

Given |z+1| = 2 and z = (w+1)/(w-1), substitute: $\left|\tfrac{w+1}{w-1} + 1\right| = 2$. Combine: $\left|\tfrac{2w}{w-1}\right| = 2$. Split: $\tfrac{2|w|}{|w-1|} = 2$, giving $|w| = |w-1|$. This is the perpendicular bisector of $0$ and $1$, i.e. $\operatorname{Re}(w) = \tfrac{1}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Original locus is a circle |z - a| = r with z = (αw+β)/(γw+δ).
Combine -a over the denominator γw+δ; after applying the quotient rule, you typically get |linear in w| = r|γw+δ|, a circle or line in w (square both sides if needed).
Original locus is a perpendicular bisector |z - a| = |z - b|.
Substitute and combine each side separately over the common denominator; the denominators cancel, leaving |linear| = |linear| directly in w.
Transformation is w = 1/z, so z = 1/w.
Substitute z = 1/w; |1/w + c| becomes |(1+cw)/w|, and the quotient rule gives |1+cw|/|w| = k, often yielding a circle in w.
Locus given as arg(z - a) = θ rather than modulus.
This move does NOT apply directly; instead use arg(a/b) = arg(a) - arg(b) after combining over a common denominator.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating |g(w) + 1| as |g(w)| + 1 and splitting the modulus over a sum.
  • Applying |a/b| = |a|/|b| before combining the additive constant into the fraction, leaving a stray term.
  • Confusing this with the arg-locus case and trying to take arguments instead of moduli.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to write the constant 1 as (w-1)/(w-1) before adding, producing an algebraic error in the numerator.
  • Dropping the factor 2 when simplifying |2w| = 2|w|.
  • Forgetting the restriction w ≠ 1 (or wherever the denominator vanishes) when stating the final locus.
  • Cancelling |w-1| from both sides without first ensuring it is non-zero, or cancelling when it does not actually appear on both sides.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope: Möbius-type transformations of standard loci (lines, circles, perpendicular bisectors) using |a/b| = |a|/|b| and |λa| = |λ||a|. No conformal-mapping theory beyond direct algebraic substitution is required.
