Decompose compound logarithm using product and power laws

Family: Logarithm Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a logarithm has an argument that is a product of a numerical constant (often a power of the base) and a power of the variable, e.g. log₁₀(100x³), ln(e⁵x⁴), or log₂(8x⁵). Especially relevant when an earlier part of the question evaluated or estimated ∫log_b(x) dx (or a similar log expression) and the current part asks you to reuse that result. Also triggers whenever you need to linearise a log expression before integrating, differentiating, or solving an equation.

Motivation — Why this works

The logarithm laws log(ab) = log a + log b and log(aⁿ) = n log a are exact identities valid for positive arguments, and they convert a single 'compound' logarithm into a linear combination of a constant and a simple log term. This linearisation makes the expression compatible with linearity of integration/differentiation, and lets you import previously computed results (like ∫log₁₀x dx) directly. Without decomposition, the compound log has no standard antiderivative form to invoke.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the log argument is a product of a constant and a power of x — exactly the shape the product/power laws were designed for.
  2. I rule out substitution and change-of-base: they would obscure the link to the earlier-part integral I'm meant to reuse.
  3. I commit to log laws because the question structure (refer back to part (a)) demands an expression of the form constant + k·log₁₀x.
  4. First operation: split log₁₀(100x³) = log₁₀100 + log₁₀(x³) using log(ab) = log a + log b.
  5. Then evaluate log₁₀100 = 2 exactly, and apply log(xⁿ) = n log x to get 3log₁₀x, yielding 2 + 3log₁₀x.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the argument as a product: constant × power of variable.
  2. Apply product rule: log_b(AB) = log_b A + log_b B.
  3. Evaluate any log of a power of the base exactly (e.g. log₁₀100 = 2, ln e⁵ = 5).
  4. Apply power rule: log_b(xⁿ) = n log_b x.
  5. Write result as a linear combination: constant + (coefficient)·log_b x.

Worked Micro-Example

Rewrite $\log_{10}(100x^3)$ for use in $\int \log_{10}(100x^3)\,dx$.\n$\log_{10}(100x^3) = \log_{10}100 + \log_{10}(x^3) = 2 + 3\log_{10}x.$\nHence $\int_1^{10}\log_{10}(100x^3)\,dx = \int_1^{10} 2\,dx + 3\int_1^{10}\log_{10}x\,dx = 18 + 3I$, where $I$ is the integral from part (a).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Natural log with exponential constant, e.g. ln(e⁵x⁴)
Use ln(ab) = ln a + ln b and ln(eⁿ) = n exactly, giving 5 + 4ln x.
Base-2 log with power-of-2 constant, e.g. log₂(8x⁵)
log₂8 = 3 exactly, giving 3 + 5log₂x.
Quotient form, e.g. log₁₀(x²/1000)
Use log(a/b) = log a − log b first: 2log₁₀x − 3.
Differentiation reuse, e.g. d/dx[ln(5x²)]
Decompose to ln5 + 2ln x; constant differentiates to 0, leaving 2/x.
Solving log equations, e.g. log₁₀(100x³) = 5
Decompose to 2 + 3log₁₀x = 5, then log₁₀x = 1, x = 10.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 100x³ as (100x)³ = 10⁶x³, leading to 6 + 3log₁₀x instead of 2 + 3log₁₀x.
  • Treating log₁₀(100x³) as log₁₀100 · log₁₀(x³) (product instead of sum).
  • Pulling the exponent 3 outside the entire log to get 3log₁₀(100x), confusing log(xⁿ) with (log x)ⁿ-style manipulation.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting that log₁₀100 = 2 exactly, or leaving it unsimplified as log₁₀10² = 2log₁₀10.
  • Applying the power rule before the product rule and getting stuck with log₁₀(100x³) = 3log₁₀(100x^?).
  • Using change-of-base unnecessarily, which introduces ln10 factors and breaks the link to the earlier part's result.
  • Dropping the constant term when integrating, e.g. writing ∫(2 + 3log₁₀x)dx as 3∫log₁₀x dx and forgetting the 2x contribution.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the standard log laws (product, quotient, power) from Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics, and is commonly paired with C34/P3-P4 integration of log functions or log equation solving.
