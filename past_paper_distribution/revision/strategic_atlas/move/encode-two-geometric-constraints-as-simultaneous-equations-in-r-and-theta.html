Trigger — When to use

Fires when a problem describes a single geometric object (typically a circular sector, cone, cylinder, or prism) using two independent numerical constraints — e.g. 'area = 6 and perimeter = 10' — and asks for a single-variable equation or value. Look for cues like 'area of the sector', 'perimeter', 'arc length', 'volume', 'surface area' paired with two given numbers and two unknown dimensions. Also fires when the final goal is a quadratic or equation in just one of the unknowns, signalling that elimination must follow.

Motivation — Why this works

Two numerical constraints on two unknowns form a 2×2 system that determines (or reduces) the geometry. Writing both standard formulae explicitly is the prerequisite for any elimination or substitution. Until both relationships are committed to paper, there is no algebraic bridge from the geometric description to the single-variable equation the question demands.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice two pieces of numerical data are attached to one shape, but the shape is parameterised by two unknowns (r and θ) — so I'm in a 2-equation/2-unknown setting.
  2. I rule out trying to guess r or θ directly, and rule out one-equation shortcuts: with two unknowns I genuinely need two relationships.
  3. I commit to the standard sector formulae (½r²θ for area, rθ + 2r for perimeter) because these are the canonical encodings and the question is phrased in radians.
  4. Bridge: I write each constraint as an equation, clear fractions where convenient (e.g. multiply the area equation by 2), and label them Equation 1 and Equation 2 ready for elimination.
  5. Tactically I pick the form that isolates the cleanest quantity to substitute later — usually keeping r²θ or rθ as a single block so it can be substituted into the other equation.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two unknowns parameterising the shape (here r and θ in radians).
  2. Recall the two standard formulae that match the given quantities (area of sector = ½r²θ; perimeter of sector = rθ + 2r).
  3. Substitute the given numerical values to obtain Equation 1: 6 = ½r²θ ⇒ r²θ = 12.
  4. Obtain Equation 2: 10 = rθ + 2r.
  5. Label the equations and check both involve only r and θ, confirming the system is closed and ready for elimination.

Worked Micro-Example

Sector has area 6 and perimeter 10. Area: $\tfrac{1}{2}r^2\theta = 6 \Rightarrow r^2\theta = 12$ (1). Perimeter: $r\theta + 2r = 10$ (2). From (2), $r\theta = 10 - 2r$, so multiplying by $r$: $r^2\theta = r(10-2r)$. Combined with (1): $r(10-2r) = 12$, giving $2r^2 - 10r + 12 = 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cone with given volume and curved surface area
Use $V = \tfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$ and $S = \pi r l$ with $l = \sqrt{r^2+h^2}$; eliminate $h$ or $l$ to reduce to one variable.
Closed cylinder with fixed volume and surface area
Use $V = \pi r^2 h$ and $S = 2\pi r^2 + 2\pi r h$; substitute $h$ from the volume equation into the surface-area equation.
Open box (cuboid) with given volume and surface area
Two equations in base length $x$ and height $h$; eliminate $h$ to get a single equation in $x$.
Sector with given arc length and area
Use $s = r\theta$ and $A = \tfrac{1}{2}r^2\theta$; the block $r\theta$ substitutes directly.
Triangle with given perimeter and area (isoceles or right-angled)
Encode perimeter and $\tfrac{1}{2}\times\text{base}\times\text{height}$ (or Pythagoras) as two equations in the two side-unknowns.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the perimeter as just the arc length $r\theta$, omitting the two straight radii.
  • Reading 'area' as the area of the full circle $\pi r^2$ rather than the sector $\tfrac{1}{2}r^2\theta$.
  • Assuming θ is in degrees and inserting a $\pi/180$ factor when the question is in radians.
  • Believing one equation is enough because only θ is asked for, and so failing to write the second constraint.

Pitfalls

  • Using degree-mode formulae (e.g. $\pi r^2 \theta/360$) when θ is in radians.
  • Algebraic slip when clearing the $\tfrac{1}{2}$: forgetting to double the right-hand side.
  • Mislabelling equations and substituting back into the equation you derived from, producing $0=0$.
  • Carrying through the perimeter as $r\theta + r$ (only one radius) or $2r\theta + 2r$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (sector area/arc length in radians, P2) and standard mensuration; the elimination that follows produces a quadratic solvable by C1/C2 techniques.
