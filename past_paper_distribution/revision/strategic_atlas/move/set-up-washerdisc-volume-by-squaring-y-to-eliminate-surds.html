Strategic Atlas / Integration / Volume Of Revolution Setup
Integration

Set up washer/disc volume by squaring y to eliminate surds

Family: Volume Of Revolution Setup Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question asks for the volume generated when a region is rotated 360° (or 2π radians) about the x-axis, and the bounding curve y = f(x) contains a square root such as √x or (x−a)^{1/2}, possibly together with a constant horizontal line y = k forming the upper or lower boundary. Visually: an enclosed region between a curve and a horizontal line, with explicit functions of x available. Equivalently phrased: 'find the volume of the solid of revolution', 'rotate R about the x-axis through 2π', or 'the region bounded by y = f(x), y = k, x = a, x = b is rotated about the x-axis'.

Motivation — Why this works

The volume-of-revolution formula V = π∫ y² dx (single curve) or V = π∫(y_upper² − y_lower²) dx (washer, region between two curves) requires squaring the bounding function(s). Examiners deliberately place a square root in y so that squaring kills the surd and produces a polynomial or rational integrand that yields to standard A-level techniques (direct integration or reverse chain rule). Recognising this design feature gives confidence the integral is tractable and tells you which method (disc/washer in x) is intended over the shell method, since both boundaries are already given as functions of x.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the rotation axis is the x-axis and the boundaries are given as y in terms of x — so cross-sections perpendicular to the x-axis are discs or washers, and integration should be with respect to x.
  2. Rule out the shell method: it would require inverting y = f(x) to get x = g(y), an unnecessary algebraic step when f(x) is already explicit.
  3. Rule out splitting into two separate integrals (πk²·length minus π∫y² dx) — the washer formula combines them in one pass and is the standard A-level presentation.
  4. Commit to V = π∫(y_upper² − y_lower²) dx (or V = π∫y² dx if only one curve bounds the region against the axis), and identify which boundary is upper over the interval to avoid sign errors.
  5. Square the curve and observe the surd disappears: e.g. ((9/2)√(x−3))² = (81/4)(x−3), or (12√x/(2x²+3))² = 144x/(2x²+3)². Confirm the resulting integrand is either a polynomial (direct integration) or has numerator proportional to the derivative of an inner function (reverse chain rule / substitution).
  6. Read off limits from the geometry: x-values where the curve meets the horizontal line, or the explicit vertical bounds x = a, x = b given in the question.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the appropriate formula: V = π∫_{a}^{b} y² dx for a single curve against the x-axis, or V = π∫_{a}^{b}(y_upper² − y_lower²) dx for a washer.
  2. Square each bounding function carefully — square BOTH numerator and denominator if y is a quotient, and square the constant for a horizontal boundary (e.g. 9² = 81).
  3. Simplify: surds of the form (√(linear))² collapse to the linear expression; (k√x)² = k²x.
  4. Substitute squared expressions into the integral and identify the integration technique (polynomial, reverse chain rule, or u-substitution) suggested by the simplified integrand.
  5. Determine limits from intersection points or stated bounds, and keep the π outside the integral throughout.

Worked Micro-Example

Region R bounded by $y = \tfrac{9}{2}\sqrt{x-3}$, $y = 9$, and $x = 6$ is rotated about the x-axis. Washer setup: $V = \pi\int_{3}^{6}\left(9^2 - \left(\tfrac{9}{2}\sqrt{x-3}\right)^2\right)dx = \pi\int_{3}^{6}\left(81 - \tfrac{81}{4}(x-3)\right)dx$. The surd is gone; the integrand is linear in $x$ and integrates directly.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Single curve y = f(x) rotated about the x-axis with no second boundary curve
Use V = π∫y² dx (disc method) — same squaring step, but no subtraction term.
Rotation about the y-axis instead of the x-axis
Either invert to x = g(y) and use V = π∫x² dy (squaring still removes surds in y), or use the shell method 2π∫x·f(x) dx if rearrangement is awkward.
Curve contains a cube root, e.g. (x−3)^{1/3}
Squaring gives (x−3)^{2/3}, which does not fully eliminate the fractional power; a substitution u = x−3 may be needed.
Squared integrand has form (linear)/(polynomial)² with numerator ∝ derivative of inner
Apply reverse chain rule / u-substitution u = denominator's inner function rather than expanding.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating a region between a curve and y = k as if the lower boundary were the x-axis, omitting the outer cylinder term k².
  • Confusing the rotation axis and integrating with respect to the wrong variable.
  • Forgetting that V requires y², so attempting to integrate y directly.

Pitfalls

  • Squaring only the numerator of a quotient and leaving the denominator un-squared.
  • Reversing y_upper and y_lower, producing a negative volume — always verify which function is larger on the interval.
  • Dropping the π factor outside the integral, or absorbing it inconsistently.
  • Using the wrong limits — e.g. y-values when integrating in x, or failing to find the intersection of the curve with y = k.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 'volumes of revolution about the x-axis' (formula V = π∫y² dx is in the formula booklet). The washer extension V = π∫(y_upper² − y_lower²) dx is a direct application, not a separate booklet entry. Avoid Pappus's theorem and rotations about arbitrary axes — these are out of scope.
