Coordinate Geometry

Replace x with (x - a) to translate a curve horizontally by +a

Family: Curve Transformation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question presents a new curve of the form $y = f(x - a)$, $y = f(x + a)$, or asks for the effect on roots/stationary points/intercepts of such a substitution. Common phrasings include 'sketch the curve $y = f(x - a)$', 'state the coordinates of the image of the point...', or 'find the values of $a$ for which the transformed curve...'. The cue is the bracketed argument $(x \pm a)$ inside $f$, signalling a horizontal shift rather than a vertical one. Often appears as the final part of a curve-sketching question after the original $y = f(x)$ has been analysed.

Motivation — Why this works

If $(x_0, y_0)$ lies on $y = f(x)$, then $(x_0 + a, y_0)$ lies on $y = f(x - a)$ because substituting $x = x_0 + a$ gives $f((x_0 + a) - a) = f(x_0) = y_0$. So every point translates by $+a$ in the $x$-direction with $y$ unchanged. This preserves the geometric nature of features — roots stay roots, repeated roots stay touches, stationary points stay stationary points — so the student can read off new coordinates without re-expanding or re-differentiating.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the bracketed argument $(x - a)$ inside $f$ — this signals a horizontal translation, not a stretch or vertical shift.
  2. Rule out re-expanding $f(x - a)$ algebraically; the transformation rule gives all the geometric information directly.
  3. Commit to the rule: $y = f(x - a)$ shifts the entire graph $a$ units to the right (sign of $a$ matters).
  4. Bridge to action by listing every key feature of the original curve (roots, turning points, $y$-intercept) with their coordinates.
  5. Apply $x \mapsto x + a$ to each feature's $x$-coordinate; leave $y$-coordinates untouched; preserve the nature (touch/cross) of each root.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify all key features of $y = f(x)$: roots (with multiplicity), stationary points, $y$-intercept, asymptotes.
  2. For each feature at $(x_0, y_0)$, write the image as $(x_0 + a, y_0)$.
  3. Preserve the nature of each feature: a touching (repeated) root remains a touching root; a crossing root remains a crossing root.
  4. Update the $y$-intercept by evaluating $f(-a)$ if required (the new curve crosses the $y$-axis at $(0, f(-a))$, not at the original intercept).
  5. Use the shifted coordinates to answer the specific question (sketch, find $a$, state image points, etc.).

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose $y = f(x)$ has a repeated root at $x = \tfrac{1}{2}$ and a simple root at $x = 4$. For $y = f(x - 3)$, add $3$ to each root: the curve touches the $x$-axis at $x = \tfrac{7}{2}$ and crosses it at $x = 7$. The $y$-intercept of the new curve is $f(-3)$, generally not equal to $f(0)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$y = f(x + a)$ with $a > 0$
Shift left by $a$: subtract $a$ from each $x$-coordinate.
$y = f(x) + b$ (vertical translation)
Add $b$ to each $y$-coordinate; $x$-coordinates unchanged; roots generally move.
$y = f(x - a) + b$ combined translation
Apply both: $(x_0, y_0) \mapsto (x_0 + a, y_0 + b)$.
$y = f(kx)$ horizontal stretch
Different family: divide $x$-coordinates by $k$; not a translation.
$y = |f(x - a)|$ or $y = f(|x - a|)$
Translate first, then apply the modulus rule (reflect negative $y$ part, or reflect right half across $x = a$).

Common Misreadings

  • Reading $f(x - a)$ as a shift left by $a$ instead of right by $a$ (sign confusion).
  • Trying to expand $f(x - a)$ algebraically when $f$ is given as a polynomial, wasting time.
  • Believing the $y$-intercept is preserved — it generally is not, since $f(0 - a) = f(-a) \neq f(0)$.
  • Confusing $y = f(x - a)$ (horizontal) with $y = f(x) - a$ (vertical).

Pitfalls

  • Mixing up horizontal and vertical shifts when both appear together.
  • Forgetting that the multiplicity of a root (and hence touch-vs-cross behaviour) is preserved under translation.
  • Failing to translate asymptotes: a vertical asymptote $x = c$ becomes $x = c + a$.
  • Translating a stationary point's $y$-coordinate by mistake — only $x$ moves.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure content (P2/P3 graph transformations). All translations $y = f(x - a)$, $y = f(x) + b$, and combinations are within scope, as are sketches involving translated polynomial, modulus, exponential and logarithmic curves.
