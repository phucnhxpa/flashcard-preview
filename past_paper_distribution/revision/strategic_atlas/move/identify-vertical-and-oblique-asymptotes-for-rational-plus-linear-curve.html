Coordinate Geometry

Identify vertical and oblique asymptotes for rational-plus-linear curve

Family: Asymptotic Analysis Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when sketching a curve of the form y = (rational term) + (linear term), e.g. y = A/(x−a)^n + mx + k, often arising as the general or particular solution of a first-order ODE. Cues: a denominator that vanishes at a finite x-value, plus a linear (non-vanishing) tail as |x| → ∞. Also fires whenever an exam asks to 'sketch', 'state the asymptotes of', or 'describe the behaviour' of such a curve.

Motivation — Why this works

The two terms dominate in disjoint regimes: near the singularity of the rational term, that term blows up and the linear term is bounded, giving a vertical asymptote. For large |x|, the rational term tends to 0 and the linear term dominates, giving an oblique asymptote y = mx + k. Because lim(y − (mx+k)) = 0, the line is genuinely an asymptote, and the sign of the rational correction tells you which side of the line the curve sits on.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the curve splits as 'rational piece + linear piece' — a classic asymptote-pair signature.
  2. Rule out treating the linear part as a tangent or chord; its role is end-behaviour, not local contact.
  3. Commit to the two-asymptote sketch because the rational term controls one limit (vertical) and is negligible in the other (oblique).
  4. Bridge: locate the vertical asymptote from the zero of the denominator, then read off the oblique asymptote as the linear part itself.
  5. Decide which side of y = mx + k each branch lies on by checking the sign of the rational correction, and pin down a single point (often from the initial condition) to fix the branch.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Set the denominator equal to zero to locate vertical asymptote(s): here x² = 0 ⟹ x = 0.
  2. Compute lim_{x→±∞} (y − mx − k); if it is 0, then y = mx + k is the oblique asymptote. Here lim 1/x² = 0, so y = 2x.
  3. Determine the sign of the rational correction on each branch: 1/x² > 0 for all x ≠ 0, so both branches lie above y = 2x.
  4. Use any given point (e.g. initial condition (−1,−1)) to confirm the constant of integration and to anchor the sketch.
  5. Draw two branches: vertical asymptote at x = 0 with both branches → +∞, each tail hugging y = 2x from above as x → ±∞.

Worked Micro-Example

Sketch $y = \tfrac{1}{x^2} + 2x$. Denominator zero: $x = 0$ is a vertical asymptote. As $x \to \pm\infty$, $y - 2x = 1/x^2 \to 0^+$, so $y = 2x$ is an oblique asymptote and the curve lies above it. Check: at $x=-1$, $y = 1 + (-2) = -1$, so $(-1,-1)$ lies on the left branch. The sketch has two branches, both rising to $+\infty$ at $x=0$ and both approaching $y=2x$ from above.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$y = \dfrac{A}{x-a} + mx + k$
Vertical asymptote at $x=a$; oblique asymptote $y=mx+k$. Sign of $A$ and side of $a$ determine which branch sits above/below the oblique asymptote (opposite sides for the two branches, since $1/(x-a)$ changes sign).
$y = \dfrac{A}{(x-a)^2} + mx + k$
Vertical asymptote at $x=a$ with both branches going to $+\infty$ (if $A>0$) or both to $-\infty$ (if $A<0$); both branches lie on the same side of $y=mx+k$.
Improper rational $y = \dfrac{p(x)}{q(x)}$ with $\deg p = \deg q + 1$
Polynomial-divide to write $y = (\text{linear quotient}) + (\text{proper remainder})$, then apply the same analysis to the rewritten form.
Horizontal asymptote case $y = \dfrac{A}{x-a} + c$
The 'linear' part is constant, so the oblique asymptote degenerates to the horizontal line $y=c$; vertical asymptote analysis is unchanged.

Common Misreadings

  • Mistaking y = 2x for a tangent or chord rather than an asymptote.
  • Forgetting the curve has two branches separated by the vertical asymptote.
  • Assuming the two branches lie on opposite sides of the oblique asymptote (true for $1/(x-a)$, false for $1/(x-a)^2$).
  • Reading the linear term as 'just a translation' rather than as the dominant end-behaviour.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to note that $1/x^2 > 0$ always, so both branches are above (not split across) the oblique asymptote.
  • Drawing the curve crossing $y = 2x$, which it never does since $y - 2x = 1/x^2 \neq 0$.
  • Forgetting to verify the initial/anchor point, leading to a sketch on the wrong branch.
  • Confusing the limit $\lim(y - mx - k) = 0$ (asymptote) with $\lim y/x = m$ alone (necessary but not sufficient to fix $k$).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL curve-sketching technique (P3/P4 and FP1), often appearing as the final 'sketch' part of a first-order ODE question; uses only limits at $0$ and $\pm\infty$ of elementary rational expressions, with no formal epsilon-delta or calculus of curvature.
