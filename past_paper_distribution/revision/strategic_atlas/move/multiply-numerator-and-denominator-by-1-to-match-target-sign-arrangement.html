Multiply numerator and denominator by −1 to match target sign arrangement

Family: Sign Manipulation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in 'show that' questions when your derived fraction differs from the printed target only by an overall sign distribution — typically a leading minus on the fraction with denominator $(a-b)$, while the target has no leading minus and denominator $(b-a)$. Common after implicit differentiation, partial fractions, or rearranging an integral. Cue phrases: 'hence show that $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{x+y}{2y-x}$' when you have obtained $-\frac{x+y}{x-2y}$. Recognise that $(b-a) = -(a-b)$.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying numerator and denominator of any fraction by the same nonzero constant leaves its value unchanged, since $\frac{-1}{-1}=1$. Choosing that constant to be $-1$ flips every sign in both top and bottom simultaneously, which absorbs an external minus sign into the denominator (or numerator) and reorders subtractions. This is the cleanest way to reconcile equivalent algebraic forms without expanding or factorising further. In 'show that' questions the printed form must be reproduced exactly, so this cosmetic step is often essential.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice my answer $-\frac{x+y}{x-2y}$ and the target $\frac{x+y}{2y-x}$ have the same numerator up to sign and denominators that are negatives of each other.
  2. I rule out re-deriving from scratch or re-factorising — the algebra is already correct, only the presentation differs.
  3. I commit to a sign-flip because $(2y-x) = -(x-2y)$ is an exact identity, so one multiplication by $-1/-1$ will land on the target.
  4. I bridge to the operation: multiply numerator and denominator by $-1$ simultaneously so the value is preserved.
  5. Tactically, I push the external minus sign into the denominator (turning $x-2y$ into $2y-x$), which automatically clears the minus in front of the fraction.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify that the target denominator is the negative of your current denominator: $(2y-x) = -(x-2y)$.
  2. Write the current expression: $\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{x+y}{x-2y}$.
  3. Multiply numerator and denominator by $-1$: $\frac{-1\cdot(-(x+y))}{-1\cdot(x-2y)} = \frac{x+y}{2y-x}$.
  4. State the result and confirm it matches the printed target exactly.

Worked Micro-Example

From implicit differentiation, suppose $\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{x+y}{x-2y}$. Multiply top and bottom by $-1$: $$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{-1\cdot(-(x+y))}{-1\cdot(x-2y)} = \frac{x+y}{2y-x},$$ which is the required 'show that' form.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Partial fractions where one bracket appears as $(a-x)$ but the mark scheme uses $(x-a)$
Multiply that single fraction's numerator and denominator by $-1$, absorbing the sign into the constant on top.
Integration result printed as $\ln|b-a|$ but you obtained $-\ln|a-b|$
Use $-\ln|a-b| = \ln|a-b|^{-1} = \ln\frac{1}{|a-b|}$, or note $|a-b|=|b-a|$ to reconcile.
Quadratic factorisation giving $-(x-3)(x+1)$ when target is $(3-x)(x+1)$
Distribute the $-1$ into the chosen bracket: $-(x-3) = (3-x)$.
Vector/parametric direction printed with opposite sign convention
Multiply the direction vector by $-1$; valid because direction vectors are defined up to nonzero scalar.

Common Misreadings

  • Thinking the two forms are genuinely different and re-doing the differentiation looking for an error.
  • Believing a leading minus sign cannot be moved inside the fraction without changing its value.
  • Confusing this cosmetic sign flip with an actual algebraic simplification step that requires justification.

Pitfalls

  • Flipping the sign of only the numerator or only the denominator, which negates the whole fraction.
  • Distributing $-1$ into only one term of a bracket, e.g. writing $-(x-2y) = -x-2y$ instead of $-x+2y = 2y-x$.
  • Forgetting to also remove the external minus sign once it has been absorbed, leaving a doubled negative.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure scope; commonly required in implicit differentiation 'show that' parts (P3/P4) and partial-fraction matching. Uses only the identity $\frac{-a}{-b}=\frac{a}{b}$.
