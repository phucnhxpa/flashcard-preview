Trigonometry

Recast equation as two-curve intersection to count roots graphically

Family: Graphical Intersection Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question uses the directive 'hence' (or 'use your sketch/diagram') after a previous part has produced a graph, and asks for the NUMBER of solutions/roots of an equation involving trig functions that resists algebraic solution. Cue phrases include 'state the number of roots', 'find the number of solutions', or 'by drawing a suitable straight line on Diagram 1'. The equation typically has the form f(θ) = g(θ) where one or both sides are trig expressions with no closed-form inverse, e.g. tan θ = 1 + sin θ, or sin x = a − bx. The presence of a pre-drawn curve plus a counting question (rather than 'solve exactly') is the decisive signal.

Motivation — Why this works

Splitting an equation f(θ) = g(θ) into two graphs y = f(θ) and y = g(θ) turns 'solve' into 'count intersections', which is the only viable route when the equation is transcendental and mixes incompatible trig functions. The underlying principle is that roots of f − g = 0 correspond bijectively to points where the two curves cross. This sidesteps algebra that would either loop back on itself (e.g. squaring introduces extraneous roots) or have no elementary closed form. The 'hence' instruction makes this graphical reading mandatory, and using the existing sketch saves time.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the directive 'hence' (or 'draw a suitable straight line on the diagram') and the request for a NUMBER of roots, not exact values — this flags graphical counting.
  2. Rule out algebraic attack: rewriting tan θ = 1 + sin θ as sin θ = cos θ + sin θ cos θ leads nowhere standard; transcendental mixes of x with sin x have no closed form.
  3. Commit to the graphical method: identify the LHS and RHS as the two curves to compare. If the RHS is not yet drawn (e.g. it is linear), plan to add it to the existing diagram by marking two easy points.
  4. Bridge to operation: rearrange the equation so one side matches the curve already plotted (call this y₁) and the other side is the simpler/auxiliary curve y₂ (often a straight line).
  5. Tactical decision: locate y₂'s key features — for a line, find gradient and intercepts; for 1 + sin θ, mark its max (2), min (0), and zeros. Add it cleanly to the sketch.
  6. Systematically scan each interval bounded by asymptotes or domain endpoints, counting crossings one interval at a time — do not eyeball the whole picture at once.
  7. Verify endpoints and asymptotes carefully: a point where tan is undefined cannot be an intersection even if the other curve passes through y = 0 there.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rearrange the equation so that one side equals the already-sketched curve y = f(θ); call the other side y = g(θ).
  2. If g(θ) is not yet on the diagram, plot it by marking two or three reference points (intercepts, maxima, asymptotic behaviour) and joining them.
  3. Partition the domain into intervals separated by asymptotes of f or g (for tan θ on [0°, 360°]: (0°,90°), (90°,270°), (270°,360°)).
  4. In each interval, compare the ranges and monotonicity of the two curves to determine the number of crossings.
  5. Add up the crossings across all intervals; check endpoints separately.
  6. Exclude any 'apparent' intersection at a point where either function is undefined (e.g. θ = 90°, 270° for tan).
  7. State the total number of roots.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve: how many roots does $\tan\theta = 1 + \sin\theta$ have on $[0°,360°]$? Sketch $y_1=\tan\theta$ and $y_2 = 1+\sin\theta$ on the same axes. On $(0°,90°)$: $\tan$ rises $0\to\infty$, $y_2$ rises $1\to 2$ — exactly 1 crossing. On $(90°,270°)$: $\tan$ goes $-\infty\to+\infty$, $y_2$ stays in $[0,2]$ — gives further crossings (count carefully on sketch). On $(270°,360°)$: $\tan$ rises $-\infty\to 0$, $y_2$ rises $0\to 1$ — 1 crossing. Total: read off from the sketch.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation of the form sin x = a − bx or f(x) = linear in x
Draw the straight line y = a − bx on the trig graph by plotting its two intercepts (0, a) and (a/b, 0); count crossings.
Equation involves |f(x)| from a modulus-transformation in an earlier part
Use the modulus-transformed graph (already sketched) as y₁, not the original f(x).
Equation rearranges to f(x) = k (a horizontal line)
Draw y = k as a horizontal line and count crossings with the curve — same logic, simplest case.

Common Misreadings

  • Attempting algebraic manipulation despite 'hence' — e.g. converting tan θ = 1 + sin θ to sin θ = cos θ(1 + sin θ) and getting stuck.
  • Drawing the wrong auxiliary curve because the rearrangement put terms on the wrong side.
  • Using a calculator/numerical solver instead of the diagram, missing the 'hence' instruction.

Pitfalls

  • Counting an apparent intersection at an asymptote of tan (e.g. θ = 90° or 270°) as a root — tan is undefined there.
  • Missing a near-tangent crossing or double-counting a tangential touch.
  • Inaccurate placement of the auxiliary line so that an intersection is missed at a domain endpoint (e.g. forgetting to extend to x = 2π).
  • Confusing the number of roots in the SPECIFIED domain with all real roots.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P2/P3) graphical-solution style. No formula-booklet entry is needed; relies on standard sketches of y = sin x, cos x, tan x and linear functions. Avoid invoking calculus (e.g. derivatives of f − g) to count intersections — graphical inspection is the intended method.
