Trigger — When to use

Fires immediately after applying the product rule to a function of the form (polynomial)·e^{kx}, when the resulting derivative is a sum of two (or more) terms each containing e^{kx} as a visible common factor. Typical surface forms include 2xe^{2x} + e^{2x}, 3e^{3x}(2x+5) + 2e^{3x}, or any expression matching f'(x)e^{kx} + kf(x)e^{kx}. The cue is reinforced when the next part of the question asks for stationary points, equates dy/dx to 0, or otherwise requires solving the derivative equation. In short: 'every term has an e^{kx} in it, and I'm about to set this equal to zero'.

Motivation — Why this works

Factoring rewrites an additive expression as a product e^{kx}·(linear/polynomial bracket), which converts dy/dx = 0 into a zero-product statement. Since e^{kx} > 0 for all real x, the exponential factor never vanishes, so the equation reduces to solving the polynomial bracket — a far cleaner algebraic problem. This avoids dead-end approaches like taking logarithms of a sum (which is invalid) or dividing through by e^{kx} (which works but obscures the structural reason). The underlying principle is the reverse of the product rule: d/dx[f(x)e^{kx}] = e^{kx}[f'(x) + kf(x)].

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I look at the derivative I just produced and notice that every term contains the same exponential factor e^{kx}.
  2. I consider alternatives: could I expand everything out and solve the resulting equation directly? No — that gives a sum of exponentials and polynomials with no clean route to a solution. Could I take logs? No — log of a sum is not the sum of logs.
  3. I commit to factoring because I anticipate the next part will require dy/dx = 0, and a product form e^{kx}·(bracket) = 0 immediately collapses to (bracket) = 0 since e^{kx} ≠ 0.
  4. I extract e^{kx} carefully — taking only one copy, not 2e^{kx} or ke^{kx} — and write what remains inside the bracket.
  5. I simplify the bracket by expanding and collecting like terms, double-checking the arithmetic since this linear/polynomial expression is what I will actually solve.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify e^{kx} as a factor common to every term of dy/dx.
  2. Write dy/dx = e^{kx}·[remaining bracket], placing exactly what is left after removing one e^{kx} from each term.
  3. Expand and simplify inside the bracket (e.g. 3(2x+5) + 2 = 6x + 17).
  4. State the factored form, e.g. dy/dx = e^{kx}(ax + b), ready for setting equal to zero.
  5. When solving dy/dx = 0, note e^{kx} ≠ 0 and solve the bracket = 0.

Worked Micro-Example

Given y = (2x+5)e^{3x}, the product rule gives dy/dx = 2e^{3x} + 3(2x+5)e^{3x}. Factor: dy/dx = e^{3x}[2 + 3(2x+5)] = e^{3x}(6x+17). Setting dy/dx = 0 and using e^{3x} \neq 0 gives 6x+17 = 0, so x = -\tfrac{17}{6}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Polynomial × negative exponential, e.g. y = x²e^{-3x}
Same logic: dy/dx = e^{-3x}(-3x² + 2x) = xe^{-3x}(2 - 3x); factor out both e^{-3x} and any common power of x.
General linear-times-exponential y = (ax+b)e^{kx}
Derivative always factors as e^{kx}[k(ax+b) + a]; the bracket is linear in x with a single root.
Polynomial × trig product, e.g. y = x²sin(x)
After product rule, factor out the common power of x (or trig factor) to produce a solvable product form for stationary-point analysis.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 2xe^{2x} + e^{2x} = 0 as if logs can be applied directly to the sum — this is invalid since log(A+B) ≠ log A + log B.
  • Believing that because e^{kx} appears, the equation has no real stationary point — forgetting that the polynomial factor can vanish.

Pitfalls

  • Factoring out 2e^{kx} or ke^{kx} instead of just e^{kx}, producing an incorrect linear factor.
  • Arithmetic slips when expanding inside the bracket, e.g. writing 3(2x+5) = 6x+5 instead of 6x+15.
  • Leaving the derivative unfactored and attempting to solve a messy mixed equation.
  • Forgetting to state e^{kx} ≠ 0 when justifying that only the bracket factor produces solutions.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 differentiation of exponential functions and product rule (formula booklet: derivative of e^{kx} is ke^{kx}; product rule (uv)' = u'v + uv'). Do not generalise to complex exponentials or to claims about e^{kx} = 0 having complex solutions — outside scope.
