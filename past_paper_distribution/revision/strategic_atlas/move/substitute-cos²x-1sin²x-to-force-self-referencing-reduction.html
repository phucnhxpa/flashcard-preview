Strategic Atlas / Integration / Identity Driven Recurrence
Integration

Substitute cos²x = 1−sin²x to force self-referencing reduction

Family: Identity Driven Recurrence Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when deriving a reduction formula for I_n = ∫sinⁿx dx and integration by parts has produced a residual integral of the form ∫sin^{n−2}x · cos²x dx. The mixed-power integrand is not yet recognisable as any I_k in the target family. The cue is: 'I have sin and cos appearing together, but my recurrence must be expressed purely in I_n and I_{n−2}.' Also fires whenever a Pythagorean identity can collapse a two-trig-function integrand into a single-function one matching the indexed family.

Motivation — Why this works

The Pythagorean identity cos²x = 1 − sin²x converts the cos² factor into a constant minus sin², so the integrand splits into sin^{n−2}x − sinⁿx, exactly matching I_{n−2} − I_n. This deliberately re-introduces I_n on the right-hand side, producing a linear equation in I_n that solves algebraically to give the reduction. Double-angle forms like cos²x = (1+cos2x)/2 do not produce indexed members of the family, so they fail to close the recurrence.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the post-IBP integral contains sin^{n−2}x · cos²x — two different trig functions, so it is not yet an I_k.
  2. I rule out double-angle (cos²x = (1+cos2x)/2) because it introduces cos2x, which lives outside my family.
  3. I commit to cos²x = 1 − sin²x because it eliminates cosine entirely and keeps every term as a pure power of sinx.
  4. Bridging step: distribute to get sin^{n−2}x − sinⁿx, then integrate term-by-term to get I_{n−2} − I_n.
  5. Tactically, I expect I_n to reappear on the right; I plan to collect I_n terms and divide by the resulting coefficient (here n).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Start from the post-IBP expression I_n = [boundary term] + (n−1)∫sin^{n−2}x · cos²x dx.
  2. Replace cos²x by 1 − sin²x inside the integral.
  3. Expand: (n−1)∫(sin^{n−2}x − sinⁿx) dx = (n−1)I_{n−2} − (n−1)I_n.
  4. Substitute back to obtain I_n = [boundary term] + (n−1)I_{n−2} − (n−1)I_n.
  5. Collect: nI_n = [boundary term] + (n−1)I_{n−2}, then divide by n to isolate I_n.

Worked Micro-Example

For I_n = ∫₀^{π/2} sinⁿx dx, IBP with u = sin^{n−1}x, dv = sinx dx gives I_n = [−sin^{n−1}x cosx]₀^{π/2} + (n−1)∫₀^{π/2} sin^{n−2}x cos²x dx. The boundary term vanishes. Replace cos²x = 1 − sin²x: I_n = (n−1)(I_{n−2} − I_n). Solving: nI_n = (n−1)I_{n−2}, so I_n = ((n−1)/n) I_{n−2}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Reduction for J_n = ∫cosⁿx dx
After IBP, replace sin²x = 1 − cos²x to split the residual into J_{n−2} − J_n and obtain J_n = ((n−1)/n)J_{n−2}.
Reduction for ∫secⁿx dx
After IBP with dv = sec²x dx, use sec²x = 1 + tan²x (or tan²x = sec²x − 1) to express the residual as a combination of the same family at indices n and n−2.
Reduction for ∫tanⁿx dx
Use tan²x = sec²x − 1 directly (no IBP needed) to write tanⁿx = tan^{n−2}x·sec²x − tan^{n−2}x, integrating the first piece by inspection.
Mixed integral ∫sin^m x cosⁿx dx with even cos power
Apply cos²x = 1 − sin²x repeatedly to convert all cosine factors to sines, producing a sum of pure-sine integrals.

Common Misreadings

  • Choosing the double-angle form cos²x = (1+cos2x)/2, which introduces cos2x and breaks the indexed family — no recurrence forms.
  • Treating ∫sin^{n−2}x cos²x dx as if it were already I_{n−2} by ignoring the cos² factor.
  • Trying to integrate sin^{n−2}x cos²x by a fresh IBP instead of the identity, generating an even messier expression.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when distributing (n−1) across (I_{n−2} − I_n).
  • Forgetting to move the (n−1)I_n term back to the left, leading to an incorrect coefficient on I_n.
  • Dropping or mis-evaluating the boundary term [−sin^{n−1}x cosx] before collecting.
  • Dividing by (n−1) instead of n at the final isolation step.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope: reduction formulae for ∫sinⁿx dx, ∫cosⁿx dx, ∫secⁿx dx and ∫tanⁿx dx are standard FP topics, using only Pythagorean identities and integration by parts.
