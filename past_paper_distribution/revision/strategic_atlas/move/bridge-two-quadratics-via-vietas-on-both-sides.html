Algebra & Functions

Bridge two quadratics via Vieta's on both sides

Family: Vieta'S Bridge Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Two quadratics appear in the same problem and the roots of the second are described as symmetric transformations of the roots of the first (e.g. α+1, β+1; 2α, 2β; α², β²; 1/α, 1/β). One or both quadratics contain unknown coefficients (A, B, k). The wording asks to find these unknowns or to relate the coefficients without solving either quadratic explicitly. Phrases like 'the roots of the second equation are…' or 'given that α and β are roots of…' are strong cues.

Motivation — Why this works

Vieta's formulas turn the roots of any quadratic into two symmetric functions of its coefficients: α+β = −b/a and αβ = c/a. If the new roots are symmetric expressions in α and β, their sum and product can also be rewritten in terms of α+β and αβ. This gives two parallel descriptions of the same two symmetric quantities — one from each quadratic — which can be equated to pin down the unknown coefficients. No individual root values are ever needed.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the second quadratic's roots are defined symmetrically in terms of α and β, so they are functions of α+β and αβ only.
  2. Rule out solving the first quadratic for α and β explicitly — the leading coefficient A is unknown, and direct values aren't required.
  3. Commit to Vieta's because both quadratics expose α+β, αβ (or the new sum/product) directly from coefficients.
  4. Bridge by writing all four Vieta expressions: (α+β, αβ) from quadratic 1, and (sum, product of new roots) from quadratic 2.
  5. Tactically, expand the new sum/product in terms of α+β and αβ, substitute the coefficient expressions, then equate to the second quadratic's Vieta relations to form equations in A and B.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Label roots of quadratic 1 as α, β; apply Vieta's: α+β = −b₁/a₁, αβ = c₁/a₁.
  2. Identify the new roots (transformations of α, β) and apply Vieta's to quadratic 2 to get their sum and product in terms of its coefficients.
  3. Expand the new sum and new product as symmetric polynomials in α+β and αβ (using identities like α²+β² = (α+β)² − 2αβ).
  4. Substitute the expressions from step 1 into the expanded forms.
  5. Equate to the Vieta relations from step 2 to obtain simultaneous equations in the unknown coefficients; solve.

Worked Micro-Example

Let α, β be roots of $Ax^2 + 5x - 12 = 0$, so $\alpha+\beta = -5/A$, $\alpha\beta = -12/A$. Suppose $2\alpha, 2\beta$ are roots of $4x^2 - 5x + B = 0$. Then $2\alpha + 2\beta = 5/4$ gives $-10/A = 5/4$, so $A = -8$. Also $4\alpha\beta = B/4$, so $B = 16\alpha\beta = 16(-12/A) = 24$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

New roots are α+k, β+k (shift)
Sum becomes (α+β)+2k; product becomes αβ + k(α+β) + k².
New roots are α², β² (squares)
Sum = (α+β)² − 2αβ; product = (αβ)².
New roots are 1/α, 1/β (reciprocals)
Sum = (α+β)/(αβ); product = 1/(αβ); requires αβ ≠ 0.
Cubic with roots α, β, γ
Use three Vieta relations: Σα, Σαβ, αβγ; bridge via symmetric functions of the transformed roots.
Form a new quadratic whose roots are given expressions in α, β
Compute new sum S and product P, then write x² − Sx + P = 0.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating αβ as the constant term directly when the leading coefficient is not 1 (writing αβ = −12 instead of −12/A).
  • Getting the sign wrong on α+β: it is −b/a, not b/a.
  • Trying to solve for α and β individually when only symmetric combinations are needed.
  • Assuming the two quadratics share the same roots rather than transformed roots.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to divide by the leading coefficient when it is not 1.
  • Algebraic slip when expanding (α+β)² − 2αβ for α²+β².
  • Mixing up which quadratic's coefficients give which Vieta relations.
  • Dividing by αβ in the reciprocal variant without checking it is non-zero.

A-level Scope Notes

This is a core technique in Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP1) on roots of polynomial equations and forming new equations with transformed roots, and is fully within A-level scope.
