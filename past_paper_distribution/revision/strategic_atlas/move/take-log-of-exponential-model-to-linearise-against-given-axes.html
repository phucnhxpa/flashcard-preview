Take log of exponential model to linearise against given axes

Family: Log Linearization Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

An exponential model of the form $A = pq^t$ (or similar with a variable in the exponent) is given alongside a graph whose axes are $\log_{10} A$ against $t$ shown as a straight line. The wording typically asks to 'show that' the relationship is linear, or to find $p$ and $q$ from the gradient and intercept. Cues include log-scaled axes, a straight-line plot of transformed data, or a question stem giving the linear form $Y = mX + c$ to be matched. The base of the logarithm on the axis label dictates the base to use.

Motivation — Why this works

Logarithms convert products into sums and exponents into coefficients via $\log(ab) = \log a + \log b$ and $\log(a^n) = n\log a$. Applying $\log_{10}$ to $A = pq^t$ therefore produces $\log_{10} A = \log_{10} p + t\log_{10} q$, which is linear in $t$. This matches the straight-line template $Y = c + mX$ with $Y = \log_{10} A$, $X = t$, intercept $\log_{10} p$, and gradient $\log_{10} q$. The graph's measurable features then directly recover the unknown constants.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the y-axis is $\log_{10} A$ and the x-axis is $t$, so the working equation must be in those two variables, not in $A$ and $t$.
  2. I rule out rearranging $A = pq^t$ algebraically (e.g. dividing or substituting) — none of those produce a $\log_{10} A$ term, so logs are unavoidable.
  3. I commit to taking $\log_{10}$ of both sides because the axis specifies base 10; using $\ln$ would give a correct linear form but with intercept $\ln p$ and gradient $\ln q$, mismatching the graph's readings.
  4. The first concrete step is $\log_{10} A = \log_{10}(pq^t)$, then I expand using the product and power laws.
  5. Finally I match terms with $Y = mX + c$: gradient of the line equals $\log_{10} q$ and y-intercept equals $\log_{10} p$, so $q = 10^{\text{gradient}}$ and $p = 10^{\text{intercept}}$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Apply $\log_{10}$ to both sides: $\log_{10} A = \log_{10}(pq^t)$.
  2. Use the product law: $\log_{10} A = \log_{10} p + \log_{10}(q^t)$.
  3. Use the power law to bring $t$ down: $\log_{10} A = \log_{10} p + t\log_{10} q$.
  4. Identify gradient $= \log_{10} q$ and y-intercept $= \log_{10} p$ by comparison with $Y = mX + c$.
  5. If values are required, recover $p = 10^{\text{intercept}}$ and $q = 10^{\text{gradient}}$.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $A = pq^t$ and a straight-line graph of $\log_{10} A$ against $t$ with gradient $0.2$ and y-intercept $0.7$: $\log_{10} A = \log_{10} p + t\log_{10} q$, so $\log_{10} p = 0.7 \Rightarrow p = 10^{0.7} \approx 5.01$. Also $\log_{10} q = 0.2 \Rightarrow q = 10^{0.2} \approx 1.58$. Hence $A \approx 5.01(1.58)^t$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Power-law model $y = ax^n$ plotted as $\log_{10} y$ against $\log_{10} x$
Take logs of both sides to get $\log_{10} y = \log_{10} a + n\log_{10} x$; gradient gives $n$ directly, intercept gives $\log_{10} a$.
Graph uses $\ln$ instead of $\log_{10}$
Apply $\ln$ throughout; intercept becomes $\ln p$ and gradient becomes $\ln q$, so $p = e^{\text{intercept}}$, $q = e^{\text{gradient}}$.
Exponential model written as $A = pe^{kt}$
Taking $\ln$ gives $\ln A = \ln p + kt$; the gradient is $k$ itself (no further log conversion needed).
Only one of the axes is logarithmic, e.g. $\log_{10} A$ vs $t$ vs. $A$ vs $\log_{10} t$
Match the log to whichever variable appears as an exponent or as a power-law base; the choice of which side to log depends on the axis labels.

Common Misreadings

  • Using $\ln$ when the axis is $\log_{10}$ (or vice versa), so numerical readings of gradient/intercept give the wrong $p$ and $q$.
  • Treating $\log_{10}(pq^t)$ as $\log_{10} p \cdot \log_{10}(q^t)$ instead of a sum.
  • Confusing this with a power-law setup and logging $t$ as well, producing a $\log_{10} t$ term that does not match the axes.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to apply log to $p$, writing $\log_{10} A = p + t\log_{10} q$.
  • Leaving the answer as $\log_{10} p$ and $\log_{10} q$ when the question asks for $p$ and $q$ themselves — must antilog with $10^{(\cdot)}$.
  • Mis-assigning gradient and intercept (swapping which corresponds to $p$ and which to $q$).
  • Dropping the coefficient when applying the power law: writing $\log_{10}(q^t) = \log_{10} q$ instead of $t\log_{10} q$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 logarithm content: laws of logs and exponential models linearised on log-axes are standard exam material, including recovering constants from gradient and intercept.
