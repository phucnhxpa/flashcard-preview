Complex Numbers

Pair conjugate roots into a real quadratic factor via difference of squares

Family: Conjugate Pair To Quadratic Frequency: 6 questions

Trigger — When to use

Two roots of a real-coefficient polynomial are complex conjugates a ± bi (or have been deduced to be, by the conjugate root theorem), and you need a real quadratic factor to divide into the polynomial or to compare coefficients. Visual cue: the question states one complex root and asks for the others, OR gives both conjugate roots and asks you to find the remaining factor. Equivalent phrasing: 'one root is …, find the other roots', 'show that … is a factor', or 'hence factorise f(z) over ℝ'. The move fires whenever you would otherwise face FOIL-expansion of two complex linear factors.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying (z − (a+bi))(z − (a−bi)) directly is error-prone because of the cross-terms in i. Rewriting the product as ((z − a) + bi)((z − a) − bi) exposes the difference-of-squares identity A² − B² with A = z − a and B = bi. Since (bi)² = −b², the result collapses immediately to (z − a)² + b² = z² − 2az + (a² + b²), a real quadratic equal to z² − (sum of roots)z + (product of roots). This bypasses all complex arithmetic and produces the clean real factor needed for polynomial division or coefficient matching.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the two given (or deduced) roots are a complex conjugate pair a ± bi, so the product (z − (a+bi))(z − (a−bi)) must be a real quadratic — the imaginary parts are guaranteed to cancel.
  2. Rule out brute-force FOIL expansion of the two complex linear factors: it works but tracks four terms, two of which involve i, and is a frequent source of sign errors.
  3. Rule out using Vieta on the entire (cubic/quartic) polynomial simultaneously — possible but heavier; reconstructing one factor at a time is cleaner.
  4. Commit to the difference-of-squares route: group the real shift (z − a) together and isolate ±bi, giving the form ((z − a) + bi)((z − a) − bi).
  5. Apply A² − B² with A = z − a, B = bi. Compute B² = (bi)² = −b² carefully (this is where the sign work lives).
  6. Combine: (z − a)² − (bi)² = (z − a)² + b², then expand to z² − 2az + (a² + b²).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the two linear factors as (z − (a + bi))(z − (a − bi)).
  2. Regroup as ((z − a) + bi)((z − a) − bi).
  3. Apply difference of squares: (z − a)² − (bi)².
  4. Use (bi)² = −b² to rewrite as (z − a)² + b².
  5. Expand to obtain the real quadratic z² − 2az + (a² + b²).
  6. Cross-check via Vieta: sum of roots = 2a (coefficient of z is −2a), product = a² + b² (constant term).

Worked Micro-Example

Roots $1 \pm 2i$ → factor $(z - (1+2i))(z - (1-2i)) = ((z-1) + 2i)((z-1) - 2i) = (z-1)^2 - (2i)^2 = (z-1)^2 + 4 = z^2 - 2z + 5$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Roots given in polar form r(cos θ ± i sin θ)
Sum of roots = 2r cos θ, product = r²; quadratic factor is z² − (2r cos θ)z + r².
Surd-conjugate roots a ± b√c in a rational-coefficient polynomial
Same difference-of-squares logic gives (z − a)² − b²c = z² − 2az + (a² − b²c).
Quartic with two distinct conjugate pairs
Apply the move twice, once per pair, to obtain two real quadratic factors whose product is the quartic.
Only one complex root z₁ is stated explicitly
Invoke the conjugate root theorem (real coefficients ⇒ z̄₁ is also a root) before forming the quadratic factor.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating one complex root as sufficient and trying to divide f(z) by a single complex linear factor (z − z₁) — produces complex coefficients and is much harder.
  • Trying to apply Vieta's formulas across all roots of the cubic/quartic simultaneously when reconstructing one quadratic factor would be quicker.
  • Mistaking a problem with surd roots for a non-conjugate problem and missing that the conjugate surd is also a root.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error on (bi)²: writing +b² instead of −b², which flips the constant term.
  • Sign error in the linear coefficient: writing z² + 2az + … instead of z² − 2az + … (forgetting the minus from z² − (sum)z + product).
  • Arithmetic slips computing a² + b² when a or b is a fraction (e.g. forgetting to square the denominator).
  • Mis-expanding (z − a)², especially the middle term −2az.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP1/FP2) — uses the conjugate root theorem for polynomials with real coefficients and the standard identity (A+B)(A−B) = A² − B². No formula booklet entry is needed; the identity is assumed known. Do not generalise to polynomials with complex coefficients, where the conjugate root theorem fails.
