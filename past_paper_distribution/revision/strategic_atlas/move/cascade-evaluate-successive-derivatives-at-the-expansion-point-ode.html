Differential Equations

Cascade-evaluate successive derivatives at the expansion point

Family: Sequential Substitution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when constructing a Maclaurin or Taylor series for y(x) defined implicitly by an ODE, where coefficients require numerical values y(0), y'(0), y''(0), y'''(0), … . Initial conditions supply only the lowest two values, and each higher derivative is given symbolically in terms of lower ones via the ODE and its repeated differentiations. Cue phrases: 'find the series expansion up to and including the term in x^n', 'given y(0)=… and y'(0)=…', 'hence find y'''(0)'.

Motivation — Why this works

The ODE y''=f(x,y,y') and its successive derivatives form a triangular dependency: y^{(k)}(0) depends only on y(0),…,y^{(k-1)}(0). Evaluating bottom-up turns each symbolic relation into a concrete number, because the inputs needed have already been pinned down at the previous step. This is exactly the recursion that defines Maclaurin coefficients a_k = y^{(k)}(0)/k!, so the cascade is the natural computational order.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice I need numerical values of y, y', y'', y''' at x=0 to write the Maclaurin polynomial, but the ODE only gives these symbolically.
  2. I rule out solving the ODE in closed form (often impossible at A-level) and rule out substituting x=0 before differentiating — that would freeze the expression too early.
  3. I commit to a cascade: differentiate the ODE in general form first, then evaluate at x=0 in increasing order of derivative.
  4. I start by reading off y(0) and y'(0) from the given initial conditions, then substitute x=0 into the original ODE to get y''(0).
  5. For y'''(0), I differentiate the ODE symbolically (product/chain rule on terms containing y and y'), then substitute x=0 along with y(0), y'(0), y''(0); I keep fractions exact rather than decimalising to avoid feeding errors forward.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Record y(0) and y'(0) from the stated initial conditions.
  2. Substitute x=0, y=y(0), y'=y'(0) into the original ODE to compute y''(0).
  3. Differentiate the ODE with respect to x, treating y, y', y'' as functions of x (chain/product rule).
  4. Substitute x=0 together with y(0), y'(0), y''(0) into the differentiated ODE to compute y'''(0).
  5. Repeat: differentiate again and substitute, using all previously computed values, until the required order is reached.
  6. Assemble the series y(x) ≈ y(0) + y'(0)x + y''(0)x^2/2! + y'''(0)x^3/3! + ⋯

Worked Micro-Example

Given $y'' + xy' + y = 0$ with $y(0)=1$, $y'(0)=0$. At $x=0$: $y''(0) = -0\cdot 0 - 1 = -1$. Differentiate: $y''' + xy'' + y' + y' = 0$, i.e. $y''' = -xy'' - 2y'$. At $x=0$: $y'''(0) = 0$. Hence $y(x) \approx 1 - \tfrac{1}{2}x^2 + \cdots$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Taylor series about x=a (not 0)
Use the conditions given at x=a; substitute x=a at every stage and form $\sum y^{(k)}(a)(x-a)^k/k!$.
Second-order ODE with y(0) and y''(0) given instead of y'(0)
Rearrange the ODE at x=0 to solve for y'(0) first, then continue the cascade upward.
Implicit relation F(x,y)=0 differentiated repeatedly
Same cascade applied to implicit-differentiation outputs: evaluate dy/dx at the point, then d²y/dx², feeding each into the next.
First-order ODE y' = f(x,y) for a Maclaurin series
Only y(0) is needed initially; differentiate y' = f to get y'', y''', … and cascade-substitute as before.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating y, y', y'' as constants when differentiating the ODE, missing chain-rule contributions.
  • Believing y'''(0) can be obtained by differentiating a numerical value of y''(0) (zero), instead of differentiating the symbolic ODE.
  • Thinking the initial conditions must directly supply every y^{(k)}(0), and giving up when only y(0), y'(0) are stated.

Pitfalls

  • Substituting x=0 into the ODE BEFORE differentiating — differentiation must occur on the general expression first.
  • Arithmetic slips when y''(0) is a fraction that propagates into y'''(0) and beyond.
  • Forgetting to differentiate the x in terms like $xy'$, which contributes an extra $y'$ via the product rule.
  • Dividing by k! incorrectly when assembling the series (e.g. using k instead of k!).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure technique for Maclaurin/Taylor series of functions defined by ODEs; uses only product/chain rule and substitution, no convergence analysis required.
