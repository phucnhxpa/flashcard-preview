Trigger — When to use

The problem contains an expression of the form |E(x)| inside an equation or inequality (e.g. |2x − 5| = 3x − 5, |2 − 4ln(x+1)| > 3, |kx − 9| = 5 − 2x). The bars cannot be manipulated algebraically until removed, and the inside expression changes sign at a single critical point E(x) = 0. Recognise this whenever you see |linear| or |simple function| equated to or compared with another expression — especially when the right-hand side is itself variable, making the 'compound inequality' shortcut awkward. Equivalent phrasings: 'a piecewise-defined absolute value', 'a sign change at a critical point', 'two scenarios for the inside of the modulus'.

Motivation — Why this works

By definition, |E| = E when E ≥ 0 and |E| = −E when E < 0. Splitting at the critical point where E changes sign lets you replace the modulus with an ordinary expression in each region, reducing to standard linear (or transcendental) equations/inequalities. For inequalities, |f| > c becomes f > c OR f < −c (a union), while |f| < c becomes −c < f < c (an intersection); for equations, |E| = F becomes E = F or E = −F (provided F ≥ 0). This exhaustive decomposition guarantees no solutions are lost or fabricated, provided each candidate is checked against the case's domain.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice a modulus |E(x)| in the problem; I cannot do algebra through the bars, so I need to eliminate them.
  2. I consider alternatives: squaring both sides works for |f| = g but introduces extraneous roots and a quadratic; the compound-inequality shortcut works only for |f| < c or |f| > c with constant c. Here, with a variable RHS or a strict inequality, explicit casework is cleanest.
  3. I identify the critical point where E(x) = 0 — this is where the sign of E flips, splitting the real line into two regions.
  4. Case 1 (E ≥ 0): replace |E| by E. Case 2 (E < 0): replace |E| by −E. Each case yields an ordinary equation/inequality to solve.
  5. Tactical decision for inequalities: '|f| > c' produces a UNION of two regions; '|f| < c' produces an INTERSECTION (compound inequality). I must use the right connective.
  6. I solve each case, then for each candidate I check it lies in the case's defining region (and any external domain, e.g. x > −1 for ln(x+1)). Discard candidates that fail the domain check; take the union of valid solutions.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the critical point: solve E(x) = 0 to find x = x₀.
  2. Set up Case 1 on the region where E(x) ≥ 0: replace |E(x)| by E(x); solve the resulting equation/inequality.
  3. Set up Case 2 on the region where E(x) < 0: replace |E(x)| by −E(x); solve the resulting equation/inequality.
  4. When manipulating each case, watch sign-flips: dividing/multiplying an inequality by a negative number reverses the inequality.
  5. Check every candidate solution against (i) its case's domain and (ii) any external domain (e.g. x+1 > 0 for logs).
  6. Combine results: union of solution sets for an inequality of form |f|>c or an equation; intersection (compound) for |f|<c.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $|2-x| = 5x - 21$ rearranged as $21 - 2|2-x| = 5x$. Critical point: $x=2$. Case 1 ($x \le 2$): $21 - 2(2-x) = 5x \Rightarrow 17 + 2x = 5x \Rightarrow x = 17/3 \approx 5.67$, which violates $x \le 2$, reject. Case 2 ($x > 2$): $21 - 2(x-2) = 5x \Rightarrow 25 - 2x = 5x \Rightarrow x = 25/7 \approx 3.57$, which satisfies $x > 2$, accept. Solution: $x = 25/7$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Inequality |f(x)| > c with constant c > 0
Decompose into f(x) > c OR f(x) < −c; final solution is the UNION of the two solution sets, intersected with any external domain.
Inequality |f(x)| < c with constant c > 0
Decompose into the compound inequality −c < f(x) < c; this is an INTERSECTION, not a union.
Equation |f(x)| = |g(x)| (modulus on both sides)
Use f = g OR f = −g (no domain subdivision needed), or equivalently square both sides to get f² = g².
Equation |E(x)| = F(x) with F a non-constant linear expression
Split into E = F or E = −F; each candidate must be checked back against F(x) ≥ 0 in the original.
Sum of two moduli, e.g. |x − a| + |x − b| = k
Split at BOTH critical points, giving three cases on the resulting subintervals.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing |f| > c (union) with |f| < c (intersection) and writing the wrong connective in the final answer.
  • Setting up too many cases (e.g. three cases for a single critical point) by mistakenly splitting at x = 0 or other irrelevant points.
  • Defaulting to squaring both sides when the equation has terms outside the modulus, producing a messy quadratic and extraneous roots.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when removing modulus in the negative case: writing |2x − 5| = 2x − 5 instead of 5 − 2x for x < 5/2.
  • Forgetting to flip an inequality when dividing by a negative coefficient (e.g. dividing by −4).
  • Failing to intersect each case's algebraic solution with the case's domain (and with any external domain such as x > −1 for ln(x+1)).
  • Misreading exponentials of negatives: e^(−1/4) is a positive number less than 1, NOT −e^(1/4).
  • Forgetting that solutions from Case 2 of |E| = F require F ≥ 0 to be valid; not back-substituting to verify.

A-level Scope Notes
