Complex Numbers

Convert Cartesian complex numbers to modulus-argument form with quadrant check

Family: Modulus Argument Conversion Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question (a) asks for a complex number to be expressed in the form r(cos θ + i sin θ) with −π < θ ≤ π, or (b) gives an equation z^n = w with w in Cartesian form a + bi (or purely real/imaginary) that needs to be solved by de Moivre's theorem. Visual cues: the words 'modulus-argument form', 'exponential form', 'r e^{iθ}', or an nth-root equation. Equivalent phrasings include 'find arg(z) and |z|', 'express in polar form', or 'solve z^n = ... giving roots in the form r(cos θ + i sin θ)'.

Motivation — Why this works

The modulus r = √(x²+y²) measures distance from the origin and the argument θ measures the anticlockwise angle from the positive real axis. De Moivre's theorem and root-extraction operate on r and θ separately (modulus is powered, argument is scaled), so converting out of Cartesian is a prerequisite for any multiplication, division, or root operation in trig form. The crucial subtlety is that arctan(y/x) only returns the correct principal argument when x > 0 (quadrants I and IV); points in quadrants II and III need ±π adjustment, and points on the axes are special cases (real positive: θ = 0; real negative: θ = π; positive imaginary: θ = π/2; negative imaginary: θ = −π/2).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question demands trigonometric/polar form, or sets up z^n = w which signals de Moivre — either way, Cartesian must be converted.
  2. Rule out algebraic factoring or expansion: for n ≥ 3 or for finding all nth roots, brute algebra is impractical compared to de Moivre.
  3. Commit to computing r and θ for each complex number involved, treating the conversion as the first concrete step.
  4. Plot or mentally locate the point (Re, Im) to identify its quadrant — this determines whether arctan(y/x) gives the correct argument or needs a ±π correction.
  5. Decide on the form of the answer: if the question wants exact values in terms of π (e.g. for ±1, ±i, 1+i), keep θ symbolic; if numerical, compute arctan to the precision the mark scheme expects (typically 3 s.f. or 3 d.p.) without premature rounding.
  6. Exploit symmetry: conjugate pairs share modulus and have opposite arguments — a useful self-check.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute modulus: r = |w| = √(x² + y²) where w = x + iy.
  2. Identify the quadrant of (x, y).
  3. Compute the reference/principal argument: if x > 0, θ = arctan(y/x); if x < 0 and y ≥ 0, θ = arctan(y/x) + π; if x < 0 and y < 0, θ = arctan(y/x) − π; handle axis cases separately (x=0, y>0 → π/2; x=0, y<0 → −π/2; y=0, x>0 → 0; y=0, x<0 → π).
  4. Verify −π < θ ≤ π.
  5. Write w = r(cos θ + i sin θ) (or r e^{iθ}).

Worked Micro-Example

Convert $w = 1 - 2i$ to modulus-argument form. Modulus: $r = \sqrt{1^2 + (-2)^2} = \sqrt{5}$. Quadrant IV (x>0, y<0), so $\theta = \arctan(-2/1) = -\arctan 2 \approx -1.107$ rad, which lies in $(-\pi, \pi]$. Hence $w = \sqrt{5}\bigl(\cos(-1.107) + i\sin(-1.107)\bigr)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

w lies in quadrant II or III (x < 0)
arctan(y/x) gives an angle in (−π/2, π/2) which is wrong by π; add π if y ≥ 0, subtract π if y < 0 to land in (−π, π].
w is purely real negative (e.g. z^n = −8)
r = |a|, θ = π (NOT 0). A common slip is to write θ = 0.
w is purely imaginary (e.g. z^n = 4i or −4i)
r = |b|, θ = π/2 for +bi, θ = −π/2 for −bi.
Conjugate pair appears (e.g. roots a±bi of a real polynomial)
Compute r and θ once; the conjugate has the same r and argument −θ — saves work and provides a check.
Question expects exact arguments in terms of π
Use the reference-angle method: find the acute angle arctan(|y/x|) recognising standard values (π/6, π/4, π/3) and place it in the correct quadrant — avoids decimal approximation.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming arctan(y/x) always gives the principal argument, ignoring quadrant adjustment for x < 0.
  • Treating z = 1 (or any real) as having no argument rather than θ = 0.
  • Confusing modulus-argument form with the exponential form and dropping the cos/sin notation when the question explicitly asks for r(cos θ + i sin θ).

Pitfalls

  • Giving θ in degrees when the question's range is stated in terms of π (radians).
  • Producing an argument outside (−π, π], e.g. writing 3π/2 instead of −π/2.
  • Premature rounding of arctan(y/x) — keep extra digits internally, round only at the final answer.
  • Sign error on the argument for points with negative imaginary part (forgetting θ should be negative).

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP1/FP2) under complex numbers and de Moivre's theorem. The formula booklet provides the de Moivre identity (cos θ + i sin θ)^n = cos nθ + i sin nθ but expects students to compute r and θ themselves. Do not invoke the multivalued complex logarithm or branch cuts — stay with the principal argument in (−π, π].
