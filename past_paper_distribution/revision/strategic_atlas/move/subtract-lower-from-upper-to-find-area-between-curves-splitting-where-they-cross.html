Strategic Atlas / Integration / Top Minus Bottom
Integration

Subtract lower from upper to find area between curves, splitting where they cross

Family: Top Minus Bottom Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question asks for the geometric area enclosed between two graphs, or between a curve and a line, on an interval where their relative vertical position may switch. Cues: phrases like 'the area of the region bounded by', 'the shaded region', or a sketch showing two curves intersecting inside the interval. If you can identify intersection points strictly between the endpoints, expect a swap of which function is on top. Also fires when the integrand could plausibly be negative on part of the interval.

Motivation — Why this works

The definite integral $\int_a^b [f(x)-g(x)]\,dx$ measures signed area: positive where $f>g$ and negative where $f<g$. Geometric (unsigned) area requires the integrand to be non-negative, which forces the order to be (top) $-$ (bottom) on each sub-interval. Splitting at intersection points lets us pick the correct order piecewise so the contributions add rather than cancel. This is more reliable than integrating once and taking $|\cdot|$, which can hide sign errors and fails when there are multiple sign changes.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the two graphs intersect inside the integration interval, so 'top' and 'bottom' are not fixed throughout.
  2. Rule out a single integral $\int_a^b (f-g)\,dx$: the signs of the two pieces would partially cancel, giving a wrong (signed) total.
  3. Commit to splitting the interval at every intersection point and writing one integral per sub-interval.
  4. Test a sample $x$-value inside each sub-interval to decide which function is larger there; that fixes the subtraction order.
  5. Write each integrand as (top) $-$ (bottom), distribute carefully, and check it is $\geq 0$ on its sub-interval before integrating.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Solve $f(x) = g(x)$ to find all intersection points in $[a,b]$; these are the split points.
  2. On each sub-interval, evaluate $f$ and $g$ at a convenient interior point to decide which is on top.
  3. Write the area as a sum of integrals: $\int (\text{top} - \text{bottom})\,dx$ on each piece.
  4. Simplify each integrand by distributing the minus sign carefully, then integrate term-by-term.
  5. Evaluate using the limits and add the (now non-negative) contributions to get the total area.

Worked Micro-Example

Region between $y = x^2 - 4x + 5$ and $y = 2$ on $[0,3]$; they meet at $x=1$ and $x=3$. On $[0,1]$ curve is above line, on $[1,3]$ line is above curve. Area $= \int_0^1 (x^2-4x+3)\,dx + \int_1^3 (-x^2+4x-3)\,dx = \tfrac{4}{3} + \tfrac{4}{3} = \tfrac{8}{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two curves with no swap on $[a,b]$
A single integral $\int_a^b (\text{top}-\text{bottom})\,dx$ suffices; still verify orientation with a test point.
Three or more intersection points inside $[a,b]$
Split at every root; the top/bottom may alternate, so determine the order separately on each piece.
Region bounded by a curve and the $x$-axis where the curve dips below
Treat $y=0$ as the second function; split at the roots and use $|\int|$ on negative pieces, or equivalently $\int (0 - f)$ there.
Area expressed as a function of $y$ (curves given as $x = h(y)$)
Use (right) $-$ (left) and integrate with respect to $y$; same logic of splitting at $y$-intersections.
One curve and a tangent/chord line forming a closed region
Intersection points are the limits themselves; only one integral needed, but still check which is on top.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming one function is on top across the entire interval and using a single subtraction order throughout.
  • Treating the intersection point as merely a feature of the sketch rather than as the required split point for the integral.
  • Confusing 'signed area' (value of the integral) with 'geometric area' (what the question asks for).

Pitfalls

  • Reversing the subtraction and getting a negative answer, then taking $|\cdot|$ without recognising the structural sign issue.
  • Failing to distribute the minus sign when expanding $b - (ax^2+bx+c)$, especially flipping the sign of the linear term.
  • Forgetting one of the intersection points (e.g. only solving for the positive root) and so missing a sub-interval.
  • Adding the two pieces with the wrong sign, so they cancel instead of combine.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure content (C2/C4): area between a curve and a line, or between two curves, using definite integrals of polynomial or simple integrands. Splitting at intersection points and choosing top$-$bottom is explicitly examinable.
