Coordinate Geometry

Build normal line via negative reciprocal of known tangent gradient

Family: Perpendicularity Gradient Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks for the normal at a point where the tangent gradient has already been (or can easily be) computed. Phrasings include 'find the equation of the normal at P', 'show that the normal at P is …', or 'the normal meets the curve again at Q'. The defining cue is that no new differentiation is needed — only the perpendicularity relation between two lines through the same point.

Motivation — Why this works

Two non-vertical lines are perpendicular iff the product of their gradients is $-1$. Once $m_{\text{tan}}$ is known at $P$, the normal gradient is forced to be $m_{\text{nor}} = -1/m_{\text{tan}}$. Combining this with the same point $P$ in point–slope form gives the normal equation directly, with no re-derivation. This converts a calculus question into a one-line algebraic step.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the word 'normal' and recall it is perpendicular to the tangent at the same contact point P.
  2. Rule out re-differentiating or solving simultaneous equations — the gradient information is already in hand.
  3. Commit to the negative-reciprocal route: $m_{\text{nor}} = -1/m_{\text{tan}}$, valid whenever $m_{\text{tan}}\neq 0$ and is finite.
  4. Bridge: substitute $m_{\text{nor}}$ and the known coordinates of $P$ into $y - y_P = m_{\text{nor}}(x - x_P)$.
  5. Tactically, clear denominators early (multiply by $b\cos\theta$, etc.) to match the target form requested by the question.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Quote $m_{\text{tan}}$ at $P$ (already computed earlier in the question).
  2. Form $m_{\text{nor}} = -1/m_{\text{tan}}$, simplifying the sign and any reciprocal of a fraction.
  3. Write the normal as $y - y_P = m_{\text{nor}}(x - x_P)$ using the same point $P$.
  4. Clear fractions by multiplying through by the denominator of $m_{\text{nor}}$.
  5. Rearrange into the form requested (e.g. $ax\sin\theta - by\cos\theta = (a^2-b^2)\sin\theta\cos\theta$).

Worked Micro-Example

At $P(a\cos\theta, b\sin\theta)$ on $\tfrac{x^2}{a^2}+\tfrac{y^2}{b^2}=1$, the tangent gradient is $m_T=-\tfrac{b\cos\theta}{a\sin\theta}$. Hence $m_N=\tfrac{a\sin\theta}{b\cos\theta}$. The normal is $y-b\sin\theta=\tfrac{a\sin\theta}{b\cos\theta}(x-a\cos\theta)$, which tidies to $ax\sin\theta - by\cos\theta = (a^2-b^2)\sin\theta\cos\theta$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Hyperbola $\tfrac{x^2}{a^2}-\tfrac{y^2}{b^2}=1$ at $(a\sec\theta, b\tan\theta)$
Tangent gradient $\tfrac{b}{a\sin\theta}$ becomes normal gradient $-\tfrac{a\sin\theta}{b}$; same point–slope step.
Parabola $y^2=4ax$ at $(at^2,2at)$
$m_T=1/t$ gives $m_N=-t$; normal becomes $y+tx=2at+at^3$.
Rectangular hyperbola $xy=c^2$ at $(ct,c/t)$
$m_T=-1/t^2$ gives $m_N=t^2$; normal is $y-c/t=t^2(x-ct)$.
General curve $y=f(x)$ at $x=a$
Compute $m_T=f'(a)$ via differentiation, then $m_N=-1/f'(a)$ through $(a,f(a))$.
Implicit curve via $\tfrac{dy}{dx}$
Same rule applies once $\tfrac{dy}{dx}$ at $P$ has been extracted by implicit differentiation.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'normal' as another tangent and reusing $m_{\text{tan}}$ in the line equation.
  • Believing a fresh differentiation is required for the normal.
  • Reading 'normal at P' as 'normal from an external point', which is a different problem.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the minus sign and writing $m_{\text{nor}}=1/m_{\text{tan}}$.
  • Inverting a fraction incorrectly, e.g. $-1\big/\!\left(-\tfrac{b\cos\theta}{a\sin\theta}\right)$ mis-simplified.
  • Failing to handle the special cases $m_{\text{tan}}=0$ (normal is vertical) or undefined tangent (normal is horizontal).
  • Algebraic slips when clearing $b\cos\theta$ across both sides, especially the constant term $a\cos\theta$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (P3/P4) and Further Pure scope: parametric/implicit differentiation supplies $m_{\text{tan}}$, and the perpendicularity criterion $m_1 m_2=-1$ is standard from C1/P1 coordinate geometry.
