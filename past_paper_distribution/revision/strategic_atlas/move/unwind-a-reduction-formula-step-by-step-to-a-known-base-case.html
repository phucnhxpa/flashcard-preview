Sequences & Series

Unwind a reduction formula step-by-step to a known base case

Family: Recursive Evaluation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires once a reduction formula like I_n = f(n)·I_{n-k} has been established and a specific numerical case (e.g. I_6, I_7) is requested. Cues: 'Hence evaluate…', 'Use your reduction formula to find…', or a definite integral with a high integer index. The index gap k (usually 1 or 2) determines whether the chain terminates at I_0, I_1, or some other small index whose value is directly computable.

Motivation — Why this works

The recurrence reduces the index by a fixed amount each time, so finitely many applications reach a base case that can be integrated directly. Composing the recurrence yields a telescoping product of rational factors multiplied by the base value. This is the entire payoff of deriving the reduction formula: turning a hard integral into a chain of arithmetic plus one easy integral. Parity (or residue mod k) of the starting index determines which base case is hit.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the target index n and the step size k of the recurrence (usually k = 2 for trig powers, k = 1 for x^n e^x).
  2. Rule out direct integration of I_n — the whole point is that the recurrence is easier than tackling n directly.
  3. Commit to descending: list the chain n → n−k → n−2k → … until reaching a base index (0 or 1) whose integral is elementary.
  4. Compute each rational coefficient from the formula and the base integral separately, keeping them symbolic to avoid premature arithmetic.
  5. Multiply numerators and denominators in one pass at the end, then simplify the fraction and attach the base value (often π/2 or 1).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the recurrence I_n = c(n)·I_{n−k} and the target index n.
  2. Apply repeatedly: I_n = c(n)·c(n−k)·c(n−2k)·…·I_b, where b ∈ {0,1} is the base index reached.
  3. Compute the base integral I_b directly (e.g. I_0 = ∫_0^{π/2} 1\,dx = π/2, I_1 = ∫_0^{π/2} \sin x\,dx = 1).
  4. Form the product of rational coefficients, keeping numerators and denominators separate.
  5. Multiply by I_b and simplify the resulting fraction to lowest terms.

Worked Micro-Example

Given I_n = \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^n x\,dx satisfies I_n = \tfrac{n-1}{n}I_{n-2}, evaluate I_6. Then I_6 = \tfrac{5}{6}I_4 = \tfrac{5}{6}\cdot\tfrac{3}{4}I_2 = \tfrac{5}{6}\cdot\tfrac{3}{4}\cdot\tfrac{1}{2}I_0. Since I_0 = \pi/2, we get I_6 = \tfrac{15}{48}\cdot\tfrac{\pi}{2} = \tfrac{5\pi}{32}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Odd-index Wallis-type integral I_7 with the same recurrence
Chain terminates at I_1 = 1, so the answer is purely rational: I_7 = (6/7)(4/5)(2/3)·1 = 16/35.
Reduction formula for I_n = ∫ x^n e^x dx with step k = 1
Each step subtracts 1 from the index; chain terminates at I_0 = ∫ e^x dx = e^x + C, producing a polynomial-in-x times e^x.
Recurrence with non-constant step (e.g. I_n = (n-1)I_{n-2} − something)
Same descending strategy but track the additive correction terms; the chain becomes a sum of partial products rather than a single product.
Integrals over [0,1] or general limits where I_0 ≠ π/2
Recompute the base case under the actual limits before multiplying through; the rational coefficients are unchanged.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the recurrence as defining I_n in terms of I_{n−1} when the actual step is k = 2, leading to a wrong base case.
  • Believing the answer must contain π regardless of parity, overlooking that odd indices give purely rational results on [0,π/2].
  • Reading 'Hence' as permission to start from scratch rather than a directive to use the just-derived recurrence.

Pitfalls

  • Confusing even/odd chains and using the wrong base value (I_0 = π/2 vs I_1 = 1).
  • Arithmetic slips when multiplying several fractions — keep numerators and denominators separate until the final simplification.
  • Forgetting that I_0 on [0, π/2] equals π/2, not 1.
  • Stopping one step early (e.g. evaluating down to I_2 and quoting that as the answer).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard FP2/FP3-style reduction-formula evaluation within Edexcel IAL Further Pure; uses only definite integration of elementary functions and arithmetic with rationals.
