Binomial Expansion

Determine over/underestimate by checking sign of omitted binomial tail terms

Family: Truncation Error Analysis Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A binomial-expansion question asks whether a truncated approximation is an overestimate or an underestimate of the exact value. Cues include 'state, with a reason, whether your approximation is an overestimate or underestimate', or computing $(a+bx)^n$ to a given power of $x$ then comparing with the true value at a specific $x$. The approximation was formed by keeping only the first few terms of an expansion (finite for integer $n$, infinite for fractional/negative $n$).

Motivation — Why this works

The exact value equals the partial sum (the approximation) plus the tail (sum of all omitted terms). If every omitted term has the same sign, the sign of the tail is fixed: a positive tail means the approximation is too small (underestimate), a negative tail means it is too large (overestimate). Sign of each omitted term is determined by the signs of the binomial coefficient, the constant base $a^{n-r}$, and the variable base $(bx)^r$.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the approximation comes from truncating an expansion, so error = sum of omitted terms.
  2. Rule out 'we stopped early so it's smaller' as a complete reason — sign of omitted terms must be checked, not assumed.
  3. Commit to inspecting term-by-term sign: examine $\binom{n}{r}$, $a^{n-r}$, and $(bx)^r$ for the omitted $r$ values.
  4. Bridge to operation: substitute the given $x$ value and identify whether $bx$ is positive or negative, then track signs of $(bx)^r$.
  5. Tactically: if all omitted terms share one sign, conclude immediately; if signs alternate (e.g. $bx<0$), check whether the first omitted term dominates the tail to fix the overall sign.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the range of omitted $r$ (e.g. $r=4,\dots,n$ if four terms were kept).
  2. Substitute the numerical $x$ used in the approximation and determine the sign of $bx$.
  3. Check signs of all three factors $\binom{n}{r}$, $a^{n-r}$, $(bx)^r$ for each omitted $r$.
  4. If all omitted terms are positive, state: true value > approximation, so it is an underestimate. If all negative, overestimate.
  5. Write a one-line justification quoting the sign argument (this is the mark-scheme requirement).

Worked Micro-Example

Approximate $(2+\tfrac{x}{8})^{13}$ at $x=0.1$ using terms up to $r=3$. The omitted terms have $r=4,\dots,13$ with $\binom{13}{r}>0$, $2^{13-r}>0$, and $(\tfrac{0.1}{8})^r>0$, so every omitted term is positive. Hence true value $>$ approximation, so the approximation is an underestimate.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Same expansion but with $x<0$ (e.g. evaluating at $x=-0.1$)
Odd-$r$ omitted terms become negative and even-$r$ remain positive; signs alternate. Use the fact that for small $|x|$ the first omitted term dominates the tail, and read off the sign from that leading omitted term.
Generalised binomial $(1+x)^n$ with $n$ fractional or negative, $|x|<1$
Expansion is infinite; argue convergence is given by $|x|<1$, then apply the same sign-of-omitted-terms argument to the (infinite) tail, again often dominated by the first omitted term.
Approximation used to estimate $\sqrt{a+bx}$ or similar via $(1+u)^{1/2}$
Coefficients in the generalised expansion can be negative (e.g. $\binom{1/2}{2}=-\tfrac{1}{8}$); must track sign of each generalised coefficient, not just $\binom{n}{r}>0$.
Two-sided question: compare approximation with exact value computed by calculator
Same sign reasoning supports the conclusion; the numerical comparison only confirms it, it is not itself a 'reason'.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'truncated early' as automatically meaning 'underestimate', without checking the sign of the omitted terms.
  • Swapping over- and under-estimate: positive omitted terms make the approximation too small (underestimate), not too large.
  • Assuming the expansion is infinite when $n$ is a positive integer (here exactly $n+1$ terms), or vice versa.
  • Forgetting that for $x<0$ odd powers flip sign, so the 'all positive' argument fails.

Pitfalls

  • Stating the conclusion without justification — the mark scheme requires an explicit reason about the sign of the omitted terms.
  • Computing only the first omitted term and asserting its sign equals the tail's sign without noting that for small $|x|$ this term dominates.
  • Mis-signing $(bx)^r$ when $b<0$ or $x<0$; bracket carefully before raising to power $r$.
  • Confusing the approximation error (truncation) with rounding error from decimal computation.

A-level Scope Notes

Fits Edexcel IAL P2/P4 binomial expansion: finite expansions for positive integer $n$ and generalised expansions for fractional/negative $n$ with $|x|<1$. No formal remainder theorems are required — a sign-of-omitted-terms argument with a one-line justification is sufficient.
