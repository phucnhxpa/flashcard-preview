Strategic Atlas / Trigonometry / Reflex Angle Complement
Trigonometry

Take 2π minus the interior angle to get the major sector angle

Family: Reflex Angle Complement Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a sector problem asks for the MAJOR sector (area or arc length) but the angle naturally produced — typically from the cosine rule, a right triangle, or arctan/arcsin in a composite figure — is the minor (interior) angle at the centre. Visual cues: the shaded region wraps around the 'outside' of a triangle, kite, or chord; the diagram shows a logo, badge, or curved boundary larger than a semicircle; the interior angle computed is less than π but the desired region clearly exceeds half the disc.

Motivation — Why this works

Angles around a point sum to 2π radians, so the minor and major sector angles at the same vertex are complementary with respect to a full turn. The standard sector formulae A = ½r²θ and s = rθ require θ to be the angle subtended by the arc of THAT sector. Since the triangle's included angle subtends only the minor arc, the reflex angle 2π − θ is what subtends the major arc and must be substituted into both formulae.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the shaded sector in the diagram is the larger piece — it lies outside the triangle, not inside it.
  2. Rule out using the triangle's interior angle θ directly, since ½r²θ would compute the minor sector enclosed by the two radii and the chord.
  3. Commit to the reflex-angle complement because angles at the centre sum to 2π and the two sectors partition the disc.
  4. Bridge to operation: keep θ in radians, in unrounded form if possible, and form α = 2π − θ.
  5. Tactical decision: store θ to full calculator precision before subtracting; only round α at the very end of part (b)/(d).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify θ = interior angle at the centre (in radians) from the triangle.
  2. Compute α = 2π − θ.
  3. Substitute α (not θ) into A = ½r²α for the major sector area.
  4. Substitute α into s = rα for the major arc length.

Worked Micro-Example

A circle has centre $X$, radius $r = 5$. The triangle at $X$ gives interior angle $\theta = 2.2217$ rad. Major sector angle: $\alpha = 2\pi - 2.2217 = 4.0615$ rad. Major sector area: $A = \tfrac{1}{2}(5)^2(4.0615) = 50.77$. Major arc length: $s = 5 \times 4.0615 = 20.31$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Degree-based sector problem
Use α = 360° − θ and the degree forms A = (θ/360)πr², s = (θ/360)·2πr.
Segment area = circle − minor segment
Equivalently, major segment area = πr² − minor segment area; the 2π − θ idea is the angular version of the same complement.
Chord subtending reflex angle in circle theorems (Further Pure)
Same 2π − θ relation; ensure inscribed-angle theorem uses the correct arc.
Perimeter of major sector
Perimeter = 2r + rα with α = 2π − θ; the two straight edges are still radii.

Common Misreadings

  • Substituting the minor angle θ into ½r²θ and reporting it as the major sector area.
  • Treating the triangle's angle at X as already being the major sector angle because the diagram 'looks big'.
  • Confusing the major-sector angle with the exterior angle of the triangle (π − θ instead of 2π − θ).

Pitfalls

  • Premature rounding of θ before subtracting from 2π, compounding error in area and arc length.
  • Mixing radians and degrees — e.g. computing θ in radians then doing 360 − θ.
  • Forgetting to use the SAME α in both the area part and the arc-length part of the question.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics P2 (radian measure, arc length and sector area). No content beyond standard A-level is required.
