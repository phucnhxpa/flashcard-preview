Factor a quadratic with leading coefficient ≠ 1 by splitting the middle term

Family: Quadratic Factorisation Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The problem reduces to a quadratic ax² + bx + c = 0 with a ≠ 1 and integer coefficients, and the question asks for 'exact values' or is a 'show that' (suggesting clean rational roots). A quick check that ac has a factor pair summing to b confirms the quadratic factorises over the integers. Cues: small integer coefficients (often after dividing through by a common factor), context such as Edexcel AP/GP/geometry problems where rational roots are expected, and a discriminant that looks like a perfect square.

Motivation — Why this works

Splitting the middle term reverses the expansion (px + q)(rx + s) = prx² + (ps + qr)x + qs, where ps · qr = pr · qs = ac and ps + qr = b. Finding the factor pair of ac that sums to b therefore unlocks an immediate factorisation by grouping. This bypasses the quadratic formula and completing the square, giving exact rational roots in the cleanest possible form — ideal for 'show that' or 'exact value' questions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the quadratic has integer coefficients with leading coefficient a ≠ 1, and the question expects exact (likely rational) roots, so factorisation is worth attempting before resorting to the quadratic formula.
  2. Rule out the quadratic formula and completing the square as first choices: they work but are heavier, and if ac has a clean factor pair summing to b, factorisation is faster and cleaner.
  3. Compute ac and search systematically for a factor pair summing to b. Pay attention to signs: if c > 0 and b < 0, both factors are negative; if c < 0, the factors have opposite signs.
  4. Once the pair (m, n) with mn = ac and m + n = b is found, commit to splitting the middle term: ax² + mx + nx + c, then factor by grouping.
  5. Tactical check: before factoring, divide through by any common factor of a, b, c (e.g. replace 10r² − 48r + 32 = 0 by 5r² − 24r + 16 = 0) to keep ac small and the search easy.
  6. After factoring, set each bracket to zero, read off the roots, and verify they satisfy any implicit constraints from the original problem (e.g. r > 0, |r| < 1 for a convergent GP, radius² > 0).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the quadratic in standard form ax² + bx + c = 0; divide through by gcd(a,b,c) if > 1.
  2. Compute the product ac and list factor pairs (m, n) with mn = ac.
  3. Select the pair with m + n = b (mind the signs).
  4. Split the middle term: ax² + mx + nx + c.
  5. Group and factor: take a common factor from the first two terms and from the last two, producing a shared bracket.
  6. Write as (px + q)(rx + s) = 0 and solve to get x = −q/p or x = −s/r.
  7. Check both roots in the original equation and against any contextual constraints.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $5k^2 - 17k + 6 = 0$. Here $ac = 30$ and we need a pair summing to $-17$: take $-15$ and $-2$. Split: $5k^2 - 15k - 2k + 6 = 0 \Rightarrow 5k(k-3) - 2(k-3) = 0 \Rightarrow (5k-2)(k-3) = 0$. Hence $k = \tfrac{2}{5}$ or $k = 3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Coefficients share a common factor (e.g. 10r² − 48r + 32 = 0)
Divide through first to get 5r² − 24r + 16 = 0, so ac shrinks from 320 to 80 and the factor search is much easier.
Discriminant is not a perfect square
No integer factor pair exists; abandon factorisation and use the quadratic formula or completing the square.
Direct two-bracket inspection
For small a (e.g. a = 2, 3, 5) write (ax + ?)(x + ?) and adjust the constants from factor pairs of c until the cross-terms produce b — same logic, no explicit splitting step.
Quadratic in a substitution (e.g. in $u = x^2$ or in a common ratio r)
Factor in the substituted variable first, then back-substitute and apply contextual constraints (e.g. |r| < 1 for a convergent geometric series).

Common Misreadings

  • Failing to divide through by a common factor first, making ac unnecessarily large and the factor search harder.
  • Treating a = 1 inspection (just factor pairs of c summing to b) as sufficient when a ≠ 1 — this misses the ac product step.

Pitfalls

  • Choosing the wrong sign combination for the factor pair (e.g. +15 and +2 instead of −15 and −2 when b is negative and c is positive).
  • Grouping error: pulling out the wrong common factor so the two brackets don't match, breaking the factorisation.
  • Forgetting to check both roots against the problem's implicit constraints (e.g. r > 0, r² + 7 > 0, |r| < 1).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 algebra. No formula booklet entry is needed for factorisation; the quadratic formula is the booklet fallback. Avoid generalising to quadratics over non-integer or complex coefficients — for IAL purposes this technique is for integer-coefficient quadratics with rational roots.
