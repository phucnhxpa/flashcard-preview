Matrices

Normalise eigenvectors and assemble orthogonal P with matching D

Family: Orthogonal Diagonalisation Assembly Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question asks to express a symmetric matrix M in the form PDPᵀ (or PᵀMP = D), or to find an orthogonal matrix P that diagonalises M. Cues include the explicit word 'orthogonal' attached to P, or the requirement PᵀP = I. Fires once eigenvalues and a full set of mutually orthogonal eigenvectors have already been obtained.

Motivation — Why this works

Orthogonality of P means its columns form an orthonormal set, so P⁻¹ = Pᵀ and the factorisation M = PDPᵀ is available without inverting any matrix. For a real symmetric M, eigenvectors of distinct eigenvalues are automatically orthogonal, so only normalisation is needed. Each unit eigenvector becomes a column of P, and the corresponding eigenvalue must occupy the matching diagonal position of D for PᵀMP = D to hold.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Student notices the question demands an orthogonal P, not merely any diagonalising matrix.
  2. They rule out leaving eigenvectors unnormalised, since non-unit columns would give PᵀP ≠ I.
  3. They commit to normalising because M is symmetric, so the eigenvectors are already orthogonal — only scaling is missing.
  4. They compute each ‖vᵢ‖ and divide to obtain v̂ᵢ, ready to slot into P.
  5. They fix an ordering for the eigenvalues on D and place each v̂ᵢ in the column matching its λᵢ.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Verify the eigenvectors v₁, v₂, v₃ are pairwise orthogonal (automatic for symmetric M with distinct eigenvalues).
  2. For each vᵢ compute ‖vᵢ‖ = √(v₁² + v₂² + v₃²).
  3. Form v̂ᵢ = vᵢ / ‖vᵢ‖.
  4. Assemble P = (v̂₁ | v̂₂ | v̂₃) with the unit eigenvectors as columns.
  5. Assemble D = diag(λ₁, λ₂, λ₃) with eigenvalues in the column order chosen for P.
  6. State M = PDPᵀ (equivalently PᵀMP = D); a quick check PᵀP = I confirms orthogonality.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose M has eigenpairs (λ₁,v₁)=(1,(1,1,−1)ᵀ), (λ₂,v₂)=(4,(1,−1,0)ᵀ), (λ₃,v₃)=(−2,(1,1,2)ᵀ). Norms: √3, √2, √6. Then $P=\begin{pmatrix}1/\sqrt3 & 1/\sqrt2 & 1/\sqrt6\\ 1/\sqrt3 & -1/\sqrt2 & 1/\sqrt6\\ -1/\sqrt3 & 0 & 2/\sqrt6\end{pmatrix}$ and $D=\operatorname{diag}(1,4,-2)$, giving $M=PDP^{\mathsf T}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Non-symmetric M with distinct real eigenvalues
Drop normalisation; use P = (v₁|v₂|v₃) and write P⁻¹MP = D, computing P⁻¹ explicitly since columns need not be orthogonal.
Symmetric M with a repeated eigenvalue
Within the repeated eigenspace, first apply Gram–Schmidt to the chosen basis vectors, then normalise so that all columns of P are orthonormal.
2×2 symmetric M
Same procedure with two columns; P = (v̂₁|v̂₂), D = diag(λ₁,λ₂), often chosen as a rotation matrix.
Question asks for Mⁿ via diagonalisation
After assembling P and D, use Mⁿ = PDⁿPᵀ; orthogonality makes the inverse step trivial.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'diagonalise' and 'orthogonally diagonalise' as the same task, so omitting the normalisation step.
  • Believing P must be unique — any reordering of columns (with matching D) or sign flip on a column also works.
  • Assuming Gram–Schmidt is needed for distinct eigenvalues; for symmetric M with distinct λ the eigenvectors are already orthogonal.

Pitfalls

  • Mismatching column order: the i-th column of P must correspond to the i-th diagonal entry of D.
  • Forgetting to normalise, leaving P with non-unit columns so that PᵀP ≠ I.
  • Arithmetic slips in ‖v‖, especially squaring negative components incorrectly or omitting a component.
  • Writing M = PᵀDP instead of M = PDPᵀ — the transpose placement matters.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL Further Pure orthogonal diagonalisation of 2×2 and 3×3 real symmetric matrices, where eigenvalues are real and eigenvectors of distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal.
