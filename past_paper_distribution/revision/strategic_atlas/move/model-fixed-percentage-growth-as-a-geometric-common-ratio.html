Sequences & Series

Model fixed percentage growth as a geometric common ratio

Family: Geometric Modelling Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a quantity changes by a fixed percentage per equal time step (year, month, day) — e.g. 'a population grows by p% each year', 'the value depreciates by 8% annually', 'a salary rises by 3% each year'. The key cue is a constant multiplicative rate applied repeatedly to the previous value, producing a sequence of values rather than a one-off calculation. Distinguish from arithmetic growth, which adds a fixed amount each step.

Motivation — Why this works

A constant percentage change means each term equals the previous term multiplied by the same factor (1 + p/100), which is exactly the definition of a geometric sequence with common ratio r. Encoding the percentage as r = 1 + p/100 (or 1 − p/100 for decrease) lets us deploy the standard nth-term formula U_n = ar^n and the geometric series sum S_n = a(1−r^n)/(1−r). This unifies population, compound interest, and depreciation problems under one algebraic framework.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the phrase 'increases/decreases by p% each year' — a repeated proportional change.
  2. Rule out an arithmetic model: the change each year is not a fixed amount, it scales with the current value.
  3. Commit to a geometric model with first term a = starting value and common ratio r = 1 + p/100.
  4. Bridge to operation: write U_n = a r^n and decide carefully what n counts (years elapsed vs year number).
  5. Tactical: for decay use r = 1 − p/100; for cumulative totals (e.g. total earnings over N years) switch to the geometric series sum formula.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the starting value a (population/principal/initial amount at time 0).
  2. Convert the percentage rate to a multiplier: r = 1 + p/100 for growth, r = 1 − p/100 for decay.
  3. Write the model U_n = a r^n, taking care that n = 0 gives the initial value.
  4. Match n to the wording: 'after n years' / 'at the end of year n' usually means exponent n; 'in the nth year' may mean exponent n − 1.
  5. For totals across multiple periods, apply S_n = a(1 − r^n)/(1 − r).

Worked Micro-Example

A colony of 30000 grows by 4% each year. Then $r = 1.04$ and the population after $n$ years is $U_n = 30000 \times 1.04^n$. After 10 years: $U_{10} = 30000 \times 1.04^{10} \approx 44407$. To find when it first exceeds 50000, solve $1.04^n > 5/3$, giving $n > \log(5/3)/\log(1.04) \approx 13.02$, so $n = 14$ years.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Depreciation (a car loses 15% of its value each year)
Use r = 1 − 0.15 = 0.85; same formula U_n = a r^n, with 0 < r < 1.
Compound interest on savings
A = P(1 + r/100)^n; identical structure with P as first term.
Annual salary rising by p% with total earnings asked
Switch from nth-term to sum: total = a(1 − r^n)/(1 − r).
Growth per month/day instead of year
Reinterpret n as number of those periods; the ratio per period must match the period of the percentage.
Long-run limit when |r| < 1 (e.g. recurring doses, decaying amount)
Apply sum to infinity S_∞ = a/(1 − r) when convergence is justified.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 'after 3 years' as exponent 2 instead of 3 (off-by-one in indexing).
  • Taking r = p/100 rather than 1 + p/100, dropping the retained original amount.
  • Treating a constant percentage change as arithmetic and adding p% of the original each year.
  • Confusing 'in the nth year' (exponent n − 1) with 'after n years' (exponent n).

Pitfalls

  • Mismatching the time index between question wording (start vs end of year) and the formula's n.
  • Mixing units: applying an annual rate over a monthly period without adjusting r.
  • When solving r^n > k by logs with 0 < r < 1, forgetting to reverse the inequality.
  • Using nth-term formula when the question actually asks for the cumulative total (or vice versa).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P2 sequences and series): geometric sequences, nth term ar^(n−1) or ar^n, finite sum, and sum to infinity for |r| < 1. No calculus or continuous exponential modelling required.
