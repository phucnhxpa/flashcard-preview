Differentiation

Apply the product rule when a term is two x-dependent factors multiplied

Family: Product Rule Selection Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a single term in the function (or equation) is structurally u(x)·v(x), where BOTH factors genuinely depend on x and neither is a constant coefficient. Visual cues include: a power of x times a surd or exponential (e.g. x³√(4x−1), x²eˣ), an x times a y in an implicit equation (e.g. xy, x²y³), or two transcendental functions multiplied (e.g. eˣ sin x, y·ln x). Equivalently: 'I see two distinct x-dependent things juxtaposed by multiplication, with no obvious algebraic simplification that removes the product.' If the term cannot be expanded, factored away, or rewritten as a single composite, the product rule is the correct tool.

Motivation — Why this works

The product rule d/dx(uv) = u'v + uv' is the unique correct way to differentiate a product of two non-constant functions of x — it captures contributions from BOTH factors changing simultaneously. In implicit-differentiation terms like xy or ye^{−2x}, it is also the gateway that produces the dy/dx term we will eventually solve for. Skipping the product rule (e.g. treating one factor as a constant) systematically loses an entire term of the derivative, leading to a wrong final answer. The rule converts one hard differentiation into two simpler ones that can each be tackled by power, chain, or standard derivatives.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the term in question is two x-dependent objects multiplied together — e.g. x·y, x³·√(4x−1), or y·e^{−2x} — and that no algebraic move (expansion, cancellation, log-laws) cleanly removes the product.
  2. Rule out alternatives: it isn't a single composite (so chain rule alone won't do), it isn't a quotient, and although logarithmic differentiation could work it is overkill and introduces fractions; for implicit terms, treating y as a constant is illegal because y = y(x).
  3. Commit to the product rule because it is the minimal-cost tool that respects both factors' dependence on x and, in implicit cases, automatically generates the dy/dx contribution we need.
  4. Bridge to execution: assign u to the algebraically simpler / more explicit factor and v to the composite or implicit factor, so that du/dx is trivial and any chain-rule work is localised inside dv/dx.
  5. Tactical check: confirm du/dx and dv/dx individually (chain rule on exponentials like e^{−2x} → −2e^{−2x}; implicit factor y → dy/dx), then assemble u'v + uv' carefully, keeping every term.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the product term and label u and v, e.g. u = x³, v = (4x−1)^{1/2}; or u = x, v = y; or u = y, v = e^{−2x}.
  2. Differentiate each factor separately: compute du/dx (power rule or '1' or 'dy/dx') and dv/dx (chain rule where needed).
  3. Substitute into d/dx(uv) = u·(dv/dx) + v·(du/dx), being careful with signs from chain-rule factors.
  4. Simplify and, in implicit problems, keep dy/dx terms visible so they can be collected later when solving for dy/dx.

Worked Micro-Example

Differentiate $xy$ implicitly with respect to $x$. Set $u=x$, $v=y$, so $\tfrac{du}{dx}=1$ and $\tfrac{dv}{dx}=\tfrac{dy}{dx}$. Then $\tfrac{d}{dx}(xy) = x\cdot\tfrac{dy}{dx} + y\cdot 1 = x\tfrac{dy}{dx} + y$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Explicit product like y = x³√(4x−1)
Pick u = x³ (power rule) and v = (4x−1)^{1/2} (chain rule inside dv/dx); apply u'v + uv' directly.
Implicit term like ye^{−2x} or x·sin(y)
One factor's derivative carries a dy/dx (since y = y(x)); the product rule output will contain a dy/dx term to be collected later.
Both factors need chain/power rules, e.g. x²y³
Apply product rule first, then chain rule inside each piece: d/dx(x²y³) = 2x·y³ + x²·3y²·dy/dx.
Two transcendentals multiplied, e.g. eˣ sin x
Same template with u = eˣ, v = sin x, giving eˣ sin x + eˣ cos x.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating an implicit factor y as if it were a constant coefficient (so d/dx(xy) is wrongly written as just y), losing the x·dy/dx term.
  • Seeing x³√(4x−1) and trying to apply the chain rule to the whole thing as if it were a single composite, ignoring the multiplicative structure.
  • Attempting to absorb x³ into the surd as a single power of (4x−1) — algebraically invalid.

Pitfalls

  • Sign/coefficient errors from the chain rule inside dv/dx, e.g. forgetting the −2 in d/dx(e^{−2x}) = −2e^{−2x}.
  • Computing only u·(dv/dx) and forgetting the v·(du/dx) term (or vice versa).
  • Writing d/dx(xy) = x·y·dy/dx, which conflates product rule with chain rule.
  • After applying the rule, failing to keep dy/dx terms grouped, making the later 'solve for dy/dx' step error-prone.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 scope: the product rule is in the formula booklet as d/dx(uv) = u(dv/dx) + v(du/dx), and combines naturally with the chain rule and implicit differentiation. Avoid generalising to vector-valued or multivariable products — those are out of scope.
