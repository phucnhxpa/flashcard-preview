Trigonometry

Convert tan (or cot/sec) into sin/cos to homogenize the equation

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 7 questions

Trigger — When to use

The equation or identity mixes tan θ (or cot θ, sec θ, cosec θ) with sin θ and/or cos θ of the same angle, so two different trig ratios appear with no single identity directly linking them. Typical surface cues: a tan θ multiplied by, divided by, or set equal to an expression in cos θ; a target RHS that is a polynomial in sin θ alone (or a single fraction with denominator sin θ cos θ); compound-angle versions like tan(θ+α) appearing alongside cos(θ+α). Equivalent phrasings: 'mixed ratios of the same angle', 'need a common trig currency', 'must reduce to one trig function before applying a Pythagorean identity'.

Motivation — Why this works

Rewriting tan θ = sin θ / cos θ (and analogously cot = cos/sin, sec = 1/cos, cosec = 1/sin) re-expresses every term in the primitive currencies sin and cos, which are linked by the Pythagorean identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1. Once homogenized, multiplying through by the offending denominator clears the fraction, leaving a polynomial relationship in sin and cos that collapses to a single variable via Pythagoras. This is mathematically cleaner than squaring (which introduces spurious solutions via 1+tan²=sec²) or using compound-angle expansions (which over-complicate). It is the standard gateway to forming a quadratic in sin θ or cos θ, or to proving an identity by recombining fractions over a common denominator sin θ cos θ.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the equation/identity contains two or more different trig ratios of the same angle (e.g. tan θ alongside cos θ, or tan θ and 1/tan θ alongside sin θ cos θ).
  2. Identify the target form: a quadratic in one trig function, or a single combined fraction. This dictates that all terms must share a common representation.
  3. Rule out alternatives: squaring both sides (introduces spurious roots and a sec² term that still needs converting); rewriting cos in terms of tan (produces sec, complicating things); compound-angle expansion of tan(θ+α) (over-engineered).
  4. Commit to tan θ = sin θ / cos θ because sin and cos are the primitives connected by sin²+cos²=1, and any cos θ already present in the equation will cancel the new denominator.
  5. Tactical step: after substitution, multiply through by cos θ (or sin θ cos θ) to clear fractions. Justify that this does not lose solutions: cos θ = 0 makes tan θ undefined, so it cannot satisfy the original equation.
  6. Apply sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 to eliminate the remaining squared term, producing a polynomial in a single trig function ready to factor or solve.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Substitute tan θ = sin θ / cos θ (and/or cot θ = cos θ / sin θ, sec θ = 1/cos θ, cosec θ = 1/sin θ) throughout the equation.
  2. Multiply both sides by the denominator (typically cos θ, or sin θ cos θ if both appear) to clear fractions.
  3. State the domain restriction (e.g. cos θ ≠ 0) and verify it doesn't lose solutions of the original.
  4. Use sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 to replace cos²θ with 1 − sin²θ (or vice versa), reducing to a single trig function.
  5. Solve the resulting polynomial (usually a quadratic) for that trig function, then for θ.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $8\tan\theta = 3\cos\theta$. Substitute: $8\,\dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} = 3\cos\theta$. Multiply by $\cos\theta$ (valid since $\cos\theta=0$ makes LHS undefined): $8\sin\theta = 3\cos^2\theta = 3(1-\sin^2\theta)$. Rearrange: $3\sin^2\theta + 8\sin\theta - 3 = 0$, giving $\sin\theta = \tfrac{1}{3}$ (rejecting $\sin\theta=-3$).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation mixes cot θ with sin θ
Write cot θ = cos θ / sin θ and multiply through by sin θ; check sin θ ≠ 0 (cot would be undefined).
Equation mixes sec θ with sin θ (e.g. a sec θ = b sin θ)
Write sec θ = 1/cos θ, multiply by cos θ to obtain a sin·cos product, then use double-angle or Pythagoras as needed.
Compound-angle versions, e.g. tan(θ+α) = k cos(θ+α)
Treat (θ+α) as a single block: substitute tan(θ+α) = sin(θ+α)/cos(θ+α) and multiply by cos(θ+α). Solve for (θ+α) first, then subtract α.
Identity proof with tan θ + 1/tan θ on LHS
Convert both terms to sin/cos, take common denominator sin θ cos θ; numerator becomes sin²θ + cos²θ = 1.
Bracketed expression like cos θ (3cos θ − tan θ)
Substitute tan θ = sin θ / cos θ inside the bracket; the outer cos θ cancels the new denominator immediately, leaving 3cos²θ − sin θ.

Common Misreadings

  • Squaring both sides immediately to invoke 1 + tan²θ = sec²θ — works but introduces spurious solutions and rarely produces a clean quadratic in sin θ.
  • Expanding the bracket before substituting, leaving a tan θ · cos θ term that is not simplified to sin θ.
  • Using the compound-angle expansion of tan(θ+α) when the angle is already a single block — massively over-complicates the problem.
  • Trying to convert cos θ into tan/sec form rather than tan into sin/cos — produces a messier sec-laden equation.

Pitfalls

  • Multiplying by cos θ (or sin θ) without justifying that this cannot lose solutions — examiners require the explicit note that cos θ = 0 ⇒ tan θ undefined.
  • Forgetting the implicit domain restriction cos θ ≠ 0 when later listing solutions in [0°, 360°).
  • Cross-multiplying carelessly when both sides are fractions, leading to sign errors.
  • Failing to apply sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 after homogenizing, and instead trying to solve a mixed sin–cos² equation directly.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 trigonometry. Uses the definition tan θ = sin θ / cos θ (not in formula booklet — assumed knowledge) and the Pythagorean identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1. Avoid invoking t = tan(θ/2) Weierstrass substitutions or non-syllabus identities; stay with the three primary Pythagorean identities and reciprocal definitions.
