Coordinate Geometry

Pick the axis-aligned side as base for triangle area

Family: Strategic Base Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when computing the area of a triangle whose vertices have known coordinates and at least two vertices share a coordinate value (both on the x-axis, both on the y-axis, or both on a common horizontal/vertical line). Cue phrases: 'find the area of triangle OPN', 'P, Q lie on the x-axis', or one vertex is the origin and another lies on an axis. Recognise it whenever two of the three points have y = 0 (or x = 0, or any common constant).

Motivation — Why this works

Area = ½ × base × height collapses to a one-line calculation when the base lies along (or parallel to) a coordinate axis: the base length is a single coordinate difference, and the perpendicular height is just the absolute value of the third vertex's other coordinate (or its distance to the shared line). This avoids the shoelace/determinant formula and reduces the chance of sign or arithmetic errors. It is the cheapest route whenever the geometry hands you a free axis-aligned side.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that two of the three vertices share a coordinate (e.g. both have y = 0), so one side of the triangle is horizontal/vertical.
  2. Rules out the shoelace formula and the distance-formula-then-perpendicular approach as unnecessarily heavy for this configuration.
  3. Commits to taking the axis-aligned side as the base because both base length and perpendicular height become single-coordinate reads.
  4. Bridges to computation: write base = |x-difference| (or |y-difference|), and height = |y-coordinate| (or |x-coordinate|) of the off-axis vertex.
  5. Decides on signs/absolute values up front and plans the algebraic expansion of ½ × base × height carefully to avoid distribution slips.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two vertices sharing an axis (or a common horizontal/vertical line) and take the segment between them as the base.
  2. Compute base length as the absolute difference of the non-shared coordinates of those two vertices.
  3. Compute height as the perpendicular distance from the third vertex to that line — usually a single coordinate or |coord − constant|.
  4. Apply Area = ½ × base × height.
  5. Expand and simplify, keeping surds and π in exact form.

Worked Micro-Example

Triangle $OPN$ with $O=(0,0)$, $N=\left(2+\tfrac{\pi\sqrt{3}}{18},\,0\right)$, $P=\left(2,\tfrac{\pi}{3}\right)$. Both $O$ and $N$ lie on the x-axis, so take $ON$ as base: $|ON| = 2+\tfrac{\pi\sqrt{3}}{18}$. Height = y-coordinate of $P$ = $\tfrac{\pi}{3}$. Area $= \tfrac{1}{2}\left(2+\tfrac{\pi\sqrt{3}}{18}\right)\cdot\tfrac{\pi}{3} = \tfrac{\pi}{3}+\tfrac{\pi^{2}\sqrt{3}}{108}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two vertices share the y-axis (x = 0)
Take that vertical side as base; height is then |x-coordinate| of the third vertex.
Two vertices lie on a common horizontal line y = k (not the x-axis)
Base length = |x-difference|; height = |y₃ − k| for the third vertex.
Two vertices lie on a common vertical line x = h
Base length = |y-difference|; height = |x₃ − h|.
No two vertices are collinear with an axis or simple line
Fall back to the shoelace formula ½|x₁(y₂−y₃)+x₂(y₃−y₁)+x₃(y₁−y₂)| or to ½|AB||AC|sin θ.
Triangle in 3D with vertices given as position vectors
Use ½|\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}| where a, b are two edge vectors from a chosen vertex; the axis-aligned trick generally does not apply.

Common Misreadings

  • Choosing OP or PN as the base because they are listed first, then doing distance formula plus perpendicular-from-point-to-line work unnecessarily.
  • Failing to spot that 'on the x-axis' means y = 0 for both points, and so missing that one side is already horizontal.
  • Treating the height as the distance |OP| or |PN| rather than the perpendicular distance to the chosen base.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the ½ factor in Area = ½ × base × height.
  • Dropping absolute value signs when one coordinate could in principle be negative.
  • Distribution errors when multiplying ½ × (sum of two terms) × (single factor); expand term-by-term.
  • Mixing exact form and decimals — keep π and surds symbolic to the end.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure content: triangle area via ½ × base × height with coordinate geometry, fully within P1/P2 scope and compatible with later use alongside ½|AB||AC|sin C in P3.
