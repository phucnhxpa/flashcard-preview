Integration

Convert Cartesian volume of revolution to parametric integral via dx = (dx/dθ)dθ

Family: Parametric Integration Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The curve is given parametrically as x = f(θ), y = g(θ) (or x = f(t), y = g(t)), and a volume of revolution about the x-axis is required. The standard formula V = ∫πy² dx cannot be applied directly because y is not expressed as a function of x. Cues include explicit parametric equations, integration limits given in θ (or t), and y² being a clean trig expression that suggests a θ-integration is intended.

Motivation — Why this works

Since x and y are both functions of a single parameter, the chain rule gives dx = (dx/dθ)dθ, allowing the entire integrand to be rewritten in terms of θ. This converts ∫πy² dx into ∫πy²(dx/dθ)dθ, a single-variable integral in θ. The factor dx/dθ often simplifies the integrand by combining productively with y² (e.g. sec²θ pairing with sin² terms), making evaluation tractable via standard trig integration techniques.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the curve is parametric: x and y are both given in terms of θ, so y is not directly a function of x.
  2. Rule out separating y as an explicit function of x — inverting x = tanθ to write y in terms of x would be messy and defeat the parametric form.
  3. Commit to the parametric volume formula V = ∫πy²(dx/dθ)dθ, with limits expressed in θ.
  4. Compute dx/dθ from the given x(θ) and square y(θ), then assemble the θ-integrand.
  5. Inspect the resulting trig expression for likely simplifications (double-angle, identities) before integrating.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write V = ∫πy²(dx/dθ)dθ with θ-limits matching the original x-limits.
  2. Differentiate x(θ) to obtain dx/dθ (e.g. x = tanθ ⇒ dx/dθ = sec²θ).
  3. Square y(θ): y² = (2sin2θ)² = 4sin²2θ.
  4. Assemble the integrand: V = ∫ 4π sin²2θ · sec²θ dθ.
  5. Simplify using trig identities (e.g. sin2θ = 2sinθcosθ) before integrating.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $x = \tan\theta$, $y = 2\sin 2\theta$, find the volume of revolution about the $x$-axis for $\theta \in [0, \tfrac{\pi}{4}]$. Then $\frac{dx}{d\theta} = \sec^2\theta$ and $y^2 = 4\sin^2 2\theta = 16\sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta$. So $V = \int_0^{\pi/4} \pi \cdot 16\sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta \cdot \sec^2\theta\, d\theta = 16\pi\int_0^{\pi/4}\sin^2\theta\, d\theta = 16\pi \cdot \tfrac{1}{2}\left[\theta - \tfrac{\sin 2\theta}{2}\right]_0^{\pi/4} = 2\pi(\pi - 2)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Volume of revolution about the y-axis with parametric curve
Use V = ∫πx²(dy/dθ)dθ; differentiate y(θ) instead and square x(θ).
Area under a parametric curve
Use A = ∫y(dx/dθ)dθ — same chain-rule conversion, but linear in y instead of y².
Arc length of a parametric curve
Use s = ∫√((dx/dθ)² + (dy/dθ)²) dθ; both derivatives are required and squared.
Surface area of revolution about x-axis (Further Maths)
Use S = ∫2πy√((dx/dθ)² + (dy/dθ)²) dθ; same parametric philosophy with an arc-length element.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the integral as ∫πy² dθ, omitting the dx/dθ factor.
  • Trying to eliminate the parameter to get y as a function of x, which is unnecessary and often algebraically painful.
  • Using the original x-limits instead of converting them to θ-limits.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the dx/dθ factor entirely, producing ∫πy² dθ.
  • Differentiating x = tanθ incorrectly (e.g. writing secθ or secθtanθ instead of sec²θ).
  • Failing to convert the integration limits from x-values to θ-values.
  • Squaring y incorrectly, e.g. writing (2sin2θ)² as 2sin²2θ instead of 4sin²2θ.
