Trigonometry

Cascade double-angle identities from known sin²φ to evaluate sin 4φ exactly

Family: Exact Evaluation Cascade Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

An exact answer requires sin nφ (or cos nφ) where φ is defined implicitly, e.g. φ = arctan(1/√2), and is not a standard angle. The problem demands surd/rational form, so numerical evaluation of φ is forbidden. Typical cues: 'give your answer in exact form', 'show that A = p arctan(...) + q', or sin²φ / tan φ being directly available from an earlier part.

Motivation — Why this works

Double-angle identities sin 2θ = 2 sin θ cos θ and cos 2θ = 1 − 2sin²θ let us climb from sin²φ to sin 2φ, cos 2φ, then to sin 4φ without ever isolating φ. Each rung uses only rational arithmetic on the known squared ratio, so exactness is preserved throughout. This is faster and cleaner than expanding sin 4φ directly in powers of sin φ and cos φ.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice sin 4φ is needed exactly, and φ = arctan(1/√2) is not a standard angle.
  2. I rule out computing φ numerically or expanding sin 4φ as a quartic in sin φ — both lose clarity or exactness.
  3. I commit to a double-angle cascade because sin²φ (hence cos²φ) is already known as a clean rational.
  4. First concrete step: extract sin φ and cos φ as surds from sin²φ = 1/3, choosing positive roots since φ is acute.
  5. Then build sin 2φ and cos 2φ in one line each, and finish with sin 4φ = 2 sin 2φ cos 2φ; keep all denominators rational.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. From sin²φ = 1/3 (and φ acute): sin φ = 1/√3, cos φ = √2/√3.
  2. Compute sin 2φ = 2 sin φ cos φ = 2·(1/√3)(√2/√3) = 2√2/3.
  3. Compute cos 2φ = 1 − 2 sin²φ = 1 − 2/3 = 1/3.
  4. Compute sin 4φ = 2 sin 2φ cos 2φ = 2·(2√2/3)·(1/3) = 4√2/9.
  5. Substitute back into the target expression, e.g. A = (9/4)φ − (9/16) sin 4φ = (9/4) arctan(1/√2) − √2/4.

Worked Micro-Example

Given tan φ = 1/√2 with φ acute, find sin 4φ exactly. Then sin²φ = 1/3, cos²φ = 2/3, so sin 2φ = 2·(1/√3)(√2/√3) = 2√2/3 and cos 2φ = 1 − 2/3 = 1/3. Therefore sin 4φ = 2·(2√2/3)·(1/3) = 4√2/9.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Need cos 4φ exactly from the same φ
Use cos 4φ = 2 cos²2φ − 1 = 2(1/9) − 1 = −7/9, bypassing sin entirely after cos 2φ is found.
Need sin 3φ or cos 3φ from known sin φ
Use the triple-angle forms sin 3φ = 3 sin φ − 4 sin³φ and cos 3φ = 4 cos³φ − 3 cos φ directly on the surd values.
φ given via cos²φ = k or tan φ = m instead of sin²φ
Convert to sin φ and cos φ as surds first using 1 + tan²φ = sec²φ, then run the same double-angle cascade.
Integral bound from area produces sin 4φ + linear φ term
Evaluate the φ-term as arctan(...) and the sin 4φ term via the cascade, then combine into the required p arctan(...) + q form.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating arctan(1/√2) as a standard angle and trying to look up sin 4φ from a table.
  • Thinking sin 4φ requires expanding (sin φ + cos φ)⁴ or a quartic identity rather than two double-angle steps.
  • Believing exact evaluation is impossible and reverting to a decimal answer.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error in cos 2φ: writing 2 sin²φ − 1 instead of 1 − 2 sin²φ (these differ in sign).
  • Choosing the wrong sign for sin φ or cos φ when φ is not specified to be acute — always check the quadrant.
  • Rationalising errors when squaring √2/√3 type expressions; keep denominators consistent.
  • Forgetting to apply the outer coefficient (e.g. (9/16)·sin 4φ) when substituting back into the area/integral expression.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the standard double-angle identities sin 2θ = 2 sin θ cos θ and cos 2θ = 1 − 2 sin²θ from Edexcel IAL P3/P4 trigonometry, applied twice. No compound-angle theorems beyond syllabus are required.
