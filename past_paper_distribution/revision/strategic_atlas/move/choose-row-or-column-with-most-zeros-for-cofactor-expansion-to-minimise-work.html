Matrices

Choose row or column with most zeros for cofactor expansion to minimise work

Family: Cofactor Expansion Choice Frequency: 5 questions

Trigger — When to use

A 3×3 (or larger) determinant must be evaluated — typically det(A − λI) for a characteristic polynomial, or det(M) for a singularity/eigenvalue condition. Before expanding, scan every row and every column for zeros or simple entries. If one row/column contains two zeros, the determinant collapses to a single 2×2 minor; if it contains one zero, you save one 2×2 calculation. The cue is any visible 0 in A − λI (often produced by an off-diagonal zero in the original matrix surviving the λI subtraction).

Motivation — Why this works

The value of a determinant is independent of the row or column chosen for cofactor expansion, but the algebraic effort is not. Each zero entry kills its corresponding cofactor term, removing a full 2×2 minor from the computation. Expanding along the row/column with the most zeros (or simplest entries) is therefore a deliberate complexity-reduction choice that cuts arithmetic and reduces sign-error risk. The underlying identity is the Laplace expansion: det(A) = Σⱼ (−1)^{i+j} a_{ij} M_{ij} along any fixed row i (or analogously a column).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that a 3×3 determinant is required — typically from det(A − λI) = 0 for eigenvalues, or det(M) for a parameter condition.
  2. Rule out shortcuts: the matrix is not triangular (so I can't just multiply diagonal entries), and it's not obviously block-structured.
  3. Scan every row and every column systematically for zeros, counting them; if no zeros exist, look instead for the row/column with the smallest or most repeated entries.
  4. Commit to the row or column with the most zeros — each zero eliminates an entire 2×2 cofactor, so two zeros reduce the work to a single 2×2 determinant.
  5. Set up the expansion explicitly with the checkerboard sign (−1)^{i+j} attached to each surviving term, being especially careful at (1,2), (2,1), (2,3), (3,2) where the sign is negative.
  6. If no row/column has zeros, default to row 1 (the conventional choice) and accept three 2×2 minors; alternatively, perform a row operation first to create a zero, tracking any sign or scaling effects.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Form the matrix whose determinant is needed (e.g. A − λI).
  2. Identify the row i (or column j) containing the most zero entries; call it the pivot row/column.
  3. Apply Laplace expansion along it: det = Σⱼ (−1)^{i+j} · a_{ij} · M_{ij}, dropping all terms where a_{ij} = 0.
  4. For each surviving term, form the 2×2 minor by deleting row i and column j, and evaluate it as ad − bc.
  5. Multiply each 2×2 minor by its entry a_{ij} and the sign (−1)^{i+j}, then sum to obtain the determinant (a polynomial in λ or the parameter).

Worked Micro-Example

Find det(M − 3I) where M − 3I = \(\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 4 \\ 0 & k^2-3 & 0 \\ 5 & 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\). Row 2 has two zeros, so expand along it: det = (−1)^{2+2}(k²−3) · \(\det\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 4 \\ 5 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\) = (k²−3)(2 − 20) = −18(k²−3). One 2×2 minor instead of three.

Variants — Same move, different surface

No zeros anywhere in the matrix
Default to expanding along row 1, computing all three 2×2 minors with the +, −, + sign pattern.
Matrix is upper or lower triangular after λI subtraction
Skip cofactor expansion entirely — det = product of diagonal entries.
Block-diagonal structure visible
Use det = det(block₁) × det(block₂) instead of full expansion.
No zeros but a row/column has very simple integer entries
Expand along that row/column to keep arithmetic manageable, even though all three minors survive.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating row 1 as automatic without scanning for zeros, leading to unnecessary algebra.
  • Mistaking a small entry (like 1) for being as helpful as a zero — only a zero kills a cofactor term.
  • Assuming row operations on a determinant are free; they can introduce sign changes (row swaps) or scaling factors that must be tracked.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the checkerboard sign (−1)^{i+j} — especially at (1,2), (2,1), (2,3), (3,2). Students often write +a_{12}M_{12} when it should be −a_{12}M_{12}.
  • Deleting the wrong row or column when forming the 2×2 minor, or mixing up which entries belong to the minor.
  • Applying a minus sign to the (2,2) cofactor by reflex, when in fact (−1)^{2+2} = +1.
  • Using row indexing for signs while expanding along a column (or vice versa).

A-level Scope Notes

This is core FP1/FM territory in Edexcel IAL Further Maths: 3×3 determinants and characteristic polynomials for eigenvalue problems. The Laplace/cofactor expansion is standard and appears in the formula booklet implicitly via the 3×3 determinant formula. Avoid generalising to 4×4 cofactor expansion or to abstract multilinear-form arguments — these are out of scope.
