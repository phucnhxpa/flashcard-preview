Read outer-region solution from upward parabola for quadratic > 0

Family: Sign Analysis Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when solving a quadratic inequality of the form $ax^2+bx+c > 0$ (or $\geq 0$) with $a > 0$, once the two real roots have been found (by factorising, formula, or completing the square). Cues include phrases like 'find the set of values of $x$ for which...', a factorised form $(x-\alpha)(x-\beta) > 0$, or a sketch question immediately followed by an inequality. Recognising the upward 'U-shape' is the key visual trigger.

Motivation — Why this works

An upward-opening parabola lies above the x-axis precisely outside its roots and below the x-axis between them, by continuity and the fact it has only two sign changes. So $ax^2+bx+c > 0$ with $a>0$ is solved by the two outer intervals $x<\alpha$ or $x>\beta$. A quick sketch encodes this faster and more reliably than interval test points, and it generalises cleanly to the four cases $(>,<,\geq,\leq)$ by toggling inner/outer and strict/non-strict.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the inequality is a quadratic compared to 0, with a positive leading coefficient, so the graph is a U-shape.
  2. Rule out laborious sign tables or substituting test values into each interval — the sketch gives the answer directly.
  3. Commit to the graphical reading: 'positive' means 'above the x-axis', which for a U-shape is the two outer arms.
  4. Bridge to operation: find the roots $\alpha < \beta$ by factorising or using the quadratic formula, then mark them on the x-axis.
  5. Decide on strictness from the original inequality: $>$ excludes the roots ($x<\alpha$ or $x>\beta$); $\geq$ includes them; and join the two intervals with 'or', never 'and'.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Confirm leading coefficient $a > 0$ so the parabola opens upwards.
  2. Find the roots $\alpha < \beta$ by factorising or applying the quadratic formula.
  3. Sketch the U-shape crossing the x-axis at $\alpha$ and $\beta$.
  4. Identify the regions where $y > 0$: the two outer arms, $x < \alpha$ and $x > \beta$.
  5. Apply strictness: use $<,>$ for $>0$; use $\leq,\geq$ for $\geq 0$.
  6. Write the final answer as $x < \alpha$ or $x > \beta$ (using 'or', or set notation $\{x:x<\alpha\}\cup\{x:x>\beta\}$).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $2x^2+5x-3 > 0$. Factorise: $(2x-1)(x+3) > 0$, giving roots $x=-3$ and $x=\tfrac{1}{2}$. Since the coefficient of $x^2$ is $+2$, the parabola opens upwards, so $y>0$ on the outer regions. Hence $x < -3$ or $x > \tfrac{1}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Same quadratic but inequality $< 0$
Take the inner region instead: $\alpha < x < \beta$.
Negative leading coefficient (downward parabola)
Either multiply through by $-1$ (flipping the inequality) and reapply, or reverse the rule: $>0$ gives the inner region, $<0$ gives the outer regions.
Non-strict inequality $\geq 0$ or $\leq 0$
Include the roots: use $\leq, \geq$ in the final answer.
Repeated root (discriminant $= 0$, $a>0$)
$ax^2+bx+c \geq 0$ holds for all real $x$; $>0$ holds for all $x$ except the repeated root; $<0$ has no solution.
No real roots (discriminant $< 0$, $a>0$)
$ax^2+bx+c > 0$ for all real $x$; $<0$ has no solution.
Quadratic in disguise, e.g. $x^4-5x^2+4>0$
Substitute $u=x^2$, solve the $u$-inequality by this move, then translate back to $x$.

Common Misreadings

  • Reporting the inner region $\alpha < x < \beta$ when $>0$ was asked (confusing 'above the axis' with 'between the roots').
  • Writing 'x < \alpha and x > \beta' with 'and' rather than 'or', which describes the empty set.
  • Forgetting to check the sign of $a$ and applying the upward-parabola rule to a downward parabola.

Pitfalls

  • Writing a chained inequality such as $\beta < x < \alpha$ or $\alpha > x > \beta$, which is meaningless.
  • Mishandling strictness: using $\leq, \geq$ for a strict inequality or vice versa.
  • Dividing the inequality by a variable expression (which may be negative) instead of moving everything to one side and factorising.
  • Mis-ordering the roots so the 'outer' intervals are written incorrectly.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure 1 quadratic-inequality technique; the sketch-based reading is the expected method and pairs naturally with discriminant analysis and curve-sketching questions in P1/P2.
