Trigonometry

Expand squared linear combination and collapse via Pythagorean identity to homogeneous form

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation has the form $(a\sin\theta + b\cos\theta)^2 = c$ where $c$ is a constant (often $c = a^2 + b^2$ or simply $1$ when $a^2+b^2$ exceeds it by a clean amount). The squared linear combination is a strong cue: expansion produces $\sin^2\theta$, $\cos^2\theta$, and a $\sin\theta\cos\theta$ cross term. When the squared-term coefficients differ, splitting off a copy of $\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta$ collapses the constants. The signature outcome is a homogeneous degree-2 equation in $\sin\theta,\cos\theta$ that factors.

Motivation — Why this works

Expansion plus $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$ removes the constant on both sides, leaving only $\sin^2\theta$ and $\sin\theta\cos\theta$ (or $\cos^2\theta$ and $\sin\theta\cos\theta$) terms — a homogeneous expression that factors as $\sin\theta(\,k\sin\theta - m\cos\theta\,) = 0$. This bypasses the $R\sin(\theta\pm\alpha)$ machinery and yields exact rational $\tan\theta$ values directly. Homogeneous trig equations are a standard A-level workhorse: dividing by $\cos^2\theta$ converts them to a quadratic in $\tan\theta$, or one can factor out a common $\sin\theta$ or $\cos\theta$.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structure $(a\sin\theta - b\cos\theta)^2 = \text{const}$ — a squared linear combination set equal to a constant.
  2. Rule out square-rooting to get $a\sin\theta - b\cos\theta = \pm\sqrt{\text{const}}$ followed by two $R$-formula solves; that doubles the work and forces auxiliary angles.
  3. Commit to expanding because the cross term $-2ab\sin\theta\cos\theta$ and the squared terms will interact cleanly with the Pythagorean identity.
  4. Bridge: after expansion, regroup the squared-terms by peeling off one copy of $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta$ to absorb the constant on the RHS.
  5. Finalise by factoring the resulting homogeneous equation — pull out $\sin\theta$ or $\cos\theta$, or divide by $\cos^2\theta$ to get a quadratic in $\tan\theta$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Expand the square: $(a\sin\theta - b\cos\theta)^2 = a^2\sin^2\theta - 2ab\sin\theta\cos\theta + b^2\cos^2\theta$.
  2. Set equal to the RHS constant and regroup so one copy of $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta$ is visible.
  3. Replace $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta$ by $1$ to cancel constants on both sides.
  4. Obtain a homogeneous equation in $\sin\theta,\cos\theta$ (only $\sin^2\theta$, $\cos^2\theta$, $\sin\theta\cos\theta$ terms).
  5. Factor out the common trig factor, or divide through by $\cos^2\theta$ to get a quadratic in $\tan\theta$, then solve.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $(2\sin\theta - \cos\theta)^2 = 1$ for $\theta\in[0,2\pi)$.\\ Expand: $4\sin^2\theta - 4\sin\theta\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$.\\ Regroup: $3\sin^2\theta + (\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta) - 4\sin\theta\cos\theta = 1 \;\Rightarrow\; 3\sin^2\theta - 4\sin\theta\cos\theta = 0$.\\ Factor: $\sin\theta(3\sin\theta - 4\cos\theta) = 0$, giving $\sin\theta = 0$ or $\tan\theta = \tfrac{4}{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$(a\sin\theta + b\cos\theta)^2 = a^2 + b^2$
RHS equals $R^2$; expansion plus Pythagorean identity collapses everything to $2ab\sin\theta\cos\theta = 0$, i.e. $\sin 2\theta = 0$.
$(a\sin\theta + b\cos\theta)^2 = k$ with $k \ne 1$ and $k \ne a^2+b^2$
After expansion, peel off a copy of $\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta$ scaled by $\min(a^2,b^2)$; the residue is non-homogeneous but reduces to a quadratic in $\tan\theta$ after dividing by $\cos^2\theta$.
Equation of the form $a\sin^2\theta + b\sin\theta\cos\theta + c\cos^2\theta = d$
Replace $d$ by $d(\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta)$ on the RHS to make the whole equation homogeneous, then divide by $\cos^2\theta$.
$(\sin\theta + \cos\theta)^2 = k$
Expansion gives $1 + 2\sin\theta\cos\theta = k$, i.e. $\sin 2\theta = k-1$ — a direct double-angle solve.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading the squared bracket as a cue for the $R$-formula instead of expansion.
  • Square-rooting both sides and losing the $\pm$, halving the solution set.
  • Believing the expansion will be 'messy' and avoiding it, when in fact the constants cancel.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error on the cross term: $(a\sin\theta - b\cos\theta)^2$ has $-2ab\sin\theta\cos\theta$, not $+2ab\sin\theta\cos\theta$.
  • Failing to spot that $a^2\sin^2\theta + b^2\cos^2\theta$ can be split as $(a^2-b^2)\sin^2\theta + b^2(\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta)$ (assuming $a^2>b^2$).
  • Dividing by $\cos\theta$ or $\sin\theta$ without recording the corresponding solutions where that factor is zero.
  • Forgetting to list $\sin\theta = 0$ solutions when factoring out $\sin\theta$.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 trigonometry: uses only $\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta=1$, basic factorisation, and the $\tan\theta$ form of homogeneous equations — no techniques beyond the syllabus.
