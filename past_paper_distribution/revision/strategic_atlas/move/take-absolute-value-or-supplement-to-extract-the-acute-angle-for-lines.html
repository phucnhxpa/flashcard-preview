Vectors

Take absolute value or supplement to extract the acute angle for lines

Family: Acute Angle Adjustment Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question asks for 'the acute angle' between two lines, or between a vector like AC and a line, and your dot product comes out negative. Equivalently, you compute cos θ = (d₁·d₂)/(|d₁||d₂|) and get a negative value, which would give an obtuse θ ≈ 90°–180° from arccos. The cue is the word 'acute' (sometimes phrased as 'the angle between the two lines' — by convention this means the acute one, since lines have no fixed direction). Any time the direction vectors involved could legitimately be reversed (lines, not rays), this adjustment applies.

Motivation — Why this works

A line has no inherent direction: replacing its direction vector d by −d describes the same line but flips the sign of every dot product, swapping θ for its supplement 180°−θ. Of these two supplementary angles, exactly one is acute (unless the lines are perpendicular). The convention — and what the mark scheme expects — is the acute one. Using |d₁·d₂| in the numerator, or equivalently subtracting an obtuse result from 180°, is the formal device that picks out this acute angle automatically.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question explicitly demands the acute angle, or that I'm dealing with lines (whose direction is arbitrary up to sign), so the answer should lie in (0°, 90°].
  2. I compute the dot product and find it is negative — so the standard formula cos θ = (d₁·d₂)/(|d₁||d₂|) will yield an obtuse θ.
  3. I rule out just reporting the obtuse value: the question specifies acute, and lines don't have a fixed direction anyway, so the obtuse value is not the geometrically meaningful answer.
  4. I rule out re-doing the problem with d₂ replaced by −d₂; that works but is wasteful — the |·| shortcut, or a single 180°− subtraction at the end, is cleaner.
  5. Tactical choice: if I want the cleanest write-up I insert |·| from the start (cos θ = |d₁·d₂|/(|d₁||d₂|)); if I've already computed the obtuse angle numerically, I just take 180° − θ_obtuse at the end. Both are equivalent and both are acceptable.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute d₁·d₂ and |d₁|, |d₂| as usual.
  2. If d₁·d₂ < 0, either: (a) replace the numerator with |d₁·d₂| before applying arccos, or (b) compute θ_obtuse = arccos((d₁·d₂)/(|d₁||d₂|)) and then take θ_acute = 180° − θ_obtuse.
  3. State the final acute angle, typically to 1 d.p. in degrees, or in exact surd form where requested.
  4. Sanity check: 0° < θ_acute ≤ 90°.

Worked Micro-Example

Given d₁·d₂ = −6, |d₁| = √6, |d₂| = 3. Then cos θ = |−6|/(√6·3) = 6/(3√6) = √6/3, so θ = arccos(√6/3) ≈ 35.3°. (Without the modulus, arccos(−√6/3) ≈ 144.7° — the obtuse supplement.)

Variants — Same move, different surface

Angle between a line and a plane
Use sin θ = |d·n|/(|d||n|) (note sine, not cosine), because the formula computes the complement of the angle d makes with n. The modulus still guarantees an acute result.
Angle between two planes
Use cos θ = |n₁·n₂|/(|n₁||n₂|) with the normal vectors. Same modulus logic — normals can point either way, so take the acute angle.
Angle between a position-defined vector (e.g. AC) and a line
Same rule: AC has a definite direction but the line does not, so the line's direction vector may be reversed. Apply |AC·d|/(|AC||d|) or take 180° − θ if you've already found an obtuse value.

Common Misreadings

  • Reporting the obtuse angle (e.g. 151.7° or 144.7°) when the question asked for the acute angle — a frequent mark loss.
  • Confusing the line–line formula (cosine with |·|) with the line–plane formula (sine with |·|).
  • Applying the supplement when the dot product is already positive — this incorrectly flips a valid acute answer to an obtuse one.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the modulus and then forgetting to take 180° − θ at the end — leaves the obtuse angle as the final answer.
  • Working in radians and accidentally subtracting from 2π or 360° instead of π or 180°.
  • Rounding √6/3 or similar surds too early; keep exact form until the final arccos to avoid accumulated error.
  • Not rationalising the surd form (e.g. leaving 2/√6 instead of √6/3) when the mark scheme wants simplified form.

A-level Scope Notes

This is standard Edexcel IAL P3/FP3 vector geometry. The formula cos θ = (a·b)/(|a||b|) is in the formula booklet; the absolute-value convention for lines/planes is a textbook convention students must apply themselves. Avoid generalising to oriented angles or signed angles in higher dimensions — out of scope.
