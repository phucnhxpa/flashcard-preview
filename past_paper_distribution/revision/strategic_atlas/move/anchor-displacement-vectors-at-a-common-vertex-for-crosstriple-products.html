Vectors

Anchor displacement vectors at a common vertex for cross/triple products

Family: Common Origin Vectors Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a problem supplies three or more points (e.g. A, B, C, D) and subsequent parts ask for area of a triangle/parallelogram, a normal to a plane, volume of a tetrahedron/parallelepiped, or coplanarity tests. Cues include phrases like 'find the area of triangle ABC', 'show that ABCD is a tetrahedron of volume…', or 'find a vector perpendicular to the plane through A, B, C'. The cross product and scalar triple product require free (displacement) vectors sharing a tail, so the position vectors as given are not directly usable.

Motivation — Why this works

The cross product $\vec{u}\times\vec{v}$ and scalar triple product $\vec{u}\cdot(\vec{v}\times\vec{w})$ are defined on free vectors emanating from a common point; their geometric meanings (area of parallelogram, volume of parallelepiped) only hold when the vectors share a tail. Anchoring all displacement vectors at one chosen vertex guarantees every vector you build is reusable across the whole question, so part (a)'s cross product and part (b)'s triple product share inputs. Picking a vertex with zero or small coordinates makes the subtractions cleaner and reduces arithmetic slips.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that several points are given but later operations (area, volume, normal) need vectors, not points.
  2. Rule out using position vectors $\vec{OA},\vec{OB},\vec{OC}$ directly — those don't represent the geometry of the figure.
  3. Commit to one vertex as the shared tail so every displacement vector built is compatible with both the cross product and the scalar triple product.
  4. Bridge: pick the vertex with the simplest coordinates (often a zero entry) and compute $\vec{AB}=B-A$, $\vec{AC}=C-A$, and if needed $\vec{AD}=D-A$.
  5. Tactically, keep the order consistent (always tail-at-A, head-at-other-point) so cross-product signs and the orientation of the normal stay predictable across parts.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the vertex to use as common tail; prefer one with a zero coordinate or small entries.
  2. Compute $\vec{AB} = B - A$ component-wise.
  3. Compute $\vec{AC} = C - A$ component-wise.
  4. If a fourth point is given, compute $\vec{AD} = D - A$ for the triple product later.
  5. Record these as column/row vectors ready to feed into $\vec{AB}\times\vec{AC}$ and $\vec{AD}\cdot(\vec{AB}\times\vec{AC})$.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $A=(0,4,1)$, $B=(4,0,0)$, $C=(3,5,2)$, $D=(2,2,k)$, anchor at $A$: $\vec{AB}=(4,-4,-1)$, $\vec{AC}=(3,1,1)$, $\vec{AD}=(2,-2,k-1)$. Then area of $\triangle ABC = \tfrac{1}{2}|\vec{AB}\times\vec{AC}|$ and volume of tetrahedron $ABCD = \tfrac{1}{6}|\vec{AD}\cdot(\vec{AB}\times\vec{AC})|$, both reusing the same anchored vectors.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Parallelogram $ABCD$ — find area or a normal
Anchor at $A$ and form the two adjacent edges $\vec{AB}$ and $\vec{AD}$; the diagonal $\vec{AC}$ is not needed for area.
Equation of plane through three points $A,B,C$
Anchor at $A$, compute $\vec{n}=\vec{AB}\times\vec{AC}$, then use $\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{r}-\vec{a})=0$.
Coplanarity / volume of tetrahedron $ABCD$
Anchor at $A$ and use $\vec{AB},\vec{AC},\vec{AD}$ in the scalar triple product; coplanar iff result is $0$.
Shortest distance from a point $P$ to plane through $A,B,C$
Anchor at $A$, build $\vec{n}=\vec{AB}\times\vec{AC}$, then project $\vec{AP}$ onto $\hat{n}$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating position vectors $\vec{OA},\vec{OB},\vec{OC}$ as if they were edges of the figure and crossing them directly.
  • Using a different anchor vertex for each part of the question, forcing recomputation and introducing inconsistency.
  • Forgetting that $\vec{AB}\times\vec{AC}$ gives twice the triangle area, omitting the factor $\tfrac{1}{2}$.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when subtracting negative coordinates.
  • Computing $\vec{BA}$ instead of $\vec{AB}$, which reverses the normal direction.
  • Inconsistent ordering between parts — e.g. using $\vec{AB}\times\vec{AC}$ in one part and $\vec{AC}\times\vec{AB}$ in another, flipping the sign of the triple product.
  • Subtracting components in the wrong order (B-component minus A-component is required, not A minus B).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope: cross product, scalar triple product, areas of triangles/parallelograms, volumes of tetrahedra/parallelepipeds, and equations of planes through three points.
