Trigger — When to use

Part (a) has produced an implicit (or otherwise mixed) derivative dy/dx expressed in terms of BOTH x and y, and part (b) asks for the equation of the tangent (or normal) at a specific point P(x₁, y₁) on the curve. The cue is the pairing of two ingredients: a derivative formula containing y as well as x, and a fully specified coordinate pair. Equivalent phrasings include 'find the tangent at P', 'show the tangent has equation …', or 'find the gradient of the curve at the point where x = …, y = …'. Whenever a numerical gradient is required and the derivative is not already a function of x alone, this move fires.

Motivation — Why this works

Implicit differentiation generally yields dy/dx as a rational expression in x and y; it is a gradient FIELD, not a single number. To collapse it to the tangent's slope at one specific point we must feed in BOTH coordinates simultaneously — that is precisely why the question supplies a full ordered pair. The result is the numerical gradient m, which together with the point yields the tangent line via the point-gradient form y − y₁ = m(x − x₁). The underlying principle is that the tangent line is the linearisation of the curve at P, whose slope is the value of the derivative function at P.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that dy/dx from part (a) contains both x and y, so I cannot evaluate it from the x-coordinate alone — unlike an explicit derivative.
  2. Rule out re-deriving from the curve equation or trying to solve explicitly for y(x); the fastest route is to evaluate the dy/dx I already have.
  3. Commit to substituting both coordinates of P because the question helpfully provides the pair (x₁, y₁), and (optionally) verify P lies on the curve by plugging into the original equation.
  4. Bridge: substitute x = x₁ and y = y₁ into dy/dx, simplify the numerator and denominator separately, and read off m. Check the denominator is non-zero (otherwise the tangent is vertical).
  5. Tactical step: with m and (x₁, y₁) in hand, write y − y₁ = m(x − x₁); rearrange to the form requested (e.g. ax + by + c = 0). For a normal, replace m by −1/m before applying point-gradient form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Take dy/dx from part (a), e.g. dy/dx = f(x, y).
  2. (Optional sanity check) Verify (x₁, y₁) satisfies the original implicit equation.
  3. Substitute x = x₁, y = y₁ into f(x, y); evaluate numerator and denominator separately.
  4. Simplify to obtain the tangent gradient m = f(x₁, y₁); confirm denominator ≠ 0.
  5. Write the tangent as y − y₁ = m(x − x₁); clear fractions and rearrange to the demanded form.
  6. (For a normal) Use gradient −1/m in place of m in the point-gradient form.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{3y - 2x}{2y - 3x}$ and $P(1,3)$ on $y^2 - 3xy + x^2 = 1$: check $9 - 9 + 1 = 1$ ✓. Then $m = \frac{3(3) - 2(1)}{2(3) - 3(1)} = \frac{7}{3}$. Tangent: $y - 3 = \tfrac{7}{3}(x - 1)$, i.e. $7x - 3y + 2 = 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Normal line instead of tangent at the same point
Compute m as above, then use gradient −1/m in y − y₁ = (−1/m)(x − x₁).
Only the x-coordinate of P is given
First substitute x = x₁ into the original curve equation to solve for y₁, then evaluate dy/dx at (x₁, y₁).
Parametric curve with parameter value t = t₀
Compute dx/dt and dy/dt, evaluate each at t₀, and form m = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) at t₀.
Derivative contains exponentials/logs (e.g. e^{-2x})
Evaluate the transcendental terms at x₁ first (e.g. e^{0}=1) before substituting y, to keep arithmetic clean.

Common Misreadings

  • Substituting only the x-coordinate and trying to manipulate the remaining y in the derivative.
  • Substituting into the original curve equation instead of the derivative to look for the gradient.
  • Confusing the tangent gradient with the normal gradient (forgetting the −1/m step).
  • Mishandling e^0 (writing 0 instead of 1) when the derivative contains exponentials.

Pitfalls

  • Swapping x and y when substituting, especially when the derivative is asymmetric like (3y − 2x)/(2y − 3x).
  • Sign errors in the denominator (e.g. (2y − 2x) vs (2x − 2y)) flipping the sign of m.
  • Forgetting to check the denominator is non-zero — a zero denominator signals a vertical tangent and the linear form fails.
  • Skipping the verification that P lies on the curve when the question implicitly expects it.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 implicit differentiation and tangent/normal work; no formula booklet entry is required beyond the standard derivative rules. Avoid invoking partial derivatives or the gradient vector ∇F — these are out of scope; the implicit derivative dy/dx = −F_x/F_y should be presented purely via the chain rule on F(x,y)=const.
