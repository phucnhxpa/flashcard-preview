Algebra & Functions

Sanity-check root parsing when transformed-roots answer looks unusually ugly

Family: Diagnostic Verification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in 'form a new quadratic whose roots are f(α), f(β)' problems when the computed coefficient (sum or product) comes out as an unusually ugly fraction like −25/29 or 47/13. Edexcel IAL structured questions almost always yield small integers, simple fractions, or surds — so a denominator like 29 is a red flag. Also fires when the transformed root expression uses ambiguous notation (3α/β vs 3/α − β vs 3α − β) that could plausibly be re-parsed.

Motivation — Why this works

Edexcel mark schemes are designed around clean arithmetic, so an ugly answer is a strong Bayesian signal of either an algebra slip or a misread expression. Testing alternative parsings is cheap (each takes one sum and one product computation via Vieta's) and either confirms the original reading or surfaces the intended one. Confirming the parsing before pressing on prevents catastrophic loss of follow-through marks on the remainder of a 10–12 mark question.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the computed coefficient (e.g. A = −25/29) has a denominator that no Edexcel question would naturally produce.
  2. Rule out a pure arithmetic slip first by re-running the Vieta substitution; if the value persists, suspect the parsing of the transformed roots.
  3. Commit to a brief diagnostic sweep: list 2–3 plausible re-parsings of the ambiguous expression and test each via sum and product.
  4. Bridge to action by computing, for each candidate pair of roots, the sum α'+β' and product α'β' in terms of the known α+β and αβ.
  5. Tactically: accept the original parsing only if no alternative gives clean values for both coefficients; if one alternative gives both clean, switch to it.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Flag the suspicious coefficient (denominator unusual, or magnitude inconsistent with the mark tariff).
  2. List the plausible alternative parsings of the transformed-roots expression.
  3. For each alternative, compute the sum α'+β' using α+β and αβ from the original quadratic.
  4. For each alternative, compute the product α'β' similarly.
  5. Compare both coefficients against the 'clean answer' heuristic; reject any parsing that gives a contradiction or a still-ugly value in either coefficient.
  6. If no alternative cleans up both coefficients, retain the original parsing and present the ugly value as the genuine answer.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $x^2 - 5x + 4 = 0$ with roots $\alpha, \beta$, suppose 'roots $3\alpha/\beta$ and $3\beta/\alpha$' yields sum $= 3(\alpha^2+\beta^2)/(\alpha\beta) = 3 \cdot 17/4 = 51/4$. The 51/4 looks plausible but if instead the answer had been e.g. $-25/29$, test parsing '$3\alpha-\beta, 3\beta-\alpha$': sum $= 2(\alpha+\beta) = 10$ and product $= -3(\alpha^2+\beta^2)+10\alpha\beta = -51+40 = -11$ — both integers, so this parsing is intended.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Ambiguous fraction grouping such as $\alpha/(\beta+1)$ vs $(\alpha/\beta)+1$
Compute sum and product under each grouping; the correct grouping typically gives a coefficient expressible cleanly in $p = \alpha+\beta, q = \alpha\beta$.
Transformed roots involving $\alpha^2$ vs $\alpha\cdot 2$ misread from handwritten exam paper
Test both; $\alpha^2+\beta^2 = p^2-2q$ vs $2\alpha+2\beta = 2p$ give very different magnitudes.
Cubic with roots $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$ and transformed roots like $\alpha+\beta, \beta+\gamma, \gamma+\alpha$ vs $\alpha\beta, \beta\gamma, \gamma\alpha$
Use the three Vieta sums $e_1, e_2, e_3$ and check which transformation gives integer new coefficients.
Partial fractions question where the cover-up gives an ugly residue
Re-check whether the denominator factor was read as $(x-2)$ vs $(2x-1)$ etc. before recomputing.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating '$3\alpha/\beta$' as $(3\alpha)/\beta$ when intended as $3 \cdot (\alpha/\beta)$ (same value, but students sometimes re-parse as $3/\alpha - \beta$).
  • Reading $3\alpha-\beta$ as $3(\alpha-\beta)$, which changes both sum and product.
  • Assuming any ugly answer must be wrong — some Edexcel questions genuinely produce fractions like $-25/29$ when the original quadratic has coprime non-trivial coefficients.

Pitfalls

  • Spending more than 2–3 minutes on the diagnostic sweep and losing time for later parts.
  • Switching to an alternative parsing because one of its coefficients is clean while ignoring that the other is uglier than the original.
  • Forgetting to recompute the product (not just the sum) for each alternative — a parsing can fake-cleanly match on sum alone.
  • Mis-applying Vieta's during the test (sign errors on $\alpha+\beta = -b/a$) and rejecting a correct parsing.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL FP1/FP2 'roots of polynomial equations' material: uses only Vieta's relations $\alpha+\beta = -b/a$, $\alpha\beta = c/a$ (and cubic analogues) and standard symmetric-function identities like $\alpha^2+\beta^2 = (\alpha+\beta)^2 - 2\alpha\beta$.
