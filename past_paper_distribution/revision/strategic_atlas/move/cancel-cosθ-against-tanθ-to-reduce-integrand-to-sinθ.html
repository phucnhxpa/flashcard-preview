Integration

Cancel cosθ against tanθ to reduce integrand to sinθ

Family: Integrand Simplification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when, after parametric setup or surface/arc-length simplification, the integrand appears as a product of trig functions sharing a common factor, e.g. cosθ·tanθ, sinθ·cotθ, or secθ·cosθ. The student should pause before integrating and check whether the product collapses via tanθ = sinθ/cosθ (or cotθ = cosθ/sinθ). Cue words: 'integrand is a product of two trig terms', 'one factor is tan/cot/sec/csc'.

Motivation — Why this works

Writing tanθ = sinθ/cosθ exposes a cosθ factor that cancels with the cosθ already present, leaving a single elementary trig function. The resulting ∫sinθ dθ (or ∫cosθ dθ) is a standard antiderivative, so the whole problem collapses to a one-line evaluation. Spotting this saves attempts at substitution or by-parts that would otherwise be unnecessary.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the integrand is a product cosθ·tanθ rather than a single named function.
  2. Rules out u-substitution or integration by parts as overkill — the factors share trig structure.
  3. Commits to rewriting tanθ = sinθ/cosθ because that exposes immediate cancellation with the cosθ factor.
  4. Bridges to ∫sinθ dθ on the same θ-bounds inherited from the parametric/trig substitution.
  5. Evaluates [−cosθ] at the bounds carefully and reattaches the outer 2π (or other constant) carried from the surface-of-revolution formula.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rewrite tanθ as sinθ/cosθ inside the integrand.
  2. Cancel the common cosθ factor: cosθ·(sinθ/cosθ) = sinθ.
  3. Integrate the simplified integrand: ∫sinθ dθ = −cosθ + C.
  4. Evaluate −cosθ at the upper and lower θ-bounds.
  5. Reattach any outer constants (e.g. 2π for surface area) and simplify to required form.

Worked Micro-Example

Surface area gives $S = 2\pi\int_0^{\pi/4} \cos\theta\,\tan\theta\,d\theta$. Simplify: $\cos\theta\tan\theta = \sin\theta$. Then $\int_0^{\pi/4}\sin\theta\,d\theta = [-\cos\theta]_0^{\pi/4} = 1-\tfrac{\sqrt2}{2} = \tfrac{2-\sqrt2}{2}$. Hence $S = 2\pi\cdot\tfrac{2-\sqrt2}{2} = \pi(2-\sqrt2)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Arc length / surface integrand contains sinθ·cotθ
Use cotθ = cosθ/sinθ to cancel sinθ, leaving ∫cosθ dθ = sinθ.
Integrand is secθ·cosθ
secθ·cosθ = 1, so the integral reduces to ∫1 dθ = θ over the bounds.
Integrand is tanθ·secθ
Recognise this directly as the derivative of secθ; no cancellation needed, antiderivative is secθ.
Integrand is cosθ·cscθ
Rewrite cscθ = 1/sinθ to get cosθ/sinθ = cotθ, then integrate to ln|sinθ|.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating cosθ·tanθ as needing integration by parts because it 'looks like a product'.
  • Attempting a substitution u = cosθ before noticing the algebraic cancellation.
  • Confusing tanθ with secθ and ending up with the wrong simplified integrand.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the outer 2π (or other multiplicative constant) from the surface-of-revolution formula.
  • Sign error in [−cosθ] at the bounds — subtracting in the wrong order.
  • Leaving the answer as (2−√2)/2 · 2π instead of simplifying to π(2−√2), or mis-matching p, q in a 'π(p − √q)' template.
  • Cancelling cosθ without noting the implicit assumption cosθ ≠ 0 on the integration interval.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits within Edexcel IAL Pure/Further Pure trig integration and surfaces of revolution; uses only the identity tanθ = sinθ/cosθ and the standard antiderivative of sinθ.
