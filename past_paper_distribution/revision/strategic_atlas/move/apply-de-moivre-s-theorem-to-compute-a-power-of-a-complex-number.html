Trigger — When to use

Fires when the question asks for z^n (or simplification of a power like (a+bi)^n) and z is already in — or can readily be converted to — modulus-argument form r(cos θ + i sin θ). Typical signals: the exponent n is a positive integer (often 5, 6, 8, 10), occasionally negative for reciprocal powers, and the answer is required either in polar form or as an exact Cartesian a+bi. Equivalent phrasings include 'express z^n in the form a+bi', 'find the modulus and argument of z^n', or 'hence show that z^n = …'. The presence of a 'large' exponent that would make binomial expansion painful is the strongest cue.

Motivation — Why this works

De Moivre's theorem states [r(cos θ + i sin θ)]^n = r^n(cos nθ + i sin nθ), collapsing an n-fold complex multiplication into a single modulus exponentiation and a single angle multiplication. It works because multiplication of complex numbers in polar form multiplies moduli and adds arguments, so repeated multiplication scales by r^n and nθ. This sidesteps the combinatorial explosion of a binomial expansion of (a+bi)^n, which would generate n+1 terms requiring real/imaginary collection. It also delivers the modulus and argument of the answer for free, which is often exactly what the question wants.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question asks for z^n where z is (or can be put in) polar form r(cos θ + i sin θ) — exponentiation of a complex number.
  2. I consider binomial expansion of (a+bi)^n but rule it out: for n ≥ 4 it produces too many terms, with sign-tracking on i^k and a real/imaginary collection step that is error-prone.
  3. I commit to de Moivre because polar form converts the n-fold product to two scalar operations on r and θ — the cleanest route.
  4. If z is given in Cartesian form a+bi, I first compute r = √(a²+b²) and θ = arctan(b/a) (with quadrant care) so that z is properly normalised as r(cos θ + i sin θ) — not r(cos θ − i sin θ), which would silently flip the sign of the argument.
  5. I then raise the modulus to the n-th power and multiply the argument by n, and decide whether the question wants polar form, Cartesian form, or just |z^n| and arg(z^n).
  6. Finally I check whether nθ falls outside the principal range (−π, π]; if so, I add or subtract multiples of 2π to bring it into range before stating the argument.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Ensure z is written as r(cos θ + i sin θ) with r > 0 and θ the (principal) argument.
  2. Compute the new modulus: r^n.
  3. Compute the new argument: nθ.
  4. Reduce nθ modulo 2π into the principal range (−π, π] if required by the question.
  5. Write z^n = r^n(cos nθ + i sin nθ).
  6. If a Cartesian answer is required, evaluate cos nθ and sin nθ exactly and write z^n = r^n cos nθ + i·r^n sin nθ.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $z = 1+i$. Then $r = \sqrt{2}$ and $\theta = \pi/4$, so $z = \sqrt{2}(\cos(\pi/4) + i\sin(\pi/4))$. Applying de Moivre with $n=8$: $z^8 = (\sqrt{2})^8(\cos(8\cdot\pi/4) + i\sin(8\cdot\pi/4)) = 16(\cos 2\pi + i\sin 2\pi) = 16$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Negative integer power z^{−n}
Use r^{−n}(cos(−nθ) + i sin(−nθ)); equivalent to taking the reciprocal of z^n and reflecting the argument.
n-th roots of a complex number
Use r^{1/n}(cos((θ + 2kπ)/n) + i sin((θ + 2kπ)/n)) for k = 0, 1, …, n−1, generating all n distinct roots.
Deriving multiple-angle trig identities
Combine de Moivre with the binomial expansion of (cos θ + i sin θ)^n and equate real/imaginary parts to obtain expressions for cos nθ and sin nθ in powers of cos θ, sin θ.
z given in form r(cos θ − i sin θ)
Rewrite as r(cos(−θ) + i sin(−θ)) before applying de Moivre, so the argument carries the correct sign.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating (a+bi)^n as a candidate for direct binomial expansion when n is large — workable for n=2 or 3 but slow and error-prone beyond that; polar form is faster.
  • Applying de Moivre to r(cos θ − i sin θ) without first rewriting it in standard form, then getting the argument's sign wrong.

Pitfalls

  • Multiplying the argument by n but forgetting to raise the modulus: writing cos nθ + i sin nθ instead of r^n(cos nθ + i sin nθ).
  • Failing to reduce nθ to the principal range (−π, π] when the question specifies it, losing accuracy marks even though the polar form is technically equivalent.
  • Quadrant errors when first converting Cartesian to polar — using arctan(b/a) without adjusting θ for the correct quadrant of (a, b).

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2): de Moivre's theorem for integer powers and n-th roots, and its use to derive multiple-angle identities. The theorem is stated in the FP2 formula booklet. Avoid invoking the general complex exponential e^{iθ} formalism beyond what is presented in the syllabus — stick to the cos θ + i sin θ notation expected by mark schemes.
