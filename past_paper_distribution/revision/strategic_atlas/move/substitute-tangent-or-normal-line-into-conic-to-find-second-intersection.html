Coordinate Geometry

Substitute tangent or normal line into conic to find second intersection

Family: Substitution Into Implicit Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a tangent or normal at a known point on a conic (ellipse, hyperbola, parabola, or circle) is asked to meet the conic 'again' at a second point, or to meet a different curve. Cue phrases include 'find the coordinates of the point where the normal meets the curve again', 'the tangent at P meets the hyperbola at Q', or 'find where this line cuts the parabola'. Also fires when intersecting a chord (joining two parametric points) with the conic at a third related curve. Distinguished from axis-intercept questions, which need no substitution into the conic.

Motivation — Why this works

A line and a conic together form a 2×2 system; substituting the linear relation into the implicit conic equation collapses it to a single quadratic in one variable, whose roots are exactly the x- (or y-) coordinates of the intersections. When the line is a tangent at a known point, that point's coordinate is already a (repeated) root, so Vieta's formulas — sum or product of roots — deliver the other root without re-solving from scratch. For a normal, one root is still known (the foot of the normal), giving the same factoring shortcut. This avoids messy quadratic-formula work and exploits information already in hand.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question wants a second intersection point of a line (tangent/normal) with the conic — two equations in two unknowns.
  2. I rule out solving the system blindly with the quadratic formula because one root is already known from the construction of the line.
  3. I commit to substitution because eliminating one variable yields a single polynomial whose known root unlocks Vieta's shortcut.
  4. I rearrange the line equation for whichever variable appears linearly and substitute into the conic's Cartesian form.
  5. Inside the move I decide whether to factor out the known linear factor directly, or use sum/product of roots to read off the second root in one line.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the line (tangent/normal) in the form y = mx + c or x = (something in y).
  2. Substitute into the conic equation (e.g. x²/a² ± y²/b² = 1, y² = 4ax, or x² + y² = r²).
  3. Expand and collect terms into a quadratic Ax² + Bx + C = 0 (or in y).
  4. Use the known root x₁ from the point of tangency/normal: either factor (x − x₁) out, or apply x₁ + x₂ = −B/A or x₁x₂ = C/A to get x₂.
  5. Substitute x₂ back into the line equation to recover y₂; state the second intersection point.

Worked Micro-Example

Normal to $y^2 = 4x$ at $P(1,2)$ has gradient $-1$, equation $y = -x + 3$. Substitute: $(-x+3)^2 = 4x \Rightarrow x^2 - 10x + 9 = 0$. Known root $x=1$, so by product of roots $x_1 x_2 = 9$, giving $x_2 = 9$ and $y_2 = -6$. Second intersection: $(9, -6)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Tangent to ellipse x²/a² + y²/b² = 1 meeting the ellipse again
Tangent meets only at the point of contact (repeated root); a 'second' intersection does not exist — recognise the discriminant is zero.
Normal to rectangular hyperbola xy = c² at parameter t meeting the hyperbola again
Substitute y = c²/x into the normal equation; resulting quartic has known root x = ct, factor it out to obtain the other parameter.
Chord joining two parametric points on a parabola/ellipse meeting another conic
Same substitution technique; both endpoints are known roots, so the remaining factor is linear and read off directly.
Tangent meets a coordinate axis
No conic substitution needed — set x = 0 or y = 0 in the line equation only.
Line meeting a circle (x − a)² + (y − b)² = r²
Same substitution; if line is tangent, discriminant = 0 confirms the single contact point.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the tangent–conic intersection as yielding two distinct points (it gives a repeated root only).
  • Confusing 'meets the curve again' (use known root + Vieta) with 'find points of intersection of two arbitrary lines/curves' (full quadratic solve).
  • Mistaking the normal's foot for a repeated root — it is a single root, not repeated.

Pitfalls

  • Algebraic slips when expanding (mx + c)² or substituting parametric coordinates with trig terms.
  • Forgetting to back-substitute x₂ into the line to obtain y₂.
  • Using the wrong Vieta relation (sum vs product) for the leading coefficient.
  • Dividing through by a variable that could be zero, losing a solution.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard for Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP1/FP3) coordinate-geometry questions on parabolas, ellipses, hyperbolas, and rectangular hyperbolas, and for IAL Pure circle-line problems. No conics beyond these are required.
