Algebra & Functions

Filter candidate solutions through the substitution's hidden domain

Family: Domain Filtering Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

This move fires whenever an equation has been transformed (by substitution, cross-multiplication, squaring, or composing functions) and you have arrived at a polynomial whose roots are CANDIDATE solutions, not final ones. Look for cues like: a substitution u = e^x, u = ln x, u = √x, u = cos x; an equation involving f(x) = f⁻¹(x) where each function carries its own domain; or a rational/logarithmic equation where you cleared denominators or combined logs. Whenever the original variable lives in a strictly smaller set than ℝ, every algebraic root must be tested against that set before being accepted.

Motivation — Why this works

Algebraic manipulation (multiplying out, substituting, squaring) can enlarge the solution set by erasing the domain restrictions that the original equation imposed. Each function in play carries its own domain — e^x > 0, ln requires positive argument, f⁻¹ inherits the range of f, √ requires non-negative radicand — and the genuine solutions must lie in the intersection of all these. Filtering candidates through this intersection is the step that converts 'roots of a quadratic' into 'solutions of the original equation', and it is typically where the final marks are awarded.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice I have reached a polynomial (often a quadratic) whose roots are candidate values, but the original equation involved a function with a restricted domain (exponential, log, inverse function, surd).
  2. I rule out simply reading off both roots as the answer — algebraic validity does not guarantee functional validity, and examiners reserve marks for the rejection step.
  3. I commit to domain filtering: I will write down the domain constraint imposed by EACH function appearing in the original equation, then intersect them.
  4. Concretely, I list the constraints (e.g. x > 0 from f, 0 < x < 12 from f⁻¹; or y > 0 from y = e^x), form their intersection, and test each candidate.
  5. Tactical decision: if a candidate fails any single constraint, I reject it immediately and do NOT carry it forward (e.g. I do not attempt ln of a non-positive number).
  6. I state the surviving solution(s) explicitly as the final answer, making the rejection visible in my working.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify every function in the original equation and write its domain/range constraint.
  2. Form the intersection of these constraints — this is the admissible set S.
  3. List the candidate solutions obtained algebraically (e.g. roots of the quadratic).
  4. For each candidate, check membership in S; mark valid ones with ✓ and invalid ones with ✗ giving the failed constraint.
  5. Discard rejected candidates explicitly in writing (e.g. 'reject x = −4 since x > 0 required').
  6. State the remaining value(s) as the final solution set.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $e^{2x} - 7e^x + 6 = 0$. Let $y = e^x$, so $y^2 - 7y + 6 = 0 \Rightarrow (y-1)(y-6) = 0$, giving candidates $y = 1, 6$. Domain check: $y = e^x > 0$, both pass. Then $x = \ln 1 = 0$ or $x = \ln 6$. Had a root been $y = -2$, it would be rejected before taking $\ln$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation f(x) = f⁻¹(x) with restricted-domain f
Intersect domain of f with domain of f⁻¹ (which equals range of f); reject any algebraic root outside this intersection.
Hidden quadratic in y = e^x or y = a^x
Require y > 0 before taking logarithms; reject non-positive roots of the quadratic.
Hidden quadratic in y = cos x or y = sin x
Require −1 ≤ y ≤ 1; reject roots outside this range.
Logarithmic equations after combining logs
Each ln/log argument must be > 0; substitute candidates back into the original arguments.
Equations solved by squaring both sides
Substitute every candidate back into the unsquared equation to detect sign-introduced extraneous roots.

Common Misreadings

  • Checking only one of the functions' domains and missing exclusions imposed by the other.
  • Assuming every root of the resulting polynomial is automatically a solution of the original equation.
  • Confusing the constraint y = e^x > 0 with x > 0, leading to wrongly rejecting valid positive y < 1.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to apply a domain established in an earlier part of the question (e.g. domain of f⁻¹ found in part (b) must be reused in part (c)).
  • Attempting to take ln, √, or arccos of a rejected candidate instead of discarding it immediately.
  • Writing both candidates as the final answer because both 'solve the quadratic'.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P2/P3) for exponentials, logarithms, inverse functions, and trigonometric equations. No formula booklet entry is invoked; this is a procedural/logical step. Avoid framing this as 'complex roots' filtering — at IAL we are filtering real candidates against real-domain constraints only.
