Strategic Atlas / Matrices / Determinant Volume Scaling
Matrices

Equate image volume to |det M| times original volume

Family: Determinant Volume Scaling Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a 3×3 matrix M (often containing an unknown parameter k) acts as a linear transformation on a solid (tetrahedron, parallelepiped, cube) and the question links the original volume to the transformed volume. Cue phrases include 'maps T to T'', 'image has volume…', 'find the value(s) of k such that the volume is…'. Equivalent 2D phrasings refer to area of an image parallelogram/triangle. The signal is that you are given (or can compute) both volumes and need a single equation in the unknown.

Motivation — Why this works

A linear transformation with matrix M scales every volume in 3D by |det M|, because the scalar triple product [Ma, Mb, Mc] = det(M) · [a, b, c]. Hence Volume(image) = |det M| · Volume(original). Knowing both volumes converts the geometric statement into a single algebraic equation |det M| = V_image / V_original, which is solvable for any parameter inside M. The absolute value matters because det M may be negative (orientation-reversing) yet volume is unsigned.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that two volumes (original and image) are linked by a single matrix transformation, so the determinant scaling law is the natural bridge.
  2. Rules out computing the image tetrahedron vertex-by-vertex — this is wasteful when the scale factor handles it in one step.
  3. Commits to the identity Volume(image) = |det M| · Volume(original) because it converts the geometry into one equation in the unknown parameter.
  4. Bridges to operation: expand det M as a polynomial in k, then form |det M| · V = V_image.
  5. Tactically splits the resulting absolute-value equation into two linear cases and checks each candidate does not make det M = 0 (which would collapse the image to zero volume).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute det M as an expression in the unknown parameter (e.g. det M = 5k − 10).
  2. Write the scaling equation |det M| · V_original = V_image.
  3. Isolate |det M| = V_image / V_original.
  4. Split into two cases: det M = +(V_image/V_original) and det M = −(V_image/V_original).
  5. Solve each linear equation for the parameter.
  6. Reject any value that makes det M = 0 (singular matrix); retain all others.

Worked Micro-Example

Tetrahedron $T$ has volume $\tfrac{20}{3}$. Under $M$ with $\det M = 5k-10$, the image has volume $50$. Then $|5k-10|\cdot\tfrac{20}{3}=50$, so $|5k-10|=\tfrac{15}{2}$. Cases: $5k-10=\tfrac{15}{2}\Rightarrow k=\tfrac{7}{2}$, or $5k-10=-\tfrac{15}{2}\Rightarrow k=\tfrac12$. Neither gives $k=2$, so both are valid.

Variants — Same move, different surface

2D area scaling under a 2×2 matrix (parallelogram, triangle, or unit square image)
Use Area(image) = |det M| · Area(original) with a 2×2 determinant ad − bc.
Volume of image of the unit cube / standard tetrahedron
V_original is 1 (or a known constant like 1/6), so |det M| equals the image volume directly.
Composite transformation MN applied to a solid
Use |det(MN)| = |det M||det N| as the combined scale factor.
Inverse transformation pulling image back to original
Scale factor becomes 1/|det M|; use V_original = V_image / |det M|.
Orientation-reversing transformation (reflection-type, det M < 0)
Volume formula still uses |det M|; the sign only tells you orientation has flipped.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating det M (signed) as the scale factor and discarding the negative case, losing one valid value of k.
  • Trying to transform each vertex of the tetrahedron and recompute a scalar triple product instead of using the scale-factor shortcut.
  • Confusing area scaling (2D) with volume scaling (3D) and using a 2×2 minor of M.
  • Reading 'volume of image is 50' as 'det M = 50' without dividing by the original volume.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the modulus and missing one root of |det M| = c.
  • Arithmetic slips when simplifying V_image / V_original (e.g. 50 ÷ (20/3) = 15/2).
  • Forgetting to exclude parameter values that make det M = 0.
  • Sign errors when expanding the 3×3 determinant in terms of the parameter.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP3) result: a linear transformation in 3D scales volumes by |det M|, with the 2D analogue (|det M| scales areas) appearing in FP1. No content beyond cofactor expansion of a 3×3 determinant is required.
