Coordinate Geometry

Decode 'on the negative x-axis' as y=0 with x<0

Family: Constraint Extraction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question locates a point or intersection on a named portion of an axis: 'on the negative x-axis', 'on the positive y-axis', 'meets the x-axis on the left of the origin', or 'crosses the y-axis below the origin'. The cue is a directional adjective ('negative', 'positive', 'above', 'below', 'left', 'right') paired with an axis name. It also fires when a curve and line are stated to meet at such a point and you need its coordinates to fix an unknown parameter.

Motivation — Why this works

An axis condition encodes one coordinate exactly (y=0 for the x-axis, x=0 for the y-axis), and the directional adjective adds a sign inequality on the other coordinate. Substituting the zero coordinate into the curve equation collapses it to a 1-variable equation whose roots are then filtered by the sign constraint. This usually yields a unique point, which can then be substituted into a second equation (e.g. line l with known gradient) to determine remaining unknowns.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the phrase pairing a sign word with an axis ('negative x-axis') and flags it as a two-part constraint.
  2. Rules out treating it as a generic intersection problem (solving C = l simultaneously) since the axis condition is far cheaper.
  3. Commits to substituting the zero-coordinate into the curve only, because that immediately pins the point.
  4. Bridges to the concrete step: set y = 0 in the curve, solve for x, then apply the sign filter.
  5. Tactically keeps both roots visible until the sign condition is applied, to avoid silently dropping the correct one.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the axis condition: 'x-axis' → y = 0; 'y-axis' → x = 0.
  2. Identify the sign condition from the adjective: 'negative' → < 0, 'positive' → > 0.
  3. Substitute the zero coordinate into the curve equation and solve for the remaining variable.
  4. List all roots, then discard those violating the sign condition.
  5. State the resulting point and substitute it into the other object (line, second curve) to find unknown parameters.

Worked Micro-Example

Curve $C: y = 2x^2 - 50$ meets line $l$ on the negative x-axis. Set $y=0$: $2x^2-50=0 \Rightarrow x^2=25 \Rightarrow x=\pm 5$. The condition $x<0$ selects $x=-5$, so the intersection is $(-5,0)$. If $l$ has gradient $3$, then $l: y-0 = 3(x+5)$, i.e. $y = 3x+15$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

'Meets the positive y-axis'
Set $x = 0$ in the curve, solve for $y$, then keep only $y > 0$.
'Touches the x-axis'
Set $y = 0$ and additionally require a repeated root (discriminant $= 0$ for a quadratic).
'Crosses the y-axis below the origin'
Set $x = 0$, solve for $y$, then keep only $y < 0$.
Circle meets x-axis at two points, one on the negative side
Set $y = 0$ in the circle equation, solve the quadratic in $x$, then label the negative root as the required point.
Parametric curve meets an axis
Set the relevant coordinate function to $0$, solve for the parameter $t$, then evaluate the other coordinate and apply the sign filter.

Common Misreadings

  • Taking $x = +5$ instead of $-5$ by ignoring the word 'negative'.
  • Reading 'on the x-axis' as 'has x-intercept' but forgetting that this means $y = 0$, and instead solving $C = l$ directly.
  • Confusing 'negative x-axis' (a region of the x-axis) with 'negative x-coordinate' applied to a generic point not on an axis.
  • Interpreting 'touches' as ordinary intersection and missing the repeated-root requirement.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the $\pm$ when taking a square root and accidentally keeping the wrong sign.
  • Forgetting to substitute the found point into the second equation to determine the remaining unknown (e.g. the y-intercept of $l$).
  • Applying the sign filter to the wrong coordinate (e.g. requiring $y<0$ instead of $x<0$).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P1/P2 coordinate geometry decoding: axis intersections, equations of lines and curves, and quadratic root selection — no content beyond the IAL Maths/Further Maths syllabus.
