Algebra & Functions

Back-substitute into the linear equation, not the quadratic, to find paired values

Family: Back Substitution Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

This move fires after solving a system (or a single-variable quadratic derived from a system) and obtaining two values of one variable, when you still need the corresponding values of the other variable. The structural cue is that you have BOTH a linear equation (e.g. y = mx + c, y = a − x, r = k/(θ+2)) and a non-linear equation (a quadratic, product, area, or circle equation) available for back-substitution. Equivalent phrasings: 'I've found my x-values, now I need y'; 'I need to pair up the two roots with their partners'; 'the question asks for coordinate pairs / possible (r, θ) values'. The presence of two competing equations to substitute into is the defining structural cue.

Motivation — Why this works

Substituting into the linear equation is dramatically more efficient: it requires only one multiplication and one addition (or a single subtraction/division), whereas substituting into the non-linear equation produces a fresh quadratic for each value, multiplying the work and the risk of arithmetic error. Crucially, the linear equation gives a UNIQUE partner for each root with no ± ambiguity from a square root and no risk of introducing extraneous solutions. Mathematically this works because at any solution of the system, both equations must hold simultaneously — so the linear one suffices to recover the partner. Reserving the non-linear equation as an independent check (rather than as the substitution route) is a deliberate efficiency-and-verification decision.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice I have two values for one variable (e.g. x = 1/5 and x = −2, or θ = 4/3 and θ = 3) and the question asks for the corresponding partner values.
  2. I scan the available equations: there is a linear one (y = 3x + 4, or r = 10/(θ+2), or y = 175 − x) and a non-linear one (a circle, area, or product equation).
  3. I rule out substituting into the non-linear equation: it would produce a new quadratic per root, doubling the algebra and possibly introducing spurious roots from squaring or sign choices.
  4. I commit to the linear equation because each root maps to exactly ONE partner value via simple arithmetic — no ± ambiguity.
  5. I substitute each root in turn, being careful with fraction arithmetic (converting integers to common-denominator form before adding).
  6. Optionally, I verify by plugging the resulting pair into the non-linear equation as an independent check — this is now a free sanity-check rather than the main computation.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two roots α, β of the eliminated variable.
  2. Identify the linear equation in the system, rearranged so the partner variable is the subject (e.g. y = 3x + 4).
  3. Compute partner(α) by direct substitution into the linear equation.
  4. Compute partner(β) by direct substitution into the linear equation.
  5. State both solution pairs explicitly, e.g. (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂), or (r₁, θ₁) and (r₂, θ₂).
  6. (Optional verification) Substitute each pair into the non-linear equation to confirm it is satisfied.

Worked Micro-Example

System: $y = 3x + 4$ and $x^2 + y^2 + 6x - 4y = 4$. Eliminating gives $x = \tfrac{1}{5}$ or $x = -2$. Substitute into the LINEAR equation only: $x = \tfrac{1}{5} \Rightarrow y = 3 \cdot \tfrac{1}{5} + 4 = \tfrac{23}{5}$; $x = -2 \Rightarrow y = 3(-2) + 4 = -2$. Solution pairs: $\left(\tfrac{1}{5}, \tfrac{23}{5}\right)$ and $(-2, -2)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line-meets-curve (parabola, cubic, or circle) intersection problems
After eliminating one variable and solving the resulting quadratic, substitute each root into y = mx + c rather than the curve equation.
Sum-and-product systems (e.g. x + y = S, xy = P)
Substitute into y = S − x rather than y = P/x — subtraction beats division, especially with large numbers.
Sector / geometric problems with a perimeter (linear) and area (quadratic) constraint
Use the rearranged perimeter equation r = (P − rθ)/2 form to find r from each θ, rather than the area equation which is quadratic in r.
Parametric-Cartesian intersection problems
Back-substitute into the parametric equations if simpler than the Cartesian form.

Common Misreadings

  • Substituting both roots into the quadratic/non-linear equation, obtaining two partner values per root (four total), then being unable to decide which pairs are the genuine solutions.
  • Believing the non-linear equation is 'more authoritative' and so must be used for the substitution, when in fact either equation gives the same partners at intersection points.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic errors with fractions: e.g. $\tfrac{4}{3} + 2$ must be rewritten as $\tfrac{4}{3} + \tfrac{6}{3} = \tfrac{10}{3}$; or $3 \cdot \tfrac{1}{5} + 4 = \tfrac{3}{5} + \tfrac{20}{5}$.
  • Forgetting to find the partner for BOTH roots — the question's use of 'possible values' or 'points of intersection' implies multiple solution pairs.
  • Pairing the wrong partner with the wrong root when presenting answers (mixing up which y goes with which x).
  • Mistakenly substituting into a third (non-system) equation that happens to be in the question, rather than one of the two simultaneous equations.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1–P4) and Further Pure scope; no formula booklet entry is required. Applies to simultaneous equations (one linear, one quadratic), line-meets-curve geometry, and sector problems. Avoid generalising to systems where neither equation is linear — there the move does not apply and resultants/substitution choice becomes a different judgement.
