Algebra & Functions

Solve the parameter-free subsystem first, then back-substitute for the remaining unknown

Family: Equation Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A simultaneous system (typically 3 equations in 3 unknowns) where one or more equations involve only a subset of the unknowns, and a single 'odd' equation carries the extra unknown. Cue phrases: 'find the value of p such that...' alongside vector/coefficient-comparison equations in λ and μ. Recognise it whenever one variable appears in strictly fewer equations than the others.

Motivation — Why this works

If k equations involve only a subset of variables, those k equations form a self-contained subsystem that can be solved in isolation. Substituting the resulting numerical values into the remaining equation collapses it to a single linear equation in the leftover unknown. This respects the structure of the system and avoids propagating an extra symbol through every elimination step, which would multiply the chance of algebraic slips.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that equations (i) and (iii) contain only λ and μ, while (ii) is the sole equation involving p.
  2. Rule out a brute-force simultaneous elimination across all three equations — that would needlessly carry p through the algebra.
  3. Commit to solving the (i),(iii) pair first because it is a clean 2×2 linear system in λ and μ.
  4. Bridge: pick one of the two equations, isolate λ (or μ), and substitute into the other to get a single-variable equation.
  5. Once λ and μ are pinned to numbers, plug them into (ii); p falls out from a one-line linear equation.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify which variables appear in which equations; locate the variable that appears in the fewest equations.
  2. Group the equations that exclude that variable into a self-contained subsystem.
  3. Solve the subsystem by substitution or elimination to obtain numerical values for those variables.
  4. Substitute those numerical values into the remaining equation, reducing it to a linear equation in the deferred unknown.
  5. Solve for the deferred unknown and, if required, verify by checking all original equations.

Worked Micro-Example

Given (i) $2\lambda - 4\mu = 5$, (ii) $p - 5\lambda = -2 + \mu$, (iii) $7 + 4\lambda = 5 + 2\mu$. From (i): $\lambda = \tfrac{5+4\mu}{2}$. Sub into (iii): $7 + 2(5+4\mu) = 5 + 2\mu \Rightarrow \mu = -2$, hence $\lambda = -\tfrac{3}{2}$. Sub into (ii): $p - 5(-\tfrac{3}{2}) = -2 + (-2) \Rightarrow p = -\tfrac{23}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Vector equation $\mathbf{a} + \lambda\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{c} + \mu\mathbf{d}$ with an extra parameter p in only one component
Compare the two p-free components first to find λ and μ, then substitute into the third component to solve for p.
Coordinate geometry: three conditions on a curve where one introduces an extra constant (e.g. line through a point with unknown gradient meeting a curve at two specified points)
Use the two conditions that exclude the extra constant to fix the geometric data first, then impose the third.
Partial fractions / coefficient comparison where most equations involve only A and B, but one also involves C
Solve the A,B subsystem first by equating clean coefficients, then determine C from the remaining equation.
Intersection of two lines in 3D giving 3 component equations, with a parameter p ensuring intersection
Solve any two p-free components for the line parameters, then enforce the third to pin down p.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating all three equations as equally entangled and launching a full 3×3 elimination with p in tow.
  • Mistaking equation (ii) as the 'natural' starting point because it is listed in the middle, instead of scanning for structure.
  • Assuming p must be eliminated rather than deferred, leading to unnecessary symbolic manipulation.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when isolating λ from a fractional rearrangement such as $\lambda = (5+4\mu)/2$.
  • Dropping the factor of 2 when clearing denominators after substitution.
  • Forgetting to substitute both λ and μ (only one) into the p-equation.
  • Verifying p in the wrong equation — always recheck against the original (ii).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard for Edexcel IAL Pure (vectors, simultaneous equations, partial fractions) and Further Pure (line intersections, coefficient comparison); requires only linear algebra at A-level scope.
