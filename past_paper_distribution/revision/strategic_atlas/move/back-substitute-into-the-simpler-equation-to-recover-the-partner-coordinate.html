Coordinate Geometry

Back-substitute into the simpler equation to recover the partner coordinate

Family: Efficient Substitution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires once an intersection has been reduced to a single coordinate (e.g. $x = 5.2$) and the partner coordinate is still needed. Both original equations hold at the intersection, so either may be used. The cue is having a choice between a linear equation and a quadratic/curve equation, or between any two equations of differing arithmetic complexity. Also fires when one equation is given in explicit form $y = \dots$ and the other is implicit or higher-degree.

Motivation — Why this works

At an intersection point, every equation in the system is simultaneously satisfied, so each yields the same partner coordinate. Choosing the equation with the lowest arithmetic load (fewest multiplications, no squaring, no surds) minimises slip risk. The discarded equation remains available as a free verification check. This is a pure efficiency principle, not a mathematical necessity.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the $x$-coordinate is now known and only $y$ remains; both equations of the original system are still on the page.
  2. Rule out automatically substituting into the equation that was used to form the solving polynomial — that habit wastes arithmetic.
  3. Commit to the structurally simplest equation: a line beats a quadratic, a quadratic beats a cubic, an explicit form beats an implicit one.
  4. Bridge: write down the chosen equation and substitute the known coordinate directly.
  5. Tactical choice: if time allows, evaluate the discarded equation as a sanity check that both yield the same $y$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify both equations that hold at the intersection point.
  2. Compare arithmetic cost: count multiplications, squarings, and term combinations.
  3. Substitute the known coordinate into the simpler equation.
  4. Evaluate to obtain the partner coordinate.
  5. (Optional) Cross-check by substituting into the other equation.

Worked Micro-Example

Curve $y = x^2 - 5x + 13$ meets line $y = 2.7x$ at $N$ where $x_N = 5.2$. Using the line: $y_N = 2.7 \times 5.2 = 14.04$. Hence $N = (5.2,\,14.04)$. Check (optional): $5.2^2 - 5(5.2) + 13 = 27.04 - 26 + 13 = 14.04$. ✓

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line meets circle, e.g. $y = mx + c$ and $(x-a)^2 + (y-b)^2 = r^2$
After solving for $x$, substitute into the line $y = mx + c$ rather than the circle to avoid square roots and sign ambiguity.
Two curves where one is given as $y = f(x)$ and the other implicitly
Use the explicit $y = f(x)$ form to obtain $y$; the implicit equation serves as the check.
Tangent to a curve at a known $x$-value
Use the original curve equation (not the tangent) to find $y$ directly, since the tangent's coefficients may be messier.
Parametric intersection where $x = f(t)$, $y = g(t)$ and $t$ is found
Substitute $t$ into whichever of $f$ or $g$ is algebraically simpler first; use the other as confirmation.
Simultaneous linear–quadratic system in Further Pure
After eliminating to find one variable, return to the linear equation, never the quadratic.

Common Misreadings

  • Believing one must substitute back into the equation that produced the polynomial being solved.
  • Thinking the curve equation is 'more accurate' than the line — both are exact at the intersection.
  • Treating the choice as mathematically constrained rather than as an efficiency decision.

Pitfalls

  • If the simpler equation was itself derived (e.g. a rearranged tangent), any earlier algebra error propagates silently — a quick check in the other equation catches this.
  • Arithmetic slip in a single multiplication (e.g. $2.7 \times 5.2$) when working without a calculator.
  • Forgetting to report the full coordinate pair, leaving only the $y$-value.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard practice across Edexcel IAL P1–P4 coordinate geometry, including line–curve intersections, tangents/normals, and circle problems; no content beyond syllabus is required.
