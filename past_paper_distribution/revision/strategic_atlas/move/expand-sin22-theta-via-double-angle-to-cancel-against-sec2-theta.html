Trigger — When to use

Fires when an integrand (or expression) contains sin²(2θ)·sec²θ, or more generally sin²(nθ) multiplied by sec²θ where the embedded cos²θ from the double-angle expansion will meet a 1/cos²θ. Cue words: 'sec²θ alongside a sin of double angle', 'integrand looks like a product of squared trig functions'. Also fires for cos²(2θ)·cosec²θ by symmetry. The student should recognise the cancellation potential before choosing an identity.

Motivation — Why this works

The double-angle identity sin2θ = 2sinθcosθ gives sin²2θ = 4sin²θcos²θ. Multiplying by sec²θ = 1/cos²θ cancels the cos²θ exactly, leaving 4sin²θ — a standard power-reduction target. Choosing the power-reduction identity (1−cos4θ)/2 instead keeps the sec²θ stuck and produces a much harder integral. The strategic insight is to pick the identity that creates cancellation, not just the one that 'reduces a power'.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice sin²2θ·sec²θ — a compound-angle squared sitting next to 1/cos²θ.
  2. I rule out applying power-reduction sin²2θ = (1−cos4θ)/2 directly: it leaves sec²θ untouched and the resulting product (1−cos4θ)sec²θ is harder than what I started with.
  3. I commit to the double-angle expansion sin2θ = 2sinθcosθ because the cos²θ it produces will exactly cancel with the 1/cos²θ in sec²θ.
  4. First concrete operation: write sin²2θ = (2sinθcosθ)² = 4sin²θcos²θ and combine with sec²θ.
  5. Inside the move: be careful to square the 2 (giving 4, not 2), then use power-reduction sin²θ = (1−cos2θ)/2 on the clean residue.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write sin2θ = 2sinθcosθ, so sin²2θ = 4sin²θcos²θ.
  2. Multiply by sec²θ = 1/cos²θ.
  3. Cancel cos²θ to obtain 4sin²θ.
  4. If integrating, apply sin²θ = (1−cos2θ)/2 to give 2(1−cos2θ).
  5. Integrate term-by-term and reattach any outer constants.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int \sin^2 2\theta \, \sec^2\theta \, d\theta$. Write $\sin^2 2\theta = (2\sin\theta\cos\theta)^2 = 4\sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta$. Then $\sin^2 2\theta \sec^2\theta = 4\sin^2\theta = 2(1-\cos 2\theta)$. Hence the integral is $2\theta - \sin 2\theta + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

cos²2θ·cosec²θ
Use cos2θ = ... but better expand sin2θ = 2sinθcosθ in cosec²θ context: write cos²2θ·(1/sin²θ) and exploit sin2θ identity in the reciprocal direction; or use cos²2θ = (cos²θ−sin²θ)² and cancel against sin²θ where possible.
sin²2θ·cosec²θ
Expand sin²2θ = 4sin²θcos²θ; the sin²θ cancels with cosec²θ = 1/sin²θ, leaving 4cos²θ which then uses cos²θ = (1+cos2θ)/2.
sin²2θ·tan²θ or sin²2θ·cot²θ
Same expansion creates cos²θ·tan²θ = sin²θ or cos²θ·cot²θ·... — partial cancellation followed by Pythagorean tidy-up.
Reduction-formula setting with sinⁿθ
Decompose sinⁿθ = sinⁿ⁻¹θ · sinθ to enable integration by parts; same forward-looking decomposition principle.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating sin²2θ as a single 'reduce the power' target and applying (1−cos4θ)/2, missing the sec²θ cancellation.
  • Trying a substitution u = tanθ or u = sinθ directly without first simplifying the product, leading to a messy rational integrand.
  • Reading sec²θ only as 'the derivative of tanθ' and forcing a tan-substitution route prematurely.

Pitfalls

  • Writing sin²2θ = 2sin²θcos²θ (forgetting to square the 2); the correct coefficient is 4.
  • Cancelling cos²θ but forgetting to keep the constant 4, yielding sin²θ instead of 4sin²θ.
  • After reducing to 4sin²θ, mis-applying power-reduction as (1−cos2θ) rather than (1−cos2θ)/2.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the double-angle identity sin2θ = 2sinθcosθ, sec²θ = 1/cos²θ, and the power-reduction form sin²θ = (1−cos2θ)/2 — all standard in Edexcel IAL Pure (P3/P4) and reused in Further Pure integration.
