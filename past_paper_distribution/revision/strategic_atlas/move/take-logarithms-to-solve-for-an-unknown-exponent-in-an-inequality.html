Sequences & Series

Take logarithms to solve for an unknown exponent in an inequality

Family: Log Transformation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever the unknown N (or n) sits in an exponent inside an inequality or equation, e.g. r^N > k, a \cdot r^{n-1} \geq M, or 'find the smallest n such that S_n exceeds ...'. Typical surfaces: geometric sequence/series threshold problems, compound-interest 'when does the balance first exceed' questions, and exponential growth/decay models. The cue is that no algebraic rearrangement can isolate N because it is locked in the index of a power.

Motivation — Why this works

Logarithms are the inverse of exponentiation, and the law \ln(r^N) = N\ln r brings the exponent down to a coefficient, converting an exponential inequality into a linear one in N. The direction of the resulting inequality depends on the sign of \ln r: positive when r>1 (so direction preserved), negative when 0<r<1 (so direction flips). Either natural log or log base 10 works because the base cancels in the ratio \ln k / \ln r.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the unknown N is in the exponent of r^N — standard algebra cannot isolate it.
  2. I rule out term-by-term computation: it works but is slow and unreliable for large N or in exam time.
  3. I commit to taking logs because \ln(r^N)=N\ln r is the only tool that brings N down to a coefficient.
  4. Before dividing through by \ln r, I check whether r>1 or 0<r<1 to decide if the inequality direction is preserved or flipped.
  5. After getting N > (or <) some real number, I round in the correct direction for a strict vs. weak inequality and pick the smallest/largest valid integer.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Isolate the pure exponential form, e.g. r^N > k.
  2. Apply \ln (or \log) to both sides: N\ln r > \ln k.
  3. Determine the sign of \ln r from the value of r (>1 or between 0 and 1).
  4. Divide by \ln r, flipping the inequality if \ln r < 0.
  5. Evaluate the resulting bound numerically.
  6. Round to the correct integer, respecting strict (>) vs. non-strict (\geq) inequality.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve r^N > 2.5 with r = (17/15)^{1/3}. Take \ln: N\ln r > \ln 2.5. Since r>1, \ln r>0, so N > \ln(2.5)/\ln(r) = 3\ln(2.5)/\ln(17/15) \approx 21.96. Smallest integer: N = 22.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Geometric decay, 0 < r < 1, e.g. r^N < \varepsilon
\ln r < 0, so dividing by \ln r flips the inequality; answer becomes N > \ln\varepsilon/\ln r.
Compound interest: P(1+i)^n \geq T
Divide by P first, then take logs: n \geq \ln(T/P)/\ln(1+i); since 1+i>1, direction preserved.
Geometric series sum threshold S_n > M with |r|<1 and a>0
Rearrange a(1-r^n)/(1-r) > M into r^n < 1 - M(1-r)/a, then take logs and flip because \ln r < 0.
Equality form r^N = k (find exact N)
Same log step but solve as equation: N = \ln k / \ln r, no inequality direction to track.
Exponential model A e^{kt} = B
Divide by A then take \ln: kt = \ln(B/A), so t = \ln(B/A)/k.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the problem as solvable by guess-and-check on successive terms instead of recognising the log technique.
  • Reading r^N > 2.5 as N^r > 2.5 and trying to take an N-th root.
  • Thinking the choice of log base affects the final numerical answer.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to check the sign of \ln r and failing to flip the inequality when 0 < r < 1.
  • Rounding the bound the wrong way: for N > 21.96 the answer is 22, not 21, because the inequality is strict.
  • Dividing by \ln r before isolating the exponential, leaving stray constants that contaminate the bound.
  • Mixing \ln and \log_{10} values across lines of working when transcribing from a calculator.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 (logarithms and geometric sequences) and reused in compound interest and exponential models; no techniques beyond standard log laws and inequality manipulation are required.
