Integration

Re-express x, dx, and all radical parts in the new variable u

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a substitution u = g(x) is mandated (or chosen) and the integrand contains multiple x-dependent pieces — typically x itself, a radical or composite like √x or eˣ, and dx. Recognisable cues: the substitution does not directly simplify the whole integrand in one move, and rearranging u back to x produces a non-trivial expression. Common phrasings: 'Use the substitution u = ...', 'Hence show that ∫... = ∫... du'.

Motivation — Why this works

A definite or indefinite integral in u is only valid once every x has been eliminated. The chain rule gives du/dx, which inverts to dx = h(x) du; if h(x) still contains x or √x, those residual pieces must also be rewritten in u. The underlying principle is that ∫f(x)dx = ∫f(x(u)) · (dx/du) du requires both f and dx/du to be pure functions of u before integration can proceed.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the integrand has several x-dependent pieces (e.g. x in a denominator, √x inside a bracket, and dx) — not just one composite.
  2. Rules out trying to substitute piecewise or leaving dx in mixed form, since that would yield an integral in two variables.
  3. Commits to a full conversion: every x, √x, and dx must become an expression in u alone before integrating.
  4. Bridges by rearranging u = g(x) to isolate x (and any radical) in terms of u, then differentiating to obtain dx in terms of u.
  5. Tactically checks the dx expression for hidden x or √x and back-substitutes once more so the final integrand is purely in u.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. From u = g(x), rearrange to express x (and any radical such as √x or eˣ) in terms of u.
  2. Differentiate u with respect to x to get du/dx.
  3. Invert to obtain dx = (1/(du/dx)) du, written initially in x.
  4. Replace any remaining x or radical inside the dx expression with its u-form.
  5. Substitute all pieces (x, radicals, dx) into the integrand and simplify to a pure-u integral.

Worked Micro-Example

For $\int \frac{1}{x(2+\sqrt{x})^3}\,dx$ with $u = 2+\sqrt{x}$: $\sqrt{x}=u-2$, so $x=(u-2)^2$. Then $\frac{du}{dx}=\frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$ gives $dx = 2\sqrt{x}\,du = 2(u-2)\,du$. The integral becomes $\int \frac{2(u-2)}{(u-2)^2 u^3}\,du = \int \frac{2}{(u-2)u^3}\,du$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Substitution u = 1 + eˣ in an integral containing eˣ and dx
From eˣ = u − 1, get dx = du/eˣ = du/(u−1); replace every eˣ in the integrand by u − 1.
Trigonometric substitution x = a sin θ for ∫1/√(a²−x²) dx (Further Pure)
dx = a cos θ dθ and √(a²−x²) = a cos θ; both x and the radical are rewritten in θ before integrating.
Substitution u = √(x+1) where the integrand has x and √(x+1)
x = u²−1 and dx = 2u du; the lone x in the integrand must also be replaced by u²−1.
Substitution u = ln x with 1/x in the integrand
du = dx/x means the 1/x and dx combine into du directly — a cleaner case where no residual x remains.

Common Misreadings

  • Forgetting to replace the √x (or eˣ) that hides inside the dx expression, leaving a mixed x-and-u integral.
  • Treating du/dx as a literal fraction and 'cancelling' without properly solving for dx in terms of u.
  • Substituting only the obvious composite (e.g. (2+√x)³ → u³) and leaving the standalone x or √x untouched.

Pitfalls

  • Writing dx = 2√x du and stopping there, instead of converting √x to (u−2).
  • Squaring carelessly when isolating x, e.g. writing x = u² − 2 instead of (u−2)².
  • Forgetting to also convert the limits of integration to u-values in a definite integral.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P3/P4) integration by substitution; the same total-conversion principle underlies trigonometric substitutions encountered in Further Pure.
