Trigonometry

Find vertical shift k by equating shifted extremum to given value

Family: Inverse Transformation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The curve has form y = A cos x° + k (or A sin x° + k) with k unknown, and the question states the maximum or minimum y-value of the curve. Cues include phrases like 'the minimum value of C is −1', 'the curve has greatest value 7', or a stationary point's y-coordinate is given. Fires whenever an extremum of a vertically-translated sinusoid is supplied and a constant must be recovered.

Motivation — Why this works

Adding k to f(x) translates the graph vertically by k without altering where extrema occur or their spread. Hence min(A cos x° + k) = −|A| + k and max(A cos x° + k) = |A| + k. Equating the appropriate expression to the given extremum yields a one-step linear equation for k, sidestepping any trig equation in x.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that +k is an outside transformation — it shifts y-values uniformly and leaves the x-coordinates of extrema fixed.
  2. Rule out solving A cos x° + k = (given extremum) as a trig equation in x; that conflates locating x with finding k.
  3. Commit to the extremum-shift principle: new extremum = old extremum + k.
  4. Identify the old extremum from the amplitude: min(A cos x°) = −|A|, max(A cos x°) = |A|.
  5. Decide which extremum the question gives (min vs max) so the correct sign of |A| is used, then equate and solve.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify amplitude |A| from the coefficient of cos/sin.
  2. Write min(A cos x° + k) = −|A| + k (or max = |A| + k).
  3. Set this equal to the given extremum value.
  4. Solve the resulting linear equation for k.
  5. If asked, propagate k to the other extremum (e.g. max = |A| + k).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $y = 4\cos x° + k$ has minimum $-1$. Amplitude is $4$, so $\min(4\cos x°) = -4$. Hence $-4 + k = -1 \Rightarrow k = 3$. Maximum of the curve is $4 + 3 = 7$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Maximum given instead of minimum, e.g. y = 3 sin x° + k has max 5
Use |A| + k = given max; here 3 + k = 5 so k = 2.
Curve y = A sin(Bx + C) + k with range stated
Range is [k − |A|, k + |A|]; use either endpoint to solve for k since B, C don't affect range.
Negative amplitude form y = −A cos x° + k
min still equals −|A| + k; the sign of A doesn't change the range, only where the extrema occur in x.
Both max and min given, find A and k together
Solve simultaneously: k = (max + min)/2, |A| = (max − min)/2.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating k as an amplitude scaling rather than a vertical translation.
  • Reading the given y-value as a particular point on the curve rather than as the extremum.
  • Mistaking the coefficient A for k or vice versa when both letters appear.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error: writing −4 − k = −1 instead of −4 + k = −1.
  • Using +|A| when the minimum (not maximum) was given.
  • Forgetting to add k when subsequently quoting the maximum or a stationary y-coordinate.
  • Attempting to solve cos x° = −1 to find k, introducing irrelevant x-values.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure (P2) graph-transformation content: vertical translations of y = A sin x° + k or y = A cos x° + k and their ranges/extrema.
