Integration

Apply arc-length/surface-area formula and collapse integrand to reduction family

Family: Formula Application And Simplification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A later part of an FP3 question asks for the arc length or surface area of revolution of an explicitly given curve, after earlier parts established a reduction formula for $\int \sin^n x\,dx$ or $\int \cos^n x\,dx$. The curve is engineered so that $1+(dy/dx)^2$ becomes a perfect square. Phrasings include 'find the length of the arc', 'the curve is rotated about the x-axis, find the surface area', or 'hence find...'. The 'hence' is the strongest cue that the reduction result must be reused.

Motivation — Why this works

The standard formulae $L=\int\sqrt{1+(dy/dx)^2}\,dx$ and $S=2\pi\int y\sqrt{1+(dy/dx)^2}\,dx$ turn geometry into a definite integral. Examiners choose curves so that $1+(dy/dx)^2$ factors as a perfect square, allowing the radical to vanish and leaving a clean power of $\sin$ or $\cos$. This integrand is exactly the family handled by the part (a) reduction formula, so the multi-part structure pays off via direct recursive evaluation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question sits in an FP3 reduction-formula context and asks for arc length or a surface of revolution — the parts are almost certainly linked.
  2. Rule out attempting a fresh integration technique on the raw integrand; the design intent is to reuse part (a).
  3. Commit to the relevant geometric formula and aim to force $1+(dy/dx)^2$ into a perfect square.
  4. Bridge: differentiate $y$, square it, add 1, and algebraically manipulate (factor, complete the square, use a Pythagorean identity) until the square root collapses.
  5. Tactically, after simplifying, identify the exact $\sin^n$ or $\cos^n$ form and apply the reduction formula recursively down to a base case ($n=0$ or $n=1$).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute $dy/dx$ from the given curve.
  2. Form $1+(dy/dx)^2$ and simplify into a perfect square.
  3. Take the square root cleanly (mind the sign on the chosen interval).
  4. Substitute into $L=\int_a^b \sqrt{1+(dy/dx)^2}\,dx$ or $S=2\pi\int_a^b y\sqrt{1+(dy/dx)^2}\,dx$.
  5. Recognise the integrand as $\sin^n x$ or $\cos^n x$ and apply the reduction formula from part (a).
  6. Substitute limits and reduce to the base-case integral to obtain a closed form.

Worked Micro-Example

For $y=\ln(\sec x)$ on $[0,\pi/3]$: $dy/dx=\tan x$, so $1+(dy/dx)^2=\sec^2 x$. Then $L=\int_0^{\pi/3}\sec x\,dx=[\ln|\sec x+\tan x|]_0^{\pi/3}=\ln(2+\sqrt{3})$. In a reduction-style question, $1+(dy/dx)^2$ instead simplifies to e.g. $\cos^{-2k}x$, leaving $\int \cos^{n} x\,dx$ to be finished by the part (a) recursion.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Parametric curve $x=x(t),\,y=y(t)$
Use $L=\int\sqrt{(dx/dt)^2+(dy/dt)^2}\,dt$; aim for $(dx/dt)^2+(dy/dt)^2$ to be a perfect square in $t$.
Surface of revolution about the x-axis
Use $S=2\pi\int y\sqrt{1+(dy/dx)^2}\,dx$; the extra factor of $y$ often boosts the power of $\sin$ or $\cos$ in the integrand by one.
Surface of revolution about the y-axis
Use $S=2\pi\int x\sqrt{1+(dy/dx)^2}\,dx$ (or the parametric analogue), with $x$ replacing $y$ as the radius.
Curve given as $x=g(y)$
Use $L=\int\sqrt{1+(dx/dy)^2}\,dy$ and integrate with respect to $y$.

Common Misreadings

  • Computing arc length when surface area is asked (forgetting the $2\pi y$ factor).
  • Failing to spot that the integrand is engineered to match the part (a) reduction formula and attempting a fresh substitution or integration by parts.
  • Misreading the axis of revolution and using $y$ instead of $x$ as the radius (or vice versa).

Pitfalls

  • Algebraic slips when squaring $dy/dx$ or expanding $1+(dy/dx)^2$.
  • Not forcing the expression under the radical into a perfect square, leaving an intractable integrand.
  • Dropping the $2\pi$ in surface area, or losing the $y$ factor.
  • Sign errors when removing the square root on intervals where the inside is positive but the simplified form could be negative.

A-level Scope Notes

Arc length and surfaces of revolution (Cartesian and parametric) together with reduction formulae for $\int\sin^n x\,dx$ and $\int\cos^n x\,dx$ are standard Edexcel IAL FP3 content; polar arc length is also in FP3 scope.
