Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a single fraction contains a mix of positive and negative powers of the same base — most commonly e^{-x}/e^{-2x} alongside e^x in logarithm/exponential simplification, or (ax+b)^{-1/2} alongside (ax+b)^{1/2} in a quotient-rule output. Visual cues include: (i) a mark scheme demanding 'no negative powers of e' or 'in the form p(x)/(ax+b)^{3/2}', (ii) a numerator that refuses to combine because half its terms have negative exponents, (iii) terms like e^x − e^{-x} or A(4x−1)^{1/2} − B(4x−1)^{-1/2}. Equivalent phrasings: 'clear the negative power', 'rationalise the surd/exponential', 'multiply top and bottom by the awkward factor'.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying numerator and denominator by the same quantity is multiplying by a disguised 1, so the value is unchanged — but it shifts every exponent uniformly upward. By choosing that quantity to be the most negative power present (with its sign flipped), every negative exponent is lifted to 0 or higher, leaving a polynomial-style numerator over a single clean power. This is the direct analogue of rationalising a surd denominator via the conjugate or via √a/√a: same principle (multiply by 1 to improve form), different surface. The underlying identity is the index law a^m · a^n = a^{m+n}.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the combined fraction has terms with mixed-sign exponents of one common base — e.g. both e^{x} and e^{-x}, or both (4x−1)^{1/2} and (4x−1)^{-1/2} — so no further collection is possible in this form.
  2. Rule out: collecting like terms (impossible — exponents differ), expanding (already done), factoring out a positive power (would still leave fractional or negative residues).
  3. Commit to multiplying numerator AND denominator by a power of the common base, because this is the only operation that uniformly raises every exponent without changing the value.
  4. Identify the most negative exponent in the expression — call it −n. Choose the multiplier to be (base)^{n}, so that −n + n = 0 (the worst term becomes 1) and every other exponent increases by n.
  5. Apply the multiplier carefully to EVERY term in the numerator and to the denominator, using a^m · a^n = a^{m+n}; do not multiply only one side.
  6. Verify: every exponent in the final form is non-negative (or matches the required target form, e.g. denominator (4x−1)^{3/2}).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Locate the most negative exponent of the common base; call it −n (so n>0).
  2. Multiply numerator and denominator by (base)^{n}, written as a fraction (base)^{n}/(base)^{n} = 1.
  3. Distribute (base)^{n} across every term in the numerator using the index law: (base)^{n} · (base)^{k} = (base)^{n+k}.
  4. Apply the same to the denominator: a single power becomes one cleaner power.
  5. Simplify and collect: numerator is now a polynomial in the base; denominator is a single non-negative power.

Worked Micro-Example

Simplify $\dfrac{2(4x-1)^{1/2} - 2(2x+3)(4x-1)^{-1/2}}{4x-1}$. Multiply top and bottom by $(4x-1)^{1/2}$: numerator becomes $2(4x-1) - 2(2x+3) = 8x-2-4x-6 = 4x-8$; denominator becomes $(4x-1)^{3/2}$. Result: $\dfrac{4x-8}{(4x-1)^{3/2}}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Logarithm simplification with e^{-x} terms inside a single ln
Multiply argument's numerator and denominator by e^{nx} where −n is the most negative exponent; e.g. (e^x−e^{-x})/(e^x+e^{-x}) × e^x/e^x = (e^{2x}−1)/(e^{2x}+1).
Surd denominator like 1/(√a − √b)
Multiply by conjugate (√a + √b)/(√a + √b) — same 'multiply by 1' principle, exploiting difference of squares instead of index laws.
Product-rule output rather than quotient-rule
Factor out the lowest power of the common base instead of multiplying through — achieves the same uniform-exponent effect.
Differential equation with mixed e^{kt} and e^{-kt}
Multiply integrand or solution by e^{nt}/e^{nt} to remove negative exponentials before integrating or comparing forms.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the mixed-exponent numerator as already simplified and stopping early, losing presentation marks.
  • Confusing this with conjugate-multiplication and trying to multiply by (e^x − e^{-x}) instead of by e^x.
  • Thinking a negative exponent in the denominator must be 'cancelled' rather than rationalised — leading to algebraic errors.

Pitfalls

  • Multiplying only the numerator (or only the denominator) — this changes the value of the expression.
  • Failing to distribute the multiplier across EVERY term in the numerator, especially when there are three or more terms.
  • Choosing too small a power, so some negative exponents remain; or too large, leaving unnecessary high powers.
  • Index-law slips: e^x · e^x = e^{2x} (not e^{x²}); (4x−1)^{1/2} · (4x−1)^{1/2} = (4x−1) (not (4x−1)^{1/4}).
  • Sign errors when expanding the resulting numerator, e.g. 2(4x−1) − 2(2x+3) miscomputed.

A-level Scope Notes

Wholly within Edexcel IAL Pure (P2/P3/P4) — uses only index laws (formula-booklet-free) and standard quotient-rule simplification. No appeal to hyperbolic identities or complex exponentials is needed; avoid generalising to non-commutative or matrix bases.
