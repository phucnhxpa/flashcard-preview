Trigonometry

Expand R·cos(x − α) and equate coefficients of cos x and sin x

Family: R Formula Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an expression of the form a·cos x + b·sin x must be rewritten as a single trig function, and the question explicitly prescribes the target form R·cos(x − α) with R > 0 and α acute. Typical phrasings include 'express in the form R cos(x − α)', 'hence find the maximum value', or 'hence solve a·cos x + b·sin x = k'. The presence of both a cos x and a sin x term with non-trivial coefficients is the structural cue.

Motivation — Why this works

The compound-angle identity cos(x − α) = cos x·cos α + sin x·sin α expands the target into the same shape as the given linear combination. Matching coefficients of cos x and sin x converts the identity into two simultaneous equations R cos α = a and R sin α = b, which determine R (via R² = a² + b²) and α (via tan α = b/a). This reduces a transcendental rewriting problem to elementary algebra.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the expression is a·cos x + b·sin x with two distinct coefficients, so it is not already a single sinusoid.
  2. I rule out R·sin(x + α), R·sin(x − α), and R·cos(x + α) because the question specifies R·cos(x − α) — the form dictates which compound-angle identity to use.
  3. I commit to expanding cos(x − α) because it produces a cos x·(R cos α) + sin x·(R sin α) structure that mirrors the given expression exactly.
  4. Bridging to operations: I write R·cos(x − α) = R cos α·cos x + R sin α·sin x and prepare to read off coefficients.
  5. Tactically, I label the two equations (1) and (2) so I can later divide (2)/(1) to get tan α and square-and-add to get R².

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Apply the identity: R·cos(x − α) = R cos α·cos x + R sin α·sin x.
  2. Equate the cos x coefficients: R cos α = a (the coefficient of cos x in the original).
  3. Equate the sin x coefficients: R sin α = b (the coefficient of sin x in the original).
  4. Set up for downstream work: R = √(a² + b²) from squaring and adding; tan α = b/a from dividing.

Worked Micro-Example

Express √5·cos x + 2√5·sin x in the form R·cos(x − α), R > 0, 0 < α < π/2.\nExpand: R·cos(x − α) = R cos α·cos x + R sin α·sin x.\nMatch: R cos α = √5 …(1), R sin α = 2√5 …(2).\nSquare and add: R² = 5 + 20 = 25, so R = 5.\nDivide (2)/(1): tan α = 2, so α = arctan 2 ≈ 1.107.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Target form R·sin(x + α) for a·sin x + b·cos x
Expand sin(x + α) = sin x·cos α + cos x·sin α; equate R cos α = a (coefficient of sin x) and R sin α = b (coefficient of cos x).
Target form R·sin(x − α) for a·sin x − b·cos x
Expand sin(x − α) = sin x·cos α − cos x·sin α; equate R cos α = a and R sin α = b so both are positive.
Target form R·cos(x + α) for a·cos x − b·sin x
Expand cos(x + α) = cos x·cos α − sin x·sin α; equate R cos α = a and R sin α = b, keeping α acute.
Variable other than x, e.g. 2θ or (θ/2)
Treat the whole argument as a single variable; the matching procedure is identical, only the final solution stage rescales the angle.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading cos(x − α) as cos(x + α), which flips the sign on the sin x coefficient and forces a negative α.
  • Matching R cos α to the sin x coefficient instead of the cos x coefficient (swapping the two equations).
  • Assuming R is the larger of a and b rather than √(a² + b²).

Pitfalls

  • Choosing the wrong target form (e.g. R·sin instead of R·cos) then producing α outside the stated range 0 < α < π/2.
  • Forgetting that R > 0 is a stipulation; taking the negative square root invalidates the answer.
  • Computing tan α = a/b instead of b/a by reading the equations in the wrong order.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P3): the R-formula via compound-angle identities, with R > 0 and α acute, is standard for solving a·cos x + b·sin x = k and finding extrema.
