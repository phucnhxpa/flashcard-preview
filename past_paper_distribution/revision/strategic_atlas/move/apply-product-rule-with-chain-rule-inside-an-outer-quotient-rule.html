Differentiation

Apply product rule with chain rule inside an outer quotient rule

Family: Nested Differentiation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when differentiating a quotient $p/q$ where the denominator $q$ is itself a product of two non-trivial functions, at least one of which is composite (e.g. a surd, log-power, or exponential of a function). Typical surface: finding $\mathrm{d}^2y/\mathrm{d}x^2$ when $\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x$ is already a quotient with a composite denominator such as $2x^2(4+\ln x)^{1/2}$. Cue words: 'second derivative', 'simplify to a single fraction', or any $q$ written as polynomial $\times$ (inner)$^{n}$.

Motivation — Why this works

The derivative of a product-of-composites cannot be obtained by a single rule, so rules must be nested: chain rule inside product rule, with the result then fed into the quotient rule. Consolidating $q'$ over the common surd factor before substitution prevents a four-term numerator from exploding into an unmanageable expression. The underlying principle is that differentiation operators compose in the order the function is built, so a clean algebraic tidy at each layer keeps the final $p'q-pq'$ tractable.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that $q$ factors as polynomial $\times$ composite, so $q'$ alone needs two rules.
  2. Rule out treating $q$ as a single chain (the polynomial factor blocks it) or expanding (the surd blocks it).
  3. Commit to product rule on $q$ with chain rule on the surd, planning to combine over the common surd before invoking the quotient rule.
  4. Compute each piece: $\frac{d}{dx}[2x^2]=4x$ and $\frac{d}{dx}[(4+\ln x)^{1/2}]=\frac{1}{2x(4+\ln x)^{1/2}}$.
  5. Tactically pull $(4+\ln x)^{1/2}$ out as common factor in $q'$ first, so the eventual $pq'$ shares structure with $p'q$ and simplifies.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify $p$ and $q$ from $\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x$; differentiate $p$ directly.
  2. On $q=u(x)\,v(x)^{n}$, apply product rule: $q'=u'v^{n}+u\cdot n v^{n-1}v'$.
  3. Combine $q'$ over the lowest power of the inner function, here $(4+\ln x)^{1/2}$, producing a single fraction.
  4. Substitute into quotient rule $\dfrac{p'q-pq'}{q^{2}}$.
  5. Multiply numerator and denominator by the surd to clear fractional powers and reach a single simplified fraction.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{-(7+2\ln x)}{2x^{2}(4+\ln x)^{1/2}}$, with $p=-(7+2\ln x)$, $q=2x^{2}(4+\ln x)^{1/2}$: $p'=-2/x$, and $q'=4x(4+\ln x)^{1/2}+\dfrac{x}{(4+\ln x)^{1/2}}=\dfrac{x(17+4\ln x)}{(4+\ln x)^{1/2}}$. Then $\dfrac{d^{2}y}{dx^{2}}=\dfrac{p'q-pq'}{q^{2}}$, which after multiplying through by $(4+\ln x)^{1/2}$ simplifies to a single fraction with denominator $4x^{3}(4+\ln x)^{3/2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quotient with denominator $x^{n}\sqrt{f(x)}$ where $f$ is any differentiable inner function
Same product+chain layering; the inner derivative $f'(x)$ replaces $1/x$.
Implicit second derivative where $\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x$ is a quotient containing $y$
After product rule on the denominator, replace any $\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}x$ that appears using the first-derivative expression.
Parametric second derivative $\dfrac{d}{dt}\!\left(\dfrac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}\right)\!\big/\dfrac{dx}{dt}$ where $dx/dt$ is a product
Outer quotient rule still applies; the inner $q'$ is a product-rule expression in $t$.
Denominator $e^{g(x)}h(x)$
Chain rule on $e^{g(x)}$ produces $g'(x)e^{g(x)}$; factor $e^{g(x)}$ out when combining $q'$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $2x^{2}(4+\ln x)^{1/2}$ as a single chain and forgetting the polynomial factor needs the product rule.
  • Reading the surd as $(4+\ln x)^{1/2}$ but differentiating it as if it were $\sqrt{x}$, dropping the inner $1/x$.
  • Mistaking the move for logarithmic differentiation; here direct product+chain inside quotient is cleaner.

Pitfalls

  • Omitting the chain-rule factor $1/x$ when differentiating $(4+\ln x)^{1/2}$.
  • Failing to combine $q'$ over the common surd before substituting, leading to a four-term numerator.
  • Sign errors in $p'q-pq'$ when $p$ is already negative.
  • Forgetting to square $q$ correctly: $q^{2}=4x^{4}(4+\ln x)$, not $4x^{4}(4+\ln x)^{1/2}$.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only product rule, chain rule, quotient rule, and derivatives of $\ln x$ and powers — all firmly within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 differentiation. No techniques beyond standard A-level are required.
