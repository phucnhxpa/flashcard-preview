Matrices

Use det(M − λI) = 0 to solve for unknown matrix entry

Family: Eigenvalue Condition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a matrix M contains an unknown constant (e.g. k) and the question states that a specific scalar λ is an eigenvalue of M, then asks for the possible values of that constant. Phrasings include ‘given that 3 is an eigenvalue of M, find k’ or ‘the matrix M has eigenvalue λ = …; determine the value(s) of the parameter’. The presence of both a named eigenvalue and a parametrised matrix is the key cue.

Motivation — Why this works

By definition, λ is an eigenvalue of M iff (M − λI) is singular, i.e. det(M − λI) = 0. Substituting the known λ collapses the characteristic determinant into a polynomial in the unknown parameter alone, converting a matrix problem into ordinary algebra. This is the most direct exploitation of the characteristic equation when one root is already supplied. The number of solutions for k matches the degree the parameter attains in the determinant expansion.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that one eigenvalue is given but a matrix entry k is unknown — a single equation linking them is needed.
  2. Rule out trying to find all eigenvalues from scratch (would need k first) and rule out det(M) = 0 (that is the singular condition, not the eigenvalue condition).
  3. Commit to the characteristic identity det(M − λI) = 0 because it directly encodes ‘λ is an eigenvalue’.
  4. Bridge to computation by forming M − λI explicitly: subtract λ from every diagonal entry, keeping k symbolic.
  5. Inside the move, choose an expansion row/column that minimises k-handling (often the row containing k), then solve the resulting polynomial — expect linear or quadratic in k for A-level scope.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write down M − λI by subtracting the given λ from each diagonal entry.
  2. Expand det(M − λI) along a convenient row or column, treating k as an algebraic symbol.
  3. Simplify to a polynomial equation in k.
  4. Set the polynomial equal to 0 and solve, listing all admissible values of k.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $M=\begin{pmatrix}k&2\\3&k\end{pmatrix}$ with eigenvalue $\lambda=3$. Then $M-3I=\begin{pmatrix}k-3&2\\3&k-3\end{pmatrix}$, so $\det(M-3I)=(k-3)^2-6=0$. Hence $(k-3)^2=6$, giving $k=3\pm\sqrt{6}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

An eigenvector v is given instead of an eigenvalue
Use Mv = λv as a system of linear equations to solve for the unknown entry (and possibly λ).
det(M) is specified rather than an eigenvalue
Use det(M) = product of eigenvalues, or expand det(M) directly as a polynomial in k.
Trace of M is given alongside one eigenvalue
Use trace(M) = sum of eigenvalues to deduce the remaining eigenvalue, then form det(M − λI) = 0 if k still unknown.
M is 3×3 with parameter k
Same procedure; the determinant expansion is longer and the polynomial in k may be quadratic or cubic.

Common Misreadings

  • Setting det(M) = 0 instead of det(M − λI) = 0, confusing the singularity condition with the eigenvalue condition.
  • Treating the given λ as the unknown and trying to find a characteristic polynomial in λ rather than substituting the numerical λ.
  • Assuming the question wants eigenvectors when it only asks for the parameter value.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to subtract λ from every diagonal entry (especially in 3×3 cases).
  • Sign errors when k sits on the diagonal, mishandling the (k − λ) term during expansion.
  • Discarding a valid root of the resulting polynomial because it ‘looks unusual’ — the question often expects multiple values.
  • Arithmetic slips when expanding a 3×3 determinant; choosing a row with a zero or with k can reduce error.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope: 2×2 and 3×3 real matrices, characteristic equation det(M − λI) = 0, and at most quadratic/cubic algebra in the parameter.
