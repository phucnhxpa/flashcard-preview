Choose vertex form when the turning point is given

Family: Completing The Square Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The problem states a minimum or maximum point of a quadratic curve, e.g. 'C has a minimum at Q(4, -5)' or 'the vertex of the parabola is (h, k)'. Equivalent phrasings include 'turning point', 'stationary point of a quadratic', or 'lowest/highest point on the curve'. Whenever the coordinates of the vertex are explicitly supplied, vertex form is the natural model. This fires before any substitution of additional points on the curve.

Motivation — Why this works

The vertex form y = a(x − h)² + k encodes the turning point (h, k) directly, so giving the vertex collapses two of the three quadratic parameters at once. Only the leading coefficient a remains, determined by a single extra condition (typically another point on the curve). Using the general form y = ax² + bx + c instead would require three independent conditions and a 3×3 simultaneous system. Vertex form is exactly the completed-square form, so the question is effectively handing the student the completed square for free.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the phrase 'minimum point at (4, −5)' — that is a vertex, not just a point on the curve.
  2. Rule out general form y = ax² + bx + c: it would force three equations (two points plus axis of symmetry x = −b/(2a) = 4) for three unknowns.
  3. Commit to vertex form y = a(x − h)² + k because it absorbs the vertex information into the structure, leaving only a unknown.
  4. Bridge: substitute h = 4, k = −5 immediately to write y = a(x − 4)² − 5.
  5. Tactical check: since the vertex is a minimum, expect a > 0 once the remaining condition is applied; if a maximum had been stated, expect a < 0.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the vertex (h, k) from the wording (here (4, −5)).
  2. Write y = a(x − h)² + k, substituting h and k with the correct signs: y = a(x − 4)² − 5.
  3. Note that only a remains unknown, to be found from one further condition (e.g. another point on C).
  4. Record the sign expectation: a > 0 for a minimum, a < 0 for a maximum, as a sanity check.

Worked Micro-Example

C has minimum point Q(4, −5) and passes through P(6, 3). Write y = a(x − 4)² − 5. Substitute P: 3 = a(6 − 4)² − 5, so 3 = 4a − 5, giving a = 2. Hence y = 2(x − 4)² − 5, and a > 0 confirms the minimum.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Maximum point given instead of minimum
Same form y = a(x − h)² + k, but expect a < 0 as the consistency check.
Vertex given as 'axis of symmetry x = h and minimum value k'
Read off h and k from the two statements, then proceed identically with y = a(x − h)² + k.
Three arbitrary points on the curve, no vertex stated
Vertex form is not efficient; use y = ax² + bx + c with three simultaneous equations.
Quadratic given, asked to find vertex
Reverse direction: complete the square on ax² + bx + c to extract (h, k) from a(x − h)² + k.

Common Misreadings

  • Writing y = a(x + 4)² − 5, forgetting the bracket is (x − h) so h = 4 gives (x − 4).
  • Treating the vertex coordinates as a point that the curve merely passes through and using only one substitution into general form.
  • Confusing 'minimum point (4, −5)' with 'minimum value −5 at x = 4 means y-intercept −5' and substituting into y = ax² + bx − 5.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error inside the bracket: with h = 4 the expression must be (x − 4)², not (x + 4)².
  • Forgetting to determine a and leaving the answer as (x − 4)² − 5 (i.e. assuming a = 1).
  • Not checking the sign of a against the min/max condition stated in the question.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1/P2) content: completing the square and the vertex form of a quadratic, with the turning point read off directly. No calculus is required for this move, though dy/dx = 0 at the vertex can serve as an alternative check.
