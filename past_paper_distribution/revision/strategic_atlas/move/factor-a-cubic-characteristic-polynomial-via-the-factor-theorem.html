Matrices

Factor a cubic characteristic polynomial via the factor theorem

Family: Polynomial Root Finding Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when expanding det(A − λI) for a 3×3 matrix produces a cubic in λ that does not factor by inspection. Edexcel FP3 questions are designed so at least one eigenvalue is a small integer (typically a divisor of the constant term). Use this whenever you see a cubic of the form −λ³ + aλ² + bλ + c = 0 (or its tidied form) and need three eigenvalues. Also fires when the question states one eigenvalue and asks for the others.

Motivation — Why this works

The factor theorem guarantees that if p(a) = 0 then (λ − a) divides p(λ) exactly. Cubics in A-level scope are engineered with rational roots, so trialling divisors of the constant term locates one root cheaply. Once one factor is extracted, the cofactor is a quadratic — fully within A-level toolkit (factorise or use the quadratic formula). This avoids the cubic formula, which is out of scope.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the characteristic equation is a cubic in λ that does not obviously factor as a product of linear terms.
  2. I rule out the cubic formula (out of scope) and rule out grouping unless a common (λ − k) is visible across pairs of terms.
  3. I commit to the factor theorem because Edexcel cubics almost always have an integer root; the constant term's divisors give a short candidate list.
  4. First concrete operation: arrange the cubic in standard form, identify the constant term, and list its integer divisors as trial values for λ.
  5. Tactically: test λ = 1, −1, 2, −2, 3, −3 in that order; once p(a) = 0, perform algebraic long division (or compare coefficients) by (λ − a), then solve the resulting quadratic.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the characteristic polynomial in standard form p(λ) = λ³ + Aλ² + Bλ + C (multiply by −1 if needed).
  2. List integer divisors of C and evaluate p(λ) at each until p(a) = 0.
  3. Divide p(λ) by (λ − a) using long division or coefficient comparison to obtain a quadratic q(λ).
  4. Solve q(λ) = 0 by factorisation or the quadratic formula.
  5. Report all three eigenvalues, noting any repeated root.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose the characteristic equation is $\lambda^3 - 6\lambda^2 + 11\lambda - 6 = 0$. Trial $\lambda = 1$: $1 - 6 + 11 - 6 = 0$, so $(\lambda - 1)$ is a factor. Dividing gives $\lambda^3 - 6\lambda^2 + 11\lambda - 6 = (\lambda - 1)(\lambda^2 - 5\lambda + 6)$. Factorising the quadratic: $(\lambda - 1)(\lambda - 2)(\lambda - 3) = 0$, so the eigenvalues are $\lambda = 1, 2, 3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

One eigenvalue is given in the question
Skip the trial step; divide directly by (λ − given) and solve the resulting quadratic.
Repeated eigenvalue
After dividing, the quadratic factor has a double root or shares a root with the first factor; record the eigenvalue's algebraic multiplicity for later diagonalisation work.
Cubic appears with leading coefficient −1
Multiply through by −1 first so trial substitution and division operate on a monic cubic.
Constant term is 0
λ = 0 is an immediate root; factor out λ to leave a quadratic directly.
Symmetric/orthogonal-type matrix where roots are guaranteed real
Same procedure; if the quadratic discriminant looks negative, recheck arithmetic since real roots are expected.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating −λ³ + … = 0 as monic and trialling roots with the wrong sign on p(a).
  • Confusing the eigenvalue equation det(A − λI) = 0 with det(λI − A) = 0 and getting an overall sign flip in the polynomial.
  • Assuming all eigenvalues must be distinct and stopping after two roots.
  • Trying to factorise by grouping when no genuine common factor exists.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when substituting negative trial values, e.g. mishandling (−2)³ = −8.
  • Arithmetic slips in algebraic long division, especially propagating minus signs through the subtraction lines.
  • Forgetting to divide through by −1 first, leading to a quadratic quotient with flipped signs.
  • Missing a repeated root by stopping once two distinct eigenvalues are found.
  • Listing the trial root as a factor (λ + a) instead of (λ − a).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope: the factor theorem and polynomial division are core, and FP3 eigenvalue cubics are designed with at least one rational root. The cubic formula is not required and should not be invoked.
