Algebra & Functions

Decompose a proper rational function via cover-up substitution to split into simple linear pieces

Family: Partial Fractions Setup Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when you face a proper rational expression whose denominator is a product of two (or more) DISTINCT LINEAR factors, e.g. 1/[(1+3x)(1−x)] or 1/[(H−5)(H+3)]. Typical surface cues: the question explicitly says 'express in partial fractions', or the integrand needs to be split before integrating to logs, or a series/binomial expansion is requested on a product-of-linears denominator. Equivalent phrasings: 'write in the form A/(...) + B/(...)', 'hence find ∫...', 'show that f(x) can be written as...'. If the denominator is given expanded, factorising it first reveals the same trigger.

Motivation — Why this works

When the denominator factorises into distinct linear pieces, the rational function is uniquely expressible as a sum of constants over each factor: 1/[(ax+b)(cx+d)] ≡ A/(ax+b) + B/(cx+d). The cover-up (Heaviside) method exploits the fact that substituting the root of one factor annihilates the other term, isolating each unknown in a single equation — far faster and less error-prone than expanding and equating coefficients. This is the standard gateway to integrating rational functions to logarithms, to binomial-expanding 1/(linear) pieces, and to summing telescoping series.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the denominator is already a product of two DISTINCT linear factors — no repeats, no irreducible quadratics — so the decomposition ansatz is just one constant over each bracket.
  2. Consider equating coefficients (expand, match powers of x): valid but slower, and risks arithmetic slips with two simultaneous equations.
  3. Commit to cover-up: each linear factor has exactly one root, and substituting that root into the cleared identity kills the other term, giving the constant in one line.
  4. Bridge to action: clear denominators to get 1 ≡ A·(other factor) + B·(this factor), an identity that must hold for all x, so I am free to pick the most convenient values of x.
  5. Tactical choice: pick x equal to the root of (1−x), i.e. x=1, to isolate B; then pick x equal to the root of (1+3x), i.e. x=−1/3, to isolate A. Track signs carefully when the factor is written as (1−x) (root x=1) rather than (x−1).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Factorise the denominator if not already done; confirm factors are linear and distinct.
  2. Write the ansatz: 1/[(ax+b)(cx+d)] ≡ A/(ax+b) + B/(cx+d).
  3. Multiply through by (ax+b)(cx+d): 1 ≡ A(cx+d) + B(ax+b).
  4. Substitute x = −b/a (root of first factor) to kill the A-term and solve for B.
  5. Substitute x = −d/c (root of second factor) to kill the B-term and solve for A.
  6. Write the final decomposition and (optionally) verify by substituting one extra value of x or by recombining over a common denominator.

Worked Micro-Example

Decompose $\dfrac{1}{(H-5)(H+3)}$. Set $\dfrac{1}{(H-5)(H+3)} = \dfrac{A}{H-5} + \dfrac{B}{H+3}$, so $1 = A(H+3) + B(H-5)$. Put $H=5$: $1 = 8A \Rightarrow A = \tfrac{1}{8}$. Put $H=-3$: $1 = -8B \Rightarrow B = -\tfrac{1}{8}$. Hence $\dfrac{1}{(H-5)(H+3)} = \dfrac{1}{8(H-5)} - \dfrac{1}{8(H+3)}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Three distinct linear factors, e.g. 1/[(x−1)(x−2)(x+3)]
Ansatz becomes A/(x−1) + B/(x−2) + C/(x+3); cover-up still finds all three constants directly by substituting x=1, 2, −3.
Repeated linear factor, e.g. 1/[(1+3x)²(1−x)]
Need A/(1+3x) + B/(1+3x)² + C/(1−x). Cover-up gives B (sub x=−1/3) and C (sub x=1) immediately, but A requires comparing coefficients or substituting a third value.
Irreducible quadratic factor in denominator
The numerator over that factor must be (Px+Q), not a single constant; cover-up alone is insufficient — combine with coefficient comparison.
Non-unit numerator, e.g. (2x+5)/[(x−1)(x+4)]
Same ansatz A/(x−1) + B/(x+4); after clearing, substitute roots — the left side is just (2x+5) evaluated at each root.
Improper rational function (degree of numerator ≥ degree of denominator)
Polynomial-divide first to get quotient + proper remainder, then apply cover-up to the proper part.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating a denominator like (1−x) as having root x=−1 instead of x=1 — the root is whatever makes the bracket zero.
  • Trying cover-up on a repeated factor as if it were distinct, missing the need for an extra term A/(linear)².
  • Applying cover-up directly to an improper fraction without first dividing out the polynomial part.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when substituting negative roots: e.g. with B(H−5) at H=−3, the bracket is −8, not 8.
  • Forgetting to clear the denominators before substituting — students sometimes substitute into the original fractional identity and divide by zero.
  • Mis-stating the ansatz: e.g. writing A/(1+3x) + B/(1−x) but then forgetting that 'A' multiplies the OTHER factor (1−x) after clearing.
  • Not checking the final answer by recombining or testing an extra x-value when time permits.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P3 partial fractions) and used routinely in P3 integration and C4-style binomial expansions. The Edexcel formula booklet does not list a partial-fractions formula — the technique is examinable knowledge. Stay restricted to denominators that are products of linear factors (possibly repeated) and irreducible quadratics; avoid higher-degree generalisations such as residues or complex-root decompositions.
