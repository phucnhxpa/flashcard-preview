Differentiation

Rewrite surd as fractional exponent to enable power-chain differentiation

Family: Index Law Rewrite Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever the expression to be differentiated contains a radical such as √(f(x)), ∛(f(x)), or 1/√(f(x)) where f(x) is a non-trivial inner function (commonly linear like 4x−1, but possibly quadratic). Also fires when the surd appears inside a product, quotient, or composite that will need the product/quotient rule — because each factor must still be differentiated cleanly. Visual cues: a √ symbol, an n-th root symbol, or a reciprocal of a root (1/√(...)) sitting inside a larger differentiation problem. Equivalent phrasings: 'convert root to power', 'switch to index notation before differentiating'.

Motivation — Why this works

The generalised power rule combined with the chain rule, d/dx[(f(x))^n] = n(f(x))^{n−1}·f'(x), only acts cleanly on expressions written in index form. Rewriting √(f(x)) as (f(x))^{1/2} (or ∛(f(x)) as (f(x))^{1/3}, or 1/(f(x))^k as (f(x))^{−k}) places the expression in exactly the form the rule requires, turning differentiation into a mechanical procedure. This avoids needing to memorise separate derivative formulas for surds and reciprocals, and it produces a clean negative fractional exponent that can be tidied or recombined later. The underlying principle is simply the index laws: √a = a^{1/2}, ⁿ√a = a^{1/n}, 1/a^n = a^{−n}.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice a square root (or n-th root, or reciprocal of a root) wrapped around a non-trivial inner function — this is a composite, so the chain rule will be involved.
  2. I rule out trying to recall a special 'derivative of √' formula, because that only handles √x cleanly and gets clumsy when the argument is f(x); I also rule out logarithmic differentiation as overkill for a single root.
  3. I commit to rewriting the surd in index form because the power-chain rule d/dx[(f)^n] = n·f^{n−1}·f' is the standard, mechanical tool and is exactly applicable once the exponent is explicit.
  4. Bridge to action: I write √(f(x)) = (f(x))^{1/2} (or the appropriate fractional/negative exponent) so the outer function is ( · )^{n} and the inner function is f(x).
  5. Tactically, I keep the negative exponent form during differentiation rather than re-converting to surd notation mid-calculation — that delay until the final tidying step prevents sign and exponent slips.
  6. I then apply chain rule: bring down the exponent, reduce by 1, and multiply by f'(x). Only at the end do I (optionally) rewrite back into surd notation if the answer key prefers that form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the surd: e.g. v = √(4x−1).
  2. Rewrite using index laws: v = (4x−1)^{1/2}.
  3. Identify outer function ( · )^{1/2} and inner function w = 4x−1.
  4. Differentiate outer with respect to w: dv/dw = (1/2)w^{−1/2} = (1/2)(4x−1)^{−1/2}.
  5. Differentiate inner: dw/dx = 4.
  6. Apply chain rule: dv/dx = (1/2)(4x−1)^{−1/2} · 4 = 2(4x−1)^{−1/2}.
  7. Optionally rewrite final form as 2/√(4x−1) for presentation.

Worked Micro-Example

Differentiate y = √(4x−1).\nRewrite: y = (4x−1)^{1/2}.\nChain rule: dy/dx = (1/2)(4x−1)^{−1/2} · 4 = 2(4x−1)^{−1/2} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{4x−1}}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cube or n-th root, e.g. ∛(2x+5)
Rewrite as (2x+5)^{1/3}; chain rule gives (1/3)(2x+5)^{−2/3} · 2.
Reciprocal of a power, e.g. 1/(3x−2)^4
Rewrite as (3x−2)^{−4}; chain rule gives −4(3x−2)^{−5} · 3.
Surd in a quotient, e.g. y = u(x)/√(4x−1)
Either apply quotient rule with v = (4x−1)^{1/2}, or rewrite as y = u(x)·(4x−1)^{−1/2} and use the product rule — often cleaner.
Surd of a non-linear inner function, e.g. √(x²+1)
Same rewrite to (x²+1)^{1/2}; chain rule multiplier is now 2x, not a constant — be careful to differentiate the full inner function.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating √(4x−1) as if it differentiates like √x to give 1/(2√(4x−1)) and forgetting the inner-derivative factor.
  • Mistaking 1/√(f(x)) for something requiring the quotient rule rather than rewriting it as (f(x))^{−1/2}.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to multiply by the derivative of the inner function (the chain rule factor) — the single most common error.
  • Sign error on the exponent after differentiation: writing (4x−1)^{+1/2} instead of (4x−1)^{−1/2}.
  • When the inner function is nonlinear, multiplying only by its leading coefficient instead of its full derivative.
  • Arithmetic slip when reducing the exponent: 1/2 − 1 = −1/2, not +1/2 or −3/2.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P2/P3) — uses only the standard chain rule and index laws, both in the formula booklet. Do not extend to fractional exponents of negative bases or to implicit/parametric situations beyond syllabus without checking the unit context.
