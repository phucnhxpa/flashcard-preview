Algebra & Functions

Pick the equation with a variable already isolated for substitution

Family: Strategic Substitution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when solving a small simultaneous system (typically 2×2 or the 2-unknown subsystem from equating two vector/parametric forms) and one equation already presents one unknown explicitly, e.g. y = 2x − 1 or μ = 3 + 2λ. Look for an equation of the form 'variable = expression in the other variable' or one that needs only a single rearrangement step. Phrasings like 'solve simultaneously', 'find the point of intersection', or 'find the values of λ and μ' often accompany this setup. Especially common after equating two vector lines component-by-component, where one component equation is unusually clean.

Motivation — Why this works

Substitution replaces one unknown by an equivalent expression, collapsing a two-variable system into a single-variable equation in one stroke. Choosing the equation in which a variable is already isolated avoids any preparatory rearrangement and minimises sign/coefficient manipulation. This is strictly cheaper than elimination when no scaling of equations is required, and it keeps fractions and minus signs out of intermediate work. The principle is just transitivity of equality: if μ equals an expression in λ, that expression can stand wherever μ stands.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that one equation reads variable = (expression in the other variable), so no rearrangement is needed.
  2. Rules out elimination because it would require multiplying equations through to align coefficients, adding steps and risk of sign errors.
  3. Commits to substitution because the saving is immediate: one line of algebra reduces the system to a single linear equation.
  4. Bridges to action by identifying which other equation to substitute into — never the equation used to isolate, since that would yield 0 = 0.
  5. Plans the order of operations: substitute, expand brackets carefully, collect like terms, solve for the remaining unknown, then back-substitute.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the equation already in the form X = f(Y).
  2. Substitute f(Y) for X in a different equation of the system.
  3. Expand brackets and collect like terms to get a single equation in Y.
  4. Solve for Y.
  5. Back-substitute Y into X = f(Y) to recover X.
  6. (If a third equation is available) verify the solution satisfies it as a consistency check.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $3\lambda - 2\mu = -2$ and $\mu = 3 + 2\lambda$. Substitute the second into the first: $3\lambda - 2(3 + 2\lambda) = -2 \Rightarrow 3\lambda - 6 - 4\lambda = -2 \Rightarrow -\lambda = 4 \Rightarrow \lambda = -4$. Then $\mu = 3 + 2(-4) = -5$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

2×2 linear system in x, y where one equation is y = mx + c (line meets line/curve).
Substitute mx + c for y in the other equation; for a line–curve intersection this yields a quadratic in x.
Equating two parametric vector lines r = a + λd and r = b + μe in 3D.
Pick the component equation in which λ or μ already has coefficient 1 to isolate, then substitute into a second component; use the third for consistency.
Line meets plane in vector form, giving one scalar equation in one parameter.
No selection needed — substitute the parametric expressions directly into the Cartesian plane equation to get a single equation in λ.
Curve–curve intersection where one equation gives y in terms of x (e.g. y = x² + 1).
Substitute into the second equation to obtain a single equation in x, typically polynomial.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating all equations of an over-determined system as needing to be used simultaneously, rather than seeing two are enough to pin down the unknowns and the third is a consistency check.
  • Reading μ = 3 + 2λ as a definition rather than an equation that can be substituted elsewhere.
  • Choosing elimination by reflex even when one variable is already isolated.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when distributing a negative across a substituted bracket, e.g. −2(3 + 2λ).
  • Substituting the isolated expression back into the same equation it came from, producing 0 = 0.
  • Forgetting to back-substitute, leaving only one of the two unknowns found.
  • In 3D vector problems, neglecting to verify the third component equation, which is required for the lines to actually intersect.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure (simultaneous equations, line–curve intersections) and Further Pure vector geometry (intersection of lines, line meets plane); no techniques beyond linear substitution and basic algebraic manipulation are required.
