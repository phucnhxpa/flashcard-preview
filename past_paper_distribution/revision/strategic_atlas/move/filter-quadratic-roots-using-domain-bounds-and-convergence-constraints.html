Sequences & Series

Filter quadratic roots using domain bounds and convergence constraints

Family: Validity Filtering Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a problem reduces to a quadratic in a trig ratio (e.g. cos θ, sin θ) or another bounded quantity, and that quantity also serves as the common ratio of a geometric series. Cues include phrases like 'sum to infinity', 'S∞ exists', or a quadratic whose roots may exceed natural bounds. The move applies whenever two layers of validity must be checked: the algebraic domain of the variable and the convergence condition |r| < 1.

Motivation — Why this works

Quadratics are blind to domain restrictions, so they can produce roots that violate the geometric meaning of the variable (e.g. cos θ = 3 has no real θ). Additionally, when the variable controls the common ratio of a geometric series, |r| < 1 is required for S∞ to exist. Applying both filters in sequence guarantees the surviving root is genuinely admissible, not merely algebraically correct.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the quadratic produced two candidate roots and ask which structural constraints the variable must obey.
  2. Rule out any root outside the natural domain (|cos θ| ≤ 1, |sin θ| ≤ 1, probability ∈ [0,1], etc.).
  3. For surviving roots, commit only after checking the convergence condition |r| < 1 since S∞ was used.
  4. Bridge: substitute the validated value back into the series formula to compute the requested quantity.
  5. Tactically, state both rejections explicitly so the justification is complete and no marks are lost.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Solve the quadratic in the trig ratio (or bounded variable) using factoring or the quadratic formula.
  2. Apply the domain filter: reject any root outside the variable's natural range.
  3. Apply the convergence filter: for each surviving root, check |r| < 1 explicitly.
  4. Discard roots failing either filter; retain the unique valid value.
  5. Substitute the validated root into the original series/expression to answer the question.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $5\cos^2\theta - 16\cos\theta + 3 = 0$ with $r = 2\cos\theta$ and $S_\infty$ requested. Roots: $\cos\theta = 3$ or $\cos\theta = \tfrac{1}{5}$. Reject $\cos\theta = 3$ since $|\cos\theta| \le 1$. For $\cos\theta = \tfrac{1}{5}$: $|r| = |2 \cdot \tfrac{1}{5}| = \tfrac{2}{5} < 1$ ✓. Hence $\cos\theta = \tfrac{1}{5}$ is the unique valid solution.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic in sin θ with a geometric series in sin θ
Reject roots outside [−1,1] then verify |r| < 1 on the survivors.
Quadratic in tan θ with no domain bound
Skip the [−1,1] filter; only the convergence filter |r| < 1 applies.
Quadratic giving a probability p that is also a common ratio
Reject p < 0 or p > 1, then check |p| < 1 (automatic if 0 ≤ p < 1).
Quadratic in r directly from S∞ = a/(1−r) equations
No domain filter beyond reals; apply only |r| < 1 to discard extraneous roots.

Common Misreadings

  • Checking only |cos θ| ≤ 1 and forgetting the convergence requirement |r| < 1.
  • Checking only |r| < 1 and overlooking that cos θ = 3 has no real angle.
  • Assuming both quadratic roots must yield answers and reporting two values.

Pitfalls

  • Accepting cos θ = 3 and attempting to find θ, producing 'no solution' and wasted effort.
  • Failing to state the convergence check explicitly, losing justification marks.
  • Arithmetic slip in the quadratic formula leading to roots that wrongly survive the filter.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 geometric series (S∞ requires |r| < 1) combined with C2/P2 trig identities; no material beyond standard A-level scope.
