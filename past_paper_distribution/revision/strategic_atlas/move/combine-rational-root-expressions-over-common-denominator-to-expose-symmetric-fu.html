Algebra & Functions

Combine rational root expressions over common denominator to expose symmetric functions

Family: Algebraic Recombination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The target expression involves rational combinations of the roots α and β (e.g. α/β + β/α, 1/α + 1/β, α/β² + β/α²) rather than direct polynomial combinations. Such expressions look unfamiliar relative to Vieta's σ₁ = α+β and σ₂ = αβ, but they are visibly symmetric under swapping α ↔ β. The cue to fire this move is: 'symmetric in α, β, but written as a sum of fractions.' Combining over a common denominator almost always produces a numerator and denominator that ARE elementary symmetric expressions.

Motivation — Why this works

Any symmetric rational function of α and β can be written in terms of σ₁ and σ₂ (the fundamental theorem of symmetric polynomials, in its A-level disguise). Putting fractions over the common denominator αβ — or some power of it — separates the expression into a 'numerator polynomial in α, β' over a 'product polynomial', both of which reduce via Vieta's to numbers already computed in earlier parts. This avoids ever needing α or β individually, which would require surds and risk arithmetic errors. It is the standard bridge from 'new roots' to 'new quadratic.'

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the expression is symmetric in α and β but written as a sum of fractions, so it isn't directly σ₁ or σ₂.
  2. Rule out solving for α and β explicitly via the quadratic formula — symbolic, surd-heavy, and unnecessary.
  3. Commit to recombination: any symmetric rational expression must reduce to a function of σ₁, σ₂.
  4. Bridge: add the fractions over the common denominator αβ (or (αβ)ᵏ) so the numerator becomes a polynomial symmetric expression like α² + β².
  5. Tactically, recall α² + β² = σ₁² − 2σ₂, and similarly for higher powers, so every piece is computable from Vieta's.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify σ₁ = α + β and σ₂ = αβ from the original quadratic via Vieta's.
  2. Write the new sum S' as a single fraction over a common denominator (typically αβ or a power thereof).
  3. Rewrite the numerator as a polynomial symmetric expression and convert via identities (e.g. α² + β² = σ₁² − 2σ₂).
  4. Write the new product P' similarly; cancellations often collapse it to a simple value.
  5. Form the new quadratic x² − S'x + P' = 0, clearing fractions if a tidy integer-coefficient form is required.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $2x^2 - 3x + 4 = 0$ with roots $\alpha, \beta$: $\sigma_1 = 3/2$, $\sigma_2 = 2$. For new roots $\alpha/\beta, \beta/\alpha$: $S' = \frac{\alpha^2 + \beta^2}{\alpha\beta} = \frac{\sigma_1^2 - 2\sigma_2}{\sigma_2} = \frac{9/4 - 4}{2} = -\tfrac{7}{8}$, and $P' = 1$. New quadratic: $x^2 + \tfrac{7}{8}x + 1 = 0$, i.e. $8x^2 + 7x + 8 = 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

New roots 1/α and 1/β
S' = (α+β)/(αβ) = σ₁/σ₂, P' = 1/σ₂; combining over αβ is immediate.
New roots α² and β²
No fractions needed; use σ₁² − 2σ₂ for the sum and σ₂² for the product directly.
New roots α + 1/β and β + 1/α
Expand each: sum = σ₁ + σ₁/σ₂ = σ₁(1 + 1/σ₂); product expands to σ₂ + 2 + 1/σ₂.
New roots α/β² and β/α²
Common denominator is (αβ)², numerator becomes α³ + β³ = σ₁³ − 3σ₁σ₂.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to find α and β individually via the quadratic formula, then computing α/β + β/α numerically with surds.
  • Treating α/β + β/α as if it were a new elementary symmetric polynomial requiring a separate identity, rather than a recombination of known ones.
  • Mistaking the expression for non-symmetric and abandoning Vieta's approach.

Pitfalls

  • Dividing by a fraction incorrectly: e.g. 13 ÷ (3/2) written as 13 × 3/2 instead of 13 × 2/3.
  • Forgetting the −2σ₂ term in α² + β² = σ₁² − 2σ₂.
  • Sign error when σ₁ = −b/a is negative; bracket carefully when squaring.
  • Failing to clear fractions at the end when the question asks for integer coefficients in the new quadratic.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure 'roots of polynomials' technique: forming a new quadratic whose roots are functions of α, β using Vieta's σ₁, σ₂ and the identity α² + β² = σ₁² − 2σ₂.
