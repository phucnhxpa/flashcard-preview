Integration

Link arc length or surface area integrand to a previously derived reduction formula

Family: Pattern Matching Linkage Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in a multi-part Further Pure question where parts (a)–(b) establish a reduction formula I_n = ∫ x^n f(x) dx (typically with sin x, cos x, e^x, or ln x) and a later part asks for an arc length L = ∫√(1+(dy/dx)²) dx or surface area S = 2π ∫ y √(1+(dy/dx)²) dx on a specified curve. After differentiating y and simplifying 1+(dy/dx)², the radicand collapses to a perfect square so the geometric integrand reduces to x^n times the same trig/exponential factor. Recognise the match by comparing powers of x and the accompanying function to the I_n family.

Motivation — Why this works

Examiners scaffold these questions so the curve y=f(x) is engineered to make 1+(dy/dx)² a perfect square, leaving an integrand of exactly the form I_n. Re-evaluating from scratch wastes time and risks IBP errors, whereas quoting the value from part (b) gives an exact answer immediately. The underlying principle is that the question's structure is the hint: a reduction formula in one part predicts the shape of the integral in the next.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question has just had me prove a reduction formula I_n and now asks for an arc length or surface area on a specific curve — the parts are almost certainly linked.
  2. I rule out attacking the geometric integral with fresh IBP; the scaffolding signals that simplification will produce an I_n instance.
  3. I commit to differentiating, forming 1+(dy/dx)², and looking for a perfect-square collapse under the root.
  4. First concrete operation: compute dy/dx and (dy/dx)², then add 1 and try to write the result as (something)².
  5. Tactically, I match the resulting x^k · (trig or exp) integrand to the precise n in I_n, then substitute the numerical value from the previous part.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Differentiate the given curve y=f(x) to obtain dy/dx.
  2. Compute (dy/dx)² and form 1+(dy/dx)²; factor or complete a square so the radicand becomes a perfect square.
  3. Take the square root cleanly (mind sign on the given interval) to get √(1+(dy/dx)²) as a simple expression.
  4. Substitute into L = ∫_a^b √(1+(dy/dx)²) dx or S = 2π ∫_a^b y √(1+(dy/dx)²) dx and simplify.
  5. Identify the resulting integral as I_n for the specific n; quote the value computed in the earlier part.
  6. Multiply by any outstanding constants (e.g. 2π for surface area) and state the exact answer.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose part (b) gave I_n = ∫_0^{π/2} x^n sin x dx with I_2 = π − 2. In part (c), for the curve where simplification yields √(1+(dy/dx)²) = x²/√(…) and the arc length integrand reduces to x² sin x on [0, π/2], we recognise L = I_2 = π − 2 directly, avoiding any further IBP.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Surface area of revolution about the x-axis
Use S = 2π ∫ y √(1+(dy/dx)²) dx; the extra factor y typically raises the power of x by one, so the integrand matches I_{n+1} rather than I_n.
Parametric curve arc length
Use L = ∫ √((dx/dt)² + (dy/dt)²) dt; the same perfect-square collapse occurs in t, and the integrand matches an I_n in t.
Reduction formula involving e^x or ln x
Same recognition logic — match x^n e^{ax} or x^n (ln x)^k against the derived I_n; the curve is engineered so the radicand simplifies to that exact form.
Volume of revolution V = π ∫ y² dx
No square-root simplification needed; expand y² and match the polynomial-times-function integrand directly to I_n.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating part (c) as independent and re-deriving the integral by IBP, ignoring the I_n scaffolding.
  • Misidentifying n — e.g. quoting I_4 when the integrand is x² · sin x corresponding to I_2.
  • Assuming √(1+(dy/dx)²) must be left under the root, missing the engineered perfect-square simplification.

Pitfalls

  • Algebraic slips when simplifying 1+(dy/dx)²; if it doesn't reduce to a perfect square, the link to I_n is hidden.
  • Dropping the 2π in surface area or mishandling the y factor when forming y·√(1+(dy/dx)²).
  • Sign errors when taking the square root over an interval where the inside expression could be negative.
  • Forgetting to apply the limits stated in part (c), which may differ from those used to define I_n.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure topic: reduction formulae combined with arc length / surface area of revolution / volumes of revolution. All curves and limits are engineered to keep simplification within A-level algebra.
