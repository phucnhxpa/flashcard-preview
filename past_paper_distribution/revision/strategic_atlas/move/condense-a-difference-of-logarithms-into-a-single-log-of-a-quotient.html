Condense a difference of logarithms into a single log of a quotient

Family: Log Law Simplification Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever you see two logarithm terms (same base) joined by subtraction — typically ln A − ln B or k ln A − k ln B — and you need to either solve for a variable trapped inside both logs, or evaluate the expression numerically/exactly. Common surface cues: an equation of the form ln(...) − ln(...) = c that you must solve for x; the result of evaluating an antiderivative at two limits where a ln term appears, e.g. [k ln x] from a to b giving k ln b − k ln a; or simplification of a logarithmic expression before differentiation. If the variable appears inside two separate logs, or if you are about to plug two awkward decimals into ln separately, that is the cue.

Motivation — Why this works

The quotient law ln A − ln B = ln(A/B) is the inverse of the product rule for exponents and reduces two transcendental terms to one. Mathematically, this is essential for solving equations: only when the log is condensed to ln(expression) = constant can you exponentiate cleanly to recover a polynomial/rational equation in x. Numerically, it halves the rounding error and often produces a tidier ratio (e.g. 4/2.5 = 8/5) suitable for an exact-form answer. The same identity supports leaving answers in the exact form an Edexcel mark scheme typically rewards.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structural pattern: two log terms with the same base (almost always ln in IAL) connected by a minus sign, with no other log terms blocking the merge.
  2. Rule out exponentiating term-by-term — that fails because e^(a−b) ≠ e^a − e^b, and tempting shortcuts like ln A − ln B = ln(A − B) are simply wrong.
  3. Rule out evaluating each ln separately as a decimal — this compounds rounding error and obscures any clean exact form.
  4. Commit to the quotient law because it produces the canonical form ln(expression) = constant (solvable by exponentiation) or a single ln of a tidy fraction (cleaner numerics).
  5. Tactical decision: if a coefficient k multiplies both logs, factor it out first as k(ln A − ln B) = k ln(A/B); if the coefficient is inside (e.g. 2 ln x), first rewrite as ln(x²) before combining.
  6. After condensing, simplify the ratio A/B to lowest terms (e.g. 4/2.5 = 8/5) and decide whether to leave exact or approximate based on the question.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two arguments A and B in the pattern (k)(ln A) − (k)(ln B).
  2. Factor out any common coefficient: k ln A − k ln B = k(ln A − ln B).
  3. Apply the quotient law: ln A − ln B = ln(A/B).
  4. Simplify the resulting fraction A/B to a clean form (lowest-terms fraction).
  5. If solving an equation ln(A/B) = c, exponentiate both sides: A/B = e^c, then solve for x.
  6. If evaluating numerically, compute ln(A/B) once and multiply by k.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\ln(3x+1) - \ln(x-1) = 2$. Condense: $\ln\!\left(\dfrac{3x+1}{x-1}\right) = 2$. Exponentiate: $\dfrac{3x+1}{x-1} = e^{2}$. Then $3x+1 = e^{2}(x-1)$, giving $x = \dfrac{e^{2}+1}{e^{2}-3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Sum of logs instead of difference (ln A + ln B)
Use the product rule ln A + ln B = ln(AB) — same condensation principle, multiplication instead of division.
Coefficient sitting in front of a log (k ln A)
First rewrite as ln(A^k) using the power rule, then combine with other log terms via product/quotient rule.
Evaluating a definite integral whose antiderivative contains k ln x
After substituting limits, combine k ln b − k ln a = k ln(b/a) before computing — keeps the answer in exact form and reduces rounding.
Simplifying before differentiation, e.g. y = ln(x²(x+1))
Expand using log laws to ln(x²) + ln(x+1) = 2 ln x + ln(x+1) so each term differentiates trivially.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating ln A − ln B as ln(A − B) — subtracting the arguments instead of dividing them.
  • Exponentiating term-by-term: writing e^(ln A) − e^(ln B) = e^c, which uses the false identity e^(a−b) = e^a − e^b.
  • Trying to combine logs of different bases (e.g. ln and log₁₀) without first converting — the quotient law requires matching bases.

Pitfalls

  • Reversing the quotient: writing ln(B/A) instead of ln(A/B). The first argument (the one being subtracted from) goes on top.
  • Sign errors when the log term has a negative coefficient and is evaluated between limits — double negatives like −9 ln 4 − (−9 ln 2.5) need careful tracking before factoring.
  • Forgetting to check the domain: A/B > 0 is required, and any solution must satisfy A > 0 and B > 0 in the original equation, not just A/B > 0.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 scope; the laws of logarithms (product, quotient, power) are in the formula booklet under 'Logarithms and exponentials'. Avoid extending to complex logarithms or change-of-base manipulations beyond the standard ln/log₁₀ pair, which are out of scope.
