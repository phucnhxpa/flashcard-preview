Integration

Split polynomial-over-monomial fraction into separate power terms

Family: Term By Term Division Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the integrand is a polynomial divided by a single power of one variable, e.g. (u²−2u+1)/u³, (x³+x)/x², or (3t²−5)/t⁴. The denominator is a monomial (one factor raised to a power), not a product of distinct linear/quadratic factors. Often appears immediately after a substitution that collapses the denominator to a single u^k, or after expanding a binomial in the numerator.

Motivation — Why this works

Division distributes over addition: (A+B+C)/D = A/D + B/D + C/D. When D is a monomial u^k, each piece simplifies to u^(power) using the index law u^a/u^k = u^(a−k). Every resulting term is then a standard power that integrates by ∫u^n du = u^(n+1)/(n+1) (or ln|u| when n=−1). This bypasses partial fractions entirely, since a repeated monomial denominator gives no new information beyond what term-splitting already provides.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the denominator is a single power u^k (monomial), not a product of distinct factors.
  2. Rules out partial fractions: decomposing into A/u + B/u² + C/u³ would just recover the term-by-term split after extra algebra.
  3. Commits to splitting because the numerator is already a sum, so dividing each term gives clean u^n forms.
  4. Bridges to the concrete operation: write the fraction as a sum of three simpler fractions, then apply u^a/u^k = u^(a−k) to each.
  5. Tracks signs and coefficients carefully, watching especially for the n=−1 case which integrates to ln|u| rather than the power rule.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the integrand as a sum: (u²−2u+1)/u³ = u²/u³ − 2u/u³ + 1/u³.
  2. Apply the index law to each term: u⁻¹ − 2u⁻² + u⁻³.
  3. Integrate term-by-term using ∫u⁻¹ du = ln|u| and ∫u^n du = u^(n+1)/(n+1) for n≠−1.
  4. Combine: ln|u| + 2u⁻¹ − ½u⁻² (+C).
  5. If definite, evaluate at the (possibly substituted) limits.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_2^4 \dfrac{u^2-2u+1}{u^3}\,du$.\\ Split: $\int_2^4 \left(u^{-1} - 2u^{-2} + u^{-3}\right)du$.\\ Integrate: $\left[\ln|u| + \dfrac{2}{u} - \dfrac{1}{2u^2}\right]_2^4$.\\ Evaluate: $\left(\ln 4 + \tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{1}{32}\right) - \left(\ln 2 + 1 - \tfrac{1}{8}\right) = \ln 2 - \tfrac{13}{32}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Numerator degree ≥ denominator degree, e.g. (x³+x)/x²
Splitting still works directly: x³/x² + x/x² = x + x⁻¹, integrating to ½x² + ln|x|.
Single negative power with binomial expansion needed, e.g. (1+x)²/x³
First expand the numerator to 1 + 2x + x², then split each term over x³.
Trig fraction like (sin²x + cos x)/sin³x
Same split principle gives csc x + cot x · csc²x style terms, but this exits the standard A-level scope unless trig identities reduce it first.
Post-substitution monomial denominator from a √-substitution
After u = √(...) or similar, the integrand often collapses to polynomial/u^k form; apply the same split.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the monomial denominator as needing partial fractions: writing A/u + B/u² + C/u³ unnecessarily.
  • Trying u-substitution again on an already-simplified u^n integrand instead of just applying the power rule.
  • Confusing this with the case of distinct linear factors (e.g. 1/[(x−1)(x−2)]) where partial fractions IS required.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting that ∫u⁻¹ du = ln|u|, not u⁰/0.
  • Sign errors on negative powers: ∫u⁻² du = −u⁻¹, so −2·∫u⁻² du = +2u⁻¹.
  • Dropping the coefficient when simplifying, e.g. writing −u⁻² instead of −2u⁻².
  • Forgetting absolute value bars in ln|u| when u may be negative on the interval.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Maths (Pure 2/3/4) integration techniques: index laws, the power rule, and ∫1/x dx = ln|x|. Often appears as the simplification step inside a substitution problem, before partial fractions are introduced.
