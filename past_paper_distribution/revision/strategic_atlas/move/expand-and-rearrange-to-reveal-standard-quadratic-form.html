Algebra & Functions

Expand and rearrange to reveal standard quadratic form

Family: Standard Form Reduction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation contains a product like $x(2x+3)$, $(x+a)(x+b)$, or $x^2$ buried inside brackets, with terms scattered on both sides of the equals sign. Typical cues: 'solve $x(2x+3)=15+x$', 'find the values of $x$ such that $(x-1)(x+4)=2x+5$', or any equation where expanding a bracket would generate an $x^2$ term. Also fires when a rational equation has just been cleared of denominators and the result still has terms on both sides.

Motivation — Why this works

Every A-level quadratic technique — factorisation, the quadratic formula, completing the square, discriminant analysis — requires the equation in the canonical form $ax^2+bx+c=0$. Expanding brackets exposes the highest power, and moving every term to one side ensures the right-hand side is zero, which is what makes the zero-product principle and the formula valid. Without this reduction, no systematic solution method applies, and ad-hoc manipulations (like dividing by $x$) tend to lose roots.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that one side contains a product of $x$ with a linear bracket, so expansion will produce an $x^2$ term — the equation is quadratic, not linear.
  2. Rule out shortcuts like dividing both sides by $x$, since that would discard the possible root $x=0$ and collapse the equation to a linear one.
  3. Commit to the canonical reduction: expand all brackets, then move every term to the left so the right-hand side is zero.
  4. Bridge to first operation: distribute $x$ across $(2x+3)$ to get $2x^2+3x$, giving a clean polynomial on the left.
  5. Tactical choices inside the move: track signs carefully when subtracting the RHS, then combine like terms and, if helpful, divide through by a common factor before solving.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Expand all brackets: $x(2x+3) = 2x^2 + 3x$.
  2. Subtract every RHS term from both sides: $2x^2 + 3x - 15 - x = 0$.
  3. Collect like terms: $2x^2 + 2x - 15 = 0$.
  4. Identify $a=2,\ b=2,\ c=-15$ and choose factorisation or the quadratic formula.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $x(2x+3)=15+x$.\\ Expand: $2x^2+3x = 15+x$.\\ Move all terms left: $2x^2+3x-x-15=0 \Rightarrow 2x^2+2x-15=0$.\\ Apply the quadratic formula: $x=\dfrac{-2\pm\sqrt{4+120}}{4}=\dfrac{-2\pm\sqrt{124}}{4}=\dfrac{-1\pm\sqrt{31}}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two brackets multiplied, e.g. $(x+1)(x-2)=3x$
Expand the product to $x^2-x-2$, then subtract $3x$ to get $x^2-4x-2=0$.
Rational equation reducing to a quadratic, e.g. $\dfrac{x+2}{x-1}=x$
Multiply through by $(x-1)$ first, then expand and rearrange to $x^2-2x-2=0$; note the restriction $x\ne 1$.
Surd or index equation, e.g. $\sqrt{x+5}=x-1$
Square both sides, then expand and rearrange to $x^2-3x-4=0$; check for extraneous roots introduced by squaring.
Disguised quadratic in $u=x^n$, e.g. $x^4-5x^2+4=0$
Substitute $u=x^2$ to obtain the standard form $u^2-5u+4=0$, solve, then back-substitute.
Simultaneous linear-quadratic system
Substitute the linear equation into the other, then expand and rearrange to a single quadratic in one variable.

Common Misreadings

  • Dividing both sides by $x$ to 'simplify' $x(2x+3)=15+x$, which loses the potential root $x=0$ and destroys the quadratic structure.
  • Treating the equation as already linear because only one $x^2$-producing term is visible, and forgetting to expand the bracket.
  • Stopping after expansion without moving all terms to one side, then trying to factorise an expression that still equals a non-zero RHS.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when transferring terms across the equals sign, especially turning $+x$ on the RHS into $+x$ (rather than $-x$) on the LHS.
  • Failing to combine like terms after rearranging, leaving an unsimplified form such as $2x^2+3x-x-15=0$.
  • Forgetting to divide through by a common factor (when one exists) before applying the quadratic formula, leading to messier arithmetic.
  • After squaring or clearing denominators, neglecting to check solutions against the original equation for extraneous roots.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics P1 (quadratic equations and their solution by factorisation, formula, and completing the square); the same reduction underpins later work on simultaneous equations and disguised quadratics in P2/P3.
