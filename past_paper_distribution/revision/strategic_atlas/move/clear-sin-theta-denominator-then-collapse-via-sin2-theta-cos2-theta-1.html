Trigger — When to use

Fires when deriving the tangent (or normal) to a curve at a parametric point and the gradient expression carries a trig term in the denominator, e.g. dy/dx = −(b cos θ)/(a sin θ) for an ellipse. The cue is a point-gradient equation with a single trig denominator and a parametric point whose coordinates already contain matching sin θ and cos θ factors. Alternative phrasing: 'multiply out to remove the fraction, then the squares of sine and cosine will pair up.' Whenever expanding produces both sin²θ and cos²θ with equal coefficients, the Pythagorean identity is the next step.

Motivation — Why this works

Clearing the denominator by multiplying through by a sin θ replaces a fractional gradient with a linear combination of sin θ and cos θ on each side. The parametric point's structure forces ab sin²θ and ab cos²θ to appear with the same coefficient, so collecting them produces ab(sin²θ + cos²θ) = ab. This is precisely why the standard chord/tangent forms for conics are clean: the identity is built into the parametrisation, guaranteeing constants on the RHS rather than θ-dependent debris.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the gradient has a sin θ in the denominator and the point has a sin θ in its y-coordinate — multiplying by a sin θ will tidy both at once.
  2. Rule out expanding first or substituting numerical θ — these would obscure the identity that is about to collapse the expression.
  3. Commit to the multiply-through move because the structure ab sin²θ versus ab cos²θ is visible after one line of algebra.
  4. Bridge: distribute a sin θ across every term of y − b sin θ = m(x − a cos θ), being careful with the lone b sin θ.
  5. Tactically, group the sin²θ and cos²θ terms on one side before applying the identity, then divide by ab to land on the memorised form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Start from y − b sin θ = −(b cos θ)/(a sin θ) · (x − a cos θ).
  2. Multiply every term by a sin θ to obtain ay sin θ − ab sin²θ = −bx cos θ + ab cos²θ.
  3. Rearrange so the linear terms in x, y are together: bx cos θ + ay sin θ = ab cos²θ + ab sin²θ.
  4. Factor ab and apply sin²θ + cos²θ = 1: bx cos θ + ay sin θ = ab.
  5. Divide through by ab to reach (x cos θ)/a + (y sin θ)/b = 1.

Worked Micro-Example

Tangent to $\tfrac{x^2}{9}+\tfrac{y^2}{4}=1$ at $(3\cos\theta,2\sin\theta)$: gradient $-\tfrac{2\cos\theta}{3\sin\theta}$, so $y-2\sin\theta=-\tfrac{2\cos\theta}{3\sin\theta}(x-3\cos\theta)$. Multiply by $3\sin\theta$: $3y\sin\theta-6\sin^2\theta=-2x\cos\theta+6\cos^2\theta$. Rearranging gives $2x\cos\theta+3y\sin\theta=6(\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta)=6$, i.e. $\tfrac{x\cos\theta}{3}+\tfrac{y\sin\theta}{2}=1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Hyperbola tangent at (a sec t, b tan t)
Multiply through by a tan t (or use the identity sec²t − tan²t = 1) to obtain (x sec t)/a − (y tan t)/b = 1.
Rectangular hyperbola xy = c² at (ct, c/t)
No Pythagorean identity needed; clearing the t-denominator collapses via algebra to x + t²y = 2ct.
Normal to the ellipse at (a cos θ, b sin θ)
After clearing fractions, the cross-terms produce (a²−b²)sin θ cos θ rather than collapsing — identity does not fire, so leave in form ax sec θ − by csc θ = a² − b².
Circle x² + y² = r² at (r cos θ, r sin θ)
Same clearing step gives x cos θ + y sin θ = r(cos²θ + sin²θ) = r directly.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the fraction as something to differentiate further rather than to clear.
  • Believing the identity must be applied before multiplying out, leading to algebraic dead-ends.
  • Confusing this with the normal-line derivation, where the identity does not collapse the RHS to a constant.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to multiply the standalone b sin θ term on the LHS by a sin θ, producing an unbalanced equation.
  • Sign error when distributing the negative gradient across (x − a cos θ).
  • Leaving the answer as bx cos θ + ay sin θ = ab(sin²θ + cos²θ) without invoking the identity.
  • Dividing by ab but forgetting to split the division across both terms on the LHS.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (conic sections, parametric tangents/normals); uses only the Pythagorean identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 from IAL Pure trigonometry.
