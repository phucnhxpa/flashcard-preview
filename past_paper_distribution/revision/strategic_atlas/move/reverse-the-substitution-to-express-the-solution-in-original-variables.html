Strategic Atlas / Differential Equations / Substitution Reversal
Differential Equations

Reverse the substitution to express the solution in original variables

Family: Substitution Reversal Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires at the very end of any ODE solved via a substitution (u = ..., v = ..., t = ...): you have a general solution in the auxiliary variable but the question asks for y in terms of x. Visual cues: the working has produced 'v = f(x) + C', 'u(t) = ...', or 't = √x'-style relations earlier; the question stem says 'find y' or 'show that y² = ...' or 'find the general solution of equation (I)'. Equivalent phrasings: 'undo the substitution', 'return to original variables', 'back-substitute'. Whenever the auxiliary variable still appears in your final line, this move must fire.

Motivation — Why this works

A substitution is only a temporary algebraic bridge — it linearises a Bernoulli equation, reduces an Euler equation to constant-coefficient, or homogenises a non-homogeneous ODE. Once the transformed ODE is solved, the answer must be re-expressed in the original variable to actually answer the question posed. The mechanism is pure inversion of the defining relation of the substitution (e.g. v = y^{-2} ⇒ y² = 1/v; t = √x ⇒ replace t by √x; v = y − 2x ⇒ y = v + 2x). No new calculus is needed; this is an algebraic step that closes the loop.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice my 'general solution' still contains the auxiliary variable (v, u, or t), but the question is phrased in x and y — so I'm not finished.
  2. I rule out further integration or applying boundary conditions yet: the ODE is already solved in the new variable, and applying boundary conditions is usually cleaner either before back-substitution (in v's equation) or after, but consistently — not midway.
  3. I commit to back-substitution because the substitution definition (e.g. v = y^{-2}, t = √x, v = y − 2x) is a simple algebraic relation that can be inverted directly.
  4. I write down the defining relation of the substitution and solve it for the original variable: t = √x, y² = 1/v, or y = v + 2x.
  5. I substitute this relation into EVERY occurrence of the auxiliary variable in the general solution — including inside exponentials, polynomial terms, and arbitrary constants' coefficients.
  6. I check the final form: it should contain only x, y, and arbitrary constants; the number of arbitrary constants must match the order of the original ODE; and I note any domain restriction inherited from the substitution (e.g. x > 0 if t = √x).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the substitution relation used earlier (e.g. v = y^{-2}, v = y − 2x, t = √x, v = y/x).
  2. Invert it to express the original variable in terms of the auxiliary one: y² = 1/v, y = v + 2x, t = √x, y = vx.
  3. Replace EVERY occurrence of the auxiliary variable in the general solution — paying attention to exponentials e^{kt} → e^{k√x}, powers t² → x, and linear terms t → √x.
  4. Rearrange algebraically into the form requested by the question (e.g. y = ..., y² = ..., or an implicit relation).
  5. State any domain restriction implied by the substitution (e.g. x > 0, y ≠ 0).

Worked Micro-Example

Bernoulli ODE solved in v gives $v = Ax^{-12} - 2e^x x^{-12}$ where $v = y^{-2}$. Inverting: $y^2 = 1/v = \dfrac{x^{12}}{A - 2e^x}$. Done — the answer is now in original variables with one arbitrary constant, as expected for a first-order ODE.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Bernoulli with v = y^{1−n}, e.g. v = y^{-2}
Invert by y² = 1/v (or y = 1/v if n = 2); the question often asks for y² to avoid ± ambiguity.
Euler equation with x = e^t (so t = ln x)
Replace t by ln x; exponentials e^{kt} become x^k, polynomial t^n becomes (ln x)^n.
Reduction by t = √x (so x = t²)
Replace every t by √x: e^{3t} → e^{3√x}, t² → x, t → √x. Solution valid for x > 0.
Linear shift v = y − f(x), e.g. v = y − 2x
Add f(x) back: y = v + f(x), so y = (solution in x) + 2x.
Homogeneous ODE with v = y/x
Multiply through by x: y = vx, where v is now the explicit/implicit solution in x.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the t-solution as the final answer because 'I've solved the ODE' — missing that the ODE was in x and y, not in t.
  • Misreading t = √x as t² = x and then writing e^{3t} → e^{3x} (squaring inside the exponential by mistake).

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to invert when the substitution was a reciprocal: writing y² = v instead of y² = 1/v for v = y^{-2}.
  • Replacing t in polynomial terms but forgetting to replace it inside exponentials (e^{3t} must become e^{3√x}, not e^{3x}).
  • Ignoring domain restrictions inherited from the substitution (x > 0 for t = √x; y ≠ 0 for v = y^{-2}).
  • Applying boundary conditions inconsistently — best practice is to apply them in the auxiliary variable's equation OR fully in y, not half-way through the back-substitution.

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope: substitutions appear in FP2/FP3 for Bernoulli equations, Euler equations (x = e^t), and reduction substitutions stated explicitly in the question. The reversal step is always required for full marks. No formula booklet entry is invoked — this is purely algebraic inversion. Avoid generalising to substitutions not stated in the question; IAL questions always provide the substitution.
