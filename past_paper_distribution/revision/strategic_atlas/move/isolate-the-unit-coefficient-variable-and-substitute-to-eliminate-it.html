Algebra & Functions

Isolate the unit-coefficient variable and substitute to eliminate it

Family: Directed Elimination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires on a 2×2 linear system where (i) one equation has a variable with coefficient 1 (or −1), and (ii) the question targets the *other* variable — typically a 'show that' or 'find' instruction naming a specific unknown. Common phrasings: 'show that d = …', 'hence find n', 'given a + 3d = 18 and 2a + 7d = 38.5, show that d = 2.5'. Also fires when one equation is already given in the form 'x = …' or is trivially rearrangeable without introducing fractions.

Motivation — Why this works

A unit coefficient lets you rearrange one equation into 'a = (expression in d)' with no division, so substitution introduces no fractions and preserves arithmetic cleanliness. Substituting then collapses the system to a single linear equation in the target variable. Because the goal variable is fixed by the question, directed elimination — choosing which variable to remove based on the goal, not symmetry — is the efficient route. This is preferable to coefficient-matching elimination when one variable is already 'free' to isolate.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question fixes the target: 'show that d = 2.5' means the final line must be an equation in d alone, so a must be eliminated.
  2. Rule out eliminating d (wrong direction) and rule out coefficient-matching elimination as unnecessarily heavier when Equation 1 already has a unit coefficient on a.
  3. Commit to substitution because rearranging a + 3d = 18 to a = 18 − 3d is instantaneous and fraction-free.
  4. Bridge: write a = 18 − 3d, then plan to insert this into the second equation 2a + 7d = 38.5.
  5. Tactically, expand the bracket carefully (sign on −3d when multiplied by 2), combine like terms in d, and isolate d in one final step.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the equation with a unit (or −1) coefficient on the variable to be eliminated.
  2. Rearrange that equation to express the eliminated variable explicitly: a = 18 − 3d.
  3. Substitute the expression into the *other* equation: 2(18 − 3d) + 7d = 38.5.
  4. Expand and combine like terms: 36 − 6d + 7d = 38.5 ⇒ 36 + d = 38.5.
  5. Solve the resulting single-variable equation: d = 2.5.
  6. (If required) back-substitute d into a = 18 − 3d to recover a.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $a + 3d = 18$ and $2a + 7d = 38.5$, show that $d = 2.5$.\\ From (1): $a = 18 - 3d$.\\ Sub into (2): $2(18 - 3d) + 7d = 38.5 \Rightarrow 36 - 6d + 7d = 38.5$.\\ So $36 + d = 38.5 \Rightarrow d = 2.5$. $\blacksquare$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Arithmetic sequence problem giving two term equations, e.g. $u_5 = 18$, $u_8 = 25.5$ rewritten as $a + 4d = 18$, $a + 7d = 25.5$
Both equations have unit coefficient on a; subtract directly (elimination) rather than substitute, since 3d = 7.5 falls out instantly.
Simultaneous equations from a curve/line intersection where one equation is already $y = mx + c$
Substitute the explicit y straight into the other equation; no rearrangement step needed.
Geometric sequence yielding $ar = p$ and $ar^3 = q$ (non-linear)
Use division to eliminate a (giving $r^2 = q/p$) instead of substitution; substitution still works but division is cleaner.
System with a negative unit coefficient, e.g. $-x + 2y = 5$
Rearrange to $x = 2y - 5$ (flip sign), then substitute as usual; watch the sign on the isolated term.
'Show that' question where decimals are awkward
Multiply the decimal equation through by a power of 10 first (e.g. $2a + 7d = 38.5 \Rightarrow 4a + 14d = 77$) to keep all arithmetic in integers.

Common Misreadings

  • Eliminating the target variable instead of the other one, ending with an equation in the wrong unknown.
  • Treating 'show that d = 2.5' as permission to verify by substitution rather than to derive d from scratch.
  • Choosing coefficient-matching elimination by reflex even when one equation has a unit coefficient ready for substitution.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when expanding $2(18 - 3d)$: writing $+6d$ instead of $-6d$.
  • Combining $-6d + 7d$ as $-d$ or $13d$ rather than $+d$.
  • Arithmetic slip handling the decimal $38.5$; clearing decimals first by scaling can prevent this.
  • Forgetting to back-substitute when the question also asks for the second variable.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1 simultaneous equations; recurring in arithmetic/geometric sequence problems in P1/P2). No techniques beyond linear rearrangement and substitution are required.
