Sequences & Series

Multiply truncated standard series term-by-term to expand a product

Family: Series Multiplication Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

The function to be expanded as a Maclaurin (or Taylor) series is a PRODUCT of two (occasionally three) functions whose individual series are either standard formula-booklet results or already derived earlier in the question. Typical surfaces include $e^x \sin x$, $x^2 \cos x$, $(1+x)\ln(1+x)$, $e^x \ln(1+x)$, or any 'expand $f(x)g(x)$ in ascending powers of $x$ up to the term in $x^n$'. The cue is the explicit product structure together with a stated truncation order. Quotients $f(x)/g(x)$ are a closely related variant where $1/g(x)$ is first expanded as a series.

Motivation — Why this works

The Maclaurin series of a product $f(x)g(x)$ is the Cauchy product of the individual series, valid on the intersection of their radii of convergence. Repeated differentiation of a product via the generalised Leibniz/product rule cascades into 2, 3, 4, 5, ... terms at successive orders and is highly error-prone. Multiplying two truncated polynomials and discarding terms above the required degree converts a calculus problem into a bounded, mechanical algebra problem. The booklet series ARE the pre-computed derivatives, so this method loses nothing and saves enormous effort.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the target $f(x)$ factorises as a product (or quotient) of two functions whose Maclaurin series I either know by heart or can read off the formula booklet.
  2. Consider differentiating directly: applying the product rule four times to get up to $x^4$ would generate Leibniz-style binomial sums — slow, messy, and a single sign slip kills the answer.
  3. Rule out direct differentiation; commit to multiplying truncated series because the operation is purely algebraic and easy to check.
  4. Decide the truncation order: if the question wants terms up to $x^n$, write BOTH factor-series up to $x^n$ (not lower in either factor — a common trap) and immediately discard any cross-product whose total degree exceeds $n$.
  5. Organise the multiplication by collecting contributions to each power $x^k$ via $c_k = \sum_{j=0}^{k} a_j b_{k-j}$, working from $k=0$ upwards.
  6. Sanity-check by evaluating an obvious feature: e.g. constant term should be $f(0)g(0)$; if either factor vanishes at $0$ the product should too.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write Series A truncated to order $n$: e.g. $e^x \approx 1 + x + \tfrac{x^2}{2} + \tfrac{x^3}{6}$.
  2. Write Series B truncated to order $n$: e.g. $\sin x \approx x - \tfrac{x^3}{6}$.
  3. For each target power $k = 0, 1, \ldots, n$, compute $c_k = \sum_{j=0}^{k} a_j b_{k-j}$, summing every pair whose exponents add to $k$.
  4. Discard immediately any product term of degree $> n$ to keep the work manageable.
  5. Combine fractions carefully (common denominators) and write the final series $f(x)g(x) \approx c_0 + c_1 x + c_2 x^2 + \cdots + c_n x^n$.

Worked Micro-Example

Expand $e^x \sin x$ up to $x^3$. Take $e^x \approx 1 + x + \tfrac{x^2}{2} + \tfrac{x^3}{6}$ and $\sin x \approx x - \tfrac{x^3}{6}$. Multiplying and keeping degree $\le 3$: constant $0$; $x$-term $1\cdot x = x$; $x^2$-term $x\cdot x = x^2$; $x^3$-term $\tfrac{x^2}{2}\cdot x + 1\cdot(-\tfrac{x^3}{6}) = \tfrac{x^3}{2} - \tfrac{x^3}{6} = \tfrac{x^3}{3}$. Hence $e^x \sin x \approx x + x^2 + \tfrac{x^3}{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quotient $f(x)/g(x)$ with $g(0) \ne 0$, e.g. $\sin x/(1+x)$
First expand $1/g(x)$ as a series (geometric series for $1/(1+x)$, or long division of series), then multiply by the series for $f(x)$ using the same Cauchy-product procedure.
Composition such as $e^{\sin x}$
Substitute the inner series into the outer series $e^u = 1 + u + u^2/2 + \cdots$ where $u = \sin x$, expand each $u^k$ as a polynomial in $x$, and truncate at the required order — same discard-high-powers principle.
Product of three functions, e.g. $x e^x \cos x$
Multiply two factors first (truncating to required order), then multiply the result by the third factor, again discarding excess powers at each stage.
Quotient like $\tan x = \sin x / \cos x$
Use polynomial long division of the truncated series for $\sin x$ by that of $\cos x$, equivalent to multiplying $\sin x$ by the series for $\sec x$.

Common Misreadings

  • Believing one MUST differentiate to obtain a Maclaurin series — the booklet series are the pre-computed derivative results and may be quoted directly.
  • Forgetting that $\sin x$ has no $x^2$ term (and $\cos x$ no $x$ term), then mistakenly inserting a zero-coefficient term as if it contributed.
  • Treating the question as if only one factor needs expanding and leaving the other in closed form.

Pitfalls

  • Truncating one factor too early: if the answer needs terms up to $x^n$, both factors must be carried to $x^n$ — a cross-term $a_n \cdot b_0$ or $a_0 \cdot b_n$ may be needed.
  • Accidentally retaining a cross-term whose total degree exceeds $n$, which then contaminates the collected coefficients.
  • Sign errors with alternating-sign series ($\sin x$, $\cos x$, $\ln(1+x)$).
  • Dropping factorial denominators when copying standard series from memory.
  • Arithmetic slips in combining fractional coefficients, e.g. $\tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{1}{6} = \tfrac{1}{3}$ (correct) vs the common error $\tfrac{1}{4}$.
  • On Edexcel mark schemes the final coefficients are typically 'correct answer only' A-marks — a single arithmetic slip loses the mark with no method credit.

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) Maclaurin & Taylor series. The standard series for $e^x$, $\sin x$, $\cos x$, $\ln(1+x)$, $(1+x)^n$ are all in the IAL formula booklet and may be quoted without proof. Convergence is assumed within the stated radii; questions do not normally require formal justification of the Cauchy product, so avoid invoking it by name in solutions.
