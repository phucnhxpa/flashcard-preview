Complete the square on a monic interior quadratic

Family: Completing The Square Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a monic quadratic of the form x² + bx (with or without a constant tail) appears and the target form is a(x + p)² + q, i.e. vertex/turning-point form. Typical cues include the question stem 'express in the form a(x + p)² + q', 'find the minimum value of', 'find the coordinates of the turning point', or 'sketch showing the vertex'. It also fires as a sub-step after factoring out a leading coefficient from a non-monic quadratic, leaving a monic interior to be completed.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity x² + bx ≡ (x + b/2)² − (b/2)² rewrites a linear-plus-quadratic into a perfect square minus a compensating constant. This works because (x + b/2)² expands to x² + bx + (b/2)², so subtracting (b/2)² exactly cancels the surplus constant introduced by squaring. Once in vertex form, the minimum (or maximum) and the line of symmetry can be read off by inspection, which is why this is the standard A-level mechanism for turning points without calculus.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the bracket contains x² + bx, a monic quadratic with a linear term — the canonical completing-the-square shape.
  2. Rule out the quadratic formula (gives roots, not vertex form) and differentiation (overkill, and the question demands an algebraic form).
  3. Commit to completing the square because the target form a(x + p)² + q is explicitly requested, or because the turning point is the goal.
  4. Bridge: compute b/2, then (b/2)², ready to add-and-subtract inside the bracket.
  5. Tactically, keep the outer factor outside the bracket while completing, then distribute it across both the squared term and the compensating constant at the very end.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the linear coefficient b inside the monic bracket x² + bx (+ c).
  2. Halve it: compute b/2.
  3. Square the half: (b/2)².
  4. Rewrite x² + bx as (x + b/2)² − (b/2)², leaving any existing constant c untouched.
  5. Combine the new constant: −(b/2)² + c.
  6. If a leading factor a was pulled out earlier, multiply both the squared term and the inner constant by a when un-bracketing.
  7. State the result in the form a(x + p)² + q and identify p = b/2, q = a(c − (b/2)²).

Worked Micro-Example

Express $f(x) = 2x^2 + 8x - 1$ in the form $a(x+p)^2 + q$.\ Factor the leading 2 from the quadratic terms: $f(x) = 2(x^2 + 4x) - 1$.\ Half of 4 is 2, and $2^2 = 4$, so $x^2 + 4x = (x+2)^2 - 4$.\ Substitute: $f(x) = 2[(x+2)^2 - 4] - 1 = 2(x+2)^2 - 8 - 1 = 2(x+2)^2 - 9$.\ Hence the minimum is $-9$ at $x = -2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Odd linear coefficient, e.g. x² + 5x
Use fractions: (x + 5/2)² − 25/4. The recipe is identical; expect rational constants in the final answer.
Negative linear coefficient, e.g. x² − 6x + 2
Half of −6 is −3, so write (x − 3)² − 9 + 2 = (x − 3)² − 7. The sign inside the bracket follows b/2.
Non-monic quadratic, e.g. 3x² − 12x + 5
Factor 3 out of the quadratic terms first: 3(x² − 4x) + 5, complete on the monic interior, then redistribute the 3.
Quadratic in a different variable, e.g. t² + 6t − 1
Same recipe with t in place of x: (t + 3)² − 10.
Quadratic in a substitution variable, e.g. completing on u = x² for a quartic disguised as a quadratic
Apply the move to u² + bu, then back-substitute u = x² at the end.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the move as 'just halve b and put it in a bracket' without the −(b/2)² compensation, producing an expression that no longer equals the original.
  • Mistaking a non-monic quadratic for a monic one and completing without first factoring out the leading coefficient.
  • Reading the sign of p backwards: writing (x − b/2)² when b is positive, or vice versa.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to multiply the compensating constant by the factored-out leading coefficient, e.g. writing 2(x+2)² − 4 − 1 instead of 2(x+2)² − 8 − 1.
  • Sign slip when b is negative: writing (x + 3)² for x² − 6x instead of (x − 3)².
  • Dropping the existing constant tail c when rewriting, or absorbing it incorrectly into (b/2)².
  • Leaving the answer as (x + p)² + q when the question specifies the form a(x + p)² + q with a explicit (a marks-losing presentation issue).

A-level Scope Notes

Core technique in Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics P1 (quadratics, turning points) and reused in P2/P3 for sketching, range-finding, and integration via standard forms. Stays fully within A-level scope; no calculus required.
