Set up partial fraction template with repeated linear factor terms

Family: Partial Fraction Template Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The denominator factorises into linear factors where at least one factor is repeated, e.g. (x−a)² or (x−a)³, possibly alongside other distinct linear factors. Common surface cues: a denominator written as (linear)(linear)² or a hint that 'expressing in partial fractions' is required before integration, binomial expansion, or summation. The proper degree condition holds (numerator degree < denominator degree); otherwise polynomial division is needed first.

Motivation — Why this works

For a repeated linear factor (x−a)^n, the space of proper rational functions with that denominator has dimension n, requiring n separate terms A₁/(x−a) + A₂/(x−a)² + … + Aₙ/(x−a)ⁿ. Omitting any intermediate power loses a degree of freedom, so no constants can satisfy the resulting identity for all x. The total number of unknown constants must equal the degree of the denominator, matching the number of independent coefficients available in the numerator after clearing fractions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the denominator contains a squared linear factor (x−1)² alongside a distinct linear factor (2x+1) — this is the signature of a repeated-linear partial fraction problem.
  2. Rule out the irreducible-quadratic template (Bx+C)/(x−1)²: that numerator form is reserved for genuinely irreducible quadratic denominators, whereas (x−1)² factors over the reals.
  3. Commit to the repeated-factor template: each power of (x−1) from 1 up to 2 must appear as its own term with its own constant numerator.
  4. Bridge to operation: write the identity with constants A, B, C and prepare to clear denominators by multiplying through by (2x+1)(x−1)².
  5. Tactically, count unknowns (3) against the degree of the denominator (3) to confirm the template is complete before substituting strategic x-values or comparing coefficients.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Factor the denominator fully and identify each repeated linear factor with its multiplicity.
  2. For a non-repeated linear factor (px+q), write one term A/(px+q) with a constant numerator.
  3. For a repeated linear factor (x−a)^n, write n terms B₁/(x−a) + B₂/(x−a)² + … + Bₙ/(x−a)ⁿ, all with constant numerators.
  4. Confirm: total number of unknown constants = degree of denominator.
  5. Multiply both sides by the full denominator to convert the rational identity into a polynomial identity ready for substitution or coefficient comparison.

Worked Micro-Example

Express $\dfrac{10x^2+2x-2}{(2x+1)(x-1)^2}$ in partial fractions. Template: $\dfrac{10x^2+2x-2}{(2x+1)(x-1)^2} \equiv \dfrac{A}{2x+1} + \dfrac{B}{x-1} + \dfrac{C}{(x-1)^2}$. Multiplying through: $10x^2+2x-2 \equiv A(x-1)^2 + B(2x+1)(x-1) + C(2x+1)$. Three unknowns A, B, C match the degree-3 denominator.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Triple-repeated linear factor (x−a)³ in the denominator
Include three terms: B/(x−a) + C/(x−a)² + D/(x−a)³, each with a constant numerator.
Repeated factor combined with an irreducible quadratic, e.g. (x−1)²(x²+1)
Use B/(x−1) + C/(x−1)² for the repeated linear part and (Px+Q)/(x²+1) for the irreducible quadratic.
Improper rational function (numerator degree ≥ denominator degree)
First perform polynomial division to obtain quotient + proper remainder, then apply the repeated-factor template to the remainder.
Repeated factor of the form (ax+b)², e.g. (2x+1)²
Template becomes B/(2x+1) + C/(2x+1)²; the coefficient inside the bracket is preserved, not normalised.

Common Misreadings

  • Writing only A/(2x+1) + B/(x−1)² and omitting the B/(x−1) term, leaving too few unknowns to match the numerator.
  • Putting a linear numerator (Bx+C) over (x−1)², treating the repeated linear factor as if it were an irreducible quadratic.
  • Using A/(2x+1) + B/(x−1) + C/(x−1) (two copies of the same term) instead of escalating the power in the second term.

Pitfalls

  • Confusing repeated linear factors with irreducible quadratics — (x−1)² is reducible and demands constant numerators in each term.
  • Forgetting to check that the fraction is proper before applying the template; an improper fraction needs division first.
  • Miscounting unknowns: the total constants must equal the degree of the denominator after full factorisation.

A-level Scope Notes

Partial fractions with repeated linear factors are explicitly in the Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics syllabus (P3/P4), used for integration, binomial series expansion, and summation problems.
