Trigger — When to use

The integral I_n is parameterised by an integer n appearing as a power on a polynomial-like factor (typically x^n, but also (ln x)^n) multiplied by a 'nice' function that integrates cleanly (e^{kx}, sin x, cos x). The question explicitly asks for a recurrence — phrases like 'show that I_n = ... I_{n-1}', 'establish a reduction formula', or 'express I_n in terms of I_{n-2}'. The presence of definite limits (often 0 to 1, or 0 to π/2) reinforces that boundary terms will simplify cleanly.

Motivation — Why this works

Differentiating x^n produces nx^{n-1}, which lowers the power index by one — this is the only way to forge a structural link between I_n and I_{n-1} (or, after two IBPs against a cycling trig function, I_{n-2}). The complementary factor (e^{-x}, sin x, cos x) integrates without raising any power, so the 'v du' integral inherits exactly the shape of I_{n-1} or I_{n-2}. This is integration by parts used as a 'power-lowering pump': the product rule, run backwards, transfers one differentiation from the trig/exponential onto the polynomial.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the integrand is a product: a polynomial-type factor x^n carrying the parameter n, multiplied by something that integrates cleanly (e^{-x}, sin x, cos x).
  2. I rule out substitution (e.g. t = x^n or t = x^2) — substitutions don't generate recurrences between I_n and I_{n-1}, they just rewrite the integral.
  3. I rule out the reversed IBP assignment u = e^{-x}, dv = x^n dx, because integrating x^n raises the power to x^{n+1}/(n+1), pushing me away from the target I_{n-1}.
  4. I commit to u = x^n, dv = (the other factor) dx. Differentiating x^n gives nx^{n-1} — the index drops by exactly one, which is what the recurrence needs.
  5. Tactical decision: if the partner is e^{-x} or e^x, one IBP suffices and yields I_n in terms of I_{n-1}. If the partner is sin x or cos x, I expect to apply IBP twice so the trig factor cycles back, yielding I_n in terms of I_{n-2}.
  6. I anticipate the boundary term [x^n · v]_a^b: at x = 0 it usually vanishes (since 0^n = 0 for n ≥ 1), and at the upper limit it gives a clean closed-form constant.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the polynomial factor x^n and set u = x^n, so du = n x^{n-1} dx.
  2. Set dv equal to the remaining factor times dx, and integrate to find v (e.g. dv = e^{-x}dx ⇒ v = −e^{-x}; dv = sin x dx ⇒ v = −cos x; dv = cos x dx ⇒ v = sin x).
  3. Apply the IBP formula: I_n = [u v]_a^b − ∫_a^b v du = [x^n v]_a^b − n ∫_a^b x^{n-1} v dx.
  4. Evaluate the boundary term [x^n v]_a^b carefully, watching signs.
  5. If the partner is exponential, the remaining integral is already a multiple of I_{n-1} — done. If the partner is trigonometric, apply IBP a second time with u = x^{n-1} and dv = (cycled trig) dx; after this second pass the integral becomes a multiple of I_{n-2}.
  6. Collect all boundary terms and write the recurrence in the form I_n = (boundary expression) ± n I_{n-1}, or I_n = (boundary expression) − n(n−1) I_{n-2}.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $I_n = \int_0^1 x^n e^{-x}\,dx$. Take $u = x^n$, $dv = e^{-x}dx$, so $du = nx^{n-1}dx$, $v = -e^{-x}$. Then $I_n = \bigl[-x^n e^{-x}\bigr]_0^1 + n\int_0^1 x^{n-1}e^{-x}dx = -e^{-1} + n I_{n-1}$, giving the reduction formula $I_n = nI_{n-1} - e^{-1}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Integrand is x^n e^{kx} (exponential partner, no cycling)
One IBP suffices; recurrence links I_n directly to I_{n-1}.
Integrand is x^n sin x or x^n cos x (cycling trig partner)
Apply IBP twice with u always set to the polynomial factor; the trig cycles back and the recurrence links I_n to I_{n-2} with a coefficient of −n(n−1).
Integrand is (ln x)^n (no obvious polynomial factor)
Set u = (ln x)^n, dv = dx, so du = n(ln x)^{n-1}/x dx, v = x — same logic of differentiating the power-bearing factor to drop the index.
Integrand is sin^n x or cos^n x (pure trig power, no polynomial)
Split off one factor: write sin^n x = sin^{n-1}x · sin x, take u = sin^{n-1}x, dv = sin x dx, then use sin^2 = 1 − cos^2 to recover I_{n-2}.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the parameter n as a fixed number and trying a one-off antiderivative instead of recognising the recurrence structure.
  • Attempting a substitution like t = x^2 or t = e^{-x}, which rewrites but does not reduce the index.
  • Choosing u and dv the wrong way round (u = e^{-x} or u = sin x), which raises the polynomial power and produces a harder integral.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the factor n that comes from du = n x^{n-1}dx — this corrupts the entire recurrence.
  • Sign errors from v = −e^{-x} or v = −cos x, especially when compounded across two rounds of IBP.
  • Mishandling the boundary term at x = 0 when n = 0: since 0^0 = 1, the base case I_0 must be evaluated separately, not from the recurrence.
  • Forgetting to carry both factors n and (n−1) through a double IBP for trig partners — the final coefficient should be −n(n−1).

A-level Scope Notes
