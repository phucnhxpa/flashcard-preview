The integral I_n is parameterised by an integer n appearing as a power on a polynomial-like factor (typically x^n, but also (ln x)^n) multiplied by a 'nice' function that integrates cleanly (e^{kx}, sin x, cos x). Motivation â€” Why this works

Differentiating x^n produces nx^{n-1}, which lowers the power index by one â€” this is the only way to forge a structural link between I_n and I_{n-1} (or, after two IBPs against a cycling trig function, I_{n-2}). The complementary factor (e^{-x}, sin x, cos x) integrates without raising any power, so the 'v du' integral inherits exactly the shape of I_{n-1} or I_{n-2}. This is integration by parts used as a 'power-lowering pump': the product rule, run backwards, transfers one differentiation from the trig/exponential onto the polynomial.

Thought Process â€” The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the integrand is a product: a polynomial-type factor x^n carrying the parameter n, multiplied by something that integrates cleanly (e^{-x}, sin x, cos x).
  2. I rule out substitution (e.g. t = x^n or t = x^2) â€” substitutions don't generate recurrences between I_n and I_{n-1}, they just rewrite the integral.
  3. I rule out the reversed IBP assignment u = e^{-x}, dv = x^n dx, because integrating x^n raises the power to x^{n+1}/(n+1), pushing me away from the target I_{n-1}.
  4. I commit to u = x^n, dv = (the other factor) dx. Differentiating x^n gives nx^{n-1} â€” the index drops by exactly one, which is what the recurrence needs.
  5. Tactical decision: if the partner is e^{-x} or e^x, one IBP suffices and yields I_n in terms of I_{n-1}. If the partner is sin x or cos x, I expect to apply IBP twice so the trig factor cycles back, yielding I_n in terms of I_{n-2}.

Worked Micro-Example

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the parameter n as a fixed number and trying a one-off antiderivative instead of recognising the recurrence structure.
  • Attempting a substitution like t = x^2 or t = e^{-x}, which rewrites but does not reduce the index.
  • Choosing u and dv the wrong way round (u = e^{-x} or u = sin x), which raises the polynomial power and produces a harder integral.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the factor n that comes from du = n x^{n-1}dx â€” this corrupts the entire recurrence.
  • Sign errors from v = âˆ’e^{-x} or v = âˆ’cos x, especially when compounded across two rounds of IBP.
  • Mishandling the boundary term at x = 0 when n = 0: since 0^0 = 1, the base case I_0 must be evaluated separately, not from the recurrence.
  • Forgetting to carry both factors n and (nâˆ’1) through a double IBP for trig partners â€” the final coefficient should be âˆ’n(nâˆ’1).
