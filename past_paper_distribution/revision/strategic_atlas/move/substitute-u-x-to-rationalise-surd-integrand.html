Integration

Substitute u = √x to rationalise surd integrand

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the integrand contains √x mixed with integer powers of x (e.g. x√x, x + √x, 1/(x√x + 2x)) so no standard rule applies directly. Also cued when a question explicitly suggests u = √x. The presence of fractional exponents 1/2, 3/2 alongside whole exponents is the giveaway. Definite integrals with positive limits make this especially clean.

Motivation — Why this works

Setting u = √x makes x = u² and √x = u, so every fractional power becomes a polynomial in u. The differential dx = 2u du contributes an extra factor of u that typically cancels into the denominator, leaving a rational function of u that is integrable by partial fractions, polynomial division, or standard logs. This converts a non-standard surd integral into recognisable A-level material.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice mixed fractional and integer powers of x — direct integration rules don't apply.
  2. Rule out u = denominator or u = numerator, since their derivatives don't match the rest of the integrand.
  3. Commit to u = √x because it simultaneously kills every surd and turns x into u².
  4. Compute x = u², dx = 2u du, and translate the x-limits into u-limits straight away to avoid back-substitution.
  5. Substitute and simplify carefully — expect the 2u from dx to cancel with a u in the denominator, giving a clean rational integrand.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Let u = √x, so x = u² and √x = u.
  2. Differentiate: dx/du = 2u, hence dx = 2u du.
  3. Convert limits: x = a → u = √a (e.g. x = 1 → u = 1, x = 4 → u = 2).
  4. Replace every x by u², every √x by u, and dx by 2u du in the integrand.
  5. Simplify the resulting rational function of u (cancel common u factors) and integrate using standard techniques.
  6. Evaluate using the new u-limits — no need to revert to x.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_1^4 \frac{1}{x\sqrt{x}+2x}\,dx$. Let $u=\sqrt{x}$, so $x=u^2$, $dx=2u\,du$, limits $1\to 2$. Integrand becomes $\frac{2u}{u^3+2u^2}\,du = \frac{2}{u(u+2)}\,du$. Partial fractions: $\frac{1}{u}-\frac{1}{u+2}$, giving $[\ln u - \ln(u+2)]_1^2 = \ln 2 - \ln 4 + \ln 3 = \ln(3/2)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Integrand contains ∛x with powers of x
Use u = x^{1/3}, so x = u³ and dx = 3u² du; same rationalising logic.
Integrand contains √(ax+b)
Use u = √(ax+b), so x = (u²−b)/a and dx = (2u/a) du, eliminating the surd.
Integrand contains both √x and x in an exponential, e.g. e^{√x}
Use u = √x; the 2u du from dx pairs with the √x to leave ∫2u·e^u du, integrable by parts.
Integrand has √x in numerator only, e.g. ∫√x/(1+x) dx
u = √x converts to ∫2u²/(1+u²) du, which simplifies via polynomial division.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to substitute u = entire denominator (e.g. u = x√x + 2x) — its derivative does not match the numerator.
  • Treating √x as if it could be integrated directly when combined with other powers of x without rationalising first.
  • Choosing u = x rather than u = √x, which leaves the surd untouched.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to convert the x-limits to u-limits, then evaluating in the wrong variable.
  • Forgetting to replace dx with 2u du, leaving a stray dx in a u-integral.
  • Algebra slip when simplifying x√x = u³ or x = u² inside the new integrand.
  • Failing to cancel the u from 2u du with a u in the denominator, leaving an unnecessarily complicated rational function.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure technique (C34/P3): substitution in definite integrals, including suggested substitutions involving √x. The follow-up rational integration uses partial fractions and ln, both within syllabus.
