Sequences & Series

Assemble Taylor/Maclaurin coefficients from derivative values

Family: Coefficient Assembly Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires once all required derivative values $f^{(k)}(a)$ have been computed and the question asks for the Taylor series about $x=a$ (or Maclaurin series when $a=0$) up to a stated order. Cues include phrases like 'find the Taylor series up to and including the term in $(x-a)^n$', 'expand $f(x)$ in ascending powers of $x$', or 'show that $f(x) \approx \ldots$'. The remaining task is purely organisational: pair each derivative with the matching factorial and present the polynomial.

Motivation — Why this works

The Taylor formula $f(x)=\sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k$ encodes that the $k$-th derivative of the polynomial at $a$ must reproduce $f^{(k)}(a)$, which forces the $k!$ denominator. Since A-marks in IAL are awarded only for the fully simplified final form, every coefficient must be reduced to lowest terms or integers. Correctly pairing derivatives with factorials is the bridge between the calculus work and the examinable polynomial.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice I now hold a complete column of values $f(a), f'(a), f''(a), \ldots, f^{(n)}(a)$ — the calculus is done.
  2. I rule out re-deriving or using shortcut series (e.g. known $\ln(1+x)$ expansion) because the question explicitly required the Taylor formula route.
  3. I commit to the template $\sum \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k$ and decide to write a small table: $k$, $f^{(k)}(a)$, $k!$, coefficient.
  4. First concrete operation: divide each derivative value by the matching factorial, simplifying as I go.
  5. Tactically, I keep signs explicit, check $0!=1!=1$, $2!=2$, $3!=6$, $4!=24$, and only collapse to a single polynomial line at the end.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. List $f^{(k)}(a)$ for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, n$ in a column.
  2. Write $k!$ next to each: $1, 1, 2, 6, 24, 120, \ldots$.
  3. Form the coefficient $c_k = f^{(k)}(a)/k!$ and reduce to lowest terms.
  4. Multiply each $c_k$ by $(x-a)^k$ (or $x^k$ if $a=0$).
  5. Sum the terms in ascending powers and present as a single polynomial.
  6. Sanity-check signs and that no factorial has been omitted.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose $f(0)=1,\ f'(0)=-2,\ f''(0)=6,\ f'''(0)=-12$. Then the Maclaurin series up to $x^3$ is $f(x) \approx 1 + \frac{-2}{1!}x + \frac{6}{2!}x^2 + \frac{-12}{3!}x^3 = 1 - 2x + 3x^2 - 2x^3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Maclaurin series ($a=0$)
Replace $(x-a)^k$ by $x^k$; otherwise identical assembly.
Taylor expansion about $a \neq 0$ (e.g. about $x = \pi/4$)
Keep $(x-a)^k$ explicit; do not expand the brackets unless the question asks for ascending powers of $x$.
Series for $\mathrm{e}^{g(x)}$, $\ln(1+g(x))$, $\sin x$, $\cos x$ derived via Taylor formula
Same assembly; coefficients often collapse to clean rationals like $1/2,\ 1/6,\ 1/24$.
Answer requested in factorial form
Leave $\frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}$ unsimplified, especially when coefficients are unwieldy.
Approximation question (estimate $f(x_0)$)
After assembly, substitute $x_0$ into the polynomial.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the column of derivative values as the coefficients themselves and forgetting the $k!$ denominators.
  • Confusing Taylor (general $a$) with Maclaurin ($a=0$) and writing $x^k$ when $(x-a)^k$ was required.
  • Assuming the series is finite/exact rather than an approximation truncated at the stated order.

Pitfalls

  • Dividing by the wrong factorial (e.g. using $3!=6$ for the $x^2$ term instead of $2!=2$).
  • Dropping a negative sign when simplifying $f^{(k)}(a)/k!$.
  • Forgetting $0!=1$ and omitting the constant term $f(a)$.
  • Leaving unsimplified fractions such as $\tfrac{6}{2}$ instead of $3$, costing the A-mark.
  • Mixing $(x-a)^k$ and $x^k$ within the same expansion.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (Taylor and Maclaurin series up to a finite specified order); no convergence analysis or remainder term is required.
