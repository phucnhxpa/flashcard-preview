Algebra & Functions

Factor out the common variable before splitting into cases

Family: Factor And Split Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

An equation set to zero (often a derivative dy/dx = 0 or a stationary-point condition) has every term sharing a common factor of x (or some expression in x). Typical surfaces: $\frac{6x}{x^2-5} - 8x = 0$, $xe^x - 3x = 0$, or $2x\cos x - x = 0$. The cue is 'every term carries an x' — including any rational term whose numerator is a multiple of x. The presence of a fraction does not block factoring; the x can be pulled outside the bracket while the fraction stays intact.

Motivation — Why this works

If $A(x) + B(x) = 0$ and both $A$ and $B$ share a factor $x$, then $x\,[A(x)/x + B(x)/x] = 0$, which by the zero-product principle splits into $x=0$ or the bracket $=0$. This preserves all roots, whereas dividing by $x$ silently destroys the root $x=0$. Factoring first is also strictly cheaper than clearing denominators: clearing would produce a higher-degree polynomial that still needs $x$ factored out at the end, so we save an expansion step.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice every term in the equation carries a factor of $x$ — including the rational term, whose numerator is $6x$.
  2. I rule out dividing both sides by $x$ because that would lose the solution $x=0$; I also rule out clearing the denominator first because that just inflates the algebra before I'd factor $x$ anyway.
  3. I commit to factoring $x$ out immediately, leaving the rational expression untouched inside the bracket.
  4. This rewrites the equation as $x\cdot[\text{bracket}] = 0$, which by the zero-product property splits cleanly into two independent sub-problems.
  5. Inside Case 2, I now have a simpler equation (often quadratic in $x^2$ or a single rational equation), which I solve on its own and then combine roots with $x=0$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Check that every term on the LHS shares a factor of $x$ (rational terms count if their numerator is a multiple of $x$).
  2. Write the equation as $x\cdot[\text{remaining bracket}] = 0$, leaving any fraction intact inside the bracket.
  3. Apply the zero-product principle: Case 1 is $x = 0$.
  4. Case 2: set the bracket equal to zero and solve the resulting (simpler) equation.
  5. Collect all roots from both cases and check they lie in the domain (e.g. $x^2 - 5 \neq 0$).

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\dfrac{6x}{x^2-5} - 8x = 0$. Factor: $x\left(\dfrac{6}{x^2-5} - 8\right) = 0$. Case 1: $x = 0$. Case 2: $\dfrac{6}{x^2-5} = 8 \Rightarrow 6 = 8(x^2-5) \Rightarrow x^2 = \tfrac{46}{8} = \tfrac{23}{4}$, so $x = \pm\tfrac{\sqrt{23}}{2}$. All three roots are valid since $x^2 \neq 5$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Stationary points of $y = x e^x - 3x \cdot \tfrac{x}{2}$ type expressions where $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = x(e^x - 3) = 0$
Same split: $x=0$ or $e^x = 3$, giving $x = \ln 3$.
Trigonometric stationary equation $2x\cos x - x = 0$
Factor to $x(2\cos x - 1) = 0$; solve $x=0$ or $\cos x = \tfrac{1}{2}$ over the given interval.
Polynomial $4x^3 - 12x = 0$
Factor $4x(x^2-3)=0$, giving $x=0$ or $x = \pm\sqrt{3}$.
Mixed rational/polynomial $\dfrac{2x}{x+1} - 5x^2 = 0$
Factor $x\left(\dfrac{2}{x+1} - 5x\right) = 0$; the bracket then clears to a quadratic in $x$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the rational term as 'not factorable' because of the denominator, when only the numerator's $x$ matters.
  • Believing factoring out $x$ requires first clearing all fractions.
  • Reading $x=0$ as a 'trivial' root that can be ignored, when the question asks for all stationary points.

Pitfalls

  • Dividing both sides by $x$ instead of factoring — silently discards the root $x=0$.
  • Forgetting to check the domain restriction from the original denominator (e.g. excluding $x^2 = 5$).
  • Expanding the bracket prematurely and re-introducing the fraction, undoing the simplification.
  • Reporting only Case 2 solutions and omitting $x=0$ in the final answer.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard technique for solving $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=0$ in IAL P2/P3/P4 calculus and for polynomial/rational equations in Pure. Uses only the zero-product principle and basic factoring — no tools beyond the syllabus.
