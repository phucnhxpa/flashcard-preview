Factor out the lowest common power to leave a simple linear residual

Family: Strategic Factorisation Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an expression is a sum/difference of two terms that share a non-trivial common factor — typically two powers of the same bracket (e.g. $(2x+1)^2$ and $(2x+1)^3$, or $u^{3/2}$ and $u^{5/2}$) together with a shared exponential, surd, or numerical factor. Visual cues: derivative output from a product/chain rule that contains repeated brackets to different powers; an integrated expression in $u$ before back-substitution; the question explicitly asks for a 'fully factorised' answer or for solving $f'(x)=0$. Equivalent phrasings: 'extract the highest common factor', 'pull out the lowest power', 'collect a common bracket'.

Motivation — Why this works

Factoring out the LOWEST common power (not the highest) of every shared object reduces the remaining bracket to something linear (or at worst very simple), because the difference in exponents is then 1. This exploits the identity $a^{m} \pm a^{n} = a^{\min(m,n)}(a^{|m-n|} \pm 1\text{-type residual})$. The payoff is twofold: (i) solving 'expression = 0' becomes trivial since each factor gives a root directly, and (ii) the answer is delivered in the simplest factored form that mark schemes reward. Taking out too little leaves a quadratic; taking out too much introduces fractions or negative powers.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that both terms share structural objects — for instance, both contain a power of $(2x+1)$ and an exponential $e^{-4x}$, or both contain a power of $u$.
  2. Consider the alternative of expanding everything: this would multiply out brackets to high powers, collect like terms, then attempt to refactor — error-prone and obscures structure. Rule it out.
  3. Identify the LOWEST power of each shared object: if powers are 2 and 3, take out power 2; if powers are $3/2$ and $5/2$, take out $3/2$. Also extract the numerical HCF of the coefficients.
  4. Commit to factoring because the residual bracket will then be linear in the remaining variable — exactly what is needed for root-finding (part (b) of differentiation questions) or for clean back-substitution (in integration).
  5. Execute the division term-by-term, then simplify the residual bracket carefully: distribute any negative signs across the linear factor, e.g. $3 - 2(2x+1) = 3 - 4x - 2 = 1 - 4x$.
  6. If integrating, combine the residual fractions over a common denominator AFTER back-substitution to reach the cleanest closed form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the HCF: numerical HCF of coefficients × lowest power of each shared bracket × any shared exponential/surd.
  2. Divide each term of the original expression by this HCF to obtain the residual bracket.
  3. Write expression = HCF × [residual].
  4. Simplify the residual: distribute signs, collect like terms, aiming for a linear factor such as $(1-4x)$ or $(6x-1)$.
  5. If fractions appear in the residual, combine over a common denominator and absorb any constant into the outer coefficient.
  6. Present the final form as a product of simple factors — this is the 'fully factorised' form.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $f'(x) = 6(2x+1)^2 e^{-4x} - 4(2x+1)^3 e^{-4x}$. HCF $= 2(2x+1)^2 e^{-4x}$. Dividing: $f'(x) = 2(2x+1)^2 e^{-4x}\,[3 - 2(2x+1)] = 2(2x+1)^2 e^{-4x}(1-4x)$. Roots of $f'(x)=0$ are now read off directly: $x = -\tfrac{1}{2}$ and $x = \tfrac{1}{4}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Integration result in $u$ before back-substitution, e.g. $\tfrac{2}{5}u^{5/2} - \tfrac{2}{3}u^{3/2}$ with $u = 4x+1$.
Factor out $u^{3/2}$ first to get $u^{3/2}\left[\tfrac{u}{5} - \tfrac{1}{3}\right]$; substitute, then combine the bracket over LCD 15 to obtain $\tfrac{1}{60}(4x+1)^{3/2}(6x-1)+C$.
Definite integrals where the result is evaluated numerically.
Factoring is optional — direct substitution of bounds is acceptable, though factoring can still reduce arithmetic.
Expressions with shared logarithm or trig factors, e.g. $x^2\ln x - x\ln x$.
Factor out $x\ln x$ to leave the linear residual $(x-1)$ — same lowest-power principle.

Common Misreadings

  • Expanding $(2x+1)^3$ and $(2x+1)^2$ in full and then trying to refactor — vastly more error-prone.
  • Factoring out the HIGHEST power $(2x+1)^3$ instead of the lowest, producing fractional/negative-power terms.
  • Skipping the factor step entirely after integration and leaving two separate surd terms, missing the 'simplest form' accuracy mark.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when distributing $-2$ across $(2x+1)$: must give $-4x-2$, not $-4x+2$.
  • Losing a numerical factor — e.g. writing $(2x+1)^2 e^{-4x}[6 - 4(2x+1)]$ (correct but not fully factorised; the 2 was not pulled out).
  • Miscombining the outer constant with the residual fractions: forgetting to multiply $\tfrac{1}{8}$ by $\tfrac{2}{15}$ to get $\tfrac{1}{60}$.
  • Arithmetic in $3(4x+1) - 5 = 12x - 2$ — students often write $12x - 8$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 scope. Used routinely after applying the product rule (P3 differentiation) and after integration by substitution (P4). No formula booklet entry is invoked — this is a pure algebraic manipulation. Avoid generalising to factoring multivariate polynomials or to non-integer/non-rational exponent algebra beyond what appears in standard surd/exponential simplification.
