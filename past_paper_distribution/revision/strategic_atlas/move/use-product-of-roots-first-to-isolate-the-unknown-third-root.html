Complex Numbers

Use product-of-roots first to isolate the unknown third root

Family: Strategic Sequencing Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a cubic with real coefficients has two roots forming a known complex conjugate pair, an unknown third root, and unknown coefficients (e.g. find a, b, and the third root given z³ + az² + bz + c = 0 with c known). The signal is: three Vieta relations available, three unknowns, but the product zz₂ of the conjugate pair is immediately computable as a real number. Equivalent phrasings: 'find the remaining root and coefficients', 'one root is p+qi, find the cubic'.

Motivation — Why this works

Vieta's product relation z₁z₂z₃ = −c/a contains only one unknown (z₃) once z₁z₂ is computed from the conjugate pair via (p+qi)(p−qi) = p²+q². The sum and pairwise-sum relations each mix z₃ with an unknown coefficient, so starting there forces simultaneous equations. Solving the product equation first decouples the system: z₃ is found by a single division, after which sum-of-roots gives a and sum-of-pairwise-products gives b, each from one linear equation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices three Vieta relations are available but there are three unknowns (z₃, a, b), so picking the right relation matters.
  2. Rules out starting with sum-of-roots (z₁+z₂+z₃ = −a) and pairwise-sum (z₁z₂+z₁z₃+z₂z₃ = b) because each couples z₃ with an unknown coefficient.
  3. Commits to product-of-roots because z₁z₂ is a known real number (modulus-squared of the conjugate pair), leaving z₃ as the only unknown.
  4. Bridges to action by computing z₁z₂ = p²+q² from the conjugate pair, then dividing the constant term to get z₃.
  5. Tactically uses the now-known z₃ to substitute into the remaining two Vieta relations, each of which becomes a single linear equation in a single unknown.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the conjugate pair z₁ = p+qi, z₂ = p−qi and compute z₁z₂ = p²+q² (real).
  2. Write the product-of-roots Vieta relation z₁z₂z₃ = −(constant term)/(leading coefficient).
  3. Solve the resulting linear equation for z₃.
  4. Substitute z₃ into sum-of-roots z₁+z₂+z₃ = −a/(leading) to find one coefficient.
  5. Substitute z₃ into pairwise-sum z₁z₂+z₁z₃+z₂z₃ = b/(leading) to find the other coefficient.
  6. Verify by expanding (z−z₁)(z−z₂)(z−z₃) and matching coefficients.

Worked Micro-Example

Cubic $z^3 + az^2 + bz - 10 = 0$ has roots $1-2i$, $1+2i$, $z_3$. Compute $z_1 z_2 = (1-2i)(1+2i) = 1+4 = 5$. Product of roots: $5 z_3 = 10$, so $z_3 = 2$. Sum: $(1-2i)+(1+2i)+2 = 4 = -a \Rightarrow a = -4$. Pairwise: $5 + 2(1-2i) + 2(1+2i) = 5+4 = 9 = b$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quartic with one known conjugate pair and two unknown roots
Product-of-roots gives the product of the two unknown roots; combine with sum-of-roots to get a quadratic factor for them rather than a single root.
Cubic where the third root is stated to be real but the constant term is unknown
Switch sequencing: use sum-of-roots first (one unknown if a is given) or factor out the known quadratic (z−z₁)(z−z₂) = z²−2pz+(p²+q²) by polynomial division.
Cubic given with leading coefficient ≠ 1
Use Vieta with the leading coefficient: z₁z₂z₃ = −d/a, sum = −b/a, pairwise = c/a. The sequencing logic is unchanged.

Common Misreadings

  • Starting with sum-of-roots, producing one equation in two unknowns (z₃ and a) and forcing a needless simultaneous system.
  • Treating the unknown coefficients as if they must be found before z₃, rather than recognising z₃ is the cleanest first target.
  • Forgetting that real coefficients guarantee the conjugate is automatically a root, and instead trying to derive both roots of the pair.

Pitfalls

  • Computing $(p+qi)(p-qi)$ as $p^2 - q^2$ by forgetting $i^2 = -1$; the correct value is $p^2 + q^2$.
  • Sign error on the product Vieta: for $z^3+az^2+bz+c$ the product is $-c$, not $+c$.
  • Mis-assigning the pairwise-sum sign: it equals $+b$ (with leading coefficient 1), not $-b$.
