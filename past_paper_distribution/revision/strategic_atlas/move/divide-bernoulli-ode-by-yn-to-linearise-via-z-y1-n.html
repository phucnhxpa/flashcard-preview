Differential Equations

Divide Bernoulli ODE by y^n to linearise via z = y^{1-n}

Family: Bernoulli Linearisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a first-order ODE has the Bernoulli shape dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x)y^n with n ≠ 0,1, making it nonlinear so a direct integrating factor fails. Typical Further Maths cues: a substitution z = y^{1-n} is given (e.g. z = y^{-2} when n = 3), or the question asks you to 'show that the substitution reduces the equation to a linear ODE'. Phrases like 'hence solve' after such a substitution also signal this move.

Motivation — Why this works

Dividing through by y^n produces the term y^{-n}(dy/dx), which by the chain rule equals (1/(1-n)) dz/dx where z = y^{1-n}. The remaining y term becomes y^{1-n} = z, so every term in the ODE rewrites cleanly in z and dz/dx. The result is a standard first-order linear ODE in z, solvable by integrating factor — a tool firmly in the IAL Further Pure syllabus.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the ODE is first-order but nonlinear because of the y^n on the RHS, so the basic integrating-factor template doesn't apply directly.
  2. Rule out separation (the P(x)y and Q(x)y^n terms can't be split into f(x)g(y) cleanly) and rule out exactness checks as overkill.
  3. Commit to the Bernoulli route because the suggested substitution z = y^{1-n} matches the index in the y^n term exactly.
  4. Bridge: differentiate z = y^{1-n} to get dz/dx = (1-n) y^{-n} (dy/dx), so y^{-n}(dy/dx) = (1/(1-n)) dz/dx — this tells me to divide the whole ODE by y^n.
  5. Tactically, after dividing by y^n and substituting, multiply through by (1-n) to clear fractions and present dz/dx + (1-n)P z = (1-n)Q in standard linear form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify n in dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x)y^n and the substitution z = y^{1-n}.
  2. Divide every term of the ODE by y^n to obtain y^{-n}(dy/dx) + P(x) y^{1-n} = Q(x).
  3. Compute dz/dx = (1-n) y^{-n}(dy/dx), hence y^{-n}(dy/dx) = (1/(1-n)) dz/dx.
  4. Substitute to get (1/(1-n)) dz/dx + P(x) z = Q(x).
  5. Multiply through by (1-n) to obtain the standard linear form dz/dx + (1-n)P(x) z = (1-n)Q(x), ready for an integrating factor.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve dy/dx + 2y = 3x y^3 with z = y^{-2}. Divide by y^3: y^{-3}(dy/dx) + 2y^{-2} = 3x. Since dz/dx = -2 y^{-3}(dy/dx), we have y^{-3}(dy/dx) = -\tfrac12 dz/dx, so -\tfrac12 dz/dx + 2z = 3x. Multiply by -2: dz/dx - 4z = -6x, a standard linear ODE in z.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Bernoulli with n = 2, e.g. dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x)y^2
Divide by y^2 and use z = y^{-1}; then dz/dx = -y^{-2}(dy/dx) gives dz/dx - P(x)z = -Q(x).
Bernoulli with n = 1/2 or other fractional n
Same recipe with z = y^{1-n}; the algebra of (1-n) is fractional but the structure is identical.
Substitution not given, only 'reduce to a linear ODE' is asked
Derive z = y^{1-n} from the index of the nonlinear term yourself before dividing.
Bernoulli in the form dy/dx = a(x)y + b(x)y^n (no explicit +Py term)
Rewrite as dy/dx - a(x)y = b(x)y^n first so P = -a, then divide by y^n as usual.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 3xy^3 as separable and trying to write dy/y^3 = (...)dx, ignoring the 2y term.
  • Applying the integrating factor e^{∫2 dx} directly to the nonlinear ODE without first linearising.
  • Mistaking the Bernoulli equation for a homogeneous-type ODE and trying y = vx.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to divide every term — including the RHS — by y^n.
  • Sign/coefficient error in dz/dx = (1-n) y^{-n}(dy/dx); for n = 3 the factor is -2, not +2.
  • Omitting the final multiplication by (1-n) and then misapplying the integrating factor to a non-standard form.
  • Forgetting at the end to convert z back to y via y = z^{1/(1-n)}.

A-level Scope Notes

Bernoulli equations with a stated substitution sit in Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3); the resulting linear ODE is solved by the integrating-factor method from Core/Further Pure, so all tools used remain inside the IAL syllabus.
