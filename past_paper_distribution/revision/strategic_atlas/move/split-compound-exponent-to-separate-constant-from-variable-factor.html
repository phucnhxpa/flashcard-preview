Split compound exponent to separate constant from variable factor

Family: Index Law Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an exponential term has a linear exponent of the form (c ± x) or (c ± kx) where c is a constant and x is the unknown, e.g. $6^{1-x}$, $2^{3+2x}$, $e^{2-x}$. Especially useful when the equation also contains another exponential in x with a different base, so logs cannot be applied cleanly to a single base. Cue phrases: 'show that x = ...', 'express as a single power', or a target answer involving $\log a / \log b$ that suggests a single combined exponential.

Motivation — Why this works

The index law $a^{m+n} = a^m \cdot a^n$ lets us peel off the constant part of the exponent as a numerical coefficient, leaving a clean $a^{\pm x}$ factor. This separates 'numbers' from 'unknown-in-the-exponent' terms, which is the structural prerequisite for either combining bases (via $a^x b^x = (ab)^x$) or taking a single log. Without the split, the equation mixes two roles inside one symbol and resists standard manipulation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the exponent $1-x$ is a sum of a constant and a variable — the term is doing two jobs at once.
  2. I rule out taking logs immediately: with two different bases (6 and 4) both carrying x, a direct log gives a linear-in-x equation but won't reach a clean single-log target like $\log 2/\log 24$.
  3. I commit to splitting because the index law $a^{m+n}=a^m a^n$ is exact, costs nothing, and exposes a constant factor I can move across the equals sign.
  4. Bridge: rewrite $6^{1-x} = 6^1 \cdot 6^{-x} = 6 \cdot 6^{-x}$, then convert $6^{-x}$ to $1/6^x$ so the unknown sits in a denominator I can clear.
  5. Tactically I track the sign on $-x$ carefully and decide whether to keep $6^{-x}$ or flip to $1/6^x$ depending on whether I next want to multiply through or combine with $4^x$ as $(4\cdot 6)^x$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the compound exponent and write it as a sum: $1-x = 1 + (-x)$.
  2. Apply $a^{m+n}=a^m a^n$: $6^{1-x} = 6^1 \cdot 6^{-x} = 6 \cdot 6^{-x}$.
  3. Optionally convert negative exponent: $6^{-x} = 1/6^x$, giving $6/6^x$.
  4. Substitute back into the original equation and clear the denominator (multiply by $6^x$) or regroup to use $a^x b^x = (ab)^x$.
  5. Isolate constants on one side and the single combined exponential on the other, ready for logs.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $6^{1-x} = 3 \cdot 4^x$.\\ Split: $6^{1-x} = 6 \cdot 6^{-x} = 6/6^x$, so $6/6^x = 3\cdot 4^x$.\\ Multiply by $6^x$ and divide by 3: $2 = 4^x \cdot 6^x = 24^x$.\\ Take logs: $x = \dfrac{\log 2}{\log 24}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$2^{3+2x} = 5$
Split as $2^3 \cdot 2^{2x} = 8 \cdot 4^x$, then divide by 8 before taking logs.
$e^{2-x} = k$
Split as $e^2 \cdot e^{-x}$; divide by $e^2$ then take ln, giving $x = 2 - \ln k$.
$3^{x+1} + 3^x = 12$
Split $3^{x+1} = 3 \cdot 3^x$ to factor $3^x(3+1)=12$, so $3^x=3$.
Reverse direction: $a \cdot b^x$ wanted as a single power
Combine via $a \cdot b^x = b^{x + \log_b a}$ when a single exponential form is required.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading $6^{1-x}$ as $6 - 6^x$ (treating the exponent as a subtraction at the base level).
  • Reading $6^{1-x}$ as $(6-x)^1 = 6-x$.
  • Treating $6^{1-x}$ as $6^1 - 6^x$ by false distribution over subtraction in the exponent.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors: writing $6^{1-x} = 6 \cdot 6^x$ instead of $6 \cdot 6^{-x}$.
  • Forgetting that $6^{-x} = 1/6^x$ and leaving the negative exponent unprocessed when trying to combine with $4^x$.
  • Combining $6^{-x}\cdot 4^x$ as $(6\cdot 4)^x$ — the bases can only multiply when the exponents match in sign; here you'd get $(4/6)^x$.

A-level Scope Notes

Pure index laws and logarithms at Edexcel IAL P2/P3 level; no calculus or complex numbers required. Standard tool in 'solve the exponential equation, give answer as $\log a/\log b$' style questions.
