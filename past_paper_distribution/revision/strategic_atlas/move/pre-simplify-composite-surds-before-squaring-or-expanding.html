Pre-simplify composite surds before squaring or expanding

Family: Surd Simplification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a surd √n with n having a perfect-square factor (e.g. √8, √12, √18, √50, √72) appears inside a bracket about to be squared, expanded, or rationalised. Cues include seeing (a ± k√n)² , (p + q√n)(r + s√n), or a √n term destined for repeated multiplication. Also fires when a coefficient multiplies the surd (like 5√8) and that whole term will be reused. If you can already see future steps producing (√n)² or k√n products, simplify now.

Motivation — Why this works

Every surd √n with n = a²b satisfies √n = a√b, converting a composite surd into a rational coefficient times a prime surd. Subsequent products then involve only √b, whose square is the small integer b, keeping intermediate arithmetic compact. Doing this front-loads the simplification: the same reduction would be forced later anyway, but buried in a longer expression where sign and coefficient errors multiply. Cleaner surds also make it easier to recognise the required final form a + b√c.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice a surd like √8 sitting inside a bracket that will be squared or expanded — its powers and cross-products will dominate the next few lines.
  2. I rule out expanding directly with √8: (√8)² = 8 is fine, but mixed terms like 3x·5√8 stay messier than they need to be, and I'd still have to simplify at the end.
  3. I commit to simplifying first because √8 = √(4·2) = 2√2 is a one-line rewrite that pays off in every subsequent product.
  4. Bridge: rewrite 5√8 as 5·2√2 = 10√2, then substitute back into the original bracket before any expansion.
  5. Tactically, I keep the external coefficient attached (5 × 2√2, not just 2√2), and I check the radicand factorisation uses the largest perfect square (4, not 1) so I don't have to simplify twice.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the largest perfect-square factor of the radicand: 8 = 4 × 2.
  2. Apply √(ab) = √a · √b: √8 = √4 · √2 = 2√2.
  3. Multiply through by any external coefficient: 5√8 = 5 · 2√2 = 10√2.
  4. Substitute the simplified surd back into the original expression before expanding, e.g. (3x − 5√8)² becomes (3x − 10√2)².
  5. Proceed with expansion using (√2)² = 2 wherever it appears.

Worked Micro-Example

Expand $(3x - 5\sqrt{8})^2$.\n First simplify: $5\sqrt{8} = 5 \cdot 2\sqrt{2} = 10\sqrt{2}$.\n Then $(3x - 10\sqrt{2})^2 = 9x^2 - 2(3x)(10\sqrt{2}) + (10\sqrt{2})^2$.\n $= 9x^2 - 60\sqrt{2}\,x + 100 \cdot 2 = 9x^2 - 60\sqrt{2}\,x + 200.$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Surd inside a product of conjugates, e.g. (2 + √18)(2 − √18)
Simplify √18 = 3√2 first, so the product becomes (2 + 3√2)(2 − 3√2) = 4 − 18 = −14, avoiding (√18)² = 18 mid-line.
Rationalising a denominator like 1/(3 + √72)
Rewrite √72 = 6√2 before multiplying by the conjugate, so the conjugate is 3 − 6√2 and the resulting denominator 9 − 72 = −63 is computed cleanly.
Solving quadratics whose discriminant is a composite surd, e.g. √48 in the formula
Simplify √48 = 4√3 before splitting the ± and dividing, so roots emerge directly in the form a ± b√3.
Binomial expansion with a surd term like (1 + √12)⁴
Replace √12 by 2√3 first; powers then alternate between integers and integer multiples of √3 with no further reduction needed.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 5√8 as √(25·8) = √200 and trying to work inside a single radical, which makes expansion harder, not easier.
  • Thinking the simplification can be left to the very end, not realising the intermediate algebra has already become messier than necessary.
  • Confusing √8 with ∛8 and writing √8 = 2, losing the surd entirely.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to multiply the external coefficient: writing 5√8 = 2√2 instead of 10√2.
  • Choosing a non-maximal square factor, e.g. √72 = 2√18 instead of 6√2, leaving more work to do.
  • Mis-simplifying the radical itself, e.g. √8 = 2√4 or √8 = 4√2.
  • Dropping a sign when substituting back into a bracket, e.g. turning (3x − 5√8) into (3x + 10√2).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1) surds work and reused throughout quadratics, binomial expansion, and rationalising denominators in P1–P4.
