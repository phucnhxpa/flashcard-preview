Differentiation

Implicitly differentiate a conic's Cartesian equation to obtain dy/dx for a tangent/normal at a parametric point

Family: Implicit Differentiation Frequency: 6 questions

Trigger — When to use

The curve is a conic given in Cartesian implicit form (e.g. y² = 4ax, x²/a² + y²/b² = 1, x²/a² − y²/b² = 1, or xy = c²) and the question asks you to 'show that' or 'use calculus to find' the tangent or normal at a general parametric point P (e.g. (at², 2at), (acosθ, bsinθ), (ct, c/t)). Verbal cues include 'use calculus', 'show that the tangent/normal is ...', or a 'show that' instruction that forbids quoting a formula-booklet result. The Cartesian form is not solved for y, and a clean gradient in terms of the parameter is required.

Motivation — Why this works

Implicit differentiation extracts dy/dx directly from the implicit Cartesian equation in one application of the chain rule, producing a rational expression in x and y that mirrors the structure of the original equation. This avoids solving for y (which would force ± square roots and split branches) and avoids setting up two separate parametric derivatives dx/dt and dy/dt before dividing. The underlying principle is the chain rule: differentiating y² with respect to x yields 2y(dy/dx). Once dy/dx is known in terms of x and y, the parametric coordinates of P are substituted at the end, giving the tangent gradient cleanly in terms of the parameter.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the curve equation is implicit (y² = ..., x²/a² + y²/b² = 1, xy = c²) and that 'use calculus' or 'show that' is mandated, so a formula-book tangent equation will not earn the marks.
  2. Rule out making y the subject (e.g. y = √(8x)) — this introduces fractional powers, ± branches, and sign ambiguity at points where y < 0.
  3. Consider parametric differentiation dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt): valid and equivalent, but requires two derivative computations and a division; preferred only when the Cartesian form is awkward.
  4. Commit to implicit differentiation because the equation is already in a clean implicit form and the resulting dy/dx will be a simple rational expression in x and y.
  5. Apply d/dx to every term, remembering the chain rule sends y² ↦ 2y(dy/dx) and any pure-y term ↦ (its y-derivative)·(dy/dx).
  6. Solve algebraically for dy/dx, then substitute the parametric coordinates of P to express the gradient in terms of the parameter; for a normal, take the negative reciprocal.
  7. Use point–gradient form y − y_P = m(x − x_P) and rearrange into the target form requested by the question.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Differentiate every term of the implicit equation with respect to x, using the chain rule on terms involving y (so y² gives 2y·dy/dx, and y in a product like xy gives x·dy/dx + y).
  2. Collect dy/dx terms on one side and solve algebraically: dy/dx = (expression in x and y).
  3. Substitute the parametric coordinates of P into this expression to obtain the gradient as a function of the parameter (e.g. 1/p, −bcosθ/(asinθ), −1/t²).
  4. If a normal is required, take the negative reciprocal of the tangent gradient.
  5. Apply point–gradient form y − y_P = m(x − x_P) and rearrange (often clearing denominators by multiplying through by the parameter) into the target form.

Worked Micro-Example

For $y^2 = 8x$ at $P(2p^2, 4p)$: differentiate implicitly to get $2y\,\frac{dy}{dx} = 8$, so $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{4}{y}$. At $P$: $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{4}{4p} = \frac{1}{p}$. Normal gradient $= -p$. Equation of normal: $y - 4p = -p(x - 2p^2) \Rightarrow y + px = 2p^3 + 4p$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

General parabola y² = 4ax at (at², 2at)
Implicit diff gives 2y·dy/dx = 4a, so dy/dx = 2a/y = 1/t at P. Tangent: ty = x + at².
Ellipse x²/a² + y²/b² = 1 at (acosθ, bsinθ)
Implicit diff gives 2x/a² + (2y/b²)·dy/dx = 0, so dy/dx = −b²x/(a²y) = −bcosθ/(asinθ). Tangent: (xcosθ)/a + (ysinθ)/b = 1.
Hyperbola x²/a² − y²/b² = 1
Sign flip gives dy/dx = b²x/(a²y); same procedure thereafter.
Rectangular hyperbola xy = c² at (ct, c/t)
Use product rule: y + x·dy/dx = 0, so dy/dx = −y/x = −1/t². Tangent: x + t²y = 2ct.

Common Misreadings

  • Rearranging to y = √(...) and differentiating explicitly, introducing surds and ± branch issues.
  • Misapplying the parametric chain rule: multiplying dy/dt by dx/dt instead of dividing.
  • Treating y as a constant and forgetting the chain rule entirely on y² (writing 2y instead of 2y·dy/dx).

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the dy/dx factor when differentiating any function of y (the most common error in implicit differentiation).
  • For a normal, writing the gradient as +1/m instead of −1/m (forgetting the negative in the negative reciprocal).
  • Sign and exponent slips when expanding (1/p)(x − ap²) — e.g. writing x − ap instead of x − ap².
  • Forgetting to multiply through by the parameter (or the denominator) when rearranging into the requested target form.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL scope: implicit differentiation is in P3/P4, and parametric points on conics (parabola, ellipse, hyperbola, rectangular hyperbola) are the standard FP1/FP3 'coordinate systems' material. The Edexcel formula booklet lists tangent equations for standard conics, but 'show that' / 'use calculus' wording explicitly demands a derivation — quoting the booklet result is not acceptable. Avoid generalising to non-conic implicit curves outside the syllabus.
