Trigonometry

Substitute the part (a) identity into part (b) to collapse the LHS

Family: Hence Substitution Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question uses the keyword 'hence' (or 'hence, or otherwise') in part (b)/(c), and the LHS of the new equation/integrand contains exactly the compound trigonometric expression that part (a) just proved equivalent to a simpler form (e.g. ½ tan θ, cot x, cosec x). The match may be literal, may carry a numerical scalar (e.g. ×9), or may involve an angle relabelling (x → 2θ). Whenever you see a messy fraction in (1 ± cos kθ), sin kθ, sec kθ etc. on the LHS that looks structurally identical to the identity from (a), this move fires.

Motivation — Why this works

The word 'hence' is an examiner instruction: method marks are explicitly tied to invoking the previously proven identity. Substituting collapses a multi-term, multi-angle trig expression into a single trig function (tan, cot, cosec…), turning an otherwise unwieldy equation or integral into a standard form solvable by routine A-level methods. Mathematically, the move exploits the equivalence proved in part (a) — once two expressions are proven equal, they are interchangeable everywhere. Skipping the substitution is technically possible but wastes time and risks losing the marks designed to reward joined-up reasoning.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice that part (b)'s LHS contains the same compound fraction (or a scalar multiple of it) as the identity I just proved in part (a) — the structural fingerprint matches.
  2. I rule out attacking the equation from scratch using double-angle formulae: it would reproduce part (a)'s work and, more importantly, the word 'hence' tells me the examiner does not want that path.
  3. I commit to direct substitution because the proven identity makes the two sides interchangeable, and the simplified form (single trig ratio) will leave an equation in one trig function — easily solved.
  4. Concretely, I locate the matching block, factor out any external scalar (e.g. the 9 in 9(1−cos 2θ)/(2 sin 2θ)), and replace the block with the RHS from part (a).
  5. If the angle has been relabelled (e.g. part (a) used x but part (b) uses 2θ), I carefully transcribe the identity with the new variable so the substitution remains valid.
  6. I then rewrite the resulting one-line equation (e.g. (9/2) tan θ = 2 sec²θ) and proceed with standard solving techniques.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write part (a)'s identity explicitly: f(x) ≡ g(x) (e.g. (1−cos 2x)/(2 sin 2x) ≡ ½ tan x).
  2. In part (b), isolate the sub-expression matching f(·), pulling out any external numerical scalar k.
  3. Replace k·f(·) with k·g(·) using the identity, taking care to substitute the correct argument.
  4. Rewrite the equation/integral in its simplified one-trig-function form.
  5. Solve or integrate using standard methods (e.g. tan θ = c, ∫ cosec x cot x dx = −cosec x + C).

Worked Micro-Example

Part (a) proved $\dfrac{1-\cos 2x}{2\sin 2x} = \tfrac12 \tan x$. Part (b) asks to solve $\dfrac{9(1-\cos 2\theta)}{2\sin 2\theta} = 2\sec^2\theta$. Substitute: LHS $= 9\cdot\tfrac12\tan\theta = \tfrac{9}{2}\tan\theta$. Equation becomes $\tfrac{9}{2}\tan\theta = 2\sec^2\theta$, i.e. $9\sin\theta\cos\theta = 4$, so $\tfrac{9}{2}\sin 2\theta = 4$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Part (a) result is used as an integrand in part (b) ('hence find ∫ …')
Replace the compound expression inside the integral with the simpler proven form, then integrate using standard results (e.g. ∫ cosec x cot x dx).
Part (a) is a differentiation result and part (b) says 'hence integrate'
Use the derivative result in reverse — the integrand matches the derivative, so the antiderivative is read off directly (reverse chain rule).
The angle in part (b) differs from part (a) (x → 2θ, or θ → θ/2)
Apply the identity with the new argument throughout; the structural equivalence is preserved under any consistent angle substitution.
Part (a) gives a partial-fraction decomposition; part (b) is a summation
Substitute the decomposition into ∑ and exploit telescoping — same 'use what you proved' principle.

Common Misreadings

  • Ignoring 'hence' and re-deriving the identity from scratch using double-angle formulae — wastes time and forfeits method marks.
  • Misidentifying which sub-expression matches part (a), especially when the angle has been rescaled (x vs 2θ) or a scalar multiplier is hiding the match.
  • Treating 'hence or otherwise' as licence to abandon part (a) entirely — 'hence' is still the intended (and shortest) route.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting an external scalar multiplier when substituting (e.g. yielding ½ tan θ instead of (9/2) tan θ).
  • Substituting with the wrong angle argument after a relabelling (writing tan x when the question is in θ).
  • Stopping at the simplified equation without solving — the substitution is the bridge, not the destination.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (P2/P3/P4) scope. Relies on double-angle identities from the formula booklet (sin 2x, cos 2x, tan 2x) and the standard reciprocal/quotient identities. No out-of-scope techniques (Weierstrass substitution, complex exponentials) are needed.
