Trigger — When to use

After integration by parts (often combined with a Pythagorean or trig identity), the original integral I reappears on the right-hand side, e.g. I_n = (n-1)I_{n-2} − (n-1)I_n, or I = f(x) − kI. Cue phrases: 'the integral has come back', 'I appears on both sides', or a coefficient multiplying the original integral after a second IBP. This is not circularity — it is a linear equation in the single unknown I.

Motivation — Why this works

An equation of the form I = A − kI (with k a known constant or function of n) is just a linear equation in I, so collecting like terms gives (1+k)I = A and hence I = A/(1+k). The IBP machinery has done its job by producing this equation; the final step is purely algebraic. Recognising this turns an apparent dead-end into a clean closed form or reduction formula.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the integral I (or I_n) appears on both sides of the worked equation after IBP — this is the key visual cue.
  2. Rule out starting a different technique: the reappearance is not a sign of failure but the expected endpoint of this strategy.
  3. Commit to treating I as an algebraic unknown, exactly as one would treat x in ax = b − cx.
  4. Bridge: add the RHS occurrence of I to both sides to gather all I-terms on the left.
  5. Tactically, factor the I-coefficient carefully (do not forget the implicit 1 on the LHS), then divide through.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the equation in the form I_n = (stuff) − kI_n (or I = f(x) − kI).
  2. Add kI_n to both sides to collect all I_n terms: I_n + kI_n = (stuff).
  3. Factor the LHS: (1+k)I_n = (stuff).
  4. Divide by (1+k) to isolate I_n.
  5. Simplify the resulting fraction; for reduction formulae, present as I_n = ((n-1)/n) I_{n-2} or similar.

Worked Micro-Example

From IBP on I_n = ∫ sin^n x dx with sin^2 x = 1 − cos^2 x, one obtains I_n = (n-1)I_{n-2} − (n-1)I_n. Add (n-1)I_n to both sides: nI_n = (n-1)I_{n-2}. Hence I_n = ((n-1)/n) I_{n-2}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

∫ e^x sin x dx via IBP twice
The original integral I reappears with coefficient −1, giving 2I = e^x(sin x − cos x), so I = ½ e^x(sin x − cos x) + C.
∫ sec^n x dx reduction formula
After IBP and using tan^2 x = sec^2 x − 1, collect terms to get (n-1)I_n = sec^{n-2}x tan x + (n-2)I_{n-2}.
∫ e^{ax} cos(bx) dx
Two IBPs return a multiple of the original integral; solve (1 + b^2/a^2)I = … algebraically.
Reduction formulae for ∫ x^n e^x dx variants where a substitution recreates the original
Same algebraic isolation step concludes the derivation.

Common Misreadings

  • Believing the reappearance of I means IBP has failed and abandoning the method.
  • Trying yet another IBP in the hope of 'breaking the loop', which only reproduces the same equation.
  • Reading kI as a separate new integral rather than as the original unknown.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slip in combining coefficients of I, especially confusing n with (n-1).
  • Forgetting the implicit coefficient 1 on the LHS occurrence of I when adding kI across.
  • Dropping the constant of integration when the move concludes a definite-form derivation that is later evaluated indefinitely.
  • Sign errors when the reappearing integral has a negative coefficient on the RHS.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard endgame for IBP problems in Edexcel IAL Pure (∫ e^x sin x dx type) and for reduction formulae in Further Pure (sin^n, cos^n, sec^n, x^n e^x). No content outside the specification is required.
