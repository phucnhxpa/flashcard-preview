Retain full calculator precision for intermediate values

Family: Numerical Accuracy Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an intermediate quantity (a length, angle, root, or iterate) is irrational or non-terminating and will be re-used in a later calculation — especially one involving subtraction of similar-magnitude terms (cosine rule, difference of squares), squaring, or multiplication by a large factor. Cue phrases include 'give your answer to 3 s.f.', 'hence find', or any multi-step problem where one computed value feeds into the next. Also fires in iterative numerical methods (Newton-Raphson, fixed-point iteration) where each iterate depends on the previous.

Motivation — Why this works

Rounding errors propagate and amplify: an error of magnitude δ in an intermediate value typically produces an error of order (∂f/∂x)·δ in the next stage, and subtraction of nearly equal numbers magnifies relative error catastrophically. By keeping the full calculator value (via ANS, STO/memory) and rounding only the reported answer, the working stays inside the mark scheme's tolerance window. This is a numerical-stability principle, not a notational nicety.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the value I just computed (e.g. OP = 1.5/sin 1.2) is irrational and will be substituted into another formula.
  2. I rule out writing OP ≈ 1.61 and re-entering it: the next step subtracts comparable terms, so rounding error will be amplified.
  3. I commit to storing OP exactly as the calculator holds it and only rounding the final answer to the requested precision.
  4. Operationally, I press STO→A (or use ANS) immediately after computing OP, then refer to A in the cosine rule expression.
  5. I double-check by recomputing the final answer using one extra digit of precision in the intermediate to confirm stability of the reported figure.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute the intermediate value and store it in calculator memory (e.g. STO→A) without writing down a rounded version for re-entry.
  2. Write the next-stage expression in terms of the stored variable (A), substituting the memory directly.
  3. Evaluate the full expression in one calculator step to keep all digits.
  4. Round only the final reported quantity to the required significant figures or decimal places.
  5. Optionally verify by recomputing with one fewer/more digit retained to confirm the rounded answer is stable.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $OP = \dfrac{1.5}{\sin 1.2}$ and $AP^2 = 25 + OP^2 - 10\,OP\cos 1.2$. Store $OP = 1.609365\ldots$ as $A$. Then $AP^2 = 25 + A^2 - 10A\cos 1.2 \approx 17.7286$, giving $AP \approx 4.21$ (3 s.f.). Re-entering $OP \approx 1.61$ would give $AP^2 \approx 17.74$ and $AP \approx 4.21$ here — but on borderline cases the third figure can shift.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cosine/sine rule chains where a side or angle computed in part (a) is used in part (b).
Store the part (a) value; substitute it via memory into part (b) rather than the rounded printed answer.
Newton-Raphson or fixed-point iteration $x_{n+1} = g(x_n)$ to a stated accuracy.
Keep each iterate at full calculator precision (use ANS) and only round the final iterate when checking the required d.p.
Trapezium/Simpson's rule where ordinates $y_i$ are summed.
Compute each $y_i$ to full precision (or store in a list) before summing; rounding ordinates to 2 d.p. corrupts the sum.
Statistics: computing $S_{xx}, S_{xy}$ then $r$ or regression coefficients.
Keep $S_{xx}$ etc. unrounded when forming ratios; rounding numerator and denominator separately distorts $r$.
Binomial/normal probabilities where a standardised $z$ is computed then looked up.
Use the unrounded $z$ in the calculator's normal CDF rather than rounding $z$ to 2 d.p. first.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 'give your answer to 3 s.f.' as an instruction to round every intermediate step to 3 s.f.
  • Believing that 'exact form' and 'full precision' are the same — exact form means surds/fractions kept symbolically, full precision means all calculator digits retained.
  • Assuming that because the final answer matches to 2 s.f. either way, the rounding habit is harmless — it fails on boundary cases.

Pitfalls

  • Copying a rounded intermediate value from written working back into the calculator instead of using stored memory.
  • Clearing the calculator (e.g. switching modes) and losing the stored value, then re-entering a truncated decimal.
  • Rounding an angle in degrees/radians before taking its sine or cosine, which is especially damaging for small angles.
  • Rounding both numerator and denominator of a ratio separately before dividing.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard expectation across Edexcel IAL Pure (trigonometry, numerical methods), Statistics (regression, correlation, normal distribution), and Mechanics (multi-stage force/kinematics problems); mark schemes routinely give a tolerance band that assumes unrounded intermediate working.
