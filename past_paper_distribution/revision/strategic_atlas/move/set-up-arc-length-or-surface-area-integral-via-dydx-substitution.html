Integration

Set up arc-length or surface-area integral via dy/dx substitution

Family: Geometric Integral Setup Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks for the arc length of a curve y = f(x) over [a,b], or the surface area generated when such a curve is rotated about the x- or y-axis. Cue phrases include 'length of the arc', 'find the length of the curve', 'surface area of revolution', or 'area of the surface formed when ... is rotated'. Often appears as a later part of a question whose earlier parts establish a reduction formula or a useful integral I_n that the integrand will collapse into.

Motivation — Why this works

Arc length L = ∫√(1 + (dy/dx)²) dx and surface area S = 2π ∫ y √(1 + (dy/dx)²) dx convert a geometric measurement into a definite integral via Pythagoras on an infinitesimal arc element ds. The strategic value lies in choosing the right formula (length vs surface, axis of revolution) and then exploiting the fact that exam-designed curves are engineered so 1 + (dy/dx)² simplifies to a perfect square, giving a clean integrand. This often deliberately matches an integral computed earlier in the question, tying the parts together.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question asks for a length or surface area of a specific curve — this is a geometric quantity, not a plain integral.
  2. I rule out direct integration of y or area-under-curve formulas; these compute the wrong geometric object.
  3. I commit to the arc-length / surface-of-revolution framework because the wording matches and the curve is given as y = f(x) on a closed interval.
  4. I bridge to computation by writing down the relevant formula from the booklet and differentiating y to get dy/dx.
  5. Inside the move I form 1 + (dy/dx)², actively hunt for a perfect-square factorisation, take the square root cleanly, and check whether the resulting integrand matches an earlier I_n or a standard form before evaluating.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the required formula: L = ∫_a^b √(1 + (dy/dx)²) dx, or S = 2π ∫_a^b y √(1 + (dy/dx)²) dx for rotation about the x-axis.
  2. Compute dy/dx from the given curve equation and square it carefully.
  3. Form 1 + (dy/dx)² and simplify; look for a perfect square so the square root vanishes cleanly.
  4. Substitute the simplified expression into the integrand, including the factor 2πy if surface area.
  5. Evaluate the definite integral, invoking any earlier reduction formula or standard result if the integrand matches.

Worked Micro-Example

For y = (x³)/6 + 1/(2x) on [1,2]: dy/dx = x²/2 − 1/(2x²), so (dy/dx)² = x⁴/4 − 1/2 + 1/(4x⁴). Then 1 + (dy/dx)² = x⁴/4 + 1/2 + 1/(4x⁴) = (x²/2 + 1/(2x²))². Hence L = ∫_1^2 (x²/2 + 1/(2x²)) dx = [x³/6 − 1/(2x)]_1^2 = 17/12.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Parametric curve x = x(t), y = y(t)
Use L = ∫ √((dx/dt)² + (dy/dt)²) dt; for surface about x-axis, S = 2π ∫ y √((dx/dt)² + (dy/dt)²) dt.
Curve given as x = g(y) integrated in y
Use L = ∫ √(1 + (dx/dy)²) dy; surface about y-axis becomes S = 2π ∫ x √(1 + (dx/dy)²) dy.
Surface of revolution about the y-axis (curve y = f(x))
Replace y by x as the radius: S = 2π ∫ x √(1 + (dy/dx)²) dx.
Polar curve r = f(θ) (FP scope where included)
Use L = ∫ √(r² + (dr/dθ)²) dθ over the given θ-range.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing arc length with surface area — omitting the 2πy factor, or including it when only length is required.
  • Using the wrong axis of revolution, so that the radius factor is y when it should be x (or vice versa).
  • Treating the question as a volume of revolution (πy²) instead of a surface or length problem.

Pitfalls

  • Algebraic slips when squaring dy/dx, especially with fractions or two-term derivatives — the cross term sign matters for the perfect-square recognition.
  • Attempting to integrate √(1 + (dy/dx)²) without first simplifying, producing an intractable integrand.
  • Missing the link to an earlier part's reduction formula or standard integral I_n, and so failing to evaluate the integral cleanly.
  • Forgetting to substitute the limits in x (not in any intermediate substitution variable) at the end.

A-level Scope Notes

Arc length and surface area of revolution (Cartesian and parametric) sit within Edexcel IAL Further Pure; polar arc length appears only where the spec includes it. All formulas are provided in the formula booklet, so the assessed skill is correct selection, derivative computation, and algebraic simplification.
