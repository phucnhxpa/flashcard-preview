Algebra & Functions

Collect self-referencing integral and solve as a linear equation

Family: Loop Closure Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

After applying integration by parts (often twice), the original integral $I$ or reduction term $I_n$ reappears on the right-hand side, multiplied by some constant. The equation now has the form $I = [\text{boundary terms}] + \alpha I_{\text{lower}} + \beta I$ with $\beta \neq 1$. Cues include: 'the integral has come back', a coefficient like $-n^2 I_n$ or $-I$ on the RHS, or two IBPs returning to the starting integrand. This is a stop-signal for further IBP and a start-signal for algebra.

Motivation — Why this works

Once $I$ appears on both sides, further integration by parts will only reproduce the loop. Treating the identity as a linear equation in the unknown $I$ collapses the cycle: gather $I$-terms, factor, divide. The divisor $(1-\beta)$ is non-zero in all standard A-level surfaces (e.g. $1+n^2 > 0$, or $2$ for $\int e^x\sin x\,dx$), so the closure is unconditional. This is the algebraic step that converts a circular IBP identity into a usable closed form or reduction formula.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that the integral on the RHS is the same $I_n$ (or $I$) that started on the LHS — IBP has looped.
  2. Rules out a third IBP: it would just multiply the loop, not break it.
  3. Commits to algebraic closure: this is now a linear equation in the symbol $I_n$, not an integral problem.
  4. Bridges to operation by adding the RHS $I_n$-term to the LHS, combining coefficients carefully.
  5. Decides to factor any common boundary structure (e.g. $\sin^{n-1}x$) only after dividing, to keep the algebra clean.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify all occurrences of $I_n$ (or $I$) on both sides; mark their coefficients with sign.
  2. Move every $I_n$-term to the LHS, leaving boundary terms and lower-order $I_{n-2}$ on the RHS.
  3. Combine coefficients to a single factor, e.g. $(1+n^2)I_n = \dots$ or $2I = \dots$.
  4. Verify the divisor is non-zero (always true at A-level scope here).
  5. Divide through to obtain the closed form or reduction formula; tidy boundary terms by factoring.

Worked Micro-Example

For $I=\int e^x\sin x\,dx$, two IBPs give $I = e^x\sin x - e^x\cos x - I$. Collecting: $2I = e^x(\sin x - \cos x)$, so $I = \tfrac{1}{2}e^x(\sin x - \cos x) + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$\int e^{ax}\sin(bx)\,dx$ and $\int e^{ax}\cos(bx)\,dx$ at Core level
Two IBPs return $-\tfrac{b^2}{a^2}I$ (or similar); divisor becomes $1+\tfrac{b^2}{a^2}$, equivalently $a^2+b^2$ after clearing.
$\int \sec^3 x\,dx$
One IBP plus the identity $\tan^2 x = \sec^2 x - 1$ makes $\int\sec^3 x\,dx$ reappear with coefficient $-1$; divisor is $2$.
Reduction formula for $I_n=\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^n x\,dx$ derived via IBP before using $\cos^2 = 1-\sin^2$
After substitution, $I_n$ reappears with coefficient $-(n-1)$; collecting gives $nI_n = (n-1)I_{n-2}$.
Reduction formulae involving $e^x\sin^n x$ or $e^x\cos^n x$
Coefficient on the returning $I_n$ is $-n^2$; divisor is $1+n^2$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the recurrence as 'IBP failed' and restarting with a different $u,dv$ choice.
  • Reading $-n^2 I_n$ as a separate integral to evaluate rather than a copy of the unknown.
  • Confusing this with a reduction formula in $I_{n-2}$ only — missing that $I_n$ itself is on both sides.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when moving $\beta I_n$ across the equality, especially after expanding $n(n-1)$ from a Pythagorean substitution.
  • Forgetting that expanding a bracket like $n(\sin^2 x)I_{n-2}$ via $\sin^2 = 1-\cos^2$ produces an extra $I_n$ term that must also be collected.
  • Dividing by $(1+n^2)$ but failing to divide every term on the RHS, including boundary terms.
  • Not factoring $\sin^{n-1}x$ (or analogous) from the boundary terms in the final presentation, losing marks for form.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL scope: appears in P3 (the $\int e^x\sin x\,dx$ classic), in Further Pure for $\int\sec^3 x\,dx$, and in Further Pure reduction-formula questions. No techniques beyond IBP, basic trig identities, and linear algebra are needed.
