Coordinate Geometry

Equate curve and line to find intersection x-coordinates

Family: Intersection Finding Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question presents a curve y = f(x) together with a line (often horizontal y = k or oblique y = mx + c) and asks for points of intersection, or when intersection x-values are needed as integration limits for a bounded-area part. Phrasings include 'find the coordinates where C meets l', 'the line crosses the curve at E and F', or a diagram showing a shaded region between the two graphs. Also fires implicitly when a later 'find the area enclosed' part requires limits not yet stated.

Motivation — Why this works

At an intersection both equations hold simultaneously, so equating the y-expressions reduces a 2D geometric condition to a single-variable equation in x. The roots are exactly the x-coordinates of the intersection points, and substituting back into either equation recovers y. In bounded-area problems these roots double as the lower and upper limits of the definite integral, so this one move serves both the locate-points task and the set-up-integration task.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that two graphs are given and the question wants either intersection points or an area between them — both need the meeting x-values.
  2. Rule out solving the curve and line separately or guessing from the diagram; the algebraic condition f(x) = g(x) is exact and required for limits.
  3. Commit because equating y-values is the standard, lossless reduction: any (x, y) satisfying both must satisfy f(x) = g(x).
  4. Bridge to the first operation: write f(x) = g(x), move everything to one side to get a polynomial equal to zero, ready for factoring or the quadratic formula.
  5. Tactically, try factoring first; if it fails, use the quadratic formula; finally order the roots (smaller = leftmost point) to match the labels in the diagram.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the simultaneous condition f(x) = g(x).
  2. Rearrange to f(x) − g(x) = 0 so the equation is set to zero.
  3. Factor (or apply the quadratic formula) to obtain the roots x = a, x = b.
  4. Substitute back into the line (usually simpler) to get the y-coordinates.
  5. Match roots to labelled points using the diagram (smaller x to the left).

Worked Micro-Example

Curve $y = x^2 - 4x + 5$ meets line $y = 2$. Equate: $x^2 - 4x + 5 = 2 \Rightarrow x^2 - 4x + 3 = 0$. Factor: $(x-1)(x-3) = 0$, so $x = 1, 3$. Hence $E = (1, 2)$ and $F = (3, 2)$, and these also serve as integration limits for any enclosed-area follow-up.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Oblique line y = mx + c meeting a quadratic curve
Equating still produces a quadratic in x, but the constant and linear terms are altered; the area integrand becomes f(x) − (mx + c), which is non-constant.
Curve meets a vertical line x = a
No equation to solve — substitute x = a directly into the curve to get y.
Two curves y = f(x) and y = g(x) intersecting
Same move: set f(x) = g(x) and solve; resulting polynomial may be cubic or higher and require factor theorem.
Quadratic that does not factor neatly
Apply the quadratic formula; limits become surds and the area integral is evaluated symbolically.
Tangency case (line touches curve)
The quadratic has a repeated root (discriminant zero); only one intersection point, often used to find an unknown parameter.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the line and curve as needing separate solution sets rather than a simultaneous condition.
  • Assuming intersection x-values can be read off the diagram instead of solved algebraically.
  • Confusing 'where the curve meets the x-axis' (set y = 0) with 'where the curve meets the line y = k'.

Pitfalls

  • Trying to factor f(x) before subtracting g(x), e.g. factoring x² − 4x + 5 directly.
  • Sign errors when moving the line's constant across the equals sign.
  • Mis-assigning labels E and F when the diagram orders points by x but the roots are written in the opposite order.
  • Forgetting to compute the y-coordinates when full coordinates are required.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard P1/P2 coordinate-geometry technique within Edexcel IAL Maths; the resulting equations are at most cubic and solvable by factoring, the quadratic formula, or the factor theorem.
