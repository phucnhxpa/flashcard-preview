Algebra & Functions

Apply Vieta's formulas to read off sum and product of roots

Family: Vieta'S Formulas Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0 is given with roots labelled α and β, and the question asks for symmetric expressions in those roots (e.g. α² + β², 1/α + 1/β, α³ + β³, or to form a new quadratic with related roots) rather than the roots themselves. A strong secondary cue is a negative or ugly discriminant — solving explicitly would force complex numbers or surds. Equivalent phrasings: 'without solving the equation, find...', 'the equation has roots α, β; find...', or 'form an equation whose roots are f(α), f(β)'.

Motivation — Why this works

Vieta's formulas link the coefficients of a polynomial directly to the elementary symmetric polynomials of its roots: for a quadratic, α + β = −b/a and αβ = c/a. Every symmetric polynomial in two variables can be rewritten in terms of these two quantities, so once the sum and product are known, the roots themselves are never needed. This bypasses the quadratic formula entirely and keeps the working in clean rational arithmetic, even when the actual roots are irrational or complex.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structural cue: roots α, β are named, and the target expression is symmetric in them (swapping α ↔ β leaves it unchanged).
  2. Consider the alternative — solving via the quadratic formula — and rule it out: either the discriminant is negative (giving complex roots) or it's irrational (giving messy surds), and substituting back into a symmetric expression is error-prone.
  3. Commit to Vieta's because every symmetric function of α and β can be re-expressed using only S = α + β and P = αβ.
  4. Bridge to action: identify a, b, c carefully from the given quadratic, paying attention to signs and to the leading coefficient if a ≠ 1.
  5. Tactical care: normalise by dividing by a — write S = −b/a and P = c/a as fractions, not just −b and c, so the leading coefficient is never forgotten downstream.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Read off a, b, c from ax² + bx + c = 0, including signs (e.g. from 2x² − 6x + 3, we have a = 2, b = −6, c = 3).
  2. Write α + β = −b/a.
  3. Write αβ = c/a.
  4. Record both as labelled quantities (S and P) to substitute into the symmetric expression the question actually asks for.

Worked Micro-Example

Given 2x² + 5x + 7 = 0 with roots α, β: a = 2, b = 5, c = 7, so α + β = −5/2 and αβ = 7/2. Then for example α² + β² = (α+β)² − 2αβ = 25/4 − 7 = −3/4, computed without ever finding α or β individually.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cubic ax³ + bx² + cx + d = 0 with roots α, β, γ
Three elementary symmetric relations: Σα = −b/a, Σαβ = c/a, αβγ = −d/a. Same philosophy, one more layer.
Non-monic quadratic with negative middle coefficient, e.g. 2x² − 6x + 3 = 0
Be careful with signs: α + β = −(−6)/2 = 3, αβ = 3/2. The minus sign in −b/a flips the sign of b.
Forming a new quadratic with roots f(α), f(β) (e.g. 1/α, 1/β or α+1, β+1)
Compute the new sum S' and new product P' in terms of S, P, then write x² − S'x + P' = 0.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to solve the quadratic explicitly first, then substituting — works in principle but introduces surds or complex numbers and is unnecessary.
  • Treating the question as if it asks for α and β individually when only a symmetric combination is needed.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to divide by the leading coefficient: writing α + β = −b instead of −b/a when a ≠ 1.
  • Sign error: writing α + β = +b/a or misreading b's sign from the equation (e.g. taking b = 6 instead of −6 from 2x² − 6x + 3).
  • Using c/a for the sum and −b/a for the product (swapping the two formulas).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP1) 'Roots of quadratic equations' chapter. The quadratic Vieta's relations are stated explicitly; the cubic extension appears in later FP material. Avoid invoking general symmetric-polynomial theorems or Newton's identities by name — derive higher symmetric functions from S and P case by case.
