Coordinate Geometry

Substitute the given point into dy/dx then apply point-gradient formula

Family: Tangent From Implicit Derivative Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Part (b) of an implicit-differentiation question (or a parametric/logarithmic differentiation question) asks for the equation of the tangent (or normal) at a specific point (x₀, y₀). Part (a) has produced dy/dx as an expression in BOTH x and y (or in a parameter t), not as a number. Visual cues: a previous part yielded dy/dx = (expression in x, y); a named point is given; the question asks 'find the equation of the tangent/normal' — often demanding the answer in integer-coefficient form ax + by + c = 0.

Motivation — Why this works

For an implicitly defined curve, dy/dx is genuinely a function of both x and y; the gradient at a specific point can only be obtained by substituting both coordinates simultaneously. This is precisely why we left dy/dx in mixed form in part (a) — we never had to solve for y(x) explicitly. Once the numerical gradient m is known, the tangent follows immediately from the point-gradient identity y − y₀ = m(x − x₀), which avoids the extra algebraic step of computing the y-intercept c.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that dy/dx from part (a) is an expression in x and y (or in a parameter), not a number — so the gradient at the given point is not yet known.
  2. Rule out re-differentiating, solving for y explicitly, or using a numerical/limit method: all the algebra needed has already been done in part (a), so reusing that expression is fastest.
  3. Commit to: (i) substitute the given coordinates directly into dy/dx to get a numerical gradient m; (ii) feed (x₀, y₀, m) into point-gradient form.
  4. Choose y − y₀ = m(x − x₀) over y = mx + c: the point and gradient are directly available, so this avoids a wasted step computing c and a wasted opportunity for a sign error.
  5. Watch for clean simplifications when substituting: terms like ln 1 = 0, e⁰ = 1, or sin 0 = 0 often collapse the expression — make sure to zero them out rather than carry them.
  6. Finally, clear fractions by multiplying through by the common denominator and rearrange to integer-coefficient form (ax + by + c = 0 or 'ay = bx + c'), which is the presentation the Edexcel mark scheme typically rewards.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write down dy/dx from part (a) and the target point (x₀, y₀).
  2. Substitute x = x₀ and y = y₀ into dy/dx; simplify numerator and denominator separately to obtain m as a single fraction.
  3. Write the tangent in point-gradient form: y − y₀ = m(x − x₀).
  4. Multiply both sides by the denominator of m to clear the fraction.
  5. Expand and rearrange to integer-coefficient form, e.g. ax + by + c = 0.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2x+y}{2y-x}$ and the point $(3,5)$: substitute to get $m = \frac{2(3)+5}{2(5)-3} = \frac{11}{7}$. Tangent: $y-5 = \tfrac{11}{7}(x-3)$. Multiply by 7: $7y-35 = 11x-33$, hence $11x - 7y + 2 = 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Normal line instead of tangent
Replace m by −1/m (negative reciprocal) before applying y − y₀ = m_normal(x − x₀).
Parametric curve with parameter t = t₀
Compute dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt), substitute t = t₀ to get m, then use the same point-gradient formula with (x(t₀), y(t₀)).
dy/dx involves logarithmic/exponential terms
Exploit collapses like ln 1 = 0 or e⁰ = 1 at the substitution stage to simplify before forming the tangent line.

Common Misreadings

  • Substituting only x = x₀ into dy/dx and then trying to solve the original equation for y, when y₀ is already given.
  • Treating dy/dx as if it were already a number and writing the tangent as y − y₀ = (dy/dx)(x − x₀) without performing the substitution.
  • Substituting (x₀, y₀) into the original curve equation rather than into the derivative.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors in the denominator (e.g. computing 2y − x as x − 2y).
  • Forgetting to zero out vanishing terms such as ln 1 = 0 and carrying a phantom factor through the calculation.
  • Switching to y = mx + c form and miscomputing c, when point-gradient form would have avoided the issue entirely.
  • Leaving the answer with fractional coefficients when the mark scheme expects an integer-coefficient form.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 implicit differentiation, parametric differentiation, and coordinate geometry of the straight line. No formula-booklet entry is required: the point-gradient form y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) is assumed knowledge. Avoid invoking multivariable gradient vectors ∇F or normal-vector arguments — those are out of scope.
