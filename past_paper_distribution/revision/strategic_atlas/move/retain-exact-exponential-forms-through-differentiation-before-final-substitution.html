Retain exact exponential forms through differentiation before final substitution

Family: Exact Form Retention Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Earlier parts of a question yield constants in exact exponential form (e.g. p = 10^{0.32}, q = 10^{0.03}, or a = e^{k}) that students are tempted to convert to decimals. A later part asks for a numerical value — typically a rate of change, a limit-evaluation, or a product — that requires chaining these constants through differentiation, multiplication, or a logarithm. The cue is: the exponents are simple decimals or fractions that combine cleanly, and the mark scheme specifies a precision (e.g. 3 s.f. or 2 d.p.) that is sensitive to compounded rounding.

Motivation — Why this works

Exponent and logarithm laws act exactly on symbolic forms: 10^{a} \cdot 10^{b} = 10^{a+b} and \ln(10^{c}) = c\ln 10 introduce no error, whereas decimal approximations of 10^{a} drift under multiplication. Keeping the base-and-exponent structure also exposes recognisable exact values (e.g. 10^{0.5} = \sqrt{10}) that simplify the algebra. Premature rounding of p and q to 3–4 d.p. before differentiating typically corrupts the second or third significant figure of the final answer, costing accuracy marks.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice p and q came out as 10^{0.32} and 10^{0.03}, which are exact — the decimals 2.089 and 1.072 are just convenient labels.
  2. I rule out substituting decimals into the model A = p\,q^{t} immediately, because the next part needs dA/dt at a specific t and rounding will compound.
  3. I commit to the symbolic form A = 10^{0.32+0.03t}, since exponent addition is exact and differentiation of 10^{f(t)} is routine.
  4. First operation: differentiate using \frac{d}{dt}10^{u(t)} = 10^{u}\,u'(t)\,\ln 10, keeping \ln 10 symbolic.
  5. At the substitution stage I simplify the exponent arithmetically (e.g. 0.32 + 0.03\cdot 6 = 0.50), recognise 10^{0.5} = \sqrt{10}, and only convert to decimal at the final line.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rewrite the model with constants in exact exponential form: A = 10^{0.32} \cdot (10^{0.03})^{t} = 10^{0.32+0.03t}.
  2. Differentiate symbolically: \frac{dA}{dt} = 10^{0.32+0.03t} \cdot 0.03\ln 10.
  3. Substitute the required t-value into the exponent and simplify arithmetically (e.g. 0.32+0.18 = 0.50).
  4. Recognise exact values where possible (10^{0.5} = \sqrt{10}, 10^{1} = 10, etc.).
  5. Compute the final decimal only at the last step, to the precision the mark scheme demands.

Worked Micro-Example

Given A = 10^{0.32} \cdot (10^{0.03})^{t}, find \frac{dA}{dt} at t = 6. Write A = 10^{0.32+0.03t}, so \frac{dA}{dt} = 10^{0.32+0.03t}(0.03\ln 10). At t=6 the exponent is 0.50, giving \frac{dA}{dt} = 0.03\sqrt{10}\,\ln 10 \approx 0.218 (3 s.f.).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Exponential model with base e, e.g. N = N_{0}e^{kt} where k was found as \tfrac{1}{5}\ln 2
Keep k in logarithmic form; substitute only at the final numerical line so that e^{kt} simplifies exactly to 2^{t/5}.
Carrying surds through integration before applying limits, e.g. \int_{0}^{\sqrt{3}} \dots
Leave \sqrt{3} symbolic in the antiderivative; substitute and rationalise only at the end.
Trigonometric constants such as \cos(\pi/6) = \tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} appearing inside a chain-rule derivative
Retain the exact surd through the algebra and round once at the final evaluation.
Binomial-expansion coefficients of the form \binom{n}{k}p^{k} where p is itself irrational
Keep p symbolic until the final probability or expectation is assembled.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating p \approx 2.089 as the 'real' value and 10^{0.32} as a working step, rather than the other way round.
  • Believing that 4-decimal-place intermediate values are 'accurate enough' regardless of how many operations follow.
  • Confusing exact-form retention with leaving the answer un-evaluated — the final line should still be a decimal when one is requested.

Pitfalls

  • Substituting rounded p, q into the derivative formula and reporting a final answer wrong in the second decimal place.
  • Forgetting the \ln 10 factor when differentiating 10^{f(t)} (a side-effect of switching too eagerly to decimal form).
  • Adding the exponents incorrectly, e.g. writing 0.32 + 0.03\cdot 6 = 0.5 without showing the arithmetic and then misquoting it as 0.05.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 differentiation of exponentials and logarithms, and applies equally to growth-and-decay modelling questions in S-papers; no techniques beyond standard exponent and log laws are required.
