Trigonometry

Bound a linear-in-sine expression using sine's range

Family: Range Bounding Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The expression to be maximized or minimized has the form A + B sin(kx) (or A + B cos(kx)) — linear in a single sine or cosine term. The question asks for the extreme value over all x (no domain restriction beyond a full period). Common phrasings: 'find the maximum gradient', 'greatest value of', 'least value of'. The presence of a single trig term with constant coefficients is the key cue.

Motivation — Why this works

Since sin θ and cos θ are bounded in [−1, 1], any expression A + B sin θ is bounded in [A − |B|, A + |B|]. The extremes are attained when sin θ = ±1, so no calculus is needed. The sign of B determines which bound gives the maximum: if B > 0, max at sin θ = 1; if B < 0, max at sin θ = −1. This bypasses solving derivative equations entirely.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the expression is linear in a single trig term sin(2x), with constant coefficients 4 and −12.
  2. I rule out a calculus approach (setting a second derivative to zero) as needlessly heavy for a bounded linear expression.
  3. I commit to the range argument because sin(2x) ∈ [−1, 1] forces the expression into a closed interval, with extremes at the endpoints.
  4. I check the sign of the coefficient of sin(2x): it is −12 < 0, so the expression decreases as sin(2x) increases, meaning the max occurs at sin(2x) = −1.
  5. I substitute the chosen bound directly to compute the maximum value.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the expression in the form A + B sin(kx) and identify A and B.
  2. State the bound: −1 ≤ sin(kx) ≤ 1.
  3. Determine which bound maximizes (or minimizes) by the sign of B.
  4. Substitute sin(kx) = ±1 into A + B sin(kx) to obtain the extreme value.
  5. (Optional) Verify the bound is attainable: sin(kx) = ±1 has solutions in the relevant domain.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the maximum value of $4 - 12\sin 2x$. Since $-1 \le \sin 2x \le 1$ and the coefficient $-12 < 0$, the expression is maximized when $\sin 2x = -1$. Thus the maximum value is $4 - 12(-1) = 16$, attained when $2x = -\tfrac{\pi}{2} + 2k\pi$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Expression A + B cos(kx)
Same logic: max = A + |B|, min = A − |B|; bound chosen by sign of B.
After R-form conversion of a sin θ + b cos θ = R sin(θ + α)
Range is [A − R, A + R] for A + R sin(θ + α); use R = √(a² + b²).
Maximum/minimum of a gradient dy/dx that is linear in sin or cos
Apply the same range argument to dy/dx rather than to y itself.
Reciprocal forms like 1/(A + B sin θ) with A > |B|
Bound the denominator first, then invert: max of reciprocal corresponds to min of denominator.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing the maximum of y with the maximum of dy/dx when the question explicitly asks for the maximum gradient.
  • Treating sin(2x) and sin(x) as having different ranges — both lie in [−1, 1].
  • Assuming the maximum of A + B sin θ is always A + B regardless of the sign of B.

Pitfalls

  • Substituting sin(kx) = 1 instead of −1 when the coefficient is negative, giving the minimum instead of the maximum.
  • Attempting a second-derivative or stationary-point test on the linear-in-sine function, which is unnecessary.
  • Forgetting to check that the extremal value of sin(kx) is achievable within any restricted domain stated in the question.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P2/P3): uses only the bounded range of sine and cosine and direct substitution, with optional connection to the R sin(θ + α) form from P3.
