Algebra & Functions

Take the real cube root to solve r³ = k

Family: Root Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a single-variable equation has the unknown raised to an odd power equal to a constant, e.g. r³ = 1/8, x³ = 27, or r⁵ = 1/32. Common surfaces include solving for the common ratio in a geometric sequence (after dividing aₙ by aₘ), inverting a cubic volume relation, or finishing a problem where ar^n = b reduces to a pure power equation. Cue words: 'find the common ratio', 'cube root', or any rⁿ = k with n odd.

Motivation — Why this works

For odd n, the function x ↦ xⁿ is strictly increasing on ℝ, so xⁿ = k has exactly one real solution x = k^(1/n). No ± case-split is needed (unlike even powers). Recognising k as a perfect odd power (e.g. 1/8 = (1/2)³) lets the root be read off by inspection, avoiding a calculator and keeping the answer exact.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation has the form rⁿ = k with n odd, so a unique real root exists.
  2. Rule out a ± alternative: that habit comes from even powers; here (−1/2)³ = −1/8 ≠ 1/8.
  3. Commit to taking the real cube (or odd) root because the function is one-to-one on ℝ.
  4. Look for a perfect-power factorisation of k: 1/8 = (1/2)³, so the root is exact and immediate.
  5. Express the answer as a simple fraction (or surd) rather than a decimal to preserve exactness.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the equation as rⁿ = k with n odd.
  2. Rewrite k as a perfect nth power if possible, e.g. 1/8 = (1/2)³.
  3. Apply r = k^(1/n) to obtain the unique real root.
  4. State the single solution; do not append a ± sign.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $r^3 = \tfrac{1}{8}$.\ Since the exponent is odd, there is exactly one real root: $r = \left(\tfrac{1}{8}\right)^{1/3}$.\ Recognise $\tfrac{1}{8} = \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^3$, hence $r = \tfrac{1}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Negative right-hand side, e.g. r³ = −1/27
Still one real root; r = −1/3 because (−1/3)³ = −1/27.
Higher odd power, e.g. r⁵ = 1/32
Same logic: r = (1/32)^(1/5) = 1/2; unique real fifth root.
Even power, e.g. r² = 1/4 or r⁴ = 16
Two real roots ±k^(1/n); use context (e.g. positive ratio, given range) to select one.
Geometric sequence: aₙ/aₘ = rⁿ⁻ᵐ with n−m odd
Divide terms first to isolate r to an odd power, then take that odd root uniquely.
k not a perfect cube, e.g. r³ = 5
Leave answer in exact form r = 5^{1/3} or ∛5 rather than decimalising.

Common Misreadings

  • Writing r = ±1/2 by reflex from quadratic habits, despite the power being odd.
  • Treating 1/8 as needing a calculator instead of recognising it as (1/2)³.
  • Confusing 'cube root of 1/8' with 'square root', producing 1/(2√2) or similar.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to spot the perfect cube and giving an unnecessary decimal approximation.
  • Forgetting that for negative k the cube root is also negative (sign carries through).
  • Mishandling fractional bases: (1/8)^(1/3) = 1/2, not 1/8 · 1/3.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard within Edexcel IAL Pure (P1–P2 surd/index work and geometric sequences); no use of complex cube roots of unity, which lie outside the syllabus.
