Algebra & Functions

Clear denominators to reduce a rational equation to polynomial form

Family: Denominator Elimination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation contains one or more rational expressions equated to a polynomial or to another rational expression, e.g. (3x+2)/(x−1) = 2x. The cue is a single algebraic fraction (or a sum of fractions) blocking direct factorisation, expansion, or use of the quadratic formula. Equivalent phrasings include 'solve f(x) = k' where f is rational, or 'find the x-coordinates of intersection' between a rational curve and a line/polynomial. The domain restriction (denominator ≠ 0) is usually already known from the function's definition.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying both sides of an equation by a non-zero quantity preserves the solution set, so multiplying by the denominator removes the fraction without altering valid roots. The resulting polynomial equation is solvable by factorisation, the quadratic formula, or the factor theorem — all standard A-level techniques. Validity requires the denominator to be non-zero, which is guaranteed by the function's domain (e.g. x ≠ 1). Any root coinciding with an excluded value would be rejected as extraneous.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that one side is a rational expression and direct manipulation (collecting terms, factorising) is blocked by the fraction.
  2. Rule out cross-cancellation or partial-fraction tricks because the numerator and denominator share no obvious factor.
  3. Commit to multiplying both sides by the denominator since this single operation converts the equation to polynomial form, where standard solving methods apply.
  4. Check that the multiplier is non-zero on the relevant domain (here x ≠ 1) so no spurious roots are created and no valid roots are lost.
  5. Carefully distribute the multiplication across every term on the opposite side, then rearrange to a standard polynomial equation = 0.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the denominator D(x) and confirm D(x) ≠ 0 on the domain.
  2. Multiply both sides of the equation by D(x), distributing to every term.
  3. Expand all brackets on the resulting polynomial equation.
  4. Rearrange to standard form P(x) = 0.
  5. Solve by factorisation or quadratic/cubic formula, then discard any root excluded by the original domain.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $\dfrac{3x+2}{x-1} = 2x$, with $x \neq 1$.\\ Multiply both sides by $(x-1)$: $3x+2 = 2x(x-1) = 2x^2 - 2x$.\\ Rearrange: $2x^2 - 5x - 2 = 0$.\\ Quadratic formula: $x = \dfrac{5 \pm \sqrt{25+16}}{4} = \dfrac{5 \pm \sqrt{41}}{4}$.\\ Neither root equals $1$, so both are valid.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two rational expressions equated, e.g. (x+1)/(x-2) = (3)/(x+4)
Cross-multiply, i.e. multiply both sides by the product of the two denominators (or use a×d = b×c) and then expand.
Sum of several fractions, e.g. 1/(x-1) + 2/(x+3) = 1
Multiply every term by the LCM of all denominators, here (x-1)(x+3), distributing carefully.
Rational inequality such as (3x+2)/(x-1) > 2x
Do NOT multiply directly by (x-1) since its sign is unknown; instead bring to one side, combine into a single fraction, and analyse the sign of numerator and denominator.
Parametric or trigonometric rational equation, e.g. (sin θ)/(1+cos θ) = k
Multiply by (1+cos θ), noting θ ≠ π + 2nπ, then convert to a polynomial equation in sin θ or cos θ.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the equation as if cancelling (x−1) within the fraction first, losing terms in the numerator.
  • Believing the multiplication may introduce extraneous solutions even when the domain already excludes the problem value.
  • Confusing the procedure with that for inequalities, where multiplying by an expression of unknown sign is invalid.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to multiply EVERY term on the opposite side by the denominator (e.g. writing 3x+2 = 2x rather than 2x(x−1)).
  • Sign errors when expanding 2x(x−1) or similar products.
  • Failing to rearrange fully to P(x) = 0 before applying the quadratic formula.
  • Neglecting to check that the obtained roots do not coincide with excluded domain values.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard technique in Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1–P4) for solving rational equations arising from function problems, intersections of curves, and inverse-function work; stays well within syllabus and uses only routine quadratic/cubic solving.
