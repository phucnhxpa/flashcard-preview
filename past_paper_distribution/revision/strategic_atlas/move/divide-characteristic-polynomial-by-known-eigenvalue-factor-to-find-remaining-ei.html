Matrices

Divide characteristic polynomial by known eigenvalue factor to find remaining eigenvalues

Family: Polynomial Factor Theorem Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a 3×3 matrix's characteristic polynomial is cubic in λ and one eigenvalue is already known (given, found earlier in the question, or read off from an eigenvector relation Mv = λv). Typical phrasings: 'given that λ = a is an eigenvalue, find the other eigenvalues', or a part (a) establishes one eigenvalue and part (b) asks for the rest. Also fires when a parameter k has been pinned down using one eigenvalue and the question now asks for the full spectrum.

Motivation — Why this works

If λ = a is a root of the characteristic polynomial p(λ) = det(M − λI), then by the factor theorem (λ − a) divides p(λ) exactly. Dividing reduces a cubic to a quadratic, which is solvable by the quadratic formula or by inspection. This is far cleaner than attempting to factor a generic cubic, and trace/determinant relations (sum and product of eigenvalues) provide independent checks on the answer.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the characteristic polynomial is a cubic in λ but one root is already supplied or has just been verified.
  2. Rule out solving the cubic from scratch (no rational root theorem search needed) and rule out diagonalising directly — the eigenvectors are not yet required.
  3. Commit to factor-theorem division: (λ − a) is guaranteed to be a factor, so the quadratic quotient holds the other two eigenvalues.
  4. Bridge to operation: write out det(M − λI) explicitly with any parameters substituted, expand to a cubic in λ, then perform long/synthetic division by (λ − a).
  5. Inside the move, decide whether to solve the resulting quadratic by formula, factorisation, or by using trace = sum of eigenvalues and det = product of eigenvalues as a shortcut/check.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Substitute any known parameter values into M, then form M − λI.
  2. Expand det(M − λI) to obtain a cubic p(λ) = −λ³ + c₂λ² + c₁λ + c₀ (or equivalent sign convention).
  3. Divide p(λ) by (λ − a) using polynomial long division or synthetic division.
  4. Set the resulting quadratic quotient equal to zero and solve for λ.
  5. Cross-check using trace(M) = sum of eigenvalues and det(M) = product of eigenvalues.
  6. State the two remaining eigenvalues.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose $\det(M - \lambda I) = -\lambda^3 + 11\lambda^2 - 33\lambda + 27$ and $\lambda = 3$ is known. Dividing by $(\lambda - 3)$ gives quotient $-\lambda^2 + 8\lambda - 9$. Solving $\lambda^2 - 8\lambda + 9 = 0$ yields $\lambda = 4 \pm \sqrt{7}$. Check: $3 + (4+\sqrt{7}) + (4-\sqrt{7}) = 11 = \operatorname{tr}(M)$. ✓

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two eigenvalues already known on a 3×3 matrix
Skip division entirely; the third eigenvalue is trace(M) minus the sum of the known two.
2×2 matrix with one eigenvalue known
Characteristic polynomial is quadratic; the other eigenvalue is trace − known, or det / known.
Eigenvalue identified via a given eigenvector Mv = λv
First compute λ from the eigenvector relation, then proceed with factor-theorem division as usual.
Repeated eigenvalue suspected
After dividing once by (λ − a), test whether (λ − a) divides the quadratic again; if so, a is a repeated root.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming the known eigenvalue must be repeated and writing (λ − a)² as a factor without justification.
  • Treating the known eigenvalue as the only eigenvalue and stopping after verification.
  • Confusing the characteristic polynomial det(M − λI) with det(λI − M), causing sign errors in the cubic before division.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when forming M − λI: every diagonal entry must have λ subtracted, not just one.
  • Arithmetic slips during polynomial long division, especially with surd or fractional coefficients introduced by a parameter k.
  • Forgetting to substitute the determined value of k before expanding the determinant.
  • Misapplying synthetic division by using the coefficient of (λ − a) rather than the root a itself.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure: 3×3 eigenvalue problems, characteristic polynomials, and the factor theorem are all standard FP content. Trace and determinant identities for eigenvalues are explicitly named in the specification.
