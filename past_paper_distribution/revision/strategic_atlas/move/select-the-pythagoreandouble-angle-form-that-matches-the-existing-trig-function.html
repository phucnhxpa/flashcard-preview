Trigonometry

Select the Pythagorean/double-angle form that matches the existing trig function

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

The equation contains TWO different trig expressions that cannot be combined directly — typically one squared/double-angle term (cot²x, tan²x, cos 2x, sin 2x) and one linear term in a different function (csc x, sec x, sin x, cos x). The visual cue is a mixed equation where standard algebra is blocked by having two distinct trig 'variables'. Equivalently: 'I see cos 2x and sin x', or 'I see cot²x and csc x' — a pairing where exactly one Pythagorean or double-angle identity will collapse the two into one. The aim is to convert the equation to a polynomial in a single trig function.

Motivation — Why this works

Pythagorean identities (1 + cot²x = csc²x, 1 + tan²x = sec²x, sin²x + cos²x = 1) and double-angle identities for cos 2x come in MULTIPLE equivalent forms. Each form expresses the 'compound' term in a different single trig function. Choosing the form whose output matches the OTHER term already in the equation eliminates the mismatch in one substitution and produces a quadratic (or polynomial) in one variable — the canonical solvable form. Any other form would introduce a third function and force a second identity step.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the equation contains two distinct trig expressions — for example cos 2x alongside sin x, or cot²x alongside csc x — so direct algebraic manipulation is blocked.
  2. Recall the family of identities that can rewrite the 'compound' term: cos 2x has three forms (cos²x − sin²x, 2cos²x − 1, 1 − 2sin²x); cot²x = csc²x − 1; tan²x = sec²x − 1.
  3. Rule out converting everything to sin/cos from scratch — it works but produces a rational equation that needs clearing of denominators and is error-prone. Rule out the form that would introduce a third trig function (e.g. cos²x − sin²x for cos 2x), because it forces an additional Pythagorean step.
  4. Commit to the form whose right-hand side is expressed in the SAME function as the other term in the equation (sin if the other term is sin x, cos if the other term is cos x, csc if the other term is csc x).
  5. Substitute, distribute carefully through any coefficient, and rearrange into standard quadratic form aX² + bX + c = 0 where X is the single remaining trig function — ready for factoring or the quadratic formula.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two functions present: the 'compound' term (e.g. cos 2x, cot²x) and the 'simple' term (e.g. sin x, csc x).
  2. Pick the identity form whose output is in the SAME function as the simple term (e.g. cos 2x = 1 − 2sin²x when sin x is present; cot²x = csc²x − 1 when csc x is present).
  3. Substitute into the equation, distributing any leading coefficient through the parentheses (e.g. 2(2cos²x − 1) = 4cos²x − 2, not 4cos²x − 1).
  4. Collect all terms on one side and write as a quadratic in the single trig function: aX² + bX + c = 0, where X is sin x, cos x, csc x, sec x, etc.
  5. Proceed to factor or apply the quadratic formula, then solve the resulting trig equations on the given interval.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $2\cos 2x = 7\cos x$. Since the other term is $\cos x$, choose $\cos 2x = 2\cos^2 x - 1$. Substitute: $2(2\cos^2 x - 1) = 7\cos x \Rightarrow 4\cos^2 x - 2 = 7\cos x \Rightarrow 4\cos^2 x - 7\cos x - 2 = 0$. Factor: $(4\cos x + 1)(\cos x - 2) = 0$, giving $\cos x = -1/4$ (since $\cos x = 2$ is rejected).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation mixes cos 2θ with sin θ
Use cos 2θ = 1 − 2sin²θ to land in sin θ only.
Equation mixes cos 2θ with cos θ
Use cos 2θ = 2cos²θ − 1 to land in cos θ only.
Equation mixes cot²x with csc x
Use cot²x = csc²x − 1 to produce a quadratic in csc x.
Equation mixes tan²x with sec x
Use tan²x = sec²x − 1 to produce a quadratic in sec x.
Equation mixes sin²x with cos x
Use sin²x = 1 − cos²x to produce a quadratic in cos x.
Equation mixes tan θ with cos 2θ
Use cos 2θ = (1 − tan²θ)/(1 + tan²θ) to land in tan θ only.

Common Misreadings

  • Choosing cos 2x = cos²x − sin²x and then trying to handle a two-function equation, instead of going directly to the single-function form.
  • Converting every trig function to sin/cos from scratch, leading to a rational equation that needs denominators cleared — correct but much messier.
  • Confusing 1 + cot²x = csc²x with cot²x − 1 = csc²x and getting a sign error in the substitution.
  • Confusing the double-angle formulas for sin and cos (using 2 sin x cos x where 2cos²x − 1 was needed).

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors in the identity itself: writing cot²x = csc²x + 1 or cos 2x = 2sin²x − 1 (missing the leading 1).
  • Failing to distribute a leading coefficient through the parenthesised identity, e.g. writing 2(2cos²x − 1) = 4cos²x − 1 instead of 4cos²x − 2.
  • Forgetting to rearrange to standard quadratic form before factoring — leaves terms on both sides and invites algebra slips.
  • After solving the quadratic for the trig function, forgetting to check that the value lies in [−1, 1] for sin/cos, or |X| ≥ 1 for csc/sec, before solving for x.

A-level Scope Notes

All identities used (Pythagorean and double-angle) are listed in the Edexcel IAL Mathematics formula booklet under 'Trigonometric identities' (P3). Stay with real-valued solutions on the stated interval; do not invoke complex-exponential derivations of the identities, which are out of scope here.
