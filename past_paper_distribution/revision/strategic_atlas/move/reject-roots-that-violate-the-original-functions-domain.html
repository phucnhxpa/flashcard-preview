Algebra & Functions

Reject roots that violate the original function's domain

Family: Domain Validity Check Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires after solving a polynomial (often quadratic) obtained by clearing fractions, multiplying by a power of $x$, or rationalising, when the original expression contains $\sqrt{x}$, $\ln x$, $1/x$, or another domain-restricted feature. Cues include: the original $f(x)$ has $\sqrt{x}$ in numerator or denominator; the manipulation multiplied through by $x^{1/2}$, $x^{3/2}$, or similar; the resulting quadratic yields one positive and one negative root. Also fires when the question asks for stationary points of a function whose natural domain is $x>0$ or $x\ge 0$.

Motivation — Why this works

Algebraic manipulations such as multiplying both sides by $x^{3/2}$ or squaring are not always reversible: they may produce extraneous solutions that do not satisfy the original equation. The original function's domain (here $x>0$, since $\sqrt{x}$ in a denominator demands strictly positive $x$) is the ultimate arbiter of validity. A value outside the domain cannot be a stationary point of $f$ because $f$ is not even defined there. Mark schemes therefore require an explicit rejection with a stated reason.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that the quadratic produced two roots, one positive ($x=5/3$) and one negative ($x=-5$).
  2. Rules out $x=-5$ on the grounds that the original $f(x)$ contains $\sqrt{x}$ in a denominator, so $f$ is undefined for $x\le 0$.
  3. Commits to the domain $x>0$ as the binding constraint, because $\sqrt{x}$ requires $x\ge 0$ and the denominator forbids $x=0$.
  4. Bridges to the concrete step: state the domain, then discard the offending root with a one-line justification.
  5. Decides tactically to phrase the rejection explicitly (e.g. 'reject $x=-5$ since $\sqrt{x}$ is undefined for $x<0$') to secure the mark.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Solve the derived polynomial fully (e.g. $(3x-5)(x+5)=0$ giving $x=5/3$ or $x=-5$).
  2. Identify the domain of the original function: locate any $\sqrt{\cdot}$, $\ln(\cdot)$, or denominator that restricts $x$.
  3. State the restriction explicitly (here $x>0$).
  4. Compare each root to the restriction and reject those that fail, citing the reason.
  5. Report only the surviving root(s) as the answer.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $f(x)=\dfrac{x^2-25}{\sqrt{x}}$, setting $f'(x)=0$ and clearing $2x^{3/2}$ leads to $3x^2+10x-25=0$, i.e. $(3x-5)(x+5)=0$. So $x=\tfrac{5}{3}$ or $x=-5$. Since $\sqrt{x}$ in the denominator requires $x>0$, reject $x=-5$. Hence the only stationary point is at $x=\tfrac{5}{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Logarithmic equation $\ln(x-2)+\ln(x+1)=\ln 4$ leading to a quadratic
Reject any root with $x\le 2$ since $\ln(x-2)$ requires $x>2$.
Equation solved by squaring both sides, e.g. $\sqrt{x+3}=x-3$
Substitute each candidate back into the original (unsquared) equation and reject those that fail.
Rational equation cleared by multiplying by $(x-a)$
Reject any root equal to $a$, since it would have made the original denominator zero.
Stationary points of $f(x)=x\ln x$ or $f(x)=\sqrt{x}\,g(x)$
Restrict to $x>0$ before accepting any critical point obtained algebraically.
Trigonometric equation where a substitution $u=\sin x$ gives $|u|>1$
Reject roots with $|u|>1$ as they correspond to no real $x$.

Common Misreadings

  • Accepting both roots of the quadratic without checking the original domain.
  • Stating $x\ge 0$ rather than $x>0$, missing that $x=0$ also fails because of the denominator.
  • Believing that because the algebra was performed correctly, every root must be a valid stationary point.

Pitfalls

  • Rejecting the wrong root by sign error when factoring the quadratic.
  • Failing to write a brief justification for rejection (mark schemes typically demand a reason such as 'since $\sqrt{x}$ requires $x>0$').
  • In a 'show that' or 'hence find' part, forgetting that the domain restriction still applies even though the equation was 'given'.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure content: differentiation of functions involving $\sqrt{x}$ (P2/P3), domain of $\ln$ and rational functions, and the routine expectation in mark schemes that extraneous roots be rejected with reason.
