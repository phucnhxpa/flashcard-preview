Trigger — When to use

Fires when a partial fraction decomposition has more unknown constants than distinct linear roots — typically because of a repeated factor like $(x-a)^2$ or because a quadratic factor produces two unknowns from one denominator. Root substitution kills most terms but cannot isolate every constant, so at least one must come from elsewhere. Cue phrases: 'express in partial fractions' with $\frac{A}{x-a} + \frac{B}{(x-a)^2} + \frac{C}{x-b}$ form, or any setup where the count of unknowns exceeds the count of useful roots.

Motivation — Why this works

Substituting a root of a factor zeros all terms except those whose denominator did not contain that factor, isolating one constant per substitution. Repeated factors only contribute one usable root, leaving a deficit equal to the multiplicity minus one. Comparing coefficients of the highest power then closes the gap because leading-degree terms receive contributions from the fewest unknowns, giving the cleanest equation. Lower-power coefficients can be used as a fallback or check.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the decomposition has, say, four unknowns but only two distinct linear roots available — substitution alone cannot finish the job.
  2. Rule out solving a full 4×4 simultaneous system: it works but is slow and error-prone compared to a hybrid approach.
  3. Commit to: extract every constant possible by root substitution first, then close the remaining gap by coefficient comparison.
  4. Substitute each root in turn into the cleared identity; record the constants this pins down immediately.
  5. For the leftover constants, compare the highest-power coefficient first (fewest contributors), then the next highest if needed; back-substitute previously found values to solve cleanly.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Multiply through to clear denominators, obtaining a polynomial identity in the variable.
  2. Substitute each root of a denominator factor in turn to isolate and solve for one constant per substitution.
  3. Count remaining unknowns; identify which powers of the variable will give the simplest equations (start with the highest).
  4. Expand only the terms needed to read off that coefficient on each side, then equate.
  5. Substitute the already-known constants into that equation to solve for a remaining unknown; repeat with the next coefficient if more remain.
  6. Verify by checking one further coefficient or by substituting a convenient test value.

Worked Micro-Example

Decompose $\frac{1}{(u-1)^2(1-u) + \ldots}$ via $\frac{A}{1-u} + \frac{B}{(1-u)^2} + \frac{C u^2 + D}{\ldots}$. After clearing denominators, set $u=0$ to get $C=1$, and $u=1$ to get $D=1$. Two unknowns remain. Comparing $u^3$ coefficients: $-A + D = 0 \Rightarrow A = 1$. Comparing $u^2$ coefficients: $A - B = 0 \Rightarrow B = 1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Repeated linear factor $(x-a)^2$ with form $\frac{A}{x-a} + \frac{B}{(x-a)^2} + \frac{C}{x-b}$
Substitution at $x=a$ gives $B$; substitution at $x=b$ gives $C$; coefficient of $x^2$ (highest) gives $A$.
Irreducible quadratic factor $\frac{Ax+B}{x^2+1} + \frac{C}{x-a}$
Only $x=a$ gives a clean substitution; $A$ and $B$ must come from comparing two coefficients (e.g. $x^2$ and constant).
Three distinct simple linear factors
Root substitution alone suffices — no coefficient comparison needed.
Cover-up rule shortcut
Equivalent to root substitution for simple factors and for the highest-power term over a repeated factor; still requires coefficient comparison for the lower-power constants of a repeated factor.

Common Misreadings

  • Believing root substitution alone must determine every constant, then becoming stuck when one root cannot be used twice.
  • Choosing the constant term for comparison first when the leading coefficient would have given a one-variable equation.
  • Treating the numerator over a repeated factor as a single constant rather than splitting it into $\frac{A}{x-a} + \frac{B}{(x-a)^2}$.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when expanding $(x-a)^2$ or $(1-u)$-type factors during coefficient extraction.
  • Evaluating $(x-3)^2$ at $x = \tfrac12$ as $-\tfrac{25}{4}$ instead of $\tfrac{25}{4}$ — squared brackets are non-negative.
  • Forgetting to substitute already-found constants before solving the next coefficient equation.
  • Mismatching which side of the identity has been fully expanded when reading off coefficients.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P4 / Pure partial fractions technique; applies to proper rational functions with linear factors (possibly repeated) and at most one irreducible quadratic factor.
