Integration

Pick the polynomial factor as u in IBP to drive a reduction formula

Family: Ibp Factor Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integrand is a product of a polynomial power x^n (or (ln x)^n) and a function that is easy to integrate repeatedly, such as sin x, cos x, or e^x. The question explicitly asks for a reduction formula linking I_n to I_{n-1} or I_{n-2}, or a recursive expression for a family of integrals indexed by n. Common phrasings include 'show that I_n = ...', 'hence find I_4', or 'derive a reduction formula'.

Motivation — Why this works

Integration by parts trades a derivative on one factor for an antiderivative on the other. If we differentiate x^n we drop the index by 1; if we integrate sin x, cos x, or e^x we stay at the same level of complexity. Repeating this asymmetry is exactly the mechanism that produces a reduction formula: the polynomial side erodes while the trig/exponential side cycles. The reverse choice would inflate the polynomial power and never terminate.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand is a product of two distinct families — an algebraic factor x^n and a transcendental factor (sin x, cos x, or e^x) — and that the index n suggests a recursion is sought.
  2. Rule out u = sin x (or u = e^x): integrating x^n gives x^{n+1}/(n+1), which raises the index and moves away from a reduction.
  3. Commit via the LIATE heuristic: u = x^n (Algebraic) is differentiated, dv = sin x dx (Trig) is integrated, so the polynomial index falls by 1 per IBP.
  4. Bridge to operation: compute du = n x^{n-1} dx and v = -cos x, then write I_n = [uv] - ∫ v du with the correct limits.
  5. Tactical choice: for sin x / cos x integrands, expect to apply IBP twice to reach I_{n-2}; for e^x integrands, one application suffices to reach I_{n-1}.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify I_n = ∫_a^b x^n f(x) dx with f ∈ {sin x, cos x, e^x}.
  2. Set u = x^n and dv = f(x) dx.
  3. Compute du = n x^{n-1} dx and v = ∫ f(x) dx.
  4. Apply IBP: I_n = [u v]_a^b − ∫_a^b v du.
  5. If the remaining integral still contains a trig factor, apply IBP a second time (same choice u = x^{n-1}) to express the result in terms of I_{n-2}.
  6. Simplify boundary terms using the given limits to obtain the closed reduction formula.

Worked Micro-Example

Let I_n = \int_0^{\pi/2} x^n \sin x \, dx. Take u = x^n, dv = \sin x\, dx, so du = n x^{n-1} dx, v = -\cos x. Then I_n = [-x^n \cos x]_0^{\pi/2} + n\int_0^{\pi/2} x^{n-1} \cos x\, dx = 0 + n\int_0^{\pi/2} x^{n-1} \cos x\, dx. A second IBP with u = x^{n-1}, dv = \cos x\, dx gives I_n = n(\pi/2)^{n-1} - n(n-1) I_{n-2}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

I_n = ∫ x^n e^x dx
u = x^n, dv = e^x dx gives v = e^x and the one-step recursion I_n = x^n e^x − n I_{n-1}.
I_n = ∫ x^n cos x dx
Same choice u = x^n, dv = cos x dx (so v = sin x); two IBPs produce a link between I_n and I_{n-2}.
I_n = ∫ (ln x)^n dx
Now (ln x)^n is the 'difficult' factor — let u = (ln x)^n, dv = dx, so v = x and du = n(ln x)^{n-1}/x dx, giving I_n = x(ln x)^n − n I_{n-1}.
I_n = ∫_0^1 x^n (1−x)^m dx (Beta-type)
Differentiate the higher-power algebraic factor; same principle of eroding one index while the other antiderivative stays polynomial.

Common Misreadings

  • Choosing u = sin x because trig 'looks harder' — this raises the polynomial power and prevents reduction.
  • Treating the product as needing a substitution rather than IBP, e.g. trying u = x^n as a substitution variable.
  • Stopping after one IBP for x^n sin x and concluding I_n is in terms of I_{n-1}, missing that a second IBP is required to return to a sin x integrand and obtain I_{n-2}.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors from v = −cos x, especially when combined with the −∫ v du in the IBP formula.
  • Forgetting to evaluate the boundary term [u v]_a^b at both limits, particularly that x^n vanishes at 0 only when n ≥ 1.
  • Algebra slips with the factor n(n−1) when chaining two IBPs.

A-level Scope Notes

Reduction formulae of this type are standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure content; both sin/cos and e^x recursions, and the (ln x)^n variant, appear in past papers and remain within syllabus.
