Complex Numbers

Compute modulus as Euclidean distance from origin

Family: Modulus Computation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question presents a complex number in Cartesian form $z = a + bi$ and asks for $|z|$, or asks for the distance from the origin to a point on the Argand diagram. Phrasings include 'find the modulus', 'find $|z|$', 'find the distance of $z$ from the origin', or 'express $z$ in modulus-argument form' (where $r = |z|$ is required). Also fires as a sub-step when computing arguments, dividing complex numbers, or converting to polar/exponential form.

Motivation — Why this works

By definition $|z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$, which is the Euclidean distance from $(a,b)$ to the origin on the Argand diagram, by Pythagoras. This is a direct, formulaic computation with no algebraic manipulation required once $a$ and $b$ are correctly identified. Exact surd form is preferred unless the question explicitly requests a decimal approximation for the modulus.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Student notices $z$ is given in Cartesian form $a + bi$ with both $a$ and $b$ numerical, and the question asks for $|z|$.
  2. Student rules out the need for any rationalisation, conjugate multiplication, or polar conversion — the modulus formula applies directly.
  3. Student commits to $|z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$ because this is the definition and no shortcut (like a known polar form or product rule) is available or simpler.
  4. Student reads off $a$ as the real part and $b$ as the coefficient of $i$ (not including $i$ itself), then squares each.
  5. Student decides whether to leave the answer as an exact surd or convert to a decimal, based on the question's instruction; defaults to exact surd form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the real part $a$ and the imaginary part $b$ (the coefficient of $i$, without the $i$).
  2. Square each: compute $a^2$ and $b^2$.
  3. Sum: $a^2 + b^2$.
  4. Take the positive square root: $|z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$.
  5. Simplify the surd if possible (e.g. $\sqrt{50} = 5\sqrt{2}$); otherwise leave in exact form.

Worked Micro-Example

Find $|z_1|$ where $z_1 = 3 + 2i$.\\ Real part $a = 3$, imaginary part $b = 2$.\\ $|z_1| = \sqrt{3^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{9 + 4} = \sqrt{13}$.\\ Since $13$ has no square factors, leave as $\sqrt{13}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$z$ already given in polar/modulus-argument form $r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)$ or exponential form $re^{i\theta}$ with $r > 0$.
No computation needed: $|z| = r$ directly.
Modulus of a product or quotient, e.g. $|z_1 z_2|$ or $\left|\frac{z_1}{z_2}\right|$.
Use $|z_1 z_2| = |z_1||z_2|$ and $\left|\frac{z_1}{z_2}\right| = \frac{|z_1|}{|z_2|}$ — often faster than expanding first.
Modulus of a power $z^n$.
Use $|z^n| = |z|^n$ rather than expanding $z^n$ in Cartesian form.
Distance between two complex numbers $|z_1 - z_2|$ (e.g. on a locus question).
Subtract first to get a single complex number, then apply $\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ to its real and imaginary parts.
$z$ given as a fraction such as $z = \frac{p+qi}{r+si}$.
Either rationalise first (multiply by conjugate) then apply the formula, or use $|z| = \frac{\sqrt{p^2+q^2}}{\sqrt{r^2+s^2}}$ for speed.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $|z|$ as $|a| + |b|$ (taking absolute values of parts and adding).
  • Confusing modulus with argument and computing $\arctan(b/a)$ instead.
  • Including the $i$ as part of $b$, so squaring $bi$ rather than $b$.
  • Assuming the answer must be a decimal and prematurely converting $\sqrt{13} \approx 3.61$ when an exact value is expected.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to square before adding: computing $\sqrt{a+b}$ instead of $\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ (e.g. $\sqrt{3+2} = \sqrt{5}$ rather than $\sqrt{13}$).
  • Squaring $bi$ as $b^2 i^2 = -b^2$ and getting $\sqrt{a^2 - b^2}$ instead of $\sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$.
  • Sign errors when $a$ or $b$ is negative — remember squaring removes the sign, so $|{-3+2i}| = \sqrt{13}$, same as $|3+2i|$.
  • Failing to simplify a surd (e.g. leaving $\sqrt{50}$ instead of $5\sqrt{2}$) and losing presentation marks.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope (FP1/FP2): the definition $|z| = \sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ and the multiplicative properties $|z_1 z_2| = |z_1||z_2|$ and $|z^n| = |z|^n$ are standard.
