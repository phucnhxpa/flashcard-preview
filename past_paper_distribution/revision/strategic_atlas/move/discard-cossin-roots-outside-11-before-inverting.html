Trigonometry

Discard cos/sin roots outside [−1,1] before inverting

Family: Range Filtering Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires after a substitution u = cos x or u = sin x reduces a trig equation to a quadratic (or higher polynomial) in u, and the algebraic roots are computed. At least one root has magnitude greater than 1, e.g. u ≈ −1.18 or u = (−1−√37)/6. Also fires whenever surd-form roots need a quick numerical bound check against ±1 before any arccos/arcsin step.

Motivation — Why this works

Cosine and sine map every real angle into [−1, 1], so any value of u = cos x or u = sin x outside this interval corresponds to no real angle and is extraneous. Filtering early avoids a calculator 'Math Error' from arccos/arcsin of an out-of-range input. Once the valid root(s) remain, the standard CAST/quadrant analysis determines how many solutions lie in the prescribed domain.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the quadratic in cos x has produced two algebraic roots, one of which looks numerically below −1 or above 1.
  2. Rule out the out-of-range root immediately on the grounds that cos x ∈ [−1, 1] — no angle exists.
  3. Commit to the surviving root and read off its sign to anticipate which quadrants supply solutions.
  4. Bridge: take arccos (or arcsin) of the valid value to get the principal angle, then generate any further solutions inside the stated domain.
  5. Tactically keep the surd form until the final arccos to avoid rounding drift, and confirm each candidate angle lies in the given interval.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute both roots of the quadratic in u = cos x (or sin x), e.g. u = (−1 ± √37)/6.
  2. Numerically bound each root: reject any u with |u| > 1.
  3. For each surviving root, take x = arccos(u) (or arcsin(u)) to find the principal value.
  4. Apply symmetry: cos x = k also gives x = 360° − arccos(k); sin x = k also gives x = 180° − arcsin(k).
  5. Keep only those x lying in the stated domain; report to required precision.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $6\cos^2 x + \cos x - 3 = 0$ for $0° \le x < 180°$. Setting $u = \cos x$ gives $u = \tfrac{-1 \pm \sqrt{37}}{6}$, i.e. $u \approx 0.8471$ or $u \approx -1.180$. Reject $u \approx -1.180$ since $|u|>1$. Then $x = \arccos(0.8471) \approx 32.1°$, which lies in $[0°,180°)$, and the symmetric solution $327.9°$ is outside the range, so $x \approx 32.1°$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic in sin x
Reject roots with |u|>1; for a valid root k, principal value is arcsin(k) and the second solution in [0°,360°) is 180° − arcsin(k).
Quadratic in tan x
No [−1,1] restriction — every real value of tan x is attainable; instead watch for excluded angles where tan is undefined (e.g. 90°, 270°).
Quadratic in sec x or cosec x
Convert to cos x or sin x first (or apply |sec x| ≥ 1, |cosec x| ≥ 1) and reject roots that violate these bounds.
Equation in sin(2x) or cos(x/2)
Same [−1,1] filter on the inner trig value, but adjust the working domain (e.g. 0 ≤ 2x < 720°) before inverting.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the quadratic roots as final x-values rather than as values of cos x or sin x.
  • Accepting both roots and attempting arccos(−1.18), producing a calculator 'Math Error'.
  • Confusing the [−1,1] range of cos/sin with the [0°,180°] range of arccos and rejecting valid roots.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the second-quadrant (or 360° − θ) solution when the valid root still produces another angle inside the given domain.
  • Rounding the surd root before applying arccos, which can shift the final angle by 0.1° or more.
  • Missing that a root exactly equal to ±1 is valid (gives x = 0°, 90°, or 180°) and should not be discarded.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P2/P3) trigonometric equation solving; uses only the standard ranges of sin, cos, tan and the quadratic formula.
