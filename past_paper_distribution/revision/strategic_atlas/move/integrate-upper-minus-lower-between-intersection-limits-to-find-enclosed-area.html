Integration

Integrate upper-minus-lower between intersection limits to find enclosed area

Family: Area Between Curves Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question asks for the area of a region R enclosed between two curves (or a curve and a line, or a curve and a horizontal/vertical boundary) on a given interval [a, b]. The figure or context makes clear which boundary lies above the other throughout the interval, and both boundaries are easily expressible as y = f(x). The limits a and b are either intersection x-values (often computed in an earlier part) or one is a coordinate axis. Equivalent phrasings: 'find the shaded area', 'find the exact area of R', 'the region bounded by C₁ and C₂ between A and B'.

Motivation — Why this works

The area between two curves from x = a to x = b equals ∫ₐᵇ (y_upper − y_lower) dx. Combining the two boundaries into a single integrand is cleaner and less error-prone than computing ∫y_upper dx and ∫y_lower dx separately and subtracting at the end. Because (upper − lower) is non-negative on the interval, the definite integral returns a positive area directly, with no need for absolute values. This is the path of least resistance whenever both curves are simple functions of x.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that R is enclosed between two boundaries on a fixed x-interval, so the area-between-curves template applies.
  2. Rule out integrating with respect to y (which would require inverting y = f(x), often introducing square roots) because the boundaries are already given as explicit y = f(x).
  3. Rule out the 'rectangle minus integral' or 'two separate integrals then subtract' approach — combining into ∫(upper − lower) dx is cleaner and reduces sign and arithmetic mistakes.
  4. Identify which curve is on top on the interval by reading the figure, by comparing y-values at a convenient interior test point, or by using the inequality given in the problem (e.g. 0 < m < 1).
  5. Read off the limits: either the intersection x-values from a previous part, or the boundary axes (e.g. x = 0).
  6. Write Area = ∫ₐᵇ [y_upper − y_lower] dx, simplify the integrand algebraically before integrating, then evaluate using the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Determine y_upper and y_lower on [a, b] (test a point or read the diagram).
  2. Form the integrand: (y_upper − y_lower) and simplify (combine like terms, factor where possible).
  3. Set up the definite integral: Area = ∫ₐᵇ [y_upper(x) − y_lower(x)] dx.
  4. Find an antiderivative F(x) of the simplified integrand.
  5. Evaluate F(b) − F(a), using log laws (ln a − ln b = ln(a/b)) where applicable to keep the answer exact.
  6. Factor or simplify the result to match any required exact form.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the area between $y = 17$ and $y = 2x^2 + 7$ from $x = 0$ to $x = \sqrt{5}$. Set up: $A = \int_0^{\sqrt 5} [17 - (2x^2 + 7)]\,dx = \int_0^{\sqrt 5}(10 - 2x^2)\,dx$. Antiderivative: $\left[10x - \tfrac{2x^3}{3}\right]_0^{\sqrt 5} = 10\sqrt 5 - \tfrac{2(5\sqrt 5)}{3} = \tfrac{20\sqrt 5}{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Region naturally described as bounded left/right by curves x = g(y)
Switch to ∫_c^d [x_right − x_left] dy — same upper-minus-lower logic rotated 90°.
One of the two integrals is only available as a numerical (e.g. trapezium rule) estimate
Compute the other integral exactly, then take Area = ∫(exact upper) − (numerical lower); the subtraction principle is unchanged.
The two curves cross within the interval (upper and lower swap)
Split the integral at each intersection and integrate (upper − lower) separately on each subinterval, then add.
One boundary is the x-axis
The formula reduces to ∫ y dx, a special case where y_lower = 0.
Answer required in a specific factored exact form (e.g. (1−m)³/6)
After evaluating, factor out the common power before combining the fractional coefficients.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the problem as a single ∫y dx under one curve, ignoring that the region is bounded by a second curve.
  • Missing a third boundary (such as the y-axis or a vertical line) and instead solving for two intersections of the two curves.
  • Not recognising that an earlier part (e.g. trapezium estimate, or intersection x-values) feeds directly into this calculation.

Pitfalls

  • Subtracting in the wrong order (lower − upper) and obtaining a negative area; always verify which function is greater on the interval.
  • Arithmetic errors evaluating F(b) − F(a) with logarithms — use ln a − ln b = ln(a/b) rather than computing each ln separately to reduce rounding error.
  • Forgetting to simplify the integrand before integrating, leading to messy term-by-term integration.
  • Mixing up which curve was assigned to which integral when an earlier part supplied a numerical estimate.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 (area between curves, definite integration). No formula-booklet entry is needed beyond standard antiderivatives. Avoid invoking signed-area arguments or improper integrals — the technique here assumes a finite region with a clear upper/lower designation on a closed interval.
