Differentiation

Select the product rule to differentiate a product of two non-trivial functions

Family: Product Rule Selection Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when y is written explicitly as the product of two distinct, non-constant functions of x — typically a polynomial times an exponential, trigonometric, or logarithmic factor (e.g. y = (2x+5)e^{3x}, y = x²cos(x/2), y = x^n ln x). The structural cue is two genuinely x-dependent factors juxtaposed by multiplication, with no algebraic simplification available to merge them into a single elementary form. Equivalent phrasings: 'y is uv where both u and v depend on x', 'a polynomial multiplies a transcendental', 'two factors, neither constant'.

Motivation — Why this works

The derivative of a product is NOT the product of derivatives; instead the product rule (uv)' = u'v + uv' correctly accounts for both factors varying simultaneously. This is the only standard technique that handles a product of unlike functions without first transforming the expression, and it is listed in the Edexcel formula booklet. Alternatives like logarithmic differentiation work but add unnecessary steps for a two-factor product, and expanding/rewriting is impossible when one factor is transcendental.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structural pattern: y is literally written as (something in x) × (something else in x), and both factors genuinely depend on x.
  2. Rule out simpler routes: can I expand or rewrite to collapse the product into a single standard form? With a polynomial × exponential or polynomial × trig, no — they belong to different function families.
  3. Rule out chain-rule-only thinking: there is no single 'outer/inner' decomposition because the multiplication is not composition. Logarithmic differentiation would work but is overkill for two factors.
  4. Commit to the product rule (uv)' = u'v + uv' as the most direct technique.
  5. Tactically assign u and v: choose u to be the algebraic factor (whose derivative is simple, e.g. polynomial → constant or lower-degree polynomial) and v to be the transcendental factor (which often retains a similar form on differentiation, aiding later factoring).
  6. Anticipate that differentiating v will likely require the chain rule (e.g. e^{3x} → 3e^{3x}, cos(x/2) → −½sin(x/2)) because the argument is rarely just x.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two factors and label them: let u = (first factor), v = (second factor).
  2. Differentiate each separately: compute du/dx directly; compute dv/dx using the chain rule if v has a non-trivial inner argument.
  3. Apply the product rule: dy/dx = u·(dv/dx) + v·(du/dx).
  4. Substitute the expressions for u, v, du/dx, dv/dx and simplify, factoring out any common term (often the exponential or trig factor) to tidy the result.

Worked Micro-Example

Differentiate $y = (2x+5)e^{3x}$.\n Let $u = 2x+5 \Rightarrow u' = 2$; let $v = e^{3x} \Rightarrow v' = 3e^{3x}$ (chain rule).\n Then $\frac{dy}{dx} = (2x+5)(3e^{3x}) + e^{3x}(2) = e^{3x}(6x+17).$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Polynomial × trigonometric, e.g. y = x²cos(x/2)
Same rule; chain rule inside dv/dx gives the trig argument's derivative as a multiplier (−½sin(x/2)).
Three factors y = uvw
Apply iteratively or use d(uvw)/dx = u'vw + uv'w + uvw'.
Polynomial × logarithm, e.g. y = x^n ln x
Same structure with u = x^n, v = ln x; here dv/dx = 1/x with no chain rule needed.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating y = (2x+5)e^{3x} as a pure chain-rule problem and trying to differentiate an 'outside' and 'inside' rather than recognising multiplicative structure.
  • Believing (uv)' = u'v' and multiplying the two derivatives directly.
  • Reaching for logarithmic differentiation when the product rule is more efficient.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the chain-rule factor when differentiating v (e.g. writing (e^{3x})' = e^{3x} instead of 3e^{3x}, or (cos(x/2))' = −sin(x/2) instead of −½sin(x/2)).
  • Sign error: the derivative of cos is −sin, not +sin.
  • Mixing the product rule structure: writing u'v' instead of u'v + uv'.
  • Failing to factor the final answer, leaving it in an unsimplified form that obscures later use (e.g. for finding stationary points).

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 scope; the product rule (uv)' = u(dv/dx) + v(du/dx) is given in the formula booklet alongside the chain rule. Avoid invoking generalised Leibniz rules for higher derivatives — those are out of scope.
