Coordinate Geometry

Decompose composite circular region into sectors and triangles to compute area

Family: Area Decomposition Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

The region in question (badge, lawn, sign, platform) is bounded by a mixture of circular arc(s) and straight line segments, so no single formula (sector, triangle, rectangle) covers it. Visual cues: a boundary description that switches between 'arc from A to B' and 'line from B to C'; a centre O with radii drawn to boundary points; explicit mention of a 'major' vs 'minor' arc; or a polygon vertex sitting outside/inside a circle. Whenever you see 'find the area of region [letters]' and the letters trace both an arc and chords/lines, this move fires.

Motivation — Why this works

Standard formulas exist for sectors (½r²θ), triangles (½ab sin C or ½ × base × height), kites, and rectangles, but not for arbitrary composite shapes. By drawing radii to the points where arc meets straight edge, the region splits cleanly along these radii into a sector plus one or more triangles (or, equivalently, a larger sector minus a triangle). Each sub-region then matches a booklet formula, and the total area is obtained by summing (when sub-regions tile the region) or subtracting (when one sub-region is an 'overshoot'). This is far cleaner than integration and is the standard technique for circular-geometry area questions in IAL Pure.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the region's boundary alternates between circular arc(s) and straight segments — so it is neither a pure sector nor a pure polygon.
  2. Rule out integration (overkill for a sector + polygon shape) and rule out treating the whole shape as a single sector with an enlarged angle (this fails whenever the outer vertices are not equidistant from the centre).
  3. Identify the centre O of the circular arc and draw the radii from O to the arc's endpoints; these radii are the natural 'cut lines' because they are simultaneously sides of a sector and sides of a triangle.
  4. Decide whether the decomposition is additive (sector + triangle(s), if the sub-regions tile the shape) or subtractive (major sector − triangle, if a triangle pokes out of the desired region). Sketch and shade to confirm.
  5. Decide which angle to use: minor angle θ or reflex/major angle 2π − θ. The choice depends on whether the arc traversed is the short or long way round.
  6. Exploit any symmetry/congruence (e.g. two congruent flanking triangles ⇒ compute one and double) to halve the work.
  7. Write Area = Σ(sub-areas) with signs, then evaluate each piece using ½r²θ for sectors and ½ab sin C (or ½ × base × height) for triangles.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify centre O and radius r of the circular boundary; mark the points A, B, … where arc meets straight edges.
  2. Draw radii OA, OB, … to partition the region into a sector and triangle(s).
  3. Determine the sector angle: θ if the minor arc bounds the region, 2π − θ if the major arc does.
  4. Sector area = ½ r² θ_used.
  5. For each triangle, use ½ × (side) × (side) × sin(included angle), or ½ × base × height when a right angle is present (often via Pythagoras to find a missing length).
  6. Combine: Area_total = Area_sector ± Σ Area_triangles, with sign chosen by whether triangles tile alongside or overshoot.
  7. Keep angles exact (e.g. arccos(1/3)) until the final step to avoid rounding error.

Worked Micro-Example

Region OABC has arc AB (centre O, r = 6, angle ∠AOB = 1.2 rad) and straight edges OA, OB, BC, CA, with C outside the sector and OC = 8, ∠AOC = 0.4. Then Area(sector OAB) = ½(6²)(1.2) = 21.6, Area(△OAC) = ½(6)(8) sin 0.4 ≈ 9.35, so total ≈ 21.6 + 2(9.35) = 40.3 (if a congruent △OBC also flanks the sector).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Region bounded by a major arc plus chords/lines that cut inside the sector
Use Area = ½r²(2π − θ) − Area(triangle inside sector but outside region); a subtractive decomposition.
Two congruent triangles flank a sector (badge-type problem)
Compute one triangle and double: Area = sector + 2·Area(△); halves the trig work.
Kite plus sector (sign-type problem with two equal tangent-like sides)
The two radii are also two sides of the kite; Area(kite) = 2·½·r·(side)·sin(included angle), then add ½r²(2π − θ) for the major sector.
Circular segment (chord cuts off a piece of disc)
Same logic in miniature: Segment area = ½r²(θ − sin θ) = sector − triangle.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the whole composite shape as a single sector with an enlarged angle, ignoring that outer vertices may not lie on the circle.
  • Computing only the sector and forgetting the flanking triangle(s), or vice versa.
  • Subtracting the wrong triangle (e.g. △AOC instead of △AOD) when applying a 'major sector minus triangle' decomposition.

Pitfalls

  • Using the minor angle θ when the region is bounded by the major arc (or vice versa), which gives the wrong sector area.
  • Premature rounding of θ (e.g. using 1.231 instead of arccos(1/3)) propagates error past the required tolerance.
  • Confusing a side length found by Pythagoras (e.g. AD) with a radius and substituting it into ½r²θ.
  • Forgetting to double a congruent triangle when symmetry was the whole reason the decomposition was tractable.

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 'Radian measure' chapter; uses booklet entries Area of sector = ½r²θ, Arc length = rθ, and Area of triangle = ½ab sin C. Stays inside standard circle/sector/triangle geometry — no integration or polar coordinates needed.
