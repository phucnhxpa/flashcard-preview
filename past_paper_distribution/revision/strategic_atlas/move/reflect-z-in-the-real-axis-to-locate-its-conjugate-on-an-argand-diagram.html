Complex Numbers

Reflect z in the real axis to locate its conjugate on an Argand diagram

Family: Conjugate Geometry Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks you to plot, sketch, or mark both z and its conjugate z* (sometimes written z̄) on the same Argand diagram. Also triggers when a question asks you to describe the geometric relationship between z and z*, or when a transformation between two given points is recognisable as 'flip the sign of the imaginary part'. Phrasings include 'show z and z* on a single diagram', 'mark the points representing z and its conjugate', or 'state the transformation that maps z to z*'.

Motivation — Why this works

If z = a + bi corresponds to the point (a, b), then z* = a − bi corresponds to (a, −b). This is precisely a reflection in the real axis (the x-axis of the Argand plane), so |z*| = |z| and arg(z*) = −arg(z). Treating conjugation geometrically rather than algebraically guarantees symmetric placement on the diagram and gives a free self-check via equal moduli.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the second point to plot is z* (or z̄), so it is geometrically tied to z, not an independent calculation.
  2. Rule out treating z* as −z (which would reflect through the origin) or as 1/z (which would invert in the unit circle); only the imaginary part flips sign.
  3. Commit to the rule 'conjugation = reflection in the real axis' because it both gives the coordinates and forces the visual symmetry the examiner expects.
  4. Bridge to operation: read off (Re z, Im z), then drop a vertical line to (Re z, −Im z) for z*.
  5. Tactical: keep the same horizontal coordinate, mirror the vertical, and confirm |z| = |z*| as a sanity check before labelling A and B.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Read off z = a + bi and identify the point (a, b).
  2. Write z* = a − bi by negating the imaginary part.
  3. Plot the second point at (a, −b), directly below/above the first across the real axis.
  4. Label both points clearly (e.g. A for z, B for z*) and check |z| = |z*| as verification.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $z = -4 + 3i$, plotted at $A(-4, 3)$ in quadrant 2. Then $z^* = -4 - 3i$, plotted at $B(-4, -3)$ in quadrant 3. The points are mirror images in the real axis, and $|z| = |z^*| = \sqrt{16+9} = 5$, confirming both lie on the circle of radius $5$ about the origin.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Reflect z in the imaginary axis
Negate the real part: the image is $-\overline{z} = -a + bi$, sending $(a,b)$ to $(-a,b)$.
Reflect z in the origin (half-turn)
Use $-z = -a - bi$, sending $(a,b)$ to $(-a,-b)$; this is the composition of reflections in both axes.
Plot $iz$ or $-iz$
These are rotations by $\pm 90^\circ$ about the origin, not reflections; swap and sign-flip coordinates accordingly.
Locus involving $z$ and $z^*$ such as $|z - z^*| = k$
Recognise $z - z^* = 2i\,\mathrm{Im}(z)$, so the condition becomes a horizontal-strip condition on $\mathrm{Im}(z)$.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing $z^*$ (negate Im only) with $-z$ (negate both Re and Im); for $z=-4+3i$ this would wrongly put the image at $(4,-3)$ in quadrant 4.
  • Treating the bar in $\overline{z}$ as a modulus or as $1/z$.
  • Reflecting in the imaginary axis instead of the real axis when the point lies in quadrant 2 or 3.

Pitfalls

  • Plotting $z^*$ in the wrong quadrant: if $z$ is in Q2 then $z^*$ must be in Q3 (same sign of Re, opposite sign of Im).
  • Using a non-uniform scale on the two axes so the reflection does not look symmetric — trust the coordinates, not the visual.
  • Forgetting to label both points or to indicate the axes, which loses presentation marks even when the coordinates are correct.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP1/FP2) Argand diagram work: conjugation as reflection in the real axis, with $|z|=|z^*|$ and $\arg(z^*)=-\arg(z)$. No use of Möbius transformations or higher geometry is required.
