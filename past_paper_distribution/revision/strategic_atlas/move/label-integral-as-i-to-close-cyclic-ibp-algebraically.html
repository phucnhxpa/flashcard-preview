Integration

Label integral as I to close cyclic IBP algebraically

Family: Cyclic Ibp Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integrand is a product of an exponential and a trigonometric function, e.g. e^{ax} sin(bx) or e^{ax} cos(bx), where neither factor simplifies under repeated differentiation. Standard IBP does not terminate because exponentials reproduce themselves and sin/cos cycle with period 2. The cue is recognising that two rounds of IBP will regenerate the original integrand up to a constant multiple. This pattern also fires for related cyclic pairs such as sec^3 x or products that re-emerge after a fixed number of IBP applications.

Motivation — Why this works

Two applications of IBP on exp × trig produce a constant multiple of the original integral on the right-hand side. By naming the unknown integral I from the outset, the calculus problem is converted into a linear equation in I, which is solved by rearrangement. This bypasses an infinite regress and yields a closed-form antiderivative in two IBP steps. The underlying principle is that the differential operator acts cyclically on the function space spanned by e^{ax} sin(bx) and e^{ax} cos(bx).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand is e^{2x} sin x — a product where neither factor reduces under differentiation.
  2. Rule out substitution (no inner-function structure) and rule out terminating IBP (no polynomial factor that differentiates to zero).
  3. Commit to cyclic IBP because two rounds on exp × trig must regenerate the original integral via sin → cos → −sin.
  4. Bridge: write I = ∫ e^{2x} sin x dx so the regenerated integral can be substituted as I and solved algebraically.
  5. Tactical decision: keep the SAME choice of u and dv across both IBP steps (e.g. always differentiate the trig factor, or always differentiate the exponential) to avoid undoing the first step.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Define I = ∫ e^{2x} sin x dx.
  2. Apply IBP with u = sin x, dv = e^{2x} dx: I = ½ e^{2x} sin x − ½ ∫ e^{2x} cos x dx.
  3. Apply IBP again to ∫ e^{2x} cos x dx with u = cos x, dv = e^{2x} dx: ∫ e^{2x} cos x dx = ½ e^{2x} cos x + ½ I.
  4. Substitute: I = ½ e^{2x} sin x − ½(½ e^{2x} cos x + ½ I) = ½ e^{2x} sin x − ¼ e^{2x} cos x − ¼ I.
  5. Collect I: (5/4) I = ¼ e^{2x}(2 sin x − cos x).
  6. Solve: I = (1/5) e^{2x}(2 sin x − cos x) + C.

Worked Micro-Example

Find $\int e^{2x}\sin x\,dx$. Let $I=\int e^{2x}\sin x\,dx$. IBP gives $I=\tfrac{1}{2}e^{2x}\sin x-\tfrac{1}{2}\int e^{2x}\cos x\,dx$. A second IBP yields $\int e^{2x}\cos x\,dx=\tfrac{1}{2}e^{2x}\cos x+\tfrac{1}{2}I$. Substituting and rearranging: $\tfrac{5}{4}I=\tfrac{1}{4}e^{2x}(2\sin x-\cos x)$, so $I=\tfrac{1}{5}e^{2x}(2\sin x-\cos x)+C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

∫ e^{ax} cos(bx) dx with general constants a, b
Same labelling I = ∫ e^{ax} cos(bx) dx; two IBP steps give an equation (1 + b²/a²) I = …, leading to I = e^{ax}(a cos bx + b sin bx)/(a² + b²) + C.
∫ sec^3 x dx
Label I = ∫ sec^3 x dx; one IBP with u = sec x, dv = sec² x dx regenerates ∫ sec^3 x using sec²x = 1 + tan²x, then solve algebraically for I.
Definite version ∫_0^π e^{x} sin x dx
Carry out the same cyclic IBP symbolically to find the antiderivative, then evaluate at the limits at the end.
Polynomial × trig, e.g. ∫ x² sin x dx
Do NOT label and loop; use terminating IBP instead because the polynomial differentiates to zero in finitely many steps.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating it as a substitution problem (e.g. u = 2x) — substitution does not break the exp × trig product structure.
  • Believing IBP will terminate, and continuing to apply it indefinitely without recognising the cycle.
  • Attempting tabular/DI method without tracking that the table never terminates and signs must be handled by the cyclic equation.

Pitfalls

  • Switching the roles of u and dv between the two IBP applications — this reverses the first step and returns to the original integral with no useful equation.
  • Sign errors when differentiating cos x to −sin x in the second IBP, leading to +¼ I instead of −¼ I and an incorrect coefficient.
  • Forgetting to add the constant of integration +C after solving for I.
  • Algebra slip when collecting I-terms: failing to bring −¼ I across to give (5/4) I rather than (3/4) I.

A-level Scope Notes

Cyclic IBP for ∫ e^{ax} sin(bx) dx and ∫ e^{ax} cos(bx) dx is squarely within Edexcel IAL Further Pure integration techniques; the labelling-and-solving trick is the standard expected method.
