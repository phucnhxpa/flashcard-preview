Sequences & Series

Sum one full period symbolically to collapse into a linear equation in the parameter

Family: Parametric Cycle Sum Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a recurrence has produced the first few terms as linear expressions in an unknown parameter k, and the next part asks for a finite sum (e.g. Σ from r=1 to 4 of uᵣ = S) that spans exactly one period of the cycle. Cues include: a periodic or sign-alternating recurrence (uₙ₊₁ = a − uₙ, or similar), terms already written in the form α + βk, and a sum range matching the period length. Alternative phrasings: 'given that u₁+u₂+u₃+u₄ = ...', 'the sum of the first p terms is ...' where p equals the cycle length.

Motivation — Why this works

Because the recurrence is periodic with sign-flipping structure, summing one whole period causes the constant parts to cancel and the k-coefficients to combine into a single multiple of k. This converts an otherwise awkward problem (guess-and-check, or solving a system) into a one-line linear equation ak = S. The cancellation is a direct consequence of the recurrence's symmetry — what the recurrence subtracts on one step it adds back on another within a full cycle.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that all required terms u₁,…,u₄ are already written as α + βk from the iteration in part (a).
  2. Rule out substituting a trial value of k and checking the sum — that is a verification tactic, not a solving tactic, and wastes the symbolic form already in hand.
  3. Commit to summing the symbolic expressions because the alternating signs in the recurrence strongly suggest constants will cancel over one full period.
  4. Bridge: write the sum as one long expression, being careful to bracket each term so signs propagate correctly.
  5. Inside the move, separate constants from k-terms, simplify each pile, then equate to the given total and solve for k.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. List the four terms from part (a): u₁ = 7, u₂ = −7+k, u₃ = −7+2k, u₄ = 7−k.
  2. Form the sum with explicit brackets: 7 + (−7+k) + (−7+2k) + (7−k).
  3. Collect constants: 7 − 7 − 7 + 7 = 0.
  4. Collect k-terms: k + 2k − k = 2k.
  5. Set the simplified sum equal to the given total: 2k = 30.
  6. Solve: k = 15.

Worked Micro-Example

Given u_{n+1} = k - u_n with u_1 = 3, iteration gives u_2 = k-3, u_3 = 3, u_4 = k-3. If \sum_{r=1}^{4} u_r = 20, then 3 + (k-3) + 3 + (k-3) = 2k = 20, so k = 10.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Recurrence with period 2 (e.g. u_{n+1} = c − u_n) and sum over an even number of terms
Pair consecutive terms; each pair sums to c, so the sum of 2m terms is mc, giving a linear equation in c (or in k if c depends on k).
Period-3 recurrence where Σ over 3 consecutive terms is given
Sum exactly one period; constants and k-coefficients combine to give an equation αk + β = S.
Sum range is not a multiple of the period (e.g. Σ from r=1 to 5 with period 4)
Split the sum as one full period plus the leftover term(s); the period part collapses cleanly, then add the leftover symbolically.
Arithmetic progression with first term and common difference both linear in k
Use S_n = (n/2)(2a + (n−1)d) directly; the same idea — sum is linear in k — applies, but no cancellation cycle is needed.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the recurrence as geometric or arithmetic and reaching for S_n formulas instead of summing the symbolic terms directly.
  • Believing one must determine k before summing, leading to guess-and-check.
  • Assuming the sum equals 4u₁ or some 'average' shortcut without writing the terms out.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when expanding −(−7 + 2k) or similar; always bracket each term before collecting.
  • Miscounting the period — applying the cancellation pattern to a sum that does not span a whole number of periods.
  • Dropping a term (e.g. summing only u₂+u₃+u₄) because u₁ looks 'too simple' to include.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1/P2 sequences and series: iterating a recurrence and evaluating finite sums. No use of generating functions or infinite-series machinery.
