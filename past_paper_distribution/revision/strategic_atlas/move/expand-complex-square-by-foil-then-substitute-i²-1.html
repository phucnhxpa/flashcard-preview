Complex Numbers

Expand complex square by FOIL then substitute i² = −1

Family: Identity Substitution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question requires writing a small integer power of a Cartesian complex number $z = a+bi$ (typically $z^2$ or $z^3$) in the standard form $a+bi$. Cues include phrases like 'express in the form $a+bi$', 'find $z^2$', or simplify $(a\pm bi)^2$. The exponent is small enough that binomial expansion is faster than modulus-argument methods. After expansion an $i^2$ (or $i^3$) term remains and must be eliminated.

Motivation — Why this works

By definition $i^2 = -1$, so any polynomial in $i$ collapses to a real plus an imaginary part once this substitution is made. FOIL/binomial expansion produces a sum containing $i^2$, and replacing it with $-1$ merges that contribution into the real part, yielding canonical $a+bi$ form. This is the most direct route for low powers because no trigonometric machinery is needed.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the expression is a small power of a Cartesian complex number and the target form is $a+bi$.
  2. I rule out modulus-argument / De Moivre because computing $|z|$ and $\arg z$ for a single squaring is overkill.
  3. I commit to direct binomial expansion since the exponent is 2 (or small), keeping the algebra elementary.
  4. I bridge by writing $(a+bi)^2 = (a+bi)(a+bi)$ and applying FOIL, tracking signs carefully on the $-i$ terms.
  5. Inside the move I substitute $i^2 = -1$ immediately, then group real and imaginary parts to read off $a$ and $b$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the power as a product, e.g. $(3-i)^2 = (3-i)(3-i)$.
  2. Expand by FOIL: $9 - 3i - 3i + i^2$.
  3. Combine like imaginary terms: $9 - 6i + i^2$.
  4. Substitute $i^2 = -1$: $9 - 6i - 1$.
  5. Collect real parts to get standard form: $8 - 6i$, so $a=8$, $b=-6$.

Worked Micro-Example

Find $(3-i)^2$ in the form $a+bi$.\\ $(3-i)^2 = (3-i)(3-i) = 9 - 3i - 3i + i^2$.\\ $= 9 - 6i + i^2 = 9 - 6i + (-1)$.\\ $= 8 - 6i$, so $a = 8,\ b = -6$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cubing a complex number, e.g. $(2+i)^3$
Use binomial theorem or multiply step by step, then apply $i^2 = -1$ and $i^3 = -i$ to reduce all powers of $i$.
Squaring a sum like $(a+bi)^2$ symbolically
Apply $(a+bi)^2 = a^2 - b^2 + 2abi$, which is the same identity after $i^2=-1$ substitution.
Higher integer powers (e.g. $z^{10}$)
Switch to modulus-argument form and use De Moivre's theorem instead of repeated FOIL.
Expression $(a+bi)(c+di)$ (not a square)
Same FOIL + $i^2 = -1$ procedure; just no repeated factor.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $i^2$ as $+1$ rather than $-1$, giving $10 - 6i$ instead of $8 - 6i$.
  • Reading $(3-i)^2$ as $3^2 - i^2 = 9 - (-1) = 10$, i.e. distributing the square over subtraction.
  • Confusing $(3-i)^2$ with $|3-i|^2 = 10$ and giving a real answer.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error on the cross terms: writing $+3i$ instead of $-3i$ when expanding $(-i)(3)$.
  • Mishandling $(-i)(-i) = i^2 = -1$, sometimes simplified incorrectly to $+1$.
  • Forgetting to combine the $-1$ from $i^2$ with the existing real part, leaving the answer as $9 - 6i + i^2$.
  • Dropping a negative sign when stating $b$ (e.g. writing $b = 6$ instead of $b = -6$).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP1) introduction to complex numbers; uses only the defining identity $i^2 = -1$ and elementary binomial expansion.
