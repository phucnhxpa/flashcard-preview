Sequences & Series

Take logarithms to linearise an exponential inequality

Family: Log Linearisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the unknown n (or k) appears in an exponent inside an inequality or equation, e.g. $ar^{n-1} > N$, $ar^n < \varepsilon$, or 'find the smallest n such that...'. Especially clear when the threshold is a clean power of 10 or e (so $\log_{10}$ or $\ln$ collapses one side). Common phrasings: 'first term to exceed', 'smallest n such that', 'after how many years does the amount exceed'.

Motivation — Why this works

Logarithms are strictly increasing on positive reals, so applying $\log$ to both sides of an inequality between positive quantities preserves direction and converts $r^{n-1}$ into a linear expression $(n-1)\log r$. This linearises the inequality, allowing direct rearrangement for n. Choosing the base to match the threshold (base 10 for $10^{100}$, base e for $e^k$) makes one side collapse to an integer.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the unknown n is trapped in the exponent, so no purely algebraic rearrangement can isolate it.
  2. Rule out trial-and-error or solving the related GP sum, since the question asks for a threshold-crossing index, not a sum.
  3. Commit to logs because both sides are positive and log is monotonic increasing, so the inequality direction is safe.
  4. Choose base 10 because the right-hand side is $10^{100}$, which collapses to 100 cleanly.
  5. After logging, treat $(n-1)$ as the unknown in a linear inequality, divide by $\log 6$ (positive, so no flip), and take the ceiling for the smallest integer n.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Confirm both sides are positive so logs are valid and direction is preserved.
  2. Apply $\log_{10}$ to both sides: $\log 4 + (n-1)\log 6 > 100$.
  3. Isolate the linear term: $(n-1) > \dfrac{100 - \log 4}{\log 6}$.
  4. Evaluate the right-hand side numerically (keep full calculator precision).
  5. Take the ceiling and add 1 to obtain the smallest integer n satisfying the original inequality.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $4 \times 6^{n-1} > 10^{100}$ for the smallest integer n. Apply $\log_{10}$: $\log 4 + (n-1)\log 6 > 100$, so $(n-1) > \dfrac{100 - \log 4}{\log 6} \approx 127.734$. Hence $n - 1 \geq 128$, giving $n = 129$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Decay inequality $ar^n < \varepsilon$ with $0 < r < 1$
After taking logs, dividing by $\log r < 0$ flips the inequality direction.
Threshold expressed with base e, e.g. $A e^{kt} > M$
Use $\ln$ instead of $\log_{10}$ so $\ln(e^{kt}) = kt$ collapses immediately.
Compound interest 'how many years until balance exceeds X'
Same move on $P(1+r)^n > X$; take logs and solve $(n) > \dfrac{\log(X/P)}{\log(1+r)}$.
GP sum threshold, e.g. $S_n > N$ with $S_n = a(r^n-1)/(r-1)$
First isolate $r^n$ algebraically, then apply logs to the resulting exponential inequality.
Equality version 'find n such that $ar^{n-1} = N$'
Same log step gives an exact (non-integer) n; no ceiling needed unless a smallest integer is requested.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the inequality as an equation and solving $4 \times 6^{n-1} = 10^{100}$, then forgetting whether to round up or down.
  • Misreading $4 \times 6^{n-1}$ as $(4 \times 6)^{n-1} = 24^{n-1}$ and logging the wrong expression.
  • Assuming the answer must be $n-1$ rather than n, losing one off the index.

Pitfalls

  • Rounding $\log 4$ or $\log 6$ too early, leading to the wrong integer after the ceiling.
  • Forgetting that dividing by $\log r$ flips the inequality when $0 < r < 1$ (so $\log r < 0$).
  • Taking the floor instead of the ceiling when the question asks for the smallest n exceeding the threshold.
  • Applying logs when one side could be non-positive, which would invalidate the step.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 (logarithms and exponentials) and P1/P2 sequences and series; uses only base-10 or natural logs and the identity $\log(a^k) = k\log a$.
