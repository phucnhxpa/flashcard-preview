Algebra & Functions

Combine root-fractions over αβ and reduce the numerator via symmetric identities

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an expression in the roots α, β of a quadratic appears as a sum of fractions with α and β in the denominators, e.g. α/β + β/α, kα/β + kβ/α, or α²/β + β²/α. The natural common denominator is αβ (or a power of it), which is exactly a Vieta quantity. The resulting numerator is then a power sum α^n+β^n that can be rewritten in terms of α+β and αβ. Cue phrases include 'find the value of α/β + β/α' or 'form a new equation whose roots are…'.

Motivation — Why this works

Vieta's formulas give α+β and αβ directly from the coefficients, so any symmetric expression in α and β is reachable if it can be rewritten in those two quantities. Combining the fractions over αβ converts the problem into a power-sum numerator divided by a known quantity. The standard identity α²+β² = (α+β)²−2αβ (and its cousins for α³+β³) then closes the gap. The whole move is the bridge from 'fractions in the roots' to 'polynomial in the elementary symmetric functions'.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the expression is symmetric in α and β with α, β sitting in denominators — a Vieta-friendly shape.
  2. Rules out solving the quadratic explicitly for α and β, since the symmetric structure means coefficients suffice.
  3. Commits to combining fractions over αβ because αβ is already known from Vieta.
  4. Bridges to the first operation: write the sum as (numerator)/(αβ), where the numerator is a power sum like α²+β².
  5. Tactically reduces the power sum using (α+β)²−2αβ (or α³+β³ = (α+β)³−3αβ(α+β)), substitutes Vieta values, and simplifies the resulting fraction carefully.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify α+β and αβ from the quadratic's coefficients via Vieta.
  2. Rewrite the fractional expression with common denominator αβ (or (αβ)^k as appropriate).
  3. Express the numerator as a symmetric power sum α^n+β^n.
  4. Apply the identity: α²+β² = (α+β)²−2αβ, or α³+β³ = (α+β)³−3αβ(α+β).
  5. Substitute the Vieta values and simplify the resulting rational number.

Worked Micro-Example

If α, β are roots of $2x^2+5x+7=0$, then $\alpha+\beta=-\tfrac{5}{2}$, $\alpha\beta=\tfrac{7}{2}$. Then $\tfrac{2\alpha}{\beta}+\tfrac{2\beta}{\alpha}=\tfrac{2(\alpha^2+\beta^2)}{\alpha\beta}=\tfrac{2((\alpha+\beta)^2-2\alpha\beta)}{\alpha\beta}=\tfrac{2(\tfrac{25}{4}-7)}{\tfrac{7}{2}}=\tfrac{-3/2}{7/2}=-\tfrac{3}{7}.$

Variants — Same move, different surface

α/β² + β/α²
Common denominator becomes (αβ)², numerator becomes α³+β³, reduced via (α+β)³−3αβ(α+β).
α²/β + β²/α
Common denominator αβ, numerator α³+β³ — same identity but only one power of αβ in the denominator.
1/α + 1/β
Combines directly to (α+β)/(αβ), no power-sum identity needed — the degenerate base case.
Forming a new quadratic with roots α/β and β/α
Use this move to compute the sum of new roots, and α/β · β/α = 1 for the product, then assemble x²−Sx+P=0.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing addition with a single fraction: writing 2α/β + 2β/α as 2(α+β)/(αβ) instead of 2(α²+β²)/(αβ).
  • Treating α²+β² as (α+β)² and forgetting the −2αβ correction.
  • Trying to solve the quadratic for α and β explicitly when the symmetric structure already makes Vieta sufficient.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips when computing (α+β)²−2αβ with mixed fractions, e.g. 25/4−7: failing to convert 7 to 28/4 before subtracting.
  • Dividing by αβ incorrectly: multiplying instead of inverting when dividing two fractions.
  • Sign errors when α+β is negative — forgetting that (α+β)² is positive even though α+β is not.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL Further Pure 'Roots of polynomial equations': uses only Vieta's formulas for quadratics and the standard identities α²+β² = (α+β)²−2αβ and α³+β³ = (α+β)³−3αβ(α+β).
