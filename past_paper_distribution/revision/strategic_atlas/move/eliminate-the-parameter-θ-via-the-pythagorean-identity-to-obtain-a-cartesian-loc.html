Coordinate Geometry

Eliminate the parameter θ via the Pythagorean identity to obtain a Cartesian locus

Family: Parametric Elimination Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a locus point has coordinates given parametrically as X = f(θ), Y = g(θ), where each coordinate isolates exactly one trigonometric function of the same angle θ (e.g. one involves only cos θ or sec θ, the other only sin θ or csc θ). Equivalent phrasings: 'find the equation of the locus of M', 'eliminate the parameter θ', or 'show that the point lies on a curve of the form …'. The defining visual cue is that cos θ (or sec θ) appears in X alone, and sin θ (or csc θ) appears in Y alone — separated, not mixed in a sum or product.

Motivation — Why this works

Because each coordinate cleanly isolates one trig function, we can rearrange to get cos θ and sin θ as algebraic expressions in X and Y. The identity cos²θ + sin²θ = 1 then provides a ready-made bridge: squaring and adding eliminates θ in a single step, producing a pure Cartesian relation between X and Y. This works precisely because the Pythagorean identity is the defining algebraic relation between sin and cos, and the parametrisation was designed so that this identity is available. The result is the locus equation, often recognisable as an ellipse, hyperbola, or related conic.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structural cue: X depends only on cos θ (or sec θ) and Y depends only on sin θ (or csc θ) — each coordinate isolates a single trig function of the same parameter.
  2. Rule out solving θ = arccos(...) and substituting: this introduces inverse trig functions and almost never simplifies cleanly.
  3. Rule out dividing the two equations to form tan θ: this gives one relation but θ is still implicit, so the parameter is not eliminated.
  4. Commit to the Pythagorean route because cos²θ + sin²θ = 1 directly links the two isolated trig functions.
  5. Bridge to operation: invert each equation to write cos θ = (expression in X) and sin θ = (expression in Y).
  6. Tactical decision: square first, then add — never add before squaring, since cos θ + sin θ is not a useful identity.
  7. Final step: replace (X, Y) with (x, y) if the question asks for the locus equation in standard variables.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. From X = f(θ), isolate cos θ (or sec θ) algebraically, e.g. cos θ = a/(2X).
  2. From Y = g(θ), isolate sin θ (or csc θ) algebraically, e.g. sin θ = b/(2Y).
  3. Square each: cos²θ = (expression in X)², sin²θ = (expression in Y)².
  4. Add the two squared equations and apply cos²θ + sin²θ = 1.
  5. Simplify to obtain a Cartesian equation in X and Y; rewrite using lowercase x, y for the final locus.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $M = \left(\tfrac{a\sec\theta}{2},\ \tfrac{b\csc\theta}{2}\right)$: invert to get $\cos\theta = \tfrac{a}{2X}$ and $\sin\theta = \tfrac{b}{2Y}$. Square and add: $\tfrac{a^2}{4X^2} + \tfrac{b^2}{4Y^2} = \cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$. Hence the locus is $\tfrac{a^2}{4x^2} + \tfrac{b^2}{4y^2} = 1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Hyperbola parametrisation (a sec θ, b tan θ)
Use sec²θ − tan²θ = 1 instead of the Pythagorean identity; isolate sec θ and tan θ, square, and subtract to give x²/a² − y²/b² = 1.
Parabola parametrisation (at², 2at)
No trig identity needed — solve the linear equation y = 2at for t = y/(2a) and substitute into x = at² to get y² = 4ax.
Mixed coordinates X = A sin θ + B cos θ, Y = C sin θ + D cos θ
Trig functions are not separated, so isolate sin θ and cos θ by solving the 2×2 linear system in X, Y first; then square and add. Same underlying identity, heavier algebra.
Reciprocal forms like X = p/cos θ, Y = q/sin θ
Directly read cos θ = p/X and sin θ = q/Y, then square and add — no rearrangement needed beyond inversion.

Common Misreadings

  • Reaching for double-angle identities (sin 2θ, cos 2θ) when the coordinates are already separated into one-trig-function-each — the basic Pythagorean identity is the correct, simpler tool.
  • Dividing the two equations to obtain tan θ and stopping there — this leaves θ implicit and does not produce a Cartesian locus.
  • Trying to express θ explicitly via arccos or arcsin and substitute — needlessly complicated and rarely simplifies.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to square before adding — writing cos θ + sin θ = 1 instead of cos²θ + sin²θ = 1.
  • Algebraic slips in the squaring step, e.g. writing a²/(2X²) instead of a²/(4X²) when squaring a/(2X).
  • Sign error in the hyperbolic variant: using + instead of − in sec²θ − tan²θ = 1.
  • Leaving the final answer in capital X, Y when the question expects lowercase x, y for the locus.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/FP1 scope; uses the Pythagorean identity cos²θ + sin²θ = 1 (formula booklet) and standard parametrisations of conics. The hyperbolic variant uses sec²θ − tan²θ = 1, also in the booklet. Avoid invoking matrix/eigenvector methods for the mixed-coordinate variant — keep to direct elimination at IAL level.
