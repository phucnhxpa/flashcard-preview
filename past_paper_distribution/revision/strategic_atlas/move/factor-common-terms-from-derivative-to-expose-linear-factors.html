Factor common terms from derivative to expose linear factors

Family: Strategic Factorisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires immediately after applying the product rule (or chain rule on a product) when the resulting derivative is a sum of two terms sharing visible common factors — typically a power of a linear bracket like $(x-a)^k$ and an exponential like $e^{kx}$ or a trig factor. Cue phrases: 'find stationary points', 'solve f'(x)=0', or 'show f'(x) = (...)(...)'. Recognise it whenever each term in $f'(x)$ contains the same bracket to differing powers and the same transcendental factor.

Motivation — Why this works

Setting a sum equal to zero is hard; setting a product equal to zero is trivial because at least one factor must vanish. Pulling out the highest common factor leaves a short linear (or quadratic) bracket whose roots can be read off by inspection. Since $e^{kx}>0$ always, that factor is discarded immediately, leaving only the polynomial factors to solve. This converts a stationary-point problem into a one-line root-finding step.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that after the product rule, $f'(x)$ is a sum of two terms which share a bracket like $(x-a)$ and a transcendental factor like $e^{kx}$.
  2. Rule out expanding everything into a single polynomial-times-exponential — that would lose the factored structure and force solving a messier equation.
  3. Commit to factoring because the lowest power of $(x-a)$ and the common $e^{kx}$ can be pulled out cleanly, leaving a tidy linear bracket.
  4. Bridge: write $f'(x) = (x-a)^{\min}\,e^{kx}\,[\text{leftover sum}]$ and simplify the leftover sum to a single linear (or low-degree) expression.
  5. Tactical: discard the strictly positive $e^{kx}$ when solving $f'(x)=0$, then read roots off the remaining linear factors.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the common factors across both terms: the lowest power of the repeated bracket and any shared exponential/trig factor.
  2. Extract them: write $f'(x) = (\text{common factor})\cdot[\text{remaining sum}]$.
  3. Simplify the remaining bracket by expanding and collecting like terms into a single linear (or quadratic) expression.
  4. State the fully factored form of $f'(x)$.
  5. To find stationary points, set each non-positive factor to zero (the exponential factor never vanishes).

Worked Micro-Example

Let $f(x)=(x-2)^2 e^{3x}$. Product rule: $f'(x)=2(x-2)e^{3x}+3(x-2)^2 e^{3x}$. Factor out $(x-2)e^{3x}$: $f'(x)=(x-2)e^{3x}\bigl[2+3(x-2)\bigr]=(x-2)e^{3x}(3x-4)$. Setting $f'(x)=0$ (and noting $e^{3x}>0$) gives $x=2$ or $x=\tfrac{4}{3}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$f(x)=(x-a)^n \ln x$ type after product rule
Pull out $(x-a)^{n-1}$; the remaining bracket combines a logarithm and a rational term, so usually only one factor (the polynomial one) yields a clean root, and the log equation is solved separately.
$f(x)=x^m e^{kx}$ with $m\ge 2$
Common factor is $x^{m-1}e^{kx}$, leaving the linear bracket $(m+kx)$; stationary points at $x=0$ and $x=-m/k$.
$f(x)=(ax+b)^n(cx+d)^m$ polynomial product
Factor out $(ax+b)^{n-1}(cx+d)^{m-1}$; the remaining bracket is linear in $x$ after combining.
Quotient-rule outputs like $\dfrac{(x-a)^n}{g(x)}$ differentiated
Numerator is a sum sharing $(x-a)^{n-1}$; factor it out of the numerator before equating to zero (denominator handled separately).
Trig products such as $f(x)=\sin^2 x \cos x$
After differentiating, factor out $\sin x$ to leave a bracket linear in $\cos x$ (or use $\sin^2+\cos^2=1$ to reduce).

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the two terms as 'unlike' because the powers of $(x-a)$ differ, missing that the lower power is still a common factor.
  • Believing the exponential must be 'solved' rather than discarded as strictly positive.
  • Thinking expansion is required before factoring, leading to a degree-three polynomial times $e^{kx}$ that then has to be re-factored.

Pitfalls

  • Factoring out $e^{kx}$ but forgetting $(x-a)$, leaving a quadratic in the bracket instead of a clean linear factor.
  • Sign or arithmetic slip when simplifying the inner bracket, e.g. writing $2+3(x-2)=3x-2$ instead of $3x-4$.
  • Pulling out $(x-a)^n$ instead of the lower power $(x-a)^{n-1}$, producing a bracket with a $1/(x-a)$ term.
  • Forgetting to state both stationary $x$-values (one from each linear factor).

A-level Scope Notes

Standard technique in Edexcel IAL P3/P4 differentiation and Further Pure stationary-point questions; uses only product rule, basic exponential/log derivatives, and elementary factoring.
