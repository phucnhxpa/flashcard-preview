Algebra & Functions

Clear fractions then divide by GCD before applying the quadratic formula

Family: Equation Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a quadratic (or equation soon to become one) has fractional or decimal coefficients, or when after clearing denominators the integer coefficients share an obvious common factor. Typical cues: a lone fraction like $-\frac{3}{4}$ on the RHS, coefficients in the hundreds or thousands, or a problem whose discriminant looks unwieldy. Equivalent phrasings: 'tidy the equation first', 'normalise to lowest integer form', 'pre-process before quadratic formula'. Especially valuable when factorisation over rationals will fail and the quadratic formula is inevitable.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying through by the LCM of denominators turns the equation into all-integer form, after which dividing by $\gcd$ of the coefficients gives the smallest equivalent equation. Both operations are reversible and preserve roots because they are multiplication of an equation $=0$ by a non-zero constant. Smaller coefficients shrink the discriminant, reducing arithmetic load and the chance of slips when simplifying surds. This is purely cosmetic algebra that pays off in cleaner downstream computation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices a fractional term (or decimals) sitting alongside large integer coefficients — quadratic formula on this raw form will be messy.
  2. Rules out factorisation: coefficients don't suggest a clean rational factorisation, and trying integer-root tests would waste time.
  3. Commits to pre-processing because the cost (one multiplication, one division) is tiny compared to risking errors in the discriminant.
  4. Bridges to action by identifying the LCM of denominators (here 4) to clear fractions, then rearranging to the standard form $=0$.
  5. Inside the move, scans the new integer coefficients for a common factor and divides through by the $\gcd$, checking the result is fully reduced before invoking $b^2-4ac$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the LCM $L$ of all denominators in the equation.
  2. Multiply every term by $L$ to obtain integer coefficients.
  3. Rearrange to the standard form $ap^2 + bp + c = 0$.
  4. Compute $d = \gcd(a, b, c)$.
  5. Divide every coefficient by $d$ to get the reduced quadratic.
  6. Apply the quadratic formula (or further methods) to the reduced form.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $240p^2 - 288p = -\tfrac{3}{4}$. Multiply by $4$: $960p^2 - 1152p + 3 = 0$. Since $\gcd(960,1152,3)=3$, divide by $3$: $320p^2 - 384p + 1 = 0$. Then $p = \dfrac{384 \pm \sqrt{384^2 - 4\cdot 320}}{640} = \dfrac{384 \pm \sqrt{146176}}{640}$, ready for surd simplification.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic with decimal coefficients, e.g. $0.2x^2 - 0.05x - 1.5 = 0$
Multiply by an appropriate power of 10 (here 100) to clear decimals, then divide by $\gcd$ of the resulting integers.
Line-meets-curve giving an equation like $\tfrac{x^2}{6} + \tfrac{x}{2} = 1$
Multiply by the LCM of denominators before expanding, halving the size of subsequent algebra.
Trig equation reducing to a quadratic in $\sin\theta$ with fractional coefficients
Clear fractions in the quadratic substitution $u = \sin\theta$ before solving for $u$.
Discriminant condition $b^2 - 4ac \gtrless 0$ with fractional $a,b,c$
Scale the quadratic to integer form first; the sign of the discriminant is unchanged but the inequality becomes far easier to manipulate.

Common Misreadings

  • Believing the quadratic will factorise over the rationals and wasting time on integer-root searches.
  • Treating the fraction as a reason to use completing the square rather than just clearing it.
  • Thinking that dividing by the $\gcd$ changes the roots — it does not.

Pitfalls

  • Multiplying only some terms by the LCM and missing the constant term.
  • Dividing by a common factor that doesn't actually divide every coefficient (e.g. dividing by 2 when one coefficient is odd).
  • Failing to first move all terms to one side, so the '=0' form is wrong when scaling.
  • Sign slips when the cleared term was negative (e.g. mishandling $-\tfrac{3}{4} \times 4 = -3$).

A-level Scope Notes
