Trigger — When to use

The problem states an indefinite integral equals a specific closed form containing an unknown constant (e.g. $\int f(x)\,dx = g(x) + k\cdot h(x)$) and asks for the value of that constant. The integrand is elementary (power rule, simple sum). Phrasings include 'Given that $\int \dots dx = \dots + k/x$, find $k$' or 'find the value of the constant $a$ such that $\int \dots = \dots$'. The cue is that one side is a calculation you can perform directly, and the other is a template to match.

Motivation — Why this works

Indefinite integrals are unique up to an additive constant, so if your computed antiderivative and the given form both represent $\int f(x)\,dx$, their non-constant parts must be identical as functions of $x$. Coefficients of like powers (or like rational terms) in $x$ must therefore agree, turning a calculus question into a one-line algebraic identification. This is the same coefficient-matching logic that underpins partial fractions and polynomial identities.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the RHS is given in a fixed template containing an unknown $k$ — the integral itself must be computable without knowing $k$.
  2. I rule out trying to solve for $k$ by substitution of a value of $x$ or by differentiating the RHS and matching to $f(x)$; integrating the LHS directly is shorter and is what the question signals.
  3. I commit because the integrand is a sum of power-rule terms, so the antiderivative will land in the same algebraic shape as the RHS template.
  4. I rewrite negative-power terms like $1/x^2$ as $x^{-2}$ so the power rule applies cleanly, then integrate term by term.
  5. I align my answer with the template, ignore $+C$ (absent from the RHS, so taken as $0$), and read off $k$ from the matching coefficient.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rewrite the integrand using index laws (e.g. $1/x^2 = x^{-2}$).
  2. Integrate term by term using $\int x^n\,dx = x^{n+1}/(n+1)$ for $n\neq -1$.
  3. Write the antiderivative in the same algebraic shape as the given RHS.
  4. Equate coefficients of corresponding terms (e.g. the $1/x$ terms).
  5. Solve the resulting linear equation for the unknown constant.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\int\left(\tfrac{2}{3}x + \tfrac{1}{x^2}\right)dx = \tfrac{1}{3}x^2 + \tfrac{k}{x}$, find $k$. Integrating: $\tfrac{2}{3}\cdot\tfrac{x^2}{2} + \tfrac{x^{-1}}{-1} + C = \tfrac{1}{3}x^2 - \tfrac{1}{x} + C$. Matching the $1/x$ terms: $-\tfrac{1}{x} = \tfrac{k}{x}$, so $k = -1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two unknown constants in the RHS template, e.g. $\int(\dots)dx = ax^3 + bx + C$
Compare coefficients of each power of $x$ separately to obtain a system of equations for $a$ and $b$.
Definite integral equal to expression in $k$
Evaluate the definite integral fully, then equate the numerical result to the given expression and solve for $k$.
Differentiation analogue: given $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ and $y = f(x,k)$, find $k$
Differentiate $f(x,k)$ and compare with the given derivative coefficient by coefficient.
Partial fractions setup, e.g. $\dfrac{px+q}{(x-1)(x+2)} \equiv \dfrac{A}{x-1} + \dfrac{B}{x+2}$
Multiply through and equate coefficients of like powers of $x$ to find $A$ and $B$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $k$ as the constant of integration $C$ rather than as a coefficient inside the antiderivative.
  • Differentiating the RHS template and equating to the integrand instead of integrating the LHS as the question directs (still valid but slower and easier to slip on).
  • Confusing direction: integrating the RHS expression instead of the integrand on the LHS.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error on $\int x^{-2}\,dx = -x^{-1}$ — dropping the minus gives $k = +1$ instead of $-1$.
  • Forgetting to divide by the new exponent (e.g. writing $\int \tfrac{2}{3}x\,dx$ as $\tfrac{2}{3}x^2$ instead of $\tfrac{1}{3}x^2$).
  • Worrying that the absent $+C$ makes $k$ ambiguous; the comparison of $x$-dependent coefficients is independent of $C$.
  • Mismatching terms by shape, e.g. comparing an $x^{-1}$ term to an $x$ term.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (C12/C34): power-rule integration of polynomial and negative-integer-power terms, with coefficient comparison as standard algebraic identification. No techniques beyond basic indefinite integration are required.
