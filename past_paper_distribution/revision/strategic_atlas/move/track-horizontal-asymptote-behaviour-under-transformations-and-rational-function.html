Algebra & Functions

Track horizontal asymptote behaviour under transformations and rational-function analysis

Family: Asymptote Analysis Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a horizontal asymptote y = c is involved and you must decide either (a) what happens to it under a transformation y = f(x) + a, y = af(x), y = f(x+a), y = f(-x), y = f(ax), or (b) what value is missing from the range of a Möbius rational function (ax+b)/(cx+d). Visual cues: a sketch with a labelled horizontal asymptote being transformed; a question asking for 'range of f' or 'domain of f⁻¹' for a linear-over-linear rational function. Equivalent phrasings: 'state the equation of the asymptote of the new curve', 'find the value f(x) cannot take', 'state the range of f'.

Motivation — Why this works

A horizontal asymptote y = c is a statement purely about the limiting y-value as x → ±∞; it has no x-location. Hence transformations that act only on x leave it fixed, while transformations acting on y move it in the same way they move every output. For a Möbius map (ax+b)/(cx+d), the horizontal asymptote y = a/c is exactly the output value never attained (solving (ax+b)/(cx+d) = a/c gives a contradiction), so the range is ℝ \ {a/c}. Recognising both facets turns asymptote-spotting into a one-line tool for transformation sketches and inverse-function domains.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the horizontal asymptote y = c, either given on a sketch or computed from a rational function by comparing leading coefficients of equal-degree numerator and denominator.
  2. Rule out solving y = f(x) for x algebraically (slow) when the question is really about a limiting output, and rule out treating the asymptote as a 'point' that gets reflected — it is a horizontal line stretching to ±∞.
  3. Commit to the principle: horizontal asymptotes are invariant under x-only transformations (y = f(-x), y = f(x+a), y = f(ax)) and shift/scale under y-transformations exactly as outputs do (y = f(x)+a → y = c+a; y = -f(x) → y = -c; y = kf(x) → y = kc).
  4. Bridge to concrete work: write down the asymptote in the form y = c, then apply the y-side of the transformation (or none, if x-only) to obtain the new asymptote.
  5. For range/inverse-domain questions on Möbius f(x) = (ax+b)/(cx+d): identify y = a/c as the excluded output, optionally verify by solving (ax+b)/(cx+d) = a/c and reaching a contradiction, then declare range(f) = ℝ \ {a/c} and hence domain(f⁻¹) = ℝ \ {a/c}.
  6. Sanity check: vertical asymptotes obey the mirror rule (invariant under y-transformations, shifted/scaled under x-transformations) — keep the two cases separate.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the horizontal asymptote y = c (from a given sketch, or as ratio of leading coefficients for equal-degree rationals).
  2. Classify the transformation: is it acting on x only, on y only, or both?
  3. If x-only (e.g. y = f(-x), y = f(x+a), y = f(ax)): asymptote remains y = c.
  4. If y-only (e.g. y = f(x)+a, y = -f(x), y = kf(x)): new asymptote is y = c+a, y = -c, or y = kc respectively.
  5. For range of f(x) = (ax+b)/(cx+d): horizontal asymptote y = a/c; verify (ax+b) = (a/c)(cx+d) ⇒ b = ad/c, contradiction unless degenerate.
  6. State range(f) = {y ∈ ℝ : y ≠ a/c}; if asked for f⁻¹, this becomes the domain of f⁻¹.

Worked Micro-Example

Given f with asymptote $y = 1$. (i) For $y = f(x) - 2$: y-shift by $-2$, so new asymptote $y = 1 - 2 = -1$. (ii) For $y = f(-x)$: x-only reflection, so asymptote unchanged at $y = 1$. Separately, for $g(x) = \dfrac{3x+2}{x-1}$: leading-coefficient ratio gives asymptote $y = 3$; checking $3x+2 = 3(x-1)$ yields $2 = -3$, contradiction, so $\text{range}(g) = \mathbb{R}\setminus\{3\}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Vertical asymptote x = a under transformation
Mirror rule: invariant under y-transformations; under x-transformation y = f(x+h) becomes x = a-h, under y = f(kx) becomes x = a/k, under y = f(-x) becomes x = -a.
Rational function with deg(num) < deg(den)
Horizontal asymptote is y = 0; this value may or may not be attained, so check explicitly whether p(x) = 0 has a solution in the domain before excluding 0 from the range.
Rational function with deg(num) ≥ 2 (not Möbius)
Curve can cross its horizontal asymptote, so 'asymptote value is excluded from range' fails — find range by completing the square, calculus, or solving y = f(x) for x and demanding real solutions.
Combined transformation y = af(x) + b
Apply scale then shift to the asymptote: y = c → y = ac + b.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 'reflection in the y-axis' (y = f(-x)) as also reflecting the horizontal asymptote and writing y = -c.
  • Confusing the horizontal asymptote (excluded from range) with the vertical asymptote (excluded from domain).
  • Assuming any rational function's horizontal asymptote value is automatically excluded from the range (only safe for Möbius / monotone branches).

Pitfalls

  • Confusing reflection in the y-axis (y = f(-x), x-only — asymptote fixed) with reflection in the x-axis (y = -f(x), y-only — asymptote becomes y = -c).
  • Forgetting that under y = f(x) + a both the asymptote AND every marked y-coordinate shift by a — being inconsistent across the sketch.
  • Quoting the range of a Möbius map without checking the domain restriction (e.g. if the original domain is x > 1, the range may be a one-sided interval, not all of ℝ \ {a/c}).

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 (transformations of graphs, rational functions, inverse functions). No formula booklet entry needed — this is conceptual. Avoid invoking limits formally (lim notation) or oblique/slant asymptotes (out of standard IAL scope unless explicitly introduced).
