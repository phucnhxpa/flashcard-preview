Algebra & Functions

Substitute a known point into a one-parameter form to determine the constant

Family: Parameter Determination Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever the curve/function has been reduced to a form containing exactly one unknown constant — e.g. y = a(x − h)² + k after using the vertex, y = A(x − r₁)(x − r₂)(x − r₃) after using the roots, or f(x) = kx(x − 4) given in general factored form. A separate, explicitly stated point (x₀, y₀) on the curve is available and has not yet been used. Equivalent phrasings: 'curve passes through P', 'f(2) = …', 'maximum/minimum value is …'. The signal is: one degree of freedom remaining + one unused coordinate condition.

Motivation — Why this works

A single linear equation suffices to pin down a single unknown coefficient, so substituting the coordinates of the known point converts the parametric form directly into an equation for that constant. This is the most economical route — no simultaneous equations, no calculus, no curve-fitting. It works because the parametric form was constructed to already encode all other constraints (roots, vertex, asymptotes), leaving only a vertical scale factor to be fixed by one extra data point.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the form has exactly one undetermined constant (a, k, A, λ, …) — every other feature (roots, vertex, period) is already baked in.
  2. Rule out alternatives: simultaneous equations are unnecessary (only one unknown), differentiation is unnecessary (no slope condition), and comparing coefficients is overkill.
  3. Commit to substitution because one unknown + one independent condition (the unused point) = one linear equation, which is immediate.
  4. Check that the chosen point is NOT a root of any factor in the form — otherwise the equation collapses to 0 = 0 and gives no information about the constant.
  5. Substitute the coordinates carefully, evaluating bracketed factors as signed integers before multiplying, then solve the resulting linear equation.
  6. Sanity-check the sign/magnitude of the constant against the geometry (e.g. a > 0 for a minimum, A < 0 for a curve falling at +∞).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the curve in its current one-parameter form, e.g. y = a(x − h)² + k or y = A∏(x − rᵢ).
  2. Identify the unused point (x₀, y₀) and confirm x₀ is not a root.
  3. Substitute: y₀ = (parameter) · [expression in x₀].
  4. Evaluate the bracketed/squared factors numerically with attention to signs.
  5. Solve the resulting linear equation for the parameter.
  6. Write out the final explicit equation of the curve and verify it is consistent with any qualitative feature (max/min, end behaviour).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $y = a(x-4)^2 - 5$ passing through $P(-2,13)$: substitute to get $13 = a(-6)^2 - 5 = 36a - 5$, so $36a = 18$ and $a = \tfrac{1}{2}$. Hence $y = \tfrac{1}{2}(x-4)^2 - 5$, with $a>0$ confirming the minimum.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Factored cubic y = A(x − r₁)(x − r₂)(x − r₃) with a non-root point given
Substitute the point; evaluate the three signed factors and divide y₀ by their product to get A.
Repeated-factor form g(x) = λx(x − 4)²
Same substitution, but remember to square the repeated factor before multiplying.
Trigonometric form y = a sin(bx) with a known maximum
Use the maximum value (which equals |a|) directly; the 'point' is implicit in the amplitude.
Circle (x − p)² + (y − q)² = r² with centre known and one point on the circle
Substitute the point to compute r² directly.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading g(x) = λx(x − 4)² as λx(x − 4)·2, misinterpreting the exponent as a multiplier.
  • Treating a stated root as a 'known point' for substitution — this gives 0 = 0 and determines nothing.
  • Assuming two unknowns remain when in fact one has already been fixed by an earlier condition (vertex, root, asymptote).

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors in evaluating factors like (−2 − 4)² — must give (−6)² = 36, not −36.
  • Sign errors in products of three signed factors in cubics, e.g. (3)(1)(−4) = −12.
  • Forgetting to square a repeated factor, leading to a wrong scale constant.
  • Substituting a root by mistake, yielding 0 = 0 and no information.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1–P3) for quadratics, cubics, and standard transformations; no formula-booklet entry needed. Avoid generalising to multi-parameter fitting (regression, least squares) which is out of scope.
