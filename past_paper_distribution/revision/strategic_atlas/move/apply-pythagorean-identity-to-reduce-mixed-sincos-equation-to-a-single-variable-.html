Strategic Atlas / Trigonometry / Pythagorean Reduction
Trigonometry

Apply Pythagorean identity to reduce mixed sin/cos equation to a single-variable quadratic

Family: Pythagorean Reduction Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when, after any preliminary simplification (clearing fractions, expanding brackets), the equation contains BOTH a squared trig function (sin²A or cos²A) AND another trig function of the same angle A — typically a linear sinA, cosA, or a constant. Visual cues: 'sin²' on one side and 'cos' (or 'cos²' alongside a linear cos) on the other, or any mixture of degree-2 and degree-1 terms in sin/cos of the same argument. Equivalent phrasings: 'mixed sin and cos of the same angle with at least one squared term', 'two different trig functions, one of which is squared'. The angle itself may be x, 2θ, (θ+43°), etc. — the move is invariant under the choice of argument.

Motivation — Why this works

An equation in two different trig functions cannot be solved directly, but sin²A + cos²A = 1 allows us to eliminate one of them. Replacing the squared term (sin²A → 1 − cos²A, or cos²A → 1 − sin²A) reduces the equation to a polynomial in a single trig function — typically a quadratic of the form ay² + by + c = 0. This is the standard 'Pythagorean reduction' endgame: it converts a transcendental equation into an algebraic one solvable by factorisation or the quadratic formula, after which inverse trig recovers the angles. Choosing which function to keep is dictated by which one already appears linearly: keep that one, eliminate the other.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the equation, after simplification, contains TWO different trig functions of the same angle — for example sinA and cos²A, or cos x and sin²x — with at least one of them squared.
  2. Rule out direct inverse-trig (impossible with two functions) and rule out converting the linear term via a square root (e.g. cos x = ±√(1−sin²x)) because that introduces sign ambiguity and radicals.
  3. Commit to the Pythagorean substitution: replace the SQUARED term using sin²A + cos²A = 1, keeping whichever function appears linearly so the result is a clean quadratic.
  4. Bridge: substitute sin²A = 1 − cos²A (or cos²A = 1 − sin²A), expand, and collect terms on one side to reach standard quadratic form ay² + by + c = 0 with y the surviving trig function.
  5. Tactical decision: factorise if the quadratic factors over the rationals; otherwise apply the quadratic formula. After solving, discard any root y with |y| > 1 since sin and cos are bounded by 1.
  6. Final stage: for each surviving root y = k, solve the elementary equation sinA = k or cosA = k over the required interval, remembering the symmetry branches (π − θ for sin, ±θ for cos) and the period.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the squared trig term and the linear trig term sharing the same angle A.
  2. Substitute using sin²A = 1 − cos²A or cos²A = 1 − sin²A so only ONE trig function remains.
  3. Expand brackets and bring all terms to one side: ay² + by + c = 0, where y is the surviving trig function.
  4. Solve the quadratic in y by factorisation, or apply y = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac))/(2a).
  5. Reject any root with |y| > 1 (outside the range of sin/cos).
  6. For each valid root y = k, solve the elementary trig equation (sinA = k or cosA = k) over the given domain, including all symmetry branches.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $3\sin A = 2\cos^2 A$ for $A\in[0°,360°)$.\n Substitute $\cos^2 A = 1-\sin^2 A$: $3\sin A = 2(1-\sin^2 A)$.\n Rearrange: $2\sin^2 A + 3\sin A - 2 = 0$.\n Factorise: $(2\sin A - 1)(\sin A + 2)=0$, giving $\sin A = \tfrac12$ (reject $\sin A=-2$).\n Hence $A = 30°,\ 150°$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation has linear cosA and a sin²A term
Substitute sin²A = 1 − cos²A instead, producing a quadratic in cosA.
Equation involves a double-angle term such as cos2A alongside sin²A or cos²A
Use cos2A = 1 − 2sin²A or cos2A = 2cos²A − 1 to align all terms to one trig function before reducing.
Equation mixes tanA and secA
Use 1 + tan²A = sec²A to eliminate one and form a quadratic in the other.
Argument is a compound angle like (θ + 43°) or 2θ
Treat the entire argument as a single variable A; reduce to a quadratic in sinA or cosA, then back-substitute and solve over the appropriate (rescaled) interval.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to divide through by cosA or sinA to form a tan equation — this only works when EVERY term has matching degree, which fails when a constant or unmatched-degree term is present.
  • Attempting to square both sides to eliminate the mismatch — this introduces extraneous roots and makes the equation worse.
  • Replacing the LINEAR term using cosA = ±√(1−sin²A), introducing surds and sign ambiguity instead of replacing the squared term.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when expanding the substitution and rearranging to standard quadratic form (especially the constant term and the coefficient flip).
  • Forgetting to discard roots with |y| > 1 since sinA, cosA ∈ [−1, 1].
  • Missing solution branches: for sinA = k include both A and 180° − A; for cosA = k include both A and 360° − A (or ±A); and for compound or doubled arguments, expand the search interval before back-substituting.
  • Factorising before fully rearranging, leading to a wrong product.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 scope. Invokes the Pythagorean identity sin²A + cos²A ≡ 1 (formula booklet, trig identities section), and optionally 1 + tan²A ≡ sec²A or the double-angle formulae. Stay with elementary inverse-trig solutions; do not invoke complex roots or general-solution machinery beyond the standard interval-based approach.
