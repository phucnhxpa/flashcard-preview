Trigonometry

Assign reflex angle 2π − θ to the inner arc of a composite annular sector

Family: Geometric Interpretation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a region is bounded by two concentric arcs that lie on opposite sides of the centre — i.e. a 'wrap-around' or composite platform/track shape. One arc subtends a stated angle θ at O, but the other arc traces the complementary sweep around the rest of the circle. Cues include phrases like 'the platform wraps around', diagrams showing the inner arc on the far side of O from the outer arc, or perimeters that visibly enclose more than half the disc.

Motivation — Why this works

A full revolution about O is 2π radians, so if the outer arc uses angle θ, the inner arc on the opposite side must use the reflex angle 2π − θ. The arc-length formula s = rθ and sector-area formula A = ½r²θ require the angle that the arc actually subtends, not the labelled angle from the diagram. Pairing the wrong angle with the inner radius collapses the region to a thin crescent and produces perimeters/areas off by a large multiplicative factor. Recognising which side of O each arc lies on is therefore the decisive geometric step.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the region is bounded by two concentric arcs but they sit on opposite sides of the centre O, not stacked on the same side.
  2. Rule out using the same θ for both arcs: that would describe a standard annular sector, not a wrap-around shape.
  3. Commit to the reflex-angle reading because the inner arc visibly traverses the 'long way' around O, completing what the outer arc leaves open.
  4. Translate this into the angle 2π − θ for every inner-arc computation (length and sector area).
  5. Keep all angles in radians throughout — mixing 360° − θ with rθ is a silent unit error.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the angle θ associated with the outer (minor) arc from the diagram/text.
  2. Compute the complementary reflex angle: φ = 2π − θ.
  3. Apply s_inner = r_inner · φ for the inner arc length.
  4. Apply A_inner sector = ½ · r_inner² · φ for the inner sector area.
  5. Combine with outer-arc contributions and any straight radial edges to assemble total perimeter/area.

Worked Micro-Example

Outer arc DC has radius $R = 8$ and subtends $\theta = 0.8$ rad at $O$; inner arc AEB has radius $r = 7.03125$ and wraps the opposite way. Inner angle $= 2\pi - 0.8 \approx 5.4832$ rad. Inner arc length $= 7.03125 \times (2\pi - 0.8) \approx 38.55$. Inner sector area $= \tfrac{1}{2}(7.03125)^2(2\pi - 0.8) \approx 135.5$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Running track / stadium shape with two semicircular ends and a straight section
Each curved end uses angle π; the reflex idea reduces to splitting 2π between the two ends.
Reflex-angle sector where the shaded region is the 'major' sector
Use 2π − θ directly with the single radius for both arc length and area.
Pendant / locket shape bounded by a minor arc on one circle and a major arc on another concentric circle
Same decision: pair outer radius with θ and inner radius with 2π − θ (or vice versa, dictated by the diagram).

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming both concentric arcs subtend the same angle θ, producing a standard annular sector instead of a wrap-around region.
  • Swapping which arc is 'inner' vs 'outer' and pairing the reflex angle with the wrong radius.
  • Reading the diagram as two minor arcs on the same side of O when they are actually on opposite sides.

Pitfalls

  • Writing 360° − θ instead of 2π − θ while still applying s = rθ, which mixes degrees and radians.
  • Forgetting to also use the reflex angle in the sector-area formula after correctly using it for arc length (or vice versa).
  • Omitting the two straight radial edges that join the inner and outer arcs when computing the perimeter.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL P2 radian measure: arc length s = rθ and sector area A = ½r²θ, with θ in radians and the full-turn identity 2π rad = one revolution.
