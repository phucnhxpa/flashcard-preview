Binomial Expansion

Rewrite quotient as product to reuse a binomial expansion

Family: Algebraic Restructuring Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when f(x) is presented as a fraction P(x)/Q(x)^{\alpha} (often with \alpha = 1/2, 1, 2) and an earlier part of the question has already expanded Q(x)^{-\alpha} as a binomial series. Typical cue phrases: 'Hence find the expansion of...', 'Using your answer to part (a)...', or a follow-up part where the numerator is a low-degree polynomial like (2+kx). Also fires whenever you need to multiply a series by a polynomial but the expression is written as a quotient.

Motivation — Why this works

Division by Q(x)^{\alpha} is identical to multiplication by Q(x)^{-\alpha}, so the quotient form and the product form are algebraically equivalent. The binomial series machinery only handles expressions of the form (a+bx)^{n}, not quotients directly, so rewriting exposes the structure the series was built for. Once in product form, polynomial \times series multiplication is a routine term-by-term operation, and coefficients of each power of x can be read off and matched against required conditions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the denominator Q(x)^{\alpha} matches (up to a sign in the exponent) the expression already expanded in the previous part.
  2. Rule out re-expanding the whole quotient from scratch or using long division of series — both are wasteful and the question clearly signposts reuse.
  3. Commit to the rewrite because flipping the exponent sign is a one-line algebraic identity that immediately exposes a polynomial-times-series product.
  4. Bridge to operation: write f(x) = P(x)\cdot Q(x)^{-\alpha} and substitute the truncated series from the earlier part.
  5. Tactically, only keep terms up to the required power of x during multiplication — higher-order products are discarded to avoid wasted work.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the quotient structure: f(x) = P(x)/Q(x)^{\alpha}.
  2. Flip the exponent sign: f(x) = P(x)\cdot Q(x)^{-\alpha}.
  3. Substitute the binomial expansion of Q(x)^{-\alpha} from the earlier part, truncated to the required order.
  4. Multiply P(x) by the series term-by-term, keeping only powers of x up to the required degree.
  5. Collect like terms and read off coefficients (often used to find an unknown like k by matching a given coefficient).

Worked Micro-Example

Given (4-5x)^{-1/2} = \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{5x}{16} + \tfrac{75x^{2}}{256} + \dots, expand f(x) = \dfrac{2+kx}{\sqrt{4-5x}}. Rewrite as f(x) = (2+kx)\left(\tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{5x}{16} + \tfrac{75x^{2}}{256}+\dots\right). Multiplying out: f(x) = 1 + \left(\tfrac{5}{8} + \tfrac{k}{2}\right)x + \left(\tfrac{75}{128} + \tfrac{5k}{16}\right)x^{2} + \dots, ready for coefficient matching.

Variants — Same move, different surface

f(x) = P(x)/(a+bx)^{n} with positive integer n
Rewrite as P(x)(a+bx)^{-n} and expand (a+bx)^{-n} via the generalised binomial series.
f(x) = (a+bx)^{m}/(c+dx)^{n}
Rewrite as (a+bx)^{m}(c+dx)^{-n}; expand each factor separately and multiply truncated series.
Partial fractions of the form A/(1-px) + B/(1-qx)^{2}
Treat each fraction as A(1-px)^{-1} and B(1-qx)^{-2} before applying the binomial series to each piece.
Integrand of the form P(x)/(a+bx)^{n} for series-based approximation of an integral
Rewrite as a product, expand, then integrate term-by-term.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating (4-5x)^{1/2} in the denominator as if its expansion (rather than that of (4-5x)^{-1/2}) is the one to use.
  • Believing the quotient must be expanded directly using a separate 'quotient' rule for series — no such rule is needed.
  • Assuming the numerator (2+kx) must also be re-expanded; it is already a finite polynomial.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to flip the sign of the exponent when moving Q(x)^{\alpha} from denominator to numerator.
  • Carrying too few terms of the series so that the required coefficient (e.g. of x^{2}) cannot be formed correctly.
  • Multiplying every term of the polynomial by every term of the series and keeping high-order junk, leading to arithmetic slips.
  • Losing the constant factor when Q(x) = (a+bx) with a \neq 1: the expansion of (a+bx)^{-\alpha} carries a factor a^{-\alpha} that must not be dropped.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard technique within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 binomial expansion for rational/fractional indices, often paired with 'hence' parts that supply the base expansion in an earlier sub-question.
