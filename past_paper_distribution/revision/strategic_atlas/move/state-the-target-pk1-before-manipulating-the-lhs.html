Proof

State the target P(k+1) before manipulating the LHS

Family: Goal Identification Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires at the start of the inductive step in any proof by induction, immediately after assuming P(k). Cues include phrases like 'show P(k+1)', 'hence prove the result holds for n = k+1', or any induction problem with a closed-form RHS (sums, products, divisibility, matrix powers, recurrences). Whenever the student is about to start algebra on the LHS of P(k+1), this move asks them to first write down what the LHS must equal.

Motivation — Why this works

Induction algebra is goal-directed: every factoring or simplification choice should drive the LHS toward a known target. Substituting n = k+1 into the RHS gives that target explicitly, so factor structure (e.g. needing (k+1)(k+2)(k+3)/3) is visible before any manipulation begins. This converts the inductive step from open-ended expansion into a closed search with a clear stopping condition, and prevents circular 'I think I'm done' reasoning.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice this is the inductive step, so I have ∑_{r=1}^{k} r(r+1) = k(k+1)(k+2)/3 as my assumption and need to prove the analogous statement at k+1.
  2. I rule out diving straight into algebra — without a target, I won't know which common factor to pull out or when to stop.
  3. I commit to writing P(k+1) explicitly: substitute n = k+1 into the RHS to see the exact form the LHS must reach.
  4. This gives me (k+1)(k+2)(k+3)/3, so I bridge to the algebra knowing I must produce factors (k+1), (k+2), (k+3) over 3.
  5. Tactically, I keep this target written at the top of my working and steer factoring choices (e.g. taking out (k+1)/3) toward it.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the assumption P(k) clearly.
  2. Write 'RTP: P(k+1)' and substitute n = k+1 into both sides of the original statement.
  3. Simplify the RHS index/expression to its cleanest factored form, e.g. (k+1)(k+2)(k+3)/3.
  4. Box or underline this target so it remains visible during the algebra.
  5. Begin manipulating the LHS of P(k+1) (typically LHS_k + (k+1)-th term) with the target shape in mind, factoring toward the required structure.

Worked Micro-Example

Assume $\sum_{r=1}^{k} r(r+1) = \tfrac{k(k+1)(k+2)}{3}$. Target: $\sum_{r=1}^{k+1} r(r+1) = \tfrac{(k+1)(k+2)(k+3)}{3}$. Then LHS $= \tfrac{k(k+1)(k+2)}{3} + (k+1)(k+2) = \tfrac{(k+1)(k+2)[k+3]}{3}$, matching the target exactly.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Divisibility induction (e.g. show $3^{2n}-1$ divisible by 8)
Target is written as 'must equal 8M for some integer M', so the goal becomes isolating a factor of 8 rather than matching a polynomial form.
Matrix power induction $\mathbf{A}^n = \ldots$
Substitute n = k+1 into the conjectured matrix to obtain the target matrix entries; aim to match each entry after multiplying $\mathbf{A}^k \cdot \mathbf{A}$.
Recurrence/sequence induction $u_n = f(n)$
Target is $f(k+1)$ in simplified form; use the recurrence to express $u_{k+1}$ in terms of $u_k$, then steer toward $f(k+1)$.
Product induction $\prod_{r=1}^{n} \ldots$
Target is the closed product evaluated at k+1; the algebra proceeds by multiplying the inductive product by the (k+1)-th factor and simplifying toward that target.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'write P(k+1)' as a formal proof line rather than a planning device — it is scratch work that orients the algebra.
  • Substituting n = k+1 only on the LHS (changing the upper limit of the sum) but forgetting to substitute on the RHS to obtain the target.
  • Confusing P(k+1) with P(k) + (next term); the target is the RHS at k+1, not the augmented LHS.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to fully simplify the RHS at k+1 (e.g. leaving (k+1)((k+1)+1)((k+1)+2)/3 unsimplified) so the target shape is obscured.
  • Expanding the LHS into a polynomial without keeping the factored target visible, then being unable to refactor back.
  • Declaring the proof complete after reaching a polynomial that merely 'looks right' without verifying it equals the stated target.
  • Algebraic slip when shifting indices — e.g. writing (k+2)(k+3) instead of (k+1)(k+2) for the target factors.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard practice in Edexcel IAL Further Pure proof-by-induction questions covering summations, divisibility, matrix powers, and recurrences; uses only substitution and factoring within the A-level toolkit.
