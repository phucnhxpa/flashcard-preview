Binomial Expansion

Equate expansion's base to target value to find substitution x

Family: Substitution Bridge Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

Part (a) produces a binomial expansion of $(a+bx)^n$ or $(1+kx)^n$ as a polynomial in $x$, and part (b) asks you to 'use', 'hence' or 'hence deduce' an approximation to a specific number such as $2.0125^{13}$, $\sqrt[3]{1.15}$, $0.98^{-2}$, or $\sqrt[3]{31}$. The decimal/surd target is NOT the value of $x$ — it must equal the bracketed base of the expansion. Watch for keywords 'hence', 'use your expansion', or a numerical target whose form mirrors the expansion's base. Sometimes the target is disguised as a fraction (e.g. $\tfrac{161}{80}$) and must first be converted to decimal.

Motivation — Why this works

The polynomial from part (a) is an approximation to $(a+bx)^n$ valid for small $x$ (or $|kx|<1$). To approximate a specific numerical power, we must find the unique $x$ that makes the bracket equal to the target number, so substituting that $x$ into the polynomial yields a numerical approximation to the desired power. This is the bridge between an algebraic identity (the series) and an arithmetic estimate. The technique relies on the binomial series' validity range, and the smallness of the resulting $x$ guarantees that truncation error is acceptable.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice part (b) demands 'hence' or 'use your expansion', so I cannot start a fresh expansion — I must reuse part (a)'s polynomial in $x$.
  2. I rule out substituting $x$ equal to the target directly (e.g. $x=0.98$ or $x=1.15$): that would ignore the structure of the bracket $a+bx$ and give a wildly wrong answer.
  3. I commit to matching the BASE of the expansion to the target: set $a+bx = \text{target}$ and solve for $x$.
  4. I check the resulting $x$ is small and lies inside the validity range ($|kx|<1$ for fractional/negative $n$); a 'nice' small $x$ confirms I have read the question correctly.
  5. I substitute that $x$ into the truncated polynomial, being careful to raise it to the correct power in each term, and sum without premature rounding.
  6. If the target is a surd like $\sqrt[3]{31}$, I trace any prefactor (e.g. extracting $3$ from $\sqrt[3]{27\cdot\tfrac{31}{27}}$) so the bracketed quantity matches the expansion's base.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the bracket $a+bx$ (or $1+kx$) from part (a)'s expansion.
  2. Set $a+bx$ equal to the numerical target $T$ (after converting fractions to decimals if needed).
  3. Solve the linear equation: $x = (T-a)/b$.
  4. Verify $|x|$ (or $|kx|$) lies within the radius of convergence required by part (a).
  5. Substitute $x$ into the truncated polynomial $c_0 + c_1 x + c_2 x^2 + c_3 x^3$, computing each $x^k$ separately.
  6. Sum the terms keeping enough decimal places, then round only at the final step.
  7. If a prefactor was extracted (e.g. $3$ from $\sqrt[3]{27}$), multiply the polynomial value by it to recover the target approximation.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $(2+\tfrac{1}{8}x)^{13}\approx 8192+6656x+2496x^2+572x^3$, approximate $2.0125^{13}$. Set $2+\tfrac{1}{8}x=2.0125\Rightarrow x=0.1$. Substitute: $8192+6656(0.1)+2496(0.01)+572(0.001)=8192+665.6+24.96+0.572=8883.132$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Expansion is $(1+kx)^n$ with target near 1 (e.g. approximate $1.05^8$).
Set $1+kx=1.05$ and solve for $x$; same logic, simpler base.
Target is a surd like $\sqrt[3]{31}$ that doesn't directly match the bracket.
Extract a perfect-power prefactor: $\sqrt[3]{31}=3\sqrt[3]{31/27}$, then match $1+kx=31/27$, and multiply the resulting polynomial value by $3$.
Target is given as a fraction (e.g. $\tfrac{161}{80}$).
Convert to decimal first ($=2.0125$), then equate to the bracket as usual.
Negative or fractional index with target like $0.98^{-2}$.
Set $1+kx=0.98$ giving negative $x$; check $|kx|<1$ and watch sign care in odd-power terms.

Common Misreadings

  • Setting $x$ equal to the target directly (e.g. $x=2.0125$ or $x=1.15$), ignoring the constant in the bracket.
  • Setting $kx=T$ instead of $1+kx=T$, dropping the constant 1.
  • Forgetting a coefficient and writing $x=0.0125$ instead of $x=0.1$ when $\tfrac{1}{8}x=0.0125$.
  • Starting a fresh independent expansion of $(1-0.02)^{-2}$ rather than using the part-(a) expansion as instructed by 'hence'.

Pitfalls

  • Substituting $x=0.1$ but using $0.1$ instead of $0.01$ for the $x^2$ term (forgetting to square).
  • Rounding intermediate values prematurely, losing decimal-place accuracy in the final sum.
  • Sign errors when $x$ is negative: e.g. $-6\times(-\tfrac{1}{150})$ must be positive.
  • Forgetting to check the validity range, then using an $x$ outside $|kx|<1$ where the series diverges.
  • Forgetting the prefactor when the target was reached via extraction (e.g. omitting the factor of $3$ for $\sqrt[3]{31}$).

A-level Scope Notes

This sits inside Edexcel IAL P3/P4 binomial expansion (formula booklet entry for $(1+x)^n$). The validity condition $|kx|<1$ is required for non-positive-integer $n$. Avoid invoking Taylor's remainder theorem or general convergence arguments — those are out of scope; a brief sentence about the chosen $x$ being small suffices.
