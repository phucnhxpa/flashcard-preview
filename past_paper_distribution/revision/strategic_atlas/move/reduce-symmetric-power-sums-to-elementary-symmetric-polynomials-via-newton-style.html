Algebra & Functions

Reduce symmetric power sums to elementary symmetric polynomials via Newton-style identities

Family: Power Sum Reduction Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question gives a quadratic (or higher) polynomial and asks for a symmetric expression in its roots such as α² + β², α³ + β³, or 1/α + 1/β, without asking you to find the roots themselves. Vieta's formulas give you α + β and αβ directly from the coefficients, but the target expression is a higher power sum. Equivalently: you see a 'find α^k + β^k' part, possibly scaffolded after an earlier part that found a lower power sum. The roots may be irrational or complex, ruling out direct evaluation.

Motivation — Why this works

Every symmetric polynomial in α, β can be rewritten in terms of the elementary symmetric polynomials e₁ = α + β and e₂ = αβ, which Vieta's formulas extract from the coefficients. The key identities are α² + β² = (α+β)² − 2αβ and α³ + β³ = (α+β)(α² + β² − αβ) = (α+β)³ − 3αβ(α+β). This sidesteps the need to solve the polynomial — crucial when roots are complex conjugates or messy surds — and chains naturally: each higher power sum can be built from lower ones already computed.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the target is a symmetric expression in the roots (swapping α ↔ β leaves it unchanged), so by the fundamental theorem of symmetric polynomials it must be expressible in α + β and αβ.
  2. Rule out solving the quadratic directly: the discriminant may be negative or ugly, and even if not, substituting irrational/complex roots and simplifying is far more error-prone.
  3. Read off α + β and αβ from Vieta's: for ax² + bx + c = 0, α + β = −b/a and αβ = c/a.
  4. Commit to the identity matching the target: for squares use (α+β)² − 2αβ; for cubes use (α+β)(α²+β² − αβ), especially if α²+β² has just been computed in a previous part — the scaffolding signals reuse.
  5. Substitute the known values, taking care to use a common denominator before combining fractional terms.
  6. Sanity-check the sign: e.g. if the roots are complex conjugates, α² + β² can legitimately be negative.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. From the polynomial, extract e₁ = α + β and e₂ = αβ via Vieta's.
  2. Select the identity: α² + β² = e₁² − 2e₂, or α³ + β³ = e₁(α² + β²) − e₁ e₂ = e₁³ − 3e₁ e₂.
  3. Substitute numerical values for e₁ and e₂ (and any previously-found lower power sum).
  4. Convert all terms to a common denominator before adding/subtracting.
  5. Simplify to a single fraction or integer; verify the sign is plausible.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $2x^2 - 5x + 7 = 0$ with roots $\alpha, \beta$: Vieta gives $\alpha+\beta = 5/2$, $\alpha\beta = 7/2$. Then $\alpha^2+\beta^2 = (5/2)^2 - 2(7/2) = 25/4 - 28/4 = -3/4$. Chaining: $\alpha^3+\beta^3 = (\alpha+\beta)\big((\alpha^2+\beta^2) - \alpha\beta\big) = (5/2)(-3/4 - 7/2) = (5/2)(-17/4) = -85/8$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Three roots of a cubic (Further Maths)
Use α² + β² + γ² = (α+β+γ)² − 2(αβ+βγ+γα), and α³+β³+γ³ − 3αβγ = (α+β+γ)(α²+β²+γ² − αβ−βγ−γα).
Reciprocal power sums like 1/α + 1/β or 1/α² + 1/β²
Combine over a common denominator: 1/α + 1/β = (α+β)/(αβ); 1/α² + 1/β² = (α²+β²)/(αβ)². Same Vieta inputs, different surface.
Difference of roots is known instead of sum
Use α² + β² = (α − β)² + 2αβ as the alternative bridge.
Forming a new quadratic with roots α², β² or α³, β³
Use the same identities to compute the new sum and new product, then write x² − (new sum)x + (new product) = 0.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating α² + β² as if it equalled (α + β)² and forgetting the −2αβ correction.
  • Trying to solve the quadratic explicitly when the discriminant is negative, and getting stuck on complex arithmetic.
  • Confusing α² + β² with (α + β)² in the cube-sum bracket: the factor is (α² + β² − αβ), not (α² − αβ + β²) interpreted with α² alone.

Pitfalls

  • Fractional arithmetic errors: e.g. computing 25/4 − 7 without converting 7 to 28/4 first.
  • Sign error in Vieta's: for ax² + bx + c, α + β = −b/a (the minus is easily dropped).
  • In the cube identity, bracketing the substitution as ((α²+β²) − αβ) rather than α² + β² − αβ avoids treating α² as a single substitutable quantity.
  • Forgetting that the scaffolding from an earlier part is meant to be reused — re-deriving α²+β² wastes time and risks inconsistency.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Core Pure / Further Pure (roots of polynomials). The identities (α+β)² − 2αβ and (α+β)³ − 3αβ(α+β) are standard and not in the formula booklet — they are expected to be known. Avoid invoking general Newton's identities or symmetric function theory by name; the A-level expectation is the pattern for n = 2, 3 (and n = 2, 3 for three roots in Further Pure).
