Binomial Expansion

Apply generalised binomial expansion for non-positive-integer index

Family: Generalised Binomial Series Frequency: 6 questions

Trigger — When to use

The exponent n in (1+u)^n is negative, fractional, or both — so it is not a positive integer and the finite Pascal/nCr binomial theorem cannot apply (the sum would not terminate). Visual cues include square roots written as powers of 1/2, reciprocals written as negative powers, and explicit instructions to expand 'in ascending powers of x up to and including the term in x^k', which signals an infinite series truncated at a chosen order. Equivalent phrasings: 'find the first n terms of the binomial expansion', 'expand and state the range of validity', or a question giving (a + bx)^n with non-integer/negative n that has been pre-factored to (1+u)^n form.

Motivation — Why this works

For any real n with |u| < 1, the generalised binomial series (1+u)^n = 1 + nu + n(n−1)u²/2! + n(n−1)(n−2)u³/3! + … converges and gives a polynomial approximation to any required order. Each coefficient is the falling-factorial n(n−1)…(n−r+1)/r!, which makes sense for non-integer n where nCr would not. This is the only available expansion tool at A-level when n ∉ ℕ, and truncating after the term in x^k delivers exactly the required approximation.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the exponent is non-positive-integer (e.g. −1/2, 1/3, −8) and that the question asks for an ascending-powers expansion truncated at some x^k.
  2. Rule out the finite nCr / Pascal's-triangle binomial theorem (it requires n ∈ ℕ) and rule out direct multiplication for high negative powers.
  3. Commit to the generalised binomial series because it is the only A-level method that handles real n, producing precisely the polynomial approximation requested.
  4. Bridge to the first operation: rewrite the bracket in the standard form (1+u)^n, identifying both n and the substitution u (e.g. u = x/4, u = −5x/4, u = 2x/5), and note any external scalar to be multiplied at the end.
  5. Plan term-by-term: compute coefficients from the falling factorial (paying attention to signs of (n−1), (n−2), …), then raise u to each power carefully — each u^r contributes its own constant and its x^r factor.
  6. Anticipate the sign pattern: with n negative, the descending factors n, n−1, n−2 are all negative, so successive products alternate in sign; with u itself negative, an additional sign flip occurs at every odd power.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the target as (1+u)^n with n and u explicitly identified; pull out any scalar prefactor.
  2. Compute the r=0 term: 1.
  3. Compute the r=1 term: n · u.
  4. Compute the r=2 term: n(n−1)/2! · u² (e.g. for n = −1/2: (−1/2)(−3/2)/2 = 3/8).
  5. Compute the r=3 term: n(n−1)(n−2)/3! · u³ (e.g. for n = −1/2: (−1/2)(−3/2)(−5/2)/6 = −5/16).
  6. Substitute u and expand each u^r fully (e.g. (x/4)³ = x³/64; (−5x/4)² = 25x²/16, the sign squared away).
  7. Collect terms in ascending powers of x and multiply through by any external scalar to obtain the final approximation.
  8. If asked, state the range of validity |u| < 1, i.e. |x| < 1/|coefficient of x in u|.

Worked Micro-Example

Expand (1+\tfrac{x}{4})^{-1/2} up to x³.\n Term 1: 1. Term 2: (-\tfrac{1}{2})(\tfrac{x}{4}) = -\tfrac{x}{8}.\n Term 3: \tfrac{(-1/2)(-3/2)}{2!}(\tfrac{x}{4})^2 = \tfrac{3}{8}\cdot\tfrac{x^2}{16} = \tfrac{3x^2}{128}.\n Term 4: \tfrac{(-1/2)(-3/2)(-5/2)}{3!}(\tfrac{x}{4})^3 = -\tfrac{5}{16}\cdot\tfrac{x^3}{64} = -\tfrac{5x^3}{1024}.\n Result: 1 - \tfrac{x}{8} + \tfrac{3x^2}{128} - \tfrac{5x^3}{1024}, valid for |x|<4.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Positive fractional index, e.g. (1+u)^{1/3} or (1+u)^{1/2}
Same falling-factorial formula; sign pattern alternates from the second term onward because (n−1), (n−2), … become negative once n < 1.
Negative integer index, e.g. (1+u)^{-8} or the geometric (1+u)^{-1}
Falling factorial still applies; all factors n, n−1, n−2,… are negative, so signs alternate cleanly. (1+u)^{-1} reduces to the standard geometric series 1 − u + u² − …
Bracket of form (a+bx)^n with a ≠ 1
Factorise to a^n(1+\tfrac{bx}{a})^n first, then apply the generalised expansion to (1+\tfrac{bx}{a})^n; range of validity becomes |bx/a|<1.
Bracket with a minus sign, (1−u)^n
Substitute u → −u; even powers keep their sign, odd powers flip — track signs carefully when u itself contains a minus.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating n as a positive integer and using \binom{n}{r} (Pascal/nCr), which loses the negative signs from the falling factorial.
  • Confusing the truncation order: 'up to x²' means three terms (constant, x, x²), not two.
  • Failing to factor a non-unit constant from (a+bx)^n before expanding, which makes the |u|<1 condition wrong.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error in the falling factorial: with n = −1/2, n−1 = −3/2 (not +1/2); with n = −8, (−8)(−9)(−10) = −720 (not +720).
  • Forgetting to raise the whole substitution u to its power: (2x/5)³ = 8x³/125, not 2x³/5; (−5x/4)² = +25x²/16, not −25x²/16.
  • Omitting r! in the denominator (especially the 2! in the x² term).
  • Forgetting to multiply through by an external scalar (e.g. the 1/2 from a √-prefactor) at the end.
  • Not stating, or stating incorrectly, the range of validity |u|<1 when the question asks for it.

A-level Scope Notes

This is the generalised binomial series in the Edexcel IAL P3 formula booklet: (1+x)^n = 1 + nx + n(n−1)x²/2! + … valid for |x|<1, n∈ℝ. Stay within real n and do not invoke complex-analytic continuation, Gamma-function generalisations, or convergence at the boundary |x|=1 — these are out of scope.
