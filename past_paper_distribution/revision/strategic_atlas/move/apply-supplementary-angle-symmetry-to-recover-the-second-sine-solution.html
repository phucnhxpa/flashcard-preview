Trigonometry

Apply supplementary-angle symmetry to recover the second sine solution

Family: Cast Diagram Analysis Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

After reducing a trig equation to the form sin(θ) = k with |k| ≤ 1 (where θ may itself be 2x, x + α, or some shifted argument), the calculator's arcsin returns only ONE value — the principal value in Q1 (if k > 0) or Q4 (if k < 0). The trigger to apply this move is: you have a sine equation, you have one solution from arcsin, and the working range for θ contains a second quadrant where sine has the same sign. Visual cues include 'find ALL solutions in [...]', a range that spans 180° or more in θ, or a question worth more marks than a single arcsin would justify.

Motivation — Why this works

Sine is many-to-one: the identity sin(180° − α) = sin α means every value k ∈ (−1, 1) is hit by exactly two angles per 360° cycle. The principal arcsin value gives one; the supplement 180° − α gives the other. This is the sine-specific manifestation of CAST symmetry (sine is positive in Q1 and Q2, negative in Q3 and Q4). Missing the supplementary solution is the single most common error on these questions and typically halves the available marks.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the equation has been reduced to sin(θ) = k for some k with |k| ≤ 1, and the question asks for ALL solutions in a stated range — so one value from the calculator is unlikely to suffice.
  2. I rule out solving by squaring or by converting to cosine — those introduce extraneous roots or change the problem. I also rule out simply listing the calculator value: the CAST diagram tells me sine takes each value twice per revolution.
  3. I commit to the supplementary-angle method because sin(180° − α) = sin α is the cleanest way to generate the second base solution; any extra solutions in the range will be ±360° copies of these two.
  4. Bridge: I compute α = arcsin(k) on the calculator (keeping full precision), then form 180° − α as the Q2 (or Q3 if k < 0) partner. If k < 0 and α comes out negative, I add 360° to bring it into [0°, 360°).
  5. Tactical decision: I keep θ-values unrounded until AFTER any final transformation back to x (e.g. dividing by 2, subtracting a phase shift), to avoid compounding rounding error. I then check each candidate against the original range for x.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute the principal value: α = arcsin(k) (store full precision).
  2. Form the supplementary partner: β = 180° − α.
  3. If k < 0 and α < 0, replace α with α + 360° so it lies in [0°, 360°).
  4. If the working variable is θ = nx + φ, list ALL θ-values of the form α + 360°m and β + 360°m that fall in the θ-range determined by the x-range.
  5. Invert the substitution: x = (θ − φ)/n for each surviving θ.
  6. Verify each x lies in the given range and round only at the final step.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve sin(2x) = 1/3 for x ∈ [0°, 90°]. Then 2x ∈ [0°, 180°]. Principal: 2x = arcsin(1/3) ≈ 19.4712°. Supplement: 2x = 180° − 19.4712° ≈ 160.5288°. Both lie in [0°, 180°] ✓. Halving: x ≈ 9.74° and x ≈ 80.26° (2 d.p.).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cosine equation cos(θ) = k over a range spanning Q1 and Q4
Use the symmetry cos(360° − α) = cos α instead; the partner is 360° − α, not 180° − α.
Tangent equation tan(θ) = k
Solutions are 180° apart, not supplementary: the partner is α + 180°.
sin(θ) = k with k < 0
arcsin returns a negative α (Q4 reference); the two solutions in [0°, 360°) are 360° + α (Q4) and 180° − α (Q3). Same supplementary identity, shifted by a period.
Argument is shifted, e.g. sin(x + 53.13°) = 0.4
Solve for the shifted variable y = x + 53.13° using α and 180° − α, then subtract the phase at the end. Adjust the y-range accordingly.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating sin(θ) = k as if it had a unique solution and reporting only the calculator value.
  • Confusing the sine partner 180° − α with the cosine partner 360° − α.
  • Forgetting that when the argument is 2x (or nx), the working range for the argument is n times wider, so MORE than two θ-solutions may be needed.

Pitfalls

  • Rounding α before forming 180° − α or before inverting a substitution — this compounds error and can push answers outside the 1 d.p. tolerance.
  • When k < 0, discarding the negative principal value instead of adding 360° to bring it into the required range.
  • Forgetting to undo the substitution at the end (e.g. failing to divide by 2 to get from 2x back to x).
  • Failing to enumerate ±360° periodic copies when the θ-range exceeds 360°.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P2/P3 trigonometry. Uses the identity sin(180° − θ) = sin θ (standard, no formula booklet entry needed). Do NOT generalise to complex-valued arcsin or to ranges beyond the stated interval — A-level expects only real solutions in the explicit domain given.
