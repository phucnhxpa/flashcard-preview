Algebra & Functions

Factor mixed-degree symmetric numerator by grouping into (p+q)(1+pq)

Family: Symmetric Polynomial Reduction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in FP1/FP2 'roots of a quadratic' problems when, after combining fractions like 1/α + 1/β or β/α + α/β with extra terms, the numerator contains a mix of degree-1 and degree-3 monomials in the two roots. Concrete cue: terms of the form p + q sitting alongside p²q + pq². Equivalent phrasings include 'numerator has p, q, p²q, pq²' or 'I have a sum plus a product-weighted sum'. Whenever every term carries a factor of p or q and the leftover factors themselves form (p+q), grouping is on the table.

Motivation — Why this works

The whole roots-of-a-quadratic toolkit relies on expressing target quantities purely through the elementary symmetric polynomials s = p+q and t = pq, since these are read directly from the coefficients via Vieta. The identity p + q + p²q + pq² = (p+q) + pq(p+q) = (p+q)(1+pq) converts a mixed-degree expression into a product of two known quantities in one step. This avoids ever solving for p and q individually (which may be irrational or complex) and avoids the detour through p²+q² = (p+q)² − 2pq. It is the cleanest realisation of the chapter's guiding principle: stay symmetric, stay elementary.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the numerator splits naturally: two linear terms p + q, and two cubic terms pq² + qp² that share a pq factor.
  2. Rule out brute expansion or substituting numerical values for p and q individually — the discriminant may be negative or messy, and the question is clearly designed for symmetric reduction.
  3. Commit to grouping because each cubic term contains pq, and what remains inside is exactly q + p — the same group as the linear pair, signalling a common factor.
  4. Bridge to operation: rewrite as (p+q) + pq(p+q), then factor out (p+q) to get (p+q)(1+pq).
  5. Substitute s = p+q and t = pq from Vieta and evaluate; double-check sign of t since pq = c/a can flip sign easily.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the combined numerator: p + q + p²q + pq².
  2. Group as (p + q) + pq(p + q).
  3. Factor out the common (p + q): (p + q)(1 + pq).
  4. Read s = p+q and t = pq from the original quadratic via Vieta.
  5. Substitute and simplify the resulting numeric product, divided by whatever denominator (typically pq) was produced when fractions were combined.

Worked Micro-Example

Given p,q are roots of $x^2 - 2x + 5/2 = 0$, so $p+q=2$, $pq=5/2$. Evaluate $\tfrac{1}{p}+\tfrac{1}{q}+p+q$. Combining: $\tfrac{p+q+p^2q+pq^2}{pq} = \tfrac{(p+q)(1+pq)}{pq} = \tfrac{2\cdot(1+5/2)}{5/2} = \tfrac{2\cdot 7/2}{5/2} = \tfrac{14}{5}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Numerator p³ + q³ appears (e.g. evaluating α³+β³)
Use the identity p³+q³ = (p+q)³ − 3pq(p+q) instead of grouping; same philosophy, different symmetric identity.
Numerator p²q + pq² alone
Factor directly as pq(p+q); no second grouping needed.
Expression α²β + αβ² + α + β with a constant attached
Same grouping (α+β)(1+αβ) plus the constant; structure is invariant under renaming roots.
Forming a new quadratic with roots like α + 1/α, β + 1/β
Sum of new roots reduces to (α+β)(1+αβ)/(αβ); product handled separately — the grouping move supplies the sum cleanly.

Common Misreadings

  • Expanding everything and trying to 'somehow' recombine, which often forces an unnecessary detour through p²+q² = (p+q)² − 2pq.
  • Treating the cubic terms p²q + pq² as needing a cube identity (like p³+q³) when they actually only need a single pq factored out.
  • Believing the expression cannot be evaluated because p and q are irrational/complex, missing that only s and t are required.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to spot the shared (p+q) factor across the two groups and abandoning the line of attack.
  • Sign slip on pq when the constant term is negative (pq = c/a, not −c/a).
  • Forgetting to divide by the denominator pq that was created when fractions were combined.
  • Writing pq(p+q) as p²q + pq² incorrectly when reversing — keep symmetry visible.

A-level Scope Notes

Sits squarely in Edexcel IAL Further Pure FP1 'Roots of quadratic equations', using only Vieta's relations s = −b/a, t = c/a and elementary factoring; no techniques beyond A-level Further Maths are invoked.
