Integration

Split polynomial-over-single-power fraction into term-by-term powers

Family: Algebraic Separation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integrand is a sum/difference of polynomial terms divided by a single power of the variable, e.g. $(81-u)/\sqrt{u}$, $(3x^2+x)/\sqrt{x}$, or $(x^3-2x)/x^2$. The denominator is a single monomial — not a product of distinct factors — so each numerator term divided by it yields a clean power $u^n$. This pattern often appears after a substitution has already simplified the integral, or directly in textbook integrals designed for the power rule.

Motivation — Why this works

Division distributes over a sum: $(A+B)/C = A/C + B/C$, and $u^a/u^b = u^{a-b}$. So a single awkward quotient becomes a sum of standard power terms, each integrable by $\int u^n\,du = u^{n+1}/(n+1)$ for $n\neq -1$. This is strictly simpler than substitution or partial fractions and uses no machinery beyond index laws and the power rule.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand is a polynomial numerator over a single power of $u$ (a monomial denominator), not a product of distinct factors.
  2. Rule out partial fractions (no distinct linear factors) and further substitution (would just relabel the same power-rule task).
  3. Commit to algebraic splitting because index laws turn each term into a standard $u^n$ directly integrable by the power rule.
  4. Bridge: rewrite $(81-u)/\sqrt{u} = 81u^{-1/2} - u^{1/2}$ using $u^a/u^b = u^{a-b}$.
  5. Tactically, integrate each power term separately, then combine and apply any outer constant or limits at the end.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the denominator as a single power: $\sqrt{u} = u^{1/2}$.
  2. Distribute the division: $(81-u)/u^{1/2} = 81u^{-1/2} - u^{1/2}$.
  3. Apply the power rule term by term: $\int 81u^{-1/2}\,du = 162u^{1/2}$ and $\int u^{1/2}\,du = \tfrac{2}{3}u^{3/2}$.
  4. Combine into an antiderivative $F(u) = 162u^{1/2} - \tfrac{2}{3}u^{3/2}$.
  5. Substitute limits: at $u=81$, $F = 162(9) - \tfrac{2}{3}(729) = 1458 - 486 = 972$; at $u=0$, $F=0$.
  6. Apply any outer constant factor (here $1/512$) to the final value: $972/512 = 243/128$.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int_1^4 \frac{x^2-3}{\sqrt{x}}\,dx$. Split: $\frac{x^2-3}{x^{1/2}} = x^{3/2} - 3x^{-1/2}$. Integrate: $\tfrac{2}{5}x^{5/2} - 6x^{1/2}$. At $x=4$: $\tfrac{2}{5}(32) - 6(2) = \tfrac{64}{5} - 12 = \tfrac{4}{5}$. At $x=1$: $\tfrac{2}{5} - 6 = -\tfrac{28}{5}$. Difference: $\tfrac{4}{5} - (-\tfrac{28}{5}) = \tfrac{32}{5}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$\int (3x^2+x)/\sqrt{x}\,dx$
Split into $3x^{3/2} + x^{1/2}$ and apply the power rule term by term.
$\int (x^3-2x+1)/x^2\,dx$
Split into $x - 2x^{-1} + x^{-2}$; note the $x^{-1}$ term integrates to $\ln|x|$, not a power.
After substitution leaves $\int (a-u)/u^{1/2}\,du$
Same split: $au^{-1/2} - u^{1/2}$, then integrate and back-substitute or apply transformed limits.
Cube-root denominators, e.g. $\int (x+1)/x^{1/3}\,dx$
Use $x^a/x^{1/3} = x^{a-1/3}$ to get $x^{2/3} + x^{-1/3}$.

Common Misreadings

  • Attempting partial fractions when the denominator is a single power, not a product of distinct factors.
  • Trying a fresh substitution like $v=\sqrt{u}$, which only relabels the same power-rule problem.
  • Treating $(A+B)/C$ as if it equals $A/C \cdot B/C$ or otherwise mishandling the distribution.

Pitfalls

  • Index errors: $\int u^{-1/2}\,du = 2u^{1/2}$, not $\tfrac{1}{2}u^{1/2}$ or $-2u^{-3/2}$.
  • Arithmetic on fractional powers: $81^{1/2}=9$ and $81^{3/2}=729$; mistakes here propagate.
  • Forgetting an outer constant factor (e.g. the $1/512$) when combining at the end.
  • Missing the special case $n=-1$, where $\int u^{-1}\,du = \ln|u|$ rather than a power.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure (P2/P3) integration: index laws and the power rule $\int x^n\,dx = x^{n+1}/(n+1)$ for $n\neq -1$, applied to definite or indefinite integrals.
