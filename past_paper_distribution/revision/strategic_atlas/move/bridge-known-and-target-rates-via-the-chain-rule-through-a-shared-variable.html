Differentiation

Bridge known and target rates via the chain rule through a shared variable

Family: Related Rates Chain Rule Bridge Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question gives one rate of change with respect to time (e.g. dV/dt, dA/dt) and asks for a different rate of change with respect to time (e.g. dh/dt, dV/dt), while a derivative linking the two quantities (e.g. dV/dh, dV/dA) is either already available or easy to compute from a given formula. Visual cues: phrasing like 'water poured at a constant rate', 'expanding/shrinking at...', or a multi-part question where part (a) or (b) asks for dV/dh or dV/dA and the next part gives a time-rate. The key signal is that no direct formula links the two variables to t, but both are linked through a shared geometric variable.

Motivation — Why this works

The chain rule dY/dt = (dY/dX)(dX/dt) factorises a time rate into a geometric sensitivity (how Y responds to changes in X, fixed by the shape) and a kinematic rate (how fast X itself changes in time). Since one of these factors is given and the other can be computed from the geometric formula, the unknown rate is isolated by a single multiplication or division — no ODE need be solved. This is the universal mechanism for related-rates problems: it turns an apparently dynamic problem into a static evaluation at one instant.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice I am given one time-derivative (e.g. dV/dt) and asked for another (e.g. dh/dt), but the formula I have links V to h, not to t.
  2. I rule out trying to find h(t) explicitly by solving the ODE dV/dt = f(h)·dh/dt — that is a separable ODE and far more work than needed for a single instantaneous rate.
  3. I rule out implicit differentiation of the expanded polynomial in t — it works but reproduces exactly what the chain rule gives, less cleanly.
  4. I commit to the chain rule dV/dt = (dV/dh)(dh/dt) because it links all three quantities in a single algebraic identity, and I already have (or can quickly compute) dV/dh.
  5. Tactical choice: if an earlier part of the question has computed dV/dh or dV/dA, I use that intermediate variable rather than going via dx/dt — the question is structured to feed forward.
  6. I evaluate dV/dh at the specified instant (e.g. h = 5), then rearrange dh/dt = (dV/dt)/(dV/dh) to isolate the unknown, and finally simplify and attach units.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two time-rates (one given, one required) and the shared geometric variable X linking them.
  2. Write the chain rule explicitly: dY/dt = (dY/dX) · (dX/dt).
  3. Compute or quote dY/dX from the given formula (or from a previous part of the question).
  4. Substitute the specified instantaneous value of X into dY/dX to get a number.
  5. Rearrange: if the unknown is dX/dt, divide; if the unknown is dY/dt, multiply.
  6. Simplify the resulting fraction (cancel common factors) and attach correct units (e.g. cm s⁻¹, cm³ s⁻¹).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $V = \tfrac{1}{3}h^3 + \tfrac{4}{3}h^2$ and $\tfrac{dV}{dt} = 160$, find $\tfrac{dh}{dt}$ at $h=5$. Compute $\tfrac{dV}{dh} = h^2 + \tfrac{8}{3}h$, so at $h=5$: $\tfrac{dV}{dh} = 25 + \tfrac{40}{3} = \tfrac{115}{3}$. Then $\tfrac{dh}{dt} = \tfrac{dV/dt}{dV/dh} = \tfrac{160}{115/3} = \tfrac{480}{115} = \tfrac{96}{23} \approx 4.17 \text{ cm s}^{-1}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Reverse direction: given dh/dt, asked for dV/dt
No rearrangement — multiply directly: dV/dt = (dV/dh)(dh/dt).
Cone or other 3D shape with two geometric variables (e.g. r and h)
Eliminate one variable using a similar-triangles or geometric constraint before computing dV/dh.
Earlier part of the question pre-computes dV/dA (not dV/dx)
Use dV/dt = (dV/dA)(dA/dt) directly — the examiner has signalled this bridge by structuring the parts; do not detour through dx/dt.
Surface area or perimeter rates
Replace V with A or P; identical chain-rule structure applies.

Common Misreadings

  • Inverting the chain rule: writing dh/dt = (dV/dh)(dV/dt) instead of dividing by dV/dh.
  • Ignoring the result of an earlier part and re-deriving everything from scratch via dx/dt, wasting time and risking error.
  • Using dA/dV instead of dV/dA when chaining — i.e. inverting which derivative belongs in the numerator.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to evaluate dV/dh at the specific instant (e.g. h = 5), leaving the answer as a function of h.
  • Dividing incorrectly: must multiply by the reciprocal (e.g. multiply by 3/115, not by 115/3).
  • Failing to simplify the final fraction by common factors (e.g. 480/115 = 96/23).
  • Rounding intermediate surd or decimal values too early, producing an inaccurate final answer at the required significant figures.
  • Omitting units on the final rate — Edexcel mark schemes often require cm s⁻¹ or cm³ s⁻¹ for the final mark.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 (related rates via chain rule) and FP differentiation. The chain rule dy/dt = (dy/dx)(dx/dt) is in the formula booklet implicitly under differentiation rules. Avoid generalising to partial derivatives or multivariable chain rules — IAL treats only single-variable chains, with any extra variable eliminated geometrically before differentiating.
