Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a required line is known to pass through the origin O(0,0) — either stated explicitly or implied (e.g. 'line through O and M', 'OA produced', a chord/diameter from O on a circle centred elsewhere). The cue is: one of the two defining points is (0,0). Equivalently, any phrasing that forces the y-intercept to be zero.

Motivation — Why this works

A line through the origin satisfies y = mx + c with c = 0, collapsing the equation to y = mx. This removes one unknown and avoids carrying a constant through subsequent substitutions (e.g. into a curve equation), giving cleaner quadratics with fewer arithmetic slips. It is exactly the point-gradient form y − 0 = m(x − 0) with the most convenient base point chosen.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that one of the two points defining the line is the origin, so the y-intercept is forced to be 0.
  2. Rule out the general y = mx + c approach and the heavier point-gradient form anchored at the non-origin point — both introduce avoidable algebra.
  3. Commit to y = mx because only the gradient is unknown, and any later substitution into a curve will be tidier.
  4. Compute the gradient m = (y₂ − 0)/(x₂ − 0) = y₂/x₂ using the non-origin point.
  5. Write the line directly as y = mx and, if a fractional gradient is unwieldy, keep it as a fraction to avoid rounding inside later algebra.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Verify the line passes through O(0,0).
  2. Take the other known point M(x₁, y₁) and compute m = y₁/x₁.
  3. Write the line as y = mx (no constant term).
  4. If gradient is a decimal, also keep the fraction form for use in substitutions.

Worked Micro-Example

Line $l$ passes through $O(0,0)$ and $M(2.5,\,6.75)$. Since $c=0$, take $m=\dfrac{6.75}{2.5}=\dfrac{27}{10}=2.7$. Hence $l:\ y=2.7x$, or equivalently $y=\tfrac{27}{10}x$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line through origin and a point given in surd/fraction form, e.g. $(\sqrt{3},3)$
Compute $m = 3/\sqrt{3} = \sqrt{3}$ and write $y = \sqrt{3}\,x$ directly.
Tangent or chord from O to a curve $y = f(x)$
Substitute $y = mx$ into $f(x)$; the resulting equation in $x$ has no stray constant from the line, simplifying discriminant or factor work.
Perpendicular from O to a given line $y = m_1 x + c$
The perpendicular still passes through O, so write it as $y = -\tfrac{1}{m_1}x$ with no intercept to find.
Line through origin with a given angle $\theta$ to the x-axis
Use $m = \tan\theta$ and write $y = (\tan\theta)x$ directly.
Line through any other fixed point $(x_1,y_1)\neq O$
Shortcut does not apply; revert to $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ keeping the constant.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming any line with a small intercept is 'close enough' to pass through the origin and dropping c without justification.
  • Confusing 'passes through the origin' with 'has gradient through the origin', and writing x = my or similar.
  • Believing the shortcut requires the non-origin point to have integer coordinates.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips when dividing decimals; converting to fractions (e.g. $6.75/2.5 = 27/10$) is safer.
  • Forgetting to check the origin is genuinely on the line before discarding the constant.
  • Writing $y = mx + c$ and then setting $c = 0$ at the end — wastes time and risks carrying $c$ into substitutions.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P1 coordinate geometry: straight-line equations, gradients, and substitution into curves. No content outside the specification.
