Algebra & Functions

Factor cubic as known quadratic times unknown linear and equate coefficients

Family: Equating Coefficients Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a polynomial (typically cubic) with one or two unknown real coefficients is known to have a specific quadratic factor — often arising because a complex root and its conjugate have been identified, giving a real quadratic factor via sum/product of roots. Phrasings include 'given that z₁ is a root, find a and b', 'show that (z² + pz + q) is a factor', or 'hence find the third root'. The cue is: degree of polynomial minus degree of known factor = 1 (or small), so the missing factor is easy to parametrise. Use whenever substituting a complex root would force you to cube or take higher powers of a complex number.

Motivation — Why this works

If f(z) is cubic with leading coefficient A and (z² + pz + q) is a factor, then f(z) ≡ (z² + pz + q)(kz − m) for some real k, m. Expanding gives a cubic whose coefficients are linear in k and m, so equating with the given coefficients yields a triangular linear system solved by back-substitution. This keeps every line of arithmetic real, avoiding the error-prone cubing of (a + bi). It is the polynomial-identity manifestation of dividing f(z) by the known quadratic.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices a cubic with unknown coefficients plus a known (or easily constructed) real quadratic factor — the degree gap to the cubic is exactly 1.
  2. Rules out direct substitution of the complex root into f(z) = 0, since that requires computing z³ for z = −1 − 3i and then separating real and imaginary parts.
  3. Commits to writing f(z) ≡ (quadratic)(linear) because all unknowns then appear linearly in real coefficients of an identity in z.
  4. Bridges to the first operation by parametrising the linear factor as (kz − m), not (z − m), since the leading coefficient of f need not be 1.
  5. Tactically matches coefficients top-down: highest power pins k immediately, next power pins m, and the remaining coefficients read off the unknowns a, b directly without solving a system.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write f(z) ≡ (z² + 2z + 10)(kz − m) as a polynomial identity in z.
  2. Compare z³ coefficients: leading coefficient of f equals k, giving k = 2.
  3. Expand fully: (z² + 2z + 10)(2z − m) = 2z³ + (4 − m)z² + (20 − 2m)z − 10m.
  4. Compare z² coefficients with f to solve for m (here 4 − m = −1, so m = 5).
  5. Read off the z¹ coefficient to get a = 20 − 2m.
  6. Read off the constant term to get b = −10m.
  7. Optional check: the third root of f is m/k; verify by substitution or by Vieta on f.

Worked Micro-Example

Let $f(z) = 2z^3 - z^2 + az + b$ have $z^2 + 2z + 10$ as a factor. Write $f(z) \equiv (z^2 + 2z + 10)(kz - m)$. Comparing $z^3$: $k = 2$. Expanding gives $2z^3 + (4-m)z^2 + (20-2m)z - 10m$, so $4 - m = -1 \Rightarrow m = 5$. Hence $a = 20 - 10 = 10$ and $b = -50$, and the third root is $m/k = \tfrac{5}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quartic with a known real quadratic factor
Write f(z) ≡ (known quadratic)(αz² + βz + γ) and compare four coefficients to solve for α, β, γ and any unknowns in f.
Cubic with a known linear factor (z − α)
Write f(z) ≡ (z − α)(Az² + Bz + C); compare coefficients or use synthetic division to find A, B, C.
Quartic given a single complex root
First form the real quadratic (z − z₁)(z − z̄₁) = z² − 2Re(z₁)z + |z₁|², then apply the quadratic-times-quadratic version.
Polynomial long division as an alternative mechanic
Divide f(z) by the known quadratic; the quotient is the unknown factor and the remainder must vanish, giving the same equations.
Partial-fraction setup with a repeated/irreducible quadratic denominator
Same coefficient-matching idea: clear denominators and equate coefficients of each power of the variable to solve for the unknown numerator constants.

Common Misreadings

  • Taking the unknown linear factor as (z − m) and forcing the leading coefficient of f to be 1, contradicting a leading coefficient like 2.
  • Treating the quadratic factor as only providing one root rather than two, then trying to substitute one complex value and lose the conjugate information.
  • Reading m as the third root rather than as m/k when k ≠ 1.
  • Defaulting to substituting z = −1 − 3i into f(z) = 0, which is valid but forces complex cubing and conjugate separation.

Pitfalls

  • Sign slips in the −m(z² + 2z + 10) distribution when expanding (kz − m)(z² + 2z + 10).
  • Forgetting to compare every power: skipping the z¹ or constant comparison can leave a or b undetermined or unchecked.
  • Mislabelling which coefficient of f corresponds to which power, especially when f is written with terms out of standard order.
  • Confusing the parameter m with the third root (third root is m/k).
  • Using ≡ and = interchangeably without recognising this is a polynomial identity that must hold for all z.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL scope: the Factor Theorem and polynomial identities (Pure/Core) combined with complex conjugate root pairs for real-coefficient polynomials (Further Pure 1). No tools beyond expansion, equating coefficients, and basic complex conjugates are required.
