Sequences & Series

Substitute a chosen x-value into the truncated series to obtain a numerical approximation

Family: Series Approximation Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires on follow-up parts using the word 'hence' or 'use your series/expansion from part (a)' to estimate a function value, a definite integral, or evaluate a limit. Visual cues: a specific small x-value is given (e.g. x = 0.1), or the student is asked to choose x so the LHS becomes a known constant (e.g. ln(3/2), √(1.02), an estimate of π or e). Also fires when an expression like ∫₀^c f(x) dx must be approximated, or when a 0/0 limit involving f(x) appears. Equivalent phrasings: 'find an approximate value of...', 'estimate to 4 d.p.', 'deduce that f(c) ≈ ...'.

Motivation — Why this works

A truncated Maclaurin/Taylor series is a polynomial that approximates the original function near the expansion point; for x within the radius of convergence (and especially small x), higher-order terms are negligible. Substituting a numerical x converts the abstract polynomial into a concrete decimal estimate — this is the entire purpose of constructing the series in applied questions. For limits, replacing f(x) by its series turns indeterminate 0/0 forms into ratios of polynomials whose lowest powers of x cancel cleanly. The principle invoked is local polynomial approximation: f(x) = Pₙ(x) + O(xⁿ⁺¹).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the word 'hence' or 'use your expansion' — this signals I must use the truncated series from the previous part, not the original function (using f directly forfeits method marks).
  2. I rule out direct evaluation (calculator on f(c)) and rule out re-expanding from scratch — the series is already in hand.
  3. I commit to substitution because the requested x-value is small (or can be chosen small) and lies inside the validity interval, so the truncated polynomial gives accuracy to the required d.p.
  4. I decide which x to use: if given, I just substitute; if I must choose, I pick x so the LHS of the expansion equals the target constant (e.g. set 1+x = 3/2 ⇒ x = 1/2 to estimate ln(3/2)).
  5. I check x lies in the interval of convergence (e.g. |x| < 1 for ln(1+x), (1+x)ⁿ with non-integer n) and confirm enough terms were retained for the requested accuracy — if a limit comes out as 0/0 even after substitution of the series, I needed one more term.
  6. I substitute term-by-term, keeping extra decimal places in intermediate work, then round only at the end.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write down the truncated series from part (a), e.g. f(x) ≈ a₀ + a₁x + a₂x² + a₃x³.
  2. Identify or deduce the substitution value x = c (given, or chosen so LHS = target constant).
  3. Verify c lies inside the interval of convergence; state this if the mark scheme expects it.
  4. Replace x by c in every term: compute a₀, a₁c, a₂c², a₃c³ as exact fractions or to at least 2 more d.p. than required.
  5. Sum the terms to get the approximation.
  6. (For integrals) Integrate the polynomial term-by-term first, then evaluate between the limits.
  7. (For limits) Substitute the series into the full expression, factor out the lowest power of x from numerator and denominator, cancel, then let x → 0.
  8. Round the final answer to the specified number of decimal places or significant figures.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\ln(1+x) \approx x - \tfrac{x^2}{2} + \tfrac{x^3}{3}$, estimate $\ln(1.1)$. Substitute $x = 0.1$: $0.1 - \tfrac{0.01}{2} + \tfrac{0.001}{3} = 0.1 - 0.005 + 0.000333\ldots = 0.09533$ (5 d.p.). True value $0.09531$ — accurate to 4 d.p.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Choosing x so the LHS becomes a known irrational (e.g. ln(3/2), √(1.02))
Solve LHS = target for x, check |x| < radius of convergence, then substitute as normal.
Approximating a definite integral ∫₀^c f(x) dx
Integrate the truncated polynomial term-by-term first to get a new polynomial in c, then evaluate at the upper limit.
Evaluating a limit as x → 0 of an expression involving f(x)
Replace each function by its series, simplify numerator and denominator as polynomials, cancel the common lowest power of x, then set x = 0.
Finding the first non-zero term of f(x) − g(x) (order of zero)
Subtract series term-by-term; the lowest surviving power gives the order.

Common Misreadings

  • Using f(x) directly on a calculator when the question says 'hence' — this loses all method marks.
  • Thinking 'more terms = more marks' — only the terms required for the stated accuracy or to resolve the limit are needed.
  • Mistaking a 'show that' for an 'estimate' — if exact, do not round.

Pitfalls

  • Substituting an x outside the interval of convergence (e.g. x = 1 in a series valid only for |x| < 1) — gives a meaningless answer.
  • Rounding intermediate terms too aggressively, accumulating error in the final decimal.
  • Arithmetic slips when raising decimals to powers (e.g. 0.1³ = 0.001, not 0.01).
  • In limits: cancelling x from numerator and denominator when one side still has a non-zero constant term.
  • Retaining too few terms — if all terms cancel in a limit, the expansion in part (a) was too short.

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) Maclaurin/Taylor series scope. Standard expansions for eˣ, ln(1+x), sin x, cos x, (1+x)ⁿ are in the formula booklet with their validity intervals. Do NOT invoke Lagrange remainder bounds or rigorous error estimates — these are out of scope; just retain enough terms to meet the stated accuracy.
