Trigger — When to use

Fires when a Möbius (or rational) transformation w = f(z) is applied to a locus where z depends on a single real parameter (e.g. z = x + i, z = t + ti, or Im(z) = 0), giving u = Re(w) and v = Im(w) as two real rational functions of one real parameter. The question asks for the Cartesian equation of the image in the w-plane, typically a line ax + by + c = 0 or a circle (u−h)² + (v−k)² = r². Recognise the cue: 'find the equation of the image', 'show that the image lies on …', or 'show that w lies on a circle/line'. Both u and v sharing a common denominator is a strong structural signal.

Motivation — Why this works

Under a Möbius transformation, lines and circles map to lines or circles, so the implicit Cartesian relation in (u, v) must be linear or quadratic of the form u² + v² + Du + Ev + F = 0. Eliminating the real parameter converts the parametric description into this implicit form. The mechanic exploits the matched denominators of u and v: for a line image, u and v are linearly dependent through the parameter, so a clever rearrangement (often partial fractions) gives au + bv = c directly; for a circle image, u² + v² has a squared denominator that cancels against a linear combination αu + βv to produce u² + v² = αu + βv + γ.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that u and v are both rational functions of the same real parameter (call it t), and they share a common denominator — this is the structural signal that elimination will be clean.
  2. Rule out blunt substitution: solving one equation for t (e.g. via the quadratic formula) and substituting into the other typically introduces square roots and is messy.
  3. Decide on the target shape from Möbius theory: if the pre-image is a line/circle through the pole of f, expect a line in w; otherwise expect a circle. This dictates whether to seek au + bv = c or u² + v² = αu + βv + γ.
  4. Bridge to operation: for a line target, solve the simpler of u, v for the denominator (or 1/(t − a)) and substitute, often after rewriting the other via partial fractions like (t+1)/(t−1) = 1 + 2/(t−1). For a circle target, compute u² + v² and look for a linear combination αu + βv whose denominator structure matches.
  5. Tactical decision: choose the equation with the simpler numerator to invert for the parameter (line case), or test small-integer linear combinations of u and v to find the one that cancels (circle case).
  6. Verify by substituting one or two specific parameter values: the resulting (u, v) must satisfy the proposed Cartesian equation.
  7. State excluded points: the value of t that makes the denominator vanish is excluded, and the limit t → ∞ may add a single asymptotic image point.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write u = p(t)/q(t) and v = r(t)/q(t) with common denominator q(t).
  2. Line case: solve v = r(t)/q(t) (or the simpler of the two) for 1/q(t) or for (t − a); rewrite u using partial fractions u = A + B/q(t); substitute to get u = A − (B/r-coefficient)·v, then rearrange to au + bv + c = 0.
  3. Circle case: compute u² + v² = [p(t)² + r(t)²]/q(t)²; expand the numerator and factor.
  4. Compute candidate linear combinations αu + βv = [αp(t) + βr(t)]/q(t); choose α, β so that αp(t) + βr(t) is divisible by [p(t)² + r(t)²]/q(t) — equivalently, so the ratio (u² + v²)/(αu + βv) is a constant.
  5. Equate u² + v² = k(αu + βv) and rearrange to (u − h)² + (v − k')² = R².
  6. State the parameter exclusion (point omitted) and any limit point added.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $u = \frac{y+1}{y-1}$, $v = \frac{-2}{y-1}$ with $y \in \mathbb{R}$: rewrite $u = \frac{(y-1)+2}{y-1} = 1 + \frac{2}{y-1}$. Since $\frac{2}{y-1} = -v$, we get $u = 1 - v$, i.e. $u + v - 1 = 0$. The point $(1, 0)$ (limit $y \to \infty$) is included; $y = 1$ is excluded.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Pre-image is a horizontal/vertical line and image is expected to be a line (pole of f lies on the pre-image line)
Use partial fractions on u to express it as A + B/(t − a), then read off (t − a) from v and substitute — yields au + bv = c in one step.
Pre-image is a line not through the pole, so image is a circle
Compute u² + v² and seek a linear combination αu + βv with the same numerator (up to constant); equate to obtain u² + v² = k(αu + βv).
Both u and v are rational with matching denominators
Cross-multiply αu + βv = (linear in t)/q(t) and match to u² + v² to determine α, β by comparing numerator polynomials.
Inverse-map alternative
Solve w = f(z) for z = g(w), then impose the pre-image condition (e.g. Im(z) = 0 or |z| = r) directly on g(u + iv); often faster than parametric elimination.

Common Misreadings

  • Squaring and adding to form u² + v² when the image is actually a line — produces a true but redundant identity that hides the linear relation.
  • Solving u = f(t) for t via the quadratic formula and substituting — generates square roots that obscure the clean Cartesian form.
  • Forgetting that a Möbius map sends lines/circles to lines/circles and trying to fit higher-degree curves.

Pitfalls

  • Algebraic slips when handling fractions-within-fractions; keeping a partial-fraction decomposition like $1 + \tfrac{2}{t-1}$ avoids nested fractions.
  • Arithmetic errors when expanding $(at+b)^2 + (ct+d)^2$ in the circle case — double-check coefficients before testing linear combinations.
  • Failing to state the excluded point (the parameter value that is the pole of f) and the limit point (as t → ∞) — the image is the line/circle minus one point, plus possibly one limit point.
  • Picking the wrong α, β by guessing; if trial-and-error stalls, set up αp(t) + βr(t) = λ·(p(t)² + r(t)²)/q(t) and equate coefficients.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) treatment of transformations of the complex plane. Uses only standard real algebra and the fact that Möbius maps send lines/circles to lines/circles — no use of cross-ratio or projective machinery. No formula booklet entry is invoked.
