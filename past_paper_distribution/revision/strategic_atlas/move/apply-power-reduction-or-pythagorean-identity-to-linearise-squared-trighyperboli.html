Integration

Apply power-reduction or Pythagorean identity to linearise squared trig/hyperbolic terms

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

After expanding r² in a polar area integral ½∫r²dθ, or after a substitution in a hyperbolic integral, the integrand contains a squared trigonometric or hyperbolic term — typically cos²θ, sin²θ, cos²(nθ), sin²(nθ), or coth²θ — that has no direct entry in the formula booklet. Visual cues: an even power of a single trig/hyperbolic function, or a ratio like cosh²θ/sinh²θ. The integrand cannot be tackled by u-substitution (since the power is even, no spare factor exists) and is too elementary to require parts. This is the standard fork after expanding (1 + cosθ)², (a + b sinθ)², or recognising coth²θ in a hyperbolic context.

Motivation — Why this works

Power-reduction identities (cos²θ = ½(1 + cos2θ), sin²θ = ½(1 − cos2θ)) replace a squared trig term with a constant plus a single cosine of double angle — both immediately integrable. The hyperbolic analogue uses the Pythagorean identity cosh²θ − sinh²θ = 1, dividing through by sinh²θ to give coth²θ = 1 + cosech²θ, which decomposes into ∫1 dθ + ∫cosech²θ dθ = θ − cothθ. The underlying principle is the same: convert an unintegrable squared form into a linear sum whose terms each have a standard antiderivative. Without this reduction, the integral is stuck.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand contains an even-powered trig term (cos²θ, sin²(2θ)) or a squared hyperbolic ratio (coth²θ) — none of which appear directly in the standard antiderivative table.
  2. Rule out u-substitution: an even power leaves no 'spare' differential factor (unlike odd powers, where one factor pairs with du). Rule out integration by parts: it works for cos²θ but creates a circular loop and is far slower.
  3. Commit to an identity-based linearisation: for cos²θ use cos²θ = ½(1 + cos2θ); for sin²θ use sin²θ = ½(1 − cos2θ); for coth²θ use coth²θ = 1 + cosech²θ. Choose the form that isolates the squared term on one side.
  4. Bridge: substitute the identity directly into the integrand, then collect like terms (constants together, cosine terms together) before integrating.
  5. Tactical decision: if a higher even power appears (e.g. cos⁴θ), apply the identity twice — first reducing cos⁴θ = [½(1+cos2θ)]², then reducing the resulting cos²(2θ) using the same identity with doubled argument.
  6. Track coefficients carefully: in polar area problems the ½ from ½∫r²dθ combines with the ½ from the identity, so keep numerators and denominators tidy.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Expand the squared expression fully (e.g. (1 + cosθ)² = 1 + 2cosθ + cos²θ).
  2. Replace the squared term using the appropriate identity: cos²θ → ½(1 + cos2θ), sin²θ → ½(1 − cos2θ), or coth²θ → 1 + cosech²θ.
  3. Combine constants and like terms (e.g. 1 + 2cosθ + ½ + ½cos2θ = 3/2 + 2cosθ + ½cos2θ).
  4. Integrate term by term using standard results: ∫c dθ = cθ, ∫cos(kθ) dθ = sin(kθ)/k, ∫cosech²θ dθ = −cothθ.
  5. Evaluate at the limits, applying any symmetry multiplier (e.g. doubling for symmetric polar curves).

Worked Micro-Example

Compute $\int_0^{\pi}(1+\cos\theta)^2\,d\theta$. Expand: $\int_0^{\pi}(1+2\cos\theta+\cos^2\theta)\,d\theta$. Replace $\cos^2\theta=\tfrac12(1+\cos2\theta)$: $\int_0^{\pi}\!\left(\tfrac32+2\cos\theta+\tfrac12\cos2\theta\right)d\theta = \left[\tfrac{3\theta}{2}+2\sin\theta+\tfrac{\sin2\theta}{4}\right]_0^{\pi} = \tfrac{3\pi}{2}.$

Variants — Same move, different surface

For sin²θ in the integrand
Use sin²θ = ½(1 − cos2θ) — same logic, opposite sign on the cosine term.
For cos⁴θ or sin⁴θ (higher even powers)
Apply the identity twice: cos⁴θ = [½(1+cos2θ)]² = ¼(1 + 2cos2θ + cos²2θ), then reduce cos²2θ = ½(1 + cos4θ).
For products like sinθ cosθ
Use the double-angle product identity sin2θ = 2sinθ cosθ, so sinθ cosθ = ½sin2θ.
For sin²(nθ) or cos²(nθ) with multiple angle
Same identity with scaled argument: cos²(nθ) = ½(1 + cos(2nθ)); the antiderivative of cos(2nθ) is sin(2nθ)/(2n).
For coth²θ in hyperbolic integrals
Use coth²θ = 1 + cosech²θ (derived from cosh²θ − sinh²θ = 1 divided by sinh²θ); then ∫coth²θ dθ = θ − cothθ + C.
For tan²θ or sec²θ-related forms
Use tan²θ = sec²θ − 1 (the trig analogue of the coth² move); ∫tan²θ dθ = tanθ − θ + C.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying u = cosθ when the power is even — this only works cleanly for odd powers where a spare sinθ dθ is available.
  • Attempting integration by parts on cos²θ — works but loops back and is much slower than the identity.
  • Confusing coth²θ = 1 + cosech²θ with the trig version cot²θ = cosec²θ − 1 (the signs differ between trig and hyperbolic).

Pitfalls

  • Sign error: writing cos²θ = ½(1 − cos2θ) instead of ½(1 + cos2θ), or vice versa for sin²θ.
  • Forgetting the factor of ½ in the identity: writing cos²θ = 1 + cos2θ.
  • Integrating cos(2θ) as sin(2θ) instead of sin(2θ)/2 — missing the chain-rule denominator.
  • In polar area problems, losing track of the ½ from ½∫r²dθ combined with the ½ from the identity.
  • For coth²θ, forgetting that ∫cosech²θ dθ = −cothθ (sign error from the antiderivative).

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 (trig identities and integration) and FP2/FP3 (hyperbolic functions and their identities). Identities cos²θ = ½(1+cos2θ), sin²θ = ½(1−cos2θ), and the hyperbolic Pythagorean identity cosh²θ − sinh²θ = 1 are all in the formula booklet. Avoid generalising to complex exponential methods (Euler's formula linearisation) — that is beyond IAL scope.
