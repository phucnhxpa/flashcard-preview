Strategic Atlas / Proof / Exponent Peeling
Proof

Peel one growth factor from each exponential to expose f(k) components

Family: Exponent Peeling Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in divisibility induction when f(n) is a sum of two (or more) exponentials with different bases, e.g. f(n) = 2^{n+2} + 3^{2n+1}, and you need to relate f(k+1) back to f(k). Cue phrases: 'prove f(n) is divisible by m for all n', combined with f(n) being a sum of base-a^{...} + base-b^{...} terms. Also fires when one term has a linear-in-n exponent like 2n+1 or 3n-2, where the growth factor per induction step is a^c rather than just a.

Motivation — Why this works

Exponent laws guarantee a^{(k+1)+c} = a · a^{k+c} and a^{d(k+1)+c} = a^d · a^{dk+c}, so each exponential in f(k+1) is just a constant multiple of the corresponding exponential in f(k). Peeling these growth factors makes the building blocks of f(k) — namely 2^{k+2} and 3^{2k+1} — literally appear inside f(k+1). This is the prerequisite step before any factoring or recombination that introduces f(k) explicitly; without it, f(k) cannot be substituted in.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice f(k+1) = 2^{k+3} + 3^{2k+3}, but my inductive hypothesis is about 2^{k+2} + 3^{2k+1}, so I need those exact powers to surface.
  2. I rule out expanding everything in terms of 2^k and 3^k — that would lose the alignment with f(k)'s building blocks and force me to redo the bookkeeping.
  3. I commit to peeling exactly one 'step' of growth from each base, since each induction step multiplies the exponent by a fixed amount per base.
  4. Concretely: 2^{k+3} = 2 · 2^{k+2}, and because the exponent in 3^{2n+1} jumps by 2 when n → n+1, I get 3^{2k+3} = 3^2 · 3^{2k+1} = 9 · 3^{2k+1}.
  5. I leave the result as f(k+1) = 2·2^{k+2} + 9·3^{2k+1} — unsimplified — because the next move (splitting 9 = 2 + 7 to manufacture f(k)) needs both growth factors visible.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Substitute n = k+1 into f: f(k+1) = 2^{(k+1)+2} + 3^{2(k+1)+1} = 2^{k+3} + 3^{2k+3}.
  2. For each exponential, identify the growth factor per induction step: base 2 with exponent k+2 → factor 2; base 3 with exponent 2k+1 → factor 3^2 = 9.
  3. Peel: 2^{k+3} = 2 · 2^{k+2} and 3^{2k+3} = 9 · 3^{2k+1}.
  4. Rewrite: f(k+1) = 2 · 2^{k+2} + 9 · 3^{2k+1}, leaving the expression in this peeled form ready for the next recombination move.

Worked Micro-Example

Let f(n) = 2^{n+2} + 3^{2n+1}. Then f(k+1) = 2^{k+3} + 3^{2k+3}. Peeling: 2^{k+3} = 2 \cdot 2^{k+2} and 3^{2k+3} = 3^{2} \cdot 3^{2k+1} = 9 \cdot 3^{2k+1}. Hence f(k+1) = 2 \cdot 2^{k+2} + 9 \cdot 3^{2k+1}, with both f(k)-components now visible.

Variants — Same move, different surface

f(n) = 5^n + 3 \cdot 2^{n+1} (simple linear exponents).
Peel one factor of each base: f(k+1) = 5 \cdot 5^k + 2 \cdot (3 \cdot 2^{k+1}), exposing both components of f(k).
f(n) = 7^{2n} + 5 \cdot 3^{n} (one base with a 2n exponent).
Growth factor for 7^{2n} is 7^2 = 49, while 3^n picks up factor 3, giving f(k+1) = 49 \cdot 7^{2k} + 3 \cdot (5 \cdot 3^{k}).
f(n) = 8^n - 3^n (difference of exponentials, common in 'divisible by 5' problems).
Same peeling principle: f(k+1) = 8 \cdot 8^k - 3 \cdot 3^k; then split 8 = 3 + 5 (or 8 = 5 + 3) to manufacture f(k).
Single exponential plus polynomial, e.g. f(n) = 4^n + 6n - 1.
Peel only the exponential term: 4^{k+1} = 4 \cdot 4^k; the linear part is handled by direct substitution, not peeling.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the exponent 2n+1 as if it grew by 1 per step, producing growth factor 3 instead of 9.
  • Trying to peel both terms down to the same base or the same exponent — the move keeps each base separate.
  • Believing the peeling itself completes the induction; it only sets up the next step (manufacturing f(k)).

Pitfalls

  • Mishandling the exponent in 3^{2n+1}: replacing n by k+1 gives 2k+3, which exceeds 2k+1 by 2, so the growth factor is 3^2 = 9, not 3.
  • Prematurely simplifying 2 \cdot 2^{k+2} back to 2^{k+3}, undoing the move and hiding the f(k) component.
  • Arithmetic slips when the constant coefficient is not 1, e.g. forgetting that 3 \cdot 2^{n+1} becomes 2 \cdot (3 \cdot 2^{k+1}), not 6 \cdot 2^{k+1} (both are correct, but mixing the two forms causes errors in the next step).

A-level Scope Notes

This is a standard step in Edexcel IAL Further Pure proof-by-induction divisibility questions; only basic index laws (a^{m+n} = a^m \cdot a^n) are required, all within FP1 scope.
