Sequences & Series

Eliminate the first term a to reduce a GP system to one equation in r

Family: Parameter Elimination Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a geometric progression problem gives two pieces of information involving the two unknowns a (first term) and r (common ratio), and the goal (often a 'show that' quadratic/cubic in r, or simply finding r) requires producing a single equation in r alone. Typical surface cues: two term values given (e.g. u_2 and u_5, or u_3 and u_6); a term plus the sum to infinity; or S_∞ given as a scalar multiple of a (e.g. S_∞ = 3a). Whenever you see two unknowns a, r and the target only mentions r, this move is the right tool.

Motivation — Why this works

Both the nth-term formula ar^{n-1} and the sum-to-infinity formula a/(1-r) are linear (degree 1) in a. This homogeneity means a can always be removed — either by dividing two equations (so a cancels multiplicatively), by substituting a = … from one equation into the other, or by dividing through when a appears on both sides. The result is a single equation purely in r, which is exactly what 'show that' quadratics/cubics in r require, and what a unique value of r demands.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the target only mentions r (e.g. a quadratic in r, a value of r, or r^k = constant), so a must be eliminated.
  2. Identify the two given conditions and write each as an equation in a and r using ar^{n-1} for terms and a/(1-r) for S_∞.
  3. Rule out brute-force simultaneous solving for both a and r — unnecessary work and a is not asked for.
  4. Choose the cleanest elimination route: if both equations have the form ar^{k} = value, divide them so a cancels and you get r^{difference} directly; if one is a sum and one is a term, rearrange the simpler one for a and substitute; if a appears on both sides of one equation (e.g. S_∞ = 3a), just divide through by a.
  5. Pick the division direction that yields a positive exponent on r and matches the target form (e.g. divide u_6/u_3 to get r^3, not r^{-3}).
  6. After elimination, simplify the numerical right-hand side fully before solving, and verify |r| < 1 if S_∞ was used.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Translate each condition: u_n = ar^{n-1}; S_∞ = a/(1-r).
  2. Label the two equations (1) and (2).
  3. Decide elimination route: (a) divide (2)÷(1) if both are of the form ar^k = value; (b) rearrange one for a and substitute if forms differ; (c) cancel a directly if it appears on both sides.
  4. Carry out the operation: e.g. ar^5/ar^2 = r^3 = (RHS_2)/(RHS_1), or substitute a = 16(1-r) into ar = 1 to get 16r(1-r) = 1.
  5. Simplify numerically and rearrange to standard form (quadratic = 0, or r^k = constant).
  6. Solve for r and check validity (|r|<1 for S_∞ problems; correct sign for odd/even roots).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $u_3 = ar^2 = 12$ and $u_6 = ar^5 = \tfrac{3}{2}$. Divide: $\dfrac{ar^5}{ar^2} = r^3 = \dfrac{3/2}{12} = \dfrac{1}{8}$, hence $r = \tfrac{1}{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Two terms u_m and u_n given
Divide u_n/u_m = r^{n-m}; choose n>m so the exponent is positive.
One term value plus S_∞ given as a number
Rearrange S_∞ equation for a (a = S_∞(1-r)) and substitute into the term equation; gives a polynomial in r with no fractions.
S_∞ given as a multiple of a (e.g. S_∞ = ka)
a appears on both sides of a/(1-r) = ka; divide by a to get 1/(1-r) = k, then solve linearly for r.
AP analogue with first term a and common difference d
Same elimination idea: subtract two linear equations in a and d to remove a, isolating d.
Exponential model P = P_0 e^{kt}
Divide two data-point equations to eliminate P_0 and isolate e^{k(t_2-t_1)}.

Common Misreadings

  • Using u_n = ar^n instead of ar^{n-1}, shifting all exponents by one.
  • Subtracting the two GP equations instead of dividing — fails because the structure is multiplicative.
  • Forgetting that S_∞ requires |r|<1 and not checking the validity of the r found.
  • Solving for a first when only r is needed, introducing avoidable fractions.

Pitfalls

  • Dividing the wrong way round (lower index ÷ higher index) gives r^{-k}; correct but needs an extra reciprocal step.
  • Sign errors when rearranging into standard quadratic form (e.g. 16r - 16r^2 = 1 → must negate consistently to get 16r^2 - 16r + 1 = 0).
  • Arithmetic slips reducing fractions like 512/(25·320); reduce step-by-step.
  • When r^k = constant has even k, remember the ± solutions; for odd k, the sign of r matches the sign of the constant.
  • Choosing the substitution route a = 1/r when a = (1-r)·S_∞ is available — the latter avoids compound fractions.

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics P2 (geometric sequences and series, including S_∞). Uses the formula-booklet entries u_n = ar^{n-1} and S_∞ = a/(1-r) for |r|<1. Do not generalise to convergence tests, power series, or non-geometric recurrences — those are out of scope.
