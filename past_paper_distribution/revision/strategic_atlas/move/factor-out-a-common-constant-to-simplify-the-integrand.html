Algebra & Functions

Factor out a common constant to simplify the integrand

Family: Constant Extraction Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an integrand is a sum/difference of terms that share (possibly after a small rewrite) a common rational constant, e.g. $81 - \tfrac{81}{4}(x-3)$ or $4x^2 - 8x + 4$. Cue words: 'fractional coefficients clutter the integral', 'one term is a constant and the other carries a fraction of it'. Especially common in volume-of-revolution setups where $\pi$ and a squared radius produce a single overall scalar. Also fires when a definite integral's bounds will eventually be multiplied by a messy constant — pulling it out early keeps the integration clean.

Motivation — Why this works

By linearity of the integral, $\int k f(x)\,dx = k\int f(x)\,dx$, so any global constant can be moved outside the integral sign. Rewriting one term to expose a shared factor (e.g. $81 = \tfrac{81}{4}\cdot 4$) lets us collapse the integrand into a constant times a simple polynomial, which is integrated in a single step. This reduces fractional arithmetic inside the antiderivative and isolates the messy constant for a single multiplication at the end. The principle is purely algebraic — recognising a hidden common factor — combined with the linearity of integration.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that both terms in the integrand involve the number 81, with one carrying an extra $\tfrac{1}{4}$ and a linear factor $(x-3)$.
  2. Rules out expanding $(x-3)$ and collecting like terms, since that produces three fractional coefficients to integrate separately.
  3. Commits to factoring out $\tfrac{81}{4}$ globally because the remaining bracket will be a clean linear function, trivially integrable.
  4. Bridges to the first operation by rewriting $81 = \tfrac{81}{4}\cdot 4$ so the common factor is visible across both terms.
  5. Inside the move, decides to simplify $4 - (x-3) = 7 - x$ before integrating, and to keep $\tfrac{81\pi}{4}$ outside the integral until the very last step.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the common constant factor across all terms (here $\tfrac{81}{4}$).
  2. Rewrite any 'bare' term to expose that factor: $81 = \tfrac{81}{4}\cdot 4$.
  3. Factorise: $81 - \tfrac{81}{4}(x-3) = \tfrac{81}{4}\bigl[4 - (x-3)\bigr] = \tfrac{81}{4}(7-x)$.
  4. Pull the constant (and any $\pi$) outside: $V = \tfrac{81\pi}{4}\int_a^b (7-x)\,dx$.
  5. Integrate the simple polynomial: $\int (7-x)\,dx = 7x - \tfrac{x^2}{2}$.
  6. Evaluate at the limits, then multiply by the extracted constant once at the end.

Worked Micro-Example

Compute $\int_3^6 \bigl[81 - \tfrac{81}{4}(x-3)\bigr]\,dx$. Write $81 = \tfrac{81}{4}\cdot 4$, so the integrand becomes $\tfrac{81}{4}(7-x)$. Then $\tfrac{81}{4}\int_3^6 (7-x)\,dx = \tfrac{81}{4}\bigl[7x - \tfrac{x^2}{2}\bigr]_3^6 = \tfrac{81}{4}\cdot\tfrac{15}{2} = \tfrac{1215}{8}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Volume of revolution $V = \pi\int y^2\,dx$ where $y^2$ expands to terms sharing a constant
Pull the shared constant together with $\pi$ outside, leaving a clean polynomial in $x$ to integrate.
Integrand like $4x^2 - 8x + 4 = 4(x-1)^2$
Factor the constant 4 out, then integrate $(x-1)^2$ directly via the reverse chain rule.
Differentiation of $f(x) = \tfrac{a}{b}g(x) + c$ where $c$ can be rewritten with denominator $b$
Same factor-out idea applies before differentiating, though differentiation is linear so the gain is mainly cosmetic.
Series sum $\sum (A - Bk)$ with $A$ a multiple of $B$
Factor $B$ out of the summand to get $B\sum(\text{simple linear in }k)$ and use standard $\sum k$ formulae.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the two terms as unrelated and integrating each separately, dragging fractions through every line.
  • Expanding $(x-3)$ first and combining with $81$ to get $\tfrac{81}{4}\bigl(\tfrac{4\cdot 81 + 243}{...}\bigr)$-style messes instead of seeing the clean factor.
  • Mistaking the move for a substitution; no $u$-substitution is needed — it is pure algebraic factoring plus linearity.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slip when rewriting $81$ as $\tfrac{81}{4}\cdot 4$ — students sometimes write $\tfrac{81}{4}\cdot 81$ or drop the factor of 4.
  • Sign error in $4 - (x-3) = 7 - x$ (not $1 - x$); the negative must distribute over both parts of $(x-3)$.
  • Forgetting to multiply the final definite-integral value by the extracted constant (and by $\pi$ in volume problems).
  • Pulling out only part of the constant (e.g. extracting $81$ but leaving the $\tfrac{1}{4}$ inside), defeating the purpose.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only linearity of the integral and basic factoring, both core to Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (C12/C34). Appears routinely in volume-of-revolution and definite-integral questions across IAL Maths and Further Maths.
