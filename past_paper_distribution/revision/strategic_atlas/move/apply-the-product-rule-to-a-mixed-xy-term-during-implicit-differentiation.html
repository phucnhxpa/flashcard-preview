Strategic Atlas / Differentiation / Product Rule Within Implicit
Differentiation

Apply the product rule to a mixed x·y term during implicit differentiation

Family: Product Rule Within Implicit Frequency: 7 questions

Trigger — When to use

The expression contains a term that is a product of two factors, both of which depend on x: typically one factor is explicit in x (e.g. 2x, 4x, x², e^{-2x}) and the other is the implicit function y (or a function of y such as sin y, ln y, y³). Neither factor is a constant with respect to x, so neither plain power-rule nor chain-rule alone is sufficient. Visual cues: 'xy', '2xy', '−2xy', '4xy', 'x²y³', 'x sin y', 'y e^{-2x}' sitting as a single term inside an equation that you are about to differentiate implicitly. Equivalently: any time you see 'x-stuff multiplied by y-stuff' in an implicit equation, the product rule must fire.

Motivation — Why this works

The product rule d/dx(uv) = u'v + uv' is the correct identity whenever both factors of a product vary with the differentiation variable. In implicit differentiation, y is a function of x, so any factor containing y also varies with x. Applying the product rule generates exactly two contributions: a 'free' term (with no dy/dx) coming from differentiating the explicit factor, and a dy/dx term coming from differentiating the implicit factor via the chain rule. Both contributions are needed — dropping either one corrupts the equation and gives a wrong dy/dx. This is one of the most heavily examined and most commonly missed steps in Edexcel IAL implicit-differentiation questions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the term in question is a product of two distinct x-dependent quantities — for example 2xy is '2x' times 'y', not a single composite function.
  2. Rule out treating y as a constant (which would give just 2y and lose the dy/dx contribution) and rule out applying the chain rule alone (which would give just 2x·dy/dx and lose the 2y contribution).
  3. Rule out the quotient rule and direct power rule — the structure is a genuine product of two x-varying factors, so the product rule is the unique correct tool.
  4. Commit to the product rule: assign u to the explicit-x factor and v to the y-dependent factor, because u' is then a clean numerical/polynomial derivative while v' contributes the dy/dx that we ultimately want to isolate.
  5. Compute u' by ordinary rules and v' by the chain rule on y (so v' = dy/dx if v = y, or v' = (dv/dy)·(dy/dx) more generally).
  6. Assemble u'v + uv' carefully, watching any leading sign in front of the term (e.g. '−2xy' must have the entire bracketed expansion negated).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two factors: set u = (explicit-x factor) and v = (y-dependent factor). E.g. for 2xy, take u = 2x, v = y.
  2. Differentiate u with respect to x using ordinary rules: u' = du/dx (e.g. 2, 4, 2x, −2e^{-2x}).
  3. Differentiate v with respect to x using the chain rule on y: v' = dv/dy · dy/dx (e.g. v = y gives v' = dy/dx; v = y³ gives v' = 3y²·dy/dx; v = sin y gives v' = cos y · dy/dx).
  4. Apply the product rule: d/dx(uv) = u'·v + u·v'.
  5. Substitute back into the differentiated equation, propagating any leading sign (e.g. d/dx(−2xy) = −[2y + 2x·dy/dx] = −2y − 2x·dy/dx).
  6. Keep the dy/dx term and the non-dy/dx term clearly separated, ready for the later collect-and-isolate step.

Worked Micro-Example

Differentiate $2xy$ implicitly with respect to $x$. Set $u=2x,\ v=y$, so $u'=2,\ v'=\dfrac{dy}{dx}$. Then $\dfrac{d}{dx}(2xy) = u'v + uv' = 2y + 2x\dfrac{dy}{dx}$. The $2y$ is the 'free' term; the $2x\,dy/dx$ joins the other dy/dx terms when isolating $dy/dx$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Mixed term with a power of y, e.g. x²y³
Set u = x², v = y³; then u' = 2x and v' = 3y²·dy/dx, giving d/dx(x²y³) = 2xy³ + 3x²y²·dy/dx. Product rule combined with chain rule on the y-power.
Mixed term with a trig function of y, e.g. x·sin(y)
Set u = x, v = sin y; then u' = 1 and v' = cos y · dy/dx, giving d/dx(x sin y) = sin y + x cos y · dy/dx.
Mixed term with an exponential in x, e.g. y·e^{-2x}
Set u = y, v = e^{-2x}; then u' = dy/dx and v' = −2e^{-2x}, giving d/dx(y e^{-2x}) = e^{-2x}·dy/dx − 2y e^{-2x}.
Mixed term with ln y, e.g. e^x · ln y
Set u = e^x, v = ln y; then u' = e^x and v' = (1/y)·dy/dx, giving e^x ln y + (e^x / y)·dy/dx.
Term carries a leading minus sign, e.g. −2xy
Apply the product rule inside a bracket and then negate the entire result: −[2y + 2x·dy/dx] = −2y − 2x·dy/dx.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 2xy as a single 'y-type' term and applying only the chain rule, getting 2x·dy/dx and losing the 2y term.
  • Reading 2xy as a single 'x-type' term, treating y as a constant, and getting just 2y while losing the 2x·dy/dx term.
  • Confusing the product rule with the chain rule and writing d/dx(2xy) = 2·dy/dx.
  • Mistaking xy for a composite (e.g. trying to use chain rule on 'xy' as if it were f(g(x))).

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting v' = dy/dx (writing v' = 1) when v = y — i.e. omitting the chain rule on the implicit factor.
  • Dropping one of the two product-rule terms, especially the non-dy/dx 'free' term which is easy to forget.
  • Sign errors when the mixed term is preceded by a minus sign — failing to distribute the negative across both product-rule pieces.
  • Forgetting the chain rule on an exponent (e.g. differentiating e^{-2x} as e^{-2x} instead of −2e^{-2x}) when the explicit factor is itself a composite.

A-level Scope Notes

This sits squarely in Edexcel IAL P3 implicit differentiation. It uses the product rule (formula booklet: differentiation rules) and the chain rule applied to y(x). Stay within differentiating standard functions on the IAL syllabus (polynomials, exp, ln, sin, cos, tan); do not invoke partial derivatives or multivariable calculus, which are out of scope.
