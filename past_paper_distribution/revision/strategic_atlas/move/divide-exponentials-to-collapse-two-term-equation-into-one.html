Divide exponentials to collapse two-term equation into one

Family: Exponential Combination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation has the form A·e^{f(t)} = B·e^{g(t)}, with two exponential terms in the same variable but different linear exponents. Typical cues: setting a derivative to zero in a model like v(t) = e^{t-10} - ke^{-ct} and getting e^{t-10} = 9e^{-0.75t}, or equating two exponential growth/decay expressions. The exponents are usually distinct linear functions of t, and one or both sides carry a constant multiplier. Direct application of ln to both sides is blocked by the multiplicative constant getting tangled with the exponential.

Motivation — Why this works

Index laws give e^{a}/e^{b} = e^{a-b}, so dividing one exponential through merges two exponentials into a single e^{f(t)-g(t)}. Once the equation reads e^{linear in t} = constant, ln linearises it in one clean step. The alternative — taking ln of A·e^{f(t)} = B·e^{g(t)} directly — does work via ln(AB) = ln A + ln B, but is more error-prone with the coefficient and sign-handling. Division-first keeps the structure visibly clean.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice two exponentials in t with different linear exponents and a numerical coefficient on one side.
  2. I rule out taking ln directly: while legal here, the coefficient 9 muddies the algebra and I want a single exponential first.
  3. I commit to dividing because the structure A·e^{f(t)} = B·e^{g(t)} is the canonical setup for index-law collapse.
  4. I divide by the exponential on one side (or multiply by its reciprocal) to merge exponents via e^{f-g}.
  5. I choose which side to divide so that the surviving exponent has a positive coefficient if possible, then take ln of both sides.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Start with A·e^{f(t)} = B·e^{g(t)}; here e^{t-10} = 9e^{-0.75t}.
  2. Divide both sides by e^{g(t)}: e^{f(t)-g(t)} = B/A; here e^{(t-10)-(-0.75t)} = 9.
  3. Simplify the exponent using index laws, watching signs: 1.75t - 10.
  4. Take ln of both sides: f(t) - g(t) = ln(B/A); here 1.75t - 10 = ln 9.
  5. Solve the resulting linear equation for t: t = (10 + ln 9)/1.75.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $e^{t-10} = 9e^{-0.75t}$. Divide by $e^{-0.75t}$: $e^{(t-10)-(-0.75t)} = 9$, i.e. $e^{1.75t - 10} = 9$. Take ln: $1.75t - 10 = \ln 9$, so $t = \dfrac{10 + \ln 9}{1.75} \approx 6.97$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

General A·e^{f(t)} = B·e^{g(t)} with linear exponents
Divide to get e^{f(t)-g(t)} = B/A, then ln and solve linearly.
Equation of the form e^{ax} = ke^{bx} arising from dN/dt = 0 in a population model
Same division step; resulting linear equation in x gives the stationary point.
Trig analogue a sin θ = b cos θ
Divide by cos θ to get tan θ = b/a, the structurally identical 'collapse-to-one-function' move.
Logarithm pair log_a x = log_a y + c
Subtract logs (the additive analogue of dividing exponentials) to get log_a(x/y) = c.

Common Misreadings

  • Taking ln of both sides immediately and writing ln(e^{t-10}) = ln(9·e^{-0.75t}) but then forgetting to split the right side as ln 9 + (-0.75t).
  • Equating exponents directly, t - 10 = -0.75t, ignoring the coefficient 9 entirely.
  • Mistaking 9 for an exponential and trying to write it as e^{ln 9} prematurely without first isolating a single exponential.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when subtracting a negative exponent: (t-10) - (-0.75t) = 1.75t - 10, not 0.25t - 10.
  • Dividing only one of the two exponential factors and leaving the constant coefficient on the wrong side.
  • Forgetting to take ln of the constant on the right (writing 1.75t - 10 = 9 instead of ln 9).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P2/P3 exponentials and logarithms, P3 differentiation of e^{kt} models). Uses only index laws and the ln/exp inverse relationship.
