Trigonometry

Select R-formula form to match the sign pattern of the expression

Family: R Formula Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when you see a linear combination of $\sin x$ and $\cos x$ (e.g. $a\sin x \pm b\cos x$ or $a\cos x \pm b\sin x$) that you must rewrite as a single sinusoid $R\sin(x\pm\alpha)$ or $R\cos(x\pm\alpha)$ with $R>0$ and $\alpha$ in the first quadrant ($0<\alpha<90°$). Equivalent phrasings: 'express in the form $R\sin(x+\alpha)$', 'write as a single cosine', 'find $R$ and $\alpha$ such that...'. The key cue is that the question may either fix the form (in which case you must use it) or leave it open, in which case you should pick the form whose expansion naturally reproduces the given signs.

Motivation — Why this works

The four compound-angle expansions $R\sin(x\pm\alpha)$ and $R\cos(x\pm\alpha)$ each generate a specific sign pattern on the $\sin x$ and $\cos x$ terms. Matching that pattern to the given expression keeps both $R\cos\alpha$ and $R\sin\alpha$ positive, which automatically places $\alpha$ in the first quadrant and lets you read $\alpha=\arctan(\text{ratio})$ directly. Picking the wrong form forces $\alpha$ to be negative or obtuse, breaking the stated range and inviting sign errors. The underlying principle is just the addition formula $\sin(A\pm B)=\sin A\cos B\pm\cos A\sin B$ (and the cosine analogue) read in reverse.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the structure: identify which of $\sin x$ and $\cos x$ has the positive coefficient and which has the negative coefficient — this sign pattern is the decisive cue.
  2. Mentally list the four candidate expansions: $R\sin(x+\alpha)=R\cos\alpha\sin x+R\sin\alpha\cos x$; $R\sin(x-\alpha)=R\cos\alpha\sin x-R\sin\alpha\cos x$; $R\cos(x-\alpha)=R\cos\alpha\cos x+R\sin\alpha\sin x$; $R\cos(x+\alpha)=R\cos\alpha\cos x-R\sin\alpha\sin x$.
  3. Rule out the forms whose expansion sign pattern does not match the target — these would force $\sin\alpha$ or $\cos\alpha$ to be negative, pushing $\alpha$ out of $0<\alpha<90°$.
  4. Commit to the form where the leading term (sine or cosine of $x$, whichever is positive in the original) corresponds to $R\cos\alpha$, so the minus sign in the expansion lines up with the minus sign in the original.
  5. If the question pre-specifies the form, override the natural choice and use the prescribed form — but still anticipate that you may need to argue carefully about the sign/range of $\alpha$.
  6. Bridge to mechanics: write the chosen expansion in full, then equate $\sin x$ and $\cos x$ coefficients to set up $R\cos\alpha$ and $R\sin\alpha$ as two positive numbers.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the chosen identity in full, e.g. $R\sin(x-\alpha)=R\cos\alpha\sin x-R\sin\alpha\cos x$.
  2. Equate the $\sin x$ coefficients: $R\cos\alpha = (\text{coefficient of }\sin x)$.
  3. Equate the $\cos x$ coefficients (using the absolute value, since the minus sign was absorbed by the form): $R\sin\alpha = |\text{coefficient of }\cos x|$.
  4. Compute $R=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ and $\tan\alpha=\dfrac{R\sin\alpha}{R\cos\alpha}$, then $\alpha=\arctan(\cdot)$ in the first quadrant.

Worked Micro-Example

Express $8\sin x-15\cos x$ as $R\sin(x-\alpha)$. Expand: $R\sin(x-\alpha)=R\cos\alpha\sin x-R\sin\alpha\cos x$. Match: $R\cos\alpha=8$, $R\sin\alpha=15$. Hence $R=\sqrt{8^2+15^2}=17$ and $\tan\alpha=\tfrac{15}{8}$, giving $\alpha\approx61.93°$. So $8\sin x-15\cos x=17\sin(x-61.93°)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$a\sin x + b\cos x$ with $a,b>0$ (both positive)
Choose $R\sin(x+\alpha)$ or $R\cos(x-\alpha)$ so both $R\cos\alpha$ and $R\sin\alpha$ come out positive.
$a\cos x - b\sin x$ with $a,b>0$ (cosine-led, minus sine)
Choose $R\cos(x+\alpha)$: expansion $R\cos\alpha\cos x-R\sin\alpha\sin x$ matches the signs directly.
$a\cos x + b\sin x$ with $a,b>0$
Choose $R\cos(x-\alpha)$: expansion $R\cos\alpha\cos x+R\sin\alpha\sin x$ matches.
Question pre-specifies a form (e.g. 'in the form $R\cos(x+\alpha)$')
Use the prescribed form regardless of natural sign matching; track signs carefully when equating coefficients.

Common Misreadings

  • Defaulting to $R\sin(x+\alpha)$ out of habit and obtaining a negative $\alpha$, then ignoring or 'fixing' it incorrectly.
  • Confusing which coefficient corresponds to $R\sin\alpha$ vs $R\cos\alpha$ — always expand the chosen form in full before comparing.
  • Reading the sign of the $\cos x$ term as part of $R\sin\alpha$ (giving $R\sin\alpha<0$) instead of letting the form's built-in minus sign absorb it.

Pitfalls

  • If the question fixes the form (e.g. $R\cos(x+\alpha)$), you must use it even if another form feels more natural — using the wrong form loses all marks.
  • Forgetting to take the absolute value when reading off $R\sin\alpha$ from a $-b\cos x$ term, leading to a negative $\sin\alpha$ and an out-of-range $\alpha$.
  • Computing $\alpha$ from $\tan\alpha=b/a$ without checking that both $\sin\alpha$ and $\cos\alpha$ are positive — the quadrant must be confirmed.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 (and FP equivalents): uses only the compound-angle identities $\sin(A\pm B)$ and $\cos(A\pm B)$, which are in the formula booklet. Do not generalise to $a\sin x+b\sin(x+\phi)$-type combinations beyond what compound-angle expansion handles directly.
