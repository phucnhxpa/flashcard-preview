Algebra & Functions

Extract sum and product of roots via Vieta's formulas

Family: Vieta'S Forward Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question states that $\alpha, \beta$ are roots of a given quadratic $ax^2+bx+c=0$ and asks for symmetric expressions in the roots (e.g. $\alpha^2+\beta^2$, $\tfrac{1}{\alpha}+\tfrac{1}{\beta}$, $\alpha^3+\beta^3$, or to form a new quadratic with related roots). Individual root values are never required. Cue phrases include 'without solving the equation', 'find a quadratic whose roots are...', or any sub-part that is symmetric in $\alpha$ and $\beta$.

Motivation — Why this works

For $ax^2+bx+c=0$, factoring as $a(x-\alpha)(x-\beta)=0$ and expanding gives $\alpha+\beta=-b/a$ and $\alpha\beta=c/a$ directly from the coefficients. Every symmetric polynomial in two variables can be rewritten in terms of the elementary symmetric polynomials $\alpha+\beta$ and $\alpha\beta$, so these two quantities form a complete toolkit. Extracting them up front avoids the quadratic formula entirely — crucial when the discriminant is negative or messy.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question names roots $\alpha,\beta$ but only asks about symmetric combinations of them.
  2. Rule out solving the quadratic explicitly: the discriminant may be negative or irrational, making direct substitution painful.
  3. Commit to Vieta's because every symmetric expression reduces to functions of $S=\alpha+\beta$ and $P=\alpha\beta$.
  4. Bridge: read $a,b,c$ off the equation in standard form and compute $S=-b/a$, $P=c/a$ as the first line of working.
  5. Tactically, write $S$ and $P$ as labelled values at the top of the page so every later sub-part can quote them.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rearrange the equation into standard descending form $ax^2+bx+c=0$ if not already.
  2. Identify the coefficients $a$, $b$, $c$ paying close attention to signs.
  3. Compute $S=\alpha+\beta=-b/a$.
  4. Compute $P=\alpha\beta=c/a$.
  5. Record $S$ and $P$ as named quantities to be reused in later parts.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $2x^2-5x+4=0$ with roots $\alpha,\beta$: identify $a=2,\ b=-5,\ c=4$. Then $\alpha+\beta=-b/a=-(-5)/2=\tfrac{5}{2}$ and $\alpha\beta=c/a=4/2=2$. So $\alpha^2+\beta^2=(\alpha+\beta)^2-2\alpha\beta=\tfrac{25}{4}-4=\tfrac{9}{4}$, obtained without ever solving the (complex-rooted) quadratic.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cubic $ax^3+bx^2+cx+d=0$ with roots $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$ (FP1/FP2 scope)
Use $\sum\alpha=-b/a$, $\sum\alpha\beta=c/a$, $\alpha\beta\gamma=-d/a$.
Equation not in standard form, e.g. $4-5x+2x^2=0$ or $3x^2=2x-7$
Rearrange to $ax^2+bx+c=0$ with descending powers before reading coefficients.
Leading coefficient is $1$ (monic), e.g. $x^2+px+q=0$
Then $\alpha+\beta=-p$ and $\alpha\beta=q$ directly.
Question asks to form a new quadratic with roots $\alpha+k,\beta+k$ or $1/\alpha,1/\beta$
Compute the new sum $S'$ and new product $P'$ from $S,P$, then write $x^2-S'x+P'=0$.

Common Misreadings

  • Using $\alpha+\beta=b/a$ instead of $-b/a$, dropping the sign.
  • Reading coefficients off an unsorted equation (e.g. taking $b=-5$ from $4-5x+2x^2$ as if $a=4$).
  • Solving the quadratic with the formula despite the question being purely symmetric — leading to complex or surd-laden arithmetic.
  • Forgetting to divide by $a$ when the quadratic is non-monic.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error on $b$: for $2x^2-5x+4=0$, $b=-5$, so $-b/a=5/2$ not $-5/2$.
  • Forgetting the leading coefficient: writing $\alpha\beta=c$ instead of $c/a$ when $a\neq 1$.
  • Mislabelling $S$ and $P$ midway through the question, then substituting them in the wrong slot in identities like $\alpha^2+\beta^2=S^2-2P$.

A-level Scope Notes

Vieta's for quadratics is core P1/P2 content; the cubic extension appears in Further Pure (FP1). All identities used (e.g. $\alpha^2+\beta^2=S^2-2P$, $\alpha^3+\beta^3=S^3-3SP$) are within Edexcel IAL Maths/Further Maths scope.
