Polar Coordinates

Convert horizontal-tangent condition on a polar curve to dy/dθ = 0 via y = r sin θ

Family: Cartesian Polar Bridge Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a polar-curve question asks for tangent lines 'parallel to the initial line', or equivalently 'horizontal tangents', or 'points of greatest distance from the initial line (x-axis)'. Visual/structural cues: a polar equation r = f(θ) is given, and the question references the initial line, the x-axis, or maximum perpendicular distance from it. Alternative phrasings include 'tangent parallel to θ = 0', 'tangent parallel to the x-axis', or 'where y is maximum on the curve'. The condition is geometric (tangent direction) but no direct dy/dx formula exists in polar form, so a bridge is needed.

Motivation — Why this works

A horizontal tangent means dy/dx = 0, and treating θ as a parameter gives dy/dx = (dy/dθ)/(dx/dθ), so the condition reduces to dy/dθ = 0 (provided dx/dθ ≠ 0 there). The Cartesian-polar bridge y = r sin θ converts the polar curve into a single-variable function of θ that can be differentiated by the product rule. This turns a geometric tangent condition into one trigonometric equation in θ, which is solvable by standard A-level techniques. The underlying principle is parametric differentiation combined with the polar-to-Cartesian identity y = r sin θ.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the phrase 'parallel to the initial line' (or equivalent) — the initial line is θ = 0, i.e. the positive x-axis, so this is a horizontal-tangent condition.
  2. Rule out setting dr/dθ = 0: that finds stationary values of r (max/min distance from the pole), not horizontal tangents. Also rule out the formula tan ψ = r/(dr/dθ), which gives the angle between the radius and tangent — useful but indirect for a tangent parallel to the x-axis.
  3. Commit to parametric differentiation: with θ as parameter, dy/dx = (dy/dθ)/(dx/dθ), so dy/dθ = 0 is the required condition (and check dx/dθ ≠ 0 there to exclude cusps/vertical tangents).
  4. Bridge to a concrete expression: since y = r sin θ and r = r(θ) is given explicitly, write y as a function of θ alone, then differentiate by the product rule.
  5. Tactical decision: simplify y using double-angle/product-to-sum identities before differentiating (e.g. sin θ cos θ = ½ sin 2θ) so the derivative is cleaner and the resulting trig equation is easier to solve.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write y = r sin θ and substitute the given r(θ).
  2. Simplify using trig identities where helpful (e.g. sin θ cos θ = ½ sin 2θ).
  3. Differentiate: dy/dθ = (dr/dθ) sin θ + r cos θ.
  4. Set dy/dθ = 0 and solve the resulting trigonometric equation for θ in the required range.
  5. For each θ, compute r = r(θ) to obtain the polar coordinates of the tangent point; verify dx/dθ ≠ 0 there.
  6. If asked, convert (r, θ) to Cartesian (x, y) = (r cos θ, r sin θ) or state the tangent line y = r sin θ.

Worked Micro-Example

For $r = 1 + \cos\theta$: $y = (1+\cos\theta)\sin\theta = \sin\theta + \tfrac{1}{2}\sin 2\theta$. Then $\frac{dy}{d\theta} = \cos\theta + \cos 2\theta = 0$. Using $\cos 2\theta = 2\cos^2\theta - 1$ gives $2\cos^2\theta + \cos\theta - 1 = 0$, so $(2\cos\theta - 1)(\cos\theta + 1) = 0$, giving $\cos\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}$ (i.e. $\theta = \pm\pi/3$) — the cardioid's horizontal tangent points.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Tangent perpendicular to the initial line (vertical tangent)
Use x = r cos θ instead and set dx/dθ = (dr/dθ) cos θ − r sin θ = 0 (note the minus sign).
Tangent at the pole
Solve r(θ) = 0; the tangent at the pole is the half-line θ = θ₀ where r vanishes.
Maximum perpendicular distance from the initial line
This is asking for max y, which is exactly dy/dθ = 0 — same move, different phrasing.

Common Misreadings

  • Setting dr/dθ = 0 thinking it gives tangent conditions — it gives extrema of r (distance from pole), not horizontal/vertical tangents.
  • Confusing 'parallel to the initial line' with 'perpendicular to the initial line' (which needs dx/dθ = 0) or with 'tangent at the pole' (which needs r = 0).
  • Treating 'tangent parallel to initial line' as merely θ = 0, missing that it is a condition on the curve's slope.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors in product rule, especially for dx/dθ which has a MINUS sign: dx/dθ = (dr/dθ) cos θ − r sin θ.
  • Forgetting to verify dx/dθ ≠ 0 at the solution θ — otherwise the point may be a cusp or the pole, not a genuine horizontal tangent.
  • Solving the trig equation only in [0, π/2] when the question allows a wider range (e.g. [0, 2π] or [−π, π]) and missing valid points.
  • Not simplifying with double-angle identities first, leading to a messier equation that's harder to factor.

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) polar coordinates. The conversions x = r cos θ, y = r sin θ are in the formula booklet; the parametric form dy/dx = (dy/dθ)/(dx/dθ) is standard. Avoid invoking the angle-between-radius-and-tangent formula tan ψ = r/(dr/dθ) unless explicitly studied — the y = r sin θ approach is the expected method.
