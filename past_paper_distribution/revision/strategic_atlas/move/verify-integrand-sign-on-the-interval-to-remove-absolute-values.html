Integration

Verify integrand sign on the interval to remove absolute values

Family: Sign Analysis Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever you are about to evaluate a definite integral that represents a geometric quantity (area under a curve, arc length, volume) OR whenever an integrand contains an expression whose sign is not obvious — e.g. an absolute value |f(x)|, a square root √(g(x))² that collapses to |g(x)|, or a product of factors like 2x(x−3)⁴. The cue is: the bounds are given numerically, and you need to decide whether to integrate f(x) directly, integrate −f(x), or split the interval at zeros of f. Equivalent phrasings: 'check the curve stays above the x-axis', 'determine the sign of 1 − x² on [a,b]', 'remove the modulus before integrating'.

Motivation — Why this works

A definite integral computes signed area, but geometric quantities (area, arc length) are unsigned, so blindly writing Area = ∫ₐᵇ y dx fails if y changes sign. By analysing each factor of the integrand on the interval — using parity (even powers ≥ 0), known zeros, and simple bounds (e.g. x < 1 ⇒ 1 − x² > 0) — you justify dropping the modulus and writing a single clean expression. This both prevents sign errors and avoids unnecessary case-splitting at zeros that don't actually fall inside [a,b].

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand involves either an absolute value, a collapsed square root |g(x)|, or a product whose sign isn't immediately clear, AND the question asks for a positive geometric quantity.
  2. Rule out simply integrating as-is — that gives signed area, which could be wrong if the curve dips below the axis or if |·| flips sign on part of [a,b].
  3. Rule out always splitting the interval at every zero — many zeros lie outside [a,b], so a sign check often shows no split is needed.
  4. Commit to a sign analysis: locate the zeros of each factor, then test the sign of each factor on [a,b] using parity and inequalities.
  5. Tactical decision: if every factor has constant sign on [a,b], drop |·| with the appropriate ± and proceed with a single integral; if a sign change occurs at some c ∈ (a,b), split as ∫ₐᶜ + ∫꜀ᵇ with opposite signs.
  6. Sanity-check the conclusion against the geometric meaning (area or arc length must be positive).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify all zeros of the integrand: solve f(x) = 0 (or g(x) = 0 inside |g|).
  2. List the zeros that lie inside [a,b]; ignore those outside.
  3. On [a,b], determine the sign of each factor: even powers (x−c)^{2k} ≥ 0; linear factors via the bound; squares always ≥ 0.
  4. Combine factor signs to get the sign of the whole integrand on [a,b].
  5. If sign is constant: write integrand without |·| (with appropriate sign) and integrate over [a,b].
  6. If sign changes at c ∈ (a,b): split ∫ₐᵇ = ∫ₐᶜ (sign 1) + ∫꜀ᵇ (sign 2) with the appropriate ± to keep each piece positive.

Worked Micro-Example

Compute area under y = 2x(x−3)⁴ from x=0 to x=2. Zeros: x = 0, 3. On (0,2): 2x > 0 and (x−3)⁴ > 0 (even power, nonzero since 3 ∉ [0,2]). So y > 0 on (0,2), and Area = ∫₀² 2x(x−3)⁴ dx directly — no modulus needed.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Integrand involves √((1−x²)²) = |1−x²| with bounds [1/2, 3/4]
Since x² ≤ 9/16 < 1 throughout, 1−x² > 0, so |1−x²| = 1−x². Drop the modulus and integrate (1+x²)/(1−x²).
Curve dips below x-axis on part of [a,b], e.g. y = sin x on [0, 3π/2]
Split at the zero π: Area = ∫₀^π sin x dx + ∫_π^{3π/2} (−sin x) dx, taking absolute value on the negative piece.
Arc length / surface area integrand under a square root
After simplifying the radicand to a perfect square, the result is |·|; resolve sign using the interval before integrating.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming a curve must cross the x-axis between two zeros of the derivative — e.g. y = 2x(x−3)⁴ has a turning point at x = 3/5 but stays positive there.
  • Treating √(g²) as g rather than |g|, which silently introduces a sign error when g < 0 on the interval.
  • Splitting the interval at every zero of the integrand, even ones outside [a,b].

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to check sign for area questions — blindly equating Area to ∫ₐᵇ y dx gives the wrong answer when y < 0 on part of the interval.
  • Mishandling intervals that straddle a sign change (e.g. crossing x = 1 when 1−x² is in play) — failing to split leads to cancellation and an underestimate.
  • Confusing 'even power ≥ 0' with 'even power > 0' — even powers vanish at their root, which matters if that root lies in [a,b].

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 (areas under curves) and FP2/FP3 (arc length, surface area). No formula booklet entry is invoked — this is a reasoning step preceding the use of standard integrals. Avoid generalising to complex-valued integrands or improper integrals with sign issues at infinity, which are out of scope.
