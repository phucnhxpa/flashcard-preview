Differentiation

Select quotient rule when target form shows v² in denominator

Family: Rule Selection Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The function is presented as a single ratio y = u/v of two differentiable expressions, and the printed target form for dy/dx displays the original denominator squared (e.g. (3 + cos x)²) as one consolidated denominator. This is the signature output of the quotient rule. Equivalent phrasings include 'show that dy/dx = ... / (denominator)²' or any answer template where the denominator is already factored as v². The cue is structural — the shape of the required answer, not just the shape of y.

Motivation — Why this works

The quotient rule states that for y = u/v, dy/dx = (v·u' − u·v')/v². Applying it directly delivers the v² denominator already assembled, so only numerator simplification remains. Alternative routes (rewriting as u·v^{-1} and using product + chain rule) yield algebraically equivalent expressions but split across two fractional powers of v that must then be recombined over a common denominator — extra work that risks sign and bracket errors. Matching the rule to the target form minimises algebraic friction and aligns the working with the mark scheme's expected structure.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice y is a single fraction u/v with both numerator and denominator non-trivial functions of x.
  2. I rule out rewriting as u·v^{-1} with product/chain rule because the target answer already shows v² as one denominator — recombining fractions afterwards would be wasted effort.
  3. I commit to the quotient rule because its output template (v·u' − u·v')/v² matches the printed target form exactly.
  4. First concrete operation: label u and v, then compute u' and v' separately before substituting.
  5. Tactically, I keep brackets around u, v, u', v' on substitution to protect signs — particularly the minus from differentiating cos.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify u (numerator) and v (denominator) of y.
  2. Differentiate u and v separately to obtain du/dx and dv/dx.
  3. Substitute into dy/dx = (v·du/dx − u·dv/dx)/v², keeping each factor bracketed.
  4. Expand and simplify the numerator only; leave the denominator as v² to match the target form.
  5. Use identities (e.g. sin²x + cos²x = 1) on the numerator if the target form requires further consolidation.

Worked Micro-Example

For y = \frac{2+\sin x}{3+\cos x}, set u = 2+\sin x, v = 3+\cos x, so u' = \cos x, v' = -\sin x. Then \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{(3+\cos x)\cos x - (2+\sin x)(-\sin x)}{(3+\cos x)^2} = \frac{3\cos x + \cos^2 x + 2\sin x + \sin^2 x}{(3+\cos x)^2} = \frac{1 + 3\cos x + 2\sin x}{(3+\cos x)^2}.

Variants — Same move, different surface

y = e^x/(1+x²) with target denominator (1+x²)²
Same quotient-rule selection; u' = e^x, v' = 2x, numerator becomes e^x(1+x² − 2x) = e^x(1−x)².
y = (ln x)/x with target denominator x²
Quotient rule gives (1 − ln x)/x² directly; product-rule route would force recombination over x².
Target form is a sum of two separate terms rather than a single fraction
Prefer the product rule on u·v^{-1} so each term naturally appears separately, avoiding the need to split a single fraction.
Denominator is a simple monomial, e.g. y = (sin x)/x²
Either rule works, but rewriting as x^{-2}·sin x with product rule is often quicker since v² = x⁴ may be larger than needed.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating y = u/v as a product u·(1/v) and immediately invoking the product rule without checking the target form.
  • Cross-multiplying to clear the denominator before differentiating, which alters the relationship and does not yield dy/dx directly.
  • Reading the squared denominator in the target as a hint to square something in the numerator.

Pitfalls

  • Swapping the subtraction order to u·v' − v·u', causing a global sign error.
  • Forgetting that d/dx(cos x) = −sin x, so the double negative −u·(−sin x) becomes +u·sin x.
  • Dropping brackets on substitution, e.g. writing 3+cos x · cos x instead of (3+cos x)·cos x.
  • Expanding or 'simplifying' the v² denominator unnecessarily, losing the form demanded by the question.

A-level Scope Notes

Quotient rule is core P3/P4 content in Edexcel IAL Maths; recognising target-form cues to choose between quotient and product+chain routes is standard exam technique within scope.
