Trigger — When to use

Fires when a polynomial in x = tan θ has been recast as tan(nθ) = k (typically via a multiple-angle identity), and you need to list all n distinct roots of the polynomial. Cue phrases: 'hence find the roots of', 'show that the roots are tan(...)', or a degree-n polynomial whose roots are known to be values of tan θ. Also fires whenever a question demands every solution of tan(nθ) = k within a θ-range of length π/n × n = π.

Motivation — Why this works

Since tan has period π, the equation tan(nθ) = k has general solution nθ = arctan(k) + kπ for integer k, i.e. θ = (1/n)[arctan(k) + kπ]. Cycling k = 0, 1, …, n−1 gives n values of θ spaced by π/n, covering one full period of tan θ, hence yielding n distinct values of tan θ. Any further integer would shift θ by π and reproduce a previous tan value, so exactly n roots are produced — matching the degree of the polynomial.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the polynomial's roots have been identified as tan θ for θ satisfying tan(nθ) = k, so the problem reduces to listing solutions of a single trig equation.
  2. Rule out the temptation to solve the polynomial directly (degree ≥ 3 with no rational roots) — the trig route is the intended one.
  3. Commit to the general solution θ = (1/n)[arctan(k) + mπ] because tan has period π and we need exactly n distinct tan values.
  4. Bridge: choose m = 0, 1, …, n−1 (one full period of tan θ) and compute each θ, then take tan θ to recover each polynomial root.
  5. Tactical: keep arctan(k) in radians or degrees consistently; if the question filters (positive roots, largest root, etc.), identify which m's correspond after computing all n values.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the general solution: nθ = arctan(k) + mπ, so θ = (1/n)[arctan(k) + mπ].
  2. Substitute m = 0, 1, …, n−1 to obtain n distinct angles θ_m.
  3. Evaluate x_m = tan(θ_m) for each m — these are the n roots of the polynomial.
  4. Apply any selection criterion from the question (positive roots, sum, product, specific root).
  5. Sanity-check: sum or product of roots should match Vieta's relations from the polynomial.

Worked Micro-Example

A quartic in x has roots x = tan θ where tan 4θ = −2. General solution: 4θ = arctan(−2) + mπ, so θ = ¼[arctan(−2) + mπ]. With arctan(−2) ≈ −63.43°: m=0 gives θ ≈ −15.86°, x ≈ −0.284; m=1 gives θ ≈ 29.14°, x ≈ 0.558; m=2 gives θ ≈ 74.14°, x ≈ 3.51; m=3 gives θ ≈ 119.14°, x ≈ −1.79. The positive roots are x ≈ 0.558 and x ≈ 3.51.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cubic whose roots are tan θ with tan 3θ = k
Use m = 0, 1, 2 to obtain 3 roots, since one period of tan θ is covered by three values spaced π/3 apart.
Polynomial in x = cot θ from a cot(nθ) identity
Use the same n-fold enumeration but evaluate cot(θ_m) instead of tan(θ_m).
Roots are sin²θ or related, derived from sin(nθ) = 0 or cos(nθ) = k
Sin/cos have period 2π, so one must be careful: distinct values of sin²θ may require only m = 1, …, ⌊n/2⌋ since θ and π−θ give equal sines.
Question asks for sum/product of roots
Skip explicit enumeration; use Vieta's formulas on the polynomial directly, with the trig form only confirming the count.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 'tan 4θ = −2' as needing solutions in 0 ≤ θ < 2π and producing 8 values, doubling the root count.
  • Believing each m gives a different polynomial root only if θ_m lies in (0, π/2); negative or obtuse θ_m still yield valid roots.
  • Confusing the index m (period counter) with the polynomial degree.

Pitfalls

  • Using too few or too many values of m — must use exactly n consecutive integers for a degree-n polynomial.
  • Calculator left in wrong mode (radians vs degrees) when evaluating arctan and the subsequent tan values.
  • Assuming m = 0 yields the 'principal' positive root; arctan(k) can be negative, so n = 0 may give a negative tan value.
  • Selecting roots by the sign of θ rather than the sign of tan θ when the question filters by root sign.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope: multiple-angle identities (e.g. tan 3θ, tan 4θ via De Moivre or expansion) combined with general solutions of trig equations. No complex analysis or roots-of-unity machinery beyond De Moivre is required.
