Complex Numbers

Expand Cartesian complex product and substitute i² = −1

Family: Complex Arithmetic Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks for a product (or square) of complex numbers and both factors are presented in Cartesian form a + bi, with the answer required 'in the form a + bi'. Typical phrasings: 'Find z₁z₂', 'Express (3+2i)(1−i) in the form a+bi', 'Hence find z²'. No modulus or argument is supplied, so polar multiplication is not the natural route. Also fires for products like (a+bi)(a−bi) when not specifically using the conjugate identity.

Motivation — Why this works

Complex numbers obey ordinary algebra except for the single rule i² = −1, so any product of binomials in i can be expanded by the distributive law (FOIL) and then simplified using that rule. This automatically yields a unique decomposition into real and imaginary parts, matching the required a + bi form. The method is reliable, requires no conversion, and works regardless of the signs or sizes of a, b, c, d.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices both numbers are written as a + bi (Cartesian), and the target form is also a + bi.
  2. Rules out modulus-argument multiplication because no r, θ are given and converting would waste time.
  3. Commits to algebraic expansion since (a+bi)(c+di) is just a binomial product with one extra simplification rule.
  4. Bridges to action by writing the FOIL expansion explicitly, keeping i as a literal symbol throughout.
  5. Decides tactically to defer the i² → −1 substitution until after expansion, then collects real and imaginary terms separately.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the product (a+bi)(c+di) and expand using FOIL: ac + adi + bci + bdi².
  2. Simplify each term, keeping signs explicit.
  3. Replace i² with −1 wherever it appears.
  4. Group real parts (ac − bd) and imaginary parts (ad + bc)i.
  5. Write the final answer in the form a + bi.

Worked Micro-Example

Find $(3+2i)(1-i)$ in the form $a+bi$.\\ Expand: $(3)(1) + (3)(-i) + (2i)(1) + (2i)(-i) = 3 - 3i + 2i - 2i^2$.\\ Substitute $i^2 = -1$: $3 - 3i + 2i + 2 = (3+2) + (-3+2)i$.\\ Therefore the product is $5 - i$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Both numbers in modulus-argument form, e.g. $z_1 = r_1(\cos\theta_1 + i\sin\theta_1)$
Use the polar rule: multiply moduli and add arguments, $z_1z_2 = r_1r_2[\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2) + i\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2)]$, then convert back to Cartesian if required.
Squaring a complex number $(a+bi)^2$
Apply the binomial expansion: $a^2 + 2abi + b^2i^2 = (a^2 - b^2) + 2abi$.
Product with a conjugate $(a+bi)(a-bi)$
Recognise the difference-of-squares pattern giving $a^2 + b^2$ (a real number), shortcutting full expansion.
Product of three or more Cartesian complex numbers
Multiply two at a time, collecting into a + bi after each stage to keep terms manageable.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating i as a variable to be solved for rather than a constant satisfying i² = −1.
  • Assuming the product must be computed via polar form because the topic chapter is 'complex numbers'.
  • Reading (a+bi)(c+di) as ac + bdi, i.e. multiplying real parts and imaginary parts separately.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to apply i² = −1 and leaving a term in i² in the final answer.
  • Sign errors with the cross terms, e.g. writing $2i\cdot(-i) = +2i^2$ instead of $-2i^2$.
  • Failing to collect like terms, leaving the answer as e.g. $3 - 3i + 2i + 2$ rather than $5 - i$.
  • Mis-identifying the imaginary part as including the $i$ (the imaginary part of $5-i$ is $-1$, not $-i$).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP1/FP2) complex number arithmetic; uses only the defining relation i² = −1 and standard distributive expansion.
