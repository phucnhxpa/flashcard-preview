Audit note

While coordinate_geom/parametric-elimination mentions the Pythagorean identity, the specific three-step pattern (invert X for cosθ, invert Y for sinθ, apply identity) is the dominant locus technique in FP3 conics and deserves an explicit canonical entry distinct from general parameter elimination.

Evidence: 2::Jan_2022::Q8, 2::Oct_2021::Q3, 2::Jun_2021::Q8

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a locus is delivered as $X=f(\theta)$, $Y=g(\theta)$ (an intercept, midpoint, or foot-of-perpendicular built from a conic's tangent/normal) and the question demands a Cartesian equation. The cue is each coordinate being a clean multiple of $\cos\theta$ or $\sin\theta$, inviting you to make those the subjects.

Motivation — Why this works

If $X$ depends only on $\cos\theta$ and $Y$ only on $\sin\theta$, you can invert each to get $\cos\theta=h(X)$ and $\sin\theta=k(Y)$; substituting into the identity $\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta=1$ annihilates $\theta$ and yields the Cartesian relation directly.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. After finding the locus point's coordinates, check whether $X$ isolates $\cos\theta$ and $Y$ isolates $\sin\theta$ cleanly.
  2. If so, rearrange $X=f(\theta)$ to make $\cos\theta$ the subject and $Y=g(\theta)$ to make $\sin\theta$ the subject.
  3. Recall the only universal link between $\cos\theta$ and $\sin\theta$ is $\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta=1$, so squaring is the route.
  4. Substitute both expressions into the identity to eliminate $\theta$ entirely.
  5. Tidy to the requested form (clear denominators; relabel $X,Y$ as $x,y$), and identify any constants $a,b$ asked for.
  6. If $X,Y$ each involve BOTH $\cos\theta$ and $\sin\theta$ (a shared denominator), switch to combining $x^2+y^2$ and a weighted sum instead of direct inversion.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. From $X=f(\theta)$ solve $\cos\theta=\dfrac{X}{(\text{coeff})}$; from $Y=g(\theta)$ solve $\sin\theta=\dfrac{Y}{(\text{coeff})}$.
  2. Square each: $\cos^2\theta=\dfrac{X^2}{(\text{coeff})^2}$, $\sin^2\theta=\dfrac{Y^2}{(\text{coeff})^2}$.
  3. Add and set equal to $1$: $\dfrac{X^2}{(\text{coeff})^2}+\dfrac{Y^2}{(\text{coeff})^2}=1$.
  4. Multiply out to clear denominators and rename $X,Y\to x,y$.
  5. (Shared-denominator case) instead form $x^2+y^2$ and $\alpha x^2+\beta y^2$ to cancel the common denominator $D$.

Worked Micro-Example

A normal to $\frac{x^2}{64}+\frac{y^2}{36}=1$ gives midpoint $M\left(\frac{7\cos\theta}{4},-\frac{7\sin\theta}{3}\right)$. Invert: $\cos\theta=\frac{4X}{7}$, $\sin\theta=-\frac{3Y}{7}$. Then $\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta=1$ gives $\frac{16X^2}{49}+\frac{9Y^2}{49}=1$, i.e. $16x^2+9y^2=49$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Intersection point has a shared denominator, e.g. $x=\frac{12\cos\theta}{D}$, $y=\frac{18\sin\theta}{D}$ with $D=4\cos^2\theta+9\sin^2\theta$.
Direct inversion is blocked; instead compute $x^2+y^2=\frac{36}{D}$ and $9x^2+4y^2=\frac{1296}{D^2}$, then eliminate $D$ to get $(x^2+y^2)^2=9x^2+4y^2$.
Hyperbola locus parametrised by $\sec\theta,\tan\theta$.
Make $\sec\theta$ and $\tan\theta$ the subjects and apply $\sec^2\theta-\tan^2\theta=1$ (a difference, not a sum) to eliminate the parameter.
A half-angle parameter $t=\tan\frac{\theta}{2}$ appears (from $\frac{2\sin\theta}{1+\cos\theta}$ etc.).
Express $t$ from each line equation and equate the two expressions for $t$ rather than using $\cos^2+\sin^2$; this yields the Cartesian locus after cross-multiplying.

Common Misreadings

  • Assuming the identity route always works, even when $X$ and $Y$ each mix $\cos\theta$ and $\sin\theta$ over a common denominator (then the $x^2+y^2$ combination is needed).
  • Forgetting to halve both coordinates for a midpoint locus before inverting, so the constants come out wrong.
  • Using $\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta=1$ on a hyperbola where $\sec^2\theta-\tan^2\theta=1$ is the correct identity.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping a negative sign when inverting (e.g. $\sin\theta=-\frac{3Y}{7}$); it disappears on squaring but mis-stating it earlier causes errors if the sign is reused.
  • Squaring only one coordinate or forgetting to square the coefficient in the denominator.
  • Leaving the answer with fractions when the mark scheme wants integer coefficients (clear the common denominator).
  • In the shared-denominator case, mis-tracking $D$ versus $D^2$ when combining $x^2+y^2$ with the weighted sum.
