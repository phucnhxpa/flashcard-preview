Sequences & Series

Reapply AP sum formula with n as the unknown to obtain a quadratic

Family: Parameter Shift Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires in multi-part AP problems where an earlier part fixed a and/or d, and a later part gives a target sum S_n with n unknown — typically signalled by a 'show that' instruction producing a quadratic in n (e.g. n² + 8n − 1073 = 0). The cue is that the only remaining unknown linking S_n, a, and d is n itself. Phrasings include 'find the number of terms', 'find n such that the total is …', or 'show that n satisfies …'.

Motivation — Why this works

The standard sum formula S_n = (n/2)(2a + (n−1)d) is intrinsically quadratic in n because of the n(n−1) product. Once a and d are numerical, substituting the given S_n collapses the formula into a single-variable quadratic equation in n. The strategic insight is recognising that n has shifted role: in earlier parts it was a known parameter, but now it is the unknown to be solved for. No new identity is needed — the same formula does double duty.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the target is a quadratic in n, so I need an equation where n is the only variable.
  2. I rule out introducing new identities or the nth-term formula alone — only the sum formula contains an n(n−1) term that can yield a quadratic.
  3. I commit to reusing S_n = (n/2)(2a + (n−1)d) because a and d are already pinned down from the earlier part.
  4. Bridge: substitute the known a, d, and S_n, then clear the fraction by multiplying by 2.
  5. Tactically, I expand carefully, collect like terms, and divide through by any common factor to match the exact form in the 'show that'.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write S_n = (n/2)(2a + (n−1)d) with the known a, d, and given S_n.
  2. Substitute numerical values: e.g. 2146 = (n/2)(2(18) + (n−1)(4)).
  3. Simplify the bracket: 36 + 4n − 4 = 4n + 32.
  4. Multiply both sides by 2 to remove the fraction: 4292 = n(4n + 32).
  5. Expand and bring all terms to one side: 4n² + 32n − 4292 = 0.
  6. Divide through by the common factor (here 4) to match the target: n² + 8n − 1073 = 0.

Worked Micro-Example

Given a = 18, d = 4, S_n = 2146. Then $2146 = \tfrac{n}{2}(2(18) + (n-1)(4)) = \tfrac{n}{2}(4n+32)$. Multiply by 2: $4292 = 4n^2 + 32n$. Divide by 4 and rearrange: $n^2 + 8n - 1073 = 0$, which factorises (or solves by formula) to give n = 29.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Geometric series with known a and r, given S_n, find n
Reusing S_n = a(1−rⁿ)/(1−r) yields an exponential equation in n, solved by logarithms rather than a quadratic.
AP with a unknown but n and d known in a later part
Same formula reused but a is the free variable; result is linear in a, not quadratic.
AP given last term l and S_n with a, d known
Use S_n = (n/2)(a + l) together with l = a + (n−1)d; eliminating l still gives a quadratic in n.
Inequality version: 'find smallest n with S_n > N'
Form the same quadratic as an inequality in n, solve, then take the smallest integer n satisfying it.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating n as still a fixed parameter and trying to solve for a or d again.
  • Using only the nth-term formula u_n = a + (n−1)d, which is linear in n and cannot yield the required quadratic.
  • Assuming a new formula is needed because the question looks different from part (a).

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips when expanding (n−1)d and combining with 2a inside the bracket.
  • Forgetting to multiply both sides by 2, leaving a fraction that obscures the target form.
  • Not dividing through by a common factor at the end, so the equation does not literally match the printed 'show that'.
  • Sign errors when moving the constant term across to form the standard quadratic.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (P1/P2) arithmetic series content; uses only S_n = (n/2)(2a + (n−1)d) and standard quadratic manipulation.
