Trigonometry

Apply the Sine Rule in SSA configuration to find an angle opposite a known side

Family: Sine Rule Application Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question gives two sides of a triangle and one angle that is NOT between them — i.e. the known angle is opposite one of the known sides (the SSA configuration). Equivalently, you can identify one complete 'side–opposite-angle' pair and a second known side whose opposite angle is the unknown. This rules out the Cosine Rule (which needs SAS or SSS). Surface phrasings include: 'find angle X given AB, BC and angle BAC', or 'find the angle at C in triangle ABC where you know one other angle and its opposite side'.

Motivation — Why this works

The Sine Rule a/sin A = b/sin B = c/sin C pairs each side with its opposite angle, so when you have one full pair plus one extra side, you get a single equation in one unknown (sin of the missing angle). It exploits the fact that the ratio side/sin(opposite angle) is constant across a triangle (equal to the diameter of the circumscribed circle). The Cosine Rule could in principle work but would require either the included angle or all three sides — strictly more information than is available here. Hence the Sine Rule is the most direct and economical tool.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the data inventory: two sides and one angle are given. Identify which side each angle is opposite to by reading the vertex labels carefully.
  2. Check whether the known angle is the INCLUDED angle between the two known sides (SAS) or OPPOSITE one of them (SSA). Here it is opposite, so the configuration is SSA.
  3. Rule out the Cosine Rule: it needs SAS or SSS. With SSA we cannot form cos A = (b² + c² − a²)/2bc directly, so the Cosine Rule is rejected.
  4. Commit to the Sine Rule because we have a complete (side, opposite angle) pair and a second side whose opposite angle is the unknown — this gives exactly one equation in one unknown.
  5. Set up the proportion with the unknown's sine isolated on one side: sin(unknown angle)/(its opposite side) = sin(known angle)/(its opposite side), then rearrange.
  6. Before taking arcsin, check that the resulting value is ≤ 1 (otherwise no triangle exists), and decide whether the obtuse alternative is geometrically possible (ambiguous case).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Label the triangle and explicitly pair each known side with its opposite angle.
  2. Write the Sine Rule in the form sin(unknown angle)/opposite side = sin(known angle)/its opposite side.
  3. Substitute the numerical values, keeping surds/exact forms where possible.
  4. Rearrange to isolate sin(unknown angle) = (opposite side) × sin(known angle) / (its opposite side).
  5. Verify the right-hand side is in [−1, 1]; take arcsin to get the principal (acute) value.
  6. Decide if the obtuse supplement (π − principal value) is also a valid solution by comparing side lengths or using triangle-angle-sum constraints.
  7. State the answer in the requested units (degrees or radians) and to the requested precision.

Worked Micro-Example

In $\triangle ABC$, $AB = 21$, $BC = 13$, and $\angle BAC = 25^\circ$. Find $\angle ACB = x^\circ$.\\ Pair: $BC = 13$ opposite $25^\circ$; $AB = 21$ opposite $x^\circ$.\\ Sine Rule: $\dfrac{\sin x^\circ}{21} = \dfrac{\sin 25^\circ}{13}$.\\ So $\sin x^\circ = \dfrac{21\sin 25^\circ}{13} \approx 0.6827$, giving $x \approx 43.05^\circ$ (acute case).

Variants — Same move, different surface

Unknown is a side rather than an angle (ASA or AAS)
Same Sine Rule, but rearrange for the side: side = (sin opposite angle) × (known side)/(sin its opposite angle).
Angle given in radians (e.g. π/6 or 0.43)
Set calculator to radian mode; keep π expressions exact where possible (e.g. sin(π/6) = 1/2).
SAS or SSS data given instead
Switch to the Cosine Rule: a² = b² + c² − 2bc cos A, then optionally finish with the Sine Rule.
Genuine ambiguous SSA (acute known angle opposite the shorter known side)
Report BOTH the acute value and its obtuse supplement π − θ as candidate angles, unless triangle constraints rule one out.

Common Misreadings

  • Pairing a side with the wrong opposite angle — e.g. pairing AB with ∠BAC instead of ∠ACB — because the diagram is not to scale or vertex labels are non-standard.
  • Mistaking SSA for SAS and trying to apply the Cosine Rule with the wrong angle as the 'included' one.
  • Treating the Cosine Rule as the default for any 'find an angle' problem, even when a complete side–opposite-angle pair is already available.

Pitfalls

  • Inverting the proportion incorrectly when rearranging for sin(unknown).
  • Forgetting the ambiguous case: arcsin returns only the acute principal value, but the obtuse supplement may also fit the triangle.
  • Using degree mode when the answer is required in radians (or vice versa).
  • Rounding intermediate surd values (e.g. 6√3) to decimals too early and losing accuracy.
  • Failing to check sin(unknown) ≤ 1; a value > 1 signals no such triangle exists or a setup error.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P2/P3) — the Sine Rule a/sin A = b/sin B = c/sin C appears in the formula booklet. Stay with non-degenerate triangles; do not invoke the extended Sine Rule (= 2R, circumradius) as it is outside the IAL syllabus.
