Sequences & Series

Decompose large periodic sum via Euclidean division of index by period

Family: Modular Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when asked to evaluate $\sum_{r=1}^{N} u_r$ for a sequence with known period $p$ and known cycle sum $S$, where $N$ is not a multiple of $p$. Typical cues: a recurrence has been shown to repeat every few terms, the upper limit is large (e.g. 100, 150, 2024), and direct term-by-term addition is infeasible. Also applies when the question first asks you to 'show that $u_{r+p} = u_r$' before requesting a long sum.

Motivation — Why this works

A periodic sequence with period $p$ partitions $\{1,2,\dots,N\}$ into $q = \lfloor N/p \rfloor$ complete cycles plus a remainder block of length $N - pq$. Each complete cycle contributes the same fixed sum $S$, so the total reduces to $qS$ plus the sum of the first $(N \bmod p)$ terms of the cycle. This converts an $N$-term sum into one multiplication and at most $p-1$ additions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the sequence is periodic with period $p$ and that one full cycle sums to a known value $S$.
  2. Rule out direct summation or AP/GP formulas — the sequence is neither arithmetic nor geometric, so $S_n$ formulas don't apply.
  3. Commit to splitting the index range into 'complete cycles + leftover' because periodicity makes each cycle contribute identically.
  4. Bridge: write $N = pq + s$ via Euclidean division to expose $q$ (number of cycles) and $s$ (length of remainder).
  5. Tactically, identify the leftover terms by reducing their indices mod $p$ — e.g. $u_{N-s+1} = u_1, u_{N-s+2} = u_2, \dots$ — and add their known values to $qS$.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Verify the period $p$ and compute the cycle sum $S = u_1 + u_2 + \dots + u_p$.
  2. Apply Euclidean division: write $N = pq + s$ with $0 \le s < p$.
  3. Compute the bulk contribution $qS$ from the $q$ complete cycles (covering terms $1$ to $pq$).
  4. Identify the remainder terms as $u_{pq+1}, \dots, u_{pq+s}$, and use periodicity to set them equal to $u_1, \dots, u_s$.
  5. Add: total $= qS + (u_1 + u_2 + \dots + u_s)$.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose $u_r$ has period 4 with $u_1=7, u_2=8, u_3=-7, u_4=22$, so cycle sum $S=30$. To find $\sum_{r=1}^{150} u_r$: write $150 = 4 \times 37 + 2$, giving 37 complete cycles plus 2 leftover terms. The 37 cycles contribute $37 \times 30 = 1110$. The leftover terms are $u_{149}=u_1=7$ and $u_{150}=u_2=8$. Total $= 1110 + 7 + 8 = 1125$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Trigonometric periodic sums such as $\sum_{r=1}^{N} \sin(r\pi/2)$
Period is 4 with cycle sum 0; the answer is determined entirely by $N \bmod 4$.
Recurrence-defined sequence where periodicity must first be proved
Compute $u_1, u_2, \dots$ until repetition is observed, justify $u_{r+p}=u_r$ from the recurrence, then apply the decomposition.
Sum starts from $r=m$ rather than $r=1$
Apply Euclidean division to $N - m + 1$ terms, or compute $\sum_1^N - \sum_1^{m-1}$ using the same technique on each.
Cycle sum equals zero
Bulk contribution vanishes; answer reduces entirely to the remainder block sum.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $150/4 = 37.5$ as if 37.5 cycles contribute $37.5 \times S$, ignoring the discrete remainder.
  • Misreading 'period 4' as 'every 4th term equals' rather than 'the whole block of 4 repeats'.
  • Confusing cycle sum $S$ with the average term value.

Pitfalls

  • Off-by-one error: 37 complete cycles cover terms 1 to 148 (not 1 to 149); the remainder is terms 149 and 150.
  • Mis-mapping leftover indices — must reduce mod $p$ carefully, e.g. $149 \bmod 4 = 1$ so $u_{149}=u_1$.
  • Forgetting to verify the cycle sum by actually adding $u_1+\dots+u_p$ rather than guessing.
  • Using the wrong value of a parameter (e.g. $k$) when evaluating remainder terms.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL P1/P2 sequences material: periodic sequences from recurrences, finite sums, and integer division. Uses no tools beyond arithmetic and the periodicity property $u_{r+p}=u_r$.
