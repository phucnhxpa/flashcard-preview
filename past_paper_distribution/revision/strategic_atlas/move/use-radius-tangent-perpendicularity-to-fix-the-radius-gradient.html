Coordinate Geometry

Use radius-tangent perpendicularity to fix the radius gradient

Family: Circle Tangent Property Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a line $l$ is stated to be tangent to a circle at a known point $P$, and you need the line through $P$ and the centre $N$ (or equivalently the radius/normal direction). Typical cues: 'tangent to the circle at $P$', 'find the equation of $PN$', or 'show that the centre lies on...'. Especially indicated when the circle equation itself is unknown, so discriminant or substitution methods are unavailable. The only geometric bridge between $l$ and $N$ is the radius drawn to the contact point.

Motivation — Why this works

By the circle theorem, the radius to the point of tangency is perpendicular to the tangent. This converts a geometric tangency condition into an algebraic gradient relation $m_{PN}\cdot m_l = -1$. Once $m_l$ is read off, $m_{PN}$ is forced, and the line through $P$ with that gradient is immediate. It bypasses any need for the circle's equation, centre coordinates, or radius length.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice $l$ is tangent at $P$ and the unknown line passes through $P$ and the centre $N$ — a radius.
  2. Rule out discriminant or simultaneous-equation methods because the circle equation is not given.
  3. Commit to the radius$\perp$tangent theorem as the only available bridge between $l$ and $N$.
  4. Read the gradient $m_l$ directly from the tangent's equation, rearranging to $y=mx+c$ form if needed.
  5. Apply $m_{PN}=-1/m_l$, then write the line through $P$ in point-gradient form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify $P$ (point of tangency) and read $m_l$ from the tangent equation.
  2. If $l$ is in form $ax+by+c=0$, compute $m_l=-a/b$.
  3. Compute $m_{PN}=-1/m_l$ (negative reciprocal).
  4. Apply point-gradient form $y-y_P = m_{PN}(x-x_P)$ to write the equation of $PN$.
  5. Simplify to the form requested (e.g. $y=mx+c$ or $ax+by+c=0$).

Worked Micro-Example

Tangent $l: y=\tfrac{1}{2}x+3$ touches a circle at $P(0,0)$; find the line $PN$ through the centre. Read $m_l=\tfrac{1}{2}$, so $m_{PN}=-1/(1/2)=-2$. Through $P(0,0)$: $y-0=-2(x-0)$, giving $y=-2x$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Tangent given in general form $ax+by+c=0$
Extract $m_l=-a/b$ first, then apply $m_{PN}=-1/m_l$ as usual.
Question asks for the normal to the circle at $P$
The normal coincides with $PN$, so the same perpendicularity gives its gradient directly.
Centre $N$ is known and tangent at $P$ is requested
Reverse the move: compute $m_{PN}$ from the two points, then $m_l=-1/m_{PN}$.
Tangent is horizontal ($m_l=0$)
Radius is vertical: $PN$ has equation $x=x_P$ (perpendicularity formula does not apply directly).
Tangent is vertical (undefined gradient)
Radius is horizontal: $PN$ has equation $y=y_P$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $PN$ as parallel to the tangent (using $m_l$ itself) instead of perpendicular.
  • Confusing the tangent line with the chord of contact or with the normal at a different point.
  • Assuming the centre is at the origin when only $P$ is at the origin.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the negative sign: writing $m_{PN}=1/m_l$ instead of $-1/m_l$.
  • Failing to rearrange $ax+by+c=0$ correctly before reading the gradient.
  • Mishandling the vertical/horizontal tangent edge cases where the gradient product rule breaks down.
  • Using a wrong point in point-gradient form (e.g. plugging in $N$ when $N$ is not yet known).

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only the Edexcel IAL P2/P3 circle theorem (radius $\perp$ tangent at point of contact) and the standard perpendicular-gradients condition $m_1 m_2=-1$ from coordinate geometry.
