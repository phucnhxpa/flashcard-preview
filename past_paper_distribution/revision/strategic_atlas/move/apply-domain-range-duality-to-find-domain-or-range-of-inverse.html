Apply domain-range duality to find domain or range of inverse

Family: Inverse Function Application Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

This move fires whenever a question asks for the domain or range of f⁻¹(x) and either the domain or range of f(x) is already known (given in the question stem, or established in an earlier part). Typical phrasings: 'state the domain of f⁻¹', 'find the range of f⁻¹', or a multi-part question where part (a) found the range of f and part (b)/(c) asks about f⁻¹. The visual cue is a question structure that has already pinned down properties of f, making independent re-derivation from the formula of f⁻¹ unnecessary.

Motivation — Why this works

Because f⁻¹ reverses the mapping of f, every input of f is an output of f⁻¹ and vice versa. This gives the identity domain(f⁻¹) = range(f) and range(f⁻¹) = domain(f). Invoking this duality converts the problem into one that has already been solved, avoiding a fresh asymptote/range analysis on a second rational expression. It is also the 'method-marked' route in mark schemes, since it demonstrates conceptual understanding rather than mere formula inspection.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question asks for domain or range of f⁻¹, and scan earlier parts: has the range (or domain) of f already been established?
  2. Consider the alternative — finding f⁻¹(x) explicitly and reading off restrictions from its formula. Reject this as redundant work and as risky if the formula has a coincidental restriction that masks the true domain.
  3. Commit to the duality: domain(f⁻¹) = range(f), range(f⁻¹) = domain(f). This is the cleanest route and earns method marks.
  4. Translate the known set into the answer, being careful to preserve any artificial restriction the question imposed on the domain of f (e.g. x ≥ 0).
  5. Cross-check by inspecting the formula of f⁻¹ if available — the excluded value in its denominator should match the value excluded from range(f).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. State the theorem explicitly: domain(f⁻¹) = range(f) and range(f⁻¹) = domain(f).
  2. Quote the relevant set from earlier work or from the question (e.g. range of f is ℝ \ {3}, or domain of f is x ∈ ℝ, x ≠ 1).
  3. Write the answer for f⁻¹ using the duality (e.g. domain of f⁻¹ is x ∈ ℝ, x ≠ 3).
  4. Optional cross-verification: inspect f⁻¹(x) and confirm its natural restriction matches.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $f(x)=\dfrac{2x}{x-1}$, $x\in\mathbb{R},\,x\neq1$, with range $f(x)\neq 2$. Then by duality: domain of $f^{-1}$ = range of $f$, so domain$(f^{-1})$ is $x\in\mathbb{R},\,x\neq 2$; and range$(f^{-1})$ = domain of $f$, so range$(f^{-1})$ is $y\in\mathbb{R},\,y\neq 1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Restricted-domain functions, e.g. f(x) = x² for x ≥ 0
The artificial restriction x ≥ 0 becomes the range of f⁻¹(x) = √x, giving range y ≥ 0. The duality still applies — just carry the restriction across.
Question asks for range of f⁻¹ instead of domain
Use the other half of the duality: range(f⁻¹) = domain(f). Read the domain of f straight from the question stem.
Domain of f given but range of f not yet found
First establish range(f) via asymptote analysis or by completing the square, then apply duality.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing the direction of the duality and writing domain(f⁻¹) = domain(f) instead of range(f).
  • Quoting the wrong excluded value — e.g. saying x ≠ 1 (the f-domain restriction) when the question asks for the f⁻¹-domain (which uses the f-range restriction).

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to carry through an artificial domain restriction on f (e.g. x ≥ 0) into the range of f⁻¹.
  • Relying solely on inspecting the formula of f⁻¹ — this can miss restrictions that are not visible in the simplified algebraic form.
  • Stating the answer without first stating the theorem, which can cost the method mark.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Pure (P3) inverse-function content. No formula booklet entry is required; the duality is part of the assumed theory of inverse functions. Avoid invoking measure-theoretic or multi-valued generalisations — IAL inverses are always one-to-one on the stated domain.
