Trigger — When to use

Fires when a question asks you to 'show that angle XYZ = 90°' (or that two specific line segments are perpendicular) and gives exact coordinates of all relevant points. Equivalent phrasings: 'prove that triangle XYZ is right-angled at Y', 'show that XY is perpendicular to YZ', or 'verify the lines meet at right angles'. The visual cue is a named vertex sandwiched between two other named points — the angle lives at the middle letter, so the two segments to test are the ones emanating from that vertex.

Motivation — Why this works

Two non-vertical lines are perpendicular iff the product of their gradients equals −1. This converts an angular condition into a single algebraic check: compute m₁ and m₂ from the coordinate formula and confirm m₁·m₂ = −1. It is more efficient than the alternatives (Pythagoras needs three squared distances; the dot product needs two direction vectors) and is the canonical Edexcel coordinate-geometry method. The underlying principle is that a 90° rotation sends a direction (a,b) to (−b,a), so gradients b/a and −a/b multiply to −1.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the angle is named at a specific vertex (the middle letter), so identify the two segments meeting there — these, and only these, are the ones whose gradients matter.
  2. List the available coordinate-geometry tools for proving a right angle: (a) gradient product = −1, (b) Pythagoras AB² + BC² = AC², (c) dot product of direction vectors = 0.
  3. Rule out Pythagoras as it requires three distance computations and squaring; rule out the dot product as it is heavier notationally and not the standard P2 instrument.
  4. Commit to the gradient method: it needs only two fraction calculations and one multiplication, and it makes the conclusion (m₁m₂ = −1) directly visible.
  5. Plan the bookkeeping: compute m of the first segment using (Δy/Δx) with the vertex as one endpoint, then m of the second segment with the vertex again as one endpoint, then multiply and explicitly write '= −1'.
  6. Finish with a one-sentence conclusion linking the algebraic result back to the geometric claim: 'product is −1, hence the segments are perpendicular, hence the angle is 90°'.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the vertex V where the right angle is claimed and the two other points A, B forming the segments VA and VB.
  2. Compute m_{VA} = (y_A − y_V)/(x_A − x_V), simplifying the fraction fully.
  3. Compute m_{VB} = (y_B − y_V)/(x_B − x_V), again simplifying.
  4. Form the product m_{VA} × m_{VB} and show the arithmetic explicitly.
  5. If the product equals −1, state: 'Since m_{VA} × m_{VB} = −1, VA ⊥ VB, therefore ∠AVB = 90°.'

Worked Micro-Example

Show ∠ACB = 90° for A(1,0), B(3,−10), C(7,−6). Compute $m_{CA} = \frac{0-(-6)}{1-7} = \frac{6}{-6} = -1$ and $m_{CB} = \frac{-10-(-6)}{3-7} = \frac{-4}{-4} = 1$. Then $m_{CA} \times m_{CB} = (-1)(1) = -1$, so $CA \perp CB$ and $\angle ACB = 90°$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

One of the two segments is vertical (Δx = 0, gradient undefined)
The product rule fails; instead show the other segment is horizontal (gradient 0) and conclude perpendicularity directly from 'vertical ⊥ horizontal'.
Vector-style proof requested or natural
Form direction vectors $\vec{VA}$ and $\vec{VB}$ and show $\vec{VA}\cdot\vec{VB} = 0$ — equivalent conclusion, vector language.
No gradients given, but three side-lengths computable
Use the converse of Pythagoras: verify $|VA|^2 + |VB|^2 = |AB|^2$. Slower but works when gradients are messy surds.

Common Misreadings

  • Computing gradients of the wrong pair of segments — e.g. for ∠PQR using PQ and PR instead of QP and QR (the angle lives at Q, so both segments must start at Q).
  • Reading 'show PQ is perpendicular to QR' as 'show triangle is right-angled at P' and choosing the wrong vertex.
  • Inferring perpendicularity by inspection ('one is steep up, one is steep down') without the algebraic product.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when subtracting negatives, e.g. $-26 - (-30) = 4$, not $-56$.
  • Failing to simplify the gradients, so that the negative-reciprocal relationship is hidden in the arithmetic.
  • Stating only that the gradients are 'negative reciprocals' without explicitly multiplying to display '= −1' — examiners require the product line.
  • Confusing the parallel condition (m₁ = m₂) with the perpendicular condition (m₁m₂ = −1).

A-level Scope Notes

Stays inside Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1/P2 coordinate geometry of straight lines). The result m₁m₂ = −1 is assumed known, not in the formula booklet. Avoid invoking 3D perpendicularity or general inner-product spaces — at this level the test is strictly for non-vertical lines in the 2D plane.
