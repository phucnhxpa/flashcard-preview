Vectors

Apply dot product formula with modulus to find angle between directions in 3D

Family: Dot Product Angle Frequency: 5 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question explicitly asks for the angle between two lines, between two vectors, or between a displacement vector and a line in 3D. Visual cues include: parametric/vector line equations from which direction vectors can be read off, two displacement vectors (e.g. AB and AC) given by coordinates, or wording like 'find the acute angle between l₁ and l₂'. Alternative phrasings: 'angle that AC makes with line l', 'angle between the two lines', 'show that the lines are perpendicular' (a special case where cos θ = 0).

Motivation — Why this works

The identity a·b = |a||b|cos θ rearranges to cos θ = (a·b)/(|a||b|), which after arccos gives the angle directly — this is the only A-level tool for angles in 3D since coordinate geometry doesn't extend cleanly. For lines (which have no inherent orientation), reversing a direction vector flips the sign of the dot product and gives the supplementary obtuse angle; taking the absolute value of the numerator collapses these to the unique acute angle. For two specific vectors (displacements with definite orientation), the sign is kept and an obtuse angle is legitimate.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the object asked for is an angle between two directions in 3D — direction vectors of lines, or displacement vectors between named points.
  2. Rule out the cross product route (|a×b| = |a||b|sin θ): arcsin is ambiguous between acute/obtuse, whereas arccos on [0°,180°] is single-valued, so dot product is safer and faster.
  3. Rule out coordinate-geometry/triangle methods: in 3D these require finding distances and applying the cosine rule, which is more arithmetic than the dot product.
  4. Commit to cos θ = (d₁·d₂)/(|d₁||d₂|). Crucial decision: is this 'angle between lines' (use |d₁·d₂| → forces acute) or 'angle between specified vectors' (keep sign → may be obtuse)? Read the question wording carefully.
  5. Extract the two vectors cleanly: from a line r = a + t d, take d (ignore a); from points A, B, take AB = B − A. Then compute the dot product, the two magnitudes, and arccos in that order, deferring rounding until the very last step.
  6. Sanity check: if the dot product is 0 the lines/vectors are perpendicular (θ = 90°); if cos θ comes out > 1 in magnitude, an arithmetic slip has occurred.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two vectors u, v: read direction vectors from line equations, or compute displacement vectors from given points.
  2. Compute the scalar product u·v = u₁v₁ + u₂v₂ + u₃v₃.
  3. Compute |u| = √(u₁² + u₂² + u₃²) and |v| = √(v₁² + v₂² + v₃²).
  4. Form cos θ = (u·v)/(|u||v|). For 'angle between lines', replace the numerator with |u·v| to force an acute angle.
  5. Apply θ = arccos(...) using exact surds inside the bracket; only round at the final step (1 d.p. in degrees, unless told otherwise).
  6. State the answer with units (degrees or radians as requested) and verify it lies in the expected range.

Worked Micro-Example

Lines l₁, l₂ have directions d₁ = (1,2,3), d₂ = (2,1,−1). Then d₁·d₂ = 2+2−3 = 1, |d₁| = √14, |d₂| = √6, so cos θ = |1|/(√14·√6) = 1/√84. Hence θ = arccos(1/√84) ≈ 83.7°.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Angle between two lines (no inherent orientation)
Take |d₁·d₂| in the numerator to guarantee an acute answer.
Angle between two specific displacement vectors (e.g. ∠BAC in a triangle)
Do NOT take absolute value — keep the sign so an obtuse angle is reported correctly.
Angle α between a line and a plane
Use sin α = |d·n|/(|d||n|) where n is the plane's normal — complementary trig function, same dot-product logic.
Angle between two planes
Apply the same formula to the two normal vectors n₁, n₂.
Showing two lines/vectors are perpendicular
Show u·v = 0; no arccos needed.

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing 'angle between two lines' (always acute, use modulus) with 'angle between two vectors' (can be obtuse, keep sign).
  • Using position vectors a₁, a₂ from r = a + td instead of the direction vectors d₁, d₂.
  • Treating an angle ∠BAC at a vertex as a 'line angle' and forcing it acute when in fact it may legitimately be obtuse.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the absolute value when 'acute angle between lines' is required, returning the obtuse supplement instead.
  • Premature rounding: computing |u||v| as a decimal before dividing introduces error; keep √(|u|²·|v|²) exact until arccos.
  • Arithmetic slips in the dot product (sign errors on negative components) or in simplifying surds, e.g. √14·√2 = √28 = 2√7.
  • Returning radians when the question asks for degrees (or vice versa), or not rounding to the specified accuracy.

A-level Scope Notes

Strictly within Edexcel IAL P3/FP3 vectors. The formula a·b = |a||b|cos θ is in the formula booklet; the angle-between-line-and-plane formula is not always given and should be derived if needed. Cross product / vector product is FP3 only — at P3, stick to the dot product method.
