Strategic Atlas / Differentiation / Gradient Equation
Differentiation

Equate derivative to given gradient to form solvable equation

Family: Gradient Equation Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question links a curve y = f(x) to a numerical gradient — phrases like 'the tangent at P has gradient m', 'the gradient of the curve is m', 'find x where the slope equals m', or 'find the constant k given f'(a) has gradient m'. The structural cue is a stated slope value paired with either an unknown x-coordinate or an unknown coefficient inside f(x). Equivalent phrasings include 'tangent parallel to y = mx + c' (gradient = m) and 'stationary point' (gradient = 0). The move also fires when a normal's gradient is given — after first taking the negative reciprocal to recover the tangent gradient.

Motivation — Why this works

By definition, f'(a) is the gradient of the tangent to y = f(x) at x = a. Setting f'(x) = m therefore translates a geometric statement about slope into an algebraic equation that can be solved by standard techniques (linear, quadratic, factorising). This is the fundamental bridge between calculus (the derivative as a function) and coordinate geometry (gradient of a line). Without this translation no progress is possible; with it, the problem reduces to algebra in either x or in an unknown coefficient.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the keyword 'gradient' (or 'tangent slope', 'parallel to', 'stationary') paired with a specific numerical value — this is the visual cue that f'(x) = m is the governing equation.
  2. Rule out alternatives: integrating to recover f(x), substituting into y = f(x), or using the tangent line equation y − y₁ = m(x − x₁) — none of these are needed yet because the unknown lives in the derivative relationship, not in the function value.
  3. Commit to the move because the derivative directly encodes slope: every other geometric piece of information (point on curve, equation of tangent line) is downstream of first solving f'(x) = m.
  4. Differentiate f(x) term-by-term using the power rule (if f'(x) is not already given), then write the equation f'(x) = m and rearrange to standard form.
  5. Tactical decision: identify whether the unknown is x (locating a point) or a coefficient like k (calibrating the function) — in the first case substitute and solve for x; in the second case substitute the known x-value and solve for k.
  6. Sanity-check the expected number of solutions: a quadratic f'(x) yields up to two x-values (matching 'points P and Q'); a single-coefficient unknown yields exactly one value of k.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. If not given, compute f'(x) by applying the power rule term-by-term.
  2. Write the equation f'(x) = m, where m is the stated tangent gradient (take the negative reciprocal first if a normal gradient was given).
  3. Substitute the known quantity: either the specific x-value (when solving for an unknown coefficient) or leave x as the unknown (when solving for the point).
  4. Rearrange to standard form (linear in k, or polynomial = 0 in x).
  5. Solve by inspection, factorising, or the quadratic formula; reject any roots excluded by the question.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $f(x) = 4x^2 + kx + 3$ with $f'(3) = 24$: differentiate to get $f'(x) = 8x + k$, substitute $x = 3$ to obtain $24 + k = 24$… wait, recompute: $f'(3) = 8(3) + k = 24 + k$, so $24 + k = 24$ gives $k = 0$. (For the original cubic-style $f'(x) = 4x^2 + kx + 3$ with $f'(3) = 24$: $36 + 3k + 3 = 24 \Rightarrow 3k = -15 \Rightarrow k = -5$.)

Variants — Same move, different surface

Normal line gradient is given instead of tangent gradient
First compute m_tangent = −1/m_normal, then apply f'(x) = m_tangent.
Question asks for stationary points or horizontal tangents
Same move with m = 0: solve f'(x) = 0.
Tangent parallel to a given line y = mx + c
Read off m from the line and set f'(x) = m.
Parametric curve
Compute dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) first, then equate to the given gradient.
Implicitly defined curve
Differentiate implicitly to obtain dy/dx in terms of x and y, then substitute and equate to m.

Common Misreadings

  • Substituting into f(x) (the y-value) instead of f'(x) (the gradient).
  • Setting y = m instead of dy/dx = m when the question says 'gradient is m'.
  • Confusing 'gradient of normal' with 'gradient of tangent' and equating f'(a) to the normal's slope directly.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips when evaluating powers, e.g. writing 4(3) = 12 instead of 4(3)² = 36.
  • Sign errors when rearranging f'(x) = m to f'(x) − m = 0, especially with negative coefficients.
  • Forgetting to differentiate the constant term to 0 (harmless) or, worse, leaving it in the derivative.
  • Failing to reject extraneous roots that the question explicitly excludes.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL P1/P2/P3 differentiation: power-rule differentiation of polynomials and the interpretation of the derivative as the gradient of the tangent. No formula booklet entry is invoked — this is foundational. Avoid invoking implicit or parametric differentiation unless the question explicitly presents the curve in those forms (P3/P4 territory).
