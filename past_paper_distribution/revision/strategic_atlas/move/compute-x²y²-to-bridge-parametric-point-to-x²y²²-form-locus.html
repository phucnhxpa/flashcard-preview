Coordinate Geometry

Compute x²+y² to bridge parametric point to (x²+y²)²-form locus

Family: Parametric Elimination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a parametric point (x(θ), y(θ)) must be shown to lie on a locus of the form (x²+y²)² = αx² + βy² (a pedal-curve / foot-of-perpendicular type result). Cue 1: target equation has x²+y² explicitly on the LHS. Cue 2: x and y share a common denominator D(θ) such as 4cos²θ + 9sin²θ. Cue 3: numerators of x² and y² are constant multiples of D.

Motivation — Why this works

When x = N₁(θ)/D and y = N₂(θ)/D with D = a²cos²θ + b²sin²θ, the combination x²+y² typically collapses because its numerator factors as (constant)·D, leaving x²+y² = k/D. Then the RHS combination αx²+βy² is engineered (with α=a², β=b²) so its numerator is a constant multiple of cos²θ+sin²θ = 1, giving k²/D². Equating yields (x²+y²)² = αx²+βy² with no θ left. The identity cos²θ+sin²θ=1 is the only trig fact needed.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the target LHS is (x²+y²)², so computing x²+y² from the parametric form is the obvious opening.
  2. Rules out solving for cosθ, sinθ individually and using cos²+sin²=1, since x and y share a denominator D and that route is messy.
  3. Commits to forming x²+y² and αx²+βy² directly, hoping the common D-denominator simplifies both.
  4. First operation: square the parametric expressions, add, and look for the factor D = a²cos²θ + b²sin²θ inside the numerator.
  5. Tactically, once x²+y² = k/D is obtained, square it and check that αx²+βy² also equals k²/D² by choosing α, β to make the numerator collapse via cos²θ+sin²θ=1.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write x = N₁(θ)/D, y = N₂(θ)/D and compute x²+y² = (N₁²+N₂²)/D².
  2. Factor the numerator to expose D: typically N₁²+N₂² = k·D for some constant k.
  3. Conclude x²+y² = k/D, hence (x²+y²)² = k²/D².
  4. Form αx²+βy² with α, β chosen as a², b² (the squared semi-axes); simplify using cos²θ+sin²θ = 1 to get k²/D².
  5. Equate the two expressions to obtain (x²+y²)² = αx²+βy² and read off α, β.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $x = \dfrac{12\cos\theta}{4\cos^2\theta+9\sin^2\theta}$, $y = \dfrac{18\sin\theta}{4\cos^2\theta+9\sin^2\theta}$, write $D=4\cos^2\theta+9\sin^2\theta$. Then $x^2+y^2 = \dfrac{144\cos^2\theta+324\sin^2\theta}{D^2} = \dfrac{36D}{D^2} = \dfrac{36}{D}$. Also $9x^2+4y^2 = \dfrac{1296(\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta)}{D^2} = \dfrac{1296}{D^2}$. Hence $(x^2+y^2)^2 = \dfrac{1296}{D^2} = 9x^2+4y^2$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

General ellipse x²/a² + y²/b² = 1 with foot-of-perpendicular from centre to tangent
Same procedure yields (x²+y²)² = a²x² + b²y²; the shared denominator becomes D = a²cos²θ + b²sin²θ and α=a², β=b².
Hyperbola analogue x²/a² − y²/b² = 1 with similar pedal construction
Sign change gives (x²+y²)² = a²x² − b²y² with D = a²cos²θ − b²sin²θ; identity cos²θ+sin²θ=1 still finishes the elimination.
Target stated in polar form r² = αcos²φ + βsin²φ
Convert via x = r cosφ, y = r sinφ; computing x²+y² = r² gives the bridge directly without needing to square.
Locus form Ax² + By² = C with no x²+y² factor
Skip the x²+y² step; instead form Ax²+By² directly so the numerator collapses to a constant via cos²θ+sin²θ=1.

Common Misreadings

  • Trying to solve cosθ = f(x,y) and sinθ = g(x,y) separately and substitute into cos²θ+sin²θ=1; algebraically heavier because of the shared denominator D.
  • Attempting double-angle substitutions (cos2θ, t = tan(θ/2)) which obscure the factorisation N₁²+N₂² = kD.
  • Reading the target (x²+y²)² = αx²+βy² as needing two separate eliminations rather than one bridge through x²+y² = k/D.

Pitfalls

  • Missing that the numerator of x²+y² factors as a constant times D (e.g. 144cos²θ+324sin²θ = 36(4cos²θ+9sin²θ)).
  • Forgetting to square the denominator D when computing x², y², or αx²+βy².
  • Not collecting kcos²θ+ksin²θ = k via the Pythagorean identity at the final step.
  • Mis-assigning α and β: they correspond to a² and b² (paired with x² and y² respectively), not swapped.

A-level Scope Notes

Uses only IAL Core/Further content: parametric coordinates, algebraic manipulation, and the identity cos²θ+sin²θ=1. The locus (x²+y²)² = a²x²+b²y² is a standard Further Pure coordinate-geometry result.
