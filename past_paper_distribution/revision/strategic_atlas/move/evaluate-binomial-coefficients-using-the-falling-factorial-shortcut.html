Binomial Expansion

Evaluate binomial coefficients using the falling-factorial shortcut

Family: Coefficient Computation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a binomial expansion, term extraction, or coefficient comparison requires numerical values of $\binom{n}{r}$ for small $r$ (typically $r=0,1,2,3,4$). Cues include phrases like 'find the first four terms', 'coefficient of $x^k$', or 'expand in ascending powers of $x$'. Also triggers when checking a specific term in $(a+bx)^n$ or $(1+x)^n$ expansions. Any time $n$ is a moderate positive integer and only a few coefficients are needed, this shortcut is preferred over full factorial computation.

Motivation — Why this works

The identity $\binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!} = \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)\cdots(n-r+1)}{r!}$ lets you cancel the $(n-r)!$ in the denominator with the tail of $n!$, leaving exactly $r$ factors on top. For small $r$ this is far cheaper than evaluating $n!$ directly, and it generalises seamlessly to the series form $\binom{n}{r}$ used when $n$ is non-integer or negative in the generalised binomial theorem. The shortcut also exposes patterns ($\binom{n}{0}=1$, $\binom{n}{1}=n$, $\binom{n}{2}=\tfrac{n(n-1)}{2}$) that students should know on sight.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the expansion needs only the first few coefficients, so I do not need a full table or Pascal's triangle row.
  2. I rule out computing $n!$ explicitly — for $n=10$ this is $3628800$, unnecessary work that invites arithmetic errors.
  3. I commit to the falling-factorial form $\frac{n(n-1)\cdots(n-r+1)}{r!}$ because it scales cleanly with $r$ and the numerator has exactly $r$ terms.
  4. I bridge by writing each coefficient with $r$ descending factors on top and $r!$ underneath, then cancelling before multiplying.
  5. Tactically, I cancel pairs (e.g. $\frac{9}{3}=3$, then divide by $2$) before final multiplication to keep numbers under two digits where possible.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the values of $r$ needed (often $r=0,1,2,3$ for 'first four terms').
  2. Write $\binom{n}{r} = \frac{n(n-1)\cdots(n-r+1)}{r!}$ with exactly $r$ factors in the numerator.
  3. Use the trivial cases $\binom{n}{0}=1$ and $\binom{n}{1}=n$ without computation.
  4. For $r\geq 2$, cancel common factors between numerator and $r!$ before multiplying.
  5. Record each coefficient clearly, ready to combine with the $a^{n-r}b^r$ part of each term.

Worked Micro-Example

Find $\binom{10}{r}$ for $r=0,1,2,3$. \\ $\binom{10}{0}=1$, $\binom{10}{1}=10$. \\ $\binom{10}{2}=\frac{10\times 9}{2!}=\frac{90}{2}=45$. \\ $\binom{10}{3}=\frac{10\times 9\times 8}{3!}=\frac{720}{6}=120$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Larger $n$ but still small $r$ (e.g. $\binom{20}{3}$)
Same shortcut; cancel a factor of $r!$ into the numerator early to avoid large intermediate products.
Generalised binomial $(1+x)^n$ with $n$ non-integer or negative
Use $\frac{n(n-1)\cdots(n-r+1)}{r!}$ directly — there is no factorial form, so the falling-factorial shortcut is the only route.
Many consecutive coefficients needed (e.g. full row of Pascal)
Switch to Pascal's triangle recursion $\binom{n}{r}=\binom{n-1}{r-1}+\binom{n-1}{r}$ or to the ratio $\binom{n}{r+1}=\binom{n}{r}\cdot\frac{n-r}{r+1}$.
Symmetry around $r=n/2$ (e.g. $\binom{10}{8}$)
Apply $\binom{n}{r}=\binom{n}{n-r}$ to reduce to a smaller $r$ before using the shortcut.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $\binom{n}{r}$ as $n^r/r!$ or $\frac{n^r}{r}$ rather than a falling product.
  • Confusing $\binom{n}{r}$ with the term $a^{n-r}b^r$ itself, omitting the coefficient.
  • Reading 'first four terms' as $r=1,2,3,4$ instead of $r=0,1,2,3$.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips in the falling-factorial product, especially forgetting to divide by $r!$.
  • Off-by-one errors: using $r$ factors in the numerator but dividing by $(r-1)!$ or $(r+1)!$.
  • Stopping the numerator product too early or going one factor too far (should end at $n-r+1$).
  • Multiplying out before cancelling, leading to unnecessarily large numbers and error risk.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics: positive-integer binomial expansions in P2 and the generalised binomial (with the same falling-factorial form) in P3/P4. No content beyond standard syllabus.
