Algebra & Functions

Swap x and y then solve for y to find the inverse function

Family: Inverse Function Application Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question explicitly asks for f⁻¹(x) (or 'find the inverse function'), and f(x) is given in an algebraically invertible closed form — typically a rational expression like a/(x+b), a linear-over-linear (ax+b)/(cx+d), or a mixed form such as 3x + 11 + 49/(x−4). A second cue is that f has been (or can easily be) written as y = f(x) and is stated/known to be one-to-one on its given domain. Equivalent phrasings include 'express x in terms of y', 'find a formula for f⁻¹', or 'determine the inverse mapping'.

Motivation — Why this works

By definition, y = f⁻¹(x) ⟺ x = f(y), so swapping the variables and re-solving for y produces an explicit formula for the inverse. The procedure works whenever f is one-to-one and the defining equation can be algebraically rearranged for y. For rational forms, multiplying through by the denominator clears the fraction and reduces the problem to a linear or quadratic equation in y — both of which are within standard A-level technique.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question asks for f⁻¹(x) and that f is given (or rearrangeable into) a closed-form expression in x; check/accept that f is one-to-one on the stated domain so the inverse exists.
  2. Rule out alternatives: numerical inversion, graphical reflection in y = x, or implicit differentiation are unhelpful here because an explicit algebraic formula is required.
  3. Commit to the swap-and-solve method: write y = f(x), then interchange x and y to obtain x = f(y), and solve for y.
  4. Identify the structural obstacle to isolating y — usually a denominator containing y. Plan to clear it by multiplying both sides by that denominator.
  5. Decide whether the resulting equation in y is linear (so isolate y directly) or quadratic (so collect into ay² + by + c = 0 and apply the quadratic formula, choosing the root consistent with the range of f⁻¹, i.e. the domain of f).
  6. Finally, simplify the answer into the cleanest form (e.g. split a/x − 1 rather than (a−x)/x if later parts will benefit), and state the domain of f⁻¹ as the range of f.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write y = f(x) using the given closed form.
  2. Swap variables: x = f(y).
  3. Multiply both sides by the denominator containing y to clear fractions.
  4. Expand and collect all terms on one side, grouping by powers of y.
  5. If linear in y: isolate y directly. If quadratic in y: write as Ay² + By + C = 0 and apply y = (−B ± √(B²−4AC))/(2A).
  6. Choose the correct branch (when quadratic) using the domain of f, which is the range of f⁻¹.
  7. Simplify and write f⁻¹(x) = …, then state its domain (= range of f).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $f(x)=\dfrac{12}{x+1}$, set $y=\dfrac{12}{x+1}$ and swap: $x=\dfrac{12}{y+1}$. Cross-multiply: $x(y+1)=12 \Rightarrow xy=12-x$. Hence $y=\dfrac{12-x}{x}=\dfrac{12}{x}-1$, so $f^{-1}(x)=\dfrac{12}{x}-1$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Simple linear-over-linear f(x) = (ax+b)/(cx+d)
After swapping, cross-multiply once and collect y-terms; the equation is linear in y, no quadratic formula needed.
Mixed form like f(x) = 3x + 11 + 49/(x−4)
After swapping, multiply through by (y−4); the 3y·(y−4) term creates a quadratic in y, so solve by the quadratic formula and pick the branch matching the original domain.
Exponential form f(x) = eˣ + k
After swapping, isolate the exponential and apply ln to undo it: y = ln(x − k).
Quadratic with restricted domain, e.g. f(x) = (x−h)² + k for x ≥ h
After swapping, complete-the-square form gives y − h = ±√(x − k); pick the + root to match x ≥ h.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating f⁻¹(x) as 1/f(x) rather than the inverse function.
  • Attempting to invert without checking one-to-one — leads to ambiguous ± branches with no rule for choosing.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to actually swap x and y, ending up with x = f⁻¹(y) instead of y = f⁻¹(x).
  • Sign/distribution errors when multiplying out, e.g. writing the coefficient of y as −(1 − x) instead of −(1 + x).
  • Failing to choose the correct root of the quadratic in y, giving a formula that doesn't match the original domain of f.
  • Leaving the answer in an unsimplified form like (12−x)/x when 12/x − 1 would be cleaner for follow-up parts.
  • Omitting the domain of f⁻¹ (which equals the range of f).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure (P2/P3) — inverse functions of one-to-one mappings, with the standard 'swap and solve' procedure. No formula booklet entry is required beyond the quadratic formula when the rearrangement is quadratic. Avoid invoking implicit differentiation or general inverse-function theorems; the syllabus expects only explicit algebraic inversion on a stated one-to-one domain.
