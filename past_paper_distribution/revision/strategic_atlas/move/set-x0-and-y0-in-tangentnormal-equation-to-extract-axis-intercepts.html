Coordinate Geometry

Set x=0 and y=0 in tangent/normal equation to extract axis intercepts

Family: Intercept Extraction Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question defines points A and B as the meeting points of a tangent or normal with the coordinate axes (typically as a setup for finding a midpoint locus, area of triangle OAB, or eliminating the parameter θ). You already have the line in a clean form — most often the standard tangent form (x cos θ)/a + (y sin θ)/b = 1 (intercept form), or a normal equation linear in x and y with θ-dependent coefficients. The phrases 'meets the x-axis at A', 'cuts the y-axis at B', or 'crosses the coordinate axes' are the verbal cues.

Motivation — Why this works

Any straight line is linear in x and y, so the x-intercept is found by setting y = 0 and the y-intercept by setting x = 0 — each substitution collapses the equation to a single-variable solve. For the standard tangent form (x cos θ)/a + (y sin θ)/b = 1, this is essentially reading off intercept form X/p + Y/q = 1, giving x = a sec θ and y = b csc θ in one line. Keeping the intercepts as clean single-trig expressions (sec θ, csc θ, or cos θ, sin θ from a normal) is essential because the next part of the question almost always requires inverting these to eliminate θ via sin²θ + cos²θ = 1.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that A and B are defined as axis crossings, and the line equation is already linear and tidy — this is a pure intercept-finding task.
  2. Rule out solving simultaneously with y = 0 / x = 0 as separate equations — that's just substitution; rule out projecting onto axes or using distance formulas, which would be needed only for foot-of-perpendicular questions.
  3. Commit to the substitution approach because the tangent/normal is already in a form where one variable's coefficient is θ-dependent but separable — setting the other variable to zero isolates it instantly.
  4. Substitute y = 0 first: the y-term vanishes, leaving a one-step solve for x. Then x = 0: the x-term vanishes, one-step solve for y.
  5. Tactical choice: convert 1/cos θ and 1/sin θ into sec θ and csc θ (or leave as cos θ and sin θ for normals) depending on which form the next part of the question wants — typically sec/csc when an x²/a² + y²/b² = 1 elimination is coming.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the tangent/normal equation in its given linear form, e.g. (x cos θ)/a + (y sin θ)/b = 1.
  2. Set y = 0: equation reduces to (x cos θ)/a = 1, so x = a/cos θ = a sec θ. Record A = (a sec θ, 0).
  3. Set x = 0: equation reduces to (y sin θ)/b = 1, so y = b/sin θ = b csc θ. Record B = (0, b csc θ).
  4. (For a normal like 4x sin θ − 3y cos θ = 14 sin θ cos θ: y = 0 ⇒ x = 7 cos θ / 2; x = 0 ⇒ y = −14 sin θ / 3, after legitimately cancelling the non-zero trig factor.)
  5. State any restriction on θ (e.g. θ ≠ 0, π/2) that was needed to divide by sin θ or cos θ.

Worked Micro-Example

Tangent to $\frac{x^2}{9}+\frac{y^2}{4}=1$ at $(3\cos\theta,2\sin\theta)$ is $\frac{x\cos\theta}{3}+\frac{y\sin\theta}{2}=1$. Setting $y=0$: $x=3\sec\theta$, so $A=(3\sec\theta,0)$. Setting $x=0$: $y=2\csc\theta$, so $B=(0,2\csc\theta)$. Midpoint $M=\left(\tfrac{3}{2}\sec\theta,\csc\theta\right)$, ready for $\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta=1$ elimination.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Normal equation rather than tangent (e.g. ax sec θ − by csc θ = a² − b², or 4x sin θ − 3y cos θ = 14 sin θ cos θ)
Same substitutions y = 0 and x = 0; the resulting intercepts come out in cos θ / sin θ rather than sec θ / csc θ, but the principle is identical. Be careful with signs and with cancelling trig factors.
Foot of perpendicular from origin to the line is asked instead of axis intercepts
Intercept-extraction does not apply directly; project onto the normal direction or solve the line ⊥ from origin simultaneously.
Line given in general form ax + by + c = 0 rather than intercept form
Same substitution principle: y = 0 ⇒ x = −c/a; x = 0 ⇒ y = −c/b. No conceptual change.

Common Misreadings

  • Computing the foot of perpendicular from origin to the tangent instead of axis intercepts when the question says 'the tangent meets the axes'.
  • Finding intercepts correctly but then averaging the wrong pair of coordinates when computing the midpoint of AB.
  • Confusing the tangent and normal — using the tangent equation when the question specified the normal.

Pitfalls

  • Leaving the answer as a/cos θ when the mark scheme (or the next part) wants a sec θ — or vice versa. Match the form needed downstream.
  • Cancelling sin θ or cos θ from a normal equation without flagging that this assumes sin θ ≠ 0 (resp. cos θ ≠ 0); state the restriction.
  • Sign errors, especially when the normal has a term like −3y cos θ on the LHS — forgetting the minus when isolating y.
  • Forgetting that sec θ and csc θ are undefined at θ = 0, π/2, π, 3π/2 — note the excluded parameter values.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4/FP coverage of conics (ellipse, hyperbola, parabola in parametric form). Tangent and normal equations to standard conics appear in the formula booklet; intercept extraction itself is elementary coordinate geometry. Do not stray into projective or polar-line generalisations — those are out of scope.
