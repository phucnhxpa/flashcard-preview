Complex Numbers

Square both sides to convert modulus locus into Cartesian circle equation

Family: Modulus Elimination Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a locus equation has the form $|z - a| = k|z - b|$ with $k > 0$, $k \neq 1$ (Apollonius circle), or $|z - a| = |z - b|$ (perpendicular bisector). Cue phrases include 'find the Cartesian equation of the locus', 'show the locus is a circle', or 'find the centre and radius'. Both sides must be non-negative moduli so squaring is reversible. Especially appropriate when geometric arguments would be cumbersome compared with algebra.

Motivation — Why this works

Each modulus $|z - a|$ with $z = x + iy$ equals $\sqrt{(x-\operatorname{Re}a)^2 + (y-\operatorname{Im}a)^2}$, a square root that obstructs algebraic manipulation. Squaring both sides simultaneously eliminates every root in one stroke, leaving a polynomial equation in $x$ and $y$. Because moduli are non-negative, squaring is an equivalence (no spurious solutions), and the resulting equation is at most quadratic — guaranteed to rearrange into a circle (or line, if $k=1$).

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice both sides are moduli of linear expressions in $z$, with a constant ratio $k \neq 1$ — this is an Apollonius circle.
  2. I rule out a purely geometric attack: extracting centre and radius from the two foci and ratio is awkward without algebra.
  3. I commit to squaring because moduli are non-negative, so the operation is reversible and kills both square roots at once.
  4. First concrete step: substitute $z = x + iy$ and rewrite each modulus as $\sqrt{(\,)^2 + (\,)^2}$, ready to square.
  5. Tactically, I will square the constant $k$ to get $k^2$ on one side, expand carefully, collect like terms, and finally complete the square to read off centre and radius.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Substitute $z = x + iy$ so $|z - a| = \sqrt{(x - \operatorname{Re}a)^2 + (y - \operatorname{Im}a)^2}$ on each side.
  2. Square both sides: $(x - \operatorname{Re}a)^2 + (y - \operatorname{Im}a)^2 = k^2\bigl[(x - \operatorname{Re}b)^2 + (y - \operatorname{Im}b)^2\bigr]$.
  3. Expand all brackets fully, remembering to distribute $k^2$ across every term on the right.
  4. Bring all terms to one side; the $x^2$ and $y^2$ coefficients should match (both $1 - k^2$), confirming a circle.
  5. Divide through by the common coefficient to normalise to $x^2 + y^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$.
  6. Complete the square in $x$ and $y$ to read off centre $(-D/2, -E/2)$ and radius $\sqrt{(D/2)^2 + (E/2)^2 - F}$.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $|z + 1 - 13i| = 3|z - 7 - 5i|$. Setting $z = x + iy$: $(x+1)^2 + (y-13)^2 = 9\bigl[(x-7)^2 + (y-5)^2\bigr]$. Expanding: $x^2 + 2x + 1 + y^2 - 26y + 169 = 9x^2 - 126x + 441 + 9y^2 - 90y + 225$. Collecting: $8x^2 - 128x + 8y^2 + 64y + 496 = 0$, i.e. $x^2 - 16x + y^2 + 8y + 62 = 0$. Completing the square: $(x-8)^2 + (y+4)^2 = 18$, a circle centre $(8, -4)$, radius $3\sqrt{2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

$|z - a| = |z - b|$ (ratio $k = 1$)
After squaring, the $x^2$ and $y^2$ terms cancel, giving a linear equation — the perpendicular bisector of $a$ and $b$.
$|z - a|^2 - |z - b|^2 = c$
No squaring needed; the squares are already present and cancel to a linear equation directly.
$|z| = k|z - b|$ (one focus at origin)
Same squaring procedure with $a = 0$, simplifying the left side to $x^2 + y^2$.
$|z - a| = r$ (single modulus equals constant)
Square once to get $(x - \operatorname{Re}a)^2 + (y - \operatorname{Im}a)^2 = r^2$ — the circle equation immediately.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $|z - a| = k|z - b|$ as if $k=1$ and jumping to a perpendicular bisector.
  • Squaring each modulus separately while ignoring the equation as a whole, losing the link between the two sides.
  • Reading the locus as an ellipse or hyperbola because two foci appear, when in fact it is an Apollonius circle.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to square the constant: writing $3\bigl[(x-7)^2 + (y-5)^2\bigr]$ instead of $9\bigl[\cdots\bigr]$.
  • Sign errors when expanding $(y - 13)^2$ or moving terms across the equals sign.
  • Failing to distribute $k^2$ across every term inside the right-hand bracket.
  • Stopping at the expanded quadratic without completing the square to give centre and radius.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2) loci of complex numbers; both Apollonius circles and perpendicular bisectors are explicitly examinable, and Cartesian conversion via $z = x + iy$ is the standard expected method.
