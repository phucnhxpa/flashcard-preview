Coordinate Geometry

Use diameter endpoints to find centre (midpoint) and radius (distance)

Family: Circle From Diameter Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when the question explicitly states that a given line segment PQ (or AB) is a diameter of a circle and gives the coordinates of its two endpoints. Typical phrasings: 'PQ is a diameter', 'the points A and B are at opposite ends of a diameter', or 'the line segment joining P and Q is a diameter of circle C'. The task usually asks for the centre, the radius, and/or the equation (x−a)² + (y−b)² = r². Recognise this whenever two named points are tagged as endpoints of a diameter — that single word 'diameter' is the structural cue.

Motivation — Why this works

The centre of a circle is equidistant from every point on the circumference, so it must lie at the midpoint of any diameter — this collapses the problem of locating the centre into a one-line midpoint computation. Once the centre is known, the radius is the distance from the centre to either endpoint, exploiting the fact that both endpoints lie on the circle. This bypasses the heavier machinery of perpendicular bisectors of chords or solving the general circle equation, and directly produces r (not r²), which is what subsequent parts typically need.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the keyword 'diameter' attached to two named points — this is the strongest structural piece of information in the question.
  2. Rule out alternative methods: perpendicular bisectors of chords (only needed when three circumference points are given), solving the general equation x² + y² + Dx + Ey + F = 0 (overkill here), or completing the square from a given equation (no equation given).
  3. Commit to the midpoint-then-distance route: the centre is forced to be the midpoint of the diameter, and the radius is forced to be the distance from that centre to either endpoint.
  4. Bridge to operation: write down the midpoint formula ((x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2) for the centre, and the distance formula √((Δx)² + (Δy)²) for the radius.
  5. Tactical choice for the radius: pick whichever endpoint gives the friendlier arithmetic (often the one producing recognisable Pythagorean triples like 3-4-5 or 5-12-13). Either endpoint must give the same r, so this can also serve as a self-check.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Label the diameter endpoints P = (x₁, y₁) and Q = (x₂, y₂).
  2. Compute the centre: C = ((x₁ + x₂)/2, (y₁ + y₂)/2). Take care with signs when averaging negative coordinates.
  3. Compute Δx = x₂ − a and Δy = y₂ − b, where C = (a, b).
  4. Compute the radius: r = √((Δx)² + (Δy)²). Simplify to a numerical value, not r².
  5. (Optional check) Repeat the distance calculation from C to the other endpoint; both must give the same r.
  6. (Optional next step) Write the circle equation (x − a)² + (y − b)² = r².

Worked Micro-Example

Given P(-11, 6) and Q(-3, 12) as diameter endpoints. Centre: $C = \left(\tfrac{-11+(-3)}{2}, \tfrac{6+12}{2}\right) = (-7, 9)$. Radius from C to Q: $\Delta x = -3-(-7)=4$, $\Delta y = 12-9=3$, so $r = \sqrt{4^2+3^2} = \sqrt{25} = 5$. Equation: $(x+7)^2 + (y-9)^2 = 25$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Three points on the circumference are given instead of a diameter
Cannot use the midpoint shortcut. Find two perpendicular bisectors of chords and solve simultaneously for the centre, then use the distance formula for r.
Only the diameter length is wanted (not the centre)
Skip the midpoint step and apply the distance formula directly to P and Q; then halve if r is needed (r = |PQ|/2).
The circle equation is given in expanded form x² + y² + Dx + Ey + F = 0
Complete the square in x and y to read off centre (−D/2, −E/2) and r² = (D/2)² + (E/2)² − F.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating PQ as a chord rather than a diameter — the midpoint of a chord is NOT generally the centre.
  • Assuming the centre is the origin or a marked point in a diagram without checking the wording.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when averaging negative coordinates, e.g. computing (−11) + (−3) as −8 instead of −14.
  • Leaving the answer as r² = 25 when the question asks for the radius r = 5.
  • Forgetting to take the square root after applying the distance formula, especially when r² is what is needed in the next part — read the question carefully.
  • Mis-pairing coordinates in the distance formula (mixing Δx with a y-difference).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P2 circle geometry). The midpoint formula and distance formula are standard and not in the formula booklet — they must be memorised. Stay with circles of the form (x−a)² + (y−b)² = r²; do not invoke parametric or polar forms.
