Integration

Apply LIATE priority to choose u in integration by parts

Family: Integration By Parts Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The integrand is a product of two functions of fundamentally different types — typically Logarithmic, Inverse-trig, Algebraic (polynomial/power), Trigonometric, or Exponential — with no obvious antiderivative and no clean substitution available. Visual cues include things like x²eˣ, x sin x, x ln x, (ln x)/x², x·arctan x. Equivalently: 'product of a polynomial and a transcendental function', or 'product of ln x with a power of x'. When a single substitution u = … leaves the other factor entangled, this signals integration by parts is the right tool, and LIATE tells you which factor to label u.

Motivation — Why this works

Integration by parts (∫u dv = uv − ∫v du) is the reverse of the product rule and converts one integral into a boundary term plus a new integral. The method only helps if the new integral ∫v du is genuinely simpler than the original, which depends entirely on which factor is named u. LIATE (Logarithmic, Inverse-trig, Algebraic, Trigonometric, Exponential) ranks function types by how much they simplify when differentiated: logs and inverse-trig functions become rational/algebraic, polynomials drop in degree, while trig and exp are essentially invariant. Choosing u from the leftmost LIATE category that appears in the product guarantees du is simpler, while dv (from the rightmost category) remains tractable to integrate.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand is a product of two qualitatively different function types (e.g. polynomial × exponential, logarithm × power), so no direct antiderivative formula applies.
  2. Rule out u-substitution: setting u equal to either factor leaves the other one un-cancelled in terms of x, so substitution alone cannot finish the job.
  3. Commit to integration by parts because the product structure of two different types is its hallmark trigger.
  4. Apply LIATE to decide the labelling: scan the integrand for the function type appearing earliest in L-I-A-T-E and assign that factor to u; the remaining factor (with dx) becomes dv.
  5. Sanity-check by considering the alternative assignment: if swapping would force you to integrate ln x or raise the polynomial degree, the LIATE choice is confirmed as the one that strictly simplifies.
  6. Compute du by differentiation and v by integration of dv, then substitute into uv − ∫v du; check that the new integral is indeed simpler (lower polynomial degree, or a basic power/exponential).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the two factors of the integrand and classify each as L, I, A, T or E.
  2. Set u = (factor of leftmost LIATE class) and dv = (remaining factor) dx.
  3. Differentiate to obtain du = (du/dx) dx, taking care with chain rule and sign.
  4. Integrate dv to obtain v, including any constant factor (e.g. v = (1/k)e^{kx} for dv = e^{kx}dx).
  5. Substitute into the formula: ∫u dv = uv − ∫v du.
  6. Simplify the new integral and either evaluate directly or repeat integration by parts if a polynomial factor of degree ≥ 2 remains.

Worked Micro-Example

Evaluate $\int x e^{2x}\,dx$. LIATE: Algebraic before Exponential, so $u = x$, $dv = e^{2x}dx$. Then $du = dx$ and $v = \tfrac{1}{2}e^{2x}$. Therefore $\int x e^{2x}dx = \tfrac{1}{2}xe^{2x} - \int \tfrac{1}{2}e^{2x}dx = \tfrac{1}{2}xe^{2x} - \tfrac{1}{4}e^{2x} + C$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Polynomial × exponential, e.g. ∫x²e^{2x}dx
u = x² (Algebraic), dv = e^{2x}dx; apply parts twice to reduce the polynomial to degree 0.
Polynomial × trigonometric, e.g. ∫x sin x dx
u = x (Algebraic before Trig), dv = sin x dx so v = −cos x.
Logarithm × power, e.g. ∫x^n ln x dx (n ≠ −1)
u = ln x (Log before Algebraic), dv = x^n dx; the new integral becomes a clean power-rule integral.
Logarithm divided by a power, e.g. ∫(ln x)/x² dx
Rewrite as (ln x)(x^{−2}) dx; u = ln x, dv = x^{−2}dx so v = −x^{−1}.
∫ ln x dx alone (no obvious second factor)
Treat the integrand as (ln x)(1) dx; u = ln x, dv = 1 dx so v = x — this is the standard 'phantom 1' trick.

Common Misreadings

  • Choosing u = e^{kx} because exponentials 'feel easy' to differentiate — this raises the polynomial degree on integration of dv and makes the new integral worse.
  • Letting u = the algebraic factor when a logarithm is present, forcing you to integrate ln x as a sub-problem.
  • Failing to recognise an integrand like (ln x)/x² as a product, and instead chasing a substitution t = ln x that converts the problem into ∫t e^{−t}dt — valid but unnecessarily heavy.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the chain-rule factor when integrating dv: ∫e^{2x}dx = (1/2)e^{2x}, not e^{2x}.
  • Sign errors integrating x^{−2} to v = −x^{−1}, or mishandling the double negative in − ∫v du when v itself is negative.
  • Forgetting to apply integration by parts a second time when the polynomial factor has degree ≥ 2 (e.g. x²e^{2x} requires two passes).
  • Dropping the constant of integration +C in indefinite integrals.

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL P3 scope: integration by parts formula ∫u (dv/dx) dx = uv − ∫v (du/dx) dx is given in the formula booklet. LIATE is a memory aid, not an examinable rule — students should justify their choice by showing the resulting integral is simpler. Tabular integration and reduction formulae are out of scope for IAL Maths (the latter appears only in Further Pure).
