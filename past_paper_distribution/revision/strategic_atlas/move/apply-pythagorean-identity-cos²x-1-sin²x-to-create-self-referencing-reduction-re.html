Trigonometry

Apply Pythagorean identity cos²x = 1 − sin²x to create self-referencing reduction recurrence

Family: Identity Selection Frequency: 8 questions

Trigger — When to use

After applying integration by parts to I_n = ∫sin^n x dx, the remaining integral contains a mixed-power product sin^{n-2}x · cos²x that cannot be directly identified as any I_k in the family. The cue is the appearance of an isolated cos²x (or a single cos²x factor) alongside a power of sin, in a context where you are deriving a reduction formula. Equivalently: 'IBP has left me with both sin and cos powers, and I need to express everything back in terms of ∫sin^k x dx only.' The same cue applies, mirrored, when reducing ∫cos^n x dx and seeing sin²x · cos^{n-2}x.

Motivation — Why this works

Substituting cos²x = 1 − sin²x converts ∫sin^{n-2}x cos²x dx into ∫sin^{n-2}x dx − ∫sin^n x dx = I_{n-2} − I_n. This deliberately re-introduces I_n on the right-hand side, creating a linear equation in I_n that can be rearranged algebraically to give I_n in terms of I_{n-2}. The Pythagorean identity is the unique choice that achieves this clean two-term split — double-angle forms like cos²x = ½(1 + cos 2x) introduce cos 2x and break the I_k structure entirely. The underlying principle is choosing an identity that maps every term back into the target family.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the post-IBP integrand sin^{n-2}x · cos²x: it has both sin and cos, so it is NOT directly any I_k.
  2. Recognise the goal: I want everything expressed purely as integrals of sin to some power, so I can read off I_n and I_{n-2}.
  3. Rule out the double-angle identity cos²x = ½(1 + cos 2x): this would introduce a cos 2x term at a different frequency that does not belong to the I_k family.
  4. Commit to the Pythagorean identity cos²x = 1 − sin²x because sin²x · sin^{n-2}x = sin^n x, which is exactly I_n — re-introducing I_n is the FEATURE, not a bug.
  5. Bridge: substitute, distribute the (n−1) coefficient inherited from IBP, and identify the two pieces as (n−1)I_{n-2} − (n−1)I_n.
  6. Tactical step: collect I_n terms on one side: I_n + (n−1)I_n = (n−1)I_{n-2}, hence n·I_n = (n−1)I_{n-2}, giving the reduction I_n = ((n−1)/n) I_{n-2}.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Inside the post-IBP integral, replace cos²x with 1 − sin²x: (n−1)∫sin^{n-2}x · cos²x dx = (n−1)∫sin^{n-2}x (1 − sin²x) dx.
  2. Distribute: (n−1)∫sin^{n-2}x dx − (n−1)∫sin^n x dx.
  3. Identify each piece with the I_k notation: (n−1)I_{n-2} − (n−1)I_n.
  4. Write the full equation from IBP: I_n = (n−1)I_{n-2} − (n−1)I_n.
  5. Collect I_n: n·I_n = (n−1)I_{n-2}, so I_n = ((n−1)/n) I_{n-2} (for definite integral over [0, π/2]).

Worked Micro-Example

Starting from IBP on $I_n = \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^n x\,dx$ with $u=\sin^{n-1}x$, $dv=\sin x\,dx$, the boundary term vanishes and we get $I_n = (n-1)\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^{n-2}x\cos^2 x\,dx$. Apply $\cos^2 x = 1 - \sin^2 x$: $I_n = (n-1)\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^{n-2}x\,dx - (n-1)\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^n x\,dx = (n-1)I_{n-2} - (n-1)I_n$. Rearranging: $nI_n = (n-1)I_{n-2}$, so $I_n = \tfrac{n-1}{n}I_{n-2}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Reduction formula for ∫cos^n x dx
After IBP you obtain cos^{n-2}x · sin²x; replace sin²x = 1 − cos²x — identical logic, mirrored trig roles, yielding I_n = ((n−1)/n) I_{n-2}.
Reduction formula for ∫sec^n x dx or ∫tan^n x dx
Use tan²x = sec²x − 1 (or sec²x = 1 + tan²x) to play the same self-referencing role, mapping the leftover term back into the sec^k or tan^k family.
Indefinite version (no limits)
Same identity substitution applies; you simply track the boundary term from IBP rather than dropping it, but the algebraic collection of I_n is unchanged.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating ∫sin^{n-2}x cos²x dx as 'almost I_{n-2}' and ignoring the cos²x — leads to a wrong recurrence missing the I_n term.
  • Thinking the appearance of I_n on both sides means the method has failed and abandoning it, rather than collecting algebraically.
  • Reaching for a double-angle identity because cos²x 'looks like' a candidate for power-reduction in standard integrals — wrong context here.

Pitfalls

  • Using the double-angle form cos²x = ½(1 + cos 2x) instead — this introduces cos 2x · sin^{n-2}x with no clean reduction back into the I_k family.
  • Failing to distribute the (n−1) factor across BOTH resulting integrals, leading to an incorrect coefficient on I_n or I_{n-2}.
  • Sign error when expanding sin^{n-2}x(1 − sin²x): writing sin^{n-2}x + sin^n x instead of sin^{n-2}x − sin^n x.
  • Forgetting to verify that the IBP boundary term [sin^{n-1}x · (−cos x)]_0^{π/2} vanishes before equating to (n−1)I_{n-2} − (n−1)I_n.

A-level Scope Notes

Squarely within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3) reduction-formula techniques. Uses only the Pythagorean identity sin²x + cos²x = 1 (formula booklet) and integration by parts. Avoid invoking complex-exponential or beta-function generalisations — these are out of scope and will confuse rather than clarify.
