Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation has the form $\tan(\text{expression in }x) = f(x)$ and the target/required form expresses $x$ (or the inner expression) as $\arctan(\cdot)$. Typical cues: a 'show that' or 'hence show' instruction ending in $x = k\arctan(\ldots)$, or a fixed-point iteration setup $x_{n+1} = k\arctan(\ldots)$. Also fires when the inner angle is confined to an interval lying inside $(-\pi/2, \pi/2)$, making arctan inversion unambiguous.

Motivation — Why this works

Since $\arctan$ is the inverse of $\tan$ on its principal branch $(-\pi/2, \pi/2)$, applying $\arctan$ to both sides of $\tan\theta = y$ recovers $\theta = \arctan y$ exactly when $\theta$ lies in that principal interval. Verifying the angle's range first guarantees the inversion is bijective and introduces no spurious or missed solutions. The remaining algebra (multiplying by a constant, rearranging) then converts to the requested form.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation has $\tan$ wrapped around the variable I need to isolate, while the target form has $\arctan$ on the outside.
  2. Rule out further trig manipulation (identities, double-angle expansion) — they would move away from the target form, not toward it.
  3. Commit to inverting because $\arctan\circ\tan$ collapses cleanly, provided the inner angle sits in $(-\pi/2, \pi/2)$.
  4. Check the domain: from the problem context $x \in (0,\pi]$ gives $x/2 \in (0,\pi/2]$, safely inside the principal range.
  5. Apply $\arctan$ to both sides, then clear the coefficient on the angle (multiply by 2) to land on the target equation.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Confirm the inner angle (here $x/2$) lies in $(-\pi/2, \pi/2)$ using the stated domain of $x$.
  2. From $\tan(x/2) = 4/x$, apply $\arctan$ to both sides: $x/2 = \arctan(4/x)$.
  3. Multiply both sides by 2: $x = 2\arctan(4/x)$, the required form.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\tan(x/2) = 4/x$ for $x \in (0,\pi]$, note $x/2 \in (0,\pi/2] \subset (-\pi/2,\pi/2)$, so $\arctan$ inverts $\tan$ unambiguously. Apply $\arctan$: $x/2 = \arctan(4/x)$. Multiply by 2: $x = 2\arctan(4/x)$, as required.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation $\sin\theta = c$ with $\theta \in [-\pi/2,\pi/2]$, target $\theta = \arcsin c$.
Apply $\arcsin$ instead; verify $\theta$ lies in $[-\pi/2,\pi/2]$ and $|c|\le 1$.
Equation $\cos\theta = c$ with $\theta \in [0,\pi]$, target $\theta = \arccos c$.
Apply $\arccos$; verify $\theta \in [0,\pi]$ (the principal range of $\arccos$).
Iteration setup: rearrange $\tan(x) = g(x)$ into $x_{n+1} = \arctan(g(x_n)) + k\pi$.
Add the appropriate multiple of $\pi$ if the required root lies outside $(-\pi/2,\pi/2)$.
Coefficient on the angle, e.g. $\tan(3x) = y$ with $3x \in (-\pi/2,\pi/2)$.
Invert to $3x = \arctan y$, then divide by the coefficient: $x = \tfrac{1}{3}\arctan y$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $\arctan$ as $1/\tan$ (cotangent) rather than the inverse function.
  • Reading the target $x = 2\arctan(4/x)$ as $\arctan(x/2) = 4/x$ — applying arctan to the wrong side.
  • Assuming arctan inversion is universally valid and skipping the principal-range check.

Pitfalls

  • Failing to verify that the inner angle lies in $(-\pi/2, \pi/2)$, which can miss solutions in other branches.
  • Forgetting to multiply through by the coefficient on the angle after inverting (e.g. leaving $x/2 = \arctan(4/x)$ as the final answer).
  • Confusing $\arctan$ with $\tan^{-1}$ written as a reciprocal; in IAL notation $\tan^{-1}$ means the inverse function.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL technique: inverse trig functions and their principal ranges are core P3/P4 content, and 'show that' style equations of this kind appear in fixed-point iteration questions.
