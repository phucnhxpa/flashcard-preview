Complex Numbers

Rationalise a complex constant ratio by conjugate multiplication

Family: Rationalisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an expression contains a ratio of two complex constants (or a complex denominator) that must be reduced to standard $a+bi$ form. Typical cues: a denominator of the form $p+qi$ with $q\ne 0$; a working line like $w = \frac{1+i}{2-i}\cdot f(y)$ where $f(y)$ is real; or a request to find the locus/argument/modulus of a quotient. Also fires when simplifying $z_1/z_2$ before plotting on an Argand diagram or before extracting real/imaginary parts.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying numerator and denominator by the conjugate of the denominator turns the denominator into $|p+qi|^2 = p^2+q^2$, a positive real number. This relies on the identity $(p+qi)(p-qi)=p^2+q^2$, the same principle as rationalising a surd denominator. Once the denominator is real, real and imaginary parts separate cleanly, and any subsequent geometric structure (line through origin, fixed argument, etc.) becomes visible by inspection.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the only obstruction to reading off $a+bi$ form is a complex number sitting in a denominator.
  2. Rule out splitting prematurely into $u+iv$ via $z=x+iy$ — that explodes the algebra when the complex part is just a constant ratio.
  3. Commit to isolating the constant ratio and rationalising it once, so the rest of the expression (which is real) can ride along as a scalar.
  4. Identify the conjugate of the denominator and multiply top and bottom by it.
  5. Expand carefully, applying $i^2=-1$, and divide each term of the numerator by the now-real denominator to land in $a+bi$ form.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the complex denominator $p+qi$ and its conjugate $p-qi$.
  2. Multiply numerator and denominator by $p-qi$.
  3. Expand the numerator using FOIL, replacing $i^2$ by $-1$.
  4. Compute the denominator as $p^2+q^2$ (a real number).
  5. Split into real and imaginary parts: $\frac{a+bi}{p^2+q^2} = \frac{a}{p^2+q^2}+\frac{b}{p^2+q^2}i$.
  6. Re-attach any real factor that was pulled aside.

Worked Micro-Example

Simplify $\dfrac{1+i}{2-i}$. Multiply by the conjugate: $\dfrac{1+i}{2-i}\cdot\dfrac{2+i}{2+i} = \dfrac{(1+i)(2+i)}{(2-i)(2+i)}$. Numerator: $2+i+2i+i^2 = 1+3i$. Denominator: $4-i^2 = 5$. Hence $\dfrac{1+i}{2-i} = \dfrac{1+3i}{5} = \tfrac{1}{5}+\tfrac{3}{5}i$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Single complex denominator with variable numerator, e.g. $\frac{z}{2-i}$
Multiply by $\frac{2+i}{2+i}$; the numerator becomes $z(2+i)$, then expand using $z=x+iy$ if real/imaginary parts are needed.
Surd denominator, e.g. $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}-1}$
Same idea: multiply by the conjugate surd $\sqrt{2}+1$ to obtain a rational denominator.
Modulus or argument of a quotient $\left|\frac{z_1}{z_2}\right|$
Often faster to use $|z_1|/|z_2|$ and $\arg z_1-\arg z_2$, but rationalising still works and gives Cartesian form directly.
Locus problems where $w=\frac{az+b}{cz+d}$
After substituting $z=x+iy$, rationalise by the conjugate of the denominator $cz+d$ to extract $\operatorname{Re}(w)$ and $\operatorname{Im}(w)$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the conjugate of $p+qi$ as $-p+qi$ or $-p-qi$ instead of $p-qi$.
  • Believing the technique is only for $\frac{1}{z}$ rather than for any complex denominator.
  • Thinking one must split into $x+iy$ first before rationalising — missing that a constant ratio can be cleared once and for all.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic slips in $(1+i)(2+i)$: forgetting $i^2=-1$ and writing $3+3i$ instead of $1+3i$.
  • Computing $(2-i)(2+i)$ as $4-1=3$ instead of $4+1=5$ (sign error from $-i^2$).
  • Forgetting to divide both real and imaginary parts of the numerator by the denominator.
  • Multiplying only the denominator by the conjugate, breaking the value of the expression.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Core/Further Pure technique for complex numbers (FP1/FP2): conjugate multiplication to express a quotient in $a+bi$ form, used in Argand diagrams, loci, and transformations $w=f(z)$.
