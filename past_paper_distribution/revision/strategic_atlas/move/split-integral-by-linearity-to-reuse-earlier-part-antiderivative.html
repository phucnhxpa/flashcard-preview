Trigger — When to use

Fires in multi-part exam questions where a later part asks for a definite integral whose integrand is a sum or difference, and one of the summands is exactly the function whose antiderivative (or 'show that' result) was established in an earlier part. Cues include phrases like 'Hence find...', 'Use part (b) to evaluate...', or simply matching limits/integrand fragments between parts. Also fires when one term is a trivial polynomial and the other is the hard IBP/substitution piece already done.

Motivation — Why this works

Linearity of integration lets ∫(f − g) dx be split into ∫f dx − ∫g dx, so any earlier scaffolded antiderivative can be quoted verbatim. Exam papers are deliberately structured: each part is a stepping-stone to the next, and the marker expects reuse rather than re-derivation. Spotting the reuse saves several minutes and reduces the chance of algebraic error from re-doing IBP under pressure.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand is a sum/difference and one piece matches an earlier part's result.
  2. Rule out re-deriving the hard piece from scratch — that would duplicate work and ignore the 'Hence' signal.
  3. Commit to splitting via linearity because the earlier antiderivative is exam-sanctioned and quotable.
  4. Bridge to operation: write the integral as two separate definite integrals over the same limits.
  5. Tactically evaluate the easy polynomial term directly and substitute the quoted antiderivative for the hard term, watching the sign carefully.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write the integral as a difference (or sum) of two definite integrals over the same limits using linearity.
  2. Evaluate the elementary term directly (e.g. ∫4x dx = 2x²) and apply the limits.
  3. Quote the antiderivative from the earlier part for the hard term, including its constant pieces.
  4. Apply the same limits to the quoted antiderivative — do not re-derive.
  5. Combine the two evaluated pieces with the correct sign and simplify.

Worked Micro-Example

Given part (b): ∫xe^{x/2} dx = 2xe^{x/2} − 4e^{x/2}. Find ∫₀^{4ln2} (4x − xe^{x/2}) dx. Split: ∫₀^{4ln2} 4x dx − ∫₀^{4ln2} xe^{x/2} dx = [2x²]₀^{4ln2} − [2xe^{x/2} − 4e^{x/2}]₀^{4ln2} = 32(ln2)² − (32ln2 − 12) = 32(ln2)² − 32ln2 + 12.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Parametric area: part (a) shows dx/dt, part (b) asks ∫y(dx/dt) dt
Substitute the shown dx/dt into the area integral rather than re-differentiating x(t).
Volume of revolution where part (a) gives ∫y² dx and part (b) asks for the volume between two curves
Split π∫(y₁² − y₂²) dx and quote the part (a) integral for one of the curves.
Part (a) establishes a reduction or 'show that' identity; part (b) asks for a definite integral whose integrand contains that identity plus an elementary term
Rewrite using the identity, split off the elementary term, integrate each piece separately.
Differential equations: part (a) finds an integrating factor or general solution; part (b) asks to evaluate a related definite integral
Reuse the antiderivative implicit in the general solution rather than redoing the integration.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the later part as independent and starting IBP from scratch instead of recognising the 'Hence' link.
  • Misreading the sign of the matched term and adding instead of subtracting the quoted antiderivative.
  • Assuming the limits in the later part match those used in the earlier part when they don't — always re-apply the stated limits.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the minus sign so the quoted IBP result is added rather than subtracted.
  • Re-deriving the IBP unnecessarily and burning exam time.
  • Dropping a factor (e.g. writing 2x² as x² for ∫4x dx) when rushing the easy piece.
  • Substituting the upper limit only and forgetting to subtract the lower-limit value of the quoted antiderivative.
