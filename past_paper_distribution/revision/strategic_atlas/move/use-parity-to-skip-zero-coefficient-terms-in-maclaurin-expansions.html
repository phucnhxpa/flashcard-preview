Sequences & Series

Use parity to skip zero-coefficient terms in Maclaurin expansions

Family: Symmetry Shortcut Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when a Maclaurin series is requested for a function that is visibly even (e.g. $\cos x$, $\sec x$, $\cosh x$, $\cos(x^2)$) or visibly odd (e.g. $\sin x$, $\tan x$, $\sinh x$, $x\cos x$). Cues include questions asking for 'the first few non-zero terms' or 'terms up to $x^4$'. Also fires when the function is built from a known parity (sums/products of even/odd functions whose parity is preserved).

Motivation — Why this works

If $f(-x)=f(x)$ then its Maclaurin series $\sum f^{(n)}(0)x^n/n!$ must equal its reflection, forcing all odd-order coefficients to vanish; symmetrically, $f(-x)=-f(x)$ forces even-order coefficients to vanish. This halves the differentiation workload and provides a self-check: any non-zero value at a 'forbidden' order signals an algebraic slip. The principle is just uniqueness of power series combined with the parity of $x^n$.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the function looks symmetric in $x$: testing $f(-x)$ returns either $f(x)$ or $-f(x)$.
  2. I rule out applying this to expansions about a non-zero point, and to functions like $e^x$ or $\ln(1+x)$ which have no parity.
  3. I commit because uniqueness of the Maclaurin series guarantees that the 'wrong-parity' coefficients are exactly zero — no computation needed.
  4. I plan to differentiate only to the orders matching the correct parity, and to use the zero-orders as built-in checks of my working.
  5. Tactically I still carry the full expression through intermediate derivatives (since $f^{(k+1)}$ depends on $f^{(k)}$), but I evaluate at $0$ only at the orders I need.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Test parity: compute $f(-x)$ and compare with $\pm f(x)$.
  2. If even, declare $f^{(2k+1)}(0)=0$ for all $k$; if odd, declare $f^{(2k)}(0)=0$ for all $k$.
  3. Differentiate to the required order, but only substitute $x=0$ at the non-zero orders.
  4. At each substitution, confirm any odd (resp. even) derivative you do evaluate gives $0$ — if not, recheck the differentiation.
  5. Assemble the series using only the surviving powers: $f(x)=\sum a_{2k}x^{2k}$ or $\sum a_{2k+1}x^{2k+1}$.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the Maclaurin series of $f(x)=\sec x$ up to $x^4$. Since $\sec(-x)=\sec x$, $f$ is even, so $f'(0)=f'''(0)=0$ without computation. Compute $f(0)=1$, $f''(x)=\sec x\tan^2 x+\sec^3 x$ giving $f''(0)=1$, and $f^{(4)}(0)=5$. Hence $\sec x = 1+\tfrac{1}{2}x^2+\tfrac{5}{24}x^4+\cdots$, with the absent $x$ and $x^3$ terms guaranteed by parity.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Odd function such as $\sinh x$ or $x\cos x$
All even-order derivatives at $0$ vanish; expand as $a_1 x+a_3 x^3+\cdots$.
Composition like $\cos(x^2)$ or $\ln(\cos x)$
Both are even, so use the same shortcut; often faster to substitute into a known even series than to differentiate.
Product of two functions of opposite parity (e.g. $x\sin x$)
Result is even (odd$\times$odd), so still apply the shortcut — only even powers survive.
Taylor expansion about $x=a\neq 0$
Parity in $x$ does not transfer to parity in $(x-a)$; the shortcut does not apply unless $f$ has symmetry about $x=a$.
Function with no parity (e.g. $e^x$, $\ln(1+x)$, $\tan(x+1)$)
Do not invoke this move; every coefficient must be computed.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $e^x$ or $\ln(1+x)$ as having a parity because their series 'look patterned'.
  • Assuming a Taylor series about $x=1$ inherits the parity of $f$ on $\mathbb{R}$.
  • Reading 'symmetric graph' loosely and forgetting to algebraically verify $f(-x)=\pm f(x)$.
  • Believing that even parity makes $f''(0)=0$ — it is the odd-order derivatives that vanish, not the even ones.

Pitfalls

  • Misclassifying parity, e.g. claiming $\cos x+\sin x$ is even or odd when it is neither.
  • Skipping the differentiation chain entirely and being unable to obtain the next non-zero derivative, which depends on the previous expression.
  • Forgetting to factor in constant terms: e.g. $1+\sin x$ is not odd, even though $\sin x$ is.
  • Using the shortcut on a Maclaurin expansion of $f(x-1)$ where the shifted argument destroys the parity in $x$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure Maclaurin/Taylor series content; uses only parity of standard functions ($\sin,\cos,\tan,\sinh,\cosh,\sec$) and the uniqueness of power-series coefficients.
