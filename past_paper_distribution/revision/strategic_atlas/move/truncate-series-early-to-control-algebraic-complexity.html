Sequences & Series

Truncate series early to control algebraic complexity

Family: Order Management Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question specifies a definite truncation point such as 'up to and including the term in x^n', 'first four non-zero terms', or 'in ascending powers of x as far as the term in x^4'. It also fires whenever a Maclaurin/binomial expansion is to be multiplied, substituted, or composed with another series. Any phrasing that fixes a target degree n upfront is the cue.

Motivation — Why this works

Computing terms beyond the required order is wasted effort and creates opportunities for arithmetic slips. By committing to a maximum degree n at the outset, every subsequent multiplication, substitution, or addition can immediately discard contributions of degree greater than n. This is especially important when multiplying or composing series, where cross-terms grow combinatorially. The principle relies on the fact that higher-order terms in one factor cannot lower the degree of their products with another factor's terms.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the explicit phrase 'up to and including x^n' or 'first k non-zero terms' and translate it into a single integer N = target degree.
  2. Rule out any approach that expands everything fully and simplifies later — that path multiplies error risk.
  3. Commit to carrying '+ O(x^{N+1})' (or '+ ...') through every line so truncation is visible and disciplined.
  4. Bridge: rewrite each factor to its own minimal degree needed, then plan which cross-products (i,j) with i+j ≤ N are required.
  5. Tactical decision: when raising a series to a power u^k, stop computing u^k once the lowest-degree term of u^k exceeds N.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the target degree N from the question (or convert 'first k non-zero terms' to a degree).
  2. Expand each component series only up to degree N, writing '+ ...' to signal truncation.
  3. When multiplying two series A·B for target degree N, list only pairs (i,j) with i+j ≤ N and sum their products.
  4. When substituting u = g(x) (with g(0)=0) into a series Σ a_k u^k, compute u^k only while k·(lowest degree of g) ≤ N.
  5. Collect like powers, double-check no required cross-term has been dropped, then state the answer with the agreed truncation.

Worked Micro-Example

Find the expansion of $e^x \sin x$ up to and including $x^4$. Take $e^x = 1 + x + \tfrac{x^2}{2} + \tfrac{x^3}{6} + \tfrac{x^4}{24} + \dots$ and $\sin x = x - \tfrac{x^3}{6} + \dots$. Only keep products with total degree $\le 4$: $1\cdot x = x$, $x\cdot x = x^2$, $\tfrac{x^2}{2}\cdot x = \tfrac{x^3}{2}$, $1\cdot(-\tfrac{x^3}{6}) = -\tfrac{x^3}{6}$, $\tfrac{x^3}{6}\cdot x = \tfrac{x^4}{6}$, $x\cdot(-\tfrac{x^3}{6}) = -\tfrac{x^4}{6}$. Sum: $x + x^2 + \tfrac{x^3}{3} + 0\cdot x^4 + \dots$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Binomial expansion (1+ax)^n multiplied by a polynomial
Decide how many binomial terms are needed: if multiplied by a polynomial of degree d and target is N, take binomial terms up to degree N − 0 (since constant in poly pairs with degree N in binomial), but stop at degree N overall in any product.
Composing series, e.g. ln(1 + sin x) up to x^n
Substitute u = sin x truncated to degree N, then in ln(1+u) = u − u^2/2 + u^3/3 − ... only compute u^k while the leading degree of u^k ≤ N.
Integrating/differentiating a Maclaurin series
Differentiation lowers all degrees by 1, integration raises by 1; adjust the working truncation degree accordingly so the final answer still reaches degree N.
Using truncated series to estimate a definite integral to given d.p.
Choose N so that the first discarded term, after integration, is bounded below the required accuracy (e.g. < 0.00005 for 4 d.p.).

Common Misreadings

  • Confusing 'first four non-zero terms' with 'up to x^4' — for an odd-only series like sin x, four non-zero terms reach x^7.
  • Confusing 'up to and including x^3' with 'up to but not including x^3'.
  • Treating 'in ascending powers of x as far as the term in x^n' as 'exactly n+1 terms' rather than 'all powers ≤ n'.

Pitfalls

  • Truncating a factor too early so a needed cross-term is missing — e.g. dropping the x^3 term of one series when it pairs with the constant term of the other to give x^3 in the product.
  • Forgetting that u = g(x) must have g(0)=0 before bounding u^k by leading-degree arguments; otherwise infinitely many u^k contribute to each degree.
  • Carrying inconsistent truncation across lines (some terms kept to x^4, others to x^3), causing missing or spurious final coefficients.
  • Using a binomial expansion outside its radius of convergence after truncation, then trusting the truncated polynomial outside |x| < 1.

A-level Scope Notes

Applies throughout Edexcel IAL Pure (binomial series for rational/negative indices, Maclaurin/Taylor series in Further Pure) whenever an expansion is requested to a stated order or used inside a product, quotient, composition, or integration.
