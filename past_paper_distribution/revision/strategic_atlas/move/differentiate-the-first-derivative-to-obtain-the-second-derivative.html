Differentiation

Differentiate the first derivative to obtain the second derivative

Family: Sequential Differentiation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever a question asks for d²y/dx² (or f''(x)) after a previous part has already produced dy/dx (or f'(x)). Typical phrasings include 'Hence find d²y/dx²', 'Find f''(x)', or stationary-point questions that require the second derivative test. The cue is the explicit second-order notation combined with an available first-derivative expression from earlier work.

Motivation — Why this works

By definition d²y/dx² = d/dx(dy/dx), so the second derivative is obtained by treating the first derivative as a new function and differentiating it once more. Reusing the part (a) result avoids redundant work and prevents the algebraic errors that arise from trying to 'square' the differentiation in one go. Each pass through the power rule reduces the polynomial degree by one and annihilates the constant term.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the question asks for d²y/dx², and I already have dy/dx from the previous part — that expression is my new starting point.
  2. I rule out re-differentiating the original y from scratch or trying to apply some 'second-derivative formula' directly, since the definition is simply repeated differentiation.
  3. I commit because the structure of dy/dx is again a polynomial, so the same term-by-term power rule will work cleanly.
  4. I bridge to action by writing dy/dx out explicitly and lining up each term ready for differentiation.
  5. Tactically, I flag any constant term in dy/dx so I remember its derivative is 0, and I double-check the part (a) answer before propagating it.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write down dy/dx from the previous part as the function to be differentiated.
  2. Apply the power rule term by term: d/dx(ax^n) = a·n·x^(n−1).
  3. Set the derivative of any constant term to 0 and drop it.
  4. Combine the resulting terms to give d²y/dx².
  5. Simplify and, if the question continues, substitute the relevant x-value (e.g. for the second-derivative test).

Worked Micro-Example

Given $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 15x^2 - 12x - 7$ from part (a). Differentiate term by term: $\dfrac{d}{dx}(15x^2) = 30x$, $\dfrac{d}{dx}(-12x) = -12$, $\dfrac{d}{dx}(-7) = 0$. Therefore $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 30x - 12$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Higher-order derivatives such as d³y/dx³ or f'''(x)
Repeat the same sequential strategy: differentiate the previous order's result once more using the power rule.
First derivative contains exponential, logarithmic or trigonometric terms (e.g. e^{kx}, ln x, sin x)
Switch from pure power rule to the appropriate rule for each term (chain, product, standard derivatives), but the sequential 'differentiate dy/dx' framing is unchanged.
First derivative is a quotient or product (e.g. dy/dx came from quotient rule)
Often simplify or rewrite dy/dx (e.g. as a sum of powers of x) before differentiating again to avoid nested quotient-rule applications.
Second-derivative test for stationary points
After obtaining d²y/dx², substitute the stationary x-value and use the sign to classify the point (positive ⇒ minimum, negative ⇒ maximum).
Parametric form (Further/IAL P3 contexts)
Compute dy/dx as (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) first, then d²y/dx² = d/dt(dy/dx) ÷ (dx/dt) — sequential differentiation is with respect to the parameter, not x directly.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating d²y/dx² as (dy/dx)² and squaring the first derivative instead of differentiating it.
  • Differentiating the original y twice in a single mental step and miscounting exponents.
  • Mistaking d²y/dx² for d/dx(y²) or some other product.

Pitfalls

  • Carrying a constant term from dy/dx into the second derivative instead of setting its derivative to 0.
  • Propagating an error from part (a) without checking it, so the second derivative is wrong even though the method is right.
  • Dropping the coefficient when applying the power rule (e.g. writing d/dx(15x²) = 2x instead of 30x).
  • Sign slips on negative terms when reducing the exponent.

A-level Scope Notes

This move is core to Edexcel IAL Pure Mathematics (P1/P2) for polynomial second derivatives and the second-derivative test, and extends naturally to P3/P4 contexts involving exponentials, logs, trig, and parametric differentiation.
