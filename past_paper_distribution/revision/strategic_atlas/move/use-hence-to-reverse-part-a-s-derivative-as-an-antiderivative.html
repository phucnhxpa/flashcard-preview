Trigger — When to use

Fires when a multi-part question has a 'show that' or differentiation result in part (a) and part (b) opens with 'Hence' followed by an integral or differential equation. The integrand in part (b) closely mirrors the structure of part (a)'s derivative — same composite function, same denominator, possibly off by a constant multiple. The word 'hence' is the strongest cue; 'use part (a) to ...' or 'deduce' play the same role. Recognise that the antiderivative is essentially the function that was differentiated in part (a), up to a constant.

Motivation — Why this works

By the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, if F'(y) = f(y) was established in part (a), then ∫f(y) dy = F(y) + C. Examiners deliberately stage the differentiation first so the candidate does not have to hunt for a substitution; the chain-rule output IS the substitution. Matching the integrand against k·F'(y) for some constant k gives the antiderivative as (1/k)F(y) + C immediately. This converts a potentially awkward integral into a one-line read-off and avoids redundant work.

Thought Process — Step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the word 'hence' linking part (b) to part (a) — the examiner is signalling reuse, not a fresh start.
  2. Rule out independent techniques (parts, partial fractions, fresh substitution) since the structure of the integrand visibly echoes part (a)'s derivative.
  3. Commit by writing part (a)'s derivative beside part (b)'s integrand and comparing factors to find the scalar k linking them.
  4. Bridge: declare u = (the inner function from part (a)) or simply quote ∫F'(y) dy = F(y), so the antiderivative is (1/k)·F(y) + C.
  5. Tactically, track the constant multiplier carefully (here the factor of 1/2 from du = (2/y)dy) and keep the modulus inside any logarithm.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Write down part (a)'s result: d/dy[F(y)] = G(y), where F and G are explicit.
  2. Compare the integrand in part (b) to G(y); identify the scalar k such that integrand = k·G(y) (or G(y) = k·integrand).
  3. Conclude ∫(integrand) dy = (1/k)·F(y) + C, quoting 'by part (a)'.
  4. If part (b) is a differential equation, separate variables first, then apply this recognition to the side that matches part (a).
  5. Apply boundary/initial conditions to fix C and simplify to the requested form.

Worked Micro-Example

Part (a): show d/dy[(1+2\ln y)^{1/2}] = \dfrac{1}{y\sqrt{1+2\ln y}}. Part (b): hence find \int \dfrac{1}{y(1+2\ln y)}\,dy. Let u=1+2\ln y so du=\tfrac{2}{y}dy. Then \int \dfrac{1}{y(1+2\ln y)}\,dy = \tfrac{1}{2}\int \dfrac{du}{u} = \tfrac{1}{2}\ln|1+2\ln y| + C.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Part (a) gives d/dx[ln(sin x)] = cot x
Hence ∫cot x dx = ln|sin x| + C — direct read-off, no scalar adjustment needed.
Part (a) gives d/dx[x e^{x}] = (1+x)e^{x}
Hence ∫(1+x)e^{x} dx = x e^{x} + C, sidestepping integration by parts.
Part (a) gives d/dx[(1+x^{2})^{3/2}] = 3x\sqrt{1+x^{2}}
Hence ∫x\sqrt{1+x^{2}}\,dx = \tfrac{1}{3}(1+x^{2})^{3/2} + C using k=3.
Separable differential equation where one side matches part (a)
Separate first, then quote part (a) for the matching side; the other side is integrated directly.

Common Misreadings

  • Ignoring 'hence' and starting a fresh substitution like t = ln y — gets the answer but loses the marks tied to citing part (a).
  • Mistaking the integrand for k·F(y) rather than k·F'(y), leading to a wrong antiderivative.
  • Treating 'hence' as 'or otherwise' and bypassing part (a) entirely, missing the structural hint.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the scalar factor (e.g. the 1/2 from du = (2/y)dy) when reading off the antiderivative.
  • Omitting the modulus inside ln, costing a presentation mark.
  • Forgetting +C, especially in indefinite integrals embedded in a differential equation.
  • Back-substituting incorrectly when u was introduced explicitly.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL C34/P3 'show that ... hence integrate' pattern; uses only chain rule, separation of variables, and ln|·| antiderivative — all firmly in syllabus.
