Algebra & Functions

Collect dy/dx terms and factor to isolate the derivative

Family: Collect And Factor Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires immediately after implicit differentiation produces an equation containing dy/dx in two or more additive terms. Cues: the equation is linear in dy/dx (no (dy/dx)^2 or higher), with dy/dx multiplied by different expressions in x and y. Also fires after differentiating parametric/implicit relations where products like xy, e^{f(x)}y, or y^n appear. The goal phrase is usually 'find dy/dx' or 'show that dy/dx = ...'.

Motivation — Why this works

Because differentiation is linear, dy/dx appears only to the first power and can be treated as a single algebraic unknown. Grouping all terms containing dy/dx on one side gives an equation of the form A(x,y)·(dy/dx) = B(x,y), a linear equation in dy/dx. Factoring then dividing is the unique algebraic move that solves a linear equation for its unknown. This is the standard endgame of implicit differentiation in A-level.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that dy/dx appears in more than one term but always to the first power — the equation is linear in dy/dx.
  2. Rule out solving as a quadratic or rearranging y explicitly; neither is needed because the structure is linear.
  3. Commit to treating dy/dx as a common factor: this reduces the problem to (coefficient)·(dy/dx) = RHS.
  4. Bridge: separate terms into those containing dy/dx and those that don't, moving the latter to the opposite side.
  5. Tactically choose which side keeps the dy/dx terms (usually the side that gives a positive or simpler coefficient) and watch signs when transposing.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify every term containing dy/dx after implicit differentiation.
  2. Move all dy/dx terms to one side and all remaining terms to the other side, tracking sign changes.
  3. Factor dy/dx out of its side, giving (sum of coefficients)·(dy/dx) = (remaining expression).
  4. Divide both sides by the bracketed coefficient to obtain dy/dx as a single fraction.
  5. Simplify the resulting expression where possible (cancel common factors, combine like terms).

Worked Micro-Example

Differentiate $e^{-2x}y + 2y^2 = x$ implicitly: $-2e^{-2x}y + e^{-2x}\frac{dy}{dx} + 4y\frac{dy}{dx} = 1$. Collect: $(e^{-2x} + 4y)\frac{dy}{dx} = 1 + 2ye^{-2x}$. Hence $\frac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{1 + 2ye^{-2x}}{e^{-2x} + 4y}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Implicit polynomial relation, e.g. $x^3 + xy + y^3 = 7$
After differentiating, collect $\frac{dy}{dx}$ terms from $x\frac{dy}{dx}$ and $3y^2\frac{dy}{dx}$, factor to get $(x + 3y^2)\frac{dy}{dx} = -(3x^2 + y)$.
Logarithmic implicit equation, e.g. $\ln(xy) + y = x$
Differentiating gives $\frac{1}{x} + \frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{dy}{dx} = 1$; factor $\frac{dy}{dx}$ from the last two terms.
Trig implicit relation, e.g. $\sin(x+y) = xy$
Both sides produce dy/dx terms; bring all dy/dx terms to one side before factoring.
Parametric second derivative setups where $\frac{dy}{dx}$ appears inside another expression
Treat $\frac{dy}{dx}$ as the unknown symbol $p$, factor, then back-substitute.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating dy/dx as if it were dy and dx separately, then trying to 'cancel' them.
  • Believing the equation is nonlinear in dy/dx because dy/dx is multiplied by y (it is still linear in dy/dx).
  • Trying to solve for y explicitly first, when isolating dy/dx directly is sufficient.

Pitfalls

  • Sign errors when transposing terms across the equals sign.
  • Missing a dy/dx term (often the one hidden inside a product rule expansion) and factoring incompletely.
  • Forgetting to bracket the coefficient before dividing, leading to division by only part of it.
  • Dropping the factor of y' from chain-rule terms like $\frac{d}{dx}(y^n) = ny^{n-1}\frac{dy}{dx}$.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 implicit differentiation; the algebra used is standard linear-equation manipulation taught from GCSE onward.
