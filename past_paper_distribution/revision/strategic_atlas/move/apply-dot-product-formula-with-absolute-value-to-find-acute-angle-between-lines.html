Vectors

Apply dot-product formula with absolute value to find acute angle between lines

Family: Direction Vector Angle Frequency: 2 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question asks for the acute angle (or its cosine) between two lines, or between a line and a specific vector such as a displacement AP. Direction vectors d₁, d₂ are readable directly from parametric/vector equations of the form r = a + λd. Phrasings include 'find the acute angle between l₁ and l₂', 'find the angle the line makes with AP', or 'show that cos θ = ...'. The key cue is 'angle between lines' (orientationless) rather than 'angle between two specific directed vectors'.

Motivation — Why this works

A line has no intrinsic orientation: its direction vector d and −d describe the same line, so the angle must be the acute one. The formula cos θ = |d₁ · d₂| / (|d₁| |d₂|) achieves this by taking the absolute value of the dot product, automatically discarding the obtuse supplement that would arise if the chosen direction vectors point 'opposite ways'. This works because the dot product encodes cos of the angle between directed vectors, and |cos θ| corresponds to the acute angle in [0, π/2]. The direction vectors are read straight off the parametric forms — no normal, projection, or cross product is needed.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the question asks for an angle between two lines (or between a line and a vector), not between two specific directed vectors — so orientation must not matter.
  2. Rule out alternatives: cross-product magnitude gives sin θ but is heavier; projection methods are unnecessary; for line–plane problems we'd use the normal with sin θ instead — but here both objects are lines/vectors.
  3. Commit to the dot-product route because direction vectors are immediately available from r = a + λd, making the calculation a one-formula job.
  4. Bridge to operation: read off d₁ and d₂ from the equations, then plan to compute d₁·d₂, |d₁|, |d₂| separately to keep arithmetic clean.
  5. Tactical decision: include the absolute value bars |d₁·d₂| in the numerator from the start, so a negative dot product (which would correspond to a particular choice of direction sense) automatically yields the acute angle without a sign-correction afterthought.
  6. After computing cos θ, sanity check: cos θ should lie in [0,1]; if you get a value in [−1,0), you forgot the absolute value and must take its modulus.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Read direction vectors d₁ and d₂ (or AP and d) directly from the parametric equations / coordinates.
  2. Compute the dot product d₁ · d₂ = x₁x₂ + y₁y₂ + z₁z₂.
  3. Take the absolute value: |d₁ · d₂|.
  4. Compute magnitudes |d₁| = √(x₁²+y₁²+z₁²) and |d₂| similarly; simplify surds (e.g. √45 = 3√5).
  5. Form cos θ = |d₁ · d₂| / (|d₁| |d₂|) and simplify the surd denominator.
  6. If a numerical angle is required, evaluate θ = cos⁻¹(...) keeping full precision until the final step.

Worked Micro-Example

Lines have direction vectors $d_1=(2,-5,4)$ and $d_2=(4,1,2)$. Then $d_1\cdot d_2 = 8-5+8=11$, $|d_1|=\sqrt{45}=3\sqrt5$, $|d_2|=\sqrt{21}$, so $\cos\theta = \dfrac{|11|}{3\sqrt5\cdot\sqrt{21}} = \dfrac{11}{3\sqrt{105}}$, giving $\theta\approx 69.1°$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Angle between a line and a plane
Use sin θ = |d · n| / (|d| |n|), where n is the plane's normal — same absolute-value logic, but sine because d is perpendicular-complement to n.
Angle between two specific directed vectors (not lines)
Drop the absolute value: cos θ = (a · b) / (|a| |b|). The sign of the dot product genuinely conveys whether the angle is acute or obtuse.
Angle between a displacement vector AP and a line l
Treat AP as d₁ and the line's direction as d₂; keep the absolute value, since the line still has no fixed orientation.
Negative dot product encountered without absolute value
The angle obtained is the obtuse supplement; take π − θ, or equivalently restart with |d₁·d₂|.

Common Misreadings

  • Substituting position vectors (points on the lines) into the dot product instead of direction vectors.
  • Treating 'angle between lines' as 'angle between directed vectors' and reporting an obtuse answer.
  • Confusing this set-up with a line-and-plane angle and mistakenly using the normal vector.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the absolute value in the numerator and reporting cos θ < 0 as the final answer.
  • Surd-simplification slips, e.g. not recognising √45 = 3√5 or √5·√21 = √105.
  • Rounding cos θ before applying cos⁻¹, losing accuracy in the final angle.
  • Using the position vector of P (e.g. OP) instead of the displacement vector AP in line-and-vector problems.

A-level Scope Notes

Within Edexcel IAL Core Pure / FP3 vectors. The scalar product formula a·b = |a||b|cos θ is in the formula booklet; the absolute-value variant for lines is a standard application, not a separate booklet entry. Stay in 3D Cartesian vectors — do not generalise to higher dimensions or to oriented angles in the plane.
