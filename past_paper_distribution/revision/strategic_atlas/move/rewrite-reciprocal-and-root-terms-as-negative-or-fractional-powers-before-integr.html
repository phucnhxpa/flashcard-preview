Integration

Rewrite reciprocal and root terms as negative or fractional powers before integrating

Family: Algebraic Preprocessing Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever the integrand contains terms not in the explicit ax^n form required by the power rule. Typical cues are reciprocal powers like 2/x^3, 1/x^5, or surd forms like √x, 1/√x, and mixed expressions such as (x^2 + 1)/x^2 after splitting. Any term written as a fraction with x in the denominator or as a root of x signals that index-law preprocessing is needed before applying ∫ax^n dx.

Motivation — Why this works

The power rule ∫ax^n dx = ax^(n+1)/(n+1) + c (for n ≠ −1) only applies when the term is in explicit power form ax^n. Index laws 1/x^n = x^(−n) and √[m]{x^k} = x^(k/m) convert reciprocal and root expressions into this canonical form, so the exponent arithmetic n+1 becomes immediate and mechanical. Without this rewrite there is no direct integration formula to invoke.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I scan each term of the integrand and check whether it is already in ax^n form; reciprocal or root terms flag the need for preprocessing.
  2. I rule out substitution or partial fractions because the term is just a single power of x in disguise — no composition, no rational function decomposition required.
  3. I commit to rewriting because index laws give a one-line conversion to the form the power rule demands.
  4. I bridge to operation by mapping 1/x^n ↦ x^(−n), √x ↦ x^(1/2), 1/√x ↦ x^(−1/2), preserving every coefficient and sign.
  5. I check that no exponent equals −1 (which would require ln|x| instead of the power rule) before proceeding.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify each term not in ax^n form (reciprocals, roots, or constants).
  2. Apply index laws: 1/x^n = x^(−n); x^(p/q) = √[q]{x^p}; constants k = kx^0.
  3. Carry coefficients and signs through unchanged: −2/x^3 becomes −2x^(−3).
  4. Confirm no resulting exponent equals −1 (else use the ln|x| rule for that term).
  5. Rewrite the full integrand as a sum of ax^n terms ready for term-by-term integration.

Worked Micro-Example

Integrate $\int\left(3x^2 - \dfrac{2}{x^3} + 5\right)dx$. Rewrite as $\int(3x^2 - 2x^{-3} + 5x^0)\,dx$. Apply the power rule: $\dfrac{3x^3}{3} - \dfrac{2x^{-2}}{-2} + 5x + c = x^3 + x^{-2} + 5x + c$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Surd term such as ∫√x dx
Rewrite √x = x^(1/2), then power rule gives (2/3)x^(3/2) + c.
Reciprocal surd ∫1/√x dx
Rewrite as x^(−1/2); integral is 2x^(1/2) + c.
Higher reciprocal ∫3/x^5 dx
Rewrite as 3x^(−5); integral is 3x^(−4)/(−4) = −(3/4)x^(−4) + c.
Mixed numerator over single power, e.g. ∫(x^2+1)/x^2 dx
Split first to 1 + x^(−2), then integrate term by term.
Cube-root term ∫x^(1/3) dx or ∫1/∛x dx
Use x^(1/3) or x^(−1/3); the same index-law principle applies.

Common Misreadings

  • Reading 2/x^3 as (2/x)·3 or as 2x^3, losing the negative exponent entirely.
  • Trying to integrate 1/x^n directly as ln of something, confusing the n ≥ 2 case with the special 1/x → ln|x| rule.
  • Treating √x as if it were x and getting (1/2)x^2 instead of (2/3)x^(3/2).

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the negative sign: −2/x^3 must become −2x^(−3), not +2x^(−3).
  • Forgetting that n+1 in the denominator of the power rule fails when n = −1 — that case needs ln|x|.
  • Mishandling fractional arithmetic: 1/2 + 1 = 3/2, and dividing by 3/2 means multiplying by 2/3.
  • Leaving the answer in negative-exponent form when the question expects surd or fractional notation (or vice versa).

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (C2/P2 onward): index laws and the power rule for integration of x^n with n ≠ −1, including negative and fractional exponents.
