Reconstruct polynomial from known roots using factored form

Family: Root Factor Reconstruction Frequency: 5 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question gives all (or enough) roots of a polynomial of known degree and asks for its coefficients, or for unknown letters A, B, C, D in its expanded form. Visual cues: x-intercepts read off a sketch, a stated repeated root ('touches the x-axis'), a complex conjugate pair already producing a real quadratic factor, or a monic polynomial with all roots listed. Equivalent phrasings: 'find the equation of the curve given its roots and one further point', 'determine p, q, r if the polynomial has roots …', 'find A, B, C given the quartic has roots …'. Whenever the count of known roots equals (or nearly equals) the degree, this move fires.

Motivation — Why this works

By the Factor Theorem, x = r being a root is equivalent to (x − r) being a factor. A degree-n polynomial with n known roots is determined up to a single leading coefficient: p(x) = a(x − r₁)(x − r₂)…(x − rₙ). This collapses what would otherwise be an n-by-n simultaneous system in the unknown coefficients into a single multiplication followed by coefficient comparison, and uses only one extra condition (a known point, or monic-ness) to pin down a. It is the same underlying identity that powers Vieta's formulas, but applied directly via expansion rather than by symmetric-function lookup.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the count: how many roots are given vs the degree of the polynomial? If they match (or match up to one leading coefficient), reconstruction is on the table.
  2. Rule out the brute-force alternative: substituting each root and any extra point into the expanded form Ax^n + … to build a simultaneous system. This is correct but slow and sign-error-prone.
  3. Commit to factored form because each root contributes a clean linear factor (x − rᵢ); a repeated root contributes (x − r)², and a complex conjugate pair α ± βi contributes the real quadratic x² − 2αx + (α² + β²).
  4. Decide the leading coefficient: if the polynomial is stated monic, a = 1 directly; otherwise use the one remaining condition (a given point on the curve, or a stated leading coefficient) to solve for a.
  5. Decide the expansion order tactically: pair complex-conjugate factors first (to keep arithmetic real), and pair repeated factors together; then multiply the resulting quadratics, which is cleaner than chaining four linear distributions.
  6. Finally, compare coefficients of like powers of x against the target form to read off the unknowns, and sanity-check the constant term as a quick verification.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. List all known roots, including multiplicities and conjugate pairs.
  2. Write each root r as a factor (x − r); for a conjugate pair α ± βi, combine immediately into x² − 2αx + (α² + β²); for a repeated root r of multiplicity k, write (x − r)^k.
  3. Insert the leading coefficient a in front: p(x) = a · (product of factors).
  4. Determine a: set a = 1 if monic; otherwise substitute the given point (x₀, y₀) and solve a = y₀ / [product of (x₀ − rᵢ)].
  5. Expand by multiplying paired quadratics first, then collect like terms.
  6. Match coefficients of x^n, x^{n−1}, … with the target form to read off the requested unknowns.
  7. Verify by checking the constant term equals a · ∏(−rᵢ).

Worked Micro-Example

Find p, q, r if x³ + px² + qx + r is monic with roots 2 + 3i, 2 − 3i, 1.\n Conjugate pair → x² − 4x + 13. Real root → (x − 1).\n Multiply: (x² − 4x + 13)(x − 1) = x³ − 4x² + 13x − x² + 4x − 13 = x³ − 5x² + 17x − 13.\n Compare: p = −5, q = 17, r = −13.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Quadratic with two known roots and one extra point
Write y = a(x − r₁)(x − r₂); substitute the extra point to find a.
Cubic with three x-intercepts read from a sketch and a labelled point P
Write y = a(x − r₁)(x − r₂)(x − r₃); use P to solve for a, then expand.
Repeated root (curve touches the x-axis at r)
Include (x − r)² as a factor, contributing multiplicity 2.
Complex conjugate pair α ± βi
Combine into the real quadratic x² − 2αx + (α² + β²) before multiplying further.
Non-monic polynomial (leading coefficient stated, e.g. 2x³ + …)
Pull the stated leading coefficient out front: p(x) = 2(x − r₁)(x − r₂)(x − r₃).
Quartic with one conjugate pair and one repeated real root
Form two quadratics — one from the conjugate pair, one as (x − r)² — and multiply the two quadratics.

Common Misreadings

  • Setting up a full simultaneous system in A, B, C, D by substituting each root into the expanded form — correct but vastly slower and prone to arithmetic slips.
  • Forgetting the leading coefficient a and assuming the polynomial equals just the product of factors when it is not stated to be monic.
  • Treating a conjugate pair as two separate complex factors and expanding them last instead of first, leaving complex arithmetic strewn through the workings.
  • Attempting polynomial long division by an unknown-coefficient polynomial — impossible when A, B, C are still unknown.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error in factors: a root x = −4 gives the factor (x + 4), not (x − 4).
  • Sign error during distribution, especially −1 · (−4x) = +4x or −8x · (−6x) = +48x².
  • Misreading the constant term: the constant in (x − r₁)(x − r₂)(x − r₃) is (−1)³ r₁r₂r₃, so signs must be tracked.
  • Assuming a = 1 without checking that the curve passes through the additional given point.
  • For conjugate pairs, miscomputing the real quadratic: it is x² − 2αx + (α² + β²), not x² − 2αx + α² − β².

A-level Scope Notes

Stays inside Edexcel IAL Core Pure (factor theorem, P3/P4) and Further Pure (complex roots come in conjugate pairs for real-coefficient polynomials, FP1). The factor theorem and Vieta-style relations between roots and coefficients are in scope; Cardano's formula and general quintic methods are out of scope and should not be invoked.
