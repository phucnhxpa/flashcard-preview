Differentiation

Differentiate or integrate term-by-term using linearity on a sum of power terms

Family: Linearity Decomposition Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever the expression has been rewritten (or already appears) as a finite sum/difference of terms each of the form ax^n, with constant coefficients and real exponents. Cues: a polynomial, a sum after expanding brackets, or a sum after rewriting roots/reciprocals as powers (e.g. 1/x² → x^{-2}, √x → x^{1/2}). No term contains a product of two x-dependent factors, no composition, and no quotient that hasn't already been split. The presence of '+' or '−' between independent power terms is the green light.

Motivation — Why this works

Differentiation and integration are linear operators: d/dx[af(x) ± bg(x)] = af'(x) ± bg'(x), and likewise for ∫. This means a sum of ax^n terms can be handled by applying the power rule (d/dx(ax^n)=anx^{n−1}, ∫ax^n dx = ax^{n+1}/(n+1)+C for n≠−1) to each term in isolation. No product, quotient, or chain rule is needed because the structure is additive, not multiplicative or composite. Recognising this turns a multi-term expression into a list of mechanical single-term computations.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I scan the expression and see it is a sum/difference of terms, each of which is a constant times a power of x.
  2. I rule out product rule (no two x-dependent factors multiplied), quotient rule (no quotient left after rewriting), and chain rule (no composition like (…)^n with an inner function).
  3. I commit to linearity: I will differentiate or integrate each term independently and then add the results.
  4. I rewrite any roots or reciprocals as x^n with explicit (possibly negative or fractional) exponents so the power rule applies cleanly.
  5. Term-by-term I track coefficient and exponent separately, watching sign interactions when the exponent is negative.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Ensure every term is in the form ax^n; rewrite 1/x^k as x^{-k} and √[m]{x^k} as x^{k/m}.
  2. For differentiation of each ax^n: new coefficient = a·n, new exponent = n−1, giving anx^{n−1}.
  3. For integration of each ax^n (n≠−1): new exponent = n+1, new coefficient = a/(n+1), giving a x^{n+1}/(n+1).
  4. Carry the original ± sign of each term through unchanged; reattach signs after computing each piece.
  5. Sum the resulting terms; for indefinite integrals append a single constant +C at the end.

Worked Micro-Example

Given y = 4x^{3} - 5x^{-2}.\n Differentiate: \frac{dy}{dx} = 4\cdot 3\,x^{2} + (-5)(-2)\,x^{-3} = 12x^{2} + 10x^{-3}.\n Integrate: \int y\,dx = \frac{4}{4}x^{4} + \frac{-5}{-1}x^{-1} + C = x^{4} + 5x^{-1} + C.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Polynomial in x, e.g. y = 7x^{5} - 2x^{3} + x - 9
Apply power rule to each term; the constant -9 differentiates to 0 and integrates to -9x.
Sum of fractional powers, e.g. y = 3x^{1/2} + 2x^{-3/2}
Power rule still applies; arithmetic on fractions: derivative gives \tfrac{3}{2}x^{-1/2} - 3x^{-5/2}.
Expression needing pre-expansion, e.g. y = (2x+1)(x-3)
Expand to 2x^{2} - 5x - 3 first, then differentiate/integrate term-by-term.
Quotient that splits, e.g. y = (x^{3}+2)/x
Rewrite as x^{2} + 2x^{-1}, then differentiate term-by-term (note ∫2x^{-1}dx = 2\ln|x| if integrating).
Definite integral of a sum, e.g. \int_{1}^{2}(3x^{2}-4x^{-2})dx
Integrate term-by-term to get x^{3}+4x^{-1}, then evaluate at the limits — no +C needed.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 4x^{3} - 5x^{-2} as a product (4x^{3})(-5x^{-2}) and reaching for the product rule.
  • Seeing x^{-2} and reaching for the chain rule as if it were a composition.
  • Reading a quotient like (x^{3}+2)/x as needing the quotient rule before noticing it splits into a sum.
  • Believing the +C must be attached to each integrated term separately rather than once at the end.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when differentiating ax^{n} with negative n: e.g. (−5)(−2) miscomputed as −10 instead of +10.
  • Sign error when integrating: −5/(−1) miscomputed as −5 instead of +5.
  • Reducing exponent incorrectly: from x^{-2}, derivative exponent is −3 (not −1); integral exponent is −1 (not −3).
  • Forgetting that integrating x^{-1} requires \ln|x|, not the power rule (the rule fails at n=−1).
  • Dropping the constant of integration +C on indefinite integrals.
  • Differentiating a constant term to something other than 0, or integrating it to 0 instead of (constant)·x.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1/P2): linearity of d/dx and ∫, power rule for real exponents, with the standard caveat that ∫x^{-1}dx = \ln|x| + C is treated separately once logarithmic integration is introduced.
