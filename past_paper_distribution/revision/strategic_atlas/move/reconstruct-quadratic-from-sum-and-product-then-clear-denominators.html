Algebra & Functions

Reconstruct quadratic from sum and product, then clear denominators

Family: Reverse Vieta'S Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when you already have (or have just computed) the sum S and product P of two target roots — typically transformed roots like α+β, αβ, α², 1/α, 2α−1, etc. — and the question asks for 'a quadratic equation with integer coefficients'. Phrasings include 'find a quadratic equation whose roots are…', 'show that … satisfies an equation of the form ax²+bx+c=0', or 'find integers a, b, c such that…'. The article 'a' (not 'the') signals any nonzero scalar multiple is acceptable.

Motivation — Why this works

By Vieta's, if r₁ and r₂ are roots of a monic quadratic, then r₁+r₂ = −b and r₁r₂ = c, so the monic reconstruction is x² − Sx + P = 0. Reversing Vieta's is exact, not approximate. When S and P are rational, the monic form has fractional coefficients; multiplying through by the LCM of the denominators preserves the root set and yields the required integer form. Any positive scalar multiple is also a valid answer.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice that the problem has already delivered (or is one step from delivering) S and P for the new pair of roots.
  2. Rule out re-deriving the roots individually or solving the original equation explicitly — that is unnecessary and often impossible cleanly.
  3. Commit to x² − Sx + P = 0 as the monic skeleton because it is the unique minimal-degree fit.
  4. Bridge: substitute the numerical S and P, being careful with the sign flip on S.
  5. Tactical: identify the LCM of all denominators appearing in S and P, multiply every term by it, and check the answer by recomputing −b/a and c/a.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Compute or read off S = sum of new roots and P = product of new roots.
  2. Write the monic quadratic x² − Sx + P = 0.
  3. Substitute the numerical values, taking care with the sign of −S.
  4. Find the LCM L of the denominators of the coefficients.
  5. Multiply every term by L to obtain ax² + bx + c = 0 with a, b, c ∈ ℤ.
  6. Verify: check that −b/a equals S and c/a equals P.

Worked Micro-Example

Suppose the new roots have $S = -\tfrac{65}{28}$ and $P = \tfrac{25}{7}$. The monic quadratic is $x^2 + \tfrac{65}{28}x + \tfrac{25}{7} = 0$. The LCM of $28$ and $7$ is $28$, so multiplying through gives $28x^2 + 65x + 100 = 0$. Check: $-b/a = -65/28 = S$ ✓ and $c/a = 100/28 = 25/7 = P$ ✓.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Cubic reconstruction from three transformed roots
Use x³ − (Σroots)x² + (Σ pairwise products)x − (product of all three) = 0, then clear denominators by the LCM.
Question specifies a > 0 or a particular leading coefficient
After clearing denominators, multiply by −1 if needed to make a positive, or scale to match the required leading coefficient.
S or P is irrational (e.g. contains √k)
Either the surds cancel by symmetry of the new roots, or the question expects coefficients in ℝ rather than ℤ — re-read the requirement.
New roots are α² and β² (or 1/α and 1/β, etc.)
First express the new S and P in terms of the original sum and product via identities (e.g. α²+β² = S² − 2P), then apply the standard reconstruction.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'a quadratic equation' as requiring the monic form and leaving fractional coefficients in the final answer.
  • Reading S and P as the sum/product of the original roots when the question is asking about a transformed pair.
  • Assuming the answer is unique rather than unique up to a nonzero scalar.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting the sign flip: writing x² + Sx + P instead of x² − Sx + P.
  • Clearing denominators on only one term — e.g. multiplying by 7 to fix 25/7 but leaving 65/28 untouched.
  • Sign errors when S itself is negative, leading to the wrong sign on the linear coefficient.
  • Dividing out a common factor at the end and producing non-integer coefficients again.

A-level Scope Notes

This is the standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure 'roots of polynomials' technique (FP1/FP2), used routinely for transformed roots of quadratics and cubics with rational coefficients.
