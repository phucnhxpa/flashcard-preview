Integration

Split x^n as x^{n-1}·x to enable IBP against a surd denominator

Family: Reduction Formula Ibp Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when deriving a reduction formula for I_n = ∫ x^n / √(a²−x²) dx (or similar surd-denominator integrals) and a step-down of 2 in the index is required. Cue: the denominator √(a²−x²) is awkward to differentiate, but x/√(a²−x²) integrates cleanly to −√(a²−x²). Also triggered by phrasings like 'show that I_n = … I_{n−2}' or 'establish a reduction formula for ∫₀^a x^n (a²−x²)^{−1/2} dx'.

Motivation — Why this works

The factor x in the numerator is exactly the chain-rule derivative (up to a constant) of the surd a²−x², so pairing it with dx as dv yields v = −√(a²−x²) without effort. Leaving u = x^{n−1} ensures du = (n−1)x^{n−2} dx, which lowers the polynomial degree and steers the IBP output toward an x^{n−2} integral. Any other splitting either fails to integrate cleanly or fails to reduce the index.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the integrand has the structural pair (polynomial)·(surd⁻¹), and a reduction by 2 is requested — a signal for IBP rather than substitution.
  2. Rule out u = x^n, dv = (a²−x²)^{−1/2} dx because v would be arcsin, producing x^n·arcsin terms that don't fit an I_{n−2} pattern.
  3. Commit to peeling one x off the polynomial to ride along with the surd: x/√(a²−x²) is a recognisable exact-derivative form.
  4. Set u = x^{n−1}, dv = x/√(a²−x²) dx, anticipating v = −√(a²−x²) and du = (n−1)x^{n−2} dx.
  5. Plan the next algebraic step: rewrite √(a²−x²) = (a²−x²)/√(a²−x²) so the resulting ∫ x^{n−2}√(a²−x²) dx splits into a²·I_{n−2} − I_n, giving a self-referential equation for I_n.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Rewrite I_n = ∫ x^{n−1} · [x / √(a²−x²)] dx.
  2. Choose u = x^{n−1}, dv = x/√(a²−x²) dx.
  3. Compute du = (n−1)x^{n−2} dx and v = −√(a²−x²).
  4. Apply IBP: I_n = [−x^{n−1}√(a²−x²)]_limits + (n−1) ∫ x^{n−2}√(a²−x²) dx.
  5. Convert √(a²−x²) = (a²−x²)/√(a²−x²) to express the remaining integral as (n−1)[a² I_{n−2} − I_n], then solve for I_n.

Worked Micro-Example

For I_n = ∫₀¹ x^n/√(10−x²) dx, take u = x^{n−1}, dv = x/√(10−x²) dx, so du = (n−1)x^{n−2} dx and v = −√(10−x²). IBP gives I_n = [−x^{n−1}√(10−x²)]₀¹ + (n−1)∫₀¹ x^{n−2}√(10−x²) dx = −3 + (n−1)[10 I_{n−2} − I_n]. Solving: n I_n = 10(n−1) I_{n−2} − 3.

Variants — Same move, different surface

∫ x^n / √(a²−x²) dx with general a
Same split; v = −√(a²−x²) and the recurrence becomes n I_n = a²(n−1) I_{n−2} − [boundary].
∫ x^n √(a²−x²) dx
Split x^n = x^{n−1}·x and pair x√(a²−x²) dx as dv, giving v = −⅓(a²−x²)^{3/2}.
∫ x^n e^{x²} · x dx style integrals
Same idea: peel off the factor that completes a chain-rule derivative of the awkward inner function.
∫ x^n e^x dx
Analogous principle but reversed: u = x^n, dv = e^x dx, since e^x is the 'integrates unchanged' piece.

Common Misreadings

  • Choosing u = x^n, dv = (a²−x²)^{−1/2} dx, forcing v = arcsin(x/a) and an intractable x^n·arcsin integral.
  • Trying x = a sin θ trig substitution, which works but is heavier and obscures the reduction structure.
  • Splitting x^n as x^{n−2}·x², which leaves a non-exact-derivative dv.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the negative sign in v = −√(a²−x²) (the chain rule gives d/dx[−√(a²−x²)] = x/√(a²−x²)).
  • Misevaluating the boundary term: confirm x^{n−1}√(a²−x²) → 0 at x = 0 for n ≥ 1, and evaluate carefully at the upper limit.
  • Forgetting the algebraic step √(a²−x²) = (a²−x²)/√(a²−x²) needed to close the recursion onto I_n and I_{n−2}.
  • Failing to solve for I_n after the recursion produces I_n on both sides.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard Edexcel IAL Further Pure reduction-formula technique; uses only IBP, chain rule, and elementary surd algebra, all within FP2/FP3 scope.
