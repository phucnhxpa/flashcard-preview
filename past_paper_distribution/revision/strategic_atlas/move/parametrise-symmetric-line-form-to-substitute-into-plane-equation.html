Parametrise symmetric line form to substitute into plane equation

Family: Parametric Substitution Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

A line is presented in symmetric (Cartesian) form (x−a)/p = (y−b)/q = (z−c)/r and must be intersected with a plane given by ax+by+cz=d. The line cannot be substituted directly because x, y, z are tangled in a chain of equalities. The same move applies whenever a single scalar parameter is needed to reduce a multi-variable geometric condition to one equation. Cues include the words 'find the point of intersection', 'where the line meets the plane', or 'foot of perpendicular' once a perpendicular line has been built.

Motivation — Why this works

Setting the common ratio equal to a parameter t decouples the three coordinates into independent linear functions x(t), y(t), z(t). Because a non-parallel line meets a plane in exactly one point, substituting into the plane equation produces a single linear equation in t with a unique solution. This converts a 3D intersection problem into one-variable algebra, which is both faster and less error-prone than solving the symmetric chain simultaneously with the plane.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice the line is in symmetric form, so x, y, z are not yet expressed individually — I cannot substitute into 2x+4y−z=1 as it stands.
  2. I rule out manipulating two symmetric equalities at once (e.g. solving (x−3)/4=(y−5)/(−2) for y in terms of x and chaining); that route multiplies the chance of sign slips.
  3. I commit to introducing a single parameter t equal to the common ratio, because a line is naturally one-dimensional and t will appear linearly after substitution.
  4. First concrete operation: read off direction components (4, −2, 7) and base point (3, 5, 4) to write x=3+4t, y=5−2t, z=4+7t.
  5. Tactically, I substitute, collect t-terms and constants separately, solve for t, then back-substitute once into the parametric expressions to recover the point — I do not re-solve the plane equation.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Set each ratio equal to t: (x−3)/4 = (y−5)/(−2) = (z−4)/7 = t.
  2. Read off x = 3+4t, y = 5−2t, z = 4+7t.
  3. Substitute into the plane equation 2x + 4y − z = 1.
  4. Expand and collect: combine constant terms and t-coefficients to get a linear equation At + B = d.
  5. Solve for t, then back-substitute into (x(t), y(t), z(t)) to obtain the intersection point.
  6. Optionally verify by checking the point satisfies the original plane equation.

Worked Micro-Example

Line: $(x-3)/4=(y-5)/(-2)=(z-4)/7=t$ gives $x=3+4t,\ y=5-2t,\ z=4+7t$. Substitute into $2x+4y-z=1$: $2(3+4t)+4(5-2t)-(4+7t)=22-7t=1$, so $t=3$. Hence the intersection is $(15,-1,25)$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Line given in vector form r = a + td
Skip the parametrisation step — read x, y, z directly off the components of r and substitute.
Foot of perpendicular from a point to a plane
First build the perpendicular line through the point with direction equal to the plane's normal, then apply this same parametric substitution.
Foot of perpendicular from a point to a line
Parametrise the line, form the vector from the point to the general point, set its dot product with the direction to zero — same one-variable reduction.
Distance from a point to a plane via a line
Parametrise the perpendicular line, find t at the intersection, then compute |t|·|d| as the distance.
Two planes intersecting in a line, then meeting a third surface
Parametrise the line of intersection first, then substitute into the third equation.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the symmetric form as three separate equations in x, y, z to be solved simultaneously with the plane, instead of recognising one degree of freedom.
  • Reading the denominators as coordinates of a point rather than direction components.
  • Forgetting that a negative denominator (e.g. −2) belongs to the direction vector, and writing y = 5+2t.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error on the y-component when the symmetric denominator is negative.
  • Arithmetic slip when collecting t-coefficients across three substituted expressions.
  • Failing to check the case where the t-coefficient vanishes — this signals the line is parallel to the plane (no intersection, or line lies in the plane).
  • Back-substituting t into only two coordinates and forgetting the third.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL Further Pure scope: symmetric and parametric forms of a line in 3D, Cartesian equation of a plane, and intersection of a line with a plane are all standard FP3 content.
