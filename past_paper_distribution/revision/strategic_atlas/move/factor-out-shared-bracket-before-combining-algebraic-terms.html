Algebra & Functions

Factor out shared bracket before combining algebraic terms

Family: Strategic Factorisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Two (or more) terms being added or subtracted share a visible common bracketed factor, and the target form also contains that factor. Typical in induction step algebra (e.g. \sum_{r=1}^{k}r(r+1) + (k+1)(k+2)-style sums), partial fraction recombination, or simplification before differentiation. Cue phrases: 'show that... = (k+1)(k+2)(k+3)/3', or any target whose factored shape mirrors part of the input. Fires whenever expanding would obliterate a structure you will need to rebuild.

Motivation — Why this works

Pulling out the common bracket replaces a near-cubic (or higher) expansion with a trivial linear combination inside one set of brackets. The underlying principle is the distributive law a\cdot c + b\cdot c = (a+b)c applied with c a polynomial factor rather than a number. Because the target form already exhibits c, preserving it means the remaining work is just simplifying a+b — usually a one-line fraction sum. This avoids expansion-then-refactor cycles that are slow and error-prone under exam conditions.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice both terms share (k+1)(k+2); the target also displays (k+1)(k+2).
  2. I rule out full expansion: turning this into a cubic and re-factoring is wasteful and risky.
  3. I commit to factoring (k+1)(k+2) out because it aligns my expression with the target's shape.
  4. Bridge: write the sum as (k+1)(k+2)\big[\,\tfrac{k}{3}+1\,\big] and focus only on the bracket.
  5. Inside the bracket I put terms over a common denominator so the leftover factor becomes a single linear bracket like (k+3)/3.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the largest common bracketed factor across all terms.
  2. Write the expression as (common factor) \times [residual sum].
  3. Combine the residual sum over a common denominator if fractions are present.
  4. Simplify the residual to a single linear (or simplest) bracket.
  5. Reassemble: (common factor) \times (simplified residual), and check it matches the target form.

Worked Micro-Example

Show \tfrac{k(k+1)(k+2)}{3} + (k+1)(k+2) = \tfrac{(k+1)(k+2)(k+3)}{3}. Factor: (k+1)(k+2)\!\left[\tfrac{k}{3}+1\right]. Combine: \tfrac{k}{3}+1=\tfrac{k+3}{3}. Hence the sum equals \tfrac{(k+1)(k+2)(k+3)}{3}, as required.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Induction step for \sum r(r+1)(r+2)/... type sums
Factor out the longest bracket chain shared by the assumed sum and the new term, leaving a single linear residual.
Partial fractions recombination check
Factor the common denominator product and combine numerators only, rather than expanding both fractions.
Differentiation simplification (product/quotient rule output)
Factor the lowest power of each repeated bracket out of the derivative before tidying, e.g. (x+1)^2 from (x+1)^3 and (x+1)^2 terms.
Series with factorials in Further Pure
Factor out the smaller factorial (e.g. k!) and combine the residual integers, rather than expanding (k+1)! fully.
Matrix induction (FP1)
Factor a common matrix on one side rather than multiplying entry-by-entry, leaving a scalar/linear matrix residual.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the two terms as unrelated and expanding both into a cubic before attempting to factorise.
  • Spotting only (k+1) as common and missing that (k+2) is shared too, leading to a messier residual.
  • Believing the 1/3 prevents factoring, and so clearing the fraction first by multiplying through — which complicates rather than simplifies.

Pitfalls

  • Dropping the 1/3: writing (k+1)(k+2)(k+1) instead of (k+1)(k+2)\!\left[\tfrac{k}{3}+1\right].
  • Failing to put k/3 + 1 over a common denominator and leaving the answer un-simplified.
  • Sign errors when the second term is subtracted; the residual becomes k/3 - 1, not k/3 + 1.
  • Mis-identifying the common factor when one term is written in expanded form — always rewrite both in factored form first.

A-level Scope Notes

Standard technique throughout Edexcel IAL Pure (P3/P4 algebra, partial fractions) and Further Pure induction proofs (FP1); uses only the distributive law and fraction addition.
