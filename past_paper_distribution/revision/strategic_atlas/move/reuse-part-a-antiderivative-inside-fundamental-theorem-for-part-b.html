Integration

Reuse part (a) antiderivative inside Fundamental Theorem for part (b)

Family: Structured Exam Linkage Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Part (b) of a structured question asks for a definite integral whose integrand is identical (or trivially related) to one whose antiderivative was just established in part (a). Common phrasings include 'Hence find...', 'Hence evaluate...', or simply quoting the same integrand with explicit limits. The limits are usually 'special' values such as 0, 1, e, π/2, π that simplify exponentials, logs, or trig terms to integers. The word 'Hence' is the strongest cue — it signals dependence on the previous result.

Motivation — Why this works

By the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, once F(x) is known with F'(x) = f(x), the definite integral over [a,b] is just F(b) − F(a). Re-deriving F via integration by parts or substitution would duplicate work and introduce new error risk. Examiners deliberately choose limits like 1 and e so that ln(1) = 0 and ln(e) = 1 annihilate or collapse logarithmic terms, yielding a clean closed form (typically rational in e or π). Recognising this scaffolding is rewarded by both speed and exactness.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices that the integrand in (b) is identical to the one integrated in (a), and the word 'Hence' or context links the parts.
  2. Rules out starting integration by parts / substitution from scratch — the antiderivative is already in hand.
  3. Commits to the Fundamental Theorem route because the limits look 'designed' (e.g. 1 and e, or 0 and π) to simplify ln or trig values cleanly.
  4. Bridges by writing the antiderivative in square-bracket notation with the given limits.
  5. Tactically evaluates each limit separately, exploiting ln(1)=0, ln(e)=1, sin(0)=0, etc., before subtracting carefully to avoid sign slips.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Quote the antiderivative F(x) from part (a) and write [F(x)]_a^b.
  2. Substitute the upper limit b, simplifying special values (e.g. ln e = 1, e^0 = 1).
  3. Substitute the lower limit a, again simplifying (e.g. ln 1 = 0).
  4. Compute F(b) − F(a), being careful with the sign of a negative lower-limit contribution.
  5. Present the answer in the exact form requested (single fraction, factor of 1/9, etc.).

Worked Micro-Example

From part (a): $\int x^2 \ln x\,dx = \tfrac{x^3}{3}\ln x - \tfrac{x^3}{9} + C$. Then $\int_1^e x^2 \ln x\,dx = \left[\tfrac{x^3}{3}\ln x - \tfrac{x^3}{9}\right]_1^e = \left(\tfrac{e^3}{3} - \tfrac{e^3}{9}\right) - \left(0 - \tfrac{1}{9}\right) = \tfrac{2e^3}{9} + \tfrac{1}{9} = \tfrac{2e^3+1}{9}.$

Variants — Same move, different surface

Part (a) gives $\int x e^x\,dx = (x-1)e^x + C$, part (b) asks $\int_0^1 x e^x\,dx$.
Use limits 0 and 1: upper gives 0·e¹ = 0, lower gives (−1)·1 = −1, so answer is 0 − (−1) = 1.
Part (a) yields antiderivative for $\sin x \cos x$ or $\cos^2 x$; part (b) integrates from 0 to π/2.
Exploit sin 0 = 0, cos(π/2) = 0 to collapse boundary terms.
Part (a) finds $\int \tfrac{1}{x(x+1)}\,dx$ via partial fractions; part (b) evaluates from 1 to 2.
Substitute log limits and combine using $\ln A - \ln B = \ln(A/B)$ for an exact log answer.
'Hence, or otherwise' phrasing with reversed limits or a constant multiple of the integrand.
Either swap limits and negate, or factor the constant outside before applying [F(x)].

Common Misreadings

  • Treating part (b) as independent and redoing integration by parts from scratch.
  • Assuming the +C from part (a) matters in the definite integral (it cancels).
  • Misreading 'Hence' as 'Hence or otherwise' and abandoning the scaffolded antiderivative.

Pitfalls

  • Sign error when subtracting a negative lower-limit value: $\tfrac{2e^3}{9} - (-\tfrac{1}{9})$ must give $+\tfrac{1}{9}$.
  • Writing $\tfrac{1}{3}\ln 1 = \tfrac{1}{3}$ instead of 0.
  • Leaving the answer as a decimal approximation when an exact form is required.
  • Forgetting to multiply through by a constant factor that was outside the original integral.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P3/P4 calculus: Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, integration by parts, and standard log/exponential/trig values. No improper integrals or techniques beyond the syllabus are required.
