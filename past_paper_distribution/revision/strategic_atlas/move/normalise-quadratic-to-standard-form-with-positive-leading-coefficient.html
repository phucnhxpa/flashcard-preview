Normalise quadratic to standard form with positive leading coefficient

Family: Standard Form Normalisation Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires whenever an equation, after expansion or rearrangement, takes a quadratic shape but the x² coefficient is negative or the equation is not yet set equal to zero (e.g. 175x − x² = 7350, or 12 − x − x² = 0). It also fires before applying the quadratic formula, completing the square, or searching for factor pairs, since each of these methods is cleaner with a > 0. Cue phrases: 'leading term is −x²', 'RHS is non-zero', 'need ax² + bx + c = 0 form'.

Motivation — Why this works

Multiplying an equation through by −1 (or moving every term to the other side) preserves the solution set because 0 = 0 under negation. A positive leading coefficient stabilises sign tracking when reading off a, b, c for the discriminant b² − 4ac, and it makes factor pair searches more intuitive (both factors of c can be considered with their natural signs determined by b). Standard form is also the contract assumed by the quadratic formula and by completing the square after dividing by a.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation is quadratic but either RHS ≠ 0 or the x² coefficient is negative — not yet in usable form.
  2. Rule out attempting to factorise or apply the formula directly: sign errors are likely with −x² leading, and the formula needs ax² + bx + c = 0.
  3. Commit to a one-line normalisation: bring everything to one side and ensure the x² coefficient is positive.
  4. Decide direction: move terms so that x² lands on the side where it becomes +x², which usually means subtracting the linear/constant terms from the side that originally had −x².
  5. Double-check that every term flipped sign consistently, then read off a, b, c for the next step.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the side containing −x² and the side containing the constant/linear remainder.
  2. Add x² to both sides (or multiply through by −1) so the x² term becomes positive.
  3. Subtract the remaining terms to collect everything on one side, leaving 0 on the other.
  4. Write the result as x² + bx + c = 0 (or ax² + bx + c = 0) and verify each sign by back-substitution of one term.
  5. Proceed to factorisation, quadratic formula, or completing the square.

Worked Micro-Example

Start: $175x - x^2 = 7350$. \ Add $x^2$ and subtract $175x$ from both sides: $0 = x^2 - 175x + 7350$. \ Standard form: $x^2 - 175x + 7350 = 0$. \ Now seek factor pair of $7350$ summing to $175$: $105 \times 70 = 7350$ and $105 + 70 = 175$, giving $(x-105)(x-70) = 0$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Equation given as $c - bx - x^2 = 0$ (e.g. from a projectile height set to zero)
Multiply through by $-1$ to obtain $x^2 + bx - c = 0$ before factorising or applying the formula.
Quadratic inequality $-x^2 + bx + c > 0$
Multiplying by $-1$ flips the inequality sign to $x^2 - bx - c < 0$; required before sketching the standard upward parabola.
Completing the square on $ax^2 + bx + c$ with $a \neq 1$
First divide (or factor out $a$) so the leading coefficient is $+1$ inside the bracket before halving $b$.
Disguised quadratic such as $e^{2x} = 5e^x - 6$ after substitution $u = e^x$
Rearrange to $u^2 - 5u + 6 = 0$ before factorising; same normalisation principle on the substituted variable.
Quadratic in trig, e.g. $3 - 2\cos\theta = \cos^2\theta$
Move all terms to the side that makes $\cos^2\theta$ positive: $\cos^2\theta + 2\cos\theta - 3 = 0$.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating $175x - x^2 = 7350$ as linear because the $x$ term is written first.
  • Believing the equation must be solved in the form it is presented, rather than rearranged.
  • Reading off $a = -1, b = 175, c = -7350$ and then losing track of signs in the discriminant.

Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to negate every term when multiplying by $-1$, e.g. writing $x^2 - 175x - 7350 = 0$ instead of $x^2 - 175x + 7350 = 0$.
  • Flipping the inequality direction incorrectly (or not at all) when the same move is applied to an inequality.
  • Dividing through by a variable expression instead of rearranging, which can lose the root $x = 0$.
  • Leaving the equation as $-x^2 + 175x - 7350 = 0$ and then misapplying the quadratic formula by forgetting $a = -1$.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL P1 quadratic techniques (factorisation, completing the square, quadratic formula, discriminant) and reused throughout P2–P4 for disguised quadratics in exponentials, logarithms, and trigonometry.
