Strategic Atlas / Proof / Perfect Square Anchoring
Proof

Reverse-engineer a perfect square from the target inequality

Family: Perfect Square Anchoring Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires on 'prove that' inequalities of the form f(k) ≥ c (or f(k) ≤ c) where f(k) mixes a variable with its reciprocal, e.g. k + a/k ≥ 2√a for k > 0, or symmetric forms like a² + b² ≥ 2ab. Cues include a positivity assumption on the variable, a constant on the right that equals 2√(coefficient), and the instruction to argue 'from first principles' rather than quote AM-GM. Also fires when rearranging the target to one side gives a quadratic-looking expression after clearing a denominator.

Motivation — Why this works

Any real expression squared is ≥ 0, giving a logically unassailable starting point. Working backwards from the target — clearing denominators and collecting terms — typically yields a quadratic that factors as a perfect square, revealing exactly which square to anchor the forward proof on. Reversing the scratch-work then produces a valid deductive chain: (expr)² ≥ 0 ⇒ … ⇒ target. This converts a 'prove' task into a sequence of reversible algebraic steps from a self-evident truth.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notices the target has the shape variable + constant/variable ≥ number, with the right-hand side equal to 2√(constant) — a tell-tale AM-GM signature.
  2. Rules out 'starting from the conclusion and working forwards' (circular) and rules out citing AM-GM as a black-box theorem since the question demands a proof.
  3. Commits to the perfect-square-anchoring template: every such inequality descends from (something)² ≥ 0.
  4. Bridges by doing scratch-work backwards: subtract the RHS, multiply by k > 0, and inspect the resulting quadratic for a perfect-square factorisation.
  5. Decides the inner term of the square by taking √(constant numerator); writes the forward proof starting from (k − √a)² ≥ 0 and expanding toward the target.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the target: f(k) ≥ c with k > 0.
  2. Scratch-work: rearrange to f(k) − c ≥ 0 and clear the denominator by multiplying by k (valid since k > 0).
  3. Factorise the resulting quadratic as a perfect square (k − m)², where m = √(constant).
  4. Write the forward proof: state (k − m)² ≥ 0 as the axiomatic anchor.
  5. Expand: k² − 2mk + m² ≥ 0.
  6. Divide through by k (positive, inequality preserved) to recover k + m²/k ≥ 2m, i.e. the target.
  7. State equality condition k = m for completeness if asked.

Worked Micro-Example

Prove that for k > 0, k + 9/k ≥ 6.\n Since (k − 3)² ≥ 0 for all real k, expanding gives k² − 6k + 9 ≥ 0.\n As k > 0, divide by k to obtain k − 6 + 9/k ≥ 0, i.e. k + 9/k ≥ 6, with equality iff k = 3. ∎

Variants — Same move, different surface

Prove a + b ≥ 2√(ab) for a, b > 0 (AM-GM for two terms)
Anchor on (√a − √b)² ≥ 0, expand to a − 2√(ab) + b ≥ 0.
Prove a² + b² ≥ 2ab for real a, b
Anchor on (a − b)² ≥ 0; no positivity needed since no division occurs.
Prove k + 16/k ≥ 8 for k > 0
Anchor on (k − 4)² ≥ 0, since 4 = √16.
Prove x/y + y/x ≥ 2 for x, y > 0
Multiply target by xy; anchor on (x − y)² ≥ 0.
Prove (a + b)² ≥ 4ab
Anchor on (a − b)² ≥ 0 and add 4ab to both sides.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating the proof as 'start from the conclusion and rearrange', which is circular unless every step is explicitly bidirectional.
  • Quoting AM-GM as a named theorem on a 'prove from first principles' question and skipping the algebraic derivation.
  • Misreading the constant: thinking the inner term of the square is the numerator itself (e.g. 9) rather than its square root (3).

Pitfalls

  • Picking the wrong perfect square, e.g. (k − 9)² instead of (k − 3)², by confusing the numerator with its square root.
  • Multiplying or dividing by k without stating k > 0, which is required to preserve the inequality direction.
  • Presenting backwards scratch-work as the final proof; examiners require the deduction to flow from (…)² ≥ 0 to the target.
  • Forgetting to mention the equality case when the question asks 'state when equality holds'.

A-level Scope Notes

Fully within Edexcel IAL P1–P4 proof and algebraic manipulation; uses only the axiom that a real square is non-negative and standard quadratic factorisation, with no appeal to named inequalities beyond what can be derived on the page.
