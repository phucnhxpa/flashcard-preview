Algebra & Functions

Take ± square root then reject branches violating real-domain constraints

Family: Modulus Case Analysis Frequency: 1 questions

Trigger — When to use

Fires when an equation has the form (expression)² = k with k > 0, or |expression|² = k, and you need to extract the inner expression by square-rooting both sides. Also fires after squaring a modulus equation, or when |z|² = x² + y² = k arises in complex numbers. The cue is a perfect square equalling a constant: square-rooting introduces a ± branch, and at least one branch is typically incompatible with the real-number domain (e.g. a sum of squares cannot be negative).

Motivation — Why this works

Square-rooting both sides of A² = k yields A = ±√k, and both branches must initially be retained to avoid losing solutions. However, when A itself is constrained (e.g. A = p²+1 ≥ 1, or A = x²+y² ≥ 0), the negative branch produces an impossible equation and is rejected on domain grounds. This 'generate all, then filter' discipline is the standard A-level technique for ensuring the solution set is both complete and valid, and it avoids the twin errors of losing solutions (only taking +√) or admitting non-real ones.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Notice the equation has shape (something)² = positive constant, so square-rooting is the natural unwinding step.
  2. Rule out simply writing the positive root only: that would silently discard a potential branch and lose marks for incomplete reasoning.
  3. Commit to writing both ± branches explicitly, with the plan to filter afterwards using the real-domain constraint on the inner expression.
  4. Bridge: identify what the inner expression is bounded by — here p²+1 ≥ 1, so only the +10 branch can possibly hold.
  5. Tactically, show the rejected branch's contradiction (p² = −11) explicitly rather than skipping it, then solve the surviving branch for all values of p (remembering p² = 9 gives p = ±3, not just p = 3).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the equation as (A)² = k with k ≥ 0.
  2. Square-root both sides: A = ±√k.
  3. Write both branches as separate cases.
  4. For each branch, apply the real-domain constraint on A (e.g. A = p²+1 ≥ 1, or A = x²+y² ≥ 0) and reject any branch that violates it.
  5. Solve the surviving branch(es), taking ± where a further square root appears (e.g. p² = 9 ⇒ p = ±3).
  6. State the full solution set.

Worked Micro-Example

Solve $(p^2+1)^2 = 100$ for real $p$.\\ Square-rooting: $p^2+1 = \pm 10$.\\ Branch $p^2+1 = -10$ gives $p^2 = -11$, impossible for real $p$ — reject.\\ Branch $p^2+1 = 10$ gives $p^2 = 9$, so $p = \pm 3$.\\ Hence $p = 3$ or $p = -3$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Complex modulus equation $|z|^2 = k$ leading to $x^2 + y^2 = k$
If $k < 0$ there are no real $(x,y)$; if $k \geq 0$ proceed to parametrise the circle of radius $\sqrt{k}$.
Equation of form $(x-a)^2 = b$ in coordinate geometry (e.g. circle/locus)
Take $x - a = \pm\sqrt{b}$; reject the branch only if an additional inequality (e.g. $x \geq 0$) is imposed.
After squaring a modulus equation $|f(x)| = g(x)$
Both branches $f(x) = \pm g(x)$ must be considered, then candidate solutions filtered by checking $g(x) \geq 0$ and substituting back.
Quadratic in $u = p^2$ such as $u^2 + u - 90 = 0$
Solve for $u$, then reject any negative root before taking $p = \pm\sqrt{u}$.

Common Misreadings

  • Taking only the positive square root and never mentioning the negative branch — the rejection must be shown, not skipped.
  • Stopping at $p^2 = 9$ and writing only $p = 3$, forgetting the second ± from the inner square root.
  • Treating $(p^2+1)^2 = 100$ as $p^2+1 = 10$ by 'cancelling' the square, losing the case-analysis structure entirely.

Pitfalls

  • Accepting the rejected branch and chasing complex values of $p$ when the question specifies $p \in \mathbb{R}$.
  • Writing $p^2 = \pm 9$ — confusing the ± from the outer square root with a non-existent ± on $p^2$ itself.
  • Failing to justify the rejection in writing (just crossing it out), which can lose method marks.
  • Expanding $(p^2+1)^2 = 100$ into a quartic and losing the clean structure, rather than square-rooting directly.

A-level Scope Notes

Entirely within Edexcel IAL Pure Maths (P1–P4) for real-variable work and Further Pure for the complex-modulus variant; no analysis beyond elementary square-rooting and domain checking is required.
