Sequences & Series

Select the expansion point (Taylor vs Maclaurin) to match the question

Family: Expansion Point Selection Frequency: 3 questions

Trigger — When to use

The question explicitly names a centre — e.g. 'Taylor series about x = a', 'expansion in ascending powers of (x − 1)', or 'find an approximation valid near x = π/4'. Alternatively, the function is undefined or badly behaved at 0 (e.g. ln x, √x, 1/x), forcing a non-zero centre. A 'hence' part asking for an approximation near a specific non-zero value is also a tell. If none of these cues appear and the question says 'Maclaurin' or 'in ascending powers of x', the centre is a = 0.

Motivation — Why this works

A Taylor series f(x) = Σ f⁽ⁿ⁾(a)(x−a)ⁿ/n! captures the local behaviour of f near x = a, so the choice of a controls both the region where the polynomial is a good approximation and the difficulty of computing the derivative values. Centring at a = 0 (Maclaurin) is simplest algebraically — plain powers of x — but only works when f and its derivatives are defined and clean at 0. For functions like ln x at 0, or trig functions whose values are nicest at multiples of π/4 or π/3, a non-zero centre is either forced or strategically preferable. The Maclaurin series is simply the Taylor series with a = 0; the underlying identity is the same.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. Read the question for the centre cue: words like 'Maclaurin', 'about x = a', 'in ascending powers of (x − a)', or 'near x = a'.
  2. Check whether f(0), f'(0), f''(0), … are defined and produce clean numbers — if the function is singular at 0 (ln x, 1/x) Maclaurin is impossible.
  3. Rule out alternative techniques (binomial expansion, known standard series like e^x, sin x, cos x in the formula booklet) — these only apply directly if a = 0 or after a substitution.
  4. Commit to Taylor about a when the question fixes a ≠ 0 or when derivatives are markedly cleaner there (e.g. sin(π/4) = √2/2 gives tidy surds).
  5. Decide tactically whether to write the series directly in (x − a)ⁿ or to substitute h = x − a (or u = x − a) first and run a Maclaurin-style expansion in h — algebra is identical, but the substitution can reduce slips.
  6. Set up a derivative table evaluated at x = a to the required order, keeping exact values (surds, fractions, π-multiples).

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the expansion point a from the question wording.
  2. Write the template f(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x−a) + f''(a)(x−a)²/2! + f'''(a)(x−a)³/3! + ⋯ to the required order.
  3. Differentiate f(x) successively and evaluate each derivative at x = a, tabulating f⁽ⁿ⁾(a).
  4. Substitute the values into the template; keep coefficients exact (surds/fractions).
  5. Optionally substitute h = x − a to present the series in powers of h, if the question prefers that form.

Worked Micro-Example

Expand $f(x)=\sin x$ about $x=\pi/4$ to order 2. Compute $f(\pi/4)=\tfrac{\sqrt2}{2}$, $f'(\pi/4)=\cos(\pi/4)=\tfrac{\sqrt2}{2}$, $f''(\pi/4)=-\sin(\pi/4)=-\tfrac{\sqrt2}{2}$. Then $\sin x \approx \tfrac{\sqrt2}{2}\left[1+(x-\tfrac{\pi}{4})-\tfrac{1}{2}(x-\tfrac{\pi}{4})^2\right]$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Question gives the form f(a+h) = f(a) + f'(a)h + f''(a)h²/2! + ⋯
Same coefficients; just write the series in powers of h instead of (x − a).
Function suggests a shift to a known Maclaurin series, e.g. ln(2 + x) = ln 2 + ln(1 + x/2)
Rewrite as a standard form in u = x/2 and use the booklet Maclaurin series for ln(1+u), avoiding direct Taylor differentiation.
Function is singular at 0 (e.g. ln x, 1/x)
Forced Taylor expansion about a convenient a > 0 (typically a = 1), where f and its derivatives are clean.

Common Misreadings

  • Treating 'in ascending powers of (x − 1)' as a Maclaurin cue and expanding about 0.
  • Seeing a known Maclaurin series in the formula booklet and using it unchanged when the centre is not 0.
  • Assuming 'Taylor' always means a ≠ 0 — Maclaurin is just the special case a = 0.

Pitfalls

  • Writing plain xⁿ instead of (x − a)ⁿ in the series — a notational slip that loses all marks.
  • Evaluating derivatives at 0 out of habit when the centre is a ≠ 0.
  • Errors in exact trig values at the centre (e.g. cos(π/3) = 1/2, sec(π/3) = 2 — not 1).
  • Forgetting that the constant term is f(a)·(x−a)⁰ = f(a), not f(a)(x−a).

A-level Scope Notes

Stays within Edexcel IAL Further Pure (FP2/FP3 depending on spec) Taylor and Maclaurin series. The Maclaurin expansions of e^x, sin x, cos x, ln(1+x), (1+x)ⁿ are in the formula booklet; Taylor expansions about a ≠ 0 must be derived from the general formula. Do not invoke convergence/radius-of-convergence theory beyond the syllabus.
