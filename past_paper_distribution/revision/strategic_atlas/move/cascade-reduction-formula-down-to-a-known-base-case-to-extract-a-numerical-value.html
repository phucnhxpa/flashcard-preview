Integration

Cascade reduction formula down to a known base case to extract a numerical value

Family: Recursive Descent Frequency: 4 questions

Trigger — When to use

Part (a) of the question has established a reduction formula linking $I_n$ to $I_{n-1}$ or $I_{n-2}$, and part (b) now asks for a specific numerical value such as $I_3$, $I_4$, or $I_6$. The structural cue is the explicit appearance of an indexed integral $I_n$ defined by an integral involving $\sin^n x$, $\cos^n x$, $x^n e^{-x}$, $e^x \sin^n x$, or similar. Equivalent phrasings: 'Hence find $I_4$', 'Use the result in part (a) to evaluate...', 'Deduce the exact value of $I_n$'. The word 'hence' is a strong signal that the recursion must be deployed rather than re-deriving from scratch.

Motivation — Why this works

A reduction formula is not itself a closed-form answer — it is a recurrence relation. Each application replaces $I_n$ with a simpler $I_{n-1}$ or $I_{n-2}$ plus elementary terms, stepping the index down until reaching a base case ($I_0$ or $I_1$) that is directly integrable. This converts an otherwise intractable high-order integral into a chain of arithmetic substitutions. The technique exploits the fact that the same integration-by-parts work has already been encoded in the formula, so re-doing IBP for each $n$ is wasteful.

Thought Process — The step-by-step reasoning

  1. I notice that part (b) asks for a specific $I_n$ and part (a) gave me a reduction formula — the word 'hence' confirms I should use it rather than restart from the integral definition.
  2. I rule out attacking $I_n$ directly via repeated integration by parts: that would re-derive the reduction formula, wasting time and risking sign errors.
  3. I commit to recursive descent: starting at the target index, apply the formula repeatedly to step down until I hit a base case I can evaluate by elementary antidifferentiation.
  4. I check the parity/step of the recurrence: if it links $I_n$ to $I_{n-2}$ (step-2), I terminate at $I_0$ for even $n$ or $I_1$ for odd $n$; if it links $I_n$ to $I_{n-1}$ (step-1), I terminate at $I_0$.
  5. I compute the base case directly: e.g. $I_0 = \int_0^{\pi/2} 1\,dx = \pi/2$, or $I_0 = \int_0^1 e^{-x}\,dx = 1 - e^{-1}$, or $I_0 = \int e^x\,dx$ between bounds.
  6. I then work upward, substituting each computed value into the next, keeping intermediate results in exact form ($a + b\pi$, $a - be^{-1}$, fractions over a common denominator) to avoid premature decimals and to match mark-scheme presentation.
  7. I cancel common factors before multiplying chains of fractions, and I track the constant term (e.g. $-e^{-1}$) carefully — it must reappear at every level of the recursion.

Mechanic — Concrete operations

  1. Identify the target index $n$ and the step of the recursion (1 or 2).
  2. Identify the correct base case: $I_0$ if even chain (or step-1), $I_1$ if odd chain.
  3. Compute the base case by direct antidifferentiation, e.g. $I_0 = [-\cos x]_0^{\pi/2} = 1$ or $I_0 = [-e^{-x}]_0^1 = 1 - e^{-1}$.
  4. Apply the reduction formula at the lowest non-base index, substituting the base value.
  5. Repeat upward: $I_2$ from $I_0$, then $I_4$ from $I_2$, etc., until reaching the target $I_n$.
  6. Simplify, collecting powers of $\pi$ or $e$ over a common denominator, presenting the answer in exact form.

Worked Micro-Example

Given $I_n = nI_{n-1} - e^{-1}$ and $I_0 = 1 - e^{-1}$, find $I_3$.\n$I_1 = 1\cdot(1-e^{-1}) - e^{-1} = 1 - 2e^{-1}$.\n$I_2 = 2(1-2e^{-1}) - e^{-1} = 2 - 5e^{-1}$.\n$I_3 = 3(2-5e^{-1}) - e^{-1} = 6 - 16e^{-1}$.

Variants — Same move, different surface

Step-2 reduction formula (Wallis-type, e.g. $\int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^n x\,dx$)
The recursion skips indices: $I_6 \to I_4 \to I_2 \to I_0 = \pi/2$. Multiply the chain of fractions $\frac{n-1}{n}$, cancelling before multiplying.
Odd target index with step-2 recurrence
Terminate at $I_1$ instead of $I_0$. The base $I_1$ may itself require a single IBP (e.g. $I_1 = \int_0^{\pi/2} x\sin x\,dx = 1$).
Reduction formula with both a boundary closed-form part and a recursive part (e.g. $\int_0^{\pi/2} e^x \sin^n x\,dx$)
At each application, evaluate the boundary expression at the limits AND substitute the lower $I_{n-2}$ — students often forget the boundary part.
Question asks for a ratio or product formula rather than an explicit value
Apply the recurrence symbolically without evaluating the base case; the answer is left as a product of fractions.

Common Misreadings

  • Substituting the formula in the wrong direction — trying to express $I_{n-1}$ in terms of $I_n$ rather than descending.
  • Treating the reduction formula as a closed-form solution and reporting it directly instead of recursing to a base case.
  • Re-deriving the reduction formula from scratch by IBP for each $n$, ignoring the 'hence'.

Pitfalls

  • Arithmetic errors in tracking coefficients (especially the constant term like $-e^{-1}$) through multiple substitutions — write each step explicitly.
  • Forgetting to apply the additive constant term at every level of the recursion (treating $-e^{-1}$ as appearing only once).
  • Confusing even/odd base cases: $I_0 = \pi/2$ for even chains vs $I_1 = 1$ for odd chains in Wallis-type integrals.
  • Forgetting the boundary-evaluation part of formulas that combine boundary and recursive terms — substituting only the $I_{n-2}$ piece.
  • At $x = 0$, mishandling $\sin^{n-1}(0)$ — it is $0$ for $n \geq 2$, killing many boundary terms.

A-level Scope Notes
