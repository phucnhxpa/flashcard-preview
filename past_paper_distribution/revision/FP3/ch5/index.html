Triggersangle between the line and the planeacute angleangle that the line makes with
Assumptions
  • The angle between a line and a plane is the COMPLEMENT of the angle between the line direction and the plane normal
  • The answer should be the acute angle (0° ≤ θ ≤ 90°)
  • Take the absolute value of the dot product to ensure a positive (acute) result
Approaches
A: sin θ formula (direct)
When: Always — this is the standard and expected method for line-plane angle
  1. Identify the direction vector d of the line and the normal vector n of the plane
  2. Compute d · n (scalar/dot product)
  3. Compute |d| and |n|
  4. Use sin θ = |d · n| / (|d| × |n|)
  5. Find θ = arcsin(result) and give the answer in degrees (or radians if specified)
+ Pros: Direct formula; one-step calculation after dot product
– Cons: Students must remember to use SIN not COS — the most common error in this chapter
B: Find angle with normal first, then subtract from 90°
When: If you forget the sin formula or want to verify your answer
  1. Compute the angle α between d and n using cos α = |d · n| / (|d| × |n|)
  2. The angle between the line and the plane is θ = 90° − α
  3. This is equivalent to sin θ = |d · n| / (|d| × |n|)
+ Pros: Uses the familiar cos dot-product formula; acts as a check
– Cons: Extra step; risk of forgetting to subtract from 90°
Formulas
$\sin \theta = \frac{|\mathbf{d} \cdot \mathbf{n}|}{|\mathbf{d}||\mathbf{n}|}$ — angle between line (direction $\mathbf{d}$) and plane (normal $\mathbf{n}$) (MEMORIZE — NOT in formula booklet)
$\cos \alpha = \frac{|\mathbf{d} \cdot \mathbf{n}|}{|\mathbf{d}||\mathbf{n}|}$ gives the angle $\alpha$ between line and NORMAL; then $\theta = 90° - \alpha$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Using cos instead of sin — THE most repeated pitfall across all 11 papers; the angle between a LINE and a PLANE uses SIN, not COS
  • Forgetting to take the absolute value of d · n, giving a negative or obtuse angle
  • Confusing line-plane angle with plane-plane angle (which uses cos between normals)

Triggersperpendicular distanceshortest distancedistance from point to planedistance from A to the plane
Assumptions
  • The plane equation must be in the form ax + by + cz = d (or ax + by + cz + d = 0) before applying the formula
  • The distance is always positive (use absolute value)
Approaches
A: Point-to-plane distance formula
When: Whenever you need perpendicular distance from a specific point to a plane with known equation
  1. Write the plane in the form ax + by + cz = d (or equivalently ax + by + cz − d = 0)
  2. Let the point be P = (x₀, y₀, z₀)
  3. Compute distance = |ax₀ + by₀ + cz₀ − d| / √(a² + b² + c²)
  4. Simplify the surd if possible; rationalise if required
+ Pros: Quick one-line formula; very efficient
– Cons: Must have the plane in Cartesian form first; easy to get the sign of d wrong
B: Projection method using dot product
When: When you want to derive or verify; also useful when you have a point on the plane already
  1. Let Q be any known point on the plane and P be the external point
  2. Form vector QP
  3. The perpendicular distance = |QP · n̂| where n̂ is the unit normal to the plane
  4. This equals |QP · n| / |n|
+ Pros: Conceptually clear; doesn't require Cartesian form of plane
– Cons: Need a known point on the plane; slightly more steps
Formulas
$d = \frac{|ax_0 + by_0 + cz_0 - d|}{\sqrt{a^2 + b^2 + c^2}}$ — perpendicular distance from $(x_0, y_0, z_0)$ to plane $ax + by + cz = d$ (formula booklet)
$d = \frac{|\overrightarrow{QP} \cdot \mathbf{n}|}{|\mathbf{n}|}$ — projection method (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting the absolute value — the numerator must be positive
  • Inconsistent form: if the plane is written as ax + by + cz + d = 0, the formula numerator is |ax₀ + by₀ + cz₀ + d|, not minus d
  • Arithmetic errors in computing the denominator √(a² + b² + c²)

Triggersline of intersectionline common to both planesline where the planes meet
Assumptions
  • The planes are not parallel (their normals are not parallel), so they intersect in a line
  • The direction of the line of intersection is perpendicular to both normals
Approaches
A: Direction via cross product, point via simultaneous equations
When: Standard method — always
  1. Find the direction vector of the line: d = n₁ × n₂
  2. To find a specific point on the line, set one variable (e.g. z = 0) and solve the two plane equations simultaneously for the other two variables
  3. Write the line equation as r = a + λd where a is the found point
+ Pros: Systematic and reliable
– Cons: Setting one coordinate to 0 may not always work (check the determinant); may need to try a different coordinate
B: Eliminate one variable from the two plane equations
When: When cross product is not needed or when the algebra is simpler by elimination
  1. Write both plane equations in Cartesian form
  2. Eliminate one variable (e.g. z) by adding/subtracting multiples of the equations
  3. Express two variables in terms of the third, set the third as parameter λ
  4. Write parametric equations and hence the line equation
+ Pros: Avoids cross product; may be faster for simple coefficients
– Cons: Can be messy with awkward coefficients; direction vector less immediately obvious
Formulas
$\mathbf{d} = \mathbf{n_1} \times \mathbf{n_2}$ — direction of line of intersection (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Choosing to set a variable to 0 that makes the remaining system inconsistent (check first)
  • Errors in the cross product of the two normals

Triggersskew linesshortest distance betweenperpendicular distance between two linesclosest approach
Assumptions
  • Lines are skew (not parallel, not intersecting)
  • The shortest distance is along the common perpendicular direction d₁ × d₂
Approaches
A: Scalar triple product / projection formula
When: Standard method for skew lines
  1. Identify direction vectors d₁ and d₂ of the two lines, and points a₁ and a₂ on each line
  2. Compute n = d₁ × d₂ (the common perpendicular direction)
  3. Form the vector a₂ − a₁ (connecting a point on each line)
  4. Shortest distance = |(a₂ − a₁) · n| / |n|
+ Pros: One formula; very efficient
– Cons: Cross product must be correct; absolute value must not be forgotten
B: Plane method — construct a plane containing one line with normal d₁ × d₂, then use point-to-plane distance
When: When the question explicitly asks for a plane equation (as in Oct 2021)
  1. Compute n = d₁ × d₂
  2. Form the plane Π that contains line l₁ (or l₂) with normal n: use a point on l₁ and n to write the plane equation r · n = d
  3. Use the point-to-plane distance formula with a point on the other line l₂
  4. Distance = |n₁x₀ + n₂y₀ + n₃z₀ − d| / |n|
+ Pros: Shows full working; builds plane equation which may earn method marks
– Cons: More steps; more room for error
Formulas
$d = \frac{|(\mathbf{a_2} - \mathbf{a_1}) \cdot (\mathbf{d_1} \times \mathbf{d_2})|}{|\mathbf{d_1} \times \mathbf{d_2}|}$ — shortest distance between skew lines (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting the absolute value, obtaining a negative 'distance'
  • Using a point from the wrong line when constructing the plane
  • Sign errors in the cross product d₁ × d₂

Triggersarea of trianglevolume of tetrahedronscalar triple productcross product
Assumptions
  • Displacement vectors must be formed from the same vertex for the cross/triple product to work correctly
  • The area formula gives a positive scalar; the volume uses the scalar triple product which may be negative — take absolute value
Approaches
A: Cross product for area, scalar triple product for volume
When: Standard method — always for this type of question
  1. For area: choose a vertex, say A. Form vectors AB and AC
  2. Compute AB × AC
  3. Area of triangle = ½|AB × AC|
  4. For volume of tetrahedron ABCD: also form AD
  5. Volume = ⅙|AB · (AC × AD)| = ⅙|scalar triple product|
+ Pros: Clean, systematic, and exactly what the examiner expects
– Cons: Must compute a cross product and potentially a triple product — high chance of arithmetic errors
Formulas
$\text{Area of triangle} = \frac{1}{2}|\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}|$ (MEMORIZE)
$\text{Area of parallelogram} = |\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}|$ (MEMORIZE)
$\text{Volume of tetrahedron} = \frac{1}{6}|\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c})|$ (MEMORIZE)
$\text{Volume of parallelepiped} = |\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c})|$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting the factor of ½ for triangle area or ⅙ for tetrahedron volume
  • Forming displacement vectors from different vertices (must all originate from the same point)
  • Sign errors in the cross product determinant expansion, especially the j-component

Triggersequation of the planefind the plane containingnormal to the planeCartesian equationscalar product form
Assumptions
  • Two non-parallel direction vectors lying in the plane are needed to find the normal via cross product
  • Any known point on the plane can be used to find the constant d in r·n = d
  • The normal vector n is perpendicular to every vector lying in the plane
Approaches
A: Cross product of two in-plane vectors
When: When you have two direction vectors in the plane (e.g. direction vectors of two lines, or displacement vectors from three points)
  1. Identify two vectors that lie in the plane: e.g. direction vectors of two lines, or AB and AC from three points A, B, C
  2. Compute n = v₁ × v₂ using the 3×3 determinant: n = |i j k; a₁ a₂ a₃; b₁ b₂ b₃|
  3. Expand carefully: n = (a₂b₃ − a₃b₂)i − (a₁b₃ − a₃b₁)j + (a₁b₂ − a₂b₁)k
  4. Use a known point P on the plane to find d: d = n · OP where OP is the position vector of P
  5. Write plane equation as r · n = d, or in Cartesian form n₁x + n₂y + n₃z = d
+ Pros: Systematic, works for any configuration; gives Cartesian form directly
– Cons: Cross product computation is error-prone, especially the j-component sign
B: Parametric form r = a + sb + tc then convert
When: When the question asks for the parametric/vector form first, or when you have a point and two direction vectors readily available
  1. Write r = a + sb + tc where a is a position vector of a known point, b and c are direction vectors in the plane
  2. If Cartesian form is needed, compute n = b × c
  3. Find d = n · a
  4. Write Cartesian equation n₁x + n₂y + n₃z = d
+ Pros: Gives parametric form directly which some parts may require
– Cons: Still requires cross product to convert to Cartesian form
Formulas
$\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{v_1} \times \mathbf{v_2}$ — normal to the plane (MEMORIZE)
$\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = d$ where $d = \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{n}$ for a point $\mathbf{a}$ on the plane (MEMORIZE)
Cross product determinant: $\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \\ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \end{vmatrix}$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • Sign error on the j-component of the cross product: it is MINUS (a₁b₃ − a₃b₁), not plus
  • Using a point not on the plane to compute d
  • Swapping the order of vectors in the cross product (a × b = −b × a); this changes the sign of n but the plane equation is still valid if d is recalculated consistently

Triggersintersectionline meets the planepoint wheresubstitute
Assumptions
  • The line is not parallel to the plane (if it is, there is no unique intersection — check d·n ≠ 0)
  • The parametric form of the line uses a single parameter (usually λ or t)
Approaches
A: Substitute parametric line into Cartesian plane equation
When: Standard method — always works when line and plane equations are given
  1. Write the line as r = a + λd, giving x = a₁ + λd₁, y = a₂ + λd₂, z = a₃ + λd₃
  2. Substitute these expressions for x, y, z into the plane equation n₁x + n₂y + n₃z = k
  3. Solve the resulting linear equation in λ
  4. Substitute the value of λ back into the line equation to get the coordinates of the intersection point
+ Pros: Straightforward and quick; minimal room for error
– Cons: Requires the plane equation in Cartesian form first
Formulas
Line: $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + \lambda\mathbf{d}$ (MEMORIZE)
Plane: $n_1 x + n_2 y + n_3 z = k$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Arithmetic errors when expanding brackets after substitution — write each component separately
  • Forgetting to substitute the found λ value back into ALL three components of the line
  • Getting the parametric equations wrong by misreading the direction vector or starting point

Triggersangle between the planesangle between plane Π₁ and plane Π₂dihedral angle
Assumptions
  • The angle between two planes equals the angle between their normal vectors
  • Use the acute angle convention (take absolute value of dot product)
Approaches
A: Dot product of normal vectors
When: Standard method — always
  1. Identify normal vectors n₁ and n₂ of the two planes
  2. Compute n₁ · n₂
  3. Compute |n₁| and |n₂|
  4. Use cos θ = |n₁ · n₂| / (|n₁| × |n₂|)
  5. Find θ = arccos(result)
+ Pros: Simple direct formula using COS (unlike line-plane which uses SIN)
– Cons: None — this is the only standard method
Formulas
$\cos \theta = \frac{|\mathbf{n_1} \cdot \mathbf{n_2}|}{|\mathbf{n_1}||\mathbf{n_2}|}$ — angle between two planes (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Confusing this with the line-plane formula — plane-plane uses COS between normals, line-plane uses SIN
  • Forgetting to take absolute value, getting an obtuse angle instead of acute

Must memorize

  • $\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \\ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \end{vmatrix} = (a_2 b_3 - a_3 b_2)\mathbf{i} - (a_1 b_3 - a_3 b_1)\mathbf{j} + (a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1)\mathbf{k}$ (formula booklet — but must be fluent)
  • $\text{Plane equation: } \mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{n}$ where $\mathbf{a}$ is a point on the plane (MEMORIZE)
  • $\text{Point-to-plane distance: } d = \frac{|ax_0 + by_0 + cz_0 - d|}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}}$ (formula booklet)
  • $\text{Line-plane angle: } \sin\theta = \frac{|\mathbf{d} \cdot \mathbf{n}|}{|\mathbf{d}||\mathbf{n}|}$ (MEMORIZE — not in formula booklet)
  • $\text{Plane-plane angle: } \cos\theta = \frac{|\mathbf{n_1} \cdot \mathbf{n_2}|}{|\mathbf{n_1}||\mathbf{n_2}|}$ (MEMORIZE)
  • $\text{Skew lines distance: } d = \frac{|(\mathbf{a_2}-\mathbf{a_1}) \cdot (\mathbf{d_1} \times \mathbf{d_2})|}{|\mathbf{d_1} \times \mathbf{d_2}|}$ (MEMORIZE)
  • $\text{Area of triangle} = \frac{1}{2}|\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}|$; $\text{Volume of tetrahedron} = \frac{1}{6}|\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c})|$ (MEMORIZE)

Global strategy

  • Almost every question in this chapter requires a cross product — practise the 3×3 determinant expansion until it is automatic, especially the NEGATIVE sign on the j-component
  • Know the THREE angle formulas cold: line-plane uses SIN, plane-plane uses COS between normals, line-line uses COS between direction vectors
  • The standard question template is: find plane equation → find line-plane intersection → find angle or distance. Prepare a flowchart for this 3-part structure
  • Always clearly identify your direction vectors vs normal vectors — mislabelling these is the root of most errors
  • When computing distances, always take the ABSOLUTE VALUE — negative distances are meaningless and lose marks
  • Read the question carefully for the required form of the answer (Cartesian vs vector/parametric form of the plane or line)
  • Verify your plane equation by substituting a known point back in — a 10-second check that catches most errors
  • For 10-12 mark questions, expect 3-4 linked parts: equation of plane, intersection, angle, and distance. Budget time accordingly (~2-3 min per part)

Exam tips

  1. Write out the cross product determinant in full every time — do NOT try to do it in your head. This single habit prevents the most common errors in this chapter.
  2. CIRCLE the j-component minus sign in every cross product you write — examiners report this is the #1 source of lost marks.
  3. When you see 'angle between a line and a plane', immediately write sin θ = ... on your page BEFORE computing anything. This prevents the cos/sin confusion.
  4. After finding a plane equation, always verify it by substituting a known point — if the LHS doesn't equal the RHS, you have an error.
  5. For distance formulas, write |...| around the numerator from the very start — if you compute first and add absolute value later, you may forget or think a negative answer is correct.
  6. If a question asks for an exact answer (surd form), do NOT convert to decimals. Leave as e.g. 7√3/3.
  7. When the question is worth 10-13 marks, expect it to be a structured multi-part question — the output of each part feeds into the next. If you're stuck on part (a), try to use the given answer (if provided) to continue with later parts.
  8. Keep your vector notation consistent: underline vectors in handwriting, use column vectors for computation to reduce copying errors.
