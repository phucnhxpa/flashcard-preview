Reduction formula derivation and recursive evaluation
#1 most important
18×
7-12m

You are given an integral $I_n = \int f(x)^n \, dx$ (or a definite version) and asked to show that $I_n$ satisfies a recurrence relation such as $nI_n = (n-1)I_{n-2} + \text{boundary terms}$. Then use it to evaluate a specific case like $I_4$ or $I_6$.

Assumptions
  • The integral $I_n$ is defined for integer $n \geq 0$ (or $n \geq 1$)
  • Boundary terms from integration by parts must be evaluated at both limits — they often vanish
  • Base cases $I_0$ or $I_1$ must be computed directly by standard integration
  • The recurrence connects $I_n$ to $I_{n-k}$ where $k$ is typically 1, 2, 3, or 4
Approaches
A: Integration by parts with strategic splitting
When: Almost always the primary method; split $f(x)^n$ into $f(x)^{n-k} \cdot g(x)$ where $g(x)$ is directly integrable
  1. Identify how to split the integrand: e.g. $\sec^n x = \sec^{n-2} x \cdot \sec^2 x$, or $x^n \sqrt{a-x^2} = x^{n-1} \cdot x/\sqrt{a-x^2}$
  2. Set $u = $ the part containing the power (e.g. $\sec^{n-2}x$ or $x^{n-1}$) and $dv = $ the integrable remainder
  3. Differentiate $u$ and integrate $dv$ to find $v$
  4. Apply IBP: $I_n = [uv] - \int v\,du$
  5. Evaluate the boundary term $[uv]$ at both limits (often zero for definite integrals with limits like $0$ and $\pi/2$)
  6. In the remaining integral, use an identity (e.g. $\tan^2 x = \sec^2 x - 1$, $\cos^2 x = 1 - \sin^2 x$, $x^2 = -(a-x^2)+a$) to express the integral in terms of $I_n$ and $I_{n-k}$
  7. Collect the $I_n$ terms on one side and solve for $I_n$ in terms of $I_{n-k}$
  8. Apply the formula recursively: e.g. $I_6 \to I_4 \to I_2 \to I_0$
  9. Compute the base case $I_0$ or $I_1$ directly
+ Pros: Systematic and works for all reduction formula types in this syllabus
– Cons: Requires careful algebra — easy to drop signs or factors during the identity substitution step
B: Substitution before IBP (for integrands like $x^n \cos(x^2)$)
When: When the integrand contains a composite function like $\cos(x^2)$ or $(x^3+1)^{1/2}$ that suggests an inner substitution
  1. Identify the inner function (e.g. $t = x^2$ or $t = x^3 + 1$) and split off the appropriate power of $x$ for the substitution
  2. Transform the integral into a simpler form in terms of $t$
  3. Apply integration by parts in the new variable
  4. Convert back and express in terms of original $I_n$ and $I_{n-k}$
  5. Rearrange algebraically to isolate the reduction formula
+ Pros: Simplifies the integrand before IBP, reducing algebraic complexity
– Cons: The substitution step itself can introduce tricky factors (e.g. $2/9$ coefficient)
Formulas
$\text{IBP: } \int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$ (formula booklet)
$\sin^2 x = 1 - \cos^2 x$ (MEMORIZE)
$\tan^2 x = \sec^2 x - 1$ (MEMORIZE)
$\cosh^2 \theta - \sinh^2 \theta = 1$ (formula booklet)
$I_n(\sec): nI_n = \sec^{n-2}x\tan x + (n-2)I_{n-2}$ — derive, don't memorize
$I_n(\sin^n x, 0 \text{ to } \pi/2): nI_n = (n-1)I_{n-2}$ — derive, don't memorize
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting that after IBP the original integral $I_n$ reappears — you MUST collect like terms and solve for $I_n$
  • Failing to replace $\cos^2 x$ with $1 - \sin^2 x$ (or $\tan^2 x$ with $\sec^2 x - 1$) to split the remaining integral into $I_n$ and $I_{n-k}$
  • Dropping the boundary term $[uv]$ or evaluating it incorrectly — always check at BOTH limits
  • Using wrong base case: $I_0 = \pi/2$ vs $I_1 = 1$ depends on whether $n$ is even or odd
  • Algebraic error in the coefficient when integrating to find $v$ (e.g. missing $2/9$ factor)
  • Sign errors from the derivative of $u$ in IBP, especially with chain rule on $\sec^{n-2}x$
  • Forgetting to expand $(x^3+1)^{3/2}$ as $(x^3+1)(x^3+1)^{1/2}$ to recover both $I_n$ and $I_{n-3}$

Assumptions
  • The reduction formula from part (a) is designed to evaluate the integral in part (b) — always use it
  • Arc length and surface area integrands often simplify to a form involving $\sin^n x$, $\cos^n x$, or $\sec^n x$
  • Boundary terms in IBP typically vanish for standard limits like $[0, \pi/2]$
Approaches
A: Derive reduction formula, then apply to geometric integral
When: Always — this is the standard structure for these combined questions
  1. Part (a): Derive the reduction formula using integration by parts (see Reduction Formula type)
  2. Part (b): Set up the arc length or surface area integral using the appropriate formula
  3. Differentiate to find $dy/dx$ or $dx/dt, dy/dt$
  4. Simplify $1 + (dy/dx)^2$ — it should form a perfect square
  5. The resulting integral should match $I_n$ from part (a) for some specific $n$
  6. Apply the reduction formula recursively to evaluate
  7. Compute the base case and multiply through
+ Pros: Highly structured — parts are designed to fit together
– Cons: If you cannot derive the reduction formula, you cannot complete the question — but you CAN use it if stated
Formulas
$L = \int_a^b \sqrt{1 + (dy/dx)^2}\,dx$ (formula booklet)
$S = 2\pi\int_a^b y\sqrt{1 + (dy/dx)^2}\,dx$ (formula booklet)
Standard reduction formula results for $\int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^n x\,dx$ and $\int_0^{\pi/2} \cos^n x\,dx$: Wallis's formulae (MEMORIZE or derive)
Pitfalls
  • Not recognising that the integral from the arc length/surface area setup matches the $I_n$ from part (a)
  • Errors in simplifying $1 + (dy/dx)^2$ — always check it gives a perfect square
  • Forgetting to multiply by $2\pi$ for surface area problems
  • Using wrong limits when connecting the geometric integral to $I_n$

Triggerssurface arearevolutionrotated about2πsurface of revolutionrotate
Assumptions
  • Rotation is about the $x$-axis unless stated otherwise (if about $y$-axis, swap roles of $x$ and $y$)
  • $y \geq 0$ on the interval (otherwise take $|y|$)
  • The $2\pi$ factor must be included — it is NOT optional
Approaches
A: Cartesian surface area formula
When: When the curve is $y = f(x)$, rotated about the $x$-axis
  1. Compute $\frac{dy}{dx}$
  2. Form $1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2$ and simplify to a perfect square if possible
  3. Set up $S = 2\pi \int_a^b y\sqrt{1 + (dy/dx)^2}\,dx$
  4. Simplify the product $y \times \sqrt{1 + (dy/dx)^2}$ before integrating
  5. Integrate — may need trig substitution (e.g. $x = a\sin\theta$) or a reduction formula
+ Pros: Direct for Cartesian curves
– Cons: The product $y \cdot ds$ can be complex to simplify
B: Parametric surface area formula
When: When the curve is parametric: $x = x(t), y = y(t)$
  1. Compute $\frac{dx}{dt}$ and $\frac{dy}{dt}$
  2. Form $ds = \sqrt{(dx/dt)^2 + (dy/dt)^2}\,dt$
  3. Set up $S = 2\pi \int_{t_1}^{t_2} y(t) \cdot \sqrt{(dx/dt)^2 + (dy/dt)^2}\,dt$
  4. Simplify using trig identities (e.g. $1 + \tan^2\theta = \sec^2\theta$)
  5. Evaluate the definite integral
+ Pros: Natural for parametric curves; avoids eliminating the parameter
– Cons: Requires correct parametric differentiation of potentially complex expressions
Formulas
$S = 2\pi\int_a^b y\sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2}\,dx$ — rotation about $x$-axis (formula booklet)
$S = 2\pi\int_{t_1}^{t_2} y(t)\sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2}\,dt$ (formula booklet)
$S = 2\pi\int_a^b x\sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{dx}{dy}\right)^2}\,dy$ — rotation about $y$-axis (MEMORIZE adaptation)
Pitfalls
  • Omitting the $2\pi$ factor entirely
  • Not simplifying $y \times \sqrt{1+(dy/dx)^2}$ before attempting to integrate — missing cancellations
  • Using $x$ instead of $y$ (or vice versa) when rotating about the wrong axis
  • Forgetting to differentiate $\ln(\sec\theta + \tan\theta)$ correctly — the derivative is $\sec\theta$, not $\sec\theta\tan\theta$

Triggerspartial fractionsintegraterational functionalgebraic divisionimproper fractioncompleting the square
Assumptions
  • If degree of numerator ≥ degree of denominator, perform polynomial long division first
  • Denominators must be fully factorised before decomposition
  • Irreducible quadratic factors require $Ax + B$ in the numerator of that partial fraction
Approaches
A: Long division then partial fractions
When: When the fraction is improper (degree of numerator ≥ degree of denominator)
  1. Perform algebraic long division to write $\frac{p(x)}{q(x)} = \text{polynomial} + \frac{r(x)}{q(x)}$
  2. Factorise $q(x)$ completely (linear and/or irreducible quadratic factors)
  3. Decompose $\frac{r(x)}{q(x)}$ into partial fractions
  4. Determine constants by substituting convenient values of $x$ or comparing coefficients
  5. Integrate each term: polynomials, $\frac{A}{x-a} \to A\ln|x-a|$, $\frac{Bx+C}{x^2+k} \to $ log + arctan terms
+ Pros: Systematic and reliable for all rational functions
– Cons: Long division step is error-prone, especially with sign errors
B: Direct partial fractions (proper fractions only)
When: When the fraction is already proper
  1. Factorise the denominator
  2. Set up partial fraction form with unknown constants
  3. Find constants using cover-up rule or comparing coefficients
  4. Integrate each partial fraction
+ Pros: Quick when the fraction is already proper and denominator factorises easily
– Cons: Cannot be used directly if the fraction is improper
Formulas
$\int \frac{1}{x-a}\,dx = \ln|x-a| + C$ (MEMORIZE)
$\int \frac{1}{x^2+a^2}\,dx = \frac{1}{a}\arctan\frac{x}{a} + C$ (formula booklet)
$\int \frac{2x}{x^2+a^2}\,dx = \ln(x^2+a^2) + C$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to perform algebraic long division when the fraction is improper
  • Sign errors in completing the square, especially with the constant term
  • Forgetting to factorise $(1-x^2) = (1-x)(1+x)$ before decomposing
  • Dividing only one term of the numerator by the denominator instead of every term

Triggersarccosarcsinarctanarcosharsinhinverse trigonometricinverse hyperbolicdifferentiatehence integrate
Assumptions
  • Chain rule must be applied when differentiating $\arccos(kx)$ — the factor $k$ is easily forgotten
  • The derivative of $\arccos(x)$ is $-1/\sqrt{1-x^2}$ — note the MINUS sign
  • Domain restrictions apply: $\arccos(kx)$ requires $|kx| \leq 1$
Approaches
A: Differentiate then use IBP
When: When asked to integrate a product involving an inverse trig function
  1. Differentiate the inverse trig function using the chain rule
  2. Set $u = \text{inverse trig function}$ and $dv = \text{other factor}\,dx$
  3. Apply IBP: $\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$
  4. The remaining integral is typically a standard form (possibly needing trig substitution)
  5. Evaluate at limits if definite
+ Pros: The inverse trig function simplifies upon differentiation, making the remaining integral tractable
– Cons: Sign errors from the negative derivative of $\arccos$ are very common
B: Trigonometric substitution to eliminate square root
When: When the remaining integral after IBP contains $\sqrt{1-k^2x^2}$
  1. Let $x = \frac{1}{k}\sin\theta$ (or $\cos\theta$)
  2. Transform $dx$ and the square root
  3. Integrate the resulting trig expression
  4. Convert back to $x$
+ Pros: Eliminates the square root cleanly
– Cons: Adds an extra layer of substitution and back-substitution
Formulas
$\frac{d}{dx}[\arccos(x)] = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}$ (formula booklet)
$\frac{d}{dx}[\arcsin(x)] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}$ (formula booklet)
$\frac{d}{dx}[\operatorname{arcosh}(x)] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2-1}}$ (formula booklet)
$\frac{d}{dx}[\arccos(kx)] = \frac{-k}{\sqrt{1-k^2x^2}}$ (MEMORIZE chain rule application)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting the chain rule factor $k$ when differentiating $\arccos(kx)$ or $\operatorname{arcosh}(kx)$
  • Getting the sign wrong: derivative of $\arccos$ is NEGATIVE
  • Sign propagation errors in IBP when the $du$ term is negative

Assumptions
  • For $x = a\cosh\theta$: requires $x \geq a$ and $\theta \geq 0$
  • Hyperbolic identity $\cosh^2\theta - \sinh^2\theta = 1$ is fundamental
  • $e^{-\ln k} = 1/k$, not $-k$
Approaches
A: Direct hyperbolic substitution
When: When the integrand contains $\sqrt{x^2 - a^2}$ (use $x = a\cosh\theta$) or $\sqrt{x^2 + a^2}$ (use $x = a\sinh\theta$)
  1. Identify the appropriate substitution based on the form under the square root
  2. Compute $dx = a\sinh\theta\,d\theta$ (or $a\cosh\theta\,d\theta$)
  3. Substitute into the integral and simplify using $\cosh^2\theta - 1 = \sinh^2\theta$
  4. Be careful with powers: $(a^2\sinh^2\theta)^{3/2} = a^3\sinh^3\theta$, NOT $a^2\sinh^3\theta$
  5. Integrate the resulting hyperbolic expression (may need further identities like $\cosh 2\theta = 2\cosh^2\theta - 1$)
  6. Convert back to $x$ using $\theta = \operatorname{arcosh}(x/a)$ and triangle relations
+ Pros: Eliminates the square root cleanly
– Cons: Students often make errors with powers when simplifying $(a^2 f(\theta))^{n/2}$
B: Use logarithmic form of inverse hyperbolics
When: When the result needs to be expressed in terms of logarithms rather than inverse hyperbolic functions
  1. Complete the substitution and integration as in Approach A
  2. Convert the inverse hyperbolic answer using $\operatorname{arcosh}(x) = \ln(x + \sqrt{x^2-1})$
  3. Simplify the logarithmic expression
+ Pros: Gives answer in a form that may be easier to evaluate at specific limits
– Cons: Extra step of conversion can introduce errors
Formulas
$\cosh^2\theta - \sinh^2\theta = 1$ (formula booklet)
$\int \operatorname{cosech}^2\theta\,d\theta = -\coth\theta + C$ (MEMORIZE — note the minus sign)
$\operatorname{arcosh}(x) = \ln(x + \sqrt{x^2-1})$, $x \geq 1$ (formula booklet)
$\operatorname{arsinh}(x) = \ln(x + \sqrt{x^2+1})$ (formula booklet)
$\sinh(2\theta) = 2\sinh\theta\cosh\theta$ (formula booklet)
$\cosh(2\theta) = 2\cosh^2\theta - 1$ (formula booklet)
Pitfalls
  • $(16\sinh^2\theta)^{3/2} = 64\sinh^3\theta$, NOT $16\sinh^3\theta$ — handle the power of the constant correctly
  • The integral of $\operatorname{cosech}^2\theta$ is $-\coth\theta$, NOT $+\coth\theta$
  • Forgetting $e^{-\ln 2} = 1/2$, not $-2$
  • Not showing compound angle formula usage when the mark scheme requires it

Triggersarc lengthlength of the curvedssqrt(1 + (dy/dx)^2)show that the length
Assumptions
  • The curve is smooth (continuously differentiable) on the interval of integration
  • For parametric curves, $t$ increases monotonically over the given range
  • $1 + (dy/dx)^2$ should simplify — if it doesn't, re-check your differentiation
Approaches
A: Cartesian arc length formula
When: When the curve is given as $y = f(x)$
  1. Compute $\frac{dy}{dx}$
  2. Square it: $\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2$
  3. Form $1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2$ and simplify — combine into a single fraction
  4. Look for a perfect square under the square root (this is almost always the case)
  5. Take the square root
  6. Set up $L = \int_a^b \sqrt{1 + (dy/dx)^2}\,dx$
  7. Integrate (may require partial fractions, substitution, or a reduction formula)
+ Pros: Direct and systematic when curve is in Cartesian form
– Cons: The algebraic simplification of $1 + (dy/dx)^2$ can be messy
B: Parametric arc length formula
When: When the curve is given parametrically as $x(t), y(t)$
  1. Compute $\frac{dx}{dt}$ and $\frac{dy}{dt}$
  2. Form $\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2$
  3. Simplify using identities (e.g. $1 + \tan^2\theta = \sec^2\theta$)
  4. Take the square root to get $ds/dt$
  5. Integrate: $L = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \sqrt{(dx/dt)^2 + (dy/dt)^2}\,dt$
+ Pros: Natural when the curve is parametric; avoids needing to eliminate the parameter
– Cons: Requires differentiating both parametric equations correctly
Formulas
$L = \int_a^b \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2}\,dx$ (formula booklet)
$L = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2}\,dt$ (formula booklet)
$\frac{d}{dx}[\operatorname{arcosh}(kx)] = \frac{k}{\sqrt{k^2x^2 - 1}}$ (MEMORIZE the chain rule factor)
$\frac{d}{dx}[\ln(1-x^2)] = \frac{-2x}{1-x^2}$ (MEMORIZE)
Pitfalls
  • Forgetting to square $dy/dx$ before adding 1
  • Sign errors when differentiating $\ln(\sec\theta + \tan\theta)$ — derivative is just $\sec\theta$
  • Forgetting the chain rule factor (e.g. factor of 2 in $\frac{d}{dx}[\operatorname{arcosh}(2x)]$)
  • Not recognising that $1 + (dy/dx)^2$ forms a perfect square — always try to factorise
  • Algebra errors when combining $1 + \frac{p(x)}{q(x)}$ into $\frac{q(x)+p(x)}{q(x)}$

Must memorize

  • $L = \int_a^b \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2}\,dx$ — arc length, Cartesian (formula booklet)
  • $L = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2}\,dt$ — arc length, parametric (formula booklet)
  • $S = 2\pi\int y\,ds$ — surface area of revolution about $x$-axis (formula booklet)
  • $\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$ — integration by parts (formula booklet)
  • $\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1 \Rightarrow \cos^2 x = 1 - \sin^2 x$ (MEMORIZE — used in almost every reduction formula)
  • $\tan^2 x = \sec^2 x - 1$ (MEMORIZE — key for $\sec^n x$ reduction formulae)
  • $\cosh^2\theta - \sinh^2\theta = 1$ (formula booklet)
  • $\int \operatorname{cosech}^2\theta\,d\theta = -\coth\theta + C$ (MEMORIZE — note the minus sign)
  • $\frac{d}{dx}[\arccos(kx)] = \frac{-k}{\sqrt{1-k^2x^2}}$ (MEMORIZE with chain rule)
  • $\frac{d}{dx}[\operatorname{arcosh}(kx)] = \frac{k}{\sqrt{k^2x^2-1}}$ (MEMORIZE with chain rule)
  • $\frac{d}{dx}[\ln(\sec\theta + \tan\theta)] = \sec\theta$ (MEMORIZE — appears in parametric surface area questions)

Global strategy

  • Reduction formula questions appear in almost every sitting (18/30 questions) — master IBP with the identity-substitution trick ($\cos^2 x = 1-\sin^2 x$, $\tan^2 x = \sec^2 x - 1$, $x^2 = -(a-x^2)+a$) as it is the single most important technique in this chapter.
  • Arc length and surface area questions almost always produce $1 + (dy/dx)^2$ that simplifies to a perfect square — if yours doesn't, recheck differentiation.
  • Many questions combine reduction formulae with arc length/surface area: expect part (a) = derive formula, part (b) = use it in a geometric context. Even if you can't prove the formula, STATE it and use it for part marks.
  • Always evaluate boundary terms in IBP explicitly — write $[uv]_a^b$ and substitute both limits. For limits $0$ to $\pi/2$, check $\sin 0 = 0$, $\cos(\pi/2) = 0$.
  • Practise the full recursive chain: e.g. $I_6 \to I_4 \to I_2 \to I_0$ — arithmetic errors in the final multiplication are common and costly.
  • For hyperbolic substitution problems, be very careful with powers: $(a^2 f(\theta))^{n/2} = a^n f(\theta)^{n/2}$, not $a^2 f(\theta)^{n/2}$.
  • Keep a mental checklist of key identities: $\sin^2+\cos^2=1$, $1+\tan^2=\sec^2$, $\cosh^2-\sinh^2=1$ — these appear in nearly every question.
  • When simplifying $1 + (dy/dx)^2$, always combine over a common denominator first, then try to factorise the numerator as a perfect square.

Exam tips

  1. If asked to 'show that' a reduction formula holds, you MUST show every line of working — the answer is given, so marks are for method.
  2. Even if you cannot derive the reduction formula in part (a), write it down and use it in subsequent parts — you will still earn method marks.
  3. When computing $1 + (dy/dx)^2$, write it as a single fraction over a common denominator, then look for a perfect square in the numerator — this is almost always the intended simplification.
  4. In definite integral reduction problems, compute the base case ($I_0$ or $I_1$) FIRST before applying the recursive formula — this avoids wasted work if you made an error.
  5. For surface area questions, double-check: did you include the $2\pi$? Did you use $y$ (not $x$) for rotation about the $x$-axis?
  6. When IBP gives you the original $I_n$ on the RHS, do NOT integrate again — collect like terms: e.g. $I_n = \text{boundary} + aI_{n-2} - bI_n \Rightarrow (1+b)I_n = \text{boundary} + aI_{n-2}$.
  7. Always check dimensions/units of your answer: arc length should have units of length, surface area should have units of length², and definite integrals of trig functions over $[0,\pi/2]$ should be positive.
  8. Write out the full IBP table ($u, du, v, dv$) explicitly — this prevents sign and differentiation errors and earns method marks even if the final answer is wrong.
