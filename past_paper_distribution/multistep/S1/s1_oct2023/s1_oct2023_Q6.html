Q6 — Regression Equations, $S_{xy}$, PMCC

Question

From the same 12 observations the two regression equations between $x$ and $y$ are

$y$ on $x$: $\;y = 1.4x + 1.5$

$x$ on $y$: $\;x = 1.2 + 0.2y$

(a) (i) Find the point of intersection of these two lines. (ii) Hence show that $\sum x = 25$. (4)
Given $\displaystyle \sum xy = \tfrac{6961}{60}$,
(b) find $S_{xy}$. (4)
(c) Find the product moment correlation coefficient between $x$ and $y$. (4)

[12 marks]

Part (a)(i)3 steps

STEP 1/3

Solve simultaneously. Sub $y = 1.4x + 1.5$ into $x = 1.2 + 0.2y$:
$$x = 1.2 + 0.2(1.4x + 1.5) = 1.2 + 0.28x + 0.3 = 1.5 + 0.28x.$$
$$x - 0.28x = 1.5 \implies 0.72x = 1.5 \implies x = \tfrac{1.5}{0.72} = \tfrac{150}{72} = \tfrac{25}{12}.$$

STEP 2/3

Then $y = 1.4 \cdot \tfrac{25}{12} + 1.5 = \tfrac{35}{12} + \tfrac{18}{12} = \tfrac{53}{12}.$

STEP 3/3

$$\boxed{\left(\tfrac{25}{12},\ \tfrac{53}{12}\right)}.$$

Target: $\boxed{\left(\tfrac{25}{12},\ \tfrac{53}{12}\right)}$

Part (a)(ii)2 steps

STEP 1/2

The point of intersection of the two regression lines is $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$. So $\bar{x} = \tfrac{25}{12}$, hence
$$\sum x = n\bar{x} = 12 \cdot \tfrac{25}{12} = \boxed{25}.\;\blacksquare$$

Target: $\boxed{25}$
STEP 2/2

Similarly $\sum y = 12 \cdot \tfrac{53}{12} = 53$.

Part (b)[4 marks] · 2 steps

Carry-in: Part (a) result: $\boxed{25}$
STEP 1/2

Apply $S_{xy} = \sum xy - n\bar{x}\bar{y}$:
$$S_{xy} = \tfrac{6961}{60} - 12 \cdot \tfrac{25}{12} \cdot \tfrac{53}{12} = \tfrac{6961}{60} - \tfrac{25 \cdot 53}{12} = \tfrac{6961}{60} - \tfrac{1325}{12}.$$

STEP 2/2

Common denominator $60$:
$$= \tfrac{6961}{60} - \tfrac{6625}{60} = \tfrac{336}{60} = \tfrac{28}{5} = \boxed{5.6}.$$

Target: $\boxed{5.6}$

Part (c)[4 marks] · 3 steps

Carry-in: Part (a) result: $\boxed{25}$
Part (b) result: $\boxed{5.6}$
STEP 1/3

Shortcut: $r^2 = b_{yx}\cdot b_{xy}$.

From the two equations:
- $b_{yx} = 1.4$ (slope of $y$-on-$x$).
- $b_{xy} = 0.2$ (slope of $x$-on-$y$).

STEP 2/3

$$r^2 = 1.4 \times 0.2 = 0.28.$$
$$r = \sqrt{0.28} = 0.5291\ldots \approx \boxed{0.529}.$$

Both slopes positive $\to$ $r$ positive.

Verification via the long route:
- $b_{yx} = \tfrac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}} = 1.4 \implies S_{xx} = \tfrac{5.6}{1.4} = 4$.
- $b_{xy} = \tfrac{S_{xy}}{S_{yy}} = 0.2 \implies S_{yy} = \tfrac{5.6}{0.2} = 28$.
- $r = \tfrac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx} S_{yy}}} = \tfrac{5.6}{\sqrt{4 \cdot 28}} = \tfrac{5.6}{\sqrt{112}} = \tfrac{5.6}{10.583} \approx 0.529.$ ✓

Target: $\boxed{0.529}$
STEP 3/3

Key formula / concept.

  • Intersection of the two regression lines is $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$.
  • $S_{xy} = \sum xy - n\bar{x}\bar{y}$.
  • $b_{yx} = S_{xy}/S_{xx}$ and $b_{xy} = S_{xy}/S_{yy}$.
  • PMCC shortcut: $r^2 = b_{yx} \cdot b_{xy}$ (sign of $r$ matches sign of either slope).

Exam checklist.

  • [ ] Solve the two regression equations as a 2$\times$2 linear system.
  • [ ] State explicitly "intersection = $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$" before computing $\sum x = n\bar{x}$.
  • [ ] When computing $S_{xy}$, use $\sum xy - n\bar{x}\bar{y}$ (not $\sum xy - \tfrac{\sum x \sum y}{n}$ both forms are equivalent but the first plugs straight from (a)).
  • [ ] For PMCC, check whether both regression lines are given --- if yes, use $r^2 = b_{yx} b_{xy}$.
  • [ ] Sign of $r$ matches the common sign of the two regression slopes.

Common pitfalls.

  • Computing $\bar{x}\bar{y}$ as a single decimal and rounding too early --- keep the fractions $\tfrac{25}{12} \cdot \tfrac{53}{12} = \tfrac{1325}{144}$ until the very end.
  • Forgetting that "hence" in (a)(ii) means "use the intersection".
  • Computing $S_{xx}$ from $\sum x^2$ when it's neither given nor needed.

See also Q5(e) --- both questions reduce to a 2$\times$2 linear system once you've read the right structural fact (intersection = means in Q6; two percentiles in Q5).

Key Formulas --- NOT in Formula Booklet (memorise)

  1. $P(A \mid B) = \dfrac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}.$
  2. Independence: $P(A\cap B) = P(A)P(B).$
  3. Mutually exclusive: $P(A\cap B) = 0.$
  4. $P(A\cup B) = P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B).$
  5. $P(A\cap B') = P(A) - P(A\cap B).$
  6. $E(g(X)) = \sum g(x) P(X=x).$
  7. $E(aX+b) = aE(X)+b,\ \text{Var}(aX+b) = a^2\text{Var}(X).$
  8. $\text{Var}(X) = E(X^2) - [E(X)]^2.$
  9. Standardisation: $Z = (X-\mu)/\sigma \sim N(0,1).$
  10. Box-plot outlier fences: $Q_1 - 1.5\,\text{IQR}$ and $Q_3 + 1.5\,\text{IQR}.$
  11. Quartile positions: $Q_1 \to \tfrac{n+1}{4}$, median $\to \tfrac{n+1}{2}$, $Q_3 \to \tfrac{3(n+1)}{4}.$
  12. Intersection of regression lines = $(\bar{x},\bar{y})$.
  13. $r^2 = b_{yx}\cdot b_{xy}$.
  14. Two-line gradients: $b_{yx} = S_{xy}/S_{xx}$, $b_{xy} = S_{xy}/S_{yy}$.

Key Formulas --- IN Booklet (know where to find)

  1. $S_{xx} = \sum x^2 - \frac{(\sum x)^2}{n}$, $S_{yy} = \sum y^2 - \frac{(\sum y)^2}{n}$, $S_{xy} = \sum xy - \frac{\sum x \sum y}{n}.$
  2. $r = \dfrac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx} S_{yy}}}.$
  3. Regression $y = a + bx$: $b = \tfrac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}}$, $a = \bar{y} - b\bar{x}.$
  4. $\text{Var}(X) = \sum x^2 P(X=x) - [E(X)]^2.$
  5. Yellow booklet: $\Phi(z)$ table and inverse-normal percentage points.

Exam Strategy

  1. Time budget: 90 min $\div$ 75 marks = 1.2 min/mark. Leave 5 min at the end for checking. Time per question --- Q1: 13 min, Q2: 16 min, Q3: 14 min, Q4: 14 min, Q5: 18 min, Q6: 14 min.
  2. Conditional probability discipline: write $P(A|B) = P(A\cap B)/P(B)$ at the top of every part with "given that". Enumerate the intersection event explicitly.
  3. "Compare distributions" template: median + IQR + one context sentence each. Three statements only. Do not embellish.
  4. Normal distribution: sketch the bell curve and shade for every probability question. For inverse normal, decide the sign of $z$ from the sketch.
  5. Simultaneous equations from percentiles or regression lines: always subtract first to eliminate one unknown. Carry $z$-values to 4 d.p.; round only at the end.
  6. Regression questions with both lines given: jump to $r^2 = b_{yx}\cdot b_{xy}$ --- it bypasses $S_{xx}$ and $S_{yy}$ entirely.
  7. "Show that" parts: every algebraic line written out. Don't skip. Method marks reward the chain even when arithmetic slips.
  8. "Hence" parts: must reference the previous part's result. If you find yourself starting over, you're missing the link.
  9. Tables in full: quote $\Phi(1.20) = 0.8849$ to 4 d.p. Don't truncate.
  10. Quartile positions: S1 uses $(n+1)/4$ etc.; average flanking values when the position is non-integer.
