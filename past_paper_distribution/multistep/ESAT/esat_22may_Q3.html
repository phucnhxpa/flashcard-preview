Q3 · Unit cube: vertex to midpoint of opposite face

Question

A cube has sides of unit length. What is the length of a line joining a vertex to the midpoint of one of the opposite faces (the dashed line in the diagram below)? [diagram not to scale]

A. $\dfrac{\sqrt3}{2}$    B. $\sqrt2$    C. $\dfrac{\sqrt5}{2}$    D. $\sqrt3$    E. $\dfrac{\sqrt6}{2}$

[1 marks]

Solution [1 marks] · 3 steps

All edges $=1$. Place the start vertex at the origin $(0,0,0)$. The midpoint of the opposite face has coordinates $\left(1,\tfrac12,\tfrac12\right)$ — one full unit across the cube, then half a unit in each of the two in-face directions. Solve by two successive 2D-Pythagoras steps.
STEP 1/3step 1 diagonal

Strategy. First find $x$: the distance from the vertex across one face direction (length $1$) and one half-direction (length $\tfrac12$). This is a 2D Pythagoras in a right triangle whose legs are $1$ and $\tfrac12$.

Formula

$$x=\sqrt{a^{2}+b^{2}}\quad(a=1,\;b=\tfrac12).$$

Substitute

$$x=\sqrt{1^{2}+\left(\tfrac12\right)^{2}}=\sqrt{1+\tfrac14}=\sqrt{\tfrac54}=\dfrac{\sqrt5}{2}.$$

Sense check. $\tfrac{\sqrt5}{2}\approx1.118$ — longer than $1$, correct for a diagonal that includes a full edge.

$x=\dfrac{\sqrt5}{2}$.
↳ from STEP 2: $x=\dfrac{\sqrt5}{2}$, so $x^{2}=\dfrac54$.
STEP 2/3step 2 full line

Strategy. Now find the full slant $y$: combine $x$ with the remaining perpendicular half-unit ($\tfrac12$) using Pythagoras again.

Formula

$$y=\sqrt{x^{2}+\left(\tfrac12\right)^{2}}.$$

Substitute (using $x^{2}=\tfrac54$)

$$y=\sqrt{\tfrac54+\tfrac14}=\sqrt{\tfrac64}=\sqrt{\tfrac32}=\dfrac{\sqrt3}{\sqrt2}.$$

Sense check. $\sqrt{6}/2$ exceeds $x$, as the hypotenuse of the second right triangle must exceed its leg $x$.

$y=\sqrt{\tfrac32}=\dfrac{\sqrt3}{\sqrt2}$.
↳ from STEP 3: $y=\dfrac{\sqrt3}{\sqrt2}$.
STEP 3/3rationalise

Strategy. Rationalise to the option form and check it against the distractors.

Rationalise

$$y=\dfrac{\sqrt3}{\sqrt2}\cdot\dfrac{\sqrt2}{\sqrt2}=\dfrac{\sqrt6}{2}.$$
Common trap (do not confuse the surds). $\dfrac{\sqrt6}{2}\approx1.225$ but $\dfrac{\sqrt3}{2}\approx0.866$ — these are different values, so Option A is a deliberate look-alike distractor. The body diagonal $\sqrt3$ is corner-to-corner, not corner-to-face-midpoint; applying Pythagoras only once is the classic error.

Sense check. $\tfrac{\sqrt6}{2}\approx1.225$ lies between the face diagonal $\sqrt2\approx1.414$ and the edge $1$ — geometrically reasonable for a vertex-to-face-centre line, confirming Option E.

$\boxed{y=\dfrac{\sqrt6}{2}\approx1.225}$ — Option E.
