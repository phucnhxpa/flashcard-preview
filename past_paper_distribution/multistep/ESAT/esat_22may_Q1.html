Q1 · Semicircle-in-square area

Question

A square piece of metal has a semi-circular piece cut out of it as shown. The area of the remaining metal is 100 cm². The semicircle’s diameter equals the full side of the square [diagram not to scale].

Which one of the following is a correct expression for the length of the side of the square in centimetres?

A. $20\sqrt{\dfrac{2}{8-\pi}}$    B. $10\sqrt{\dfrac{2}{4-\pi}}$    C. $20\sqrt{\dfrac{2}{8+\pi}}$    D. $10\sqrt{\dfrac{1}{8-\pi}}$    E. $10\sqrt{\dfrac{1}{4-\pi}}$

[1 marks]

Solution [1 marks] · 5 steps

Let the side of the square be $m$. The semicircle’s diameter lies along one full edge, so its radius is $r=\dfrac{m}{2}$. Target form: all options are in $m$ (not $r$), so substitute $r=m/2$ from the start.
STEP 1/5set up

Strategy. Remaining metal = area of square − area of the semicircle. Write the full semicircle formula first, then substitute $r=m/2$.

Formula

$$A_{\text{square}}-A_{\text{semicircle}}=100,\qquad A_{\text{semicircle}}=\tfrac{1}{2}\pi r^{2}.$$

Substitute $r=m/2$

$$m^{2}-\tfrac{1}{2}\pi\!\left(\tfrac{m}{2}\right)^{2}=100.$$
Phuc's mistake (≈08:00). Phuc first wrote $m^{2}-\pi r^{2}=100$, using the full-circle area and dropping the $\tfrac12$. Harry red-boxed it. A semicircle is half a circle: its area is $\tfrac12\pi r^{2}$, never $\pi r^{2}$.

Sense check. The subtracted region is a half-disc, so the coefficient is $\tfrac12$; the equation balances area (cm²) on both sides.

Equation: $m^{2}-\tfrac{1}{2}\pi\left(\tfrac{m}{2}\right)^{2}=100$.
↳ from STEP 2: $m^{2}-\tfrac{1}{2}\pi\left(\tfrac{m}{2}\right)^{2}=100$.
STEP 2/5expand

Strategy. Square the bracket and simplify the $\pi$ term so the equation contains a single $m^{2}$ coefficient.

Expand

$$m^{2}-\tfrac{1}{2}\pi\cdot\dfrac{m^{2}}{4}=100$$$$m^{2}-\dfrac{\pi m^{2}}{8}=100.$$

Sense check. $\left(\tfrac{m}{2}\right)^{2}=\tfrac{m^2}{4}$ and $\tfrac12\cdot\tfrac14=\tfrac18$, so the $\pi$-term denominator is $8$ — this $8$ is what later appears in the answer.

$m^{2}-\dfrac{\pi m^{2}}{8}=100$.
↳ from STEP 3: $m^{2}-\dfrac{\pi m^{2}}{8}=100$.
STEP 3/5factor

Strategy. Factor $m^{2}$ out of the left side and combine the bracket over a common denominator of $8$.

Factorise

$$m^{2}\!\left(1-\dfrac{\pi}{8}\right)=100$$$$m^{2}\cdot\dfrac{8-\pi}{8}=100.$$
Technique. Factor before expanding numerically — keeps the algebra compact (Harry, technique #8). The bracket $1-\tfrac{\pi}{8}=\tfrac{8-\pi}{8}$ is the key combine.

Sense check. $8-\pi\approx4.86>0$, so $m^{2}>0$ — consistent with a real square.

$m^{2}\cdot\dfrac{8-\pi}{8}=100$.
↳ from STEP 4: $m^{2}\cdot\dfrac{8-\pi}{8}=100$.
STEP 4/5solve m^2

Strategy. Multiply both sides by $\dfrac{8}{8-\pi}$ to isolate $m^{2}$.

Rearrange

$$m^{2}=\dfrac{100\times 8}{8-\pi}=\dfrac{800}{8-\pi}.$$

Sense check. Numerically $m^{2}\approx 800/4.86\approx165$, so $m\approx12.8$ cm — a sensible side length for a $100$ cm² remainder.

$m^{2}=\dfrac{800}{8-\pi}$.
↳ from STEP 5: $m^{2}=\dfrac{800}{8-\pi}$.
STEP 5/5surd form

Strategy. Take the positive square root and simplify $\sqrt{800}$ to match the option format.

Square root + simplify

$$m=\sqrt{\dfrac{800}{8-\pi}}=\dfrac{\sqrt{800}}{\sqrt{8-\pi}}$$$$\sqrt{800}=\sqrt{400\times2}=20\sqrt{2}$$$$m=\dfrac{20\sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{8-\pi}}=20\sqrt{\dfrac{2}{8-\pi}}.$$
Common trap. Options with $4-\pi$ in the denominator come from omitting the $\tfrac12$ (the full-circle slip); the coefficient $10$ comes from mis-simplifying $\sqrt{800}$. Eliminate $8+\pi$ by sign: a larger denominator would give a smaller area, contradicting ‘cut out’.

Sense check. $20\sqrt{2/(8-\pi)}\approx20\sqrt{0.412}\approx12.8$ cm — matches the Step 4 estimate, confirming Option A.

$\boxed{m=20\sqrt{\dfrac{2}{8-\pi}}}$ — Option A.
