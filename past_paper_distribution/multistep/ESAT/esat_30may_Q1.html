Q1 · Area of a right triangle with surd sides

Question

The right-angled triangle shown has horizontal and vertical sides measuring $(4+\sqrt2)$ cm and $(2-\sqrt2)$ cm respectively [diagram not to scale]. What is the area of the triangle in cm²?

A. $(5+3\sqrt2)$    B. $(3-\sqrt2)$    C. $(3+3\sqrt2)$    D. $(5-\sqrt2)$    E. $(5-\sqrt2)$

[1 marks]

Solution [1 marks] · 4 steps

In a right-angled triangle the two perpendicular sides are the base and height, so $A=\tfrac12\times(4+\sqrt2)\times(2-\sqrt2)$. Harry’s rule: expand the surd product fully first, then multiply by $\tfrac12$ — introducing the fraction early creates mixed fractions and surds where sign slips hide. Note this is NOT a difference of two squares ($(a+b)(a-b)$ needs the same first term in both brackets; here they differ, $4$ vs $2$), so the cross terms will not fully cancel.
STEP 1/4area formula

Strategy. Write the area formula and place the two given sides as base and height.

Formula

$$A=\tfrac12\times\text{base}\times\text{height}.$$

Substitute

$$A=\tfrac12\,(4+\sqrt2)(2-\sqrt2).$$

Sense check. Both sides are positive ($4+\sqrt2\approx5.41$, $2-\sqrt2\approx0.59$), so the area is positive.

$A=\tfrac12\,(4+\sqrt2)(2-\sqrt2)$.
↳ from STEP 2: $A=\tfrac12\,(4+\sqrt2)(2-\sqrt2)$.
STEP 2/4expand

Strategy. Expand the bracket product using FOIL before introducing the $\tfrac12$, keeping rational and surd terms separate.

Expand (FOIL)

$$(4+\sqrt2)(2-\sqrt2)=8-4\sqrt2+2\sqrt2-2.$$
Technique (expand before the fraction). Harry: expand the surd product first; introducing $\tfrac12$ early creates mixed fractions and surds where sign slips creep in. The cross terms $-4\sqrt2+2\sqrt2$ do NOT cancel — they combine to $-2\sqrt2$.

Sense check. The last term is $\sqrt2\cdot(-\sqrt2)=-2$ (a rational), as expected from $(\sqrt2)^2=2$.

$(4+\sqrt2)(2-\sqrt2)=8-4\sqrt2+2\sqrt2-2$.
↳ from STEP 3: $8-4\sqrt2+2\sqrt2-2$.
STEP 3/4collect

Strategy. Collect the rational parts and the surd parts separately.

Collect like terms

$$8-2=6,\qquad -4\sqrt2+2\sqrt2=-2\sqrt2$$$$\Rightarrow\;(4+\sqrt2)(2-\sqrt2)=6-2\sqrt2.$$

Sense check. $6-2\sqrt2\approx3.17$, which is twice the eventual area — sensible before halving.

$(4+\sqrt2)(2-\sqrt2)=6-2\sqrt2$.
↳ from STEP 4: $(4+\sqrt2)(2-\sqrt2)=6-2\sqrt2$.
STEP 4/4halve

Strategy. Multiply the simplified product by $\tfrac12$, dividing every term — both the rational $6$ and the surd $-2\sqrt2$.

Multiply by $\tfrac12$ (term by term)

$$A=\tfrac12\,(6-2\sqrt2)=\tfrac12\cdot6-\tfrac12\cdot2\sqrt2=3-\sqrt2.$$
Common trap (halve the surd term too). Both $6$ and $2\sqrt2$ get halved. Forgetting to halve the surd gives $3-2\sqrt2$; halving only the surd gives $6-\sqrt2$ — neither is offered, so a stray answer flags an arithmetic slip.

Sense check. $3-\sqrt2\approx3-1.41=1.59$ cm², a small positive area consistent with base $\approx5.41$ and height $\approx0.59$ (half their product $\approx1.59$). Confirms Option B.

$\boxed{A=3-\sqrt2\text{ cm}^2}$ — Option B.
