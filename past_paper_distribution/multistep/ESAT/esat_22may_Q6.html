ESAT · Maths 1 · 22 May 2026 · Harry Williams

Q6 · Mean age when two members join

Question

The mean age of the twenty members of a running club is exactly 28. The mean age increases by exactly 2 years when two new members join. What is the mean age of the two new members?

A. 20 years    B. 22 years    C. 30 years    D. 40 years    E. 50 years    F. 52 years

[1 marks]

Solution [1 marks] · 4 steps

algebra-only; no diagram needed. The only tool is the mean identity $\bar{x}=\dfrac{\text{sum}}{\text{count}}$, rearranged to sum $=\text{mean}\times\text{count}$. Harry’s scaffold: read the question one sentence at a time and turn each into a total $\Sigma$. Two facts must be extracted before any arithmetic: the new mean is $28+2=30$ (it ‘increases by 2’) and the new count is $20+2=22$ (two members join).
STEP 1/4original sum

Strategy. Convert the first sentence, ‘mean of 20 members is 28’, into the total age of those 20 members.

Formula

$$\bar{x}=\dfrac{\Sigma}{n}\;\Leftrightarrow\;\Sigma=\bar{x}\times n.$$

Substitute $\bar{x}=28,\;n=20$

$$\Sigma_{1}=28\times20=560.$$
Technique (one equation per sentence). Don’t try to handle the whole problem at once. ‘Mean of 20 is 28’ becomes exactly one equation, $\Sigma_1=560$ — a single concrete number to carry forward.

Sense check. $560$ years shared by $20$ people averages $\tfrac{560}{20}=28$ — consistent with the statement.

$\Sigma_{1}=560$ years (20 members).
↳ from STEP 2: $\Sigma_{1}=560$. New mean $=30$, new count $=22$.
STEP 2/4new sum

Strategy. Convert the post-join situation. The mean rises by 2 to 30, and the count rises by 2 to 22.

Substitute (new mean 30, new count 22)

$$\Sigma_{2}=30\times22=660.$$
Common trap (update the count). Use $n=22$, not $20$, and mean $=30$, not $28$. Writing $\Sigma/20=30$ gives $\Sigma=600$, extra $=40$, mean $=20$ (Option A) — a deliberate distractor that hits two mistakes at once.

Sense check. $660>560$: adding two members must increase the total age.

$\Sigma_{2}=660$ years (22 members).
↳ from STEP 3: $\Sigma_{1}=560$, $\Sigma_{2}=660$.
STEP 3/4new-member sum

Strategy. The combined age of just the two new members is whatever the total had to rise by, i.e. the difference of the two totals.

New members’ total = rise in $\Sigma$

$$\Sigma_{\text{new}}=\Sigma_{2}-\Sigma_{1}=660-560=100.$$

Sense check. The 20 original members contribute the same $560$ years in both clubs, so the entire increase of $100$ years is due to the two newcomers — $100$ years for two people is plausible (each around $50$).

$\Sigma_{\text{new}}=100$ years (two members).
↳ from STEP 4: $\Sigma_{\text{new}}=100$ years for two members.
STEP 4/4their mean

Strategy. Divide the two new members’ total by their count, $2$, to get their mean age.

Mean of the two new members

$$\bar{x}_{\text{new}}=\dfrac{\Sigma_{\text{new}}}{2}=\dfrac{100}{2}=50.$$
Full re-check. Recombine all 22 members: $\dfrac{\Sigma_1+\Sigma_{\text{new}}}{22}=\dfrac{560+100}{22}=\dfrac{660}{22}=30$ — exactly the stated new mean, so $50$ is consistent end-to-end.
Common trap (why 50, not 30). Option C ($30$ years) is the new club mean, not the new members’ mean. Two people pulling the average up must be older than the new mean of $30$ — and they are, at $50$ each.
Exam pace. Two ‘sum = mean × count’ lines ($560$, $660$), a subtraction, and a halving — a $\approx30$ s question once each sentence is turned into a $\Sigma$.

Sense check. Two members averaging $50$ are well above the club mean, which is what raises a 20-person average of $28$ up to $30$. Confirms Option E.

$\boxed{\bar{x}_{\text{new}}=50\text{ years}}$ — Option E.
