Q6(c) — Equation of normal to circle $x^2+y^2+8x-4y=0$ at $P$, form $y = mx + c$

Question

The circle $C$ has equation $x^2 + y^2 + 8x - 4y = 0$.

(a) Find (i) the centre, (ii) the exact radius. (3)

The point $P$ lies on $C$. The tangent to $C$ at $P$ has equation $x + 2y + 10 = 0$.

(b) Find the coordinates of $P$. (4)

(c) Find the equation of the normal to $C$ at $P$, giving your answer in the form $y = mx + c$ where $m$ and $c$ are integers to be found. (3)

Scope of this card: part (c) only. From part (a): centre $= (-4, 2)$, radius $= 2\sqrt{5}$. From part (b): $P = (-6, -2)$. Phuc's slack: "redo question c only".

[3 marks]

Part (c) — Normal to $C$ at $P$ in form $y = mx + c$ [3 marks] · 3 steps

Carry-ins from (a)/(b): centre $= (-4, 2)$, radius $= 2\sqrt{5}$, $P = (-6, -2)$. Tangent at $P$: $x + 2y + 10 = 0$.
STEP 1/3 M1 — gradient of normal

Recognise the trigger. The normal to a curve at a point is the line through that point perpendicular to the tangent. For a circle, this normal has a beautiful extra property: it always passes through the centre. Two routes are available:

Route 1 (perpendicular gradients): rearrange the tangent into $y = mx + c$ form to read off its gradient $m_T$, then use $m_T \cdot m_N = -1$.

Route 2 (use the centre): the normal is the line through $P$ and the centre $(-4, 2)$. Use the two-point gradient formula.

Both must give the same answer — using both as a cross-check is the safest exam strategy. We'll do Route 1 first, then verify with Route 2.

Route 1 — Rearrange the tangent. From $x + 2y + 10 = 0$:

$$2y \;=\; -x - 10 \;\Longrightarrow\; y \;=\; -\tfrac{1}{2} x - 5.$$

So the tangent has gradient $m_T = -\tfrac{1}{2}$.

Apply perpendicularity. The product of perpendicular gradients is $-1$:

$$m_N \cdot m_T \;=\; -1 \;\Longrightarrow\; m_N \;=\; \frac{-1}{m_T} \;=\; \frac{-1}{-\tfrac{1}{2}} \;=\; 2.$$

So the normal has gradient $\boxed{m_N = 2}$.

Route 2 cross-check. Two-point gradient through $P(-6,-2)$ and centre $(-4, 2)$:

$$m \;=\; \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \;=\; \frac{2 - (-2)}{-4 - (-6)} \;=\; \frac{4}{2} \;=\; 2. \;\;\checkmark$$

A frequent error is using $m_N = -m_T$ instead of $m_N = -1/m_T$ — that's reflection, not perpendicularity. Another slip: rearranging $x + 2y + 10 = 0$ to $y = -x/2 + 10$ (sign error on the constant). The double-check via the centre route catches both of these instantly. On any circle-tangent-normal question, always verify the normal gradient using the centre — it's free arithmetic and removes the sign-flip risk.
$m_N \;=\; 2$ (verified two ways: perpendicular to tangent, and via line through centre).
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $m_N = 2$. Normal passes through $P = (-6, -2)$.
STEP 2/3 M1 — point-gradient form

Recognise the trigger. You have a gradient and a point on the line, so the cleanest line-finder is the point-gradient form.

State the tool.

$$y - y_1 \;=\; m(x - x_1) \qquad \text{where } (x_1, y_1) \text{ is a known point on the line.}$$

This is preferred over $y = mx + c$ at the substitution step because it avoids first computing $c$ via solving — you just plug in directly.

Apply with $m = m_N = 2$ and $(x_1, y_1) = P = (-6, -2)$:

$$y - (-2) \;=\; 2\bigl(x - (-6)\bigr)$$

$$y + 2 \;=\; 2(x + 6).$$

Expand the right-hand side carefully.

$$2(x + 6) \;=\; 2x + 12.$$

So

$$y + 2 \;=\; 2x + 12.$$

The two sign traps in this step are subtracting a negative coordinate: $y - (-2) = y + 2$ and $x - (-6) = x + 6$. Writing them with the minus-minus explicit ($y - (-2)$, $x - (-6)$) before simplifying makes both flips visible. The question stipulates that $m$ and $c$ are integers — that's a check on your final answer: if you end up with a fraction or decimal, something has gone wrong upstream (likely in $P$ or $m_N$). The integer-only stipulation is a hint that the geometry conspires to give a clean line.
$y + 2 = 2x + 12$ (point-gradient form expanded).
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 2: $y + 2 = 2x + 12$.
STEP 3/3 A1 — rearrange to y = mx + c

Recognise the trigger. The required form is $y = mx + c$ with $m, c$ integers. Isolate $y$ on the left.

Apply. Subtract 2 from both sides:

$$y \;=\; 2x + 12 - 2$$

$$y \;=\; 2x + 10.$$

Read off: $m = 2$, $c = 10$. Both integers ✓.

$$\boxed{\,y \;=\; 2x + 10, \quad m = 2, \quad c = 10\,}$$

Triple-verify.

(i) Passes through $P(-6, -2)$? $y = 2(-6) + 10 = -12 + 10 = -2$ ✓.

(ii) Passes through centre $(-4, 2)$? $y = 2(-4) + 10 = -8 + 10 = 2$ ✓.

(iii) Perpendicular to tangent $x + 2y + 10 = 0$ (gradient $-\tfrac{1}{2}$)? Product: $2 \times (-\tfrac{1}{2}) = -1$ ✓.

All three checks pass — the normal $y = 2x + 10$ is the unique line through $P$ perpendicular to the given tangent and through the centre of $C$.

When the question fixes the form ($y = mx + c$, $m, c$ integers), always read off both numbers and write them out separately — markers want to see $m$ and $c$ identified, not just the equation. Verifying that the line passes through both $P$ and the centre is a free sanity check that costs no time and catches arithmetic slips. If either check fails, the error is upstream — most likely in $P$ from part (b) or in the gradient from step 1.
$\boxed{y = 2x + 10}$ ($m = 2$, $c = 10$, both integers).
