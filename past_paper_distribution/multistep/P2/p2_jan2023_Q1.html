Q1 — Trapezium rule + integral transformations on $f(x)$

Question

Figure 1 shows a sketch of part of the curve with equation $y = f(x)$. The table below shows some corresponding values of $x$ and $y$ for this curve (values of $y$ given to 3 d.p.):

$$\begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|c|c|} \hline x & -1 & -0.5 & 0 & 0.5 & 1 \\ \hline y & 2.287 & 4.470 & 6.719 & 7.291 & 2.834 \\ \hline \end{array}$$

Using the trapezium rule with all the values of $y$ in the given table,

(a) obtain an estimate for $\displaystyle\int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx$, giving your answer to 2 decimal places. (3)

(b) Use your answer to part (a) to estimate

$\quad$(i) $\displaystyle\int_{-1}^{1}\bigl(f(x)-2\bigr)\,dx$

$\quad$(ii) $\displaystyle\int_{1}^{3} f(x-2)\,dx$ (3)

[6 marks]

Part (a) — Trapezium-rule estimate of $\int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx$ [3 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2 M1

Recognise the trigger. The question hands you a table of $(x,y)$ values with no formula for $f(x)$. Whenever you can't symbolically integrate (because you don't have $f$ explicitly), and the $x$-values are equally spaced, the trapezium rule is the standard numerical-integration tool the exam expects. The phrase "using all the values of $y$ in the given table" is the explicit instruction to use them all — don't skip any.

Why this method. Alternatives like Simpson's rule are off-syllabus for P2. The trapezium rule is the only numerical method you have; the fact that the table gives 3 d.p. accuracy is a hint that the examiner has already done the function-evaluation work for you and just wants the arithmetic.

State the tool. For $n$ strips of equal width $h$:

$$\int_{a}^{b} y\,dx \;\approx\; \tfrac{h}{2}\Bigl[\underbrace{y_0 + y_n}_{\text{endpoints}} + 2\underbrace{(y_1 + y_2 + \cdots + y_{n-1})}_{\text{interior sum}}\Bigr]$$

where $h = (b-a)/n$. The factor $\tfrac{h}{2}$ is the average of "two sides of one trapezium of base $h$".

Set up. Read off from the table — the $x$-values $-1,\,-0.5,\,0,\,0.5,\,1$ are equally spaced, so

$$h \;=\; -0.5 - (-1) \;=\; 0.5, \qquad n \;=\; \text{(no. of }y\text{-values)} - 1 \;=\; 5 - 1 \;=\; 4 \text{ strips}.$$

Sanity-check: $h \times n = 0.5 \times 4 = 2 = b - a = 1 - (-1)$ ✓.

Identify endpoints and interior:

$$\underbrace{y_0 = 2.287}_{x=-1},\; \underbrace{y_4 = 2.834}_{x=1};\qquad \underbrace{y_1=4.470,\, y_2=6.719,\, y_3=7.291}_{\text{interior, multiplied by 2}}.$$

The classic slip is reading 5 $y$-values as 5 strips. The rule is always $n = (\text{values}) - 1$ because each strip needs two ordinates (left + right edge), and adjacent strips share an ordinate. If you ever start the arithmetic and realise $h \times n \ne b-a$, you've miscounted — stop and recount.
$h = 0.5$, $n = 4$ strips; endpoints $y_0=2.287,\, y_4=2.834$; interior $4.470,\, 6.719,\, 7.291$.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $h=0.5$; endpoints $y_0=2.287,\,y_4=2.834$; interior $y_1=4.470,\,y_2=6.719,\,y_3=7.291$.
STEP 2/2 M1 A1

Plug into the formula from STEP 1. Be deliberate: write the formula with the symbols first, then substitute on the next line, then evaluate. This protects you from sign-and-factor-of-2 slips that are the dominant error on this question type.

$$\int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx \;\approx\; \tfrac{h}{2}\bigl[(y_0+y_n) + 2(y_1+y_2+y_3)\bigr]$$

$$\;\approx\; \tfrac{0.5}{2}\bigl[(2.287 + 2.834) + 2(4.470 + 6.719 + 7.291)\bigr]$$

Compute the interior sum first (one piece at a time — don't try to do it in your head):

$$4.470 + 6.719 + 7.291 \;=\; 18.480$$

Then the endpoint sum:

$$2.287 + 2.834 \;=\; 5.121$$

Combine inside the bracket:

$$5.121 + 2(18.480) \;=\; 5.121 + 36.960 \;=\; 42.081$$

Multiply by $\tfrac{h}{2} = 0.25$:

$$0.25 \times 42.081 \;=\; 10.52025$$

Round once at the end to the required 2 d.p.:

$$\boxed{\int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx \;\approx\; 10.52}$$

Sense check. Average ordinate $\approx 10.52 / 2 = 5.26$ — sits comfortably between min ($2.287$) and max ($7.291$) of the table values, ✓. If you had got, say, $30$ or $0.5$, you'd know the arithmetic was wrong.

A1 is awarded only for 10.52, not 10.5. Keep all decimals until the very last line. Two common slips: (i) forgetting to multiply the interior sum by 2; (ii) rounding mid-calculation. Doing it on one clean line with the interior, endpoint, bracket, and multiplication on separate lines makes both errors visible.
$\displaystyle\int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx \approx \boxed{10.52}$

Part (b)(i) — Estimate $\int_{-1}^{1}\bigl(f(x)-2\bigr)\,dx$ [1 marks] · 1 step

From part (a): $\displaystyle\int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx \approx 10.52$.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Recognise the trigger. The integrand is $f(x) - 2$ on the same interval as (a). The phrase "use your answer to part (a)" is your green light to reuse the (a) estimate — you should not re-run the trapezium rule with adjusted $y$-values (that would work but wastes time and the examiner has signalled "don't").

Why this method. Linearity of the integral lets you split a sum/difference inside the integral into two separate integrals. The second piece $\int_{-1}^{1} 2\,dx$ is exact — it's just "constant times width" — so no estimation needed.

State the tool. Linearity:

$$\int_{a}^{b}\bigl(f(x) + k\bigr)\,dx \;=\; \int_{a}^{b} f(x)\,dx \;+\; k(b-a) \qquad (k\text{ constant})$$

Apply with $k = -2$, $a = -1$, $b = 1$, so $b - a = 2$:

$$\int_{-1}^{1}\bigl(f(x) - 2\bigr)\,dx \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx \;-\; 2 \cdot 2 \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx \;-\; 4$$

Substitute the estimate from part (a):

$$\approx 10.52 - 4 \;=\; \boxed{6.52}$$

Sense check. Geometric picture: subtracting 2 from $f$ shifts the curve down by 2, removing a rectangle of width $(b-a) = 2$ and height 2, i.e. area $= 4$. New area $= 10.52 - 4 = 6.52$ matches ✓.

The change is additive, not multiplicative — never divide or scale the (a) answer. The width factor $(b-a) = 2$ matters: if the interval were $[0, 1]$ (width 1), you'd subtract only 2, not 4. Always recompute $(b-a)$ from the limits.
$\displaystyle\int_{-1}^{1}\bigl(f(x)-2\bigr)\,dx \approx \boxed{6.52}$

Part (b)(ii) — Estimate $\int_{1}^{3} f(x-2)\,dx$ [REDO — Phuc could not justify] [2 marks] · 2 steps

From part (a): $\displaystyle\int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx \approx 10.52$.
STEP 1/2 M1

Recognise the trigger. The integrand is $f(x-2)$ — i.e. the same function $f$ but with its argument shifted. You have no table of values for $f(x-2)$ directly, only for $f$ on $[-1, 1]$. The instruction "use your answer to part (a)" again means: relate this integral to the one from (a), don't re-tabulate.

Why this method (substitution). Two valid approaches:

(i) Geometric: $f(x-2)$ is the graph of $f$ shifted right by 2. The interval $[1, 3]$ is the original $[-1, 1]$ also shifted right by 2. A horizontal translation preserves area when the limits move with it — so the answer equals (a).

(ii) Algebraic (substitution): change variable $u = x - 2$, transforming the integral and limits.

Both reach the same conclusion. Substitution is what the mark scheme rewards — it forces you to show why the area is preserved, rather than asserting it.

State the tool.

$$\int_{a}^{b} f(x - c)\,dx \;=\; \int_{a-c}^{b-c} f(u)\,du \qquad (\text{let } u = x - c,\; du = dx)$$

Apply with $c = 2$, $a = 1$, $b = 3$. Let $u = x - 2$. Then $du = dx$, and the limits transform:

$$x = 1 \;\Longrightarrow\; u = 1 - 2 = -1$$

$$x = 3 \;\Longrightarrow\; u = 3 - 2 = 1$$

The integral rewrites as

$$\int_{1}^{3} f(x - 2)\,dx \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1} f(u)\,du$$

This is the move you missed under pressure. The trap is thinking you need to evaluate $f$ at shifted $x$-values from the table — but the table only knows $f$ on $[-1, 1]$, and outside that you have no information. Substitution sidesteps this entirely: the integral on the right uses $f$ on $[-1, 1]$, which is exactly what (a) gave you. The arrow you drew on the exam was correct intuition; the substitution writes it down formally.
$\displaystyle\int_{1}^{3} f(x - 2)\,dx \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1} f(u)\,du$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $\displaystyle\int_{1}^{3} f(x-2)\,dx \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1} f(u)\,du$.
STEP 2/2 A1

Recognise. The integral $\int_{-1}^{1} f(u)\,du$ is identical to $\int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx$ from part (a) — the symbol $u$ vs $x$ is just a dummy variable, it doesn't affect the value. (You could relabel $u \to x$ everywhere with no change in meaning.)

Apply.

$$\int_{1}^{3} f(x - 2)\,dx \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1} f(u)\,du \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx \;\approx\; \boxed{10.52}$$

Sense check via the geometric picture (now that we have the answer). The graph of $f(x - 2)$ is the graph of $f$ slid 2 units right. The strip from $x = 1$ to $x = 3$ under $f(x - 2)$ is congruent (same shape, same width, same heights) to the strip from $x = -1$ to $x = 1$ under $f$. Therefore the areas are equal — which is exactly what the algebra produced. ✓

What you couldn't articulate before: the limits shifting in step with the function is exactly what makes the area invariant. If the limits had stayed at $[-1, 1]$ while $f$ was shifted (i.e. you were asked for $\int_{-1}^{1} f(x - 2)\,dx$), the integral would not equal (a) — you'd be integrating a shifted $f$ over the original window, capturing a different region. The matching shift in both function and limits is the whole point.
$\displaystyle\int_{1}^{3} f(x-2)\,dx \approx \boxed{10.52}$
