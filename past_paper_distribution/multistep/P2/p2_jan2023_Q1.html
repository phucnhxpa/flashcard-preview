Q1 — Trapezium rule + integral transformations on $f(x)$

Question

Figure 1 shows a sketch of part of the curve with equation $y = f(x)$. The table below shows some corresponding values of $x$ and $y$ for this curve (values of $y$ given to 3 d.p.):

$$\begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|c|c|} \hline x & -1 & -0.5 & 0 & 0.5 & 1 \\ \hline y & 2.287 & 4.470 & 6.719 & 7.291 & 2.834 \\ \hline \end{array}$$

Using the trapezium rule with all the values of $y$ in the given table,

(a) obtain an estimate for $\displaystyle\int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx$, giving your answer to 2 decimal places. (3)

(b) Use your answer to part (a) to estimate

$\quad$(i) $\displaystyle\int_{-1}^{1}\bigl(f(x)-2\bigr)\,dx$

$\quad$(ii) $\displaystyle\int_{1}^{3} f(x-2)\,dx$ (3)

[6 marks]

Part (a) — Trapezium rule estimate of $\int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx$ [3 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2 M1

Formula (trapezium rule): for $n$ strips of equal width $h$,

$$\int_{a}^{b} y\,dx \;\approx\; \tfrac{h}{2}\bigl[y_0 + y_n + 2(y_1+y_2+\cdots+y_{n-1})\bigr]$$

Identify the strip width $h$ from the table — the $x$-values $-1, -0.5, 0, 0.5, 1$ are equally spaced, so

$$h = -0.5 - (-1) = 0.5, \qquad n = 4 \text{ strips}.$$

Identify the endpoints and interior ordinates from the table:

$$y_0 = 2.287,\quad y_4 = 2.834,\qquad y_1=4.470,\; y_2=6.719,\; y_3=7.291.$$

5 $y$-values $\Rightarrow$ 4 strips, not 5. Counting strips wrong is the classic trap — always do $n = (\text{number of }y\text{-values}) - 1$. Equal spacing is required; check $h$ is constant before applying the formula.
$h = 0.5$, $n = 4$ strips; endpoints $y_0=2.287$, $y_4=2.834$; interior $4.470, 6.719, 7.291$.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $h=0.5$; endpoints $y_0=2.287,\,y_4=2.834$; interior $y_1=4.470,\,y_2=6.719,\,y_3=7.291$.
STEP 2/2 M1 A1

Substitute into the trapezium rule formula from STEP 1:

$$\int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx \;\approx\; \tfrac{0.5}{2}\bigl[2.287 + 2.834 + 2(4.470 + 6.719 + 7.291)\bigr]$$

Compute the interior sum first, then the bracket:

$$4.470 + 6.719 + 7.291 = 18.480$$

$$2.287 + 2.834 + 2(18.480) = 5.121 + 36.960 = 42.081$$

Multiply by $\tfrac{h}{2}=0.25$:

$$0.25 \times 42.081 = 10.52025$$

Round to 2 d.p. as required:

$$\boxed{\int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx \;\approx\; 10.52}$$

Sense check: average $y \approx 10.52/2 = 5.26$ over width 2 — sits between min $2.287$ and max $7.291$, ✓. Watch the rounding: keep all decimals until the final step, then round once. The mark scheme gives A1 only for 10.52 (not 10.5).
$\displaystyle\int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx \approx \boxed{10.52}$

Part (b)(i) — Estimate $\int_{-1}^{1}\bigl(f(x)-2\bigr)\,dx$ [1 marks] · 1 step

From part (a): $\displaystyle\int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx \approx 10.52$.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (linearity of the integral): for any constant $k$,

$$\int_{a}^{b}\bigl(f(x)+k\bigr)\,dx \;=\; \int_{a}^{b} f(x)\,dx \;+\; k(b-a)$$

Apply with $k=-2$, $a=-1$, $b=1$, so $b-a = 2$:

$$\int_{-1}^{1}\bigl(f(x)-2\bigr)\,dx \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx \;-\; 2(2) \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx \;-\; 4$$

Substitute the estimate from part (a):

$$\approx 10.52 - 4 \;=\; \boxed{6.52}$$

Geometric picture: subtracting 2 from $f(x)$ shifts the curve down by 2, removing a rectangle of width $(b-a)=2$ and height 2, i.e. area $= 4$. Don't multiply the (a) answer by anything — the change is additive.
$\displaystyle\int_{-1}^{1}\bigl(f(x)-2\bigr)\,dx \approx \boxed{6.52}$

Part (b)(ii) — Estimate $\int_{1}^{3} f(x-2)\,dx$ [REDO — Phuc could not justify] [2 marks] · 2 steps

From part (a): $\displaystyle\int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx \approx 10.52$.
STEP 1/2 M1

Substitution rule for translated integrals:

$$\int_{a}^{b} f(x-c)\,dx \;=\; \int_{a-c}^{b-c} f(u)\,du \quad(\text{let } u = x-c)$$

Here $c=2$, $a=1$, $b=3$. Let $u = x-2$, so $du = dx$, and the limits transform:

$$x = 1 \;\Longrightarrow\; u = -1, \qquad x = 3 \;\Longrightarrow\; u = 1$$

The integral rewrites as

$$\int_{1}^{3} f(x-2)\,dx \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1} f(u)\,du$$

This is the recall you missed. The substitution is bookkeeping — the function $f$ is unchanged, only the dummy variable. Don't try to compute $f(x-2)$ at the table values; you only have $f$ tabulated on $[-1,1]$, not on $[1,3]$.
$\displaystyle\int_{1}^{3} f(x-2)\,dx \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1} f(u)\,du$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $\displaystyle\int_{1}^{3} f(x-2)\,dx = \int_{-1}^{1} f(u)\,du$.
STEP 2/2 A1

Recognise that $\int_{-1}^{1} f(u)\,du$ is identical (just a relabelling of the dummy variable) to $\int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx$, which was estimated in part (a).

Therefore

$$\int_{1}^{3} f(x-2)\,dx \;=\; \int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx \;\approx\; \boxed{10.52}$$

Geometric picture (Phuc's instinct was right): the graph of $f(x-2)$ is $f$ translated 2 units to the right. The interval $[1,3]$ is the original $[-1,1]$ also shifted right by 2. Area is preserved by a horizontal translation — so the value must equal part (a). What you couldn't articulate before: the limits shifting in step with the function is exactly what makes the area invariant. If the limits had stayed at $[-1,1]$, the area would not equal part (a).
$\displaystyle\int_{1}^{3} f(x-2)\,dx \approx \boxed{10.52}$
