Q4(ii) — $\sum_{r=1}^{2} \log_a(y^r) \,=\, \sum_{r=1}^{2} (\log_a y)^r$ — find $y$ in terms of $a$

Question

(ii) Given that

$$\sum_{r=1}^{2} \log_a(y^r) \;=\; \sum_{r=1}^{2} (\log_a y)^r \qquad y > 1,\; a > 1,\; y \ne a$$

find $y$ in terms of $a$, giving your answer in simplest form. (3)

Scope of this card: part (ii) only. Phuc's slack note (verbatim): "$(\log_a^p)^2$ is not the same as $\log_a x^2$" — the trap this card is built around.

[3 marks]

Part (ii) — Expand both sigmas; solve for $\log_a y$ [3 marks] · 3 steps

Distinguish carefully: $\log_a(y^r)$ (argument raised to $r$, log brings it out) vs $(\log_a y)^r$ (the entire log expression raised to $r$). These are not the same.
STEP 1/3 M1 — expand LHS via power law

Recognise the trigger. Two sigma notations with $r = 1, 2$ — just write the two terms of each side explicitly. This is the safest move because the LHS and RHS look superficially similar but use the logarithm laws differently.

State the tool (LHS). The power law of logarithms:

$$\log_a(y^r) \;=\; r \log_a y \qquad (a, y > 0, \; a \ne 1).$$

The exponent $r$ on the argument $y$ comes down as a multiplier in front of the log.

Apply to LHS. Expand the sum $r = 1, 2$:

$$\text{LHS} \;=\; \log_a(y^1) \;+\; \log_a(y^2)$$

$$\;=\; 1 \cdot \log_a y \;+\; 2 \cdot \log_a y \;=\; 3 \log_a y.$$

So the entire left-hand side collapses to a single multiple of $\log_a y$.

Substitution to clean things up. Let $L = \log_a y$. Then LHS $= 3L$.

Phuc's confusion (from the slack note): "$(\log_a^p)^2$ is not the same as $\log_a x^2$". The power law $\log_a(y^r) = r \log_a y$ applies only when the exponent is on the argument inside the log. If you mistakenly bring $r$ down on the RHS too — i.e. write $(\log_a y)^r = r \log_a y$ — you've made exactly this error. They're genuinely different objects: $(\log_a y)^2$ is a number times itself, while $\log_a(y^2)$ is a log of a different argument. The shorthand notation $\log^2 a$ used by some textbooks is what causes this trap.
LHS $\;=\; 3 \log_a y \;=\; 3L$ (where $L := \log_a y$).
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: LHS $= 3L$ where $L = \log_a y$.
STEP 2/3 M1 — expand RHS

Recognise the trigger. The RHS is structurally different. The exponent $r$ is now outside the log:

$$(\log_a y)^r \;\equiv\; \underbrace{(\log_a y) \cdot (\log_a y) \cdots (\log_a y)}_{r \text{ factors}}.$$

The power law does not apply here, because the exponent $r$ is not on $y$ inside the log — it's on the entire expression $\log_a y$, treating it as a number. There's no logarithm identity that simplifies $(\log_a y)^2$ — you have to leave it as a square.

Apply. Expand the RHS sum $r = 1, 2$:

$$\text{RHS} \;=\; (\log_a y)^1 \;+\; (\log_a y)^2 \;=\; \log_a y \;+\; (\log_a y)^2.$$

In substituted form: RHS $= L + L^2$.

Set up the equation. Now combine LHS and RHS into one equation in the single unknown $L$:

$$\underbrace{3L}_{\text{LHS}} \;=\; \underbrace{L + L^2}_{\text{RHS}}.$$

Rearrange to standard form. Bring all terms to one side:

$$L^2 \;+\; L \;-\; 3L \;=\; 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; L^2 \;-\; 2L \;=\; 0.$$

Factor:

$$L(L - 2) \;=\; 0.$$

The substitution $L = \log_a y$ is the technique that protects you from the trap. By replacing the log with a single letter, the equation becomes a clean quadratic in $L$ — and you immediately see that LHS and RHS are different polynomial expressions in $L$. Without the substitution, students sometimes "simplify" $(\log_a y)^2$ to $\log_a(y^2) = 2\log_a y$ and end up with $3L = L + 2L$, i.e. $3L = 3L$ — a trivial identity that gives no information about $y$. The fact that you derive a quadratic is the signal that you've handled the two sides correctly.
Equation in $L = \log_a y$: $\;L^2 - 2L = 0 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; L(L-2)=0$.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 2: $L(L-2)=0$ with $L = \log_a y$, given $y > 1, a > 1, y \ne a$.
STEP 3/3 A1 — solve and reject

Read off the candidate roots.

$$L = 0 \quad \text{or} \quad L = 2.$$

Reject the trivial root. $L = 0$ means $\log_a y = 0$, i.e. $y = a^0 = 1$. But the problem states $y > 1$, so $y = 1$ is excluded. Discard $L = 0$.

Take the valid root. $L = 2$ means $\log_a y = 2$, which by the definition of logarithm gives

$$y \;=\; a^2.$$

Verify the remaining constraint. The problem also stipulates $y \ne a$. With $y = a^2$, equality $y = a$ would force $a^2 = a$, i.e. $a(a-1) = 0$, so $a = 0$ or $a = 1$. But $a > 1$ is given, so $y = a^2 \ne a$ automatically ✓. The condition $y > 1$ also holds: $a > 1 \Longrightarrow a^2 > 1$ ✓.

$$\boxed{\,y \;=\; a^{2}\,}$$

Sense check. Substitute back: $\log_a y = \log_a(a^2) = 2$. LHS $= \log_a(a^2) + \log_a(a^4) = 2 + 4 = 6$. RHS $= 2 + 2^2 = 2 + 4 = 6$ ✓.

Phuc's slack note nails the exact confusion: "$(\log_a{}^p)^2$ is not the same as $\log_a x^2$". On this question, that distinction is the entire point — getting two distinct algebraic forms (linear on LHS, quadratic on RHS) is what forces $L = 2$. If you collapsed both sides via the power law, you'd get a vacuous identity. The general lesson: always check whether the exponent is on the argument (inside the log) or on the whole log expression (outside) before applying any log identity. Treat $\log_a y$ as a single algebraic letter — that mental move resolves 90% of "log of squared / squared log" confusions.
$\boxed{y = a^2}$ (rejecting $L=0$ since $y > 1$).
