Q9 — Ratio of two integration regions $R_1/R_2$ on $y = x^2-4x+5$

Question

In this question you must show all stages of your working. Solutions based entirely on calculator technology are not acceptable.

Figure 3 shows

  • the curve $C$ with equation $y = x^2 - 4x + 5$
  • the line $l$ with equation $y = 2$

The curve $C$ intersects the $y$-axis at the point $D$.

(a) Write down the coordinates of $D$. (1)

The curve $C$ intersects the line $l$ at the points $E$ and $F$, as shown in Figure 3.

(b) Find the $x$-coordinate of $E$ and the $x$-coordinate of $F$. (2)

Shown shaded in Figure 3 is

  • the region $R_1$ which is bounded by $C$, $l$ and the $y$-axis
  • the region $R_2$ which is bounded by $C$ and the line segments $EF$ and $DF$

Given that $\dfrac{\text{area of }R_1}{\text{area of }R_2} = k$, where $k$ is a constant,

(c) use algebraic integration to find the exact value of $k$, giving your answer as a simplified fraction. (5)

[8 marks]

Part (a) — Coordinates of $D$ [1 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 B1

Recognise the trigger. "Intersects the $y$-axis" $\iff$ "$x = 0$". The $y$-axis is the line $x = 0$, so the intersection point has $x$-coordinate zero.

Apply. Substitute $x = 0$ into $y = x^2 - 4x + 5$:

$$y \;=\; 0 - 0 + 5 \;=\; 5$$

$$\boxed{D \;=\; (0,\,5)}$$

Sense check. Figure 3 shows $D$ above the line $y = 2$; we got $y = 5 > 2$, ✓.

For "write down" questions, no extended working is needed — but it's still worth one line of substitution so you don't silently mis-read the question (e.g. confusing $y$-axis with $x$-axis). Costs 5 seconds, prevents a 1-mark giveaway loss.
$\boxed{D = (0,\,5)}$

Part (b) — $x$-coordinates of $E$ and $F$ [2 marks] · 1 step

From part (a): $D = (0,5)$. Curve $C:\, y = x^2 - 4x + 5$; line $l:\, y = 2$.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Recognise the trigger. "$C$ intersects $l$" $\iff$ "the two equations have the same $y$ at the same $x$". The intersection condition is: set the $y$-values equal and solve for $x$.

Why this method. Only one route here — substitution/equating. Don't try to read coordinates off Figure 3; the question is asking you to prove them.

Apply. Set the two $y$-expressions equal:

$$x^2 - 4x + 5 \;=\; 2$$

Move everything to the left to get a standard quadratic $= 0$:

$$x^2 - 4x + 3 \;=\; 0$$

Check the discriminant before choosing a solve method: $b^2 - 4ac = 16 - 12 = 4 = 2^2$, perfect square, so it factorises cleanly. Look for two numbers multiplying to $+3$ and adding to $-4$: that's $-1$ and $-3$.

$$(x - 1)(x - 3) \;=\; 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; x = 1 \;\text{or}\; x = 3$$

Match labels to the figure. Figure 3 shows $E$ to the left of $F$, so $E$ gets the smaller $x$-value:

$$\boxed{x_E \;=\; 1,\quad x_F \;=\; 3}$$

(Full coordinates: $E(1, 2)$, $F(3, 2)$.)

Sense check. Both points should be on the line $l: y = 2$ — they are, by construction ✓. Substitute back into $C$: $C(1) = 1 - 4 + 5 = 2$ ✓; $C(3) = 9 - 12 + 5 = 2$ ✓.

Always label which root is $E$ and which is $F$ by inspecting the figure — examiners deduct A1 for unlabelled or swapped values even when the algebra is correct. The discriminant pre-check ($= 4$, perfect square) tells you factorisation will work; if you ever see a non-square discriminant on a P2 "find the intersection" question, recheck the equation setup before reaching for the quadratic formula.
$\boxed{x_E \;=\; 1,\; x_F \;=\; 3}$

Part (c) — Find $k = \text{area}(R_1)/\text{area}(R_2)$ [REDO — Phuc tried triangle formula for $R_2$, but $R_2$ has a curved boundary] [5 marks] · 4 steps

From parts (a) + (b): $D=(0,5)$, $E=(1,2)$, $F=(3,2)$, curve $C: y=x^2-4x+5$, line $l: y=2$. $R_1$ sits between $C$ (above), $l$ (below) and the $y$-axis, on $0 \le x \le 1$. $R_2$ sits between line segment $DF$ (top, on $0\le x \le 1$) and segment $EF$ (top, on $1\le x \le 3$), and the curve $C$ (bottom) — so it must be split at $x=1$ where the top boundary changes.
STEP 1/4 M1 A1

Recognise the trigger. "Use algebraic integration" + a region bounded by a curve and a line. The standard recipe is: identify top and bottom on the interval, then integrate (top $-$ bottom). The instruction "algebraic integration" specifically rules out calculator-only solutions — show all working.

Why this method. The diagram makes $R_1$ unambiguous — it's a single region with curve $C$ on top, line $l$ on the bottom, between $x = 0$ (the $y$-axis) and $x = 1$ (point $E$). No splitting needed; one integral does it.

Identify top and bottom on $[0, 1]$. Pick a test $x$ in the interval, say $x = 0$: $C(0) = 5$, $l = 2$. $C > l$, so $C$ is on top, $l$ on bottom. (You can also see this directly from Figure 3.)

State the tool.

$$\text{Area} \;=\; \int_{a}^{b}\bigl(\text{top}(x) - \text{bottom}(x)\bigr)\,dx$$

Apply with top $= x^2 - 4x + 5$, bottom $= 2$, $a = 0$, $b = 1$:

$$\text{Area}(R_1) \;=\; \int_{0}^{1}\bigl((x^2 - 4x + 5) - 2\bigr)\,dx \;=\; \int_{0}^{1}(x^2 - 4x + 3)\,dx$$

Integrate term-by-term using $\displaystyle\int x^n\,dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}$:

$$= \left[\frac{x^3}{3} - \frac{4x^2}{2} + 3x\right]_{0}^{1} \;=\; \left[\frac{x^3}{3} - 2x^2 + 3x\right]_{0}^{1}$$

Evaluate at the upper and lower limits separately:

$$\text{At } x = 1:\; \tfrac{1}{3} - 2 + 3 \;=\; \tfrac{1}{3} + 1 \;=\; \tfrac{4}{3}$$

$$\text{At } x = 0:\; 0 - 0 + 0 \;=\; 0$$

Subtract:

$$\text{Area}(R_1) \;=\; \tfrac{4}{3} - 0 \;=\; \boxed{\tfrac{4}{3}}$$

Sense check. $R_1$ is a thin sliver: width 1, max height $5 - 2 = 3$, so a bounding rectangle has area 3. Our answer $\tfrac{4}{3} \approx 1.33$ is comfortably less than 3 and more than half of 3 (since the curve bulges upward), ✓.

Always pick a test point to confirm which curve is on top — guessing from the figure causes sign-flipped integrals. The integrand $(x^2 - 4x + 5) - 2 = x^2 - 4x + 3$ factorises as $(x-1)(x-3)$ — that's why it's zero at $x=1$ (the curve meets the line at $E$) and would be zero at $x=3$ too. Coincidence? No — that's where $C$ and $l$ intersect.
$\text{Area}(R_1) \;=\; \boxed{\tfrac{4}{3}}$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $\text{Area}(R_1)=\tfrac{4}{3}$. Goal: get the first piece of $\text{Area}(R_2)$ on $[0,1]$ (under segment $DF$, above curve $C$).
STEP 2/4 M1

Recognise the trigger. $R_2$ has a piecewise-linear top: segment $DF$ from $x = 0$ to $x = 3$, segment $EF$ from $x = 1$ to $x = 3$ — and the corners at $E$ and $F$ mean the active top boundary changes at $x = 1$. Whenever the top function changes, split the integral at the change-point.

Why split, not triangle? Phuc's earlier slip: tried to use a triangle-area formula. But $R_2$ has the parabola $C$ as its bottom boundary — $R_2$ is not a polygon, so $\tfrac{1}{2}\,bh$ doesn't apply. The only correct route is to set up two integrals (one on $[0, 1]$, one on $[1, 3]$) and add them.

Find the equation of line $DF$. Points $D(0, 5)$ and $F(3, 2)$:

$$m \;=\; \frac{2 - 5}{3 - 0} \;=\; \frac{-3}{3} \;=\; -1; \qquad y\text{-intercept} \;=\; 5$$

So $DF$ has equation $y = -x + 5$.

Identify top/bottom on $[0, 1]$. Test at $x = 0$: $DF = 5$, $C = 5$ — they meet at $D$ (no preference yet). Test at $x = 1$: $DF = 4$, $C = 1 - 4 + 5 = 2$ — $DF$ is above $C$. So on $(0, 1]$, $DF$ is top, $C$ is bottom.

State + apply.

$$\text{Area}_{[0,1]} \;=\; \int_{0}^{1}\bigl(DF - C\bigr)\,dx \;=\; \int_{0}^{1}\bigl((-x + 5) - (x^2 - 4x + 5)\bigr)\,dx$$

Simplify the integrand — note the $+5$ and $-5$ cancel:

$$(-x + 5) - (x^2 - 4x + 5) \;=\; -x + 5 - x^2 + 4x - 5 \;=\; -x^2 + 3x$$

$$\text{Area}_{[0,1]} \;=\; \int_{0}^{1}(-x^2 + 3x)\,dx \;=\; \left[-\tfrac{x^3}{3} + \tfrac{3x^2}{2}\right]_{0}^{1}$$

Evaluate:

$$\text{At } x = 1:\; -\tfrac{1}{3} + \tfrac{3}{2} \;=\; \frac{-2 + 9}{6} \;=\; \tfrac{7}{6}$$

$$\text{At } x = 0:\; 0$$

$$\text{Area}_{[0,1]}(R_2) \;=\; \tfrac{7}{6}$$

Equation of a line through two known points: gradient first, then intercept. Don't try to guess $y = -x + 5$ by eye — write the gradient calculation. The clean integrand $-x^2 + 3x$ (no constant term) is the examiner's reward for setting up correctly — if you get a constant left over, you've slipped a sign in the expansion.
$\text{Area}_{[0,1]}(R_2) \;=\; \tfrac{7}{6}$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 2: $\text{Area}_{[0,1]}(R_2)=\tfrac{7}{6}$. Now compute $\text{Area}_{[1,3]}$ under segment $EF$ ($y=2$), above $C$.
STEP 3/4 M1

Recognise. On $[1, 3]$, the top of $R_2$ is segment $EF$, which lies on the line $l: y = 2$. The bottom is still curve $C$.

Identify top/bottom on $[1, 3]$. Test at $x = 2$: $l = 2$, $C = 4 - 8 + 5 = 1$. $l > C$ ✓.

Apply.

$$\text{Area}_{[1,3]} \;=\; \int_{1}^{3}\bigl(l - C\bigr)\,dx \;=\; \int_{1}^{3}\bigl(2 - (x^2 - 4x + 5)\bigr)\,dx$$

Simplify the integrand:

$$2 - (x^2 - 4x + 5) \;=\; 2 - x^2 + 4x - 5 \;=\; -x^2 + 4x - 3$$

$$\text{Area}_{[1,3]} \;=\; \int_{1}^{3}(-x^2 + 4x - 3)\,dx \;=\; \left[-\tfrac{x^3}{3} + 2x^2 - 3x\right]_{1}^{3}$$

Evaluate at each limit separately — this is where most sign errors happen, so be deliberate:

$$\text{At } x = 3:\; -\tfrac{27}{3} + 2(9) - 9 \;=\; -9 + 18 - 9 \;=\; 0$$

$$\text{At } x = 1:\; -\tfrac{1}{3} + 2 - 3 \;=\; -\tfrac{1}{3} - 1 \;=\; -\tfrac{4}{3}$$

Subtract — and beware the double negative:

$$\text{Area}_{[1,3]} \;=\; 0 - \bigl(-\tfrac{4}{3}\bigr) \;=\; +\tfrac{4}{3}$$

Sense check. The integrand $-x^2 + 4x - 3 = -(x-1)(x-3)$ — non-negative on $[1, 3]$ (parabola opens downward with roots at the limits) — so the integral must be positive. We got $+\tfrac{4}{3}$ ✓.

Sign trap: when the upper-limit value is $0$, students sometimes write the answer as $0$ — but you must subtract the lower-limit value, which here is negative, so the answer ends up positive. Always write both substitutions on their own lines before the subtraction line.
$\text{Area}_{[1,3]}(R_2) \;=\; \tfrac{4}{3}$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEPS 1–3: $\text{Area}(R_1)=\tfrac{4}{3}$; $\text{Area}_{[0,1]}(R_2)=\tfrac{7}{6}$; $\text{Area}_{[1,3]}(R_2)=\tfrac{4}{3}$.
STEP 4/4 A1

Recognise. Final step has two micro-tasks: (i) add the two $R_2$ pieces; (ii) form the ratio $R_1 / R_2$ and simplify.

Combine $R_2$. Common denominator 6:

$$\text{Area}(R_2) \;=\; \tfrac{7}{6} + \tfrac{4}{3} \;=\; \tfrac{7}{6} + \tfrac{8}{6} \;=\; \tfrac{15}{6} \;=\; \tfrac{5}{2}$$

(Simplify $\tfrac{15}{6}$ to $\tfrac{5}{2}$ — both terms divisible by 3.)

Form the ratio.

$$k \;=\; \frac{\text{Area}(R_1)}{\text{Area}(R_2)} \;=\; \frac{\,4/3\,}{\,5/2\,}$$

Divide fractions by multiplying by the reciprocal of the denominator:

$$k \;=\; \tfrac{4}{3} \times \tfrac{2}{5} \;=\; \frac{4 \times 2}{3 \times 5} \;=\; \boxed{\tfrac{8}{15}}$$

Check simplification. $\gcd(8, 15) = 1$ — already in lowest terms ✓. The question demands a simplified fraction, so this satisfies the form requirement.

Sense check on magnitude. From the diagram, $R_1$ is the small sliver, $R_2$ is the bigger region. Ratio $R_1 / R_2$ should be less than 1; $\tfrac{8}{15} \approx 0.53$, so $R_2$ is roughly twice $R_1$, consistent with the figure ✓.

Phuc's slip (per notes): tried base × height for $R_2$. $R_2$ has the parabola as its bottom boundary — it's not a polygon, so the triangle formula doesn't apply. The correct strategy: split at $x = 1$ (where the active top boundary changes from line $DF$ to line $EF$) and integrate each piece. An alternative "proxy" method is trapezium $DOEF$ minus area under $C$ from 0 to 3 — gives the same answer but is more error-prone because it involves an extra polygon-area calculation. Top$-$bottom integration on each sub-interval is the safer, syllabus-aligned route.
$\boxed{k \;=\; \tfrac{8}{15}}$
