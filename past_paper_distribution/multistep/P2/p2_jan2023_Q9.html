Q9 — Ratio of two integration regions $R_1/R_2$ on $y = x^2-4x+5$

Question

In this question you must show all stages of your working. Solutions based entirely on calculator technology are not acceptable.

Figure 3 shows

  • the curve $C$ with equation $y = x^2 - 4x + 5$
  • the line $l$ with equation $y = 2$

The curve $C$ intersects the $y$-axis at the point $D$.

(a) Write down the coordinates of $D$. (1)

The curve $C$ intersects the line $l$ at the points $E$ and $F$, as shown in Figure 3.

(b) Find the $x$-coordinate of $E$ and the $x$-coordinate of $F$. (2)

Shown shaded in Figure 3 is

  • the region $R_1$ which is bounded by $C$, $l$ and the $y$-axis
  • the region $R_2$ which is bounded by $C$ and the line segments $EF$ and $DF$

Given that $\dfrac{\text{area of }R_1}{\text{area of }R_2} = k$, where $k$ is a constant,

(c) use algebraic integration to find the exact value of $k$, giving your answer as a simplified fraction. (5)

[8 marks]

Part (a) — Coordinates of $D$ [1 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 B1

$D$ is where curve $C$ crosses the $y$-axis, i.e. where $x = 0$.

Substitute $x=0$ into $y = x^2 - 4x + 5$:

$$y = 0 - 0 + 5 = 5$$

$$\boxed{D = (0,\,5)}$$

For "write down" questions, no working is needed beyond the substitution — but always sanity-check that the diagram shows $D$ above the line $y=2$ (it does: 5 > 2). One-mark warm-up; don't overthink.
$\boxed{D = (0,5)}$

Part (b) — $x$-coordinates of $E$ and $F$ [2 marks] · 1 step

From part (a): $D = (0,5)$. Curve $C: y = x^2-4x+5$; line $l: y=2$.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Strategy: $E$ and $F$ are the intersections of $C$ and $l$, so set the $y$-values equal.

$$x^2 - 4x + 5 = 2 \;\Longrightarrow\; x^2 - 4x + 3 = 0$$

Factorise:

$$(x - 1)(x - 3) = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; x = 1 \text{ or } x = 3$$

From Figure 3, $E$ lies left of $F$, so

$$\boxed{x_E = 1,\quad x_F = 3}$$

(Full coordinates: $E(1,2)$, $F(3,2)$.)

Always label which intersection is which by inspecting the figure — examiners sometimes deduct A1 for unlabelled or swapped values. Factorising $x^2-4x+3$ is faster than the quadratic formula here; discriminant is $16-12=4$ which is a perfect square — the "this factors nicely" tell again.
$\boxed{x_E = 1,\; x_F = 3}$

Part (c) — Find $k = \text{area}(R_1)/\text{area}(R_2)$ [REDO — Phuc tried triangle formula for $R_2$, but $R_2$ has a curved boundary] [5 marks] · 4 steps

From parts (a)+(b): $D=(0,5)$, $E=(1,2)$, $F=(3,2)$, curve $C: y=x^2-4x+5$, line $l: y=2$. $R_1$ sits between $C$ (above), $l$ (below) and the $y$-axis, on $0 \le x \le 1$. $R_2$ sits between line segment $DF$ (top, $0\le x \le 1$) and segment $EF$ (top, $1\le x \le 3$), and the curve $C$ (bottom), and so must be split at $x=1$.
STEP 1/4 M1 A1

Formula (area between two curves):

$$\text{Area} \;=\; \int_{a}^{b}\bigl(\text{top}(x) - \text{bottom}(x)\bigr)\,dx$$

For $R_1$ on $[0,1]$: top is $C$ (since at $x=0$, $C$ gives $y=5 > 2$), bottom is $l$. So

$$\text{Area}(R_1) \;=\; \int_{0}^{1}\bigl((x^2-4x+5) - 2\bigr)\,dx \;=\; \int_{0}^{1}(x^2 - 4x + 3)\,dx$$

Integrate term-by-term using $\int x^n\,dx = \dfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}$:

$$\left[\tfrac{x^3}{3} - 2x^2 + 3x\right]_{0}^{1} \;=\; \Bigl(\tfrac{1}{3} - 2 + 3\Bigr) - 0 \;=\; \tfrac{1}{3} + 1 \;=\; \tfrac{4}{3}$$

$$\boxed{\text{Area}(R_1) = \tfrac{4}{3}}$$

Check the sign: $R_1$ is above $l$ and below $C$, so top$-$bottom $= C - l$ is positive on $[0,1]$ ✓. Always check which curve is on top by plugging a sample $x$ — at $x=0$, $C = 5 > l = 2$.
$\text{Area}(R_1) = \boxed{\tfrac{4}{3}}$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $\text{Area}(R_1)=\tfrac{4}{3}$. Goal: get $\text{Area}(R_2)$ on $[0,1]$ (under segment $DF$).
STEP 2/4 M1

Equation of line $DF$ through $D(0,5)$ and $F(3,2)$:

$$m = \frac{2-5}{3-0} = -1; \quad y\text{-intercept} = 5 \;\Longrightarrow\; y = -x + 5$$

On $[0,1]$, $R_2$ has top $= DF$ and bottom $= C$. Check at $x=0$: $DF=5, C=5$ — they meet at $D$. At $x=1$: $DF = 4, C = 2$ — $DF$ above $C$ ✓.

$$\text{Area}_{[0,1]} \;=\; \int_{0}^{1}\bigl((-x+5) - (x^2-4x+5)\bigr)\,dx \;=\; \int_{0}^{1}(-x^2 + 3x)\,dx$$

$$= \left[-\tfrac{x^3}{3} + \tfrac{3x^2}{2}\right]_{0}^{1} = -\tfrac{1}{3} + \tfrac{3}{2} = \tfrac{-2+9}{6} = \tfrac{7}{6}$$

Equation of line through two points: gradient first, then intercept. Don't try to spot $y=-x+5$ by eye — write it out. The combined integrand $(-x+5)-(x^2-4x+5) = -x^2+3x$ has the $+5$ and $-5$ killing each other — that's a clean form to integrate.
$\text{Area}_{[0,1]}(R_2) = \tfrac{7}{6}$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 2: $\text{Area}_{[0,1]}(R_2)=\tfrac{7}{6}$. Now compute $\text{Area}_{[1,3]}$ under segment $EF$ ($y=2$), above $C$.
STEP 3/4 M1

On $[1,3]$, $R_2$ has top $= EF$ (the line $l: y=2$) and bottom $= C$. Check at $x=2$: $l=2, C=2^2-8+5=1$ — $l$ above $C$ ✓.

$$\text{Area}_{[1,3]} \;=\; \int_{1}^{3}\bigl(2 - (x^2 - 4x + 5)\bigr)\,dx \;=\; \int_{1}^{3}(-x^2 + 4x - 3)\,dx$$

Integrate:

$$\left[-\tfrac{x^3}{3} + 2x^2 - 3x\right]_{1}^{3}$$

Evaluate at $x=3$: $-9 + 18 - 9 = 0$.

Evaluate at $x=1$: $-\tfrac{1}{3} + 2 - 3 = -\tfrac{4}{3}$.

$$\text{Area}_{[1,3]} \;=\; 0 - \bigl(-\tfrac{4}{3}\bigr) \;=\; \tfrac{4}{3}$$

Sign trap: when the upper limit gives $0$, students sometimes write the answer as 0 — but you must subtract the lower-limit value. Always write both substitutions out separately before subtracting.
$\text{Area}_{[1,3]}(R_2) = \tfrac{4}{3}$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEPS 1–3: $\text{Area}(R_1)=\tfrac{4}{3}$; $\text{Area}_{[0,1]}(R_2)=\tfrac{7}{6}$; $\text{Area}_{[1,3]}(R_2)=\tfrac{4}{3}$.
STEP 4/4 A1

Combine the two pieces of $R_2$:

$$\text{Area}(R_2) \;=\; \tfrac{7}{6} + \tfrac{4}{3} \;=\; \tfrac{7}{6} + \tfrac{8}{6} \;=\; \tfrac{15}{6} \;=\; \tfrac{5}{2}$$

Form the ratio:

$$k \;=\; \frac{\text{Area}(R_1)}{\text{Area}(R_2)} \;=\; \frac{4/3}{5/2} \;=\; \frac{4}{3}\times\frac{2}{5} \;=\; \boxed{\tfrac{8}{15}}$$

Question requires a simplified fraction; $\gcd(8,15)=1$, so $\tfrac{8}{15}$ is already in lowest terms ✓.

Phuc's slip (per notes): you tried to compute $R_2$ as base $\times$ height of a triangle. $R_2$ has the parabola as one of its boundaries — it's not a polygon. The correct strategy is split at $x=1$ (where the top boundary changes from line $DF$ to line $EF$) and integrate each piece. The "proxy" alternative is trapezium area $DOEF$ minus area under $C$ from 0 to 3, but it's more error-prone — top$-$bottom integration is safer.
$\boxed{k = \tfrac{8}{15}}$
