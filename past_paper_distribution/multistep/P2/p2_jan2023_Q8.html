Q8 — Solve $5\sin(3x+0.1)+2=0$ and $2\tan\theta\sin\theta = 5 + \cos\theta$

Question

In this question you must show all stages of your working. Solutions based entirely on calculator technology are not acceptable.

(i) Solve, for $-\dfrac{\pi}{2} < x < \pi$, the equation

$$5 \sin(3x + 0.1) \;+\; 2 \;=\; 0,$$

giving your answers, in radians, to 2 decimal places. (4)

(ii) Solve, for $0 < \theta < 360°$, the equation

$$2 \tan\theta \sin\theta \;=\; 5 + \cos\theta,$$

giving your answers, in degrees, to one decimal place. (5)

Phuc's slack: "review the other solution of sin function, period of sin function, and know how to solve the equation b" — so both parts (i) and (ii) on this card.

[9 marks]

Part (i) — $5\sin(3x+0.1)+2=0$ for $-\tfrac{\pi}{2} < x < \pi$, to 2 d.p. [4 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3 M1 — isolate + substitute

Recognise the trigger. A trig equation with the unknown inside a compound argument $3x + 0.1$. The standard move is to isolate the trig function, introduce a substitution $u = 3x + 0.1$, solve for $u$, then back-transform to $x$. The compound argument shifts the period and translates the range, so the substitution makes the range-handling explicit.

Isolate $\sin$.

$$5\sin(3x + 0.1) \;=\; -2 \;\Longrightarrow\; \sin(3x + 0.1) \;=\; -\tfrac{2}{5} \;=\; -0.4.$$

Substitute. Let $u = 3x + 0.1$. The equation becomes

$$\sin u \;=\; -0.4.$$

Transform the range. Given $-\tfrac{\pi}{2} < x < \pi$, multiply by 3 then add 0.1:

$$3 \cdot \left(-\tfrac{\pi}{2}\right) + 0.1 \;<\; u \;<\; 3\pi + 0.1$$

$$-\tfrac{3\pi}{2} + 0.1 \;<\; u \;<\; 3\pi + 0.1$$

$$-4.612 \;<\; u \;<\; 9.525.$$

That's a $u$-range spanning more than two full periods of sine ($2\pi \approx 6.283$), so expect multiple solutions.

The single most common error on this question type is transforming the range incorrectly — students multiply by 3 but forget to add 0.1, or add 0.1 before multiplying. The fix: write the substitution $u = 3x + 0.1$ and the inverse $x = (u - 0.1)/3$ on the page, then transform the inequality step-by-step. The wide $u$-range (over two periods) is the signal that one of Phuc's slack concerns ("the other solution of sin function") matters here — there will be both an arcsin-family solution and a $\pi - $arcsin-family solution within each period.
$\sin u = -0.4$ with $-4.612 < u < 9.525$, where $u = 3x + 0.1$.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $\sin u = -0.4$, $\;-4.612 < u < 9.525$.
STEP 2/3 M1 — both families of solutions

Recognise the trigger. $\sin$ is negative, so solutions live in quadrants III and IV. Two infinite families capture every solution.

State the tool. If $\sin u = k$ and $u_0 = \arcsin k$ is the principal value ($-\tfrac{\pi}{2} \le u_0 \le \tfrac{\pi}{2}$), then the full solution set is

$$u \;=\; u_0 + 2n\pi \qquad \text{or} \qquad u \;=\; \pi - u_0 + 2n\pi, \qquad n \in \mathbb{Z}.$$

These are both needed — Phuc's slack flagged "the other solution" precisely because the $\pi - u_0$ family is the one easy to forget.

Principal value.

$$u_0 \;=\; \arcsin(-0.4) \;\approx\; -0.4115 \text{ rad (4 d.p.)}.$$

Enumerate. Add multiples of $2\pi \approx 6.2832$ to each family, keeping only those $u \in (-4.612, 9.525)$:

Family A ($u = u_0 + 2n\pi$): $n=0$ gives $u \approx -0.4115$ ✓; $n=1$ gives $u \approx 5.8717$ ✓; $n=-1$ gives $u \approx -6.695$ ✗ (outside).

Family B ($u = \pi - u_0 + 2n\pi$): $\pi - u_0 \approx 3.1416 - (-0.4115) = 3.5531$. $n=0$ gives $u \approx 3.5531$ ✓; $n=-1$ gives $u \approx -2.7301$ ✓; $n=1$ gives $u \approx 9.8363$ ✗ (just outside).

So four $u$-values: $\{-2.7301,\; -0.4115,\; 3.5531,\; 5.8717\}$.

Phuc's exact slack concern: "review the other solution of sin function, period of sin function". The "other solution" is the $\pi - u_0$ family — students who only use $\arcsin$ on a calculator get one root in each period and miss the second. The period of $\sin$ is $2\pi$, but within each period $\sin u = k$ has two solutions (one in $[-\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \tfrac{\pi}{2}]$ and its reflection about $\tfrac{\pi}{2}$). On a wide range like $(-4.612, 9.525)$ — over two periods — you should expect 4 solutions, and the count is a built-in sanity check.
Four $u$-values: $\{-2.7301,\; -0.4115,\; 3.5531,\; 5.8717\}$ (rad, 4 d.p.).
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 2: $u \in \{-2.7301,\, -0.4115,\, 3.5531,\, 5.8717\}$.
STEP 3/3 A1 A1 — back to x, round to 2 d.p.

Recognise the trigger. Convert each $u$-value back to $x$ using $x = (u - 0.1)/3$, then round to the required 2 d.p. at the very end.

Compute.

$$x_1 \;=\; \frac{-2.7301 - 0.1}{3} \;=\; \frac{-2.8301}{3} \;\approx\; -0.9434 \;\to\; -0.94$$

$$x_2 \;=\; \frac{-0.4115 - 0.1}{3} \;=\; \frac{-0.5115}{3} \;\approx\; -0.1705 \;\to\; -0.17$$

$$x_3 \;=\; \frac{3.5531 - 0.1}{3} \;=\; \frac{3.4531}{3} \;\approx\; 1.1510 \;\to\; 1.15$$

$$x_4 \;=\; \frac{5.8717 - 0.1}{3} \;=\; \frac{5.7717}{3} \;\approx\; 1.9239 \;\to\; 1.92$$

Check all four lie in $\bigl(-\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \pi\bigr) = (-1.5708, 3.1416)$: all of $\{-0.94, -0.17, 1.15, 1.92\}$ are inside ✓.

$$\boxed{\,x \;\approx\; -0.94,\; -0.17,\; 1.15,\; 1.92 \text{ (rad, 2 d.p.)}\,}$$

Two cleanup rules: (i) keep at least 4 d.p. through the intermediate steps and round only at the final line — premature rounding can push a 2 d.p. answer off by a unit in the last place; (ii) re-check the final $x$-values lie in the original $x$-range, not the $u$-range — it's easy to keep a $u$ in range but lose its $x$ after dividing by 3. Listing four answers in ascending order is conventional and helps markers verify.
$\boxed{x \approx -0.94, \; -0.17, \; 1.15, \; 1.92}$ rad (2 d.p.). All four in $(-\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \pi)$ ✓.

Part (ii) — $2\tan\theta\sin\theta = 5 + \cos\theta$ for $0 < \theta < 360°$, to 1 d.p. [5 marks] · 3 steps

Recall identities: $\tan\theta = \dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}$ and $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$.
STEP 1/3 M1 — convert tan to sin/cos, multiply

Recognise the trigger. The equation mixes $\tan$, $\sin$, $\cos$. Standard move: replace $\tan$ by $\sin/\cos$ to leave only $\sin$ and $\cos$, then use $\sin^2 + \cos^2 = 1$ to reduce to a single trig ratio.

Convert $\tan$.

$$2 \tan\theta \sin\theta \;=\; 2 \cdot \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} \cdot \sin\theta \;=\; \frac{2 \sin^2\theta}{\cos\theta}.$$

So the equation becomes

$$\frac{2 \sin^2\theta}{\cos\theta} \;=\; 5 + \cos\theta.$$

Clear the denominator. Multiply both sides by $\cos\theta$ (note: $\cos\theta \ne 0$ because $\tan\theta$ would be undefined — we must reject $\theta = 90°, 270°$ later if they appear as candidate roots):

$$2 \sin^2\theta \;=\; (5 + \cos\theta) \cos\theta \;=\; 5\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta.$$

Apply the Pythagorean identity to eliminate $\sin^2$:

$$\sin^2\theta \;=\; 1 - \cos^2\theta \;\Longrightarrow\; 2(1 - \cos^2\theta) \;=\; 5\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta.$$

Expand the left side:

$$2 - 2\cos^2\theta \;=\; 5\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta.$$

A common slip is multiplying by $\cos\theta$ and forgetting the constraint $\cos\theta \ne 0$. In this equation, the original $\tan\theta$ already forbids $\cos\theta = 0$ (which is at $\theta = 90°, 270°$), so we lose nothing by multiplying — but it's a habit worth stating explicitly. Another error: replacing $2\sin^2\theta$ with $2 - 2\cos^2\theta$ but forgetting the factor of 2 on the constant, ending up with $1 - 2\cos^2\theta$. Write the substitution as a separate line to avoid this.
$2 - 2\cos^2\theta = 5\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta$ — only $\cos\theta$ appears.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $2 - 2\cos^2\theta = 5\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta$.
STEP 2/3 M1 — quadratic in cos θ

Recognise the trigger. Collect all terms on one side to get a quadratic in $\cos\theta$.

Rearrange. Move every term to the right:

$$0 \;=\; \cos^2\theta + 2\cos^2\theta + 5\cos\theta - 2$$

$$0 \;=\; 3\cos^2\theta + 5\cos\theta - 2.$$

(Or equivalently: $3\cos^2\theta + 5\cos\theta - 2 = 0$.)

Substitute $c = \cos\theta$ to make the algebra cleaner:

$$3c^2 + 5c - 2 \;=\; 0.$$

Factorise. Need two numbers with product $3 \times (-2) = -6$ and sum $+5$: try $+6$ and $-1$. Split:

$$3c^2 + 6c - c - 2 \;=\; 0$$

$$3c(c + 2) - 1(c + 2) \;=\; 0$$

$$(3c - 1)(c + 2) \;=\; 0.$$

Read off candidates.

$$3c - 1 = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; c = \tfrac{1}{3}, \qquad c + 2 = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; c = -2.$$

After getting the quadratic, the next critical move is the range check on $\cos$. $\cos\theta \in [-1, 1]$ for all real $\theta$, so $c = -2$ is impossible — discard it immediately. This is Phuc's exact slack concern: "know how to solve the equation b" — the trap is treating $c = -2$ as a valid root and chasing nonexistent angles. Always state "reject $c = -2$ because $|\cos\theta| \le 1$" on the page so the marker sees the reasoning.
$3c^2 + 5c - 2 = 0 \Longrightarrow (3c-1)(c+2)=0 \Longrightarrow \cos\theta = \tfrac{1}{3}$ (reject $\cos\theta = -2$).
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 2: $\cos\theta = \tfrac{1}{3}$, with $0 < \theta < 360°$.
STEP 3/3 A1 A1 — solve for θ in 0–360°

Recognise the trigger. $\cos$ is positive, so $\theta$ lies in quadrants I and IV (the two quadrants where cosine is positive).

Tool — solutions of $\cos\theta = k$ in $0 < \theta < 360°$: if $\theta_0 = \arccos k$ is the principal value (in $[0°, 180°]$), then within one $360°$ period:

$$\theta \;=\; \theta_0 \quad \text{or} \quad \theta \;=\; 360° - \theta_0.$$

Principal value.

$$\theta_0 \;=\; \arccos\!\left(\tfrac{1}{3}\right) \;\approx\; 70.5288°.$$

To 1 d.p.: $\theta_0 \approx 70.5°$.

Second solution (quadrant IV, reflection through $\theta = 360°$):

$$\theta \;=\; 360° - 70.5288° \;\approx\; 289.4712° \;\to\; 289.5°.$$

Check both lie in $(0°, 360°)$: $70.5°, 289.5°$ both inside ✓. Neither is $90°$ or $270°$, so the $\cos\theta \ne 0$ constraint from STEP 1 is honoured ✓.

$$\boxed{\,\theta \;\approx\; 70.5°,\; 289.5° \text{ (1 d.p.)}\,}$$

Two cleanup rules echoing part (i): (i) keep extra decimals through the calculation and round only at the final line; (ii) write both solutions — examiners deduct an A1 if only the principal value is given. Phuc's slack noted needing to "review the other solution of sin function" — the same logic applies to cosine: within each period there are two solutions, and on a $360°$ range that's exactly two. The $360° - \theta_0$ formula is the cosine analogue of $\pi - u_0$ for sine; both come from the symmetry of the unit circle.
$\boxed{\theta \approx 70.5°, \; 289.5°}$ (1 d.p.).
