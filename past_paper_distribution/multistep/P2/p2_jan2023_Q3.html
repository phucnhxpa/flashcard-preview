Q3(b) — AP-coefficient binomial expansion: find $k$

Question

$f(x) \;=\; \left(2 + \dfrac{kx}{8}\right)^{\!7}$ where $k$ is a non-zero constant.

(a) Find the first 4 terms, in ascending powers of $x$, of the binomial expansion of $f(x)$. Give each term in simplest form. (4)

Given that, in the binomial expansion of $f(x)$, the coefficients of $x$, $x^2$ and $x^3$ are the first 3 terms of an arithmetic progression,

(b) find, using algebra, the possible values of $k$.

(Solutions relying entirely on calculator technology are not acceptable.) (3)

Part (b) — Find $k$ from the AP condition [3 marks] · 3 steps

From part (a) (carry-in): expansion of $\left(2 + \dfrac{kx}{8}\right)^{\!7}$ has $c_0 = 128$, $\;c_1 = 56k$, $\;c_2 = \dfrac{21k^2}{2}$, $\;c_3 = \dfrac{35k^3}{32}$.
STEP 1/3 M1 — AP condition

Recognise the trigger. The question says the coefficients of $x$, $x^2$, $x^3$ — i.e. $c_1, c_2, c_3$ — are the first 3 terms of an arithmetic progression. The defining property of an AP is that successive terms differ by the same constant, the common difference $d$. So $c_2 - c_1 = c_3 - c_2$, which rearranges to the symmetric form

$$2c_2 \;=\; c_1 + c_3 \qquad \text{(equivalent: the middle term is the mean of its neighbours).}$$

Why this method. The AP condition gives you a single algebraic equation in $k$, which is exactly what "find $k$" needs. Alternative phrasings — "$c_2$ is the arithmetic mean of $c_1$ and $c_3$", "the common difference is the same" — all lead to the same equation. Pick whichever form makes the algebra cleanest; here $2c_2 = c_1 + c_3$ is best because both sides have whole rational expressions in $k$.

State the tool.

$$\text{AP: } a_1, a_2, a_3 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; a_2 - a_1 \;=\; a_3 - a_2 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; 2a_2 \;=\; a_1 + a_3.$$

Apply with $a_1 = c_1 = 56k$, $a_2 = c_2 = \dfrac{21k^2}{2}$, $a_3 = c_3 = \dfrac{35k^3}{32}$:

$$2 \cdot \frac{21k^2}{2} \;=\; 56k \;+\; \frac{35k^3}{32}$$

$$21k^2 \;=\; 56k \;+\; \frac{35k^3}{32}.$$

A common error is to use $c_0, c_1, c_2$ in the AP condition by mistake — but the question specifies the coefficients of $x, x^2, x^3$, i.e. $c_1, c_2, c_3$. Underline these in the question paper before writing anything. Also: the AP relation is on the coefficients alone, not on the terms $c_1 x + c_2 x^2 + c_3 x^3$ (those wouldn't form an AP in any meaningful sense because they're not constants).
$21k^2 \;=\; 56k \;+\; \dfrac{35k^3}{32}$ — single equation in $k$.

STEP 2/3 M1 — clear fractions

Recognise the trigger. The equation has a fraction $35k^3/32$. To work cleanly with algebra (no decimals, no fractions to fumble), multiply through by the denominator to clear it.

Apply. Multiply every term by $32$:

$$32 \cdot 21k^2 \;=\; 32 \cdot 56k \;+\; 32 \cdot \frac{35k^3}{32}$$

$$672 k^2 \;=\; 1792 k \;+\; 35 k^3.$$

(Arithmetic check: $32 \times 21 = 672$ ✓; $\;32 \times 56 = 1792$ ✓; $\;32 \times 35/32 = 35$ ✓.)

Bring everything to one side (collecting on the side that keeps the leading coefficient positive):

$$35 k^3 \;-\; 672 k^2 \;+\; 1792 k \;=\; 0.$$

Factor out the common $k$. Since $k \ne 0$ (given: "non-zero constant"), we may divide it out, but it's cleaner to factor it first so the rejected root $k=0$ is visible:

$$k\bigl(35k^2 \;-\; 672k \;+\; 1792\bigr) \;=\; 0.$$

So either $k = 0$ (rejected by the question's stipulation) or

$$35 k^2 \;-\; 672 k \;+\; 1792 \;=\; 0.$$

Simplify the quadratic by dividing through by the common factor 7 (since $35 = 5 \cdot 7$, $672 = 96 \cdot 7$, $1792 = 256 \cdot 7$):

$$5 k^2 \;-\; 96 k \;+\; 256 \;=\; 0.$$

Two slips to avoid: (i) forgetting to multiply every term by 32 — easy to miss the linear term — so verify by reading across left-to-right after multiplying; (ii) dividing the whole cubic by $k$ without noting the $k=0$ root is rejected, which loses a mark if a marker is being picky. Always factor, then justify rejection of the trivial root explicitly.
Quadratic in $k$: $\;5k^2 - 96k + 256 = 0$ (after dividing by $7k$).

STEP 3/3 A1 — solve the quadratic

Recognise the trigger. A monic-ish quadratic in $k$. Either factorise by inspection (if the roots are nice) or use the quadratic formula. The exam disallows pure calculator solutions for this question, so the working must show the algebra.

Try factorisation first. Need two numbers whose product is $5 \times 256 = 1280$ and whose sum is $-96$. Test: $-80$ and $-16$ — product $= 1280$ ✓, sum $= -96$ ✓. Split the middle term:

$$5k^2 \;-\; 80k \;-\; 16k \;+\; 256 \;=\; 0$$

$$5k(k - 16) \;-\; 16(k - 16) \;=\; 0$$

$$(5k - 16)(k - 16) \;=\; 0.$$

Read off the roots:

$$k - 16 = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; k = 16, \qquad 5k - 16 = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; k = \tfrac{16}{5}.$$

Both are non-zero, so both are accepted by the question's stipulation.

$$\boxed{\,k \;=\; 16 \quad \text{or} \quad k \;=\; \tfrac{16}{5}\,}$$

Verify quickly with $k = 16$. Then $c_1 = 56(16) = 896$, $\;c_2 = \tfrac{21(256)}{2} = 2688$, $\;c_3 = \tfrac{35(4096)}{32} = 4480$. Common difference: $2688 - 896 = 1792$ and $4480 - 2688 = 1792$ ✓ — equal, so AP confirmed.

If factorisation by inspection feels slow, fall back to the quadratic formula: $k = \frac{96 \pm \sqrt{96^2 - 4 \cdot 5 \cdot 256}}{10} = \frac{96 \pm \sqrt{9216 - 5120}}{10} = \frac{96 \pm \sqrt{4096}}{10} = \frac{96 \pm 64}{10}$, giving $k = 16$ or $\tfrac{32}{10} = \tfrac{16}{5}$. Either route, both roots must be written as the final answer — losing one of the two costs the A1.
$\boxed{k = 16 \;\text{ or }\; k = \tfrac{16}{5}}$
