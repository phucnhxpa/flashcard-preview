Q5 — Factor + remainder theorems give simultaneous equations in $p, q$

Question

$$f(x) = x^3 + (p+3)x^2 - x + q$$

where $p$ and $q$ are constants and $p > 0$.

Given that $(x-3)$ is a factor of $f(x)$,

(a) show that $\;9p + q = -51$. (2)

Given also that when $f(x)$ is divided by $(x+p)$ the remainder is 9,

(b) show that $\;3p^2 + p + q - 9 = 0$. (2)

(c) Hence find the value of $p$ and the value of $q$. (3)

(d) Hence find a quadratic expression $g(x)$ such that $f(x) = (x-3)\,g(x)$. (2)

[9 marks]

Part (a) — Show $9p + q = -51$ [REDO — Phuc unsure of theorem] [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Recognise the trigger. The question gives you a cubic with two unknown constants and tells you $(x-3)$ is a factor. The word "factor" is the trigger — there's exactly one theorem in the P2 syllabus that converts "is a factor" into a usable equation: the Factor Theorem.

Why this method. The alternative would be polynomial long division of $f(x)$ by $(x-3)$ and setting the remainder to zero. That works but takes 6+ lines of algebra. The Factor Theorem collapses the same logic into one substitution. For a 2-mark "show that", the examiner is signalling the short route.

State the tool. Factor Theorem: $(x - a)$ is a factor of $f(x)$ if and only if $f(a) = 0$.

Identify $a$: matching $(x - a) = (x - 3)$ gives $a = 3$. Therefore we must impose

$$f(3) \;=\; 0$$

Apply — substitute $x = 3$ into $f(x) = x^3 + (p+3)x^2 - x + q$. Go term by term to keep the algebra clean:

$$f(3) \;=\; (3)^3 \;+\; (p+3)(3)^2 \;-\; (3) \;+\; q$$

$$\;=\; 27 \;+\; 9(p+3) \;-\; 3 \;+\; q$$

Expand the bracket on its own line — this is where slips happen:

$$9(p+3) \;=\; 9p + 27$$

Substitute back:

$$f(3) \;=\; 27 + 9p + 27 - 3 + q \;=\; 9p + q + 51$$

Now set $f(3) = 0$ and rearrange to the required form:

$$9p + q + 51 \;=\; 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; \boxed{9p + q \;=\; -51}\quad\text{(equation 1)}$$

Sense check. The required form has $-51$ on the right; we got $-51$, ✓. "Show that" questions always end with you arriving at the printed expression — if you didn't, recheck the bracket expansion.

The recall you missed: factor $\iff$ $f$ at the root is zero. Don't try long division for a 2-mark show-that — it's the slow lane. The dominant slip is mis-expanding $9(p+3)$; always write it as its own line. Treat "show that" as a checkpoint: if your answer doesn't match the printed form exactly, find the error before moving on.
$\boxed{9p + q = -51}$ (equation 1)

Part (b) — Show $3p^2 + p + q - 9 = 0$ [REDO — Phuc unsure of theorem] [2 marks] · 1 step

From part (a): $f(x) = x^3 + (p+3)x^2 - x + q$ with the constraint $9p+q=-51$ (equation 1) already imposed.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Recognise the trigger. The new information is "when $f(x)$ is divided by $(x + p)$ the remainder is 9". The word "remainder" is the trigger for the Remainder Theorem — same family as Factor Theorem, but for non-zero remainders.

Why this method. Polynomial long division would also work but is brutal here because the divisor $(x + p)$ contains the unknown $p$ — you'd be dividing by an algebraic expression. The Remainder Theorem turns it into a single substitution and is the only practical route for a 2-mark show-that.

State the tool. Remainder Theorem: when $f(x)$ is divided by $(x - a)$, the remainder is $f(a)$.

Identify $a$. The divisor here is $(x + p)$. Rewrite to match the $(x - a)$ template:

$$x + p \;=\; x - (-p) \;\Longrightarrow\; a = -p$$

So the condition "remainder is 9 when dividing by $(x+p)$" becomes

$$f(-p) \;=\; 9$$

Apply — substitute $x = -p$ into $f(x)$. Be careful with signs of $(-p)^n$:

$$f(-p) \;=\; (-p)^3 \;+\; (p+3)(-p)^2 \;-\; (-p) \;+\; q$$

Simplify each piece separately (this is the slip-prone bit):

$$(-p)^3 = -p^3,\qquad (-p)^2 = +p^2,\qquad -(-p) = +p$$

So

$$f(-p) \;=\; -p^3 \;+\; (p+3) \cdot p^2 \;+\; p \;+\; q$$

Expand $(p+3)p^2 = p^3 + 3p^2$:

$$f(-p) \;=\; -p^3 \;+\; p^3 + 3p^2 \;+\; p \;+\; q$$

The $-p^3$ and $+p^3$ cancel — this is the clean simplification the examiner designed in:

$$f(-p) \;=\; 3p^2 + p + q$$

Set $f(-p) = 9$ and rearrange to the required form:

$$3p^2 + p + q \;=\; 9 \;\Longrightarrow\; \boxed{3p^2 + p + q - 9 \;=\; 0}\quad\text{(equation 2)}$$

Sense check. The required form matches exactly. If you hadn't seen the $-p^3 + p^3$ cancellation, you'd be left with a cubic in $p$ — that's a sign you forgot to expand $(p+3)p^2$ before combining.

Two sign traps: (i) $(x + p)$ corresponds to root $x = -p$, NOT $x = +p$ — the minus is what flips. (ii) The $(-p)^3 + p \cdot p^2$ cancellation only appears if you remember to distribute $(p+3) \cdot p^2 = p^3 + 3p^2$ before combining. Skip that distribution and you'll have an unwanted $p^3$ term and a wrong answer.
$\boxed{3p^2 + p + q - 9 = 0}$ (equation 2)

Part (c) — Find $p$ and $q$ [3 marks] · 3 steps

From parts (a) and (b): $9p + q = -51$ (eq. 1) and $3p^2 + p + q = 9$ (eq. 2). Constraint: $p > 0$.
STEP 1/3 M1

Recognise the trigger. Two equations in two unknowns ($p, q$), one linear (eq. 1) and one quadratic-in-$p$ (eq. 2). The word "Hence" tells you to use both previous results. This is a standard simultaneous-equations elimination setup.

Why this method (subtraction vs substitution). Two routes:

(i) Subtract eq. 1 from eq. 2 to kill $q$ in one move — cleanest.

(ii) Rearrange eq. 1 as $q = -51 - 9p$ and substitute into eq. 2 — more chances to drop a sign.

Both work; subtraction is safer because the $q$ terms cancel automatically with no sign acrobatics.

Apply. Subtract eq. 1 from eq. 2 term by term:

$$\text{(eq. 2)} - \text{(eq. 1)}:\quad (3p^2 + p + q) - (9p + q) \;=\; 9 - (-51)$$

Distribute the minus sign carefully on the left:

$$3p^2 + p + q - 9p - q \;=\; 9 + 51$$

The $+q$ and $-q$ cancel; collect the $p$-terms:

$$3p^2 - 8p \;=\; 60$$

Move everything to one side to get a standard quadratic in $p$:

$$\boxed{3p^2 - 8p - 60 \;=\; 0}$$

Sanity check. Are both $q$'s gone? Yes ✓. Did the constant on the right become $9 - (-51) = 60$ (not $-42$)? Yes ✓. Sign errors at the subtraction step kill the rest of the question.

When subtracting one equation from another, the most common slip is mis-handling the double-negative on the right: $9 - (-51) = 60$, not $-42$ and not $9 + (-51)$. Writing $\text{(eq. 2)} - \text{(eq. 1)}$ on its own line, then expanding, prevents this.
$3p^2 - 8p - 60 = 0$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $3p^2 - 8p - 60 = 0$.
STEP 2/3 A1

Recognise. A quadratic in $p$ with $a = 3,\, b = -8,\, c = -60$. Discriminant: $b^2 - 4ac = 64 - 4(3)(-60) = 64 + 720 = 784$. Since $784 = 28^2$ (perfect square), it factorises nicely — but the quadratic formula is fastest here because spotting the factor pair takes time.

Why this method. Factorisation would need $3p^2 - 8p - 60 = (3p + ?)(p - ?)$ with product $-60$ and a tricky split. Quadratic formula is mechanical and always works.

State the tool.

$$p \;=\; \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$$

Apply with $a = 3,\, b = -8,\, c = -60$:

$$\Delta \;=\; b^2 - 4ac \;=\; (-8)^2 - 4(3)(-60) \;=\; 64 + 720 \;=\; 784 \;=\; 28^2$$

$$p \;=\; \dfrac{-(-8) \pm \sqrt{784}}{2(3)} \;=\; \dfrac{8 \pm 28}{6}$$

Split into the two cases:

$$p \;=\; \dfrac{8 + 28}{6} \;=\; \dfrac{36}{6} \;=\; 6 \qquad\text{or}\qquad p \;=\; \dfrac{8 - 28}{6} \;=\; \dfrac{-20}{6} \;=\; -\tfrac{10}{3}$$

Apply the constraint $p > 0$ stated in the question:

$$p = -\tfrac{10}{3} < 0 \;\text{(rejected)};\quad \boxed{p = 6}$$

Sense check. $3(6)^2 - 8(6) - 60 = 108 - 48 - 60 = 0$ ✓. Always sub the chosen root back into the quadratic.

Perfect-square discriminants ($\Delta = 784 = 28^2$) are the examiner's "this factors nicely" signal — if you ever get an irrational discriminant on a P2 algebraic-manipulation question, double-check the earlier steps. The $p > 0$ constraint is given specifically so you reject the negative root; ignoring constraints loses A1 even with correct algebra.
$\boxed{p = 6}$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 2: $p = 6$. From eq. 1: $9p + q = -51$.
STEP 3/3 A1

Recognise. One unknown ($q$) left; sub $p = 6$ into the simpler of the two original equations (eq. 1, linear) to recover $q$ in one line. Don't use eq. 2 — quadratic and messier.

Apply.

$$9(6) + q \;=\; -51 \;\Longrightarrow\; 54 + q \;=\; -51 \;\Longrightarrow\; \boxed{q \;=\; -105}$$

Independent verification against eq. 2 (the equation we did not use to find $q$):

$$3p^2 + p + q - 9 \;=\; 3(36) + 6 + (-105) - 9 \;=\; 108 + 6 - 105 - 9 \;=\; 0 \quad\checkmark$$

Both equations satisfied, so $(p, q) = (6, -105)$ is the unique solution consistent with $p > 0$.

Always back-substitute into the other equation as a sanity check — if eq. 2 fails, you have an arithmetic slip somewhere in (a), (b), or (c) STEP 1. The check costs 10 seconds and catches sign errors that would otherwise propagate into (d).
$\boxed{p = 6,\; q = -105}$

Part (d) — Find quadratic $g(x)$ with $f(x) = (x-3)\,g(x)$ [2 marks] · 1 step

From part (c): $p=6,\,q=-105$, so $f(x) = x^3 + 9x^2 - x - 105$. We know $(x-3)$ is a factor.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Recognise the trigger. The problem asks for the quadratic quotient when $f(x)$ is divided by a known linear factor. Two standard methods:

(i) Polynomial long division — mechanical, hard to slip on if laid out carefully.

(ii) Compare coefficients — write $f(x) = (x - 3)(x^2 + bx + c)$ and match against the given cubic.

Both give the same answer and full marks. Long division is recommended here because it self-verifies: zero final remainder confirms $(x - 3)$ is indeed a factor (which we already knew, but it's a free sanity check).

Substitute $p = 6,\, q = -105$ into $f(x)$:

$$f(x) \;=\; x^3 + (6+3)x^2 - x + (-105) \;=\; x^3 + 9x^2 - x - 105$$

Long division of $x^3 + 9x^2 - x - 105$ by $(x - 3)$. Lay it out like ordinary numerical long division. At each stage: divide the leading term, multiply back, subtract, bring down the next term.

Stage 1. Leading division: $x^3 \div x = x^2$. Multiply back: $x^2(x-3) = x^3 - 3x^2$. Subtract from the dividend:

$$(x^3 + 9x^2) - (x^3 - 3x^2) \;=\; 12x^2$$

Bring down $-x$ — running remainder is $12x^2 - x$.

Stage 2. Leading division: $12x^2 \div x = 12x$. Multiply back: $12x(x-3) = 12x^2 - 36x$. Subtract:

$$(12x^2 - x) - (12x^2 - 36x) \;=\; 35x$$

Bring down $-105$ — running remainder is $35x - 105$.

Stage 3. Leading division: $35x \div x = 35$. Multiply back: $35(x-3) = 35x - 105$. Subtract:

$$(35x - 105) - (35x - 105) \;=\; 0 \quad\checkmark\text{ (zero remainder confirms factor)}$$

The quotient is the sum of leading-division results, $x^2 + 12x + 35$. Therefore

$$\boxed{g(x) \;=\; x^2 + 12x + 35}$$

Sense check. $g(x) = (x + 5)(x + 7)$ factors over the integers, so $f(x) = (x - 3)(x + 5)(x + 7)$ has roots $3,\, -5,\, -7$. Sub one back: $f(-5) = -125 + 225 + 5 - 105 = 0$ ✓.

If you forget long division under exam pressure: equate coefficients. Write $f(x) = (x - 3)(x^2 + bx + c)$, expand to $x^3 + (b-3)x^2 + (c-3b)x - 3c$, and match against $x^3 + 9x^2 - x - 105$: $b - 3 = 9 \Rightarrow b = 12$; $-3c = -105 \Rightarrow c = 35$; verify the middle: $c - 3b = 35 - 36 = -1$ ✓. Same answer, different route.
$\boxed{g(x) \;=\; x^2 + 12x + 35}$
