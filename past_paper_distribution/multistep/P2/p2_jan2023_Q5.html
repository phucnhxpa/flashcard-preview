Q5 — Factor + remainder theorems give simultaneous equations in $p,q$

Question

$$f(x) = x^3 + (p+3)x^2 - x + q$$

where $p$ and $q$ are constants and $p > 0$.

Given that $(x-3)$ is a factor of $f(x)$,

(a) show that $\;9p + q = -51$. (2)

Given also that when $f(x)$ is divided by $(x+p)$ the remainder is 9,

(b) show that $\;3p^2 + p + q - 9 = 0$. (2)

(c) Hence find the value of $p$ and the value of $q$. (3)

(d) Hence find a quadratic expression $g(x)$ such that $f(x) = (x-3)\,g(x)$. (2)

[9 marks]

Part (a) — Show $9p + q = -51$ [REDO — Phuc unsure of theorem] [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Factor Theorem: $(x-a)$ is a factor of $f(x)$ $\iff f(a) = 0$.

Here $(x-3)$ is a factor, so $a = 3$ and we must impose

$$f(3) = 0$$

Substitute $x=3$ into $f(x) = x^3 + (p+3)x^2 - x + q$:

$$f(3) \;=\; (3)^3 + (p+3)(3)^2 - (3) + q$$

$$\;=\; 27 + 9(p+3) - 3 + q \;=\; 27 + 9p + 27 - 3 + q$$

Collect like terms and set $= 0$:

$$9p + q + 51 \;=\; 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; \boxed{9p + q = -51}\quad\text{(equation 1)}$$

The recall you missed: factor $\iff$ value of $f$ at the root is zero. Don't try polynomial long division here — too slow for a 2-mark "show that". A common slip is expanding $(p+3)(9)$ wrong; always write $9(p+3) = 9p+27$ as a separate line.
$\boxed{9p + q = -51}$ (equation 1)

Part (b) — Show $3p^2 + p + q - 9 = 0$ [REDO — Phuc unsure of theorem] [2 marks] · 1 step

From part (a): $f(x) = x^3 + (p+3)x^2 - x + q$ with the constraint $9p+q=-51$ (equation 1) already imposed.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Remainder Theorem: when $f(x)$ is divided by $(x-a)$, the remainder is $f(a)$.

Here the divisor is $(x+p) = \bigl(x - (-p)\bigr)$, so $a = -p$, and the remainder being 9 means

$$f(-p) \;=\; 9$$

Substitute $x = -p$ into $f(x)$:

$$f(-p) \;=\; (-p)^3 + (p+3)(-p)^2 - (-p) + q$$

$$\;=\; -p^3 + (p+3)p^2 + p + q \;=\; -p^3 + p^3 + 3p^2 + p + q$$

The $\,-p^3$ and $+p^3$ cancel — this is the key simplification:

$$f(-p) \;=\; 3p^2 + p + q$$

Set $f(-p) = 9$ and rearrange:

$$3p^2 + p + q \;=\; 9 \;\Longrightarrow\; \boxed{3p^2 + p + q - 9 = 0}\quad\text{(equation 2)}$$

Sign trap: $(x+p)$ corresponds to root $x=-p$, NOT $x=p$. The cubic-and-quadratic-from-the-same-coefficient cancellation ($-p^3 + p\cdot p^2$) is what the examiner wants you to spot. If you forget to factor $(p+3)p^2 = p^3 + 3p^2$ before combining, you'll keep the $p^3$ and get a wrong quadratic in $p$ later.
$\boxed{3p^2 + p + q - 9 = 0}$ (equation 2)

Part (c) — Find $p$ and $q$ [3 marks] · 3 steps

From parts (a) and (b): $9p + q = -51$ (eq. 1) and $3p^2 + p + q = 9$ (eq. 2). Constraint: $p > 0$.
STEP 1/3 M1

Strategy: eliminate $q$ by subtracting equation 1 from equation 2 (or rearranging eq. 1 and substituting). Subtraction is cleanest.

$$\text{(eq. 2)} - \text{(eq. 1)}:\quad (3p^2 + p + q) - (9p + q) \;=\; 9 - (-51)$$

The $+q$ and $-q$ cancel; collect $p$-terms:

$$3p^2 - 8p \;=\; 60 \;\Longrightarrow\; 3p^2 - 8p - 60 = 0$$

Subtraction is safer than rearranging $q = -51-9p$ and substituting (more chances to drop a sign). Always check after subtracting: are both $q$'s gone? Yes — good.
$3p^2 - 8p - 60 = 0$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $3p^2 - 8p - 60 = 0$.
STEP 2/3 A1

Quadratic formula: $p = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$ with $a=3,\,b=-8,\,c=-60$.

$$\Delta = b^2 - 4ac = (-8)^2 - 4(3)(-60) = 64 + 720 = 784 = 28^2$$

$$p \;=\; \frac{8 \pm 28}{6} \;\Longrightarrow\; p = \frac{36}{6} = 6 \quad\text{or}\quad p = \frac{-20}{6} = -\tfrac{10}{3}$$

Apply constraint $p > 0$ given in the question:

$$p = -\tfrac{10}{3} < 0 \;\text{rejected};\quad \boxed{p = 6}$$

$\Delta = 784 = 28^2$ is the "this factors nicely" tell — examiners design these so the discriminant is a perfect square. If you get an irrational answer, double-check the quadratic. The $p>0$ constraint is given specifically so you reject the negative root — don't ignore it.
$\boxed{p = 6}$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 2: $p = 6$. From eq. 1: $9p + q = -51$.
STEP 3/3 A1

Substitute $p = 6$ from STEP 2 into equation 1 to recover $q$:

$$9(6) + q = -51 \;\Longrightarrow\; 54 + q = -51 \;\Longrightarrow\; \boxed{q = -105}$$

Quick verify against eq. 2: $3(6)^2 + 6 + (-105) - 9 = 108 + 6 - 105 - 9 = 0$ ✓.

Always back-substitute into the other equation as a sanity check — if eq. 2 fails, you have an arithmetic slip in (a), (b), or (c) STEP 1. Costs 10 seconds and catches sign errors.
$\boxed{p = 6,\; q = -105}$

Part (d) — Find quadratic $g(x)$ with $f(x) = (x-3)\,g(x)$ [2 marks] · 1 step

From part (c): $p=6,\,q=-105$, so $f(x) = x^3 + 9x^2 - x - 105$. We know $(x-3)$ is a factor.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Strategy: divide $f(x) = x^3 + 9x^2 - x - 105$ by $(x-3)$ using polynomial long division. The quotient is $g(x)$ — a quadratic, as required.

Set up:

$$\begin{array}{r|l} \;x-3 & x^3 + 9x^2 - x - 105 \\ \end{array}$$

Step 1: $x^3 \div x = x^2$. Multiply back: $x^2(x-3) = x^3 - 3x^2$. Subtract:

$$(x^3 + 9x^2) - (x^3 - 3x^2) = 12x^2$$

Bring down $-x$: remainder so far is $12x^2 - x$.

Step 2: $12x^2 \div x = 12x$. Multiply back: $12x(x-3) = 12x^2 - 36x$. Subtract:

$$(12x^2 - x) - (12x^2 - 36x) = 35x$$

Bring down $-105$: remainder so far is $35x - 105$.

Step 3: $35x \div x = 35$. Multiply back: $35(x-3) = 35x - 105$. Subtract:

$$(35x - 105) - (35x - 105) = 0 \quad \checkmark\text{ (zero remainder confirms factor)}$$

The quotient (and hence $g(x)$) is

$$\boxed{g(x) = x^2 + 12x + 35}$$

Sanity check (optional): $g(x) = (x+5)(x+7)$, so $f(x) = (x-3)(x+5)(x+7)$ — three real roots $3,-5,-7$, consistent with a cubic that has $(x-3)$ as a factor.

If you forget long division, you can equate coefficients: write $f(x) = (x-3)(x^2 + bx + c)$, expand to $x^3 + (b-3)x^2 + (c-3b)x - 3c$, then match $b-3=9,\,c-3b=-1,\,-3c=-105$ giving $b=12,\,c=35$. Both methods are valid for full marks. Long division is mechanical and harder to slip on.
$\boxed{g(x) = x^2 + 12x + 35}$
