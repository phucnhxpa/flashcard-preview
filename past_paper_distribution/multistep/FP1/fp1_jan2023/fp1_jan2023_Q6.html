Q6 — Rectangular hyperbola, normal at a point

Question

In this question you must show all stages of your working. Solutions relying entirely on calculator technology are not acceptable.
The rectangular hyperbola $H$ has equation $xy=20$. The point $P\left(2t\sqrt{a},\ \dfrac{2\sqrt{a}}{t}\right)$, $t\ne 0$, where $a$ is a constant, is a general point on $H$.
(a) State the value of $a$ (1)
(b) Show that the normal to $H$ at the point $P$ has equation
$$ty-t^3x-2\sqrt{5}\,(1-t^4)=0$$ (4)
The points $A$ and $B$ lie on $H$. The point $A$ has parameter $t=c$ and the point $B$ has parameter $t=-\dfrac{1}{2c}$, where $c$ is a constant. The normal to $H$ at $A$ meets $H$ again at $B$.
(c) Determine the possible values of $c$ (4)

[9 marks]

Part (a)[1 mark] · 1 step

STEP 1/1

Verify $P$ lies on $xy=20$:
$$xy=(2t\sqrt{a})\cdot\frac{2\sqrt{a}}{t}=4a$$
Requiring this to equal $20$ gives $a=5$.

Part (b)[4 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2

From $xy=20$, differentiate implicitly: $y+x\dfrac{dy}{dx}=0$, so
$$\frac{dy}{dx}=-\frac{y}{x}$$

At $P=(2t\sqrt{5},\ 2\sqrt{5}/t)$:
$$\frac{dy}{dx}\bigg|_P=-\frac{2\sqrt{5}/t}{2t\sqrt{5}}=-\frac{1}{t^2}$$

STEP 2/2

Gradient of the normal (negative reciprocal): $m_N=t^2$.

Equation of the normal through $P$:
$$y-\frac{2\sqrt{5}}{t}=t^2\left(x-2t\sqrt{5}\right)$$

Multiply through by $t$ to clear the fraction:
$$ty-2\sqrt{5}=t^3 x - 2t^4\sqrt{5}$$
$$ty-t^3x=2\sqrt{5}-2t^4\sqrt{5}=2\sqrt{5}(1-t^4)$$
$$\boxed{ty-t^3x-2\sqrt{5}(1-t^4)=0}$$ $✓$

Target: $\boxed{ty-t^3x-2\sqrt{5}(1-t^4)=0}$

Part (c)[4 marks] · 4 steps

Carry-in: Part (b) result: $\boxed{ty-t^3x-2\sqrt{5}(1-t^4)=0}$
STEP 1/4

$A$ has parameter $c$, so its normal (from part b) is
$$cy-c^3x-2\sqrt{5}(1-c^4)=0 \quad (\star)$$

$B$ has parameter $-\dfrac{1}{2c}$, so its coordinates are
$$x_B=2\left(-\frac{1}{2c}\right)\sqrt{5}=-\frac{\sqrt{5}}{c}, \qquad y_B=\frac{2\sqrt{5}}{-1/(2c)}=-4c\sqrt{5}$$

Substitute $(x_B,y_B)$ into $(\star)$:
$$c\cdot(-4c\sqrt{5})-c^3\cdot\left(-\frac{\sqrt{5}}{c}\right)-2\sqrt{5}(1-c^4)=0$$
$$-4c^2\sqrt{5}+c^2\sqrt{5}-2\sqrt{5}+2\sqrt{5}\,c^4=0$$

STEP 2/4

Divide every term by $\sqrt{5}$:
$$-4c^2+c^2-2+2c^4=0$$
$$2c^4-3c^2-2=0$$

Treat as a quadratic in $u=c^2$:
$$2u^2-3u-2=0 \implies (2u+1)(u-2)=0 \implies u=-\tfrac{1}{2} \text{ or } u=2$$

Since $u=c^2\ge 0$, reject $u=-\tfrac{1}{2}$. So $c^2=2$, giving
$$\boxed{c=\sqrt{2} \quad \text{or} \quad c=-\sqrt{2}}$$

Target: $\boxed{c=\sqrt{2} \quad \text{or} \quad c=-\sqrt{2}}$
STEP 3/4

Key formula / concept
For a curve $f(x,y)=0$, gradient of tangent $=\dfrac{dy}{dx}$, gradient of normal $=-\dfrac{1}{dy/dx}$. The product of tangent and normal gradients is $-1$.
For $xy=c^2$, parametric form: $\left(ct,\ \dfrac{c}{t}\right)$ --- here with $c=2\sqrt{5}$ (since $(2\sqrt 5)^2=20$), so $a=5$.

Exam checklist
- For part (a), check $xy=20$ at $P$ --- don't just guess.
- Multiply the normal equation by $t$ early to eliminate $1/t$.
- For part (c), substitute B's coordinates into A's normal equation --- keep the equation's parameter as $c$ throughout.
- When you see only even powers of $c$, substitute $u=c^2$ before factorising.
- Reject negative values of $c^2$ --- quote both signs of $c$ (positive and negative root).

Common pitfalls
- Mixing up parameters: substituting B's parameter $-1/(2c)$ into A's normal equation in place of $c$ --- wrong, the equation is at $A$.
- Forgetting to divide out $\sqrt{5}$ from every term --- the $\sqrt 5$ factors out cleanly.
- Solving $2c^4-3c^2-2=0$ as a quadratic in $c$ --- it's degree 4; use $u=c^2$.
- Reporting only $c=\sqrt 2$ and forgetting $-\sqrt 2$.
