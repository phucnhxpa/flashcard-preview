Q9 — Induction on a log-factorial identity

Question

Prove by induction that for all positive integers $n$
$$\sum_{r=1}^{n}\log(2r-1)=\log\!\left(\frac{(2n)!}{2^{\,n}\,n!}\right)$$ (6)

[6 marks]

Solution3 steps

STEP 1/3

Statement $P(n)$: $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^{n}\log(2r-1)=\log\!\left(\frac{(2n)!}{2^{n}\,n!}\right)$.

Base case ($n=1$).
$$\text{LHS}=\log(2(1)-1)=\log 1 = 0$$
$$\text{RHS}=\log\!\left(\frac{2!}{2^{1}\cdot 1!}\right)=\log\!\left(\frac{2}{2}\right)=\log 1 = 0$$
$P(1)$ holds.

Inductive hypothesis. Assume $P(k)$ for some positive integer $k$:
$$\sum_{r=1}^{k}\log(2r-1)=\log\!\left(\frac{(2k)!}{2^{k}\,k!}\right)$$

STEP 2/3

Inductive step. Show $P(k+1)$:
$$\sum_{r=1}^{k+1}\log(2r-1)=\sum_{r=1}^{k}\log(2r-1)+\log(2(k+1)-1)$$
$$=\log\!\left(\frac{(2k)!}{2^{k}\,k!}\right)+\log(2k+1) \quad \text{[by hypothesis]}$$
$$=\log\!\left(\frac{(2k)!\,(2k+1)}{2^{k}\,k!}\right)=\log\!\left(\frac{(2k+1)!}{2^{k}\,k!}\right) \quad \text{[since }(2k+1)!=(2k+1)\cdot(2k)!\text{]}$$

We need this to equal $\log\!\left(\dfrac{(2(k+1))!}{2^{k+1}\,(k+1)!}\right)=\log\!\left(\dfrac{(2k+2)!}{2^{k+1}\,(k+1)!}\right)$.

Manipulate the target expression:
$$\frac{(2k+2)!}{2^{k+1}\,(k+1)!}=\frac{(2k+2)(2k+1)!}{2\cdot 2^{k}\cdot(k+1)\cdot k!}$$

Note $(2k+2)=2(k+1)$:
$$=\frac{2(k+1)\,(2k+1)!}{2\cdot 2^{k}\cdot(k+1)\cdot k!}=\frac{(2k+1)!}{2^{k}\,k!}$$

So
$$\sum_{r=1}^{k+1}\log(2r-1)=\log\!\left(\frac{(2k+1)!}{2^{k}\,k!}\right)=\log\!\left(\frac{(2k+2)!}{2^{k+1}\,(k+1)!}\right)$$

i.e. $P(k+1)$ holds.

STEP 3/3

Conclusion. $P(1)$ holds and $P(k)\Rightarrow P(k+1)$, so by the Principle of Mathematical Induction, $P(n)$ is true for all positive integers $n$. $\blacksquare$

Key formula / concept
$\log A+\log B=\log(AB)$. Factorial recursion: $n!=n\cdot(n-1)!$. The clean way to handle ratios with factorials in inductive steps is to expand both top and bottom one step, then cancel.

From the Loom (Mr Fuat, Loom 1): "You need some extra extra terms. This is always the same. $F(k+1)$ equals to something times $F(k)$ plus something." Apply the same template here: $\text{target}(k+1)=\text{hypothesis}(k)\cdot\text{(small factor)}$.

Exam checklist
- State $P(n)$ explicitly at the top.
- Verify base case both sides --- show both reduce to the same number.
- Begin inductive step with "Assume $P(k)$" and write out the hypothesis.
- Add the new term using $\sum_{r=1}^{k+1}=\sum_{r=1}^{k}+(\text{new})$.
- Write the full chain: $(2k+2)!=(2k+2)(2k+1)(2k)!$ and $2^{k+1}=2\cdot 2^{k}$ and $(k+1)!=(k+1)\cdot k!$.
- End with the formal induction conclusion sentence ("$P(1)$ true, $P(k)\Rightarrow P(k+1)$, therefore by induction\ldots ").

Common pitfalls
- Combining $\log A + \log(2k+1)$ as $\log(A+2k+1)$ --- the log-sum law is product, not sum.
- Stopping at $(2k+1)!$ without showing it equals the target's reduced form.
- Forgetting to split $2^{k+1}=2\cdot 2^k$ --- this is where the factor of $2$ comes from to cancel against $(2k+2)=2(k+1)$.
- Omitting the conclusion sentence --- examiners look for it specifically.

See also Q5 for another identity-heavy manipulation problem.

Key formulas

NOT in the formula booklet --- memorise

  • Vieta for $ax^2+bx+c=0$: $\alpha+\beta=-b/a$, $\alpha\beta=c/a$.
  • Power-sum identities: $\alpha^2+\beta^2=(\alpha+\beta)^2-2\alpha\beta$, $\alpha^3+\beta^3=(\alpha+\beta)^3-3\alpha\beta(\alpha+\beta)$, $\alpha^4+\beta^4=(\alpha^2+\beta^2)^2-2(\alpha\beta)^2$.
  • Rectangular hyperbola $xy=c^2$: parametric form $(ct,c/t)$, gradient at $(ct,c/t)$ is $-1/t^2$.
  • Parabola $y^2=4ax$: parametric form $(at^2,2at)$, focus $(a,0)$, directrix $x=-a$, gradient at $(at^2,2at)$ is $1/t$, focus-directrix: $PS=x_P+a$.
  • Complex conjugate factor: if $z=\alpha\pm i\beta$ are roots of a real-coefficient polynomial, then $z^2-2\alpha z+(\alpha^2+\beta^2)$ is a real quadratic factor.
  • Newton-Raphson: $x_{n+1}=x_n-f(x_n)/f'(x_n)$ --- requires $f'(x_n)\ne 0$.
  • Linear interpolation: $x\approx x_1 - f(x_1)\cdot\dfrac{x_2-x_1}{f(x_2)-f(x_1)}$.
  • Rotation: $\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta & -\sin\theta\\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta\end{pmatrix}$ anticlockwise.
  • Combined transformation order: "$U$ then $V$" $\Rightarrow$ matrix $VU$.

IN the booklet --- know how to find quickly

  • $\sum r$, $\sum r^2$, $\sum r^3$.
  • Quadratic formula.
  • Standard parabola, hyperbola, ellipse parametric forms.
  • de Moivre.

Exam strategy (FP1, 90 minutes, 75 marks)

  1. Pace = 1.2 minutes per mark. Q1 (5 marks) should take $\leq$ 6 minutes. Q9 (6 marks, induction) $\leq$ 7 minutes. If you're stuck past the pace budget, flag and move on --- return at the end with fresh eyes.
  2. Annotate every question before solving. Circle constants, underline unknowns, draw a diagram for any geometry question (Q6, Q7, Q8). Two minutes on annotation prevents ten minutes of confusion.
  3. For "show that" questions (Q2, Q6b, Q8a, Q8b, Q9): the target form telegraphs the manipulation. Work towards it. Every algebraic step earns method marks even if the final form has a typo.
  4. Use the calculator strategically. For matrix multiplication with numerical entries (Q7i parts), input the matrices and compute. For matrices with unknowns (Q1), you must work by hand --- Mr Fuat is explicit on this.
  5. Identity-recall is the single highest-leverage skill on this paper. Q3, Q5, and Q9 hinge on remembering algebraic identities cold. Spend 10 minutes daily writing them out from memory.
  6. Always state your conclusion sentence on induction questions (Q9). The mark scheme grants the closing mark only if the formal "true for $n=1$, assumed for $n=k$, shown for $n=k+1$, therefore by induction true for all $n\in\mathbb{Z}^+$" sentence appears.
  7. Final 5 minutes: sanity-check arithmetic. Re-evaluate one term per part --- a sign error caught is a mark saved.
