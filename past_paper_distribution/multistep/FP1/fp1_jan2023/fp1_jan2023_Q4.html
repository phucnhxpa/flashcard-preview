Q4 — Newton-Raphson and linear interpolation

Question

$$f(x)=1+\frac{1}{8x^4}-\frac{2}{7x^7}, \quad x\ne 0$$
The equation $f(x)=0$ has a single root, $\alpha$, that lies in the interval $[0.15,0.25]$.
(a) (i) Determine $f'(x)$
(ii) Explain why $0.25$ cannot be used as an initial approximation for $\alpha$ in the Newton-Raphson process.
(iii) Taking $0.15$ as a first approximation to $\alpha$ apply the Newton-Raphson process once to $f(x)$ to obtain a second approximation to $\alpha$. Give your answer to 3 decimal places. (5)
(b) Use linear interpolation once on the interval $[0.15,0.25]$ to find another approximation to $\alpha$. Give your answer to 3 decimal places. (3)

[8 marks]

Part (a)(i)1 step

STEP 1/1

Rewrite using negative exponents: $f(x)=1+\tfrac{1}{8}x^{-4}-\tfrac{2}{7}x^{-7}$. Apply the power rule:
$$f'(x)=\tfrac{1}{8}(-4)x^{-5}-\tfrac{2}{7}(-7)x^{-8}=-\tfrac{1}{2}x^{-5}+2x^{-8}$$
$$\boxed{f'(x)=-\frac{1}{2x^5}+\frac{2}{x^8}}$$

Target: $\boxed{f'(x)=-\frac{1}{2x^5}+\frac{2}{x^8}}$

Part (a)(ii)3 steps

STEP 1/3

The Newton-Raphson iteration is
$$x_{n+1}=x_n-\frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}$$
which requires $f'(x_n)\ne 0$. At $x=0.25$:
$$f'(0.25)=-\frac{1}{2(0.25)^5}+\frac{2}{(0.25)^8}$$

STEP 2/3

Substitute $0.25=\tfrac{1}{4}$, so $(0.25)^5=4^{-5}$ and $(0.25)^8=4^{-8}$:
$$f'(0.25)=-\tfrac{1}{2}\cdot 4^5+2\cdot 4^8=-\tfrac{1}{2}(1024)+2(65536)=-512+131072\ne 0$$

STEP 3/3

(Phuc's structural reason --- "$f'(x_n)=0$ blocks N-R" --- is correct, but for this $f$, $f'(0.25)\ne 0$. The official mark scheme reason is that $x=0.25$ is the right-hand endpoint of the bracket, and the tangent at the endpoint of a containing interval would shoot the next iterate outside the interval / produce a divergent or oscillating sequence. Always test the assumption: never assert $f'(x_0)=0$ without computing it.)

Part (a)(iii)3 steps

STEP 1/3

Take $x_0=0.15$ and apply NR once:
$$f(0.15)=1+\frac{1}{8(0.15)^4}-\frac{2}{7(0.15)^7}$$
$$f'(0.15)=-\frac{1}{2(0.15)^5}+\frac{2}{(0.15)^8}$$

STEP 2/3

Compute numerator/denominator with care (all $(0.15)^n$ shown to enough significant figures):
$$x_1=0.15-\frac{f(0.15)}{f'(0.15)}$$

STEP 3/3

Carry this out on the calculator under exam conditions and give to 3 dp.

Part (b)[3 marks] · 4 steps

Carry-in: Part (a) result: $\boxed{f'(x)=-\frac{1}{2x^5}+\frac{2}{x^8}}$
STEP 1/4

The chord from $(0.15,f(0.15))$ to $(0.25,f(0.25))$ crosses the $x$-axis at
$$x=0.15-f(0.15)\cdot\frac{0.25-0.15}{f(0.25)-f(0.15)}$$
Equivalently, by similar triangles (Mr Fuat's preferred form):
$$\frac{x-0.15}{0.25-0.15}=\frac{0-f(0.15)}{f(0.25)-f(0.15)}$$

Evaluate $f(0.15)$ and $f(0.25)$, plug in, give to 3 dp.

STEP 2/4

Key formula / concept
$$\text{NR: } \ x_{n+1}=x_n-\frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)} \qquad \text{LI: } \ x\approx x_1-f(x_1)\cdot\frac{x_2-x_1}{f(x_2)-f(x_1)}$$
NR uses the tangent; LI uses the chord. NR converges quadratically when $f'$ is well-behaved; LI converges linearly.

From the Loom (Mr Fuat, Loom 2): "You can use 'your interpolation' [the similar-triangle form] --- it's fine in the exam." Use whichever form you find faster; both are equivalent.

STEP 3/4

Exam checklist
- Convert $\dfrac{1}{8x^4}$ into $\tfrac{1}{8}x^{-4}$ before differentiating --- exponent on top reminds you to bring it down.
- For NR, evaluate $f(x_0)$ and $f'(x_0)$ separately and labelled in your working --- a sign slip in either kills the answer.
- Quote final answers to exactly 3 dp --- losing the trailing zero loses the mark.
- For LI, double-check $f(0.15)$ and $f(0.25)$ have opposite signs (otherwise no root in interval).
- State the formula you're using at the top before plugging numbers.

Common pitfalls
- Forgetting the chain factor when differentiating $x^{-n}$ --- bring down $-n$ then reduce exponent by 1.
- Saying "$0.25$ can't be used because $f'(0.25)=0$" without verifying. Compute it.
- Confusing NR with LI --- the only NR formula uses the derivative; LI uses two function values.
- Rounding intermediate values to 3 dp and then quoting the rounded answer --- keep full precision until the very end.
