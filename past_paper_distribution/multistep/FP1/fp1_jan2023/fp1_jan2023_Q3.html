Q3 — Cubic with complex roots

Question

In this question you must show all stages of your working. Solutions relying entirely on calculator technology are not acceptable.
$$f(z)=4z^3+pz^2+24z+108$$
where $p$ is a constant. Given that $-3$ is a root of the equation $f(z)=0$,
(a) determine the value of $p$ (2)
(b) using algebra, solve $f(z)=0$ completely, giving the roots in simplest form (4)
(c) determine the modulus of the complex roots of $f(z)=0$ (2)
(d) show the roots of $f(z)=0$ on a single Argand diagram (2)

[10 marks]

Part (a)[2 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2

Substitute $z=-3$ into $f(z)=0$:
$$4(-3)^3+p(-3)^2+24(-3)+108=0$$
$$-108+9p-72+108=0$$
$$9p=72 \implies p=8$$

STEP 2/2

So $f(z)=4z^3+8z^2+24z+108$.

Part (b)2 steps

STEP 1/2

Since $-3$ is a root, $(z+3)$ is a factor. Write
$$4z^3+8z^2+24z+108=(z+3)(4z^2+az+b)$$

Expand the right side:
$$(z+3)(4z^2+az+b)=4z^3+az^2+bz+12z^2+3az+3b=4z^3+(a+12)z^2+(b+3a)z+3b$$

Match coefficients:
- $z^2$: $a+12=8 \implies a=-4$
- constant: $3b=108 \implies b=36$
- $z$ (check): $b+3a=36-12=24$ $✓$

STEP 2/2

So $f(z)=(z+3)(4z^2-4z+36)=4(z+3)(z^2-z+9)$.

Solve $z^2-z+9=0$ using the quadratic formula:
$$z=\frac{1\pm\sqrt{1-36}}{2}=\frac{1\pm\sqrt{-35}}{2}=\frac{1\pm i\sqrt{35}}{2}$$

The three roots are
$$z=-3, \quad z=\tfrac{1}{2}+\tfrac{\sqrt{35}}{2}i, \quad z=\tfrac{1}{2}-\tfrac{\sqrt{35}}{2}i$$

Part (c)3 steps

STEP 1/3

Two routes --- both worth knowing.

STEP 2/3

Route A (direct). For $z=\tfrac{1}{2}+\tfrac{\sqrt{35}}{2}i$:
$$|z|=\sqrt{\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^2+\left(\tfrac{\sqrt{35}}{2}\right)^2}=\sqrt{\tfrac{1}{4}+\tfrac{35}{4}}=\sqrt{\tfrac{36}{4}}=\sqrt{9}=3$$

STEP 3/3

Route B (slicker --- use the quadratic). For $z^2-z+9=0$ with conjugate roots $\alpha,\bar\alpha$, the product of roots equals the constant term: $\alpha\bar\alpha=9$. But $\alpha\bar\alpha=|\alpha|^2$, so $|\alpha|=3$.

Part (d)4 steps

STEP 1/4

Argand diagram --- three points: $(-3,0)$, $(\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{\sqrt{35}}{2})$, $(\tfrac{1}{2},-\tfrac{\sqrt{35}}{2})$. Note $\tfrac{\sqrt{35}}{2}\approx 2.96$. The two complex roots are conjugates (symmetric about the real axis) and lie on the circle of radius $3$ centred at the origin (which passes through $-3$).

Key formula / concept
For a complex conjugate pair $z=x\pm iy$, $|z|^2=x^2+y^2=z\bar z$. Equivalently, for $z^2+bz+c=0$ with complex roots, $|z|^2=c$.

STEP 2/4

From the Loom (Mr Fuat, Loom 2): "If $-3$ is a root, that means $f(-3)$ must equal $0$." --- the Factor Theorem in one sentence.

Exam checklist
- Always sub the given root into $f$ first to find the unknown coefficient.
- Factor out the leading coefficient before identifying the quadratic factor --- don't drag the $4$ through the division.
- Use the constant of the reduced quadratic to get $|z|^2$ instantly when roots are a conjugate pair.
- On the Argand diagram, label all three roots with coordinates and mark the real axis / origin.
- Note conjugate symmetry on the diagram with a dashed vertical line.

STEP 3/4

Common pitfalls
- Sign error on $(-3)^2$ --- it is $+9$, not $-9$.
- Treating $(z+3)$ as the only factor and stopping after long division.
- Computing $|z|$ as $\sqrt{|x|+|y|}$ --- it is $\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$.
- Argand diagram with no axes labelled, no scale, or roots in the wrong half-plane.

See also Q1 for "match coefficients then factorise" pattern.
