Q7 — Matrix transformations and a hidden-fixed-line problem

Question

(i) $P=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\ -1&0\end{pmatrix}$. The matrix $P$ represents a geometrical transformation $U$.
(a) Describe $U$ fully as a single geometrical transformation. (2)
The transformation $V$, represented by the $2\times 2$ matrix $Q$, is a rotation through $240$^\circ$$ anticlockwise about the origin followed by an enlargement about $(0,0)$ with scale factor $6$. > (b) Determine the matrix $Q$, giving each entry in exact numerical form. **(2)** > Given that $U$ followed by $V$ is the transformation $T$, which is represented by the matrix $R$, > (c) determine the matrix $R$. **(2)** > (ii) The transformation $W$ is represented by the matrix > $$W=\begin{pmatrix}2\sqrt{2} & \sqrt{3}\\ 2\sqrt{3} & -\sqrt{2}\end{pmatrix}$$
Show that there is a real number $\lambda$ for which $W$ maps the point $(\lambda,1)$ onto the point $(4\lambda,4)$, giving the exact value of $\lambda$. (5)

[11 marks]

Part (i)(a)3 steps

STEP 1/3

Test $P$ on basis vectors:
$$P\begin{pmatrix}1\\ 0\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\ -1\end{pmatrix}, \qquad P\begin{pmatrix}0\\ 1\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\ 0\end{pmatrix}$$

STEP 2/3

The $x$-axis basis vector goes to $(0,-1)$ --- a $90$^\circ$$ **clockwise** rotation. Confirm with the standard rotation form $\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta & -\sin\theta\\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta\end{pmatrix}$: take $\theta=-90$^\circ$$, $\cos\theta=0$, $-\sin\theta=1$, $\sin\theta=-1$ --- matches.

STEP 3/3

$U$ is a **rotation of $90$^\circ$$ clockwise about the origin** (equivalently $270$^\circ$$ anticlockwise).

Part (i)(b)2 steps

STEP 1/2

Rotation by $240$^\circ$$ anticlockwise: $$\cos 240$^\circ$=-\tfrac{1}{2}, \qquad \sin 240$^\circ$=-\tfrac{\sqrt 3}{2}$$ $$R_{240}=\begin{pmatrix}-\tfrac{1}{2} & \tfrac{\sqrt 3}{2}\\ -\tfrac{\sqrt 3}{2} & -\tfrac{1}{2}\end{pmatrix}$$

STEP 2/2

Enlargement scale 6 about $(0,0)$ scales every entry by 6:
$$Q=6R_{240}=\begin{pmatrix}-3 & 3\sqrt 3\\ -3\sqrt 3 & -3\end{pmatrix}$$

Part (i)(c)3 steps

STEP 1/3

"U followed by V" means apply $U$ first, then $V$. The combined matrix is $R=QP$ (order matters: rightmost acts first):
$$R=QP=\begin{pmatrix}-3 & 3\sqrt 3\\ -3\sqrt 3 & -3\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}0 & 1\\ -1 & 0\end{pmatrix}$$

STEP 2/3

Row 1: $\left((-3)(0)+(3\sqrt 3)(-1),\ (-3)(1)+(3\sqrt 3)(0)\right)=(-3\sqrt 3,\ -3)$
Row 2: $\left((-3\sqrt 3)(0)+(-3)(-1),\ (-3\sqrt 3)(1)+(-3)(0)\right)=(3,\ -3\sqrt 3)$

STEP 3/3

$$R=\begin{pmatrix}-3\sqrt 3 & -3\\ 3 & -3\sqrt 3\end{pmatrix}$$

Part (ii)[5 marks] · 4 steps

STEP 1/4

$W\begin{pmatrix}\lambda\\ 1\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}4\lambda\\ 4\end{pmatrix}$:
$$\begin{pmatrix}2\sqrt 2 \cdot \lambda + \sqrt 3\\ 2\sqrt 3 \cdot \lambda - \sqrt 2\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}4\lambda\\ 4\end{pmatrix}$$

Two equations:
- (E1): $2\sqrt 2\,\lambda + \sqrt 3 = 4\lambda \Rightarrow \lambda(2\sqrt 2 - 4)=-\sqrt 3 \Rightarrow \lambda=\dfrac{-\sqrt 3}{2\sqrt 2-4}=\dfrac{\sqrt 3}{4-2\sqrt 2}$
- (E2): $2\sqrt 3\,\lambda - \sqrt 2 = 4 \Rightarrow \lambda=\dfrac{4+\sqrt 2}{2\sqrt 3}$

Rationalise (E1) by $\dfrac{4+2\sqrt 2}{4+2\sqrt 2}$:
$$\lambda=\frac{\sqrt 3(4+2\sqrt 2)}{(4-2\sqrt 2)(4+2\sqrt 2)}=\frac{\sqrt 3(4+2\sqrt 2)}{16-8}=\frac{\sqrt 3(4+2\sqrt 2)}{8}=\frac{2\sqrt 3+\sqrt 6}{4}$$

STEP 2/4

Rationalise (E2) by $\dfrac{\sqrt 3}{\sqrt 3}$:
$$\lambda=\frac{(4+\sqrt 2)\sqrt 3}{6}=\frac{4\sqrt 3+\sqrt 6}{6}$$

Set equal:
$$\frac{2\sqrt 3+\sqrt 6}{4}=\frac{4\sqrt 3+\sqrt 6}{6}$$
$$6(2\sqrt 3+\sqrt 6)=4(4\sqrt 3+\sqrt 6) \Rightarrow 12\sqrt 3+6\sqrt 6=16\sqrt 3+4\sqrt 6 \Rightarrow 2\sqrt 6 = 4\sqrt 3$$

Square both sides (both positive): $4\cdot 6 = 16 \cdot 3 \Rightarrow 24=48$ --- contradiction. This shows that the matrix $W$ as transcribed cannot simultaneously satisfy both equations. The exact $W$ in the original PDF will give a self-consistent system; reproduce the exact entries from the paper, then the method above yields a clean $\lambda$. (Phuc --- verify the matrix entries from the original PDF before finalising your written answer; the bundle source is unclear about signs in this matrix.)

STEP 3/4

Key formula / concept
Combined transformations: "$U$ then $V$" $=VU$ (rightmost first). Rotation by $\theta$ anticlockwise has matrix $\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta & -\sin\theta\\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta\end{pmatrix}$. Enlargement by factor $k$ about origin is $kI$. An enlargement composed with a rotation just scales every entry.

From the Loom (Mr Fuat, Loom 1): "This must be rotation\ldots otherwise it is going to be either $1\ 0\ 0\ 1$ or $\pm 1$ entries only. If you get values different from $1$ and $-1$, it must be a rotation." --- heuristic for spotting rotation matrices instantly.

Exam checklist
- State the order convention: "$U$ then $V$" $\Rightarrow$ matrix $VU$.
- For describing a rotation, give angle, direction (clockwise/anticlockwise), centre.
- Use exact surd forms --- no decimals in the final answer.
- For (ii), write both component equations from $W\mathbf v=\mathbf w$ side by side; solve one for $\lambda$, substitute into the other (consistency check).
- Rationalise denominators when comparing two expressions for $\lambda$.

STEP 4/4

Common pitfalls
- Computing $PV$ instead of $VP$ --- order is everything in matrix multiplication.
- $\sin 240$^\circ$=+\tfrac{\sqrt 3}{2}$ --- it is $-\tfrac{\sqrt 3}{2}$ (third quadrant).
- Forgetting to multiply the rotation matrix by the enlargement factor $6$.
- Solving only the first component equation in (ii) without checking the second is consistent.
