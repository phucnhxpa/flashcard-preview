Q8 — Parabola, focus, directrix, area ratio

Question

A parabola $C$ has equation $y^2=4ax$ where $a$ is a positive constant. The point $S$ is the focus of $C$. The line $l_1$ with equation $y=k$ where $k$ is a positive constant, intersects $C$ at the point $P$.
(a) Show that $PS=\dfrac{k^2+4a^2}{4a}$. (3)
The line $l_2$ passes through $P$ and intersects the directrix of $C$ on the $x$-axis. The line $l_2$ intersects the $y$-axis at the point $A$.
(b) Show that the $y$-coordinate of $A$ is $\dfrac{4a^2k}{k^2+4a^2}$. (3)
The line $l_1$ intersects the directrix of $C$ at the point $B$. Given that the areas of triangles $BPA$ and $OSP$, where $O$ is the origin, satisfy the ratio $\text{area }BPA:\text{area }OSP=4k^2:1$,
(c) determine the exact value of $a$. (5)

[11 marks]

Part (a)[3 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2

For $y^2=4ax$, the focus is $S=(a,0)$, the directrix is $x=-a$. Set $y=k$ in the parabola: $k^2=4ax \Rightarrow x_P=\dfrac{k^2}{4a}$. So $P=\left(\dfrac{k^2}{4a},\ k\right)$.

STEP 2/2

By the focus-directrix property (distance from $P$ to focus equals distance from $P$ to directrix):
$$PS=x_P-(-a)=x_P+a=\frac{k^2}{4a}+a=\frac{k^2+4a^2}{4a}$$ $✓$

Part (b)[3 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2

Directrix meets $x$-axis at $(-a,0)$. Line $l_2$ passes through $P\left(\dfrac{k^2}{4a},\ k\right)$ and $(-a, 0)$:
$$\text{slope}=\frac{k-0}{\tfrac{k^2}{4a}-(-a)}=\frac{k}{\tfrac{k^2+4a^2}{4a}}=\frac{4ak}{k^2+4a^2}$$

STEP 2/2

Equation: $y-0=\dfrac{4ak}{k^2+4a^2}(x+a)$. At $A$ on the $y$-axis, $x=0$:
$$y_A=\frac{4ak}{k^2+4a^2}\cdot a=\frac{4a^2k}{k^2+4a^2}$$ $✓$

Part (c)[5 marks] · 4 steps

STEP 1/4

$B$ is the intersection of $l_1$ ($y=k$) with the directrix ($x=-a$):
$$B=(-a,\ k)$$

Triangle $BPA$: vertices $B(-a,k)$, $P\left(\tfrac{k^2}{4a},k\right)$, $A\left(0,\tfrac{4a^2k}{k^2+4a^2}\right)$.

Note that $B$ and $P$ have the same $y$-coordinate ($=k$), so $BP$ is horizontal. Take $BP$ as base:
$$|BP|=\frac{k^2}{4a}-(-a)=\frac{k^2+4a^2}{4a}$$

The height is the perpendicular distance from $A$ to the line $y=k$, i.e., $|k - y_A|$:
$$\text{height}=k-\frac{4a^2k}{k^2+4a^2}=\frac{k(k^2+4a^2)-4a^2k}{k^2+4a^2}=\frac{k^3}{k^2+4a^2}$$

STEP 2/4

$$\text{Area}(BPA)=\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{k^2+4a^2}{4a}\cdot\frac{k^3}{k^2+4a^2}=\frac{k^3}{8a}$$

Triangle $OSP$: $O(0,0)$, $S(a,0)$, $P\left(\tfrac{k^2}{4a},k\right)$. Base $OS$ on the $x$-axis, length $a$; height $=k$ (perpendicular from $P$ to $x$-axis):
$$\text{Area}(OSP)=\frac{1}{2}\cdot a\cdot k=\frac{ak}{2}$$

Form the ratio:
$$\frac{\text{Area}(BPA)}{\text{Area}(OSP)}=\frac{k^3/(8a)}{ak/2}=\frac{k^3}{8a}\cdot\frac{2}{ak}=\frac{k^2}{4a^2}$$

Set equal to $\dfrac{4k^2}{1}$:
$$\frac{k^2}{4a^2}=4k^2 \implies \frac{1}{4a^2}=4 \implies 4a^2=\frac{1}{4} \implies a^2=\frac{1}{16}$$

STEP 3/4

Since $a>0$:
$$\boxed{a=\frac{1}{4}}$$

Key formula / concept
For $y^2=4ax$: focus $(a,0)$, directrix $x=-a$. Focus-directrix: for any point $P$ on the parabola, $PS=x_P+a$ --- saves the distance-formula algebra every time.

Exam checklist
- Always use the focus-directrix property to express $PS$ --- never the raw distance formula.
- When two vertices share a coordinate, that edge is the natural base of the triangle.
- For "perpendicular distance from a point to a horizontal line $y=c$": just $|y_{\text{point}}-c|$.
- Substitute the ratio symbolically before plugging numerical $k$; the $k$'s often cancel.
- $a>0$ --- discard negative root.

Common pitfalls
- Mistaking $PS$ for the distance from $P$ to the vertex $O$.
- Choosing $AP$ as the base of triangle $BPA$ --- possible but messy; horizontal $BP$ is far cleaner.
- Forgetting that the directrix meets the $x$-axis at $(-a,0)$ (not the origin).
- Reporting $a=\pm 1/4$ --- the question states $a$ is positive.

Target: $\boxed{a=\frac{1}{4}}$
