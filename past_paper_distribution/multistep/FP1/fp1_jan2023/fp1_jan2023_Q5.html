Q5 — Quadratic roots manipulation

Question

The quadratic equation
$$4x^2-3x+k=0$$
where $k$ is an integer, has roots $\alpha$ and $\beta$.
(a) Write down, in terms of $k$ where appropriate, the value of $\alpha+\beta$ and the value of $\alpha\beta$ (2)
(b) Determine, in simplest form in terms of $k$, the value of $\dfrac{\alpha}{\beta^2}+\dfrac{\beta}{\alpha^2}$ (4)
(c) Determine a quadratic equation which has roots $\dfrac{\alpha}{\beta^2}$ and $\dfrac{\beta}{\alpha^2}$, giving your answer in the form $px^2+qx+r=0$ where $p$, $q$ and $r$ are integer values in terms of $k$ (3)

[9 marks]

Part (a)[2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1

For $ax^2+bx+c=0$: $\alpha+\beta=-\tfrac{b}{a}$, $\alpha\beta=\tfrac{c}{a}$.
$$\alpha+\beta=\frac{3}{4}, \qquad \alpha\beta=\frac{k}{4}$$

Part (b)[4 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2

Combine the fractions over the common denominator $\alpha^2\beta^2$:
$$\frac{\alpha}{\beta^2}+\frac{\beta}{\alpha^2}=\frac{\alpha^3+\beta^3}{\alpha^2\beta^2}=\frac{\alpha^3+\beta^3}{(\alpha\beta)^2}$$

Apply the cubic identity (this is the critical step Phuc missed):
$$\alpha^3+\beta^3=(\alpha+\beta)^3-3\alpha\beta(\alpha+\beta)$$

Substitute:
$$\alpha^3+\beta^3=\left(\tfrac{3}{4}\right)^3-3\cdot\tfrac{k}{4}\cdot\tfrac{3}{4}=\tfrac{27}{64}-\tfrac{9k}{16}=\tfrac{27}{64}-\tfrac{36k}{64}=\tfrac{27-36k}{64}=\tfrac{9(3-4k)}{64}$$

STEP 2/2

And $(\alpha\beta)^2=\dfrac{k^2}{16}$.

Divide:
$$\frac{\alpha^3+\beta^3}{(\alpha\beta)^2}=\frac{9(3-4k)/64}{k^2/16}=\frac{9(3-4k)}{64}\cdot\frac{16}{k^2}=\frac{9(3-4k)}{4k^2}$$

$$\boxed{\frac{\alpha}{\beta^2}+\frac{\beta}{\alpha^2}=\frac{9(3-4k)}{4k^2}}$$

Target: $\boxed{\frac{\alpha}{\beta^2}+\frac{\beta}{\alpha^2}=\frac{9(3-4k)}{4k^2}}$

Part (c)[3 marks] · 3 steps

Carry-in: Part (b) result: $\boxed{\frac{\alpha}{\beta^2}+\frac{\beta}{\alpha^2}=\frac{9(3-4k)}{4k^2}}$
STEP 1/3

Let the new roots be $\alpha'=\dfrac{\alpha}{\beta^2}$ and $\beta'=\dfrac{\beta}{\alpha^2}$.

Sum (from part b): $\alpha'+\beta'=\dfrac{9(3-4k)}{4k^2}$.

Product:
$$\alpha'\beta'=\frac{\alpha}{\beta^2}\cdot\frac{\beta}{\alpha^2}=\frac{\alpha\beta}{\alpha^2\beta^2}=\frac{1}{\alpha\beta}=\frac{1}{k/4}=\frac{4}{k}$$

The quadratic with roots $\alpha',\beta'$ is $x^2-(\alpha'+\beta')x+\alpha'\beta'=0$:
$$x^2-\frac{9(3-4k)}{4k^2}x+\frac{4}{k}=0$$

STEP 2/3

Multiply through by $4k^2$ to clear denominators (the question demands integer $p,q,r$ in terms of $k$):
$$4k^2x^2-9(3-4k)x+16k=0$$
$$\boxed{4k^2x^2+(36k-27)x+16k=0}$$

So $p=4k^2$, $q=36k-27$, $r=16k$.

Key formula / concept
Memorise: $\alpha^3+\beta^3=(\alpha+\beta)^3-3\alpha\beta(\alpha+\beta)$. Likewise $\alpha^2+\beta^2=(\alpha+\beta)^2-2\alpha\beta$ and $\alpha^4+\beta^4=(\alpha^2+\beta^2)^2-2(\alpha\beta)^2$. None are in the booklet.

From the Loom (Mr Fuat, Loom 3):
"Since there are two equations [sum, product] and three unknowns $p,q,r$, we are free to select the value for one variable. After that, two equations and two unknowns --- we can solve." That is: scale freely to make $p,q,r$ integers.

Target: $\boxed{4k^2x^2+(36k-27)x+16k=0}$
STEP 3/3

Exam checklist
- State Vieta's formulas explicitly: $\alpha+\beta=\tfrac{3}{4}$, $\alpha\beta=\tfrac{k}{4}$.
- Write the identity $\alpha^3+\beta^3=(\alpha+\beta)^3-3\alpha\beta(\alpha+\beta)$ before substituting --- earns method marks even if arithmetic slips.
- Always factor out $\dfrac{1}{(\alpha\beta)^2}$ when you see $\dfrac{\alpha}{\beta^2}+\dfrac{\beta}{\alpha^2}$.
- Multiply through to clear denominators in (c); $p,q,r$ must be integers in terms of $k$ --- not fractions.
- Sanity check at $k=0$ (would give $\alpha\beta=0$ so denominators blow up) --- the case is excluded.

Common pitfalls
- Writing $\alpha^3+\beta^3=(\alpha+\beta)^3-\alpha\beta(\alpha+\beta)$ --- missing the $3$.
- Forgetting that $(\alpha\beta)^2=\alpha^2\beta^2$ and writing $\alpha\beta^2$ in the denominator.
- Leaving fractions in the final quadratic; rubric requires integers.
- Forgetting the sign in $-(\alpha'+\beta')$ when assembling $x^2-(\text{sum})x+\text{product}=0$.
