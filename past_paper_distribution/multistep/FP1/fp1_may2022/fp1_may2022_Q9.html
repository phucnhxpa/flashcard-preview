Q9 — Proof by Induction

Question

(i) A sequence is defined by $u_1 = 3$, $u_{n+1} = 2u_n - 2^{n+1}$ for $n\ge 1$. Prove by induction that for all $n\in\mathbb N$:
$$u_n = 5\cdot 2^{n-1} - n\cdot 2^n.$$ --- (5)
(ii) Prove by induction that for all $n\in\mathbb N$:
$$f(n) = 5^{n+2} - 4n - 9\ \text{is divisible by 16}.$$ --- (5)

STEP 4/11Inductive step

Strategy. Reveal this box only after you have chosen the method for Inductive step. The goal is to reproduce the exam-safe working, not just the final answer.

Inductive step. Show it holds for $n = k+1$. Use the recurrence $u_{k+1} = 2u_k - 2^{k+1}$:

$$u_{k+1} = 2\left(5\cdot 2^{k-1} - k\cdot 2^k\right) - 2^{k+1}$$
$$= 5\cdot 2^k - k\cdot 2^{k+1} - 2^{k+1}$$
$$= 5\cdot 2^k - (k+1)\cdot 2^{k+1}.$$

We want $u_{k+1} = 5\cdot 2^{(k+1)-1} - (k+1)\cdot 2^{k+1} = 5\cdot 2^k - (k+1)\cdot 2^{k+1}$ --- match ✓.

STEP 8/11Inductive assumption

Inductive assumption. Suppose $f(k) = 5^{k+2} - 4k - 9 = 16m$ for some integer $m$.

STEP 9/11Inductive step

Strategy. Reveal this box only after you have chosen the method for Inductive step. The goal is to reproduce the exam-safe working, not just the final answer.

Inductive step. Consider $f(k+1) - 5\,f(k)$ (the "multiply-by-5" trick eliminates the $5^{k+2}$ term):

$$f(k+1) = 5^{k+3} - 4(k+1) - 9.$$
$$5\,f(k) = 5\cdot 5^{k+2} - 20k - 45 = 5^{k+3} - 20k - 45.$$
$$f(k+1) - 5\,f(k) = \big[5^{k+3} - 4(k+1) - 9\big] - \big[5^{k+3} - 20k - 45\big]$$
$$= -4k - 4 - 9 + 20k + 45 = 16k + 32 = 16(k+2).$$

Therefore
$$f(k+1) = 5\,f(k) + 16(k+2) = 5(16m) + 16(k+2) = 16(5m + k + 2).$$

Since $5m + k + 2$ is an integer, $f(k+1)$ is divisible by 16.

STEP 10/11Conclusion

Strategy. Reveal this box only after you have chosen the method for Conclusion. The goal is to reproduce the exam-safe working, not just the final answer.

Conclusion. True for $n=1$; if true for $n=k$ then true for $n=k+1$. Hence by induction, $f(n) = 5^{n+2} - 4n - 9$ is divisible by 16 for all positive integers $n$. $\blacksquare$
