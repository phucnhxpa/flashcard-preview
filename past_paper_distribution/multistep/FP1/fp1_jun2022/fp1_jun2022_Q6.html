Q6 — Parabola normals, focus-directrix, triangle area

Question

The parabola $C$ has equation $y^2 = 36x$
The point $P(9t^2, 18t)$, where $t \neq 0$, lies on $C$
(a) Use calculus to show that the normal to $C$ at $P$ has equation $y + tx = 9t^3 + 18t$ (4)
(b) Hence find the equations of the two normals to $C$ which pass through the point $(54, 0)$, giving your answers in the form $y = px + q$ where $p$ and $q$ are constants to be determined. (4)
Given that
• the normals found in part (b) intersect the directrix of $C$ at the points $A$ and $B$
• the point $F$ is the focus of $C$
(c) determine the area of triangle $AFB$ (3)

[11 marks]

Part (a)[4 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2

Implicit differentiate $y^2 = 36x$:

$$2y\,\frac{dy}{dx} = 36 \;\Longrightarrow\; \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{18}{y}$$

STEP 2/2

At $P(9t^2, 18t)$: $\dfrac{dy}{dx}\bigg|_P = \dfrac{18}{18t} = \dfrac{1}{t}$.

Normal gradient $= -t$. Use point-slope:

$$y - 18t = -t(x - 9t^2)$$
$$y - 18t = -tx + 9t^3$$
$$y + tx = 18t + 9t^3 \quad✓$$

Part (b)[4 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2

Substitute $(54, 0)$:

$$0 + 54t = 18t + 9t^3$$
$$9t^3 - 36t = 0$$
$$9t(t^2 - 4) = 0$$
$$9t(t-2)(t+2) = 0$$

STEP 2/2

Reject $t = 0$ (given $t \neq 0$). So $t = 2$ or $t = -2$.

For $t = 2$: $y + 2x = 18(2) + 9(8) = 36 + 72 = 108$, so $y = -2x + 108$.
For $t = -2$: $y - 2x = 18(-2) + 9(-8) = -36 - 72 = -108$, so $y = 2x - 108$.

$$\boxed{y = -2x + 108 \quad \text{and} \quad y = 2x - 108}$$

Target: $\boxed{y = -2x + 108 \quad \text{and} \quad y = 2x - 108}$

Part (c)[3 marks] · 3 steps

Carry-in: Part (b) result: $\boxed{y = -2x + 108 \quad \text{and} \quad y = 2x - 108}$
STEP 1/3

From $y^2 = 36x = 4(9)x$: $a = 9$.

  • Focus $F = (a, 0) = (9, 0)$
  • Directrix $x = -a = -9$

Intersect each normal with $x = -9$:

STEP 2/3

$A$: $y = -2(-9) + 108 = 18 + 108 = 126$ → $A = (-9, 126)$
$B$: $y = 2(-9) - 108 = -18 - 108 = -126$ → $B = (-9, -126)$

$AB$ is vertical (both on $x = -9$): $|AB| = |126 - (-126)| = 252$.
Perpendicular distance from $F(9, 0)$ to the line $x = -9$ is $|9 - (-9)| = 18$.

$$\text{Area} = \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot |AB| \cdot d = \tfrac{1}{2}(252)(18) = \boxed{2268}$$

Target: $\boxed{2268}$
STEP 3/3

Common trap (this part).

Two redo images. The two failure modes Phuc's Loom describes were: (i) trying to find $A, B$ before realising the normals must be equated to the directrix line $x = -9$, and (ii) computing distances algebraically instead of recognising that $AB$ is vertical (so the "base" is just the $y$-difference and the "height" is the horizontal gap to $F$).

Phuc's plan (verbatim from slack): "So we can find the A and B points because the normalised intersect the directrix, so that means we set them equal and we solve for the x value\ldots apply the formula for the area of a triangle. This is the right triangle, so the area is one half base multiplied by height."

Key formula / concept.

  • Parabola $y^2 = 4ax$: focus $(a, 0)$, directrix $x = -a$ (IN booklet for standard parabola)
  • Parametric form $(at^2, 2at)$ --- here $a = 9$
  • Normal at $P$ has gradient $-t$ (tangent gradient $1/t$)
  • Triangle area $= \tfrac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}$

Exam checklist.

  • [ ] In (a), the question says "Use calculus" --- implicit differentiation must be shown (parametric also acceptable but show derivation)
  • [ ] In (b), reject the spurious $t = 0$ explicitly (the problem says $t \neq 0$)
  • [ ] Always identify $a$ from $y^2 = 4ax$ form before reading focus/directrix
  • [ ] Sketch the parabola, focus, directrix, and triangle --- turns (c) into a one-line arithmetic
  • [ ] Check symmetry: $A$ and $B$ are mirror images about the $x$-axis since the two normals' constants are $\pm 108$

Common pitfalls.

  • Reading $4a = 36$ as $a = 36$ --- wrong focus, wrong directrix
  • Forgetting that $AB$ lies on $x = -9$ so its length is a single subtraction in $y$
  • Using shoelace formula and making sign/order errors when a base--height calculation is trivial
  • Writing the cubic as $9t^3 + 36t = 0$ (sign error) --- gives no real non-zero roots

See also Q7 (geometric transformations and determinant for area).
