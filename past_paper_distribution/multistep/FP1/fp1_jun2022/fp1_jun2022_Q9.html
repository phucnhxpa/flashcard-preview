Q9 — Two induction proofs

Question

(i) A sequence of numbers is defined by $u_1 = 3$, $u_{n+1} = 2u_n - 2^{n+1}$, $n \geq 1$
Prove by induction that, for $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$,
$$u_n = 5 \times 2^{n-1} - n \times 2^n$$ (5)
(ii) Prove by induction that, for $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$, $f(n) = 5^{n+2} - 4n - 9$ is divisible by 16 (5)

[10 marks]

Solution4 steps

STEP 1/4

(i) Sequence induction

Base case ($n = 1$): Formula gives $u_1 = 5 \times 2^0 - 1 \times 2^1 = 5 - 2 = 3$. Matches given $u_1 = 3$. $✓$

Inductive hypothesis: Assume true for $n = k$: $u_k = 5 \times 2^{k-1} - k \times 2^k$.

Inductive step: Show true for $n = k+1$, i.e. $u_{k+1} = 5 \times 2^k - (k+1) \times 2^{k+1}$.

Using the recurrence:

$$u_{k+1} = 2u_k - 2^{k+1}$$
$$= 2\bigl[5 \times 2^{k-1} - k \times 2^k\bigr] - 2^{k+1}$$
$$= 5 \times 2^k - k \times 2^{k+1} - 2^{k+1}$$
$$= 5 \times 2^k - (k+1) \times 2^{k+1}\quad✓$$

STEP 2/4

Conclusion: Since the formula holds for $n = 1$ and the truth at $n = k$ implies truth at $n = k+1$, by the principle of mathematical induction the result holds for all $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$.

(ii) Divisibility induction

Base case ($n = 1$): $f(1) = 5^3 - 4 - 9 = 125 - 13 = 112 = 16 \times 7$, divisible by 16. $✓$

Inductive hypothesis: Assume $f(k) = 5^{k+2} - 4k - 9$ is divisible by 16, i.e. $f(k) = 16M$ for some integer $M$.

Inductive step: Consider $f(k+1) - f(k)$ (the cleanest framing):

$$f(k+1) = 5^{k+3} - 4(k+1) - 9 = 5^{k+3} - 4k - 13$$

STEP 3/4

$$f(k+1) - f(k) = (5^{k+3} - 4k - 13) - (5^{k+2} - 4k - 9)$$
$$= 5^{k+3} - 5^{k+2} - 4$$
$$= 5^{k+2}(5 - 1) - 4$$
$$= 4 \times 5^{k+2} - 4$$
$$= 4\bigl(5^{k+2} - 1\bigr)$$

Now $5 \equiv 1 \pmod 4$, so $5^{k+2} \equiv 1 \pmod 4$, hence $5^{k+2} - 1$ is divisible by $4$. Write $5^{k+2} - 1 = 4N$ for integer $N$:

$$f(k+1) - f(k) = 4 \times 4N = 16N$$
$$\therefore f(k+1) = f(k) + 16N = 16M + 16N = 16(M+N)$$

So $f(k+1)$ is divisible by 16. $✓$

Conclusion: As before, by induction $f(n)$ is divisible by 16 for all $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$.

Alternative for (ii) --- Mr Fuat's framing: Rewrite $f(k+1) = 5f(k) + 16(k + 2)$.
Check: $5f(k) + 16(k+2) = 5(5^{k+2} - 4k - 9) + 16k + 32 = 5^{k+3} - 20k - 45 + 16k + 32 = 5^{k+3} - 4k - 13 = f(k+1)$ $✓$. Both pieces are divisible by 16, hence the sum is.

STEP 4/4

Common trap (this part).

Slack note: "will come back to (i) later" --- the recurrence shape is unusual (subtracting $2^{n+1}$) and the target formula mixes powers and an $n\cdot 2^n$ term. Phuc's hesitation is about algebraic bookkeeping during the inductive step: keeping track of the factor of 2, the $-2^{k+1}$, and combining $-k \cdot 2^{k+1} - 2^{k+1}$ into $-(k+1) \cdot 2^{k+1}$.

Phuc's memorisation reminder (slack): "memorise sum of $r = \tfrac{1}{2}n(n+1)$" --- same flavour of formula-as-tool muscle memory.

Key formula / concept.

  • Standard induction template: Base → Hypothesis → Step → Conclusion
  • For divisibility, use either $f(k+1) - f(k)$ (clean) or $f(k+1) = c\cdot f(k) + \text{(multiple of target)}$
  • $5 \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ → $5^m \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ for all $m \geq 0$

Exam checklist.

  • [ ] State the proposition $P(n)$ explicitly
  • [ ] Write the base case computation in full --- both sides
  • [ ] State the inductive hypothesis as an equation (or "is divisible by 16")
  • [ ] In the step, end with exactly the target form, not "$\ldots$ which is the formula"
  • [ ] Write a complete conclusion sentence invoking the principle of induction

Common pitfalls.

  • Skipping the base case or computing only the LHS
  • Stating "assume true for all $n$" --- wrong; only for $n = k$
  • In (ii), claiming $4(5^{k+2} - 1)$ is divisible by 16 without justifying that $5^{k+2} - 1$ is divisible by 4
  • Forgetting to factor $-(k+1)\cdot 2^{k+1}$ in part (i) --- leaves an unsimplified expression that examiners won't accept

See also Q4, Q5 (algebraic manipulation under controlled hypotheses); Q8 (formula-driven, indexed proofs).

Exam Strategy

  1. Reading time discipline. Open the formula booklet first --- confirm parabola/hyperbola sections, the sum formulas, and N-R. Mark each question with the topic tag in 10 seconds.
  2. Two-pass order. Pass 1: hit Q1, Q3, Q4(a), Q7(a/d/e), Q8(a), Q9 base cases --- these are quick, high-confidence marks (~25 marks in ~25 minutes). Pass 2: heavy questions Q5, Q6, Q9 step proofs.
  3. Show structure on Vieta/induction problems. Examiners reward layout: write $S, P$ identities, $S', P'$ identities, then substitute --- never collapse steps in symmetric-function questions.
  4. For "show that" questions, transform LHS → RHS, not equal-and-rearrange. Especially in Q8(a) --- write one clean factorisation chain.
  5. Use the matrix calculator function for $A^n$ checks, composition spot-checks, and determinants. Only do matrix multiplication by hand when an unknown is present (Q3).
  6. In coordinate geometry, sketch every time --- Q6(c) becomes one line once $A$, $B$, $F$ are plotted.
  7. In induction, the conclusion sentence is worth a mark. Memorise: "Since the result holds for $n = 1$ and the truth at $n = k$ implies the truth at $n = k+1$, by the principle of mathematical induction the result holds for all $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$."
  8. Time budget. Roughly 1.2 min/mark. Q5 (10) and Q6 (11) deserve ~13 minutes each; if Q5 is going past 15 minutes, leave $P'$ and come back.
  9. Sign-checking ritual on rotation matrices. Always read both signs (cos and sin) before picking the quadrant. Mr Fuat: "In which quadrant is cosine plus and sine minus? Fourth quadrant --- that's why."
  10. Keep at least 5 d.p. in numerical questions until the final rounding step.

Pattern Summary

This paper rewards structural discipline over raw computation: every redo question (Q1, Q4, Q5, Q6, Q7, Q9) failed at a specific decision point --- choosing the right framing before computing. Q1 needs $\lvert z_1 z_2 \rvert = \lvert z_1\rvert\lvert z_2\rvert$ recognised instantly; Q4 needs "factor ⟹ remainder $\equiv 0$" as a trigger to switch from polynomial division to a linear system; Q5 needs Newton-style symmetric-function identities ($\alpha^4+\beta^4$ from $(\alpha^2+\beta^2)^2$) rather than direct expansion; Q6 needs $4a = 36 \Rightarrow a = 9$ before any focus/directrix calculation; Q7 needs both sign entries to be read before quoting a rotation angle; Q9(i) needs careful $2^{k+1}$ bookkeeping during the step. The pattern: identify the structural lever first, then compute. Drilling the "trigger" recognitions (Vieta, conjugate-pair factors, quadrant logic, base--height shortcuts) will save more marks than additional algebra practice.
