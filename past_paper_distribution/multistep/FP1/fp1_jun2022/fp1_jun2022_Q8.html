Q8 — Standard sums, target identity, telescoping range

Question

(a) Use the standard results for $\sum_{r=1}^n r^2$ and $\sum_{r=1}^n r$ to show that for all positive integers $n$
$$\sum_{r=0}^n (r+1)(r+2) = \tfrac{1}{3}(n+1)(n+2)(n+3)$$ (5)
(b) Hence determine the value of $10 \times 11 + 11 \times 12 + 12 \times 13 + \dots + 100 \times 101$ (3)

[8 marks]

Part (a)[5 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3

Expand the summand:

$$(r+1)(r+2) = r^2 + 3r + 2$$

Split the sum (note: $r$ ranges from $0$ to $n$, so $\sum_{r=0}^n 1 = n+1$, while the $r$ and $r^2$ sums are unchanged from the $r=1$ versions because the $r=0$ term contributes $0$):

$$\sum_{r=0}^n (r^2 + 3r + 2) = \sum_{r=1}^n r^2 + 3\sum_{r=1}^n r + 2(n+1)$$

STEP 2/3

Substitute standard results $\sum r = \tfrac{n(n+1)}{2}$, $\sum r^2 = \tfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}$ (IN booklet):

$$= \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} + \frac{3n(n+1)}{2} + 2(n+1)$$

Common denominator 6:

$$= \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1) + 9n(n+1) + 12(n+1)}{6}$$

STEP 3/3

Factor out $(n+1)$:

$$= \frac{(n+1)\bigl[n(2n+1) + 9n + 12\bigr]}{6} = \frac{(n+1)[2n^2 + 10n + 12]}{6}$$

Factor 2 from the bracket:

$$= \frac{2(n+1)(n^2 + 5n + 6)}{6} = \frac{(n+1)(n+2)(n+3)}{3}\quad✓$$

Part (b)[3 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3

Each term $k(k+1)$ matches $(r+1)(r+2)$ with $r = k - 1$.
- $k = 10$ → $r = 9$
- $k = 100$ → $r = 99$

$$\sum_{r=9}^{99}(r+1)(r+2) = \sum_{r=0}^{99}(r+1)(r+2) - \sum_{r=0}^{8}(r+1)(r+2)$$

STEP 2/3

Using the result from (a):

$$= \tfrac{1}{3}(100)(101)(102) - \tfrac{1}{3}(9)(10)(11)$$
$$= \tfrac{1}{3}(1{,}030{,}200) - \tfrac{1}{3}(990)$$
$$= 343{,}400 - 330$$

$$\boxed{= 343{,}070}$$

Target: $\boxed{= 343{,}070}$
STEP 3/3

Common trap (this part).

Three images, slack note: "will come back to a later". The breakdown is the lower-limit-zero detail: students automatically write $\sum_{r=0}^n 2 = 2n$ instead of $2(n+1)$, costing the constant-term contribution and breaking the factorisation. Second hazard is part (b)'s range conversion: students set $r = k$ instead of $r = k - 1$ and end at $r = 100$ instead of $r = 99$.

Key formula / concept.

(IN booklet)
- $\sum_{r=1}^n r = \tfrac{1}{2}n(n+1)$
- $\sum_{r=1}^n r^2 = \tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)(2n+1)$
- $\sum_{r=1}^n r^3 = \tfrac{1}{4}n^2(n+1)^2$
- For lower limit $0$: $r$ and $r^2$ sums unchanged, constant sum becomes $c(n+1)$

Exam checklist.

  • [ ] Check the lower limit of the sum before quoting standard results
  • [ ] Factor systematically --- get the $(n+1)$ out as early as possible
  • [ ] Show factorisation $n^2 + 5n + 6 = (n+2)(n+3)$ explicitly
  • [ ] For "hence" parts, convert the new range back to $r$ values and use Difference-of-Sums
  • [ ] Sanity-check: the formula gives positive integers when $n$ is a positive integer

Common pitfalls.

  • Writing $\sum_{r=0}^n 2 = 2n$ --- off by $2$, breaks the final factorisation
  • Setting $r = k$ (so range becomes $10$ to $100$) --- fences off by one
  • Forgetting to use the result from (a) in (b) --- re-deriving from scratch wastes 5+ minutes
  • Arithmetic on $100 \times 101 \times 102$ --- break it as $100 \times 10302 = 1{,}030{,}200$, then divide by 3

See also Q9 (formula-driven proof --- careful indexing of $n$ vs $k$ vs $r$).
