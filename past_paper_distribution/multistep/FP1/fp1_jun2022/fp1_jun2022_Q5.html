Q5 — Sum and product of roots, build new quadratic

Question

The quadratic equation $2x^2 - 3x + 5 = 0$ has roots $\alpha$ and $\beta$
Without solving the equation,
(a) write down the value of $(\alpha + \beta)$ and the value of $\alpha\beta$ (1)
(b) determine the value of (i) $\alpha^2 + \beta^2$ (ii) $\alpha^3 + \beta^3$ (4)
(c) find a quadratic equation which has roots $(\alpha^3 - \beta)$ and $(\beta^3 - \alpha)$, giving your answer in the form $px^2 + qx + r = 0$ where $p$, $q$ and $r$ are integers to be determined. (5)

[10 marks]

Part (a)[1 mark] · 1 step

STEP 1/1

For $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$: $\alpha + \beta = -b/a = \tfrac{3}{2}$, $\alpha\beta = c/a = \tfrac{5}{2}$.

Part (b)(i)1 step

STEP 1/1

$$\alpha^2 + \beta^2 = (\alpha + \beta)^2 - 2\alpha\beta = \left(\tfrac{3}{2}\right)^2 - 2\cdot\tfrac{5}{2} = \tfrac{9}{4} - 5 = -\tfrac{11}{4}$$

Part (b)(ii)1 step

STEP 1/1

$$\alpha^3 + \beta^3 = (\alpha + \beta)^3 - 3\alpha\beta(\alpha + \beta) = \tfrac{27}{8} - 3\cdot\tfrac{5}{2}\cdot\tfrac{3}{2} = \tfrac{27}{8} - \tfrac{45}{4} = \tfrac{27 - 90}{8} = -\tfrac{63}{8}$$

Part (c)[5 marks] · 5 steps

STEP 1/5

Compute the new sum $S'$ and new product $P'$ in terms of the original symmetric functions.

Sum $S' = (\alpha^3 - \beta) + (\beta^3 - \alpha) = (\alpha^3 + \beta^3) - (\alpha + \beta)$:

$$S' = -\tfrac{63}{8} - \tfrac{3}{2} = -\tfrac{63}{8} - \tfrac{12}{8} = -\tfrac{75}{8}$$

Product $P' = (\alpha^3 - \beta)(\beta^3 - \alpha)$:

STEP 2/5

$$P' = \alpha^3\beta^3 - \alpha^4 - \beta^4 + \alpha\beta$$
$$= (\alpha\beta)^3 - (\alpha^4 + \beta^4) + \alpha\beta$$

Compute each piece:
$(\alpha\beta)^3 = (5/2)^3 = 125/8$

$\alpha^4 + \beta^4 = (\alpha^2 + \beta^2)^2 - 2(\alpha\beta)^2 = (-11/4)^2 - 2(5/2)^2 = \tfrac{121}{16} - \tfrac{50}{4} = \tfrac{121}{16} - \tfrac{200}{16} = -\tfrac{79}{16}$

$\alpha\beta = 5/2$

STEP 3/5

$$P' = \tfrac{125}{8} - \left(-\tfrac{79}{16}\right) + \tfrac{5}{2} = \tfrac{250}{16} + \tfrac{79}{16} + \tfrac{40}{16} = \tfrac{369}{16}$$

New equation (monic): $x^2 - S' x + P' = 0$:

$$x^2 + \tfrac{75}{8}x + \tfrac{369}{16} = 0$$

Multiply by 16:

STEP 4/5

$$\boxed{16x^2 + 150x + 369 = 0}$$

So $p = 16$, $q = 150$, $r = 369$.

Target: $\boxed{16x^2 + 150x + 369 = 0}$
STEP 5/5

What went wrong (Phuc, this part).

Five images were tagged for this question --- it's the most error-prone in the paper. The breakdown points are: (i) expanding $(\alpha^3 - \beta)(\beta^3 - \alpha)$ and missing one of the four terms, (ii) computing $\alpha^4 + \beta^4$ from scratch instead of using the identity $(\alpha^2+\beta^2)^2 - 2(\alpha\beta)^2$, (iii) forgetting to multiply through by 16 to clear fractions.

From the Loom (Mr Fuat, on the unknown coefficient freedom): "Because we have two equations and two unknowns, we're free to select the value for one of the variables." --- Here $p$ is fixed by the LCM clearance (16), so this freedom collapses to one integer triple.

Key formula / concept.

(NOT in formula booklet --- all must be memorised)
- $\alpha + \beta = -b/a$, $\alpha\beta = c/a$
- $\alpha^2 + \beta^2 = (\alpha+\beta)^2 - 2\alpha\beta$
- $\alpha^3 + \beta^3 = (\alpha+\beta)^3 - 3\alpha\beta(\alpha+\beta)$
- $\alpha^4 + \beta^4 = (\alpha^2+\beta^2)^2 - 2(\alpha\beta)^2$
- New equation from new roots: $x^2 - (\text{sum})x + (\text{product}) = 0$

Exam checklist.

  • [ ] Write $S'$ and $P'$ formulas explicitly before substituting numbers
  • [ ] For $\alpha^4 + \beta^4$, use the square-of-square identity --- never expand directly
  • [ ] Use a common denominator on the new quadratic before clearing fractions
  • [ ] Verify $p, q, r$ are integers and gcd$(p,q,r) = 1$ if the question demands simplest integer form (here gcd is 1: $16, 150, 369$ $✓$)

Common pitfalls.

  • Sign error in $(\alpha^3 - \beta)(\beta^3 - \alpha)$: the cross terms are $-\alpha^4$ and $-\beta^4$, both negative
  • Confusing $(\alpha\beta)^3$ with $\alpha^3 + \beta^3$
  • Multiplying by 8 instead of 16 to clear --- gives non-integer $r$
  • Writing $-S'$ as $+S'$ when going from $x^2 - S'x + P' = 0$ to the explicit form
