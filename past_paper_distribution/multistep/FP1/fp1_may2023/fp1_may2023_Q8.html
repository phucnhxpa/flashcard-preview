Q8 — Parabola: focal chord and tangent intersection

Question

The point $P(2p^2, 4p)$ lies on the parabola with equation $y^2 = 8x$.
(a) Show that the point $Q\!\left(\dfrac{2}{p^2},\ -\dfrac{4}{p}\right)$, where $p \neq 0$, lies on the parabola. (1)
(b) Show that the chord $PQ$ passes through the focus of the parabola. (4)
The tangent to the parabola at $P$ and the tangent to the parabola at $Q$ meet at the point $R$.
(c) Determine, in simplest form, the coordinates of $R$. (8)

Setup

$y^2 = 8x = 4 \cdot 2 \cdot x$, so $a = 2$. Focus is $(2, 0)$; directrix is $x = -2$.

[13 marks]

Part (a)[1 mark] · 1 step

STEP 1/1

Check $Q$ on parabola: $y^2 = \left(-\tfrac{4}{p}\right)^2 = \tfrac{16}{p^2}$, and $8x = 8 \cdot \tfrac{2}{p^2} = \tfrac{16}{p^2}$. Equal, so $Q$ lies on the parabola. $✓$

Part (b)[4 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2

Slope of chord $PQ$:
$$m = \frac{4p - (-4/p)}{2p^2 - 2/p^2} = \frac{(4p^2 + 4)/p}{(2p^4 - 2)/p^2} = \frac{4(p^2+1)/p}{2(p^4-1)/p^2} = \frac{4(p^2+1)\,p^2}{2p(p^2-1)(p^2+1)} = \frac{2p}{p^2 - 1}.$$

Equation of chord at $P$: $y - 4p = \dfrac{2p}{p^2-1}\,(x - 2p^2)$.

STEP 2/2

Check it passes through focus $(2, 0)$:
$$0 - 4p \stackrel{?}{=} \frac{2p}{p^2-1}(2 - 2p^2) = \frac{2p\cdot(-2)(p^2-1)}{p^2-1} = -4p. \quad ✓$$

So $PQ$ passes through $(2, 0)$, the focus. $✓$ (And this confirms $PQ$ is a focal chord --- useful framing for part (c).)

Part (c)[8 marks] · 5 steps

STEP 1/5

Differentiate implicitly: $2y \tfrac{dy}{dx} = 8 \implies \dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{4}{y}$.

Tangent at $P(2p^2, 4p)$: gradient $= \tfrac{4}{4p} = \tfrac{1}{p}$.
$$y - 4p = \tfrac{1}{p}(x - 2p^2) \implies py = x - 2p^2 + 4p^2 = x + 2p^2 \implies py = x + 2p^2 \quad (T_P)$$

STEP 2/5

Tangent at $Q(2/p^2, -4/p)$: gradient $= \dfrac{4}{-4/p} = -p$.
$$y - (-\tfrac{4}{p}) = -p\bigl(x - \tfrac{2}{p^2}\bigr) \implies y + \tfrac{4}{p} = -px + \tfrac{2}{p} \implies y = -px + \tfrac{2}{p} - \tfrac{4}{p} = -px - \tfrac{2}{p} \quad (T_Q)$$

Intersect $T_P$ and $T_Q$: from $T_P$: $x = py - 2p^2$. Sub into $T_Q$:
$$y = -p(py - 2p^2) - \tfrac{2}{p} = -p^2 y + 2p^3 - \tfrac{2}{p}.$$
$$y + p^2 y = 2p^3 - \tfrac{2}{p} \implies y(1 + p^2) = 2\bigl(p^3 - \tfrac{1}{p}\bigr) = \frac{2(p^4 - 1)}{p} = \frac{2(p^2-1)(p^2+1)}{p}.$$
$$y = \frac{2(p^2-1)}{p} = 2p - \tfrac{2}{p}.$$

STEP 3/5

Then $x = py - 2p^2 = p\bigl(2p - \tfrac{2}{p}\bigr) - 2p^2 = 2p^2 - 2 - 2p^2 = -2$.

$$\boxed{R = \left(-2,\ \ 2p - \tfrac{2}{p}\right)}.$$

Target: $\boxed{R = \left(-2,\ \ 2p - \tfrac{2}{p}\right)}$
STEP 4/5

Observation from the Slack thread:

"$x = -2$ is the directrix, so tangents at the ends of any focal chord meet on the directrix (general theorem worth remembering)."

STEP 5/5

Key formula / concept.

For $y^2 = 4ax$ at $(at^2, 2at)$: tangent gradient $= 1/t$, tangent equation $ty = x + at^2$. Theorem (worth memorising): Tangents at the endpoints of a focal chord intersect on the directrix.

Exam checklist.

  • [ ] Identified $a = 2$, focus $(2,0)$, directrix $x = -2$ at the start.
  • [ ] In (a), checked $y^2 = 8x$ rather than just $y = \pm\sqrt{8x}$.
  • [ ] In (b), substituted the focus $(2,0)$ into the chord equation and verified algebraically.
  • [ ] Used the tangent-form $ty = x + at^2$ (or derived from $dy/dx$).
  • [ ] Final $R$ in simplest form: $\left(-2, 2p - \tfrac{2}{p}\right)$, not left as ugly fractions.

Common pitfalls.

  • Misreading $Q$ as $(2/p^2, +4/p)$ --- then $pq = 1$ (not a focal chord) and the algebra goes nowhere.
  • Forgetting $4a = 8$ gives $a = 2$, not $a = 8$.
  • Algebra fatigue when intersecting tangents --- keep the factor $(p^2+1)$ visible and cancel late.
  • Not recognising that the answer must have $x = -2$ (directrix). If you get $x$ depending on $p$, you've made an error.

See also Q3 (parametric point on a conic, intersection algebra) and Q6 (multi-line simultaneous solution).
