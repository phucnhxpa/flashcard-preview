Q2 — Complex roots of a quartic + Argand

Question

In this question you must show all stages of your working. Solutions relying on calculator technology are not acceptable.
Given that $x = 2 + 3i$ is a root of the equation
$$2x^4 - 8x^3 + 29x^2 - 12x + 39 = 0$$
(a) write down another complex root of this equation. (1)
(b) Use algebra to determine the other 2 roots of the equation. (4)
(c) Show all 4 roots on a single Argand diagram. (2)

[7 marks]

Part (a)[1 mark] · 1 step

STEP 1/1

All coefficients are real, so complex roots occur in conjugate pairs. Conjugate of $2+3i$ is $\boxed{2-3i}$.

Target: $\boxed{2-3i}$

Part (b)[4 marks] · 4 steps

Carry-in: Part (a) result: $\boxed{2-3i}$
STEP 1/4

Form the quadratic factor from the conjugate pair using the shortcut $x^2 - 2\operatorname{Re}(z)\,x + |z|^2$:

  • $\operatorname{Re}(2+3i) = 2$
  • $|2+3i|^2 = 2^2 + 3^2 = 13$
STEP 2/4

Quadratic factor: $x^2 - 4x + 13$.

Divide $2x^4 - 8x^3 + 29x^2 - 12x + 39$ by $x^2 - 4x + 13$:

STEP 3/4
step quotient term multiply back running remainder
1 $2x^2$ $2x^4 - 8x^3 + 26x^2$ $3x^2 - 12x + 39$
2 $0\cdot x$ $0$ $3x^2 - 12x + 39$
3 $+3$ $3x^2 - 12x + 39$ $0$

So $2x^4 - 8x^3 + 29x^2 - 12x + 39 = (x^2 - 4x + 13)(2x^2 + 3)$.

STEP 4/4

Solve $2x^2 + 3 = 0$: $x^2 = -\tfrac32$, $x = \pm i\sqrt{\tfrac32} = \pm\tfrac{\sqrt{6}}{2}\,i$.

Other two roots: $\boxed{\;x = \tfrac{\sqrt 6}{2}i\quad\text{and}\quad x = -\tfrac{\sqrt 6}{2}i\;}$.

Target: $\boxed{\;x = \tfrac{\sqrt 6}{2}i\quad\text{and}\quad x = -\tfrac{\sqrt 6}{2}i\;}$

Part (c)[2 marks] · 4 steps

Carry-in: Part (a) result: $\boxed{2-3i}$
Part (b) result: $\boxed{\;x = \tfrac{\sqrt 6}{2}i\quad\text{and}\quad x = -\tfrac{\sqrt 6}{2}i\;}$
STEP 1/4

Plot on the Argand plane:

STEP 2/4
  • $2 + 3i$ → first quadrant, $(2,3)$
  • $2 - 3i$ → fourth quadrant, $(2,-3)$
  • $\tfrac{\sqrt 6}{2}i \approx 1.22i$ → on positive imaginary axis, near $(0, 1.22)$
  • $-\tfrac{\sqrt 6}{2}i \approx -1.22i$ → on negative imaginary axis, near $(0, -1.22)$
STEP 3/4

The two real-coefficient conjugate pairs each form a vertical line of symmetry --- pairs reflect across the real axis.

STEP 4/4

Key formula / concept.

Conjugate-pair → real quadratic factor: $(x-z)(x-\bar z) = x^2 - 2\operatorname{Re}(z)\,x + |z|^2.$ This shortcut saves a full FOIL expansion every time you encounter this question type.

From the Loom (general FP1 approach, recorded for the May 2022 paper Q4 which uses the identical method):

"This is a specific kind of multiplication. It can be written as $z^2 - 2\cdot\operatorname{Re}(z_1)\cdot z + |z_1|^2$." --- Mr. Fuat
"Because if this is the factor of this quartic equation, that means that this quartic equation must be divided with zero remainder with this equation. So I think you know the rest here ." --- Mr. Fuat

Exam checklist.

  • [ ] Quoted "real coefficients $\Rightarrow$ complex roots come in conjugate pairs" verbally (the examiner sometimes wants this stated for part a).
  • [ ] Quadratic factor verified by FOIL or by the shortcut formula.
  • [ ] Long division remainder is exactly zero --- sanity check.
  • [ ] Surd form $\tfrac{\sqrt 6}{2}i$ kept exact (not decimal).
  • [ ] Argand diagram has labelled axes (Re, Im) and the four points clearly marked.

Common pitfalls.

  • Computing $|2+3i|^2 = 2^2 - 3^2$ (i.e. forgetting that the modulus squared has a plus, not a minus from "$-i^2$" --- easy mental slip).
  • Dividing by $x^2 - 4x - 13$ (wrong sign on 13).
  • Forgetting the leading 2 in the quotient --- the first quotient term is $2x^2$, not $x^2$.
  • Writing $x = \pm\sqrt{-\tfrac32}$ and leaving it un-simplified --- full credit requires the clean $\pm\tfrac{\sqrt 6}{2}i$ form.

See also Q6 (complex arithmetic with $z_1, z_2, z_3$).
