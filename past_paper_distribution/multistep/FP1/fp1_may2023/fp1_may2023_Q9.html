Q9 — Proof by induction: divisibility

Question

Prove, by induction, that for $n \in \mathbb{Z}$, $n \geq 2$,
$$4^n + 6n - 10$$
is divisible by 18.

[5 marks]

Solution5 steps

STEP 1/5

Let $f(n) = 4^n + 6n - 10$. Show $f(n)$ is divisible by 18 for all integers $n \geq 2$.

Base case ($n = 2$):
$$f(2) = 4^2 + 6(2) - 10 = 16 + 12 - 10 = 18 = 18 \times 1.$$
Divisible by 18. $✓$

Inductive hypothesis: Assume $f(k)$ is divisible by 18 for some integer $k \geq 2$. That is, $f(k) = 4^k + 6k - 10 = 18M$ for some integer $M$.

STEP 2/5

Inductive step: Show $f(k+1)$ is divisible by 18.

$$f(k+1) = 4^{k+1} + 6(k+1) - 10 = 4 \cdot 4^k + 6k + 6 - 10 = 4\cdot 4^k + 6k - 4.$$

Express $f(k+1)$ in terms of $f(k)$:
$$f(k+1) = 4\,(4^k + 6k - 10) - 4(6k - 10) + 6k - 4 = 4\,f(k) - 24k + 40 + 6k - 4 = 4\,f(k) - 18k + 36.$$

STEP 3/5

So
$$f(k+1) = 4\,f(k) - 18(k - 2) = 4\,f(k) + 18(2 - k).$$

By the inductive hypothesis, $f(k) = 18M$, so $4\,f(k) = 72M$, which is divisible by 18. And $18(2-k)$ is obviously divisible by 18.

Hence $f(k+1) = 4\cdot 18M + 18(2-k) = 18\,(4M + 2 - k)$, which is divisible by 18. $✓$

STEP 4/5

Conclusion: $f(2)$ is divisible by 18, and if $f(k)$ is divisible by 18 then $f(k+1)$ is divisible by 18. By the principle of mathematical induction, $4^n + 6n - 10$ is divisible by 18 for all integers $n \geq 2$. $\blacksquare$

STEP 5/5

Key formula / concept.

Divisibility induction template: rewrite $f(k+1) = A\cdot f(k) + B$ where $A$ is an integer and $B$ is explicitly divisible by the target modulus. Then both terms are divisible by the modulus, so $f(k+1)$ is too.

Exam checklist.

  • [ ] Base case uses $n = 2$ (the lower bound stated in the question), not $n = 1$.
  • [ ] Inductive hypothesis is stated clearly: "Assume $f(k)$ is divisible by 18 \ldots "
  • [ ] Inductive step shows $f(k+1)$ as a sum of two parts, each divisible by 18.
  • [ ] Conclusion sentence is written out with full induction logic --- markers want the wording.
  • [ ] Stated $f(k+1) = 18(\ldots)$ explicitly, where the expression in brackets is clearly an integer.

Common pitfalls.

  • Starting at $n = 1$: $f(1) = 4 + 6 - 10 = 0$. Zero IS divisible by 18 (technically), but the question's lower bound is $n = 2$, so use $n = 2$.
  • Computing $f(k+1) - f(k) = 3\cdot 4^k + 6$ and getting stuck --- this is divisible by 18 (because $4^k \equiv 4 \pmod 6$ for $k \geq 1$, so $3\cdot 4^k + 6 \equiv 0 \pmod{18}$), but the modular argument is harder to write than the $4f(k) + 18(\ldots)$ rewrite.
  • Skipping the conclusion sentence --- markers explicitly check for it.

See also Q1 (algebraic identity manipulation) and Q5 (factoring before substituting).

Key formulas --- split by booklet status

NOT in the formula booklet (memorise)

  • Conjugate-pair quadratic factor: $(x-z)(x-\bar z) = x^2 - 2\operatorname{Re}(z)\,x + |z|^2$
  • $\alpha^2 + \beta^2 = (\alpha+\beta)^2 - 2\alpha\beta$
  • $\alpha^3 + \beta^3 = (\alpha+\beta)^3 - 3\alpha\beta(\alpha+\beta)$
  • Determinant of $2\times 2$: $\det\!\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix} = ad - bc$
  • Inverse of $2\times 2$: $\frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{pmatrix}d & -b \\ -c & a\end{pmatrix}$
  • Area scale factor $= |\det M|$
  • Rectangular hyperbola $xy = c^2$: parameter $(ct, c/t)$, tangent $-1/t^2$, normal $t^2$
  • Parabola $y^2 = 4ax$: focus $(a,0)$, directrix $x=-a$, tangent at $(at^2, 2at)$: $ty = x + at^2$
  • Tangents at endpoints of a focal chord meet on the directrix
  • Linear interpolation: partition $[a,b]$ in ratio $|f(a)| : |f(b)|$ to find $x$-intercept of chord

IN the formula booklet

  • $\sum_{r=1}^n r = \tfrac12 n(n+1)$
  • $\sum_{r=1}^n r^2 = \tfrac16 n(n+1)(2n+1)$
  • $\sum_{r=1}^n r^3 = \tfrac14 n^2(n+1)^2$
  • Newton--Raphson: $x_{n+1} = x_n - \dfrac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}$
  • Standard rotation/reflection/enlargement matrices

Exam strategy (tactical points)

  1. Read the format: When a question says "give your answer in the form $px^2+qx+r=0$ where $p,q,r$ are integers," that is an instruction, not a suggestion. Clear all fractions at the end and state $p,q,r$.

  2. Use the question structure as scaffolding: Q2(a) (write down conjugate) feeds into (b) (form quadratic factor, divide). Q5(a) (compute $(\alpha^2+1)(\beta^2+1) = 40$) feeds into (b) (denominator of new sum and product is 40). If a part feels disconnected, you've probably missed the link.

  3. "In simplest form" matters: Q8(c) wants $R = (-2, 2p - 2/p)$, not $\left(-2, \tfrac{2p^2-2}{p}\right)$. Examiners deduct for "simplification" failures.

  4. Carry precision in numerical methods: Q7 needs 3 dp. Work to 5 dp internally; rounding to 3 dp at every step compounds error and you'll miss the right answer by 0.001.

  5. Quadrant check for arguments: In Q6(d), $\arg w$ depends on the signs of real and imaginary parts. $\arctan(58/4) = 1.502$ works because both are positive. If either flipped sign, you'd need to add $\pi$ or subtract from $\pi$.

  6. State the IVT / induction conclusion in words: Q7(a) and Q9 both demand a verbal closing statement. Markers explicitly check for "continuous, sign change ⇒ root by IVT" and the standard induction conclusion sentence.

  7. Calculator-banned questions (Q2, Q6): The paper flags "solutions relying on calculator technology are not acceptable." Show every algebraic step --- partial credit depends on the working, not the final answer.

  8. Time guide: at 75 marks in 90 minutes, you have ~1.2 min/mark. Q1 (4 marks) = 5 min, Q8 (13 marks) = 15 min. If you spend >2 min staring without writing, move on.

Pattern summary

This paper is a textbook FP1 distribution: one each of series proof, complex polynomial, conic geometry, matrices, roots-of-quadratics, complex arithmetic, numerical methods, parabola coordinate geometry, and induction. The unifying skill across the seven flagged redo questions is strategic algebraic factoring before numerical substitution --- Q1 needs $n(n+1)$ pulled out early, Q5(b) needs $(\alpha+\beta)(\alpha\beta+1)$ spotted, Q3(b) needs the trivial $x=6$ root used to factor the quadratic, Q8(c) needs $(p^2+1)$ kept symbolic until the last cancellation, Q9 needs $f(k+1) = 4f(k) + 18(2-k)$ written as the sum of two divisible-by-18 pieces. Drill the factor early instinct, and these question patterns become mechanical rather than algebraically explosive.
