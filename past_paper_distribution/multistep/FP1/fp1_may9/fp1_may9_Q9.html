Q9 — Proof by Induction

Question

(i) A sequence is defined by $u_1 = 3$, $u_{n+1} = 2u_n - 2^{n+1}$ for $n\ge 1$. Prove by induction that for all $n\in\mathbb N$:
$$u_n = 5\cdot 2^{n-1} - n\cdot 2^n.$$ --- (5)
(ii) Prove by induction that for all $n\in\mathbb N$:
$$f(n) = 5^{n+2} - 4n - 9\ \text{is divisible by 16}.$$ --- (5)

[10 marks]

Setup4 steps

STEP 1/4

Base case $(n=1)$:
RHS at $n=1$: $5\cdot 2^0 - 1\cdot 2^1 = 5 - 2 = 3$.
LHS: $u_1 = 3$. So the formula holds for $n=1$.

STEP 2/4

Inductive assumption. Suppose the formula holds for $n = k$:
$$u_k = 5\cdot 2^{k-1} - k\cdot 2^k.\quad (\star)$$

STEP 3/4

Inductive step. Show it holds for $n = k+1$. Use the recurrence $u_{k+1} = 2u_k - 2^{k+1}$:

$$u_{k+1} = 2\left(5\cdot 2^{k-1} - k\cdot 2^k\right) - 2^{k+1}$$
$$= 5\cdot 2^k - k\cdot 2^{k+1} - 2^{k+1}$$
$$= 5\cdot 2^k - (k+1)\cdot 2^{k+1}.$$

We want $u_{k+1} = 5\cdot 2^{(k+1)-1} - (k+1)\cdot 2^{k+1} = 5\cdot 2^k - (k+1)\cdot 2^{k+1}$ --- match ✓.

STEP 4/4

Conclusion. The statement is true for $n=1$. If true for $n=k$, then true for $n=k+1$. Hence by the principle of mathematical induction, the formula holds for all positive integers $n$. $\blacksquare$

Part (ii)5 steps

STEP 1/5

Correct method (ii) --- divisibility by 16

Let $f(n) = 5^{n+2} - 4n - 9$.

STEP 2/5

Base case $(n=1)$: $f(1) = 5^3 - 4 - 9 = 125 - 13 = 112 = 16 \times 7$. Divisible by 16. ✓

STEP 3/5

Inductive assumption. Suppose $f(k) = 5^{k+2} - 4k - 9 = 16m$ for some integer $m$.

STEP 4/5

Inductive step. Consider $f(k+1) - 5\,f(k)$ (the "multiply-by-5" trick eliminates the $5^{k+2}$ term):

$$f(k+1) = 5^{k+3} - 4(k+1) - 9.$$
$$5\,f(k) = 5\cdot 5^{k+2} - 20k - 45 = 5^{k+3} - 20k - 45.$$
$$f(k+1) - 5\,f(k) = \big[5^{k+3} - 4(k+1) - 9\big] - \big[5^{k+3} - 20k - 45\big]$$
$$= -4k - 4 - 9 + 20k + 45 = 16k + 32 = 16(k+2).$$

Therefore
$$f(k+1) = 5\,f(k) + 16(k+2) = 5(16m) + 16(k+2) = 16(5m + k + 2).$$

Since $5m + k + 2$ is an integer, $f(k+1)$ is divisible by 16.

Conclusion. True for $n=1$; if true for $n=k$ then true for $n=k+1$. Hence by induction, $f(n) = 5^{n+2} - 4n - 9$ is divisible by 16 for all positive integers $n$. $\blacksquare$

STEP 5/5

Key formula / concept.

Standard induction structure (4 elements). For $a^n$-divisibility: use $f(k+1) - a\,f(k)$ to cancel the exponential --- the multiplier is always the base.

Exam checklist.

  • Show arithmetic in the base case explicitly (do not just say "trivially true").
  • Write the inductive assumption with $n = k$ in a labelled line.
  • Algebra of the step ends by literally producing the formula with $k$ replaced by $k+1$.
  • Final concluding sentence mentions base, step, and "by induction, for all $n\in\mathbb N$".
  • For divisibility: factor out 16 (or whatever modulus) cleanly.

Common pitfalls.

  • Missing concluding statement (-1 mark).
  • Writing "assume true for all $n$" (circular) --- should be "assume true for $n=k$".
  • For (ii): expanding $5^{k+3}$ as $5^{k+2}\cdot 5$ but then forgetting the $\cdot 5$ when subtracting $5\,f(k)$.
  • Algebra slip: $-4(k+1) - 9 + 20k + 45$ $\Rightarrow$ ensure all four terms balanced.

See also Q8 --- both rely on tight algebraic factorisation to spot the target form.

Complex numbers
- $|z_1z_2| = |z_1||z_2|$, $\arg(z_1z_2) = \arg z_1 + \arg z_2$
- Conjugate-pair quadratic: $z^2 - 2\,\mathrm{Re}(\alpha)z + |\alpha|^2$

Roots of a quadratic ($ax^2+bx+c=0$)
- $\alpha+\beta = -b/a$, $\alpha\beta = c/a$
- $\alpha^2+\beta^2 = (\alpha+\beta)^2 - 2\alpha\beta$
- $\alpha^3+\beta^3 = (\alpha+\beta)^3 - 3\alpha\beta(\alpha+\beta)$
- $\alpha^4+\beta^4 = (\alpha^2+\beta^2)^2 - 2(\alpha\beta)^2$
- New equation: $x^2 - (\text{sum})x + (\text{product}) = 0$

Parabola ($y^2 = 4ax$)
- Focus $(a, 0)$, directrix $x = -a$
- Parametric $(at^2, 2at)$; tangent gradient $1/t$, normal gradient $-t$

Matrices
- $\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix}^{-1} = \dfrac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{pmatrix}d&-b\\-c&a\end{pmatrix}$
- $(MN)^{-1} = N^{-1}M^{-1}$
- Rotation: $\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta & -\sin\theta\\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta\end{pmatrix}$
- Reflection in $y = (\tan\theta)x$: $\begin{pmatrix}\cos 2\theta & \sin 2\theta\\ \sin 2\theta & -\cos 2\theta\end{pmatrix}$
- Stretch s.f. $k$ in $x$: $\begin{pmatrix}k&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix}$
- "$A$ followed by $B$" = $BA$
- Area scale factor $= |\det|$; $\det(BA) = \det B \det A$

Exam Strategy

  1. Time budget. 75 marks over 90 minutes $\approx$ 1.2 min/mark. Q6 (11) and Q9 (10) merit ~13 min each; Q1 (6) should be done in 7 minutes flat. Build in 5 minutes at the end for checking.
  2. Plan before writing. For long multi-part questions (Q5c, Q6c), write a 3-line skeleton: (i) what to compute, (ii) intermediate quantities, (iii) final algebraic manoeuvre. This stops the kind of muddle that gave the "two equations two unknowns" voice note.
  3. Order of operations for matrix composition. "$A$ followed by $B$" is $C = BA$. Write the order before multiplying entries.
  4. Conic constants. The moment you see $y^2 = kx$, write "$4a = k \Rightarrow a = k/4$" at the top of working --- single most efficient check in the paper.
  5. Conjugate roots first, then divide. For complex-root polynomials always build the real quadratic $z^2 - 2\,\mathrm{Re}(\alpha)z + |\alpha|^2$ before dividing. Direct substitution of $5-\mathrm i$ is a 10-minute trap.
  6. Mark scheme structure for induction. Always quote all four parts: base, assumption, step, conclusion. The conclusion sentence is 1 cheap mark every time.
  7. Calculator skill (matrices). Practise Casio MAT mode for 2$\times$2 multiplication --- saves ~2 min on Q7 and eliminates arithmetic slips. Note: not usable when the matrix contains an unknown like $k$ (Q3).
  8. "Hence" questions. Q6(b)/(c) and Q8(b) explicitly chain off the previous part. Reuse the formula you've already derived --- do not start a new method.
  9. Both roots, both points. For $q^2 = k$ give $\pm$; for Argand plot all positions on one diagram; for parabola normals find both $t$-values.
  10. Sign discipline on indices. $\tfrac{\mathrm d}{\mathrm dx}\bigl(-\tfrac12 x^{-1/2}\bigr) = +\tfrac14 x^{-3/2}$ --- the double-negative trap costs a mark every time it's missed.

Pattern Summary

Across this paper, Phuc's errors cluster in three buckets. First, arithmetic on negative/fractional indices: Q2(c) the double-negative on $-\tfrac12 x^{-1/2}$ flipping to $+\tfrac14 x^{-3/2}$, Q6 the index $a$ in $y^2 = 4ax$. Second, recall of non-booklet results: rotation matrices and the bottom-left $\sin\theta$ entry (Q7b), the inverse-of-product reversal (Q3), the conjugate-pair real quadratic (Q4). Third, structural mark-losses on long-form questions: the muddled voice note on Q5(c) and the skipped Q9(i) show that when method has many steps, the plan fails before the algebra does. The single highest-leverage intervention is to memorise the NOT-in-booklet formula table and to begin every multi-step question with a written three-line plan before any algebra.
