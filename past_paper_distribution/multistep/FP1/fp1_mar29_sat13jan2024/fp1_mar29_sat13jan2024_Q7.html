Q7 — Normal to parabola, second intersection

Question

The parabola $C$ has equation $y^2 = \dfrac{4}{3}x$. The point $P\left(\dfrac{1}{3}t^2, \dfrac{2}{3}t\right)$, where $t \neq 0$, lies on $C$.

(a) Use calculus to show that the normal to $C$ at $P$ has equation
$$3tx + 3y = t^3 + 2t \qquad (3)$$

The normal to $C$ at the point where $t = 9$ meets $C$ again at the point $Q$.
(b) Determine the exact coordinates of $Q$. (4)

Correct method

(a) Differentiate $y^2 = \tfrac{4}{3}x$ implicitly:
$$2y \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{4}{3} \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2}{3y}$$

At $P\left(\tfrac{1}{3}t^2, \tfrac{2}{3}t\right)$:
$$\frac{dy}{dx}\bigg|_P = \frac{2}{3 \cdot (2t/3)} = \frac{2}{2t} = \frac{1}{t}$$

Gradient of normal $= -t$ (negative reciprocal).

Equation of normal:
$$y - \tfrac{2t}{3} = -t\left(x - \tfrac{t^2}{3}\right)$$

Multiply through by 3:
$$3y - 2t = -3t\left(x - \tfrac{t^2}{3}\right) = -3tx + t^3$$
$$3tx + 3y = t^3 + 2t \qquad \blacksquare$$

(b) A neat trick: rather than substituting the normal into $y^2 = \tfrac{4}{3}x$ and slogging through a quadratic in $x$, parametrise the second intersection. Let $Q$ correspond to parameter $s$: $Q = \left(\tfrac{s^2}{3}, \tfrac{2s}{3}\right)$. Substitute into the normal equation $3tx + 3y = t^3 + 2t$:

$$3t \cdot \tfrac{s^2}{3} + 3 \cdot \tfrac{2s}{3} = t^3 + 2t$$
$$ts^2 + 2s = t^3 + 2t$$
$$ts^2 - t^3 + 2s - 2t = 0$$
$$t(s^2 - t^2) + 2(s - t) = 0$$
$$t(s-t)(s+t) + 2(s - t) = 0$$
$$(s - t)\bigl[t(s+t) + 2\bigr] = 0$$

Either $s = t$ (the original point $P$) or $t(s+t) + 2 = 0$, i.e.
$$s = -t - \frac{2}{t}$$

For $t = 9$:
$$s = -9 - \tfrac{2}{9} = -\tfrac{81}{9} - \tfrac{2}{9} = -\tfrac{83}{9}$$

Compute $Q$:
$$x_Q = \tfrac{s^2}{3} = \tfrac{1}{3} \cdot \left(\tfrac{83}{9}\right)^2 = \tfrac{1}{3} \cdot \tfrac{6889}{81} = \tfrac{6889}{243}$$
$$y_Q = \tfrac{2s}{3} = \tfrac{2}{3} \cdot \left(-\tfrac{83}{9}\right) = -\tfrac{166}{27}$$

$$\boxed{Q = \left(\tfrac{6889}{243}, \; -\tfrac{166}{27}\right)}$$

(Both numerator-coprime to denominator: $\gcd(6889, 243) = 1$ since $6889 = 83^2$ is coprime to $3$; $\gcd(166, 27) = 1$.)

Key formula / concept

  • For the standard parabola $y^2 = 4ax$ with parametric point $(at^2, 2at)$, the gradient at the point is $1/t$ --- memorise this. Here $4a = 4/3$ so $a = 1/3$, matching the given $P$.
  • The "second intersection" factorisation trick $(s - t)[\text{linear}] = 0$ is the cleanest route. The factor $(s - t)$ appears because $P$ itself is one of the intersection points.

Common pitfalls

  • Computing the normal gradient as $-1/t$ instead of $-t$ (the negative reciprocal of $1/t$ is $-t$).
  • Substituting $t = 9$ too early --- keep $t$ symbolic until the very end.
  • Arithmetic on $83^2 = 6889$ --- easy to drop a digit; verify $83 \times 83 = 80 \times 83 + 3 \times 83 = 6640 + 249 = 6889$.
