Q8 — Standard summation results

Question

(a) Use the standard results for summations to show that, for all positive integers $n$,
$$\sum_{r=1}^{n} r(2r-3)(r+1) = \tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)(n+2)(3n-5) \qquad (4)$$

(b) Hence show that, for all positive integers $n$,
$$\sum_{r=n}^{2n} r(2r-3)(r+1) = \tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)(an^2 + bn + c)$$
where $a, b, c$ are integers to be determined. (4)

OCR note: The exact polynomial summand and final target form rendered ambiguously in the source PDF; the version above is the one consistent with all three of $n=1, 2, 3$ giving clean values. If the printed RHS is given as a fully factorised $(an+b)(cn+d)$ structure, the numerical answers below still stand --- the polynomial $45n^2 - 5n - 28$ simply needs to be presented in whichever form the question asks.

Correct method

(a) Expand the summand:
$$r(2r-3)(r+1) = r\bigl[(2r-3)(r+1)\bigr] = r(2r^2 + 2r - 3r - 3) = r(2r^2 - r - 3) = 2r^3 - r^2 - 3r$$

Apply the standard results from the formula booklet:
$$\sum_{r=1}^{n} r = \tfrac{1}{2}n(n+1), \quad \sum_{r=1}^{n} r^2 = \tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)(2n+1), \quad \sum_{r=1}^{n} r^3 = \tfrac{1}{4}n^2(n+1)^2$$

Therefore:
$$\sum_{r=1}^{n} \bigl(2r^3 - r^2 - 3r\bigr) = 2 \cdot \tfrac{1}{4}n^2(n+1)^2 - \tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)(2n+1) - 3 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2}n(n+1)$$
$$= \tfrac{1}{2}n^2(n+1)^2 - \tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)(2n+1) - \tfrac{3}{2}n(n+1)$$

Factor out $\tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)$:
$$= \tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)\bigl[3n(n+1) - (2n+1) - 9\bigr]$$
$$= \tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)\bigl[3n^2 + 3n - 2n - 1 - 9\bigr]$$
$$= \tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)(3n^2 + n - 10)$$

Factor the quadratic $3n^2 + n - 10$. Discriminant: $1 + 120 = 121 = 11^2$. Roots: $n = \dfrac{-1 \pm 11}{6} = \dfrac{10}{6}, \dfrac{-12}{6} = \tfrac{5}{3}, -2$. So $3n^2 + n - 10 = (3n - 5)(n + 2)$.

$$\sum_{r=1}^{n} r(2r-3)(r+1) = \tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)(n+2)(3n-5) \qquad \blacksquare$$

Verification at $n = 3$: LHS $= 1\cdot(-1)\cdot 2 + 2\cdot 1 \cdot 3 + 3 \cdot 3 \cdot 4 = -2 + 6 + 36 = 40$. RHS $= \tfrac{1}{6}(3)(4)(5)(4) = \tfrac{240}{6} = 40$. $✓$

(b) Split the sum:
$$\sum_{r=n}^{2n} = \sum_{r=1}^{2n} - \sum_{r=1}^{n-1}$$

(Indices: the sum from $r = n$ to $r = 2n$ inclusive has $n+1$ terms; we subtract everything from $r = 1$ up to $r = n - 1$ from the total sum up to $r = 2n$.)

Using part (a):
- $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^{2n} r(2r-3)(r+1) = \tfrac{1}{6}(2n)(2n+1)(2n+2)(6n-5) = \tfrac{4}{6}n(n+1)(2n+1)(6n-5)$
- $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^{n-1} r(2r-3)(r+1) = \tfrac{1}{6}(n-1)(n)(n+1)(3n-8)$

Subtracting and factoring $\tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)$:
$$\sum_{r=n}^{2n} = \tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)\bigl[4(2n+1)(6n-5) - (n-1)(3n-8)\bigr]$$

Expand:
- $4(2n+1)(6n-5) = 4(12n^2 - 10n + 6n - 5) = 4(12n^2 - 4n - 5) = 48n^2 - 16n - 20$
- $(n-1)(3n-8) = 3n^2 - 8n - 3n + 8 = 3n^2 - 11n + 8$

Difference:
$$48n^2 - 16n - 20 - 3n^2 + 11n - 8 = 45n^2 - 5n - 28$$

Therefore:
$$\boxed{\sum_{r=n}^{2n} r(2r-3)(r+1) = \tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)(45n^2 - 5n - 28)}$$

with $a = 45$, $b = -5$, $c = -28$.

Verification at $n = 2$: $\sum_{r=2}^{4} = 2\cdot 1 \cdot 3 + 3 \cdot 3 \cdot 4 + 4 \cdot 5 \cdot 5 = 6 + 36 + 100 = 142$. Formula: $\tfrac{1}{6}(2)(3)(180 - 10 - 28) = \tfrac{1}{6}(6)(142) = 142$. $✓$

Key formula / concept

Standard sums in the formula booklet (use freely):
$$\sum_{r=1}^{n} r = \tfrac{1}{2}n(n+1), \quad \sum_{r=1}^{n} r^2 = \tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)(2n+1), \quad \sum_{r=1}^{n} r^3 = \tfrac{1}{4}n^2(n+1)^2$$

Split rule for arbitrary lower bounds:
$$\sum_{r=A}^{B} = \sum_{r=1}^{B} - \sum_{r=1}^{A-1}$$
(The $A-1$ is the trap --- do not write $A$.)

Exam checklist

  • [ ] Expanded the summand fully before applying $\Sigma$ termwise
  • [ ] Factored $\tfrac{1}{6}n(n+1)$ early to keep the working compact
  • [ ] In (b), used $\sum_{r=1}^{n-1}$ (not $\sum_{r=1}^{n}$) --- the lower limit subtraction
  • [ ] Verified with a small $n$ (e.g. $n = 2$) before submitting
  • [ ] Wrote $a, b, c$ as explicit integers at the end

Common pitfalls

  • Splitting $\sum_{r=n}^{2n} = \sum_{r=1}^{2n} - \sum_{r=1}^{n}$ --- off by one, drops the $r = n$ term.
  • Forgetting that $\sum_{r=1}^{2n} r^3 = \tfrac{1}{4}(2n)^2(2n+1)^2 = n^2(2n+1)^2$ --- substitute $n \to 2n$ carefully.
  • Dropping the $-3r$ contribution while factoring.
