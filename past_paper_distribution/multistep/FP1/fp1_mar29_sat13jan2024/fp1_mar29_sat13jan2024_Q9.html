Q9 — Complex equation, argument condition

Question

Given that
$$\frac{3z - 1}{2} = \frac{\lambda + 5i}{\lambda + 4i}$$
where $\lambda$ is a real constant,

(a) determine $z$, giving your answer in the form $x + yi$, where $x$ and $y$ are real and in terms of $\lambda$. (4)

Given also that $\arg z = \dfrac{\pi}{4}$,
(b) find the possible values of $\lambda$. (2)

OCR caveat: the printed denominator on the RHS may be $\lambda - 4i$ rather than $\lambda + 4i$ --- the working below uses $\lambda + 4i$ as printed. If the algebra in (b) yields no real solutions on your printed paper, suspect a sign typo in the denominator and re-run with $\lambda - 4i$ (this gives the clean answer $\lambda = 3 + \sqrt{17}$).

Correct method (as printed)

(a) Multiply both sides by $2(\lambda + 4i)$:
$$(3z - 1)(\lambda + 4i) = 2(\lambda + 5i)$$
$$3z(\lambda + 4i) - (\lambda + 4i) = 2\lambda + 10i$$
$$3z(\lambda + 4i) = 2\lambda + 10i + \lambda + 4i = 3\lambda + 14i$$
$$z = \frac{3\lambda + 14i}{3(\lambda + 4i)}$$

Realise the denominator by multiplying by $\overline{(\lambda + 4i)} = \lambda - 4i$:
$$z = \frac{(3\lambda + 14i)(\lambda - 4i)}{3(\lambda + 4i)(\lambda - 4i)} = \frac{(3\lambda + 14i)(\lambda - 4i)}{3(\lambda^2 + 16)}$$

Expand the numerator:
$$(3\lambda + 14i)(\lambda - 4i) = 3\lambda^2 - 12\lambda i + 14\lambda i - 56i^2 = 3\lambda^2 + 56 + 2\lambda i$$

Therefore:
$$\boxed{z = \frac{3\lambda^2 + 56}{3(\lambda^2 + 16)} + \frac{2\lambda}{3(\lambda^2 + 16)}\, i}$$

(b) $\arg z = \tfrac{\pi}{4}$ requires $\operatorname{Re}(z) = \operatorname{Im}(z)$ with both positive. Setting them equal:
$$\frac{3\lambda^2 + 56}{3(\lambda^2 + 16)} = \frac{2\lambda}{3(\lambda^2 + 16)} \implies 3\lambda^2 + 56 = 2\lambda \implies 3\lambda^2 - 2\lambda + 56 = 0$$

Discriminant: $4 - 4(3)(56) = 4 - 672 = -668 < 0$.

With the printed equation, this yields no real $\lambda$. This strongly indicates a sign typo in the source: re-solving with denominator $\lambda - 4i$ gives:
$$z = \frac{\lambda^2 - 8}{\lambda^2 + 16} + \frac{6\lambda}{\lambda^2 + 16}\,i$$
$$\operatorname{Re} = \operatorname{Im}: \quad \lambda^2 - 8 = 6\lambda \implies \lambda^2 - 6\lambda - 8 = 0 \implies \lambda = 3 \pm \sqrt{17}$$

Checking quadrant: for $\arg z$ to be exactly $\tfrac{\pi}{4}$ (not $-\tfrac{3\pi}{4}$) we need both Re and Im positive, so $\lambda > 0$ and $\lambda^2 > 8$, giving $\lambda = 3 + \sqrt{17}$. If the question is read as $\tan(\arg z) = 1$ (less strict), both values are admissible.

$$\boxed{\lambda = 3 + \sqrt{17} \quad (\text{strict}) \quad \text{or} \quad \lambda = 3 \pm \sqrt{17} \quad (\text{loose})}$$

Key formula / concept

  • To divide complex numbers, always multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate of the denominator.
  • $\arg z = \theta$ means both that $\tan(\arg z) = \tan\theta$ and that $z$ lies in the correct quadrant.

Exam checklist

  • [ ] Multiplied through by the denominator to clear fractions first
  • [ ] Rationalised by multiplying by the conjugate
  • [ ] Separated Re and Im in the final $x + yi$ form
  • [ ] For (b), explicitly equated Re and Im (not just $\tfrac{\operatorname{Im}}{\operatorname{Re}} = 1$)
  • [ ] Checked the quadrant for $\arg = \tfrac{\pi}{4}$ --- both parts must be positive

Common pitfalls

  • Forgetting $i^2 = -1$ when expanding the numerator (especially the $-56i^2 = +56$ term).
  • Cancelling the $(\lambda^2 + 16)$ denominator too early and losing track of the $/3$.
  • Reporting both roots without checking the quadrant condition.
