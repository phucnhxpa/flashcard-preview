Q6 — Linear interpolation & Newton--Raphson

Question

(i) $$f(x) = \sqrt{x} - 4\cos(5x), \quad x > 0$$
(a) Show that the equation $f(x) = 0$ has a root $\alpha$ in the interval $[2.5, 3.5]$. (2)
(b) Use linear interpolation once on the interval $[2.5, 3.5]$ to find an approximation to $\alpha$, giving your answer to 2 decimal places. (2)

(ii) $$g(x) = \frac{1}{10}x^2 + \frac{1}{2x} - 4, \quad x > 0$$
(a) Determine $g'(x)$. (2)
The equation $g(x) = 0$ has a root $\beta$ in the interval $[6, 7]$.
(b) Using $x_0 = 6$ as a first approximation to $\beta$, apply the Newton--Raphson procedure once to $g(x)$ to find a second approximation to $\beta$, giving your answer to 3 decimal places. (2)

Correct method --- Part (i): linear interpolation

(i)(a) Use radians throughout.

$$f(2.5) = \sqrt{2.5} - 4\cos(12.5) \approx 1.581 - 4(0.998) \approx 1.581 - 3.991 \approx -2.41$$
$$f(3.5) = \sqrt{3.5} - 4\cos(17.5) \approx 1.871 - 4(0.220) \approx 1.871 - 0.880 \approx +0.99$$

Since $f$ is continuous on $[2.5, 3.5]$ and changes sign, by the change-of-sign test there exists a root $\alpha \in (2.5, 3.5)$. ∎

(i)(b) Linear interpolation = "chord crosses x-axis" --- similar triangles:

$$\alpha \approx x_1 + \frac{|f(x_1)| \cdot (x_2 - x_1)}{|f(x_1)| + |f(x_2)|}$$

with $x_1 = 2.5$, $x_2 = 3.5$:
$$\alpha \approx 2.5 + \frac{2.41 \cdot (3.5 - 2.5)}{2.41 + 0.99} = 2.5 + \frac{2.41}{3.40} \approx 2.5 + 0.709 = 3.209$$

To 2 decimal places: $\alpha \approx \boxed{3.21}$.

Correct method --- Part (ii): Newton--Raphson

(ii)(a) Differentiate term by term. Rewrite $g$ first:
$$g(x) = \tfrac{1}{10}x^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}x^{-1} - 4$$
$$g'(x) = \tfrac{2}{10}x - \tfrac{1}{2}x^{-2} = \tfrac{x}{5} - \tfrac{1}{2x^2}$$

(ii)(b) Evaluate at $x_0 = 6$:
$$g(6) = \tfrac{36}{10} + \tfrac{1}{12} - 4 = 3.6 + 0.0833\overline{3} - 4 = -0.31\overline{6}$$

Exactly: $g(6) = \tfrac{216}{60} + \tfrac{5}{60} - \tfrac{240}{60} = -\tfrac{19}{60}$.

$$g'(6) = \tfrac{6}{5} - \tfrac{1}{72} = \tfrac{432}{360} - \tfrac{5}{360} = \tfrac{427}{360}$$

Apply Newton--Raphson:
$$x_1 = x_0 - \frac{g(x_0)}{g'(x_0)} = 6 - \frac{-19/60}{427/360} = 6 + \frac{19}{60} \cdot \frac{360}{427} = 6 + \frac{19 \cdot 6}{427} = 6 + \frac{114}{427}$$
$$x_1 \approx 6 + 0.26698 \approx 6.26698$$

To 3 decimal places: $\beta \approx \boxed{6.267}$.

What went wrong (general --- linear interpolation)

Without having seen the technique, the temptation is to compute the midpoint $(2.5 + 3.5)/2 = 3.00$ and submit that. That's bisection, not interpolation, and would lose marks. The chord method weights the answer toward whichever endpoint has $|f|$ smaller (closer to zero). Drill the formula visually: imagine the chord from $(x_1, f_1)$ to $(x_2, f_2)$ crossing the x-axis at $\alpha$ --- similar triangles give the linear-interpolation expression.

Key formula / concept

Newton--Raphson (IS in the formula booklet):
$$x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}$$

Linear interpolation (NOT in the formula booklet --- must memorise):
$$\alpha \approx x_1 - f(x_1)\cdot\frac{x_2 - x_1}{f(x_2) - f(x_1)}$$
Equivalently (using magnitudes when $f(x_1)$ and $f(x_2)$ have opposite signs):
$$\alpha \approx x_1 + |f(x_1)| \cdot \frac{x_2 - x_1}{|f(x_1)| + |f(x_2)|}$$

Exam checklist

  • [ ] Both $f(2.5)$ and $f(3.5)$ computed to at least 3 s.f. with calculator in radians
  • [ ] Explicit phrase "change of sign" and a statement that $f$ is continuous

  • [ ] Newton--Raphson formula quoted before substituting

Common pitfalls

  • Calculator in degrees instead of radians --- $\cos(12.5$^\circ$) \approx 0.976$ vs $\cos(12.5\text{ rad}) \approx 0.998$, completely different.
  • For Newton--Raphson, adding rather than subtracting $f/f'$ when $f(x_0)$ is negative --- the formula already handles the sign.
  • Computing $g'(x)$ as $\tfrac{x}{5} + \tfrac{1}{2x^2}$ (sign error on the $x^{-1}$ derivative --- recall $\tfrac{d}{dx}x^{-1} = -x^{-2}$).
  • For linear interpolation, treating it as "average of $x_1$ and $x_2$" (that's bisection --- different method, possibly zero marks).
