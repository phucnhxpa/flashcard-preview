Q3 — Tangent to rectangular hyperbola, triangle area

Question

The hyperbola $H$ has equation $xy = c^2$ where $c$ is a positive constant.

The point $P\left(ct, \dfrac{c}{t}\right)$, where $t > 0$, lies on $H$.

The tangent to $H$ at $P$ meets the x-axis at the point $A$ and meets the y-axis at the point $B$.

(a) Determine, in terms of $c$ and $t$,
(i) the coordinates of $A$,
(ii) the coordinates of $B$. (4)

Given that the area of triangle $AOB$, where $O$ is the origin, is 90 square units,
(b) determine the value of $c$, giving your answer as a simplified surd. (2)

Correct method

(a) Differentiate implicitly. From $xy = c^2$:
$$y + x\frac{dy}{dx} = 0 \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{y}{x}$$

At $P\left(ct, \tfrac{c}{t}\right)$:
$$\frac{dy}{dx}\bigg|_P = -\frac{c/t}{ct} = -\frac{1}{t^2}$$

Tangent equation (point--slope form):
$$y - \frac{c}{t} = -\frac{1}{t^2}(x - ct)$$

Multiply through by $t^2$:
$$t^2 y - ct = -(x - ct)$$
$$t^2 y - ct = -x + ct$$
$$x + t^2 y = 2ct$$

(i) $A$ is on the x-axis, so $y = 0$:
$$x = 2ct \implies A = (2ct, \; 0)$$

(ii) $B$ is on the y-axis, so $x = 0$:
$$t^2 y = 2ct \implies y = \frac{2c}{t} \implies B = \left(0, \; \frac{2c}{t}\right)$$

(b) $\triangle AOB$ is right-angled at $O$ (legs along the axes):
$$\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot |OA| \cdot |OB| = \frac{1}{2} \cdot (2ct) \cdot \frac{2c}{t} = 2c^2$$

Set equal to 90:
$$2c^2 = 90 \implies c^2 = 45 \implies c = \sqrt{45} = \boxed{3\sqrt{5}}$$

(Positive root taken because $c > 0$ is given.)

Key formula / concept

  • Standard result (worth memorising): for the rectangular hyperbola $xy = c^2$, the tangent at $P(ct, c/t)$ has equation $x + t^2 y = 2ct$, and meets the axes at $(2ct, 0)$ and $(0, 2c/t)$. The product $|OA||OB| = 4c^2$ is independent of $t$ --- a beautiful invariant.

Exam checklist

  • [ ] Used implicit differentiation rather than $y = c^2/x$ (both work; implicit is faster)
  • [ ] Tangent equation cleared of fractions before reading off intercepts
  • [ ] Stated $A$ and $B$ as coordinates (ordered pairs), not just x- and y-values
  • [ ] Simplified $\sqrt{45} = 3\sqrt{5}$ --- the question demands surd form
  • [ ] Rejected the negative root, citing "$c > 0$ given"

Common pitfalls

  • Differentiating $y = c^2/x$ as $y = c^2 x^{-1}$ and dropping the negative sign in $-c^2 x^{-2}$.
  • At $x = 0$, computing $y = 2ct/t^2 = 2c/t$ --- the $t^2$ cancels with the $t$ in $2ct$ to leave one $t$ in the denominator. Easy to leave as $2c/t^2$ in error.
  • Writing the area as $\tfrac{1}{2}(2ct)(2c/t) = 2ct \cdot c/t$ and "simplifying" to $2c^2t/t$ --- fine if done right, but a common point to drop the factor.
