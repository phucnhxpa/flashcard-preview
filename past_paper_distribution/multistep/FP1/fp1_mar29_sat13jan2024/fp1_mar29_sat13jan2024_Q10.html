This is the highest-priority redo on the paper --- induction is procedural and entirely learnable from a template.

The four-step induction template (universal)

  1. Base case $n = 1$: verify the statement.
  2. Inductive hypothesis: assume true for $n = k$.
  3. Inductive step: prove true for $n = k+1$ using the hypothesis.
  4. Conclusion: by induction, true for all $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$.

Correct method --- Part (i): matrix induction

Let $P(n)$ be the statement that $M^n = \begin{pmatrix} 1+2n & -n \\ 4n & 1-2n \end{pmatrix}$, where $M = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 \\ 4 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$.

Base case $n = 1$: RHS is $\begin{pmatrix} 1+2 & -1 \\ 4 & 1-2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 \\ 4 & -1 \end{pmatrix} = M$. So $P(1)$ is true. $✓$

Inductive hypothesis: Assume $P(k)$ is true, i.e.
$$M^k = \begin{pmatrix} 1+2k & -k \\ 4k & 1-2k \end{pmatrix}$$

Inductive step: Show $P(k+1)$ --- i.e. $M^{k+1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1+2(k+1) & -(k+1) \\ 4(k+1) & 1-2(k+1) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2k+3 & -k-1 \\ 4k+4 & -2k-1 \end{pmatrix}$.

Compute $M^{k+1} = M^k \cdot M$ (using the hypothesis):
$$M^{k+1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1+2k & -k \\ 4k & 1-2k \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 \\ 4 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Row-by-column:
- $(1,1)$: $(1+2k)(3) + (-k)(4) = 3 + 6k - 4k = 3 + 2k = 2k + 3$ $✓$
- $(1,2)$: $(1+2k)(-1) + (-k)(-1) = -1 - 2k + k = -1 - k = -(k+1)$ $✓$
- $(2,1)$: $(4k)(3) + (1-2k)(4) = 12k + 4 - 8k = 4k + 4 = 4(k+1)$ $✓$
- $(2,2)$: $(4k)(-1) + (1-2k)(-1) = -4k - 1 + 2k = -2k - 1 = 1 - 2(k+1)$ $✓$

So $M^{k+1} = \begin{pmatrix} 2k+3 & -(k+1) \\ 4(k+1) & 1-2(k+1) \end{pmatrix}$, which is exactly $P(k+1)$.

Conclusion: $P(1)$ true; $P(k) \Rightarrow P(k+1)$; therefore by the principle of mathematical induction, $P(n)$ is true for all $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$. ∎

Correct method --- Part (ii): divisibility induction

Let $P(n)$ be the statement that $f(n) = 8^{2n+1} + 6^{2n-1}$ is divisible by 7.

Base case $n = 1$:
$$f(1) = 8^3 + 6^1 = 512 + 6 = 518 = 7 \times 74$$
Divisible by 7. $✓$

Inductive hypothesis: Assume $f(k) = 8^{2k+1} + 6^{2k-1} = 7m$ for some integer $m$.

Inductive step: Show $f(k+1) = 8^{2k+3} + 6^{2k+1}$ is divisible by 7.

Method (the "$f(k+1) - \lambda f(k)$" trick): pick $\lambda = 36 = 6^2$ so that the $6^{2k-1}$ contributions cancel cleanly:
$$f(k+1) - 36 f(k) = 8^{2k+3} + 6^{2k+1} - 36\bigl(8^{2k+1} + 6^{2k-1}\bigr)$$
$$= 8^{2k+1}(8^2 - 36) + 6^{2k-1}(6^2 - 36)$$
$$= 8^{2k+1}(64 - 36) + 6^{2k-1}(0)$$
$$= 28 \cdot 8^{2k+1}$$
$$= 7 \cdot \bigl(4 \cdot 8^{2k+1}\bigr)$$

So $f(k+1) = 36 f(k) + 7 \cdot 4 \cdot 8^{2k+1}$.

  • $36 f(k)$ is divisible by 7 (since $f(k) = 7m$ by hypothesis).
  • $7 \cdot 4 \cdot 8^{2k+1}$ is divisible by 7 (explicit factor of 7).

The sum of two multiples of 7 is a multiple of 7, so $f(k+1)$ is divisible by 7.

Conclusion: $P(1)$ true; $P(k) \Rightarrow P(k+1)$; therefore by the principle of mathematical induction, $f(n) = 8^{2n+1} + 6^{2n-1}$ is divisible by 7 for all $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$. ∎

From the Loom (Mr. Fuat's universal induction template)

"$F_{k+1}$ equals to something $F_k$ plus something. \ldots This one is divisible by 16 [or 7], this one is divisible by 16 [or 7], so the sum must be divisible by 16 [or 7]."

The pattern is: express $f(k+1)$ as $A \cdot f(k) + B$ where both terms are visibly divisible by the target number. Choose $A$ to cancel one of the two exponential terms cleanly --- typically $A$ equals the square of one of the bases.

Key formula / concept

  • The conclusion paragraph is mark-bearing. Always finish induction proofs with the words "true for $n = 1$", "if true for $n = k$ then true for $n = k+1$", "therefore by induction true for all $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$". Examiners look for these explicit phrases.
  • For matrix induction, use $M^{k+1} = M^k \cdot M$ (right multiplication keeps your hypothesis on the left, where it's easier to substitute).
  • For divisibility induction, the subtraction trick $f(k+1) - \lambda f(k)$ is the cleanest method.

Exam checklist

  • [ ] Both base case and final conclusion explicitly written out
  • [ ] Hypothesis written symbolically (e.g. $f(k) = 7m$, not just "assume true")
  • [ ] In matrix proof, four matrix entries computed individually with arithmetic shown
  • [ ] In divisibility proof, the multiplier $\lambda$ chosen to expose a factor of the target
  • [ ] All four pillars present: base, hypothesis, step, conclusion

Common pitfalls

  • Computing $M^{k+1} = M \cdot M^k$ instead of $M^k \cdot M$ --- both are equal mathematically, but $M^k \cdot M$ has the hypothesis on the left, which is the natural place to substitute the assumed form.
  • Forgetting the "$+1$" in $n = k+1$: writing $f(k+1) = 8^{2k+1}$ (no!) instead of $8^{2(k+1)+1} = 8^{2k+3}$.
  • Stopping after showing the step works and skipping the conclusion sentence --- that's a lost mark.
  • Doing the subtraction trick with $\lambda = 8^2 = 64$ instead of $6^2 = 36$ --- both work, but $\lambda = 64$ leaves a $6^{2k-1}(36 - 64) = -28 \cdot 6^{2k-1}$ residue, equally fine.
