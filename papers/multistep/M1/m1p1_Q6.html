Q6 — Box on rough plane: rope at $\theta$ then rope removed

Question

A box of mass $m$ rests on a rough horizontal plane. It is pulled by a light rope inclined at angle $\theta$ above the horizontal, where $\tan\theta = 3/4$ (so $\sin\theta = 3/5$, $\cos\theta = 4/5$). The coefficient of friction between box and plane is $\mu = 1/3$.

(a) The box moves at constant speed. Find the tension $T$ in the rope, in terms of $m$ and $g$. [7]

(b) The rope is then removed and the box is projected horizontally with initial speed $u$. Find the distance $d$ travelled when its speed has dropped to $u/2$. [5]

[12 marks]

Part (a) — Constant speed: find $T$ [7 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3 M1 A1

Strategy: friction $F = \mu R$ depends on the normal reaction $R$. But $R$ isn’t simply $mg$ — because the rope pulls upward at an angle, it lifts part of the box’s weight off the plane. So I must resolve vertically first to find $R$.

Free-body diagram for the box:

• Weight $mg$ down.

• Rope tension $T$ at angle $\theta$ above horizontal: split into $T\cos\theta$ forward (horizontal) and $T\sin\theta$ up (vertical).

• Normal reaction $R$ up from plane.

• Friction $F$ backward (opposing motion).

Formula (vertical equilibrium — box stays on plane, no vertical motion):

$$\Sigma F_y = 0$$

Substitute:

$$R + T\sin\theta - mg = 0$$

$$R = mg - T\sin\theta$$

With $\sin\theta = 3/5$:

$$\boxed{\;R = mg - \tfrac{3T}{5}\;}$$

Trap: using $R = mg$ here — the rope lifts some weight off the plane, so $R < mg$. The vertical lift reduces normal reaction, which in turn reduces friction. Why $\mu R$ not $\mu mg$? Friction depends on how hard the surfaces press together, which is $R$, not $mg$. They’re only equal if the surface is horizontal AND no vertical applied force.
$R = mg - \tfrac{3T}{5}$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $R = mg - \frac{3T}{5}$.
STEP 2/3 B1

Strategy: the box moves at constant speed — no horizontal acceleration, but it IS sliding (kinetic friction is active). At the limiting case (no acceleration), friction takes its maximum value $F = \mu R$.

Formula (limiting/kinetic friction):

$$F = \mu R$$

where $\mu$ is the coefficient of friction and $R$ is the normal reaction.

Using $R$ from STEP 1 ($R = mg - \tfrac{3T}{5}$) and $\mu = 1/3$:

$$F = \tfrac{1}{3}\left(mg - \tfrac{3T}{5}\right)$$

Distribute (kept symbolic for the next step):

$$F = \tfrac{mg}{3} - \tfrac{T}{5}$$

$$\boxed{\;F = \tfrac{mg}{3} - \tfrac{T}{5}\;}$$

Why use $F = \mu R$ at constant speed? At constant speed, the box is sliding — kinetic friction is fully active. If the box were stationary, $F$ could be anything from $0$ to $\mu R$ (static friction). Here, motion guarantees we’re at the kinetic-friction value $\mu R$.
$F = \tfrac{1}{3}(mg - \tfrac{3T}{5})$
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: $F = \tfrac{mg}{3} - \tfrac{T}{5}$.
STEP 3/3 M1 A1 A1

Strategy: constant speed $\Rightarrow a = 0$ horizontally. So the horizontal forces balance: forward pull from rope ($T\cos\theta$) equals backward friction ($F$). Now substitute $F$ from STEP 2, and solve for $T$.

Formula (horizontal equilibrium, $a = 0$):

$$T\cos\theta - F = 0 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad T\cos\theta = F$$

Using $F$ from STEP 2 ($F = \tfrac{mg}{3} - \tfrac{T}{5}$) and $\cos\theta = 4/5$:

$$\tfrac{4T}{5} = \tfrac{mg}{3} - \tfrac{T}{5}$$

Add $\tfrac{T}{5}$ to both sides (collect $T$’s on the left):

$$\tfrac{4T}{5} + \tfrac{T}{5} = \tfrac{mg}{3}$$

$$\tfrac{5T}{5} = \tfrac{mg}{3}$$

$$T = \tfrac{mg}{3}$$

$$\boxed{\;T = \dfrac{mg}{3}\;}$$

Sense check (units & magnitude): $T$ has units of force (since $mg$ does) ✓. For a $1$ kg box, $T \approx 3.3$ N — about the weight of a third of an apple. Reasonable for a lightly loaded rough plane ($\mu = 1/3$ is moderate friction).
$\boxed{\,T = \dfrac{mg}{3}\,}$

Part (b) — Rope removed, find $d$ [5 marks] · 2 steps

Now: no rope, so $R = mg$, $F = \mu R = mg/3$. Initial speed $u$ along plane.
↳ from STEP 0:From STEP 1: $a = -g/3$ (deceleration, rope removed).
STEP 1/2 M1 A1

Strategy: rope removed $\Rightarrow$ no upward lift on the box. $R$ resets to $mg$ (full weight presses on the plane). Friction is now the only horizontal force, decelerating the box. Apply N2L to find the deceleration.

New normal reaction: with no rope, vertical equilibrium gives $R = mg$ directly.

New friction: $F = \mu R = \tfrac{1}{3} \cdot mg = \tfrac{mg}{3}$.

Free-body diagram (after rope removed): take direction of motion (forward) as positive.

• Friction $F = \tfrac{mg}{3}$ acts backward (opposing motion) $\Rightarrow -\tfrac{mg}{3}$.

• No other horizontal forces.

Formula (Newton’s 2nd law):

$$\Sigma F = m\, a$$

Substitute:

$$-\tfrac{mg}{3} = m\, a$$

Cancel $m$ (mass $\ne 0$):

$$a = -\tfrac{g}{3}$$

$$\boxed{\;a = -\dfrac{g}{3}\;}$$

Why $R = mg$ now? No vertical component from any applied force — just gravity down and contact force up. So vertical equilibrium gives $R = mg$ directly. Sign: negative $a$ means the box is decelerating; this matches expectation — friction always opposes motion.
$a = -g/3$
STEP 2/2 M1 A1 A1

Strategy: I have $u$, $v = u/2$, $a$, want $s = d$. The suvat formula linking $(u, v, a, s)$ — no time needed — is $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$.

List the knowns:

• $u_0 = u$ (initial speed)

• $v = u/2$ (when speed has dropped to half)

• $a = -g/3$ (from STEP 1)

• Want: $s = d$.

Formula (suvat):

$$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$

Substitute:

$$\left(\tfrac{u}{2}\right)^2 = u^2 + 2\left(-\tfrac{g}{3}\right) d$$

$$\tfrac{u^2}{4} = u^2 - \tfrac{2gd}{3}$$

Rearrange to isolate the $d$-term:

$$\tfrac{2gd}{3} = u^2 - \tfrac{u^2}{4} = \tfrac{4u^2 - u^2}{4} = \tfrac{3u^2}{4}$$

Solve for $d$:

$$d = \tfrac{3u^2}{4} \cdot \tfrac{3}{2g} = \tfrac{9u^2}{8g}$$

$$\boxed{\;d = \dfrac{9u^2}{8g}\;}$$

Sense check: $d \propto u^2$ — doubling the initial speed gives 4× the distance to halve, which makes physical sense (kinetic energy scales as $v^2$). Also $d$ has units $[u^2/g] = (\text{m/s})^2 / (\text{m/s}^2) = \text{m}$ ✓.
$\boxed{\,d = \dfrac{9u^2}{8g}\,}$
