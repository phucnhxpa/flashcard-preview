Q8 — A on rough plane, B hanging via pulley; show $a = g/3$ then total distance

Question

$A$ (mass $2m$) is on a rough plane inclined at $\alpha$ where $\tan\alpha = 5/12$ (so $\sin\alpha = 5/13$, $\cos\alpha = 12/13$). $B$ (mass $3m$) hangs vertically from a light inextensible string passing over a smooth pulley at the top of the plane. Coefficient of friction $\mu = 11/36$. The system is released from rest and $A$ moves up the plane.

(a) Show that the friction force on $A$ is $\dfrac{22mg}{39}$. [3]

(b) Write the equation of motion for $B$. [2]

(c) Show that $a = g/3$. [4]

(d) $B$ falls a distance $h$ to the ground (string then goes slack). Find the total distance $A$ travels from release until first coming to rest, in terms of $h$. [6]

[15 marks]

Part (a) — Show $F = 22mg/39$ [3 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2 M1

Strategy: resolve perpendicular to the slope to find the normal reaction $R$, since friction depends on $R$. $A$ stays on the plane (no perpendicular acceleration), so forces perpendicular to the slope balance.

Forces on $A$ (mass $2m$), perpendicular to slope:

• Weight component perpendicular to slope: $2mg\cos\alpha$ (pressing into slope).

• Normal reaction $R$ (out of slope).

Formula (perpendicular equilibrium):

$$R = m_A\, g\cos\alpha$$

Substitute $m_A = 2m$ and $\cos\alpha = 12/13$:

$$R = 2m \cdot g \cdot \tfrac{12}{13} = \tfrac{24mg}{13}$$

$$\boxed{\;R = \tfrac{24mg}{13}\;}$$

Why $\cos\alpha$ and not just $g$? On a slope, only the component of weight perpendicular to the surface presses on the surface. That’s $mg\cos\alpha$, which is < $mg$ (since $\cos\alpha < 1$ for $\alpha > 0$). So $R$ on a slope is always smaller than $R$ on a flat surface.
$R = 24mg/13$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $R = 24mg/13$.
STEP 2/2 A1 A1

Strategy: with $R$ from STEP 1 and $\mu$ given, just apply the friction formula. Since $A$ moves up the slope (and is sliding), friction is at its kinetic value $\mu R$.

Formula (friction at limiting/kinetic value):

$$F = \mu R$$

Using $R = \tfrac{24mg}{13}$ from STEP 1 and $\mu = 11/36$:

$$F = \tfrac{11}{36} \cdot \tfrac{24mg}{13}$$

Cancel: $\tfrac{24}{36} = \tfrac{2}{3}$:

$$F = \tfrac{11 \cdot 2}{3 \cdot 13}\, mg = \tfrac{22mg}{39} \quad\blacksquare$$

$$\boxed{\;F = \tfrac{22mg}{39}\;}$$

Direction of friction: $A$ moves UP the slope → friction opposes the motion, acting DOWN the slope. We’ll use this in part (c) when writing N2L.
$\boxed{\,F = 22mg/39\,}$ ✓

Part (b) — Equation of motion for $B$ [2 marks] · 1 step

Setup: $B$ (mass $3m$) hangs vertically from a string over a smooth pulley; since $A$ moves up the slope, $B$ accelerates downward with the system’s common $a$.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Strategy: $B$ hangs from a string going up to the pulley. Since $A$ moves up the slope, $B$ moves down (string is inextensible). Apply N2L to $B$ alone, with down positive (matches direction of motion).

Free-body diagram for $B$ (mass $3m$):

• Weight $3mg$ down $\Rightarrow +3mg$.

• String tension $T$ up (pulling toward pulley) $\Rightarrow -T$.

(Smooth pulley + light inextensible string → same $T$ on both sides.)

Formula (Newton’s 2nd law for B):

$$\Sigma F = m_B\, a$$

Substitute:

$$3mg - T = 3m\, a$$

$$\boxed{\;3mg - T = 3ma\;}$$

Why same $a$ for $A$ and $B$? Light, inextensible string over a smooth pulley → magnitude of acceleration is the same for both particles. Why same $T$? Light string over smooth pulley → tension uniform throughout.
$\boxed{\,3mg - T = 3ma\,}$

Part (c) — Show $a = g/3$ [4 marks] · 2 steps

From (a): $F = 22mg/39$. From (b): $3mg - T = 3ma$.
STEP 1/2 M1 A1

Strategy: apply N2L along the slope for $A$. Three forces along the slope: tension (up), gravity component (down), friction (down, since $A$ moves up). Take up-slope as positive (direction of motion).

Forces on $A$ along slope (up = +):

• Tension $T$ up the slope (toward pulley) $\Rightarrow +T$.

• Weight component along slope: $2mg\sin\alpha$ down the slope $\Rightarrow -2mg\sin\alpha$.

• Friction $F$ down the slope (opposes upward motion) $\Rightarrow -F$.

Formula (N2L for A along slope):

$$T - 2mg\sin\alpha - F = m_A\, a = 2m\, a$$

Using $F = 22mg/39$ from (a) and $\sin\alpha = 5/13$:

$$T - 2mg \cdot \tfrac{5}{13} - \tfrac{22mg}{39} = 2ma$$

$$T - \tfrac{10mg}{13} - \tfrac{22mg}{39} = 2ma$$

Common denominator 39: $\tfrac{10mg}{13} = \tfrac{30mg}{39}$.

$$T - \tfrac{30mg}{39} - \tfrac{22mg}{39} = 2ma$$

$$T - \tfrac{52mg}{39} = 2ma$$

$$\boxed{\;T = 2ma + \tfrac{52mg}{39}\;}$$

Why friction down the slope? Friction opposes the actual motion. $A$ moves up → friction down. If the system were released and $A$ moved down, we’d flip friction to point up. Always check direction of motion first.
$T = 2ma + 52mg/39$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $T = 2ma + 52mg/39$ (A-equation). From part (b): $3mg - T = 3ma$.
STEP 2/2 M1 A1

Strategy: two equations, two unknowns ($T$ and $a$). Add them to eliminate $T$ — it appears with opposite signs once the equations are aligned.

From (b): $3mg - T = 3ma \Rightarrow T = 3mg - 3ma$.

From STEP 1 of part (c): $T - \tfrac{52mg}{39} = 2ma$.

Substitute $T$ from (b) into the A-equation:

$$(3mg - 3ma) - \tfrac{52mg}{39} = 2ma$$

Move $-3ma$ to RHS:

$$3mg - \tfrac{52mg}{39} = 2ma + 3ma = 5ma$$

Common denominator on LHS: $3mg = \tfrac{117mg}{39}$.

$$\tfrac{117mg - 52mg}{39} = 5ma$$

$$\tfrac{65mg}{39} = 5ma$$

Divide both sides by $5m$:

$$a = \tfrac{65g}{39 \cdot 5} = \tfrac{65g}{195} = \tfrac{13g}{39} = \tfrac{g}{3} \quad\blacksquare$$

$$\boxed{\;a = \dfrac{g}{3}\;}$$

Sense check: $a = g/3 \approx 3.27$ m/s$^2$ — modest acceleration. Less than $g$ → reasonable, since friction and gravity-along-slope both retard motion. If $a > g$, that would be unphysical here.
$\boxed{\,a = g/3\,}$ ✓

Part (d) — Total distance $A$ travels [6 marks] · 3 steps

Phase 1: while string is taut, $a_1 = g/3$. $B$ falls distance $h$, so $A$ moves $h$ up the slope. Phase 2: $B$ hits ground → $T = 0$, but $A$ keeps moving up.
STEP 1/3 M1

Strategy: Phase 1 (taut string): system released from rest, $A$ travels up the slope with $a = g/3$ for distance $h$ (matching $B$’s drop). Use suvat $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ to find the speed when $B$ hits the ground.

Phase 1 knowns:

• $u = 0$ (released from rest)

• $a = g/3$ (from part c)

• $s = h$ (distance $A$ travels = $h$, since string inextensible and $B$ drops $h$)

• Want: $v^2$ at end of Phase 1.

Formula (suvat):

$$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$

Substitute:

$$v^2 = 0 + 2 \cdot \tfrac{g}{3} \cdot h = \tfrac{2gh}{3}$$

$$\boxed{\;v^2 = \tfrac{2gh}{3}\;}\ \text{(at end of Phase 1)}$$

Note: we keep $v^2$ symbolic — we’ll plug it directly into Phase 2’s suvat without needing $v$ alone. Saves a square root.
$v^2 = 2gh/3$ at end of Phase 1
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $v^2 = 2gh/3$ at end of Phase 1 (speed² when string goes slack).
STEP 2/3 M1 A1

Strategy: $B$ hits ground at end of Phase 1 → string goes slack $\Rightarrow T = 0$. But $A$ still has speed (from Phase 1) and keeps moving up the slope, decelerating under gravity-along-slope AND friction (both pointing down the slope, since $A$ still moves up).

Forces on $A$ in Phase 2 (up-slope = +):

• Tension: $0$ (string slack).

• Weight component down slope: $-2mg\sin\alpha = -2mg \cdot \tfrac{5}{13} = -\tfrac{10mg}{13}$.

• Friction $F = \tfrac{22mg}{39}$, still down the slope (opposes the still-upward velocity) $\Rightarrow -\tfrac{22mg}{39}$.

Formula (N2L):

$$\Sigma F = m_A\, a_2$$

Substitute:

$$-\tfrac{10mg}{13} - \tfrac{22mg}{39} = 2m\, a_2$$

Common denominator 39: $\tfrac{10mg}{13} = \tfrac{30mg}{39}$.

$$-\tfrac{30mg}{39} - \tfrac{22mg}{39} = 2m\, a_2$$

$$-\tfrac{52mg}{39} = 2m\, a_2$$

$$a_2 = -\tfrac{52g}{2 \cdot 39} = -\tfrac{26g}{39} = -\tfrac{2g}{3}$$

$$\boxed{\;a_2 = -\dfrac{2g}{3}\;}$$

Critical: friction direction always opposes the velocity, NOT the acceleration. $A$ is still moving up after the string goes slack → friction still acts down the slope. Don’t flip friction just because the string went slack — $A$’s motion direction hasn’t reversed yet. Sense check: $|a_2| = 2g/3$ is twice $|a_1| = g/3$, because in Phase 1 tension was helping accelerate $A$ up; now there’s no help, only retarding forces. Decel doubles → plausible.
$a_2 = -2g/3$
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: $a_2 = -2g/3$. From STEP 1: $u_2^2 = v^2 = 2gh/3$.
STEP 3/3 M1 A1 A1

Strategy: in Phase 2, $A$ slows from $v$ (end of Phase 1) to $0$ (momentarily at rest at top of trajectory). Use $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ to find $s_2$, then add to Phase-1 distance $h$.

Phase 2 knowns:

• $u_2^2 = \tfrac{2gh}{3}$ (from STEP 1)

• $v_2 = 0$ (first comes to rest)

• $a_2 = -\tfrac{2g}{3}$ (from STEP 2)

• Want: $s_2$.

Formula (suvat):

$$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$

Substitute:

$$0 = \tfrac{2gh}{3} + 2 \cdot \left(-\tfrac{2g}{3}\right) \cdot s_2$$

$$0 = \tfrac{2gh}{3} - \tfrac{4g}{3}\, s_2$$

$$\tfrac{4g}{3}\, s_2 = \tfrac{2gh}{3}$$

$$s_2 = \tfrac{2gh}{3} \cdot \tfrac{3}{4g} = \tfrac{h}{2}$$

Total distance $A$ travels:

$$\text{Total} = \text{Phase 1} + \text{Phase 2} = h + \tfrac{h}{2} = \tfrac{3h}{2}$$

$$\boxed{\;\text{Total distance} = \dfrac{3h}{2}\;}$$

Sense check: Phase 2 distance ($h/2$) is half of Phase 1 ($h$), even though Phase-1 acceleration was only half of Phase-2 deceleration. That’s because Phase 1 starts from rest (so it builds up speed gradually), while Phase 2 starts at full speed and just decelerates — less distance covered. ✓
$\boxed{\,\text{Total distance} = \dfrac{3h}{2}\,}$
