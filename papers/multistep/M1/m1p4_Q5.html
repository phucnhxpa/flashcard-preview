Q5 — Car · Motorbike: equal distance at $T$

Question

A car accelerates from rest for $30$ s, reaching speed $V$ m/s, then continues at constant $V$ until $t = T$ s. Total distance in $[0, T]$ is to be found.

(a) Show car's distance is $V(T - 15)$ m. [2]

(b) A motorbike (same acceleration) starts at $t = 10$ s, accelerates for $40$ s, constant for $10$ s, then decelerates to speed $V$ by $t = T$. Sketch its speed-time graph. [2]

(c) Show the motorbike's constant speed is $4V/3$. [2]

(d) Distance$_\text{car}$ = Distance$_\text{bike}$ at $t = T$. Find $T$. [5]

[11 marks]

Part (a) — Car distance [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Strategy: on a speed-time graph, area under curve $=$ distance travelled. Split into two phases (acceleration triangle, then constant-speed rectangle) and add.

Formula (speed-time area): $s = \int v\,dt = \text{area under } v(t)$.

Phase 1 (acceleration, $0 \to 30$ s): triangle with base $30$, height $V$.

$\;\;A_1 = \tfrac{1}{2}(30)(V) = 15V$

Phase 2 (constant, $30 \to T$ s): rectangle with base $(T - 30)$, height $V$.

$\;\;A_2 = V(T - 30)$

Total distance:

$\;\;s = A_1 + A_2 = 15V + V(T - 30)$

Expand and simplify:

$\;\;s = 15V + VT - 30V = VT - 15V$

$\;\;s = V(T - 15)$

$$\boxed{\;s_{\text{car}} = V(T - 15)\;}\quad\blacksquare$$

Show-question care: the answer is given, so workings must be airtight. Always state both phases explicitly with their geometric shapes, never combine or skip steps.
$s_{\text{car}} = V(T - 15)$ ✓

Part (b) — Motorbike sketch [2 marks] · 1 step

From question: bike starts at rest at $t = 10$, accelerates $40$ s (same $a$ as car) to peak $V_m$, holds for $10$ s ($t = 50 \to 60$), then decelerates to speed $V$ at $t = T$. Car peak shown in part (a) was $V$ at $t = 30$.
STEP 1/1 B1 B1

Bike starts at $(10, 0)$:

• Phase A ($10 \to 50$): linear rise, slope $V/30$, ends at $(50, V_m)$.

• Phase B ($50 \to 60$): constant $V_m$.

• Phase C ($60 \to T$): linear drop to $(T, V)$.

Three linear segments forming a 'trapezoid + ramp'.

Part (c) — Show constant speed = $4V/3$ [2 marks] · 1 step

From question / (a): car reaches $V$ in $30$ s $\Rightarrow a = V/30$. Bike has same $a$ and accelerates for $40$ s.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Strategy: the bike has the same constant acceleration as the car. Find $a$ from the car (rate of change of speed), then apply it to the bike’s longer accel phase.

Formula: for constant acceleration starting from rest, $v = at$.

Car reaches speed $V$ in $30$ s starting from rest:

$\;\;a = \dfrac{V - 0}{30} = \dfrac{V}{30}$

Bike accelerates for $40$ s at the same $a$:

$\;\;V_m = a \cdot 40 = \dfrac{V}{30} \cdot 40$

Simplify:

$\;\;V_m = \dfrac{40V}{30} = \dfrac{4V}{3}$

$$\boxed{\;V_m = \dfrac{4V}{3}\;}\quad\blacksquare$$

Same-$a$ rule: "same acceleration" means same gradient on the speed-time graph. Bike accelerates $40/30 = 4/3$ times longer \u2192 reaches $4/3$ times higher speed.
$V_m = 4V/3$ ✓

Part (d) — Solve for $T$ [5 marks] · 2 steps

From (a): $s_{\text{car}} = V(T-15)$. From (c): $V_m = 4V/3$ for the bike. Bike phases (from (b)): A ($10\to 50$, $0\to 4V/3$), B ($50\to 60$, constant $4V/3$), C ($60\to T$, $4V/3 \to V$). Set $s_{\text{bike}} = s_{\text{car}}$.
STEP 1/2 M1 A1 A1

Strategy: bike’s distance = total area under its three-phase speed-time graph. Compute each phase’s area separately, then sum.

Formula (trapezium area): $A = \tfrac{1}{2}(b_1 + b_2)h$ where $b_1, b_2$ are the parallel sides.

Phase A ($t = 10 \to 50$, $v = 0 \to 4V/3$): triangle, base $40$, height $4V/3$.

$\;\;A_A = \tfrac{1}{2}(40)\left(\tfrac{4V}{3}\right) = \dfrac{80V}{3}$

Phase B ($t = 50 \to 60$, $v$ constant at $4V/3$): rectangle, base $10$, height $4V/3$.

$\;\;A_B = (10)\left(\tfrac{4V}{3}\right) = \dfrac{40V}{3}$

Phase C ($t = 60 \to T$, $v = 4V/3 \to V$): trapezium, parallel sides $4V/3$ and $V$, base $(T - 60)$.

$\;\;A_C = \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(\tfrac{4V}{3} + V\right)(T - 60) = \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(\tfrac{7V}{3}\right)(T - 60) = \dfrac{7V(T - 60)}{6}$

Total bike distance:

$\;\;s_{\text{bike}} = \dfrac{80V}{3} + \dfrac{40V}{3} + \dfrac{7V(T-60)}{6}$

Combine first two terms (common denom 3):

$\;\;s_{\text{bike}} = \dfrac{120V}{3} + \dfrac{7V(T-60)}{6} = 40V + \dfrac{7V(T-60)}{6}$

$$\boxed{\;s_{\text{bike}} = 40V + \dfrac{7V(T-60)}{6}\;}$$

Phase-bookkeeping pitfall: the bike starts at $t=10$, not $t=0$. Phase A duration is $50 - 10 = 40$ s (not $50$). Off-by-ten errors are common here \u2014 always subtract start time from end time for each phase.
$s_{\text{bike}} = 40V + \dfrac{7V(T-60)}{6}$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $s_{\text{bike}} = 40V + \dfrac{7V(T-60)}{6}$.
STEP 2/2 M1 A1

Strategy: the question states that car distance equals bike distance at $t = T$. Set the two expressions equal and solve the linear equation in $T$.

Equate $s_{\text{car}} = s_{\text{bike}}$:

$\;\;V(T - 15) = 40V + \dfrac{7V(T-60)}{6}$

Divide every term by $V$ (assuming $V \ne 0$):

$\;\;T - 15 = 40 + \dfrac{7(T-60)}{6}$

Multiply every term by $6$ to clear the fraction:

$\;\;6(T - 15) = 6(40) + 7(T - 60)$

$\;\;6T - 90 = 240 + 7T - 420$

Simplify the RHS:

$\;\;6T - 90 = 7T - 180$

Subtract $6T$ from both sides:

$\;\;-90 = T - 180$

Add $180$:

$\;\;T = 90$

$$\boxed{\;T = 90\ \text{s}\;}$$

Sense check: $T = 90$ s is well after the bike\u2019s constant phase ends ($t = 60$), so phase C does occur \u2014 the answer is consistent with the problem geometry.
$\boxed{\,T = 90\ \text{s}\,}$
