Q2 — Resultant of $P$ (10 N due W) and $Q$ (8 N on bearing 330°)

Question

Two forces act on a particle:

• $P$ has magnitude $10$ N, acting due west.

• $Q$ has magnitude $8$ N, acting on a bearing of $330°$.

Find $|F|$ where $F = P + Q$, giving your answer to 3 s.f.

[4 marks]

Solution [4 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3bearing rule

Strategy: a bearing is a number, not a direction we can use in trig directly. I need to translate '$330°$' into an angle measured from a familiar axis (N, S, E, or W) before I can apply the cosine rule.

Rule (bearings): bearings are measured clockwise from North, in the range $0°$ to $360°$.

Decode $330°$: start at N, rotate clockwise:

$\;\;0° \to 90°$ = N to E (clockwise).

$\;\;180°$ = due S; $\;270°$ = due W; $\;360°$ = back to N.

$330°$ is $360° - 30°$, i.e. $30°$ short of N going the long way — so $30°$ west of N.

$$\boxed{\;Q\text{ points } 30°\text{ west of North}\;}$$

Phuc's slip (verbatim): “In bearing, we have to start from a north axis and go anti-clockwise.” Tutor correction: bearings go clockwise from N, NOT anti-clockwise. Ordinary trig angles are anti-clockwise from East — the two conventions are opposite, don’t conflate them. Sense check: $330°$ is just below $360°$ (which is N), so the arrow lies in the upper-left (NW) sector ✓.
$Q$ acts at $30°$ west of North.
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $Q$ acts at $30°$ west of North.
STEP 2/3 M1

Strategy: the cosine rule needs the angle between the two force vectors when drawn from the same starting point. Both $P$ and $Q$ act at the particle, so I just rotate from one direction to the other.

Directions:

• $P$ points due W (horizontal, leftward on the compass).

• $Q$ points $30°$ W of N (from STEP 1) — i.e. tilted $30°$ from the N-axis toward W.

Rotate from $P$ to $Q$:

$\;\;P$ (W) $\to$ N: that's a $90°$ rotation (one quarter-turn).

$\;\;$N $\to Q$ (just $30°$ off N, on the W side): another $30°$.

$$\theta = 90° + 30° = 120°$$

$$\boxed{\;\theta = 120°\;}$$

Sense check: $P$ and $Q$ are both in the upper-left half (W and NW). Two vectors in the same half-plane can never be more than $180°$ apart, and they’re obviously more than $90°$ apart (they don’t form a right angle). So $120°$ is in the right ballpark.
$\theta = 120°$
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: $\theta = 120°$ (included angle between $P$ and $Q$).
STEP 3/3 M1 A1 A1

Strategy: I have two sides ($|P|$, $|Q|$) and the included angle ($\theta = 120°$ from STEP 2). With these three pieces, the cosine rule directly gives me the third side — here, the magnitude of the resultant $|F|$.

Formula (cosine rule, triangle of forces with $\theta$ as the angle between $P$ and $Q$ when drawn tail-to-tail):

$$|F|^2 = P^2 + Q^2 - 2PQ\cos\theta$$

(Note: this is the standard cosine-rule identity with the included angle. Some books write $+2PQ\cos\theta$ for vector addition — that uses the supplementary angle from the parallelogram. They’re algebraically equivalent.)

Substitute $P = 10$, $Q = 8$, $\theta = 120°$:

$\;\;P^2 = 100$

$\;\;Q^2 = 64$

$\;\;2PQ = 2(10)(8) = 160$

$\;\;\cos 120° = -\tfrac{1}{2}$  (it’s in Q2, so cosine is negative)

$$|F|^2 = 100 + 64 - 160 \cdot \left(-\tfrac{1}{2}\right) = 164 + 80 = 244$$

Simplify the surd:

$\;\;244 = 4 \cdot 61$, so $\sqrt{244} = 2\sqrt{61}$.

$$\boxed{\;|F| = 2\sqrt{61} \approx 15.6\ \text{N}\;}$$

Sign trap: $\cos 120° = -\tfrac{1}{2}$, so $-2PQ\cos\theta = -160 \cdot (-\tfrac{1}{2}) = +80$ — the minus signs cancel and the cosine rule effectively adds 80 here, NOT subtract. Sense check: $|F|$ should sit between $|P-Q| = 2$ N and $|P+Q| = 18$ N — $15.6$ N ✓.
Phuc's second slip (verbatim): “In the second part, I forgot about the cosine rule.” Trigger to memorise: 2 sides + included angle (no opposite side known) → cosine rule is the unique fit. Sine rule needs at least one matched (side, opposite-angle) pair.
$\boxed{\,|F| = 2\sqrt{61} \approx 15.6\ \text{N}\,}$
