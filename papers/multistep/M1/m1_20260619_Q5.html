Part (a) — Forces & axes on an inclined plane [2 steps]

A particle on a plane inclined at $\theta$ to the horizontal. Resolving along/perpendicular to the plane minimises component splits and sign errors.
STEP 1/2list the four forces

Strategy. Draw and label each force with its variable name AND direction. Missing or unlabelled arrows lose method marks.

  • Weight $W = mg$ — vertically down, from the centre of mass.
  • Normal reaction $R$ — perpendicular to the plane, from the surface.
  • Friction $F$ — along the plane, opposing motion (or impending motion).
  • Any applied force — with its angle stated explicitly.
Phuc's mistake. Unlabelled arrows — no variable names, no direction arrows. Harry: "label each force with its variable name AND direction… that's where the method marks come from."
↳ from STEP 1: forces drawn and labelled; only weight needs splitting.
STEP 2/2choose axes along & perpendicular to the plane

Strategy. Choose axes ALONG and PERPENDICULAR to the plane — not horizontal/vertical. Then $R$ and $F$ align with the axes and only the weight needs splitting into components.

Components of weight

$$\text{Along plane: } W\sin\theta,\qquad \text{Perpendicular to plane: } W\cos\theta.$$
Why this axis choice. Fewer component splits = fewer sign errors. With horizontal/vertical axes you would have to split BOTH $R$ and $F$, doubling the chances of a sign slip.

Sense check. On a horizontal plane ($\theta = 0$): $W\sin 0 = 0$ (no along-plane weight), $W\cos 0 = W = mg$ (full weight into the plane) — matches intuition. ✓

Key: axes along & perpendicular to plane; split weight only: $mg\sin\theta$, $mg\cos\theta$.
