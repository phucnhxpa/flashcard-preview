Q6 — Particle on rough surface, force at angle: show $P \le 5W/8$

Question

A particle of weight $W$ rests on a rough horizontal surface. It is acted on by a force $P$ at angle $\alpha$ above the horizontal, where $\tan\alpha = 3/4$ (so $\sin\alpha = 3/5$, $\cos\alpha = 4/5$). The coefficient of friction is $\mu = 4/5$. The particle is in equilibrium (does not move).

Show that $P \leq \dfrac{5W}{8}$.

[7 marks]

Solution [7 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3 M1 A1

Strategy: resolve $P$ into horizontal and vertical components. The vertical component lifts the particle, reducing the normal reaction $R$ from the surface.

Formula (vertical equilibrium): $\Sigma F_y = 0 \Rightarrow R + P\sin\alpha = W$.

Components of $P$ at angle $\alpha$ above horizontal:

$\;\;\bullet$ Vertical (up): $P\sin\alpha$

$\;\;\bullet$ Horizontal (forward): $P\cos\alpha$

Vertical balance \u2014 forces up vs forces down:

$\;\;R + P\sin\alpha = W$

Rearrange for $R$:

$\;\;R = W - P\sin\alpha$

Substitute $\sin\alpha = 3/5$:

$\;\;R = W - \dfrac{3P}{5}$

$$\boxed{\;R = W - \dfrac{3P}{5}\;}$$

Lift effect: because $P$ has an upward component, the surface pushes back less than $W$. Forgetting this gives $R = W$, which is the classic mistake (and would give the wrong final inequality).
$R = W - 3P/5$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $R = W - 3P/5$.
STEP 2/3 M1

Strategy: the particle is in equilibrium (does not move), so horizontal forces also balance. Friction adjusts itself to exactly cancel the horizontal pull of $P$.

Formula (horizontal equilibrium): $\Sigma F_x = 0 \Rightarrow F = P\cos\alpha$.

Substitute $\cos\alpha = 4/5$:

$\;\;F = P \cdot \dfrac{4}{5} = \dfrac{4P}{5}$

$$\boxed{\;F = \dfrac{4P}{5}\;}$$

Static vs limiting: while the particle is at rest, $F$ matches whatever horizontal force is applied (up to the limit $\mu R$). Here we are not yet imposing the inequality \u2014 we just write $F = P\cos\alpha$ for static balance.
$F = 4P/5$
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: $F = 4P/5$. From STEP 1: $R = W - 3P/5$.
STEP 3/3 M1 A1 M1 A1

Strategy: static friction can be anything from $0$ up to its max value $\mu R$. For the particle to remain at rest, the required friction $F$ must not exceed this max. Plug in $F$ and $R$ from STEPS 1\u20132 and solve for $P$.

Formula (friction inequality): $F \le \mu R$ for no slipping.

Substitute $F = 4P/5$, $R = W - 3P/5$, $\mu = 4/5$:

$\;\;\dfrac{4P}{5} \le \dfrac{4}{5}\!\left(W - \dfrac{3P}{5}\right)$

Multiply both sides by $5$:

$\;\;4P \le 4\!\left(W - \dfrac{3P}{5}\right)$

Multiply both sides by $5$ again to clear the inner fraction:

$\;\;20P \le 20W - 12P$

Add $12P$ to both sides:

$\;\;32P \le 20W$

Divide by $32$:

$\;\;P \le \dfrac{20W}{32} = \dfrac{5W}{8}$

$$\boxed{\;P \le \dfrac{5W}{8}\;}\quad\blacksquare$$

Common pitfall (per Phuc\u2019s redo): using $R = W$ instead of $R = W - P\sin\alpha$ gives the wrong inequality. The vertical lift from $P$ reduces the normal reaction \u2014 always include the $P\sin\alpha$ term.
$\boxed{\,P \leq \dfrac{5W}{8}\,}$ ✓
