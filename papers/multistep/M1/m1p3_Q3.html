Q3 — Hammer (1.8 kg) hits peg (0.2 kg); find $v$, impulse, $R$

Question

A hammer of mass $1.8$ kg moving at $10$ m/s strikes a stationary tent peg of mass $0.2$ kg. After the impact, the hammer and peg move together with common speed $v$ m/s. The combined object is then brought to rest by a constant resistance from the ground over a depth of $12$ cm. Take $g = 9.8\,\text{m/s}^2$.

(a) Find $v$. [2]

(b) Find the magnitude of the impulse exerted on the peg by the hammer (with units). [3]

(c) Find the magnitude of the resistance $R$. [5]

[10 marks]

Part (a) — Find $v$ [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Strategy: at the moment of collision, momentum is conserved (no external horizontal impulse during the brief strike). After the strike, both move together → perfectly inelastic collision → single common velocity $v$.

Sign convention: take direction of hammer’s motion as positive.

Formula (conservation of momentum):

$$m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = (m_1 + m_2) v$$

Substitute $m_1 = 1.8$ kg, $u_1 = 10$ m/s (hammer), $m_2 = 0.2$ kg, $u_2 = 0$ (peg at rest):

$$1.8 \cdot 10 + 0.2 \cdot 0 = (1.8 + 0.2)\, v$$

$$18 + 0 = 2.0\, v$$

$$v = \tfrac{18}{2} = 9\ \text{m/s}$$

$$\boxed{\;v = 9\ \text{m/s}\;}$$

Why momentum, not energy? Inelastic collision → KE is NOT conserved (some lost to deformation/heat). But momentum IS conserved (no external horizontal force during the strike). Sense check: $v < u_1$ since the hammer transfers momentum to the previously-stationary peg — makes sense.
$\boxed{\,v = 9\ \text{m/s}\,}$

Part (b) — Impulse on peg [3 marks] · 1 step

From (a): $v = 9$ m/s. Peg starts at rest, ends at $9$ m/s.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1 A1

Strategy: impulse on the peg = change in the peg’s momentum. The peg starts at rest and ends at $9$ m/s — just compute $\Delta p$ for the peg alone.

Formula (impulse-momentum theorem):

$$I = \Delta p = m_{\text{peg}}(v_{\text{after}} - u_{\text{before}})$$

Substitute $m_{\text{peg}} = 0.2$ kg, $u_{\text{before}} = 0$, $v_{\text{after}} = 9$ m/s:

$$I = 0.2 \cdot (9 - 0)$$

$$I = 0.2 \cdot 9 = 1.8\ \text{Ns}$$

$$\boxed{\;I = 1.8\ \text{Ns}\;}$$

Units matter: impulse is in Ns (newton-seconds), equivalent to kg m/s. Always state the unit — it’s often a B1. By Newton’s 3rd law: impulse on the hammer (from the peg) has the same magnitude $1.8$ Ns but opposite direction.
$\boxed{\,I = 1.8\ \text{Ns}\,}$

Part (c) — Find $R$ [5 marks] · 2 steps

Combined mass $2$ kg, initial speed $9$ m/s, brought to rest in $0.12$ m.
STEP 1/2 M1 A1

$u = 9$, $v = 0$, $s = 0.12$, $a = ?$

Formula (suvat):

$$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$

Substitute:

$$0 = 81 + 2a(0.12) \;\Rightarrow\; 0.24a = -81$$

$$a = -\tfrac{81}{0.24} = -337.5\ \text{m/s}^2$$

$a = -337.5$ m/s$^2$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $a = -337.5\ \\text{m/s}^2$ (deceleration).
STEP 2/2 M1 A1 A1

Strategy: apply N2L to the combined hammer+peg (mass $2$ kg) as it decelerates in the ground. Take down positive (matches direction of motion). The acceleration is the deceleration found in STEP 1.

Free-body diagram (combined block in ground):

• Weight $W = (m_1 + m_2)g = 2 \cdot 9.8 = 19.6$ N down $\Rightarrow +19.6$.

• Resistance $R$ from ground up (opposing penetration) $\Rightarrow -R$.

Formula (Newton’s 2nd law):

$$\Sigma F = m\, a$$

Using $a = -337.5$ m/s$^2$ from STEP 1 (negative because decelerating in our down-positive convention):

$$2g - R = 2 \cdot (-337.5)$$

$$2g - R = -675$$

Rearrange for $R$:

$$R = 2g + 675 = 19.6 + 675 = 694.6\ \text{N}$$

$$\boxed{\;R \approx 695\ \text{N}\;}$$

Don’t forget weight: common error is writing $-R = 2a$ — but gravity still pulls the combined block down, so include $+2g$. Sense check: $R \approx 695$ N is much larger than the $19.6$ N weight — plausible because the ground must produce a large impulse to stop the block in just $12$ cm.
$\boxed{\,R \approx 695\ \text{N}\,}$
