Q6 — Sign change + linear interpolation; Newton-Raphson on $g$

Question

(i) $f(x) = x - 4 - \cos(5\sqrt{x})$, $x>0$.

  (a) Show $f(x)=0$ has a root $\alpha \in [2.5, 3.5]$. [2]

  (b) Use linear interpolation once on $[2.5,3.5]$ to find $\alpha$ to 2 d.p. [2]

(ii) $g(x) = \dfrac{1}{10}x^2 - \dfrac{1}{2x^2} + x - 11$, $x>0$.

  (a) Find $g'(x)$. [2]

  (b) Apply Newton-Raphson once with $x_0=6$ to give $x_1$ to 3 d.p. [2]

[8 marks]

Part (i)(a) — Sign-change test [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Theorem (IVT): if $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and $f(a)\cdot f(b) < 0$, then $\exists\, \alpha \in (a,b)$ with $f(\alpha) = 0$.

Use radians (default in FP1):

$$f(2.5) = 2.5 - 4 - \cos(5\sqrt{2.5}) \approx -1.5 + 0.06 \approx -1.44 \;(<0)$$

$$f(3.5) = 3.5 - 4 - \cos(5\sqrt{3.5}) \approx -0.5 + 0.997 \approx 0.497 \;(>0)$$

$f$ continuous; sign change ⇒ root $\alpha \in (2.5, 3.5)$. $\blacksquare$

$f(2.5) < 0,\ f(3.5) > 0 \Rightarrow$ root exists

Part (i)(b) — Linear interpolation [2 marks] · 1 step

From (a): $f(2.5) \approx -1.44$, $f(3.5) \approx 0.497$.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Strategy: linear interpolation models $f$ as a straight line between $\bigl(a, f(a)\bigr)$ and $\bigl(b, f(b)\bigr)$, then finds where THAT line crosses the $x$-axis. It's a one-shot improvement over the bracket midpoint — uses both endpoints, weighted by their function values.

Formula (similar triangles — NOT in booklet, MEMORIZE):

$$\alpha \;\approx\; a - \frac{f(a)\,(b - a)}{f(b) - f(a)}$$

Substitute $a=2.5$, $b=3.5$, with $f(a)\approx -1.44$, $f(b)\approx 0.497$ from (a):

$$\alpha \;\approx\; 2.5 - \frac{(-1.44)\,(3.5 - 2.5)}{0.497 - (-1.44)}$$

Compute numerator and denominator separately:

$$\text{numerator: }(-1.44)(1) = -1.44, \qquad \text{denominator: }0.497 + 1.44 = 1.937$$

$$\alpha \;\approx\; 2.5 - \frac{-1.44}{1.937} = 2.5 + 0.7434 \approx 3.2434$$

$$\boxed{\;\alpha \approx 3.24\;}\quad(2\text{ d.p.})$$

Pitfall (vs Newton-Raphson): linear interpolation uses BOTH endpoints of a bracketing interval, so the next estimate always lies inside $[a,b]$ — no divergence risk. NR is faster (quadratic convergence) but requires $f'(x_n) \ne 0$ near the root and a good starting estimate. Use LI when you only have endpoint values; NR when you have the derivative.
$\alpha \approx 3.24$ (2 d.p.)

Part (ii)(a) — Differentiate $g$ [2 marks] · 1 step

From the question: $g(x) = \dfrac{1}{10}x^2 - \dfrac{1}{2x^2} + x - 11$, $x>0$. (Independent of part (i) — that was about $f$.)
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

$$g(x) = \tfrac{1}{10}x^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}x^{-2} + x - 11$$

Formula (power rule): $\dfrac{d}{dx}(x^n) = nx^{n-1}$.

Always rewrite $\dfrac{1}{x^n} = x^{-n}$ before differentiating — same trick as for integration.

$$g'(x) = \tfrac{1}{10}(2x) - \tfrac{1}{2}(-2)x^{-3} + 1 + 0$$

$$= \tfrac{x}{5} + x^{-3} + 1 = \frac{x}{5} + \frac{1}{x^3} + 1$$

$g'(x) = \dfrac{x}{5} + \dfrac{1}{x^3} + 1$

Part (ii)(b) — Newton-Raphson one step [2 marks] · 1 step

Formula (booklet): $x_{n+1} = x_n - \dfrac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}$.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

$$g(6) = \tfrac{36}{10} - \tfrac{1}{72} + 6 - 11 = 3.6 - 0.0139 - 5 \approx -1.4139$$

$$g'(6) = \tfrac{6}{5} + \tfrac{1}{216} + 1 = 1.2 + 0.00463 + 1 \approx 2.2046$$

Substitute into $x_1 = x_0 - \dfrac{g(x_0)}{g'(x_0)}$:

$$x_1 = 6 - \frac{-1.4139}{2.2046} = 6 + 0.6413 \approx 6.641$$

$$\boxed{\;x_1 \approx 6.641\;}\ (3\text{ d.p.})$$

Calculator chain: enter $x_0=6$, press =. Then type "Ans − g(Ans)/g′(Ans)" and press = repeatedly for further iterations.
$x_1 \approx 6.641$
