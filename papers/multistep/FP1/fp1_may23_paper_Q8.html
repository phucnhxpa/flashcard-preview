Q8 — $y^2 = 8x$, $P(2p^2, 4p)$; chord $PQ$, tangents meet at $R$

Question

The point $P(2p^2,\,4p)$ lies on the parabola with equation $y^2 = 8x$.

(a) Show $Q\!\left(\tfrac{2}{p^2},\,-\tfrac{4}{p}\right)$, $p\ne 0$, lies on the parabola. [1]

(b) Show that the chord $PQ$ passes through the focus. [4]

(c) The tangent at $P$ and the tangent at $Q$ meet at point $R$. Determine, in simplest form, the coordinates of $R$. [8]

[13 marks]

Part (a) — $Q$ lies on the parabola [1 marks] · 1 step

Gap-topic primer: compare $y^2=8x$ with standard form $y^2=4ax$ → $a=2$. Parametric form $(at^2,\,2at) = (2t^2,\,4t)$; focus $S=(2,0)$; directrix $x=-2$.
STEP 1/1 B1

Substitute $Q=(2/p^2,\,-4/p)$ into $y^2 = 8x$:

$$y^2 = \!\left(\!-\tfrac{4}{p}\!\right)^{\!2} = \tfrac{16}{p^2},\quad 8x = 8\cdot\tfrac{2}{p^2} = \tfrac{16}{p^2}$$

$y^2 = 8x$ ✓ — so $Q$ lies on the parabola.

Faster reading: $Q$ has parameter $t=-1/p$ in $(2t^2,\,4t)$. Verifying that $t=-1/p$ gives $Q$ is equivalent to (a). The $t_1 t_2 = p\cdot(-1/p) = -1$ relation is what (b) will exploit.

Part (b) — Chord $PQ$ through the focus [4 marks] · 3 steps

Goal: show line through $P=(2p^2,4p)$ and $Q=(2/p^2,-4/p)$ passes through focus $S=(2,0)$.
STEP 1/3 M1

Formula (slope of $PQ$): $m = \dfrac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$.

Substitute:

$$m = \frac{4p - (-4/p)}{2p^2 - 2/p^2} = \frac{4p + 4/p}{2p^2 - 2/p^2}$$

Multiply top and bottom by $p^2$:

$$m = \frac{4p^3 + 4p}{2p^4 - 2} = \frac{4p(p^2+1)}{2(p^4-1)} = \frac{4p(p^2+1)}{2(p^2-1)(p^2+1)} = \frac{2p}{p^2-1}$$

Algebra trick: $p^4-1 = (p^2-1)(p^2+1)$. The $(p^2+1)$ cancels with the numerator — always look for difference-of-squares.
$m_{PQ} = \tfrac{2p}{p^2-1}$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $m_{PQ} = \tfrac{2p}{p^2-1}$
STEP 2/3 M1

Strategy: we have a slope (from the previous step) and a known point $P$ on the chord. Point-slope form gives the line equation in one line of algebra — cleaner than rearranging to $y = mx + c$ at this stage because the point coordinates are still in parametric form.

Formula (point-slope form of a line):

$$y - y_1 \;=\; m\,(x - x_1)$$

Substitute the point $P = (2p^2,\, 4p)$ and slope $m_{PQ} = \dfrac{2p}{p^2-1}$:

$$y - 4p \;=\; \frac{2p}{p^2 - 1}\,(x - 2p^2)$$

Leave in this form — the next step substitutes $x = 2$ (focus's $x$-coordinate) directly, so expanding now would just create extra work to undo.

When to expand vs leave factored: if the next step plugs in a specific $x$ value, leave the line in point-slope form. If the goal is the $y$-intercept or a slope-form answer, expand. Reading the next part of the question first is the cheapest way to choose.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 2: $y - 4p \;=\; \frac{2p}{p^2 - 1}\,(x - 2p^2)$
STEP 3/3 M1 A1

Substitute $x = 2$ (the focus’s $x$-coordinate):

$$y - 4p = \frac{2p}{p^2-1}\,(2 - 2p^2) = \frac{2p \cdot (-2)(p^2-1)}{p^2-1} = -4p$$

$$\Rightarrow\; y = 4p - 4p = 0$$

So $PQ$ passes through $(2,0) = S$, the focus. ■

Memorize the focal chord theorem: for $y^2=4ax$ with parametric points at $t_1,t_2$, chord passes through focus $\Leftrightarrow$ $t_1 t_2 = -1$. Here $t_1=p$, $t_2=-1/p$ → $t_1 t_2 = -1$ ✓. Saves all this algebra on a future paper.
$PQ$ passes through $S=(2,0)$ ✓

Part (c) — Intersection point $R$ of tangents [8 marks] · 4 steps

From the question / (a)/(b): parabola $y^2 = 8x$ has $a = 2$ (so focus $S=(2,0)$, directrix $x=-2$); $P = (2p^2, 4p)$ at parameter $t_1 = p$ and $Q = (2/p^2, -4/p)$ at parameter $t_2 = -1/p$ (with $t_1 t_2 = -1$).
STEP 1/4 B1

Formula (parabola tangent at parameter $t$ on $y^2 = 4ax$ — NOT in booklet):

$$ty = x + at^2$$

For $y^2=8x$: $a=2$. So tangent at parameter $t$ is $\;ty = x + 2t^2$.

Memorize: tangent $ty=x+at^2$, normal $y+tx=2at+at^3$. Both NOT in the booklet — derive once, then quote. Without this, part (c) is impossible in 8 marks of work.
Tangent at param $t$: $\,ty = x + 2t^2$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: Tangent at param $t$: $ty = x + 2t^2$
STEP 2/4 M1

Strategy: we now have the general tangent formula $ty = x + 2t^2$ from the previous step. Plug in $t_1 = p$ for the tangent at $P$, and $t_2 = -1/p$ for the tangent at $Q$. Rearranging both to $x = (\text{stuff in }y)$ form sets up an immediate equate-and-solve in the next step.

Tangent at $P$ (parameter $t_1 = p$): substitute $t = p$:

$$p\,y \;=\; x + 2p^2 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; x \;=\; py - 2p^2 \qquad (T_P)$$

Tangent at $Q$ (parameter $t_2 = -1/p$): substitute $t = -1/p$. Note $t^2 = 1/p^2$, so:

$$-\frac{1}{p}\,y \;=\; x + 2 \cdot \frac{1}{p^2} \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; x \;=\; -\frac{y}{p} - \frac{2}{p^2} \qquad (T_Q)$$

$$\boxed{\;T_P:\; x = py - 2p^2,\qquad T_Q:\; x = -\tfrac{y}{p} - \tfrac{2}{p^2}\;}$$

Sign trap on $t_2$: $t_2 = -1/p$, so $t_2 y = -(y/p)$ — the minus belongs to the WHOLE coefficient of $y$, not just the $1/p$. Also $t_2^2 = (-1/p)^2 = +1/p^2$ — squaring kills the negative. A common error is writing $t_2^2 = -1/p^2$ which corrupts the rest of the question.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 2: $\boxed{\;T_P:\; x = py - 2p^2,\qquad T_Q:\; x = -\tfrac{y}{p} - \tfrac{2}{p^2}\;}$
STEP 3/4 M1 A1

Move: set $T_P = T_Q$ and solve for $y$.

$$py - 2p^2 = -\tfrac{y}{p} - \tfrac{2}{p^2}$$

Multiply through by $p^2$ to clear fractions:

$$p^3 y - 2p^4 = -p\,y - 2$$

Group $y$:

$$y(p^3 + p) = 2p^4 - 2 \;\Rightarrow\; y\,p(p^2+1) = 2(p^2-1)(p^2+1)$$

$$y = \frac{2(p^2-1)}{p} = 2p - \frac{2}{p}$$

$y_R = 2p - \tfrac{2}{p}$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 3: $y_R = 2p - \tfrac{2}{p}$
STEP 4/4 M1 A1

Substitute $y_R$ back into $T_P$ ($x = py - 2p^2$):

$$x = p\!\left(2p - \tfrac{2}{p}\right) - 2p^2 = 2p^2 - 2 - 2p^2 = -2$$

$$\boxed{\;R = \left(-2,\;\;2p - \tfrac{2}{p}\right)\;}$$

Beautiful theorem (memorize): $x_R = -2$ is exactly the directrix. Tangents at the ends of any focal chord of a parabola meet on the directrix. Equivalent statement: the directrix is the polar of the focus. Use as a sanity check on any future focal-chord+tangent question.
$R = (-2,\;2p - 2/p)$
