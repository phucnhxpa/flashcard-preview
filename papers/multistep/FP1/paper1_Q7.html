Q7 — Parabola $y^2 = \tfrac{4}{3}x$; normal at $P$, second intersection $Q$

Question

$C$: $y^2 = \dfrac{4}{3}x$. The point $P\!\left(\dfrac{1}{3}t^2,\ \dfrac{2}{3}t\right)$, $t\ne 0$, lies on $C$.

(a) Use calculus to show that the normal to $C$ at $P$ has equation $3tx + 3y = t^3 + 2t$. [3]

(b) The normal to $C$ at $t = 9$ meets $C$ again at $Q$. Find the exact coordinates of $Q$. [4]

[7 marks]

Part (a) — Show normal equation [3 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3 M1

Differentiate both sides with respect to $x$:

$$2y\,\frac{dy}{dx} = \tfrac{4}{3} \;\Rightarrow\; \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2}{3y}$$

At $P$ where $y = \tfrac{2}{3}t$:

$$\left.\frac{dy}{dx}\right|_P = \frac{2}{3\cdot \tfrac{2}{3}t} = \frac{1}{t}$$

$\dfrac{dy}{dx}\bigg|_P = \dfrac{1}{t}$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $\dfrac{dy}{dx}\bigg|_P = \dfrac{1}{t}$
STEP 2/3 A1

Strategy: the normal at $P$ is the line through $P$ perpendicular to the tangent. We already have the tangent slope from step 1, so the perpendicular relation gives the normal slope in one move.

Rule (perpendicular gradients): for two non-vertical lines with gradients $m_1, m_2$:

$$m_1 \cdot m_2 = -1 \;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\; m_2 = -\frac{1}{m_1}$$

Substitute $m_{\text{tangent}} = \dfrac{1}{t}$ from step 1:

$$m_{\text{normal}} \;=\; -\frac{1}{\,1/t\,} \;=\; -1 \cdot t \;=\; -t$$

$$\boxed{\;m_{\text{normal}} = -t\;}$$

Reciprocal-of-reciprocal slip: $\dfrac{1}{1/t}$ simplifies to $t$, NOT $1/t$. Many students write $m_n = -1/t$ here. Quick sanity check at $t=1$: tangent slope is $+1$, so the perpendicular has slope $-1$, matching $-t = -1$ ✓.
$m_{\text{normal}} = -t$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 2: $m_{\text{normal}} = -t$
STEP 3/3 A1

Formula (point-slope): $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$.

$$y - \tfrac{2}{3}t = -t\!\left(x - \tfrac{1}{3}t^2\right)$$

Multiply by $3$:

$$3y - 2t = -3t x + t^3$$

$$\boxed{\;3tx + 3y = t^3 + 2t\;}\ \blacksquare$$

$3tx + 3y = t^3 + 2t$

Part (b) — Second intersection at $t=9$ [4 marks] · 4 steps

From (a): normal at $P$ is $3tx + 3y = t^3 + 2t$.
STEP 1/4 M1

$$3(9)x + 3y = 9^3 + 2(9) = 729 + 18 = 747$$

$$27x + 3y = 747 \;\Rightarrow\; 9x + y = 249 \;\Rightarrow\; y = 249 - 9x$$

$y = 249 - 9x$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $y = 249 - 9x$
STEP 2/4 M1

$$(249 - 9x)^2 = \tfrac{4}{3}x$$

Multiply by $3$ and expand:

$$3(81x^2 - 4482x + 62001) = 4x$$

$$243x^2 - 13446x + 186003 = 4x$$

$$243x^2 - 13450x + 186003 = 0$$

$243x^2 - 13450x + 186003 = 0$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 2: $243x^2 - 13450x + 186003 = 0$
STEP 3/4 A1

One root is $x_P = \tfrac{1}{3}(9)^2 = 27$ (point $P$ on the normal).

Vieta sum: $x_P + x_Q = \dfrac{13450}{243}$.

$$x_Q = \frac{13450}{243} - 27 = \frac{13450 - 6561}{243} = \frac{6889}{243}$$

Note $6889 = 83^2$, so $x_Q = \dfrac{83^2}{243}$. Using Vieta avoids the quadratic formula, since $x_P$ is already known to be a root.
$x_Q = \dfrac{6889}{243}$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 3: $x_Q = \dfrac{6889}{243}$
STEP 4/4 A1

$$y_Q = 249 - 9\cdot \frac{6889}{243} = 249 - \frac{6889}{27} = \frac{6723 - 6889}{27} = -\frac{166}{27}$$

$$\boxed{\;Q = \left(\tfrac{6889}{243},\ -\tfrac{166}{27}\right)\;}$$

Check: $y_Q^2 = \tfrac{166^2}{729} = \tfrac{27556}{729}$ and $\tfrac{4}{3}x_Q = \tfrac{4\cdot 6889}{3\cdot 243} = \tfrac{27556}{729}$ ✓.
$Q = \left(\tfrac{6889}{243},\ -\tfrac{166}{27}\right)$
