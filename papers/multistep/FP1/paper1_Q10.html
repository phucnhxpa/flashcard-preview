Q10 — Prove by induction: (i) matrix power; (ii) $f(n) = 8^{2n+1} + 6^{2n-1}$ divisible by $7$

Question

(i) Prove by induction that for $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 5 & -1 \\ 4 & 1 \end{pmatrix}^n = 3^{n-1}\begin{pmatrix} 2n+3 & -n \\ 4n & 3-2n \end{pmatrix}$$ [5]

(ii) Prove by induction that for $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$, $f(n) = 8^{2n+1} + 6^{2n-1}$ is divisible by $7$. [5]

[10 marks]

Part (i) — Matrix power induction [5 marks] · 5 steps

STEP 1/5 B1

LHS: $\begin{pmatrix}5&-1\\4&1\end{pmatrix}^1 = \begin{pmatrix}5&-1\\4&1\end{pmatrix}$.

RHS: $3^{0}\!\begin{pmatrix}2(1)+3 & -1 \\ 4(1) & 3-2(1)\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}5 & -1 \\ 4 & 1\end{pmatrix}$.

LHS = RHS, so the statement is true for $n=1$. ✓

True for $n=1$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: True for $n=1$
STEP 2/5 M1

Strategy: the inductive hypothesis is the "assume true for $n=k$" sentence. We treat it as a temporary axiom — everything in step 3 will be derived from it. Without writing this line down explicitly, the proof loses the M1 method mark.

Inductive hypothesis (assume true for some integer $n=k\ge 1$):

$$A^k \;=\; 3^{k-1}\!\begin{pmatrix} 2k+3 & -k \\ 4k & 3-2k \end{pmatrix}$$

where $A = \begin{pmatrix}5 & -1\\ 4 & 1\end{pmatrix}$ (the matrix from the question).

$$\boxed{\;\text{IH: }A^k = 3^{k-1}\!\begin{pmatrix}2k+3 & -k \\ 4k & 3-2k\end{pmatrix}\;}$$

Induction structure (memorize): the assume-step always comes BEFORE the prove-step. The base case ($n=1$) must come FIRST — it is what anchors the whole chain. Without a verified base, the inductive step proves nothing. Examiners look for all four sentences in order: BASE → ASSUME → SHOW → CONCLUDE.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 2: $\boxed{\;\text{IH: }A^k = 3^{k-1}\!\begin{pmatrix}2k+3 & -k \\ 4k & 3-2k\end{pmatrix}\;}$
STEP 3/5 A1

Key move: $A^{k+1} = A^k \cdot A$.

$$A^{k+1} = 3^{k-1}\!\begin{pmatrix} 2k+3 & -k \\ 4k & 3-2k \end{pmatrix}\!\begin{pmatrix} 5 & -1 \\ 4 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Row × column:

$(1,1) = 5(2k+3) + 4(-k) = 10k+15 - 4k = 6k + 15$

$(1,2) = -(2k+3) + (-k) = -3k - 3$

$(2,1) = 5(4k) + 4(3-2k) = 20k + 12 - 8k = 12k + 12$

$(2,2) = -(4k) + (3-2k) = -6k + 3$

$$A^{k+1} = 3^{k-1}\begin{pmatrix} 6k+15 & -3k-3 \\ 12k+12 & -6k+3 \end{pmatrix}$$

Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 3: $A^{k+1} = 3^{k-1}\begin{pmatrix} 6k+15 & -3k-3 \\ 12k+12 & -6k+3 \end{pmatrix}$
STEP 4/5 A1

$6k+15 = 3(2k+5) = 3[2(k+1)+3]$

$-3k-3 = 3[-(k+1)]$

$12k+12 = 3\cdot 4(k+1)$

$-6k+3 = 3(1 - 2k) = 3[3 - 2(k+1)]$

$$A^{k+1} = 3^{k-1}\cdot 3\!\begin{pmatrix} 2(k+1)+3 & -(k+1) \\ 4(k+1) & 3-2(k+1) \end{pmatrix} = 3^{k}\!\begin{pmatrix} 2(k+1)+3 & -(k+1) \\ 4(k+1) & 3-2(k+1) \end{pmatrix}$$

This matches the target with $n$ replaced by $k+1$ ($3^{(k+1)-1} = 3^k$). ✓

Critical step: the RHS has $3^{n-1}$, so after multiplying you MUST factor out a $3$ to advance the exponent. Same factor-early principle as Q8.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 4: $A^{k+1} = 3^{k-1}\cdot 3\!\begin{pmatrix} 2(k+1)+3 & -(k+1) \\ 4(k+1) & 3-2(k+1) \end{pmatrix} = 3^{k}\!\begin{pmatrix} 2(k+1)+3 & -(k+1) \\ 4(k+1) & 3-2(k+1) \end{pmatrix}$
STEP 5/5 A1

The statement is true for $n=1$. We have shown that if it is true for $n=k$ then it is true for $n=k+1$. Therefore, by the principle of mathematical induction, the statement is true for all $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$. $\blacksquare$

Examiners reward verbatim phrasing — always include all four sentences: base case true, hypothesis, inductive step, "by the principle of mathematical induction".
Statement true $\forall n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$

Part (ii) — Divisibility induction [5 marks] · 5 steps

4-step template: base case → assume → show → conclude.
STEP 1/5 B1

$$f(1) = 8^{3} + 6^{1} = 512 + 6 = 518 = 7 \times 74$$

So $f(1)$ is divisible by $7$. ✓

$f(1) = 518 = 7\cdot 74$ ✓
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $f(1) = 518 = 7\cdot 74$ ✓
STEP 2/5 M1

Assume $f(k) = 8^{2k+1} + 6^{2k-1}$ is divisible by $7$, i.e. $f(k) = 7M$ for some integer $M$.

Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 2: $M$
STEP 3/5 M1

$$f(k+1) = 8^{2(k+1)+1} + 6^{2(k+1)-1} = 8^{2k+3} + 6^{2k+1}$$

$$= 8^{2k+1}\cdot 8^2 + 6^{2k-1}\cdot 6^2 = 64\cdot 8^{2k+1} + 36\cdot 6^{2k-1}$$

Tutor trick: form $f(k+1) - f(k)$:

$$f(k+1) - f(k) = (64-1)\cdot 8^{2k+1} + (36-1)\cdot 6^{2k-1} = 63\cdot 8^{2k+1} + 35\cdot 6^{2k-1}$$

$64-1 = 63 = 7\cdot 9$ and $36-1 = 35 = 7\cdot 5$ — both multiples of $7$. This is the designed hook of the question.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 3: $f(k+1) - f(k) = (64-1)\cdot 8^{2k+1} + (36-1)\cdot 6^{2k-1} = 63\cdot 8^{2k+1} + 35\cdot 6^{2k-1}$
STEP 4/5 A1

$$f(k+1) - f(k) = 7\bigl(9\cdot 8^{2k+1} + 5\cdot 6^{2k-1}\bigr)$$

So:

$$f(k+1) = f(k) + 7\bigl(9\cdot 8^{2k+1} + 5\cdot 6^{2k-1}\bigr) = 7M + 7(\text{integer}) = 7\cdot (\text{integer})$$

Therefore $f(k+1)$ is divisible by $7$. ✓

Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 3: $f(k+1) = 7M + 7(\\text{integer}) = 7\\cdot(\\text{integer})$, so $f(k+1)$ divisible by 7 ✓
STEP 5/5 A1

The statement is true for $n=1$. We have shown that if $f(k)$ is divisible by $7$ then $f(k+1)$ is divisible by $7$. Therefore, by the principle of mathematical induction, $f(n) = 8^{2n+1} + 6^{2n-1}$ is divisible by $7$ for all $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$. $\blacksquare$

$f(n)$ divisible by $7$, $\forall n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$
