Edexcel IAL · Pure Mathematics P4 · 26 June 2026 · Harry Williams

Q4 · Implicit differentiation, gradient at a point

Question

A curve $C$ has equation $4x^2 - 2xy + y^2 = 24$.

(a) Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ in terms of $x$ and $y$, giving your answer in its simplest form.

(b) The point $P(a, b)$ lies on $C$ and at $P$ the gradient is $2$. Find the possible coordinates of $P$.

[8 marks total: (a) 4 marks, (b) 4 marks]

Implicit curve 4x² − 2xy + y² = 24 with tangent lines at gradient = 2

(a) Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ in terms of $x$ and $y$ [4 marks] · 4 steps

Differentiate every term with respect to $x$. Any $y$-term picks up a $\frac{dy}{dx}$ factor. The $xy$ term uses the product rule: $\frac{d}{dx}(xy) = \left[x\frac{dy}{dx} + y\right]$ — always write the square bracket.
STEP 1/4differentiate term-by-term

Strategy. Differentiate $4x^2 - 2xy + y^2 = 24$ w.r.t. $x$, term by term. Use the product rule on $-2xy$ and the chain rule on $y^2$.

Term-by-term

$$\frac{d}{dx}(4x^2) = 8x.$$ $$\frac{d}{dx}(-2xy) = -2\!\left[x\frac{dy}{dx} + y\right].$$ $$\frac{d}{dx}(y^2) = 2y\frac{dy}{dx}.$$ $$\frac{d}{dx}(24) = 0.$$
Trap 5 (watch-list) — the bracket rule. Harry's lesson emphasised: always write $\frac{d}{dx}(xy)$ as $\left[x\frac{dy}{dx} + y\right]$ with the square bracket. The $-2$ multiplies the entire bracket: $-2\left[x\frac{dy}{dx} + y\right] = -2x\frac{dy}{dx} - 2y$. Dropping the bracket loses the minus on the $-2y$ term.

Sense check. Each $x$-term: power rule. The $xy$ term: product rule. The $y^2$ term: chain rule, giving a $\frac{dy}{dx}$ factor. Three different rules, three terms.

$8x - 2\!\left[x\dfrac{dy}{dx} + y\right] + 2y\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 0$.
↳ from STEP 1: $8x - 2\!\left[x\dfrac{dy}{dx} + y\right] + 2y\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 0$.
STEP 2/4expand the bracket

Strategy. Distribute the $-2$ across the square bracket to get separate terms. Collect all $\frac{dy}{dx}$ terms on one side, pure $x, y$ terms on the other.

Expand

$$8x - 2x\frac{dy}{dx} - 2y + 2y\frac{dy}{dx} = 0.$$

Collect $\frac{dy}{dx}$ on the left

$$2y\frac{dy}{dx} - 2x\frac{dy}{dx} = 2y - 8x.$$
Sign-tracking. Moving $-2x\frac{dy}{dx}$ to the LHS makes it $+2x\frac{dy}{dx}$ on the right becomes $-2x\frac{dy}{dx}$ on the left. Moving $8x$ to the RHS gives $-8x$. Moving $-2y$ to the RHS gives $+2y$. Each transfer flips the sign.

Sense check. The LHS has both $\frac{dy}{dx}$ terms; the RHS has both non-$\frac{dy}{dx}$ terms. Clean separation.

$(2y - 2x)\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 2y - 8x$.
↳ from STEP 2: $(2y - 2x)\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 2y - 8x$.
STEP 3/4factor & divide

Strategy. Factor $2$ out of both sides to simplify, then divide to isolate $\frac{dy}{dx}$.

Factor out 2

$$2(y - x)\frac{dy}{dx} = 2(y - 4x).$$

Divide both sides by $2(y - x)$

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{y - 4x}{y - x}.$$
Technique. Harry's lesson tip #8: factor before expanding numerically — keeps the algebra compact. Factoring $2$ from both numerator and denominator cancels cleanly.

Sense check. Denominator $y - x$: at the origin this would be $0$, but the curve $4x^2 - 2xy + y^2 = 24$ never passes through the origin (LHS = 0 ≠ 24), so the denominator is non-zero on $C$.

$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{y - 4x}{y - x}$.
↳ from STEP 3: $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{y - 4x}{y - x}$.
STEP 4/4simplify & state

Strategy. The expression $\frac{y - 4x}{y - x}$ is already in simplest form — no common factor in numerator and denominator. Box it as the answer to part (a).

Final derivative

$$\boxed{\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{y - 4x}{y - x}}.$$
Trap (watch-list). A common error is to write $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{4x - y}{x - y}$ (signs flipped top and bottom). This is algebraically equal, but Edexcel wants the "simplest form" — the version with the leading $y$ in numerator and denominator is the standard answer.

Sense check. At a point where $y = x$ (if it existed on $C$), the gradient would be undefined — a vertical tangent. Substituting $y = x$ into $C$: $4x^2 - 2x^2 + x^2 = 3x^2 = 24$, giving $x = \pm 2\sqrt{2}$. So there ARE points with vertical tangents — good consistency.

$\boxed{\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{y - 4x}{y - x}}$ — answer to (a).

(b) Find the coordinates of $P(a, b)$ where gradient $= 2$ [4 marks] · 5 steps

At $P(a, b)$ the gradient is $2$: $\frac{dy}{dx}\big|_{(a,b)} = 2$. We also know $P$ lies on $C$, so $4a^2 - 2ab + b^2 = 24$. These are two equations in two unknowns.
STEP 1/5apply gradient = 2

Strategy. Substitute $x = a$, $y = b$, and $\frac{dy}{dx} = 2$ into the derivative from part (a). This gives one equation in $a$ and $b$.

Substitute into $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{y - 4x}{y - x}$

$$2 = \frac{b - 4a}{b - a}.$$

Cross-multiply

$$2(b - a) = b - 4a \quad\Longrightarrow\quad 2b - 2a = b - 4a.$$
$2b - 2a = b - 4a$.
↳ from STEP 1: $2b - 2a = b - 4a$.
STEP 2/5solve for b in terms of a

Strategy. Collect $b$-terms on one side and $a$-terms on the other.

Rearrange

$$2b - b = -4a + 2a \quad\Longrightarrow\quad b = -2a.$$

Sense check. $b = -2a$ means $P$ lies on the line $y = -2x$ in the $(a, b)$ plane. This is a relation between the coordinates, not yet a specific point.

$b = -2a$ — the first equation.
↳ from STEP 2: $b = -2a$.
STEP 3/5use the curve equation

Strategy. This is the critical insight: $P$ lies on $C$, so $4a^2 - 2ab + b^2 = 24$ is the SECOND equation. Substitute $b = -2a$ into it.

Substitute $b = -2a$ into $4a^2 - 2ab + b^2 = 24$

$$4a^2 - 2a(-2a) + (-2a)^2 = 24.$$ $$4a^2 + 4a^2 + 4a^2 = 24.$$ $$12a^2 = 24.$$
Trap 6 (watch-list) — two unknowns, "only" one equation. On Q4b, Phuc had the gradient condition $b = -2a$ and got stuck because that alone does not fix $a$. The curve equation $4a^2 - 2ab + b^2 = 24$ is the missing second equation. Rule: the moment you see "lies on the curve", write the curve equation in $(a, b)$ form.

Sense check. $-2a(-2a) = +4a^2$; $(-2a)^2 = 4a^2$. So $4 + 4 + 4 = 12$ coefficients on $a^2$. $12a^2 = 24$ gives $a^2 = 2$.

$12a^2 = 24 \;\Rightarrow\; a^2 = 2$.
↳ from STEP 3: $a^2 = 2 \;\Rightarrow\; a = \pm\sqrt{2}$, and $b = -2a$.
STEP 4/5solve for a and b

Strategy. Take square roots: $a = \sqrt{2}$ or $a = -\sqrt{2}$. Then compute $b = -2a$ for each case.

Two solutions

$$a = \sqrt{2} \;\Rightarrow\; b = -2\sqrt{2}.$$ $$a = -\sqrt{2} \;\Rightarrow\; b = +2\sqrt{2}.$$

Sense check. The curve $4x^2 - 2xy + y^2 = 24$ is symmetric under $(x, y) \to (-x, -y)$ (all terms are degree 2), so the two points $(\sqrt{2}, -2\sqrt{2})$ and $(-\sqrt{2}, 2\sqrt{2})$ are antipodal — consistent.

Two candidate points: $(\sqrt{2},\, -2\sqrt{2})$ and $(-\sqrt{2},\, 2\sqrt{2})$.
↳ from STEP 4: candidates $(\sqrt{2}, -2\sqrt{2})$ and $(-\sqrt{2}, 2\sqrt{2})$.
STEP 5/5verify & state

Strategy. Verify both points lie on $C$ and have gradient $2$. If the question gives a sign constraint (e.g. $a > 0$), reject one. Here both are valid.

Verify point 1: $(\sqrt{2}, -2\sqrt{2})$

$$4(2) - 2(\sqrt{2})(-2\sqrt{2}) + (8) = 8 + 8 + 8 = 24.\;\checkmark$$

Verify gradient at point 1

$$\frac{-2\sqrt{2} - 4\sqrt{2}}{-2\sqrt{2} - \sqrt{2}} = \frac{-6\sqrt{2}}{-3\sqrt{2}} = 2.\;\checkmark$$
The two-unknowns insight (watch-list). Whenever a point $P(a, b)$ lies on a curve $C$ and a gradient is given, you always have TWO equations: (1) the gradient condition and (2) the curve equation. Many students count only the gradient condition and get stuck — this was Phuc's exact trap on Q4b.

Sense check. Both points satisfy the curve (LHS = 24 ✓) and both give gradient 2 ✓. By symmetry, both are correct.

$\boxed{P = (\sqrt{2},\, -2\sqrt{2})\;\text{or}\;(-\sqrt{2},\, 2\sqrt{2})}$.
