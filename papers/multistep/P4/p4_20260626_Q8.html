Q8 · Separable differential equation with initial condition

Question

Solve the differential equation $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = xy$ given that $y = 2$ when $x = 0$.

Give your solution in the form $y = f(x)$, simplifying your answer as far as possible.

[6 marks]

Solution [6 marks] · 6 steps

The ODE $\frac{dy}{dx} = xy$ is separable: the RHS is a product of a pure-$x$ function ($x$) and a pure-$y$ function ($y$). Strategy: separate, integrate both sides, then apply the initial condition $y(0) = 2$ to find the constant.
STEP 1/6recognise separable form

Strategy. A first-order ODE is separable if it can be written as $\frac{dy}{dx} = f(x) \cdot g(y)$. Here $\frac{dy}{dx} = x \cdot y$, so $f(x) = x$ and $g(y) = y$. The strategy: move all $y$-terms to the LHS, all $x$-terms to the RHS.

Identify the form

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = x \cdot y \quad\Longrightarrow\quad f(x) = x,\;\; g(y) = y.$$
Phuc's self-assessed gap (Q8). Phuc's email listed "differential equations" as a gap. The key recognition: if $\frac{dy}{dx}$ equals a product of a pure-$x$ factor and a pure-$y$ factor, the ODE is separable. Divide by $g(y)$ and multiply by $dx$ to separate.

Sense check. There is no $y$-term mixed with $x$ in a way that prevents separation — $xy$ is already a clean product of $f(x) = x$ and $g(y) = y$.

Separable: $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = x \cdot y$.
↳ from STEP 1: $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = x \cdot y$, separable.
STEP 2/6separate the variables

Strategy. Divide both sides by $y$ (moving $g(y) = y$ to the LHS) and multiply by $dx$ (moving $dx$ to the RHS). This puts all $y$-terms on the left and all $x$-terms on the right.

Separate

$$\frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx} = x.$$ $$\frac{1}{y}\, dy = x\, dx.$$
Division-by-$y$ caveat. Dividing by $y$ assumes $y \neq 0$. Check: the initial condition $y(0) = 2 \neq 0$, and the solution we find stays positive, so the division is valid on the solution curve. The constant solution $y \equiv 0$ is a separate (singular) solution not satisfying the initial condition.

Sense check. LHS contains only $y$ and $dy$; RHS contains only $x$ and $dx$. Clean separation.

$\dfrac{1}{y}\, dy = x\, dx$.
↳ from STEP 2: $\dfrac{1}{y}\, dy = x\, dx$.
STEP 3/6integrate both sides

Strategy. Integrate the LHS w.r.t. $y$ and the RHS w.r.t. $x$. The LHS $\int \frac{1}{y}\, dy = \ln|y|$ (this IS the $\ln$ case, exponent $-1$). The RHS $\int x\, dx = \frac{x^2}{2}$ (power rule, exponent $+1$, NOT $\ln$). Add a single constant $C$ on one side.

Integrate

$$\int \frac{1}{y}\, dy = \int x\, dx + C.$$ $$\ln|y| = \frac{x^2}{2} + C.$$
Power rule vs $\ln$ (Trap 10, watch-list). $\int \frac{1}{y}\, dy = \ln|y|$ because the exponent is $-1$ — the ONLY case where the power rule fails and $\ln$ appears. But $\int x\, dx = \frac{x^2}{2}$ because the exponent is $+1$ — the power rule applies. Do NOT write $\ln x$ for $\int x\, dx$.

Sense check. Differentiate $\ln|y|$ w.r.t. $y$: $\frac{1}{y}$. ✓ Differentiate $\frac{x^2}{2}$ w.r.t. $x$: $x$. ✓

$\ln|y| = \dfrac{x^2}{2} + C$.
↳ from STEP 3: $\ln|y| = \dfrac{x^2}{2} + C$.
STEP 4/6apply initial condition y(0) = 2

Strategy. Substitute $x = 0$, $y = 2$ into $\ln|y| = \frac{x^2}{2} + C$ to find $C$. $\ln|2| = \frac{0}{2} + C$, so $C = \ln 2$.

Substitute $x = 0$, $y = 2$

$$\ln|2| = \frac{0^2}{2} + C \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \ln 2 = 0 + C \quad\Longrightarrow\quad C = \ln 2.$$

Particular solution (implicit form)

$$\ln|y| = \frac{x^2}{2} + \ln 2.$$
Initial conditions earn a mark. Always substitute the initial condition to find $C$ — this is a separate method mark. The general solution (with $C$) is the "general solution"; after fixing $C$ you have the "particular solution".

Sense check. $x = 0 \Rightarrow \frac{x^2}{2} = 0$, so $C = \ln 2$ (the $y$-intercept in log-space). $\ln 2 \approx 0.693$.

$C = \ln 2$, so $\ln|y| = \dfrac{x^2}{2} + \ln 2$.
↳ from STEP 4: $\ln|y| = \dfrac{x^2}{2} + \ln 2$.
STEP 5/6exponentiate to solve for y

Strategy. Exponentiate both sides to remove the $\ln$. $e^{\ln|y|} = |y|$, and $e^{x^2/2 + \ln 2} = e^{x^2/2} \cdot e^{\ln 2} = 2e^{x^2/2}$. Since the initial condition gives $y = 2 > 0$, we take the positive branch: $y = 2e^{x^2/2}$.

Exponentiate

$$|y| = e^{x^2/2 + \ln 2} = e^{x^2/2} \cdot e^{\ln 2} = 2\, e^{x^2/2}.$$

Choose the sign

$$y = 2\, e^{x^2/2} \quad (\text{positive branch, since } y(0) = 2 > 0).$$
Why positive branch? $|y| = 2e^{x^2/2}$ gives $y = \pm 2e^{x^2/2}$. The initial condition $y(0) = 2$ (positive) selects the $+$ sign. The exponential is always positive, so $y$ never crosses zero — the sign is fixed by the initial condition and stays fixed for all $x$.

Sense check. At $x = 0$: $y = 2e^0 = 2 \cdot 1 = 2$. ✓ Matches the initial condition. As $x$ grows, $e^{x^2/2}$ grows super-exponentially (the $x^2$ in the exponent), so $y$ increases rapidly — consistent with $\frac{dy}{dx} = xy$ having a positive, growing RHS.

$y = 2\, e^{x^2/2}$.
↳ from STEP 5: $y = 2\, e^{x^2/2}$.
STEP 6/6verify & state final answer

Strategy. Verify the solution by differentiating and substituting back into the original ODE. Then box the final answer in the form $y = f(x)$ as requested.

Differentiate $y = 2e^{x^2/2}$

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = 2 \cdot e^{x^2/2} \cdot \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{x^2}{2}\right) = 2e^{x^2/2} \cdot x = x \cdot \left(2e^{x^2/2}\right) = x \cdot y.$$

Check against original ODE

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = xy. \;\checkmark \quad\text{(matches the original equation)}.$$
Chain rule on $e^{x^2/2}$. $\frac{d}{dx}e^{u} = e^{u} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}$ with $u = \frac{x^2}{2}$, so $\frac{du}{dx} = x$. This gives $\frac{dy}{dx} = 2e^{x^2/2} \cdot x = x \cdot y$, exactly the RHS of the original ODE.

Sense check. The solution $y = 2e^{x^2/2}$ is always positive, grows faster than exponential (the exponent is quadratic in $x$), and passes through $(0, 2)$. The ODE $\frac{dy}{dx} = xy$ says "the rate of growth is proportional to both $x$ and $y$" — so growth accelerates as $x$ increases and as $y$ increases, a positive feedback loop. The super-exponential curve matches this. ✓

$\boxed{y = 2\, e^{x^2/2}}$ — verified against the ODE and initial condition.
