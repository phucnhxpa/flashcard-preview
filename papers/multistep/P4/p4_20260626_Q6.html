Q6 · Integration by trig substitution

Question

Using the substitution $u = 3 + \cos\theta$, show that

$$\int_0^{\pi/2} \frac{\sin 2\theta}{\sqrt{3 + \cos\theta}}\, d\theta$$

can be written in the form $A \displaystyle\int_3^4 \frac{u - 3}{\sqrt{u}}\, du$, where $A$ is a constant to be found.

(b) Hence evaluate the integral, giving your answer in the form $a + b\sqrt{3}$, where $a$ and $b$ are rational numbers.

[9 marks total: (a) 5 marks, (b) 4 marks]

(a) Show the integral becomes $A\int_3^4 \frac{u-3}{\sqrt{u}}\,du$ [5 marks] · 5 steps

Substitution $u = 3 + \cos\theta$. Three things to transform: (1) the integrand, (2) $d\theta$, (3) the limits. Use $\sin 2\theta = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta$ and solve the substitution for $\cos\theta = u - 3$.
STEP 1/5split sin 2θ

Strategy. Use the double-angle identity $\sin 2\theta = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta$ to split the numerator. This exposes $\sin\theta$ (which will cancel with $du$) and $\cos\theta$ (which becomes $u - 3$).

Identity

$$\sin 2\theta = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta.$$

Substitute

$$\int_0^{\pi/2} \frac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{\sqrt{3 + \cos\theta}}\, d\theta.$$
Key identity (must-memorise). $\sin 2\theta = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta$. This is the most-used trig identity in integration-by-substitution questions — without it, the $\sin\theta$ factor needed to cancel $du$ stays hidden.

Sense check. The numerator is now a product of $\sin\theta$ and $\cos\theta$. The $\sin\theta$ will pair with $d\theta$ to form $du$; the $\cos\theta$ will be replaced by $u - 3$.

Integrand: $\dfrac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{\sqrt{3+\cos\theta}}$.
↳ from STEP 1: integrand $\dfrac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{\sqrt{3+\cos\theta}}\,d\theta$.
STEP 2/5compute du and replace dθ

Strategy. Differentiate $u = 3 + \cos\theta$: $\frac{du}{d\theta} = -\sin\theta$, so $du = -\sin\theta\, d\theta$ and $d\theta = \frac{du}{-\sin\theta} = -\frac{1}{\sin\theta}du$.

Differentiate the substitution

$$\frac{du}{d\theta} = -\sin\theta \quad\Longrightarrow\quad d\theta = -\frac{du}{\sin\theta}.$$

Sense check. $\frac{d}{d\theta}(\cos\theta) = -\sin\theta$, so $\frac{d}{d\theta}(3 + \cos\theta) = -\sin\theta$. The minus sign is critical — it will later cause the limits to swap, which we handle with a second minus.

$d\theta = -\dfrac{du}{\sin\theta}$.
↳ from STEP 2: $d\theta = -\dfrac{du}{\sin\theta}$.
STEP 3/5replace cos θ and limits

Strategy. From $u = 3 + \cos\theta$, solve for $\cos\theta = u - 3$. Also $\sqrt{3 + \cos\theta} = \sqrt{u}$. Transform the limits: $\theta = 0 \Rightarrow u = 3 + \cos 0 = 4$; $\theta = \pi/2 \Rightarrow u = 3 + \cos(\pi/2) = 3$. Preserve order: old lower $\to$ new lower.

Replace $\cos\theta$

$$\cos\theta = u - 3, \qquad \sqrt{3 + \cos\theta} = \sqrt{u}.$$

Transform limits

$$\theta = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; u = 3 + \cos 0 = 3 + 1 = 4.$$ $$\theta = \tfrac{\pi}{2} \;\Rightarrow\; u = 3 + \cos\tfrac{\pi}{2} = 3 + 0 = 3.$$
Trap 8 (watch-list) — limit order on substitution. $\cos\theta$ DECREASES as $\theta$ goes from $0$ to $\pi/2$, so $u$ decreases from $4$ to $3$. The new limits look "backwards" ($4$ lower, $3$ upper). Do NOT flip them without a sign change — preserve the order. The minus from $d\theta = -du/\sin\theta$ will handle the swap.
Trap 9 (watch-list) — $\cos\theta$ is not a constant. $\cos\theta$ inside an integral w.r.t. $\theta$ is a variable, not a constant — you cannot pull it outside. Solve the substitution for $\cos\theta = u - 3$ and substitute it back inside.

Sense check. $\cos 0 = 1$, $\cos(\pi/2) = 0$ (must-memorise values). New limits: $4 \to 3$, i.e. lower $= 4$, upper $= 3$ — "backwards" but correct.

$\cos\theta = u - 3$; limits $u: 4 \to 3$ (lower $4$, upper $3$).
↳ from STEP 3: $\cos\theta = u - 3$, $\sqrt{3+\cos\theta} = \sqrt{u}$, limits $4 \to 3$, $d\theta = -du/\sin\theta$.
STEP 4/5assemble the new integral

Strategy. Substitute everything into the integral. The $\sin\theta$ from the numerator cancels with the $\sin\theta$ from $d\theta$, leaving a clean $u$-integral. The minus from $d\theta$ flips the limits so they read $3 \to 4$.

Full substitution

$$\int_0^{\pi/2} \frac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{\sqrt{u}} \cdot \left(-\frac{du}{\sin\theta}\right) = -2\int_4^3 \frac{\cos\theta}{\sqrt{u}}\, du = -2\int_4^3 \frac{u - 3}{\sqrt{u}}\, du.$$

Flip the limits (absorb the minus)

$$-2\int_4^3 \frac{u-3}{\sqrt{u}}\, du = +2\int_3^4 \frac{u-3}{\sqrt{u}}\, du.$$
The minus-limit trick. The $d\theta$ substitution brought a minus sign, and the limits were "backwards" ($4 \to 3$). Flipping the limits introduces another minus: $-\int_4^3 = +\int_3^4$. The two minuses combine to give a positive $A = 2$.

Sense check. The $\sin\theta$ cancelled cleanly (as planned in Step 1). The integral is now $\int_3^4 \frac{u-3}{\sqrt{u}}\, du$ with a positive coefficient $2$ — exactly the target form.

$2\displaystyle\int_3^4 \dfrac{u - 3}{\sqrt{u}}\, du$ — so $A = 2$.
↳ from STEP 4: $A = 2$, integral $= 2\int_3^4 \frac{u-3}{\sqrt{u}}\, du$.
STEP 5/5identify A

Strategy. The question asks for the integral "in the form $A\int_3^4 \frac{u-3}{\sqrt{u}}\,du$". Comparing, $A = 2$.

Compare with target form

$$2\int_3^4 \frac{u-3}{\sqrt{u}}\, du \;\;\text{vs.}\;\; A\int_3^4 \frac{u-3}{\sqrt{u}}\, du.$$
"Show that" command. Every step must be on the page — the examiner needs to see the identity, the $du$ computation, the limit transformation, and the assembly. Calculator-only solutions score zero on "show that".

Sense check. $A = 2$ is positive, which makes sense: the original integrand $\sin 2\theta / \sqrt{3 + \cos\theta}$ is non-negative on $[0, \pi/2]$ (both $\sin 2\theta \geq 0$ and denominator $> 0$), so the integral must be positive. $A = 2 > 0$ and the $u$-integral is also positive. ✓

$\boxed{A = 2}$ — integral $= 2\displaystyle\int_3^4 \dfrac{u-3}{\sqrt{u}}\, du$.

(b) Evaluate the integral, answer in form $a + b\sqrt{3}$ [4 marks] · 4 steps

From (a): the integral $= 2\int_3^4 \frac{u-3}{\sqrt{u}}\, du$. Split the integrand using $\frac{u-3}{\sqrt{u}} = u^{1/2} - 3u^{-1/2}$, then apply the power rule (NOT $\ln$).
STEP 1/4split the integrand

Strategy. Rewrite $\frac{u - 3}{\sqrt{u}}$ as $u^{1/2} - 3u^{-1/2}$ so the power rule applies to each term.

Algebra

$$\frac{u - 3}{\sqrt{u}} = \frac{u}{\sqrt{u}} - \frac{3}{\sqrt{u}} = u^{1/2} - 3u^{-1/2}.$$
Trap 10 (watch-list) — power rule vs ln. $\int u^{1/2}\, du = \frac{2}{3}u^{3/2}$, NOT $\ln u$. The $\ln$ result is reserved exclusively for $\int u^{-1}\, du$. Here the exponents are $+\frac{1}{2}$ and $-\frac{1}{2}$; neither is $-1$, so the power rule applies to both.

Sense check. $u/\sqrt{u} = u^{1 - 1/2} = u^{1/2}$. $3/\sqrt{u} = 3u^{-1/2}$. Both are power functions with exponents $\neq -1$.

Integrand $= u^{1/2} - 3u^{-1/2}$.
↳ from STEP 1: integrand $= u^{1/2} - 3u^{-1/2}$.
STEP 2/4integrate using the power rule

Strategy. Apply $\int u^n\, du = \frac{u^{n+1}}{n+1}$ to each term. For $n = \frac{1}{2}$: $n + 1 = \frac{3}{2}$, so the antiderivative is $\frac{2}{3}u^{3/2}$. For $n = -\frac{1}{2}$: $n + 1 = \frac{1}{2}$, so the antiderivative is $2u^{1/2}$. The $-3$ multiplies the second term.

Power rule

$$\int u^{1/2}\, du = \tfrac{2}{3}u^{3/2}.$$ $$\int 3u^{-1/2}\, du = 3 \cdot 2u^{1/2} = 6u^{1/2}.$$

Combine

$$\int_3^4 (u^{1/2} - 3u^{-1/2})\, du = \left[\tfrac{2}{3}u^{3/2} - 6u^{1/2}\right]_3^4.$$

Sense check. Differentiate $\frac{2}{3}u^{3/2}$: $\frac{2}{3} \cdot \frac{3}{2} u^{1/2} = u^{1/2}$. ✓ Differentiate $-6u^{1/2}$: $-6 \cdot \frac{1}{2} u^{-1/2} = -3u^{-1/2}$. ✓

Antiderivative: $\left[\dfrac{2}{3}u^{3/2} - 6u^{1/2}\right]_3^4$.
↳ from STEP 2: $\left[\dfrac{2}{3}u^{3/2} - 6u^{1/2}\right]_3^4$.
STEP 3/4apply limits 4 and 3

Strategy. Evaluate at $u = 4$ (upper) and $u = 3$ (lower), then subtract. $4^{1/2} = 2$, $4^{3/2} = 8$; $3^{1/2} = \sqrt{3}$, $3^{3/2} = 3\sqrt{3}$.

At $u = 4$

$$\tfrac{2}{3}(4)^{3/2} - 6(4)^{1/2} = \tfrac{2}{3}(8) - 6(2) = \tfrac{16}{3} - 12.$$

At $u = 3$

$$\tfrac{2}{3}(3)^{3/2} - 6(3)^{1/2} = \tfrac{2}{3}(3\sqrt{3}) - 6\sqrt{3} = 2\sqrt{3} - 6\sqrt{3} = -4\sqrt{3}.$$

Subtract: upper $-$ lower

$$\left(\tfrac{16}{3} - 12\right) - (-4\sqrt{3}) = \tfrac{16}{3} - 12 + 4\sqrt{3} = -\tfrac{20}{3} + 4\sqrt{3}.$$
Simplification. $\frac{16}{3} - 12 = \frac{16}{3} - \frac{36}{3} = -\frac{20}{3}$. The $-4\sqrt{3}$ subtracted gives $+4\sqrt{3}$. Keep $\sqrt{3}$ exact — the question demands $a + b\sqrt{3}$ form.

Sense check. $4^{3/2} = (4^{1/2})^3 = 2^3 = 8$. ✓ $3^{3/2} = 3 \cdot 3^{1/2} = 3\sqrt{3}$. ✓

$\int_3^4 \frac{u-3}{\sqrt{u}}\,du = -\dfrac{20}{3} + 4\sqrt{3}$.
↳ from STEP 3: $\int_3^4 = -\dfrac{20}{3} + 4\sqrt{3}$.
STEP 4/4multiply by A = 2

Strategy. The original integral equals $2 \times$ the $u$-integral from part (a). Multiply through by $A = 2$ to get the final answer in the form $a + b\sqrt{3}$.

Multiply by 2

$$2\left(-\tfrac{20}{3} + 4\sqrt{3}\right) = -\tfrac{40}{3} + 8\sqrt{3}.$$

Sense check. $2 \times (-\frac{20}{3}) = -\frac{40}{3}$. $2 \times 4\sqrt{3} = 8\sqrt{3}$. The answer is in the form $a + b\sqrt{3}$ with $a = -\frac{40}{3}$ and $b = 8$, both rational. ✓ Numerically: $-\frac{40}{3} + 8\sqrt{3} \approx -13.33 + 13.86 = 0.53$ — positive and small, consistent with a small positive integrand over a short interval.

Trap (exam technique). The question says "in the form $a + b\sqrt{3}$" — do NOT decimalise. $-\frac{40}{3} + 8\sqrt{3}$ is the exact answer; writing $0.53$ would lose the final mark.
$\boxed{-\dfrac{40}{3} + 8\sqrt{3}}$ — with $a = -\dfrac{40}{3}$, $b = 8$.
