Q2 · Volume of revolution about the $x$-axis

Question

The region $R$ is bounded by the curve $y = \sqrt{x}$, the lines $x = 1$, $x = 4$, and the $x$-axis.

The region $R$ is rotated through $2\pi$ radians about the $x$-axis.

Find, in terms of $\pi$, the exact volume of the solid of revolution generated.

[5 marks]

Annotated diagram

Solution [5 marks] · 6 steps

Volume of revolution about the $x$-axis: $V = \pi \int_a^b [f(x)]^2\, dx$. Here $f(x) = \sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}$, $a = 1$, $b = 4$. The rotation is a full $2\pi$, so no fractional factor is needed (partial rotation $\Theta$ would scale by $\Theta/2\pi$).
STEP 1/6write the formula

Strategy. Start by writing the volume formula. The curve is $y = f(x) = \sqrt{x}$, so $[f(x)]^2$ is the integrand. Limits come from the vertical bounds $x = 1$ and $x = 4$.

Volume of revolution

$$V = \pi \int_1^4 \left[\sqrt{x}\right]^2 dx = \pi \int_1^4 x\, dx.$$
Phuc's self-assessed gap (Q2). Phuc's email listed "volume of revolution" as a gap. The key move: squaring $f(x)$ turns $\sqrt{x}$ into $x$ — a huge simplification. Many students try to integrate $\sqrt{x}$ directly and forget the squaring step, which would give $\frac{2}{3}x^{3/2}$ — wrong integrand.

Sense check. $[\sqrt{x}]^2 = x$ for $x \geq 0$ — always simplify the squared function before integrating. The lower limit $a = 1$ is the left bound ($x = 1$), upper $b = 4$ is the right bound ($x = 4$).

$V = \pi \int_1^4 x\, dx$.
↳ from STEP 1: $V = \pi \int_1^4 x\, dx$.
STEP 2/6integrate x

Strategy. Use the power rule $\int x^n\, dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}$ with $n = 1$. This gives $\frac{x^2}{2}$ — NOT $\ln x$ (that is only for $n = -1$).

Power rule, $n = 1$

$$\int x\, dx = \frac{x^2}{2} + C.$$
Trap 10 (watch-list). $\int x\, dx = \frac{x^2}{2}$, NOT $\ln x$. The $\ln$ result is reserved exclusively for $\int x^{-1}\, dx$. Here the exponent is $+1$, so the power rule applies and the new exponent is $2$.

Sense check. Differentiate $\frac{x^2}{2}$: you get $x$ — the integrand. ✓

$\int_1^4 x\, dx = \left[\dfrac{x^2}{2}\right]_1^4$.
↳ from STEP 2: $\int_1^4 x\, dx = \left[\dfrac{x^2}{2}\right]_1^4$.
STEP 3/6apply limits

Strategy. Evaluate the antiderivative at the upper limit $x = 4$, then subtract the value at the lower limit $x = 1$. Write both evaluations explicitly — Edexcel awards a method mark for showing the substitution.

Upper minus lower

$$\left[\frac{x^2}{2}\right]_1^4 = \frac{4^2}{2} - \frac{1^2}{2} = \frac{16}{2} - \frac{1}{2} = 8 - \tfrac{1}{2}.$$

Sense check. $4^2 = 16$ (from the squares table). $1^2 = 1$. $\frac{16}{2} = 8$, $\frac{1}{2} = 0.5$. Difference $= 7.5$.

$\left[\dfrac{x^2}{2}\right]_1^4 = 8 - \dfrac{1}{2} = \dfrac{15}{2}$.
↳ from STEP 3: $\int_1^4 x\, dx = \dfrac{15}{2}$.
STEP 4/6simplify fraction

Strategy. Combine $8 - \frac{1}{2}$ over a common denominator: $8 = \frac{16}{2}$, so $\frac{16}{2} - \frac{1}{2} = \frac{15}{2}$. Leave as an exact fraction — the question asks for "in terms of $\pi$", so decimals would lose the exact-value mark.

Combine

$$8 - \tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{16}{2} - \tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{15}{2}.$$
Trap (exam technique). The command phrase "find the exact value" (or "in terms of $\pi$") means: leave surds and fractions, do NOT decimalise. Writing $7.5$ instead of $\frac{15}{2}$ would lose the final accuracy mark.

Sense check. $\frac{15}{2} = 7.5$. ✓

$\int_1^4 x\, dx = \dfrac{15}{2}$.
↳ from STEP 4: $\int_1^4 x\, dx = \dfrac{15}{2}$.
STEP 5/6multiply by π

Strategy. Multiply the definite integral by $\pi$ to get the volume. Keep $\pi$ symbolic — the answer is "in terms of $\pi$".

Multiply by $\pi$

$$V = \pi \cdot \frac{15}{2} = \frac{15\pi}{2}.$$

Sense check. Units: the curve $y = \sqrt{x}$ has units of length, $y^2\, dx$ has units length$^3$, so $V$ is a volume. $\frac{15\pi}{2} \approx 23.56$ cubic units.

$V = \dfrac{15\pi}{2}$.
↳ from STEP 5: $V = \dfrac{15\pi}{2}$.
STEP 6/6final answer

Strategy. Box the exact answer. If a partial-rotation variant were asked (angle $\Theta$ instead of $2\pi$), scale by $\Theta/2\pi$: $V_\Theta = \frac{\Theta}{2\pi} \cdot \frac{15\pi}{2}$.

Final volume

$$\boxed{V = \frac{15\pi}{2}}.$$
Partial-rotation note. If the rotation were only $\Theta$ radians (not $2\pi$), the volume would be $V_\Theta = \frac{\Theta}{2\pi} \cdot V = \frac{\Theta}{2\pi} \cdot \frac{15\pi}{2} = \frac{15\Theta}{4}$. The factor $\frac{\Theta}{2\pi}$ is the fraction of a full turn.

Sense check. Numerically $V \approx 23.56$. The solid is a frustum-ish shape (radii $\sqrt{1} = 1$ at $x = 1$ and $\sqrt{4} = 2$ at $x = 4$, height $3$). Frustum volume $= \frac{\pi h}{3}(r_1^2 + r_1 r_2 + r_2^2) = \frac{3\pi}{3}(1 + 2 + 4) = 7\pi \approx 21.99$ — close to our answer (not exact because the profile is curved, not linear, but a good sanity ballpark).

$\boxed{V = \dfrac{15\pi}{2}}$ cubic units.
