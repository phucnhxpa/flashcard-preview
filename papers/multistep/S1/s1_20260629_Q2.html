Part (B) — show there is only one outlier [3 marks] · 3 steps

The question specifies the outlier rule to use (IQR rule). An outlier is any value $x$ with $x < Q_1 - 1.5(Q_3 - Q_1)$ or $x > Q_3 + 1.5(Q_3 - Q_1)$. With $Q_1 = 60$, $Q_3 = 95$.
STEP 1/3compute IQR boundaries

Strategy. Compute the IQR $= Q_3 - Q_1$, then the two fences: lower $= Q_1 - 1.5 \times \text{IQR}$ and upper $= Q_3 + 1.5 \times \text{IQR}$.

IQR and fences

$$\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1 = 95 - 60 = 35.$$ $$\text{Lower fence} = 60 - 1.5 \times 35 = 60 - 52.5 = 7.5.$$ $$\text{Upper fence} = 95 + 1.5 \times 35 = 95 + 52.5 = 147.5.$$

Sense check. Any weight below 7.5 kg or above 147.5 kg is an outlier. A 7.5 kg boxer is absurd; the lower fence is effectively inactive here.

↳ from STEP 1: lower fence 7.5, upper fence 147.5.

Part (D) — skewness: describe and interpret [4 marks] · 2 steps

Club B has $Q_1 = 60$, $Q_2 = 68$, $Q_3 = 95$ kg. Two commands: describe (identify the feature) and interpret (explain in context, naming the variables).
STEP 1/2describe the skew

Strategy. Compare the two halves of the IQR. Positive (right) skew: $Q_3 - Q_2 > Q_2 - Q_1$. Negative (left) skew: $Q_3 - Q_2 < Q_2 - Q_1$. Symmetric: equal.

Compute the two halves

$$Q_3 - Q_2 = 95 - 68 = 27, \qquad Q_2 - Q_1 = 68 - 60 = 8.$$ $$27 > 8 \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{positive skew}.$$
Phuc's mistake (wrong inequality). Phuc wrote $Q_3 - Q_1 > Q_2 - Q_1$ — comparing the full IQR to the lower half. The correct comparison is between the two halves: $Q_3 - Q_2$ vs $Q_2 - Q_1$. Harry drew it in two colours: "this is $Q_3 - Q_2$; this is $Q_2 - Q_1$."

Sense check. The upper half (27 kg wide) is more than 3 times the lower half (8 kg) — a strong positive skew, consistent with the outlier being a heavy member.

↳ from STEP 1: $Q_3 - Q_2 = 27 > 8 = Q_2 - Q_1$ ⇒ positive skew.
