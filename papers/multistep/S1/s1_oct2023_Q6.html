Q6 — Regression Equations, $S_{xy}$, PMCC

Question

From the same 12 observations the two regression equations between $x$ and $y$ are

$y$ on $x$: $\;y = 1.4x + 1.5$

$x$ on $y$: $\;x = 1.2 + 0.2y$

(a) (i) Find the point of intersection of these two lines. (ii) Hence show that $\sum x = 25$. (4)
Given $\displaystyle \sum xy = \tfrac{6961}{60}$,
(b) find $S_{xy}$. (4)
(c) Find the product moment correlation coefficient between $x$ and $y$. (4)

[12 marks]

Setup2 steps

STEP 1/2

(a)(i) Solve simultaneously. Sub $y = 1.4x + 1.5$ into $x = 1.2 + 0.2y$:
$$x = 1.2 + 0.2(1.4x + 1.5) = 1.2 + 0.28x + 0.3 = 1.5 + 0.28x.$$
$$x - 0.28x = 1.5 \implies 0.72x = 1.5 \implies x = \tfrac{1.5}{0.72} = \tfrac{150}{72} = \tfrac{25}{12}.$$

Then $y = 1.4 \cdot \tfrac{25}{12} + 1.5 = \tfrac{35}{12} + \tfrac{18}{12} = \tfrac{53}{12}.$

From the source notes — common traps for this question.

What went wrong. (c) was the inefficient one --- Phuc tried to compute $S_{xx}$ and $S_{yy}$ from scratch (which needs $\sum x^2$ and $\sum y^2$, not given) instead of using the shortcut $r^2 = b_{yx} \cdot b_{xy}$.

Phuc's incorrect approach (annotated). Phuc started: "I need $S_{xx}$ and $S_{yy}$, so I need $\sum x^2$ \ldots{} which we don't have, so I'm stuck." The shortcut $r^2 = b_{yx} b_{xy}$ bypasses both.

STEP 2/2

$$\boxed{\left(\tfrac{25}{12},\ \tfrac{53}{12}\right)}.$$

(a)(ii) The point of intersection of the two regression lines is $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$. So $\bar{x} = \tfrac{25}{12}$, hence
$$\sum x = n\bar{x} = 12 \cdot \tfrac{25}{12} = \boxed{25}.\;\blacksquare$$

Similarly $\sum y = 12 \cdot \tfrac{53}{12} = 53$.

Target: $\boxed{25}$

Part (b)[4 marks] . 2 steps

STEP 1/2

Apply $S_{xy} = \sum xy - n\bar{x}\bar{y}$:
$$S_{xy} = \tfrac{6961}{60} - 12 \cdot \tfrac{25}{12} \cdot \tfrac{53}{12} = \tfrac{6961}{60} - \tfrac{25 \cdot 53}{12} = \tfrac{6961}{60} - \tfrac{1325}{12}.$$

STEP 2/2

Common denominator $60$:
$$= \tfrac{6961}{60} - \tfrac{6625}{60} = \tfrac{336}{60} = \tfrac{28}{5} = \boxed{5.6}.$$

Target: $\boxed{5.6}$

Part (c)[4 marks] . 2 steps

Part (b) result: $\boxed{5.6}$
STEP 1/2

Shortcut: $r^2 = b_{yx}\cdot b_{xy}$.

From the two equations:
- $b_{yx} = 1.4$ (slope of $y$-on-$x$).
- $b_{xy} = 0.2$ (slope of $x$-on-$y$).

STEP 2/2

$$r^2 = 1.4 \times 0.2 = 0.28.$$
$$r = \sqrt{0.28} = 0.5291\ldots \approx \boxed{0.529}.$$

Both slopes positive $\to$ $r$ positive.

Verification via the long route:
- $b_{yx} = \tfrac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}} = 1.4 \implies S_{xx} = \tfrac{5.6}{1.4} = 4$.
- $b_{xy} = \tfrac{S_{xy}}{S_{yy}} = 0.2 \implies S_{yy} = \tfrac{5.6}{0.2} = 28$.
- $r = \tfrac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx} S_{yy}}} = \tfrac{5.6}{\sqrt{4 \cdot 28}} = \tfrac{5.6}{\sqrt{112}} = \tfrac{5.6}{10.583} \approx 0.529.$ ✓

Target: $\boxed{0.529}$

STEP 1/1

Key formula / concept.

Key formula / concept

  • Intersection of the two regression lines is $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$.
  • $S_{xy} = \sum xy - n\bar{x}\bar{y}$.
  • $b_{yx} = S_{xy}/S_{xx}$ and $b_{xy} = S_{xy}/S_{yy}$.
  • PMCC shortcut: $r^2 = b_{yx} \cdot b_{xy}$ (sign of $r$ matches sign of either slope).

Exam checklist before moving on.

Exam checklist

  • [ ] Solve the two regression equations as a 2$\times$2 linear system.
  • [ ] State explicitly "intersection = $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$" before computing $\sum x = n\bar{x}$.
  • [ ] When computing $S_{xy}$, use $\sum xy - n\bar{x}\bar{y}$ (not $\sum xy - \tfrac{\sum x \sum y}{n}$ both forms are equivalent but the first plugs straight from (a)).
  • [ ] For PMCC, check whether both regression lines are given --- if yes, use $r^2 = b_{yx} b_{xy}$.
  • [ ] Sign of $r$ matches the common sign of the two regression slopes.

Common pitfalls.

Common pitfalls

  • Computing $\bar{x}\bar{y}$ as a single decimal and rounding too early --- keep the fractions $\tfrac{25}{12} \cdot \tfrac{53}{12} = \tfrac{1325}{144}$ until the very end.
  • Forgetting that "hence" in (a)(ii) means "use the intersection".
  • Computing $S_{xx}$ from $\sum x^2$ when it's neither given nor needed.
