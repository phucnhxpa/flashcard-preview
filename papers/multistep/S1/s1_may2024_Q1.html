Q1 — Back-to-back Stem-and-Leaf, Quartiles, Outliers, Box Plot

Question

A researcher is investigating the growth of two types of tree, Birch and Maple. The height, to the nearest cm, a seedling grows in one year is recorded for 35 Birch trees and 32 Maple trees. The results are summarised in the back-to-back stem and leaf diagram below.

Totals Birch (read right-to-left) Stem Maple (read left-to-right) Totals
(2) 9 8 2 5 7 7 8 9 (5)
(8) 9 9 9 6 5 3 1 1 3 0 2 6 6 8 9 9 (7)
(9) 9 8 8 7 6 3 1 1 1 4 1 1 1 k 7 8 (6)
(9) 7 7 7 5 4 3 2 1 0 5 0 1 2 3 4 4 4 (7)
(3) 7 6 5 6 3 4 6 (3)
(3) 6 5 4 7 0 7 (2)
(1) 5 8 0 0 (2)

Key: 5 | 6 | 3 means 65 cm for a Birch tree and 63 cm for a Maple tree.

The median height that these Maple trees grow in one year is 45 cm.
(a) Find the value of $k$, used in the stem and leaf diagram. (1)
(b) Find the lower quartile and the upper quartile of the height grown in one year for these Birch trees. (2)
The researcher defines an outlier as an observation that is greater than $Q_3 + 1.5 \times (Q_3 - Q_1)$ or less than $Q_1 - 1.5 \times (Q_3 - Q_1)$.
(c) Show that there is only one outlier amongst the Birch trees. (2)
The grid on page 3 shows a box plot for the heights that the Maple trees grow in one year.
(d) On the same grid draw a box plot for the heights that the Birch trees grow in one year. (4)
(e) Comment on any difference in the distributions of the growth of these Birch trees and the growth of these Maple trees. State the values of any statistics you have used to support your comment. (1)
The researcher realises he has missed out 4 pieces of data for the Maple trees. The heights each seedling grows in one year, to the nearest cm, in ascending order, for these 4 Maple trees are 27 cm, $a$ cm, 48 cm, $2a$ cm.
Given that there is no change to the box plot for the Maple trees given on page 3,
(f) find the range of possible values for $a$. Show your working clearly. (3)

[13 marks]

Part (a)[1 mark] · 5 steps

STEP 1/5

Worked solution — finding $k$

For Maple, $N=32$ (even), so the median is the mean of the 16th and 17th ordered values.

STEP 2/5

Cumulative frequencies for Maple by stem: $5,\ 12,\ 18,\ 25,\ 28,\ 30,\ 32$.

The 16th and 17th values both fall in the stem-4 row (positions 13–18). The stem-4 leaves are $1,\ 1,\ 1,\ k,\ 7,\ 8$ in ascending order:

STEP 3/5

$$\text{16th value} = 40+k,\qquad \text{17th value} = 47.$$

Setting the median equal to 45:

STEP 4/5

$$\frac{(40+k) + 47}{2} = 45 \;\Longrightarrow\; 87+k = 90 \;\Longrightarrow\; \boxed{k = 3}$$

Key concept: For $N$ even, median = $\frac{1}{2}(x_{N/2} + x_{N/2+1})$. The leaves must remain in ascending order, so $k$ is constrained between the leaf before it (1) and after it (7).

Target: $\boxed{k = 3}$
STEP 5/5

What went wrong (Phuc, this part).

Stopped before attempting — back-to-back stem-and-leaf was new IAL material. The Apr 23 Loom shows Phuc initially wrote "$k+7$" guessing $k$ was a sum; Mr. Fuat steered him to recognise $k$ as a single leaf digit.

From the Loom (Fuat): "$k$ is a digit. Not a count, not a sum. It's the fourth leaf in that row, so the value it represents is $40+k$."

Exam checklist.

  • [ ] Read the key before reading values (5 | 6 | 3 = 65 for Birch, 63 for Maple).
  • [ ] For back-to-back diagrams, the left leaves are read outwards from the stem (right-to-left).
  • [ ] Confirm the row totals add to the stated $N$ (Birch: 35; Maple: 32).
  • [ ] Check $k$ keeps leaves in ascending order.

Common pitfalls.

Treating $k$ as a frequency; reading the left-hand leaves left-to-right (wrong direction); forgetting that median of 32 values uses the 16th and 17th.

Part (b)[2 marks] · 3 steps

Carry-in: Part (a) result: $\boxed{k = 3}$
STEP 1/3

Worked solution — Quartiles for Birch

Birch is $N=35$. Using the Edexcel S1 rule for non-integer position: $Q_1$ is the $\left\lceil N/4 \right\rceil = 9$th value; $Q_3$ is the $\left\lceil 3N/4 \right\rceil = 27$th value.

Sorted Birch values:
$$28,\ 29,\ 31,\ 31,\ 33,\ 35,\ 36,\ 39,\ 39,\ 39,\ 41,\ 41,\ 41,\ 43,\ 46,\ 47,\ 48,\ 48,\ 49,\ 50,\ 51,\ 52,\ 53,\ 54,\ 55,\ 57,\ 57,\ 57,\ 65,\ 66,\ 67,\ 74,\ 75,\ 76,\ 85.$$

STEP 2/3
  • 9th value: $Q_1 = 39$
  • 27th value: $Q_3 = 57$

$$\boxed{Q_1 = 39\text{ cm},\qquad Q_3 = 57\text{ cm}}$$

Key concept (NOT in formula booklet): For $n$ values:
- If $n/4$ is not an integer → round up to the next position.
- If $n/4$ is an integer → take the average of the $(n/4)$th and $(n/4+1)$th values.

Same rule for $3n/4$.

Target: $\boxed{Q_1 = 39\text{ cm},\qquad Q_3 = 57\text{ cm}}$
STEP 3/3

What went wrong (Phuc, this part).

Phuc wrote $Q_1=35$, $Q_3=57$. He used the wrong position for $Q_1$ — counted the 6th value (35) instead of the 9th (39). The Loom analysis confirms positions are 9 and 27 for $N=35$.

Exam checklist.

  • [ ] Calculate $n/4$ and $3n/4$ first; decide round-up vs. average rule.
  • [ ] List cumulative stem totals so you can jump to the $k$-th value fast.
  • [ ] Read leaves on the Birch side right-to-left.
  • [ ] State units (cm) in your final answer.

Common pitfalls.

Using $(n+1)/4$ (that's the M-stats / GCSE rule, not Edexcel S1); miscounting the leaves on the back-to-back side.

See also Q3(b) for grouped-data quartiles using linear interpolation — different method, same statistic.

Part (c)[2 marks] · 3 steps

Carry-in: Part (a) result: $\boxed{k = 3}$
Part (b) result: $\boxed{Q_1 = 39\text{ cm},\qquad Q_3 = 57\text{ cm}}$
STEP 1/3

Worked solution — Show only one outlier

$$\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1 = 57 - 39 = 18.$$

Lower fence: $Q_1 - 1.5\,\text{IQR} = 39 - 27 = 12$.
Upper fence: $Q_3 + 1.5\,\text{IQR} = 57 + 27 = 84$.

STEP 2/3

Smallest Birch = 28 (> 12, no low outlier).
Values greater than 84: only 85.

$$\boxed{\text{Only one outlier: } 85\text{ cm}.}$$

Key formula (NOT in booklet): Outlier ⇔ $x < Q_1 - 1.5(Q_3-Q_1)$ or $x > Q_3 + 1.5(Q_3-Q_1)$.

Target: $\boxed{\text{Only one outlier: } 85\text{ cm}.}$
STEP 3/3

Common trap (this part).

outliers — the analysis flags this as the tutor using wrong $Q_1, Q_3$. With the correct $Q_1=39, Q_3=57$, the fences become 12 and 84, and the question's "only one outlier" is consistent.

Exam checklist.

  • [ ] Write $1.5 \times \text{IQR}$ as a separate line so the marker awards method marks.
  • [ ] Quote both fences even if no low outliers exist.
  • [ ] State the outlier value(s) explicitly.

Common pitfalls.

Forgetting the multiplier 1.5; comparing only the max/min instead of scanning the dataset.

See also Q1(d) — the outlier 85 affects how you draw the whisker.

Part (e)[1 mark] · 3 steps

Carry-in: Part (a) result: $\boxed{k = 3}$
Part (b) result: $\boxed{Q_1 = 39\text{ cm},\qquad Q_3 = 57\text{ cm}}$
Part (c) result: $\boxed{\text{Only one outlier: } 85\text{ cm}.}$
STEP 1/3

Worked solution — Comment on distributions

Maple summary: $Q_1=36$, median $=45$, $Q_3=54$, IQR $=18$.
Birch summary: $Q_1=39$, median $=48$, $Q_3=57$, IQR $=18$.

STEP 2/3

Comment: The median Birch growth (48 cm) is greater than the median Maple growth (45 cm), so on average Birch trees grow more in one year. The interquartile range is the same (18 cm) for both, so the central spread is similar; however, Birch has an outlier (85 cm) while Maple has none, so the Birch distribution has a heavier upper tail.

Key concept: A "compare distributions" 1-mark comment must (i) compare a specific statistic and (ii) state both values.

STEP 3/3

Exam checklist.

  • [ ] Quote two numerical values (e.g. medians 48 and 45).
  • [ ] One sentence is enough — the mark is for the right comparison.
  • [ ] Avoid the word "average" alone — say "median" or "mean".

Common pitfalls.

Saying "Birch are taller" with no values; comparing means (none were calculated).

Part (f)[3 marks] · 5 steps

Carry-in: Part (a) result: $\boxed{k = 3}$
Part (b) result: $\boxed{Q_1 = 39\text{ cm},\qquad Q_3 = 57\text{ cm}}$
Part (c) result: $\boxed{\text{Only one outlier: } 85\text{ cm}.}$
STEP 1/5

Worked solution — Range of possible $a$

New $N = 32 + 4 = 36$. The four new data are $27,\ a,\ 48,\ 2a$ with $27 \le a \le 48$ and $2a \ge 48$.

Box plot must be unchanged, so the new dataset must give:
$\text{Min}=25,\ Q_1=36,\ \text{Median}=45,\ Q_3=54,\ \text{Max}=80.$

STEP 2/5

For $N=36$ (divisible by 4): $Q_1$ = average of 9th and 10th values; median = average of 18th and 19th; $Q_3$ = average of 27th and 28th.

Constraint 1 — Max stays 80: Need $2a \le 80 \;\Rightarrow\; a \le 40$.

Constraint 2 — Min stays 25: $27 > 25$ ✓ automatically.

STEP 3/5

Constraint 3 — $Q_1 = 36$: Build the new sorted list. Adding 27 shifts the original position 3 onwards. The original 8th value is 36 (1st of two 36s) and the 9th is 36 (2nd 36). After adding 27, these slide to positions 9 and 10. For their average to remain 36, the value $a$ must not displace either — so $a \ge 36$.

Constraint 4 — Median $= 45$: With $a \in [36,40]$, the 18th and 19th values of the new list become 43 and 47 (the existing 16th and 17th values, each shifted forward by two from the two added values 27 and $a$). So median $= (43+47)/2 = 45$ ✓.

Constraint 5 — $Q_3 = 54$: The 27th and 28th values fall on two of the three 54s (positions 26, 27, 28 in the new list). $Q_3 = 54$ ✓.

STEP 4/5

Combining: $\boxed{36 \le a \le 40}.$

Key concept: "No change to the box plot" pins down five values (min, $Q_1$, med, $Q_3$, max). Each gives a constraint on the new data. Solve the strictest pair.

Target: $\boxed{36 \le a \le 40}$
STEP 5/5

What went wrong (Phuc, this part).

The tutor concluded $27 \le a \le 54$ — this ignored the $Q_1$ constraint (the 9th/10th new values would no longer be 36 if $a<36$) and ignored $2a \le 80$ (he kept $a \le 48$ from the ordering instead of the stricter $a \le 40$).

From the Loom (Fuat): "If $a$ is greater than 49, it would affect the position of the upper quartile or median." — direction is right, but the binding constraints are on $Q_1$ and on the max.

Exam checklist.

  • [ ] List the five box-plot statistics that must stay fixed.
  • [ ] Use $N = $ old + 4 (here 36) when re-deriving quartile positions.
  • [ ] State both inequality endpoints in the final range.
  • [ ] Verify a sample value (e.g. $a=38$) by re-sorting.

Common pitfalls.

Forgetting $2a$ must not exceed Max; using only the ordering constraint ($27 \le a \le 48$) without the box-plot constraint; mis-applying the $N/4$ rule when $N=36$ becomes divisible by 4.

See also Q3(b) for related "where does a new value fall" reasoning.
