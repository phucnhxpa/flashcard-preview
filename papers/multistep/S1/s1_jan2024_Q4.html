Q4 - Outlier Test, Regression, Interpolation vs Extrapolation

Question

A French test and a Spanish test were sat by 11 students. The table below shows their marks.

Student A B C D E F G H I J K
French ($f$) 24 30 32 32 36 36 40 44 50 60 68
Spanish ($s$) 16 90 24 28 32 36 38 44 48 48 68

Greg says that if these points were plotted on a scatter diagram, then the point $(30, 90)$ would be an outlier because 90 is an outlier for the Spanish marks. An outlier is defined as a value greater than $Q_3 + 1.5(Q_3 - Q_1)$ or smaller than $Q_1 - 1.5(Q_3 - Q_1)$.

(a) Show that 90 is an outlier for the Spanish marks. (3)

Ignoring the point $(30, 90)$, Greg calculated:
$$\sum f = 422,\quad \sum s = 382,\quad S_{ff} = 1667.6,\quad S_{fs} = 1735.6$$

(b) Use these summary statistics to show that the equation of the least squares regression line of $s$ on $f$ for the remaining 10 students is $s = -5.72 + 1.04 f$ where the values of the intercept and gradient are given to 3 significant figures. You must show your working. (3)

(c) Give an interpretation of the gradient of the regression line. (1)

Two further students sat the French test but missed the Spanish test.

(d) Using the equation given in part (b), estimate
(i) a Spanish mark for the student who scored 55 marks in their French test,
(ii) a Spanish mark for the student who scored 18 marks in their French test. (3)

(e) State, giving a reason, which of the two estimates found in part (d) would be the more reliable estimate. (2)

[12 marks]

STEP 1/5Part (a)

Strategy. Active recall: choose the method for Part (a) before unmasking. Reproduce exam-safe working, not just the final answer.

(a) Sort Spanish marks: $16, 24, 28, 32, 36, 38, 44, 48, 48, 68, 90$. For $n=11$:

$$Q_1\ \text{at position } \frac{n+1}{4} = 3 \Rightarrow Q_1 = 28$$
$$Q_3\ \text{at position } \frac{3(n+1)}{4} = 9 \Rightarrow Q_3 = 48$$

$$Q_3 + 1.5(Q_3 - Q_1) = 48 + 1.5 \times (48 - 28) = 48 + 1.5 \times 20 = 48 + 30 = 78$$

Since $90 > 78$, the value 90 lies above the upper fence, so 90 is an outlier ✓

What went wrong:. This was not explicitly tagged but is procedurally tricky. The main pitfalls: quartile positions for $n=11$, sign of the intercept $a = \bar{s} - b\bar{f}$, and identifying which estimate in (d) is interpolation vs extrapolation.

STEP 2/5Part (b)

Strategy. Active recall: choose the method for Part (b) before unmasking. Reproduce exam-safe working, not just the final answer.

(b) With $n = 10$ (Greg ignored point B):

$$\bar{f} = \frac{422}{10} = 42.2,\qquad \bar{s} = \frac{382}{10} = 38.2$$

$$b = \frac{S_{fs}}{S_{ff}} = \frac{1735.6}{1667.6} = 1.0408\ldots \approx 1.04\ \text{(3 s.f.)}$$

$$a = \bar{s} - b\bar{f} = 38.2 - 1.0408 \times 42.2 = 38.2 - 43.921 = -5.721 \approx -5.72\ \text{(3 s.f.)}$$

$$\therefore s = -5.72 + 1.04 f \quad ✓$$

STEP 3/5Part (c)

Strategy. Active recall: choose the method for Part (c) before unmasking. Reproduce exam-safe working, not just the final answer.

(c) For each additional mark scored in the French test, the predicted Spanish mark increases by approximately 1.04 marks.

(d)(i) $f = 55$: $s = -5.72 + 1.04 \times 55 = -5.72 + 57.2 = 51.48 \approx 51.5$ marks.

(d)(ii) $f = 18$: $s = -5.72 + 1.04 \times 18 = -5.72 + 18.72 = 13.0$ marks.

STEP 4/5Part (e)

Strategy. Active recall: choose the method for Part (e) before unmasking. Reproduce exam-safe working, not just the final answer.

(e) The estimate for $f = 55$ is more reliable because 55 lies within the observed range of French marks ($24 \le f \le 68$), so it is interpolation. The estimate at $f = 18$ is extrapolation (outside the range $24$--$68$) and so is unreliable, as the linear relationship is not guaranteed to hold outside the sampled data.

Formula anchor.

Key formula / concept:
- Outlier fence: $Q_3 + 1.5\,\text{IQR}$ upper, $Q_1 - 1.5\,\text{IQR}$ lower.
- Regression slope $b = S_{xy}/S_{xx}$, intercept $a = \bar{y} - b\bar{x}$.
- Interpolation (inside data range) is reliable; extrapolation (outside) is not.
