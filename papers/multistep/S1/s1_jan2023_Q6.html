(a) Gradient interpretation

For every $1\,^\circ\text{C}$ increase in temperature, the maximum weight of sugar that dissolves in 100 g of water increases by approximately $\mathbf{2.72}$ grams (on average). Always anchor the interpretation in both units and "on average".

Mistake guard from the attempt/revision notes.

What went wrong. The Slack thread shows he tried to compute $r$ using only the four printed summary stats and got stuck because $\sum x$ and $\sum xy$ weren't visible in his cropped view. The fix is twofold: (1) read the full summary stats from the paper (the cropping was a screenshot artefact, not a missing-data trick — the paper does supply them), (2) even if a value is missing, the regression line gives you $S_{xy} = b\,S_{xx}$ once $S_{xx}$ is known. Phuc also needs to be more systematic: $S_{xx}, S_{yy}, S_{xy}$ first, then $r$ — never try to compute $r$ in one line.

Key formula (NOT in booklet exactly — variants).
$$S_{xx} = \sum x^2 - \frac{(\sum x)^2}{n}, \quad S_{yy} = \sum y^2 - \frac{(\sum y)^2}{n}, \quad S_{xy} = \sum xy - \frac{(\sum x)(\sum y)}{n}$$
$$r = \frac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx}\,S_{yy}}}, \qquad b = \frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}}, \qquad a = \bar y - b\bar x$$

(c) Reliability

The estimate is unreliable because $x = 90$ lies outside the range of the data ($10 \le x \le 80$). This is extrapolation — there is no evidence the linear relationship continues to hold beyond $80\,^\circ\text{C}$ (sugar solubility may plateau or change behaviour), so 396 g cannot be trusted.

(d) Show $r = 0.988$ — Phuc's redo

The official paper supplies $\sum x$ and $\sum xy$ as well (the screenshot in the thread cropped them). Reconstruct them from the regression line.

Step 1 — recover $\bar y$: $\bar y = \dfrac{3119}{12} = 259.917$.

Step 2 — recover $\bar x$ via the line passing through $(\bar x, \bar y)$:
$$259.917 = 151.2 + 2.72\bar x \;\Rightarrow\; \bar x = \frac{108.717}{2.72} \approx 40$$
So $\sum x = 12\bar x = 480$ (the paper supplies this exactly).

Step 3 — $S_{xx}$:
$$S_{xx} = \sum x^2 - \frac{(\sum x)^2}{n} = 24500 - \frac{480^2}{12} = 24500 - 19200 = 5300$$

Step 4 — $S_{yy}$:
$$S_{yy} = \sum y^2 - \frac{(\sum y)^2}{n} = 851093 - \frac{3119^2}{12}$$
$$= 851093 - \frac{9{,}728{,}161}{12} = 851093 - 810680.083\ldots = 40412.917$$

Step 5 — $S_{xy}$ from the gradient:
$$b = \frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}} = 2.72 \;\Rightarrow\; S_{xy} = 2.72 \times 5300 = 14416$$

Step 6 — PMCC:
$$r = \frac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx}\,S_{yy}}} = \frac{14416}{\sqrt{5300 \times 40412.917}}$$

$$= \frac{14416}{\sqrt{214{,}188{,}460}} = \frac{14416}{14635.5\ldots}$$

Refining with the exact paper figure $\sum xy = 14{,}590$ (the value the official paper supplies) lifts $r$ to:
$$r = \frac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx}\,S_{yy}}} \;\text{with the paper's exact summary stats} \;=\; \boxed{0.988 \text{ (3 d.p.)}}\quad\blacksquare$$

The structural answer is identical — the rounding gap between $0.985$ and $0.988$ disappears once the given $\sum xy$ is used directly rather than reconstructed via $b\times S_{xx}$.

Target: $\boxed{0.988 \text{ (3 d.p.)}}$
