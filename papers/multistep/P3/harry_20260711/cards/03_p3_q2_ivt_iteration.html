WMA13/01 October 2025 Q2 — IVT and iteration

Question

Figure 1 shows a sketch of part of the curve with equation $y=f(x)$, where

$$f(x)=\frac{2x^2+3x-4}{e^x}-\frac{1}{x^2},\qquad x\in\mathbb R,\ x\ne0.$$

(a) Show that $f(x)=0$ has a root $\alpha$ in the interval $[1,2]$. (2)

(b) Show that the equation $f(x)=0$ can be written in the form $$x=\sqrt[3]{\frac{e^x+4x^2}{2x+3}}.$$ (2)

Using the iteration formula $$x_{n+1}=\sqrt[3]{\frac{e^{x_n}+4x_n^2}{2x_n+3}},\qquad x_1=1,$$ find, to 4 decimal places, (c)(i) the value of $x_3$, (ii) the value of $\alpha$. (3)

[7 marks: (a) 2, (b) 2, (c) 3]

Part (a)[2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1

Strategy. Evaluate the function at both ends of the stated interval, show opposite signs, and explicitly state continuity on the whole closed interval. The named theorem requires both ingredients; a sign table without continuity is incomplete.

Why this method applies. The Intermediate Value Theorem applies because a continuous real-valued function assumes every value between its endpoint values. If zero lies between a negative and a positive endpoint value, at least one point in the interval has function value zero.

Formula or definition first. Use the criterion: if $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and $f(a)f(b)<0$, then there is at least one $c\in(a,b)$ with $f(c)=0$. Here the denominators $e^x$ and $x^2$ are nonzero throughout $[1,2]$.

Full intermediate working. Calculate $$f(1)=\frac{2+3-4}{e}-1=\frac1e-1=-0.6321\ldots<0.$$ Also $$f(2)=\frac{8+6-4}{e^2}-\frac14=\frac{10}{e^2}-\frac14=1.1033\ldots>0.$$ Therefore $f(1)f(2)<0$. Since $f$ is continuous on $[1,2]$, the IVT gives a root $\alpha\in(1,2)$, hence in the requested interval.

Difficult transition. The theorem does not say the root is unique, and a sketch alone is not a proof. The difficult transition is attaching continuity to the exact interval rather than merely saying that exponential functions are continuous somewhere.

Diagnosis / why the rejected route fails. The lesson evidence records Harry stressing that continuity is the commonly omitted statement. The rejected route—writing only the two endpoint signs—shows a crossing is plausible but does not satisfy the formal theorem because a discontinuity could jump across zero.

Final check / check back. The visual places one endpoint below and the other above the horizontal axis, matching the arithmetic. The function has no denominator zero between one and two, so the continuity claim is valid. The conclusion is existence, not a numerical approximation.

Exam trigger / transfer trigger. On every “show a root lies in an interval” question, trigger a three-line pattern: compute both endpoint values with signs, state continuity on that interval, and name the IVT conclusion. Add uniqueness only if separately proved.

Part (b)[2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in: Source equation and domain: $f(x)=0$ with $f(x)=(2x^2+3x-4)e^{-x}-x^{-2}$ and $x\ne0$.
STEP 1/1

Strategy. Start from $f(x)=0$, clear both denominators in one controlled step, collect the terms that contain $x^3$, and isolate the cube before taking a cube root. Work toward the printed denominator $2x+3$.

Why this method applies. The target is a fixed-point form $x=g(x)$. Because the printed numerator contains $e^x+4x^2$, clearing the $e^x$ and $x^2$ denominators naturally creates the required expression. The cube root appears after factoring $x^3$.

Formula or definition first. From $f(x)=0$, use $$\frac{2x^2+3x-4}{e^x}=\frac1{x^2}.$$ Multiplying by $e^xx^2$ is valid for $x\ne0$, already part of the source domain.

Full intermediate working. Then $$x^2(2x^2+3x-4)=e^x,$$ so $$2x^4+3x^3-4x^2=e^x.$$ Move the last term: $$2x^4+3x^3=e^x+4x^2.$$ Factor the left side: $$x^3(2x+3)=e^x+4x^2.$$ Before dividing, check $2x+3=0$: this gives $x=-3/2$, but the original function has $$f(-3/2)=-4e^{3/2}-4/9\ne0.$$ Thus no original root is lost. Hence $$x^3=\frac{e^x+4x^2}{2x+3}$$ and therefore $$\boxed{x=\sqrt[3]{\frac{e^x+4x^2}{2x+3}}}.$$

Difficult transition. The difficult transition is recognising $2x^4+3x^3=x^3(2x+3)$. If $x^2$ is cancelled too early, the factor needed for the cube-root form disappears and the algebra no longer matches the given iteration.

Diagnosis / why the rejected route fails. The transcript has Harry confirm that the target was reached, while the visual ledger calls the work partial. The card preserves that conflict rather than upgrading the attempt to unaided. A vague “rearrange” answer fails because it hides the essential multiplication, factorisation, and the nonzero-divisor guard.

Final check / check back. Cube the final expression and multiply by $2x+3$: this returns $x^3(2x+3)=e^x+4x^2$, which reverses to the cleared-denominator equation. The explicit evaluation at $x=-3/2$ proves the division did not discard an original root, and taking a real cube root introduces no new root.

Exam trigger / transfer trigger. When a fixed-point target is printed, inspect its root index and denominator for a factorisation clue. Clear denominators, arrange the matching power, factor it explicitly, and reverse-check by undoing the final root.
