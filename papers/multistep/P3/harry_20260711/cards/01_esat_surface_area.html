ESAT lesson warm-up — sphere and ten closed cylinders

Question

The surface area of a solid sphere of radius $R$ equals the total surface area of 10 solid closed cylinders of radius $r$ and height $h$, where $h=4R$.

Find $R$ in terms of $r$.

Status: solved on the lesson board. The option letters were not captured reliably, so this card preserves the derivation and does not claim an official option or mark allocation.

No official mark allocation: lesson-reconstructed warm-up

Set up the surface-area equationLesson item · no official marks · 1 step

STEP 1/1

Strategy. Separate the two solids, write the surface area of one complete closed cylinder, multiply that result by ten, and only then use the height relation. Keeping this order makes it visible where every face enters the equation.

Why this method applies. The equality is between surface areas, not volumes. A closed cylinder has two circular ends and one curved side; the curved side unrolls to a rectangle of width $2\pi r$ and height $h$. Omitting either end changes the quadratic and the physical answer.

Formula or definition first. Use $S_{sphere}=4\pi R^2$ and $S_{closed\ cylinder}=2\pi r^2+2\pi rh$. The lesson gives $h=4R$, and the total for ten identical cylinders is ten times the one-cylinder expression.

Full intermediate working. Substitute without cancelling prematurely: $$4\pi R^2=10(2\pi r^2+2\pi rh).$$ Now use $h=4R$: $$4\pi R^2=10(2\pi r^2+8\pi rR)=20\pi r^2+80\pi rR.$$ Dividing by $4\pi$ produces $R^2=5r^2+20rR$. Every term still has dimensions of length squared.

Difficult transition. The difficult move is distinguishing the curved-surface term $2\pi rh$ from the cylinder volume $\pi r^2h$. The former has units $L^2$ and belongs in a surface-area equality; the latter has units $L^3$ and cannot be added to circular areas.

Diagnosis / why the rejected route fails. The lesson record identifies two concrete slips: one circular end was initially omitted, and a volume expression was considered. Both routes fail dimensionally and undercount the closed surface. The corrected setup uses two ends exactly once and keeps the square in $4\pi R^2$.

Final check / check back. Count the geometric pieces in the visual: sphere area on the left, then for each cylinder two ends plus one unrolled rectangle. Check dimensions after substitution: $R^2$, $r^2$, and $rR$ are all areas. That confirms the equation is structurally coherent before solving.

Exam trigger / transfer trigger. When a solid-comparison problem appears, sketch or mentally unfold every surface, name the formula before substitution, multiply by the number of solids last, and use units to reject an accidental volume formula.

Solve the quadratic and select the physical rootLesson item · no official marks · 1 step

Carry-in: Authorised setup from the preceding part: $R^2-20rR-5r^2=0$ with $r>0$ and $R>0$.
STEP 1/1

Strategy. Rearrange the carried surface-area equation into a quadratic in $R$, apply the quadratic formula exactly, and then impose the physical condition that a radius must be positive. Do not select a sign from habit before evaluating it.

Why this method applies. After the geometry is encoded, the unknown radius occurs both as $R^2$ and as $rR$, so a quadratic method is necessary. Algebra generates two formal roots, but the modelling context supplies the domain restriction $R>0$ and $r>0$.

Formula or definition first. From $R^2=5r^2+20rR$, use $R^2-20rR-5r^2=0$ and the quadratic formula $R=(-b\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac})/(2a)$ with $a=1$, $b=-20r$, $c=-5r^2$.

Full intermediate working. Compute the discriminant carefully: $$b^2-4ac=400r^2+20r^2=420r^2=4\cdot105r^2.$$ Hence $$R=\frac{20r\pm2r\sqrt{105}}2=r(10\pm\sqrt{105}).$$ Because $\sqrt{105}>10$, the minus branch is negative. The physical solution is therefore $$\boxed{R=r(10+\sqrt{105})}.$$

Difficult transition. The sign decision is the difficult transition. Both roots satisfy the algebraic quadratic, so substituting them back alone does not choose the modelled radius. The domain supplies the final criterion: a negative length is inadmissible.

Diagnosis / why the rejected route fails. Keeping both signs until the domain check prevents an unsupported choice. The rejected branch $r(10-\sqrt{105})$ is negative for positive $r$; it is not a second physical sphere. The lesson solution therefore retains only the plus branch without claiming a missing multiple-choice letter.

Final check / check back. Numerically, $10+\sqrt{105}\approx20.247$, so $R$ is positive and substantially larger than $r$, plausible because one sphere must match the combined area of ten tall cylinders. Substitution into $R^2-20rR-5r^2$ returns zero exactly.

Exam trigger / transfer trigger. Whenever a geometry model yields multiple algebraic roots, separate the algebra gate from the modelling gate: solve exactly, state the domain or sign restriction, reject invalid candidates explicitly, and then check the retained result in the original equation.
