Q10 — Dormice 3% growth; voles $N = ab^t$; year of equal populations

Question

A dormouse colony starts with 2000 individuals and grows by $3\%$ each year (1.03× per year).

(a) Find the dormouse population 6 years after the start, to 3 s.f. [2]

A vole colony in the same region is modelled by $N = ab^t$ where $t$ is years after start. Years 4 and 7 give populations $3690$ and $3470$ respectively.

(b) Find $a$ and $b$ to 2 s.f. [3]

(c) Find the year $T$ (after the start) when the two populations are equal. [3]

[8 marks]

Part (a) — dormice after 6 yrs [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Strategy: 3% growth per year ⇒ each year multiplies by $1.03$. After 6 years, the population has been multiplied by $1.03$ six times. Use the geometric $n$th-term formula.

Formula (geometric $n$th term — NOT in booklet, memorise):

$$u_n = ar^{n-1}$$

where $a$ is the first term and $r$ is the common ratio. Here $a = u_1 = 2000$ (year 1) and $r = 1.03$.

Indexing: 6 years after start = year 7, so $n = 7$. Then $n - 1 = 6$ multiplications by $r$:

$$u_7 = 2000 \cdot (1.03)^{7-1} = 2000 \cdot (1.03)^{6}$$

Compute $(1.03)^6$:

$$(1.03)^6 = 1.19405\ldots$$

$$u_7 = 2000 \times 1.19405 \approx 2388.1$$

$$\boxed{\;\text{population} \approx 2388\text{ (3 s.f.)}\;}$$

Indexing trap: "6 years after start" — if year 1 is the starting year ($n = 1$), then 6 years later is $n = 7$. Common slip: writing $u_n = ar^n$ gives the $(n+1)$-th term. The $n$-th term is $u_n = ar^{n-1}$.
$\boxed{\approx 2388}$ (3 s.f.)

Part (b) — find $a$, $b$ [3 marks] · 2 steps

Independent of (a) (different colony). Vole model $N = ab^t$ with two data points: $N(4) = ab^4 = 3690$  (1) and $N(7) = ab^7 = 3470$  (2). Divide $(2)/(1)$ to eliminate $a$ and isolate $b^3$, then back-substitute for $a$. Round each to 2 s.f.
STEP 1/2 M1

Strategy: two unknowns ($a$, $b$), two equations ($N(4) = 3690$, $N(7) = 3470$). Dividing the equations cancels $a$ instantly, leaving a single equation in $b$.

Set up the two equations from the model $N = ab^t$:

$$N(4) = ab^4 = 3690 \quad\quad(1)$$

$$N(7) = ab^7 = 3470 \quad\quad(2)$$

Divide (2) by (1) — $a$ cancels, $b^7/b^4 = b^3$:

$$\dfrac{ab^7}{ab^4} = \dfrac{3470}{3690} \;\Longrightarrow\; b^3 = 0.9404\ldots$$

Take cube root:

$$b = (0.9404)^{1/3} \approx 0.9799 \approx 0.98\ \text{(2 s.f.)}$$

Why divide, not subtract? $N = ab^t$ is multiplicative — division cancels $a$. (Subtraction would leave $a(b^7 - b^4)$ — still has $a$.) Sense check: $b < 1$ ⇒ vole population is decreasing, consistent with $3470 < 3690$.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $b = (0.9404)^{1/3} \approx 0.9799 \approx 0.98\ \text{(2 s.f.)}$
STEP 2/2 A1 A1

Strategy: with $b$ known, plug back into either of the original equations to solve for $a$. Equation (1) involves $b^4$ — easier than $b^7$.

From (1): $ab^4 = 3690$, so $a = \dfrac{3690}{b^4}$.

Compute $b^4$ with $b \approx 0.98$:

$$b^4 = (0.98)^4 \approx 0.9224$$

Then:

$$a = \dfrac{3690}{0.9224} \approx 4001 \approx 4000\ \text{(2 s.f.)}$$

$$\boxed{\;a \approx 4000,\quad b \approx 0.98\;}$$ (2 s.f.)

Sense check: $a$ is the population at $t = 0$ (since $N(0) = ab^0 = a$). At $t = 4$, population should be slightly less than $a$ — indeed $a \cdot (0.98)^4 \approx 4000 \cdot 0.92 \approx 3690$ ✓. Slip risk: using rounded $b = 0.98$ before the back-substitute introduces small errors; carry more digits in workings.
$\boxed{a \approx 4000,\ b \approx 0.98}$ (2 s.f.)

Part (c) — year of equal populations [3 marks] · 2 steps

From (a)/(b): Dormice (3% growth, start 2000): $D = 2000(1.03)^T$. Voles (from (b), $a \approx 4000$, $b \approx 0.98$): $V = 4000(0.98)^T$. Set $D = V$, isolate the exponential, take logs of both sides to solve for $T$.
STEP 1/2 M1

Strategy: set the two population formulas equal. We have two exponentials with different bases — combine them into ONE exponential equation in $T$ using $a^T / b^T = (a/b)^T$.

Set $D = V$:

$$2000 \cdot (1.03)^T = 4000 \cdot (0.98)^T$$

Divide both sides by $2000 \cdot (0.98)^T$:

$$\dfrac{(1.03)^T}{(0.98)^T} = \dfrac{4000}{2000}$$

Use the index law $\dfrac{a^T}{b^T} = \!\left(\dfrac{a}{b}\right)^{\!T}$:

$$\boxed{\;\left(\dfrac{1.03}{0.98}\right)^{\!T} = 2\;}$$

Why this rearrangement? Now we have one exponential = constant — perfect for taking logs. Sanity: $1.03/0.98 \approx 1.051 > 1$, so the LHS grows in $T$. We need it to reach $2$ — finite positive $T$ exists.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $\boxed{\;\left(\dfrac{1.03}{0.98}\right)^{\!T} = 2\;}$
STEP 2/2 M1 A1

Strategy: $\log_a x$ converts a multiplicative relation to additive. Take $\log$ of both sides to extract the unknown exponent $T$.

Formula (log-both-sides for unknown exponent):

$$a^T = b \;\Longrightarrow\; \log(a^T) = \log b \;\Longrightarrow\; T\log a = \log b \;\Longrightarrow\; T = \dfrac{\log b}{\log a}$$

Apply to our equation $(1.03/0.98)^T = 2$:

$$T \log\!\left(\dfrac{1.03}{0.98}\right) = \log 2$$

Compute the logs (base 10):

$$\log 2 \approx 0.30103, \quad \log(1.03/0.98) = \log 1.05102 \approx 0.02171$$

$$T = \dfrac{0.30103}{0.02171} \approx 13.87$$

$$\boxed{\;T \approx 13.9\text{ years}\;}$$

Phuc's slip: "I didn't know how to solve the exponential function by using log of both sides." Whenever the unknown is in the exponent, take log of both sides — works every time. Base-independent: $\log_{10}$, $\ln$, or any base gives the same ratio (since $\log_a x = \ln x / \ln a$ — the $\ln a$ factors cancel).
$\boxed{T \approx 13.9\text{ years}}$
