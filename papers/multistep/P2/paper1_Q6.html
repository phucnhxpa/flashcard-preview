Q6 — Sketch $y = a^x + 4$; trapezium rule for $\int_2^{3.5}(2^x-2x)dx$; linear combinations

Question

(a) Sketch $y = a^x + 4$ ($a > 1$), showing $y$-intercept and asymptote. [3]

(b) Using the table for $y = 2^x - 2x$ at $x = 2, 2.3, 2.6, 2.9, 3.2, 3.5$ (values $0, 0.3246, 0.8629, 1.6643, 2.7896, 4.3137$), use the trapezium rule with all these values to estimate $\int_2^{3.5}(2^x - 2x)\,dx$ to 2 d.p. [3]

(c) Hence estimate (i) $\int_2^{3.5}(2^x + 2x)\,dx$, (ii) $\int_2^{3.5}(2^{x+1} - 4x)\,dx$. [3]

[9 marks]

Part (a) — sketch [3 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 B1 B1 B1

Strategy: $y = a^x$ ($a > 1$) is the standard increasing exponential with $y$-intercept $1$ and horizontal asymptote $y = 0$. Adding $4$ shifts the WHOLE graph up by $4$ units — both the intercept and the asymptote move with it.

$y$-intercept (set $x = 0$):

$$y = a^{0} + 4 = 1 + 4 = 5$$

So the curve crosses the $y$-axis at $(0,\ 5)$.

Asymptote (look at $x \to -\infty$):

$$x \to -\infty \;\Longrightarrow\; a^x \to 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; y \to 4$$

Horizontal asymptote: $y = 4$.

Shape: curve lies above $y = 4$, hugs the asymptote on the left, and grows to $+\infty$ on the right ($a > 1$ so it's increasing).

$$\boxed{\;y\text{-int }(0, 5),\ \text{asymptote }y = 4,\ \text{increasing}\;}$$

Translation rule: $f(x) + c$ shifts the graph up by $c$. The asymptote shifts too: $a^x \to 0$ becomes $a^x + 4 \to 4$.
$(0, 5)$, asymptote $y = 4$

Part (b) — trapezium rule [3 marks] · 1 step

From (a): $y = 2^x + 4$ has $y$-intercept $(0, 5)$ and asymptote $y = 4$ (general $y = a^x$ shape, shifted up by 4). Now apply the trapezium rule to $\int_2^{3.5}(2^x - 2x)\,dx$ with 3 strips ($h = 0.5$).
STEP 1/1 B1 M1 A1

Why this trapezium spacing? With $n$ strips on $[a, b]$, the strip width is $h = (b-a)/n$, and there are $n+1$ ordinates. Here we have 6 tabulated values, so $n = 5$ strips, and $h = (3.5 - 2)/5 = 0.3$.

Ordinates (read straight from the table):

$y_0 = 0$   (at $x = 2$)

$y_1 = 0.3246$   (at $x = 2.3$)

$y_2 = 0.8629$   (at $x = 2.6$)

$y_3 = 1.6643$   (at $x = 2.9$)

$y_4 = 2.7896$   (at $x = 3.2$)

$y_5 = 4.3137$   (at $x = 3.5$)

Formula (trapezium rule, $n$ strips — IN booklet):

$$\int_a^b y\,dx \approx \tfrac{h}{2}\big[y_0 + y_n + 2(y_1 + y_2 + \ldots + y_{n-1})\big],\quad h = \tfrac{b-a}{n}$$

Sum of interior ordinates ($y_1 + y_2 + y_3 + y_4$):

$$0.3246 + 0.8629 + 1.6643 + 2.7896 = 5.6414$$

Substitute:

$$\approx \tfrac{0.3}{2}\big[\,0 + 4.3137 + 2(5.6414)\,\big] = 0.15\,[4.3137 + 11.2828] = 0.15 \times 15.5965$$

$$\boxed{\;\int_2^{3.5}(2^x - 2x)\,dx \approx 2.34\;\text{(2 d.p.)}\;}$$

Phuc's slip: "I forgot the trapezium rule" — it IS in the booklet, look it up. Pattern: endpoints ($y_0,\ y_n$) added ONCE; interior ordinates DOUBLED. Sense check: $2^x - 2x$ is convex on $[2, 3.5]$ ($f''(x) > 0$), so the trapezium rule gives an OVER-estimate — our $2.34$ is slightly too big.
$\int_2^{3.5}(2^x - 2x)\,dx \approx 2.34$ (2 d.p.)

Part (c) — linear combinations [3 marks] · 2 steps

From (b): $\int_2^{3.5}(2^x - 2x)\,dx \approx 2.34$.
STEP 1/2 M1 A1

Strategy: we already estimated $\int (2^x - 2x)$. The new integrand $2^x + 2x$ differs by $+4x$ (since $-2x \to +2x$ adds $4x$). Use $\int (f + g) = \int f + \int g$ to reuse the previous estimate, then evaluate $\int 4x$ exactly.

Algebraic identity:

$$2^x + 2x = (2^x - 2x) + 4x$$

Apply linearity of integration:

$$\int_2^{3.5}(2^x + 2x)\,dx = \underbrace{\int_2^{3.5}(2^x - 2x)\,dx}_{\approx\,2.34\ \text{from (b)}} + \int_2^{3.5} 4x\,dx$$

Evaluate $\int 4x\,dx = 2x^2 + C$ exactly:

$$\int_2^{3.5} 4x\,dx = \big[2x^2\big]_2^{3.5} = 2(3.5)^2 - 2(2)^2 = 2(12.25) - 2(4) = 24.5 - 8 = 16.5$$

Combine:

$$\boxed{\;\int_2^{3.5}(2^x + 2x)\,dx \approx 2.34 + 16.5 = 18.84\;}$$

Trick: "hence" questions reuse a previous estimate — never re-tabulate. Always look for an algebraic identity that links the new integrand to the old.
(i) $\approx 18.84$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: (i) $\approx 18.84$
STEP 2/2 A1

Strategy: simplify $2^{x+1}$ using the index law $a^{m+n} = a^m \cdot a^n$, then factor the integrand to match $(2^x - 2x)$ — the integrand from (b).

Formula (index law): $2^{x+1} = 2^x \cdot 2^1 = 2 \cdot 2^x$.

Rewrite the integrand:

$$2^{x+1} - 4x = 2 \cdot 2^x - 4x = 2(2^x - 2x)$$

Apply linearity (constants pull out of integrals):

$$\int_2^{3.5}(2^{x+1} - 4x)\,dx = 2\int_2^{3.5}(2^x - 2x)\,dx \approx 2 \times 2.34$$

$$\boxed{\;\int_2^{3.5}(2^{x+1} - 4x)\,dx \approx 4.68\;}$$

Trap: $2^{x+1} \ne (2^x)^2 = 2^{2x}$. The exponent added by 1 (multiplies by 2), not multiplied by 2. Common slip: writing $2^{x+1} = 2x + 1$ — that's mixing exponent and coefficient.
(ii) $= \boxed{4.68}$
