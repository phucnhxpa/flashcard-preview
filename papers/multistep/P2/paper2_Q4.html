Q4 — Sketch $y = a^{-x}+4$; trapezium for $\int_{-4}^{8.5}(3^{-x/3}+4)dx$; (c)(i),(c)(ii)

Question

(a) Sketch $y = a^{-x} + 4$ for $a > 1$, showing $y$-intercept and asymptote. [3]

Table for $y = 3^{-x/3} + 4$ at $x = -4, -1.5, 1, 3.5, 6, 8.5$ gives $y = 13, 6.280, 4.577, 4.146, 4.037, 4.009$.

(b) Use the trapezium rule with all six values to estimate $\int_{-4}^{8.5}\!(3^{-x/3} + 4)\,dx$ to 2 s.f. [3]

(c) Hence estimate (i) $\int_{-4}^{8.5} 3^{-x/3}\,dx$, (ii) $\int_{-8.5}^{4}(3^{x/3} - 4)\,dx$. [3]

[9 marks]

Part (a) — sketch [3 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 B1 B1 B1

Strategy: $y = a^{-x}$ is the reflection of $y = a^x$ in the $y$-axis (replacing $x$ with $-x$). For $a > 1$, $a^{-x}$ is decreasing from $\infty$ to $0$. Adding $4$ shifts everything up by $4$.

$y$-intercept (set $x = 0$):

$$y = a^{-0} + 4 = a^{0} + 4 = 1 + 4 = 5 \;\Longrightarrow\; (0,\ 5)$$

Asymptote (look at $x \to +\infty$):

$$x \to +\infty \;\Longrightarrow\; -x \to -\infty \;\Longrightarrow\; a^{-x} \to 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; y \to 4$$

Horizontal asymptote: $y = 4$.

Shape: curve lies above $y = 4$, decreasing, hugs $y = 4$ on the right and grows to $+\infty$ on the left.

$$\boxed{\;y\text{-int }(0, 5),\ \text{asymptote }y = 4,\ \text{decreasing}\;}$$

Why decreasing? The minus in $a^{-x}$ flips the standard increasing exponential. Common slip: drawing the curve crossing the asymptote — it never does, since $a^{-x} > 0$ for all $x$.
$y$-int $(0, 5)$, asymptote $y = 4$, decreasing curve

Part (b) — trapezium rule [3 marks] · 1 step

Independent of (a). Table values for $y = 3^{-x/3} + 4$: $y_0,\ldots,y_5 = 13,\ 6.280,\ 4.577,\ 4.146,\ 4.037,\ 4.009$ at $x = -4, -1.5, 1, 3.5, 6, 8.5$ (5 strips, so $h = 2.5$).
STEP 1/1 M1 M1 A1

Why this trapezium spacing? With 6 tabulated values on $[-4,\ 8.5]$, we have 5 strips, so $h = (b-a)/n = (8.5 - (-4))/5 = 12.5/5 = 2.5$.

Ordinates (read from the table):

$y_0 = 13$ (at $x = -4$),   $y_1 = 6.280$ (at $x = -1.5$),   $y_2 = 4.577$ (at $x = 1$),

$y_3 = 4.146$ (at $x = 3.5$),   $y_4 = 4.037$ (at $x = 6$),   $y_5 = 4.009$ (at $x = 8.5$).

Formula (trapezium rule, $n$ strips — IN booklet):

$$\int_a^b y\,dx \approx \tfrac{h}{2}\big[y_0 + y_n + 2(y_1 + y_2 + \ldots + y_{n-1})\big],\quad h = \tfrac{b-a}{n}$$

Sum of interior ordinates ($y_1 + y_2 + y_3 + y_4$):

$$6.280 + 4.577 + 4.146 + 4.037 = 19.040$$

Substitute:

$$\approx \tfrac{2.5}{2}\big[\,13 + 4.009 + 2(19.040)\,\big] = 1.25\,[17.009 + 38.080] = 1.25 \times 55.089$$

$$= 68.86\ldots \approx 69\text{ (2 s.f.)}$$

$$\boxed{\;\int_{-4}^{8.5}(3^{-x/3} + 4)\,dx \approx 69\;}$$

Trap: endpoints ($y_0, y_n$) are added ONCE; interior ordinates DOUBLED. Sense check: $y$ is large at $x = -4$ ($y_0 = 13$) and tails toward $4$ — the dominant contribution is from the left edge.
$\int_{-4}^{8.5}(3^{-x/3} + 4)\,dx \approx 69$ (2 s.f.)

Part (c) — derived integrals [3 marks] · 2 steps

From (b): $\int_{-4}^{8.5}(3^{-x/3}+4)\,dx \approx 69$.
STEP 1/2 M1 A1

Strategy: the new integrand $3^{-x/3}$ differs from $(3^{-x/3} + 4)$ by a constant. Use linearity of integration to split, then evaluate $\int 4\,dx$ exactly.

Algebraic identity:

$$3^{-x/3} = (3^{-x/3} + 4) - 4$$

Apply $\int (f - g) = \int f - \int g$:

$$\int_{-4}^{8.5} 3^{-x/3}\,dx = \underbrace{\int_{-4}^{8.5}(3^{-x/3} + 4)\,dx}_{\approx\,69\ \text{from (b)}} - \int_{-4}^{8.5} 4\,dx$$

Evaluate the constant-area integral exactly (rectangle of height 4, width $8.5 - (-4) = 12.5$):

$$\int_{-4}^{8.5} 4\,dx = 4 \times 12.5 = 50$$

Combine:

$$\boxed{\;\int_{-4}^{8.5} 3^{-x/3}\,dx \approx 69 - 50 = 19\;}$$

"Hence" tip: these questions reuse the previous trapezium estimate — never re-tabulate. The constant part $\int c\,dx = c(b-a)$ is exact, not estimated.
(i) $\approx 19$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: (i) $\approx 19$
STEP 2/2 A1

Strategy: the new integrand $3^{x/3} - 4$ has $3^{x/3}$, but our known data is for $3^{-x/3}$. The substitution $u = -x$ converts $3^{x/3} \to 3^{-u/3}$ and reflects the integration limits. After cleanup, the integral matches a known value.

Let $u = -x$, so $du = -dx$ (i.e. $dx = -du$).

Limits: $x = -8.5 \Rightarrow u = 8.5$;   $x = 4 \Rightarrow u = -4$.

Substitute:

$$\int_{-8.5}^{4}(3^{x/3} - 4)\,dx = \int_{8.5}^{-4}(3^{-u/3} - 4)(-du) = \int_{-4}^{8.5}(3^{-u/3} - 4)\,du$$

(Flipping limits absorbs the $-1$ from $(-du)$.)

Split using linearity:

$$= \underbrace{\int_{-4}^{8.5} 3^{-u/3}\,du}_{\approx\,19\ \text{from (c)(i)}} - \underbrace{\int_{-4}^{8.5} 4\,du}_{=\,50}$$

$$\approx 19 - 50 = -31$$

$$\boxed{\;\int_{-8.5}^{4}(3^{x/3} - 4)\,dx \approx -31\;}$$

Phuc's insight: "the integration of A and B is equal to the negative integration of P and A". General rule: $\int_{-b}^{-a} f(-x)\,dx = \int_a^b f(u)\,du$. Sense check: the integrand $3^{x/3} - 4$ is mostly negative on $[-8.5, 4]$ (since $3^{x/3} < 4$ over much of it), so a negative result is plausible.
(ii) $\approx -31$
