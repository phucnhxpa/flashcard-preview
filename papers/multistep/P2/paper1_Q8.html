Q8 — Solve $5\sin x \tan x + 13 = \cos x$; temperature $H = 10 + 12\sin(kt+18)°$

Question

(i) Solve, for $0 < x \leq \pi$, $5\sin x \tan x + 13 = \cos x$, to 3 s.f. radians. [5]

Greenhouse temperature: $H = 10 + 12\sin(kt + 18)°$, $0 \le t < 24$. Given $H = 20$ when $t = 6$, with $0 < k < 20$.

(ii)(a) Find all possible values of $k$. [4]

Given also $0 < k < 10$:

(ii)(b) State the maximum temperature. [1]

(ii)(c) Find the time of day when the maximum is reached. [2]

[12 marks]

Part (i) — solve $5\sin x \tan x + 13 = \cos x$ [5 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3 M1

Strategy: the equation mixes $\sin x$, $\cos x$, and $\tan x$. Goal: rewrite everything in $\sin$ and $\cos$ only, then use $\sin^2 + \cos^2 = 1$ to get a single-variable equation.

Formula: $\tan x = \dfrac{\sin x}{\cos x}$ (definition).

Substitute into the LHS:

$$5\sin x \tan x = 5\sin x \cdot \dfrac{\sin x}{\cos x} = \dfrac{5\sin^2 x}{\cos x}$$

So the equation becomes:

$$\dfrac{5\sin^2 x}{\cos x} + 13 = \cos x$$

Multiply EVERY term by $\cos x$ to clear the fraction (note $\cos x \ne 0$ in the given range — we'll check later):

$$\boxed{\;5\sin^2 x + 13\cos x = \cos^2 x\;}$$

Sense check: after multiplying through, every term has degree $2$ in trig (or constant × $\cos$). That hints at converting $\sin^2$ to $\cos^2$ next via Pythagoras.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $\boxed{\;5\sin^2 x + 13\cos x = \cos^2 x\;}$
STEP 2/3 M1 A1

Strategy: the equation has both $\sin^2 x$ and $\cos^2 x$ — a quadratic in disguise. Eliminate $\sin^2$ via the Pythagorean identity to get a pure quadratic in $\cos x$.

Formula (Pythagorean identity — IN booklet):

$$\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1 \;\Longrightarrow\; \sin^2 x = 1 - \cos^2 x$$

Substitute into $5\sin^2 x + 13\cos x = \cos^2 x$:

$$5(1 - \cos^2 x) + 13\cos x = \cos^2 x$$

Expand:

$$5 - 5\cos^2 x + 13\cos x = \cos^2 x$$

Move all terms to one side (add $5\cos^2 x$, subtract everything else):

$$0 = 6\cos^2 x - 13\cos x - 5$$

$$\boxed{\;6\cos^2 x - 13\cos x - 5 = 0\;}$$

Substitution trick: let $c = \cos x$ and the equation reads $6c^2 - 13c - 5 = 0$ — a standard quadratic ready for the formula or factoring.
$6\cos^2 x - 13\cos x - 5 = 0$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 2: $6\cos^2 x - 13\cos x - 5 = 0$
STEP 3/3 M1 A1

Strategy: apply the quadratic formula to $6c^2 - 13c - 5 = 0$ (where $c = \cos x$). Then filter solutions: $\cos x$ is bounded, $|\cos x| \le 1$. Finally take $\cos^{-1}$ in the given range.

Formula (quadratic): $c = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$ with $a = 6$, $b = -13$, $c_{\!const} = -5$.

Discriminant:

$$\Delta = (-13)^2 - 4(6)(-5) = 169 + 120 = 289 = 17^2$$

Solve:

$$\cos x = \dfrac{13 \pm 17}{12} \;\Longrightarrow\; \cos x = \dfrac{30}{12} = \tfrac{5}{2} \;\text{ or }\; \cos x = \dfrac{-4}{12} = -\tfrac{1}{3}$$

Filter: $\cos x = \tfrac{5}{2} > 1$ is impossible — reject. Keep $\cos x = -\tfrac{1}{3}$.

Take $\cos^{-1}$ in $(0, \pi]$. Since $\cos x < 0$, $x$ lies in Q2 (between $\pi/2$ and $\pi$):

$$x = \cos^{-1}\!\left(-\tfrac{1}{3}\right) = 1.9106\ldots$$

$$\boxed{\;x \approx 1.91\text{ rad (3 s.f.)}\;}$$

Phuc's slip: crossed out the correct answer. $\cos^{-1}(-\tfrac{1}{3})$ IS positive — calculator returns a value in $[0, \pi]$ for negative inputs (Q2). Trap: $|\cos x| \le 1$ ALWAYS — any candidate outside is automatically rejected.
$\boxed{x \approx 1.91\text{ rad}}$ (3 s.f.)

Part (ii)(a) — find $k$ [4 marks] · 3 steps

Equation: $H = 10 + 12\sin(kt + 18)°$, with $H = 20$ at $t = 6$, $0 < k < 20$.
STEP 1/3 M1

Strategy: a single equation $H = 20$ at $t = 6$ should give a single equation in $k$. Substitute and isolate $\sin(\cdot)$.

Sub $H = 20$, $t = 6$ into $H = 10 + 12\sin(kt + 18)°$:

$$20 = 10 + 12\sin(6k + 18)°$$

Subtract $10$ from both sides, then divide by $12$:

$$12\sin(6k + 18)° = 10 \;\Longrightarrow\; \sin(6k + 18)° = \dfrac{10}{12} = \dfrac{5}{6}$$

$$\boxed{\;\sin(6k + 18)° = \tfrac{5}{6}\;}$$

Radians vs degrees: the $°$ symbol INSIDE the bracket is the tell — work in degrees throughout. Sense check: $\tfrac{5}{6} < 1$ ✓ — a valid sine value, so solutions exist.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $\boxed{\;\sin(6k + 18)° = \tfrac{5}{6}\;}$
STEP 2/3 M1 A1

Strategy: $\sin\theta = c$ has TWO solutions in $[0°, 180°]$: the principal value $\theta_0 = \sin^{-1}(c)$ and its supplement $180° - \theta_0$. Find both, then unwind to $k$.

Principal value:

$$\sin^{-1}\!\left(\tfrac{5}{6}\right) = 56.44°\ldots$$

Symmetry of sine ($\sin\theta = \sin(180° - \theta)$):

$$180° - 56.44° = 123.56°$$

So $6k + 18 = 56.44°$  or  $6k + 18 = 123.56°$.

Branch 1: $6k + 18 = 56.44 \Rightarrow 6k = 38.44 \Rightarrow k = 6.41$.

Branch 2: $6k + 18 = 123.56 \Rightarrow 6k = 105.56 \Rightarrow k = 17.59$.

Why two solutions? $\sin$ is symmetric about $\theta = 90°$ in the first half-period. Same height ($\tfrac{5}{6}$) is achieved on both sides. Larger period? Adding $360°$ would give $k = 6.41 + 60 = 66.4$ etc., but those exceed $0 < k < 20$.
$k = 6.41$ or $k = 17.59$ (both in $0 < k < 20$)
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 2: $k = 6.41$ or $k = 17.59$ (both in $0 < k < 20$)
STEP 3/3 A1

Strategy: the question restricts $0 < k < 20$. Verify each candidate satisfies this.

Test $k = 6.41$: $0 < 6.41 < 20$ ✓ — accept.

Test $k = 17.59$: $0 < 17.59 < 20$ ✓ — accept.

Both candidates lie in the range — keep both.

$$\boxed{\;k \in \{6.41,\ 17.59\}\;}$$

Why both? The question asks for "all possible values". Without further info (like the time of max), we can't pick one. Part (ii)(c) later narrows to $k < 10$ to single out $k = 6.41$.
$\boxed{k \in \{6.41,\ 17.59\}}$

Part (ii)(b) — maximum $H$ [1 marks] · 1 step

From (ii)(a): $H = 10 + 12\sin(kt + 18)°$ with $k \in \{6.41,\ 17.59\}$. Max occurs when $\sin(\cdot) = +1$ — its value doesn't depend on $k$ or $t$.
STEP 1/1 B1

Strategy: the model is $H = 10 + 12\sin(\cdot)°$. Since $\sin$ ranges over $[-1, 1]$, the term $12\sin(\cdot)$ ranges over $[-12, 12]$. Max $H$ occurs when $\sin(\cdot) = +1$.

Range of sine: $-1 \le \sin\theta \le 1$ for all $\theta$.

Maximise by setting $\sin(kt + 18)° = 1$:

$$H_{\max} = 10 + 12 \cdot 1 = 22$$

$$\boxed{\;H_{\max} = 22°\text{C}\;}$$

Note: the maximum value doesn't depend on $k$ — that just controls WHEN the max happens, not the height. Symmetric: $H_{\min} = 10 - 12 = -2°\text{C}$ (a chilly greenhouse!).
$\boxed{H_{\max} = 22°\text{C}}$

Part (ii)(c) — time of maximum (with $0[2 marks] · 1 step

From (ii)(a)/(ii)(b): with the extra restriction $0 < k < 10$, take $k = 6.41$ (reject $17.59$). $H$ is max when $\sin(kt + 18)° = 1$, i.e. when $kt + 18 = 90°$. Solve for $t$ and convert to a time of day.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Strategy: $\sin\theta = 1$ first occurs at $\theta = 90°$. Set $kt + 18 = 90$, plug $k = 6.41$ (only allowed value with $0 < k < 10$), and solve for $t$. Then convert decimal hours to a clock time.

With $k = 6.41$:

$$6.41\,t + 18 = 90$$

Subtract $18$:

$$6.41\,t = 72$$

Divide by $6.41$:

$$t = \dfrac{72}{6.41} \approx 11.23\text{ hours}$$

Convert to clock time: $0.23 \times 60 \approx 14$ minutes, so:

$$t \approx 11\text{ h }14\text{ min} \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{11:14 am}$$

$$\boxed{\;t \approx 11.23\text{ h (≈ 11:14 am)}\;}$$

Why $k = 6.41$, not $17.59$? The extra restriction $0 < k < 10$ forces the smaller value. Trap: the next sine maximum is at $\theta = 90° + 360° = 450°$, giving $t = (450 - 18)/6.41 \approx 67.4$ h — outside $0 \le t < 24$, so $11.23$ h is the only max in the day.
$\boxed{t \approx 11.23\text{ h (around 11:14 am)}}$
