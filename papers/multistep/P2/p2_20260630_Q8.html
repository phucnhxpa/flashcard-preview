P2 Q9 · Proof — counter-example (irrational product) + deduction (odd cubes)

Question

(a) Disprove by counter-example the statement: “if $x, y$ are irrational and $x \ne y$, then $xy$ is irrational.” [3]

(b) Prove, by deduction, that if $n$ is an odd integer, then $n^{3}$ is odd, and hence that $3n^{3}$ is odd. [4]

[7 marks]

Part (a) — Disproof by counter-example [3 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2choose a pair that forces a rational product M1

Strategy. To disprove “for all irrational $x, y$ with $x \ne y$, $xy$ is irrational”, find ONE explicit pair $(x_0, y_0)$ with $x_0, y_0$ irrational, $x_0 \ne y_0$, but $x_0 y_0$ rational. Rescale one factor by a rational multiple so the product collapses to a rational.

Choose the pair

$$x = \sqrt{2}, \qquad y = 3\sqrt{2}.$$

Both are irrational; $x \ne y$ (since $3\sqrt{2} \ne \sqrt{2}$).

Phuc's mistake. Chose $x = \sqrt{2},\, y = \sqrt{5}$, but $xy = \sqrt{10}$, which is still irrational — this does NOT disprove the claim. You must rescale one factor by a rational multiple to force a rational product.
↳ from STEP 1: $x = \sqrt{2},\; y = 3\sqrt{2}$
STEP 2/2compute the product A1

Strategy. Multiply and observe the product is a rational integer.

Product

$$xy = \sqrt{2} \cdot 3\sqrt{2} = 3 \cdot (\sqrt{2})^{2} = 3 \cdot 2 = 6.$$

$6 \in \mathbb{Q}$ (rational), yet $x, y$ are irrational and $x \ne y$.

Conclusion

$$\boxed{\text{The statement is FALSE. Counter-example: } x = \sqrt{2},\, y = 3\sqrt{2},\, xy = 6 \in \mathbb{Q}.}$$

Sense check. A counter-example needs all three: (i) $x$ irrational, (ii) $y$ irrational, (iii) $x \ne y$, AND (iv) $xy \in \mathbb{Q}$. Our pair satisfies all four.

Part (b) — Proof by deduction: odd cubes [4 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3write n canonically as 2k+1 M1

Strategy. For “if $n$ is odd, then $n^{3}$ is odd”, write $n$ in its canonical odd form. An odd integer is any $n = 2k + 1$ for some $k \in \mathbb{Z}$.

Canonical form

$$\text{Let } n = 2k + 1, \quad k \in \mathbb{Z}.$$
Phuc's mistake 1. Wrote $n = 2n + 1$ — a letter clash ($n$ on both sides). Always use a fresh letter: $n = 2k + 1$ with $k \in \mathbb{Z}$.
↳ from STEP 1: $n = 2k + 1$
STEP 2/3cube and expand M1

Strategy. Cube the binomial using $(a+b)^{3} = a^{3} + 3a^{2}b + 3ab^{2} + b^{3}$.

Cube $2k + 1$

$$n^{3} = (2k + 1)^{3} = 8k^{3} + 12k^{2} + 6k + 1.$$
Phuc's mistake 2. Squared instead of cubed. Re-read the target expression: the claim is about $n^{3}$, not $n^{2}$.
↳ from STEP 2: $n^{3} = 8k^{3} + 12k^{2} + 6k + 1$
STEP 3/3factor out 2 + 1 to show odd A1

Strategy. An integer is odd iff it can be written as $2m + 1$ for some $m \in \mathbb{Z}$. Factor $2$ out of the first three terms.

Factor as 2m + 1

$$8k^{3} + 12k^{2} + 6k + 1 = 2(4k^{3} + 6k^{2} + 3k) + 1.$$

Set $m = 4k^{3} + 6k^{2} + 3k \in \mathbb{Z}$ (since $k \in \mathbb{Z}$). Then $n^{3} = 2m + 1$, which is odd.

Conclusion

$$\boxed{n \text{ odd} \Rightarrow n^{3} \text{ odd} \Rightarrow 3n^{3} \text{ odd}.} \quad \blacksquare$$

Sense check. $n = 3$: $n^{3} = 27$ (odd), $3n^{3} = 81$ (odd) ✓. $n = 5$: $n^{3} = 125$ (odd), $3n^{3} = 375$ (odd) ✓.
