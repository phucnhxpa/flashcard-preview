Q5 — $\sum_{r=1}^{\infty}6\!\cdot\!(0.25)^r$; periodic recurrence $u_{n+1} = (u_n-3)/(u_n-2)$

Question

(i) Find $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^{\infty} 6 \cdot (0.25)^r$. [3]

$u_1 = 3$ and $u_{n+1} = \dfrac{u_n - 3}{u_n - 2}$.

(ii)(a) Show the sequence is periodic. [2] (b) State its order. [1] (c) Find $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{70} u_n$. [2]

[8 marks]

Part (i) — sum to infinity [3 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 M1 A1

Strategy: the sum starts at $r = 1$ (not $r = 0$), so the first term is $6 \cdot 0.25^1 = 1.5$ — NOT $6 \cdot 0.25^0 = 6$. Identify $a$ and the common ratio $r$, check convergence ($|r| < 1$), then apply the $S_\infty$ formula.

Identify first term ($r = 1$):

$$a = u_1 = 6 \cdot (0.25)^1 = 6 \cdot 0.25 = 1.5$$

Common ratio: term $r$ has $(0.25)^r$; term $r+1$ has $(0.25)^{r+1} = 0.25 \cdot (0.25)^r$. So each term multiplies by $0.25$:

$$r_{\text{ratio}} = 0.25$$

Convergence: $|r| = 0.25 < 1$ ✓ so the series converges.

Formula (geometric sum to infinity — IN booklet):

$$S_\infty = \dfrac{a}{1 - r}, \quad |r| < 1$$

Substitute $a = 1.5$, $r = 0.25$:

$$S_\infty = \dfrac{1.5}{1 - 0.25} = \dfrac{1.5}{0.75} = 2$$

$$\boxed{\;S_\infty = 2\;}$$

Sum-index trap: $\sum_{r=1}^{\infty}$ starts at $r = 1$, not $r = 0$. Always check the lower index — many slips come from using $a = 6$ instead of $1.5$. Sense check: sum of $1.5 + 0.375 + 0.094 + \ldots$ — terms shrink fast, $S_\infty = 2$ looks right.
$\boxed{S_\infty = 2}$

Part (ii)(a) — show periodic [2 marks] · 1 step

Independent of (i). Recurrence: $u_1 = 3$, $u_{n+1} = \dfrac{u_n - 3}{u_n - 2}$. To prove periodicity, iterate until some $u_{k+1} = u_1$ — that exhibits the repeating cycle.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Strategy: a sequence is periodic if $u_{n+k} = u_n$ for some fixed $k$ and ALL $n$. Iterate the recurrence until we see $u_1$ reappear — that demonstrates the cycle.

Iterate from $u_1 = 3$:

$$u_2 = \dfrac{u_1 - 3}{u_1 - 2} = \dfrac{3 - 3}{3 - 2} = \dfrac{0}{1} = 0$$

$$u_3 = \dfrac{u_2 - 3}{u_2 - 2} = \dfrac{0 - 3}{0 - 2} = \dfrac{-3}{-2} = \tfrac{3}{2} = 1.5$$

$$u_4 = \dfrac{u_3 - 3}{u_3 - 2} = \dfrac{1.5 - 3}{1.5 - 2} = \dfrac{-1.5}{-0.5} = 3$$

$u_4 = 3 = u_1$, so the recurrence will produce the same sequence from here on. The cycle $\{3,\ 0,\ 1.5\}$ repeats indefinitely.

$$\boxed{\;\text{Periodic sequence: }\{3,\ 0,\ 1.5,\ 3,\ 0,\ 1.5,\ \ldots\}\;}\quad \blacksquare$$

Sign trap: tutor first wrote $\tfrac{-1.5}{0.5} = -3$ — WRONG! Denominator is $1.5 - 2 = -0.5$, so $\tfrac{-1.5}{-0.5} = +3$. Always track BOTH signs in fractions. Cycle structure: once a sequence returns to a previously visited value, the deterministic recurrence locks into the loop.

Part (ii)(b) — order [1 marks] · 1 step

From (ii)(a): the sequence cycles through $\{3,\ 0,\ 1.5\}$ with $u_4 = u_1$. The order = length of one full cycle.
STEP 1/1 B1

Definition: the order (or period) of a periodic sequence is the smallest $k$ such that $u_{n+k} = u_n$ for all $n$ — i.e. the length of one full cycle.

From (ii)(a), the cycle is $\{3,\ 0,\ 1.5\}$ — three distinct values before repetition.

$$\boxed{\;\text{order} = 3\;}$$

Why "smallest"? Any sequence with period $k$ also "repeats" at $2k, 3k, \ldots$ — but order = the smallest such $k$. Verification: $u_4 = u_1$ ⇒ shift by $3$ recovers $u_1$ ⇒ order $\le 3$. And no smaller cycle exists since $u_2 \ne u_1$, $u_3 \ne u_1$.
$\boxed{\text{order} = 3}$

Part (ii)(c) — sum of first 70 terms [2 marks] · 1 step

From (ii)(a)/(ii)(b): cycle $\{3,\ 0,\ 1.5\}$, order $= 3$, cycle sum $= 4.5$. Decompose $70 = 23 \cdot 3 + 1$ → 23 full cycles plus $u_{70} = u_1 = 3$.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Strategy: for a periodic sequence, $\sum_{n=1}^{N} u_n = (\text{# full cycles}) \times (\text{cycle sum}) + (\text{leftover terms})$. Decompose $N$ into full cycles plus a remainder.

Cycle sum (one period of $\{3, 0, 1.5\}$):

$$3 + 0 + 1.5 = 4.5$$

Decompose 70 (divide by order $= 3$):

$$70 = 23 \times 3 + 1 \quad\text{(23 full cycles + 1 leftover term)}$$

Identify the leftover term $u_{70}$. Since $70 \equiv 1 \pmod 3$ (the leftover is term 1 of a new cycle):

$$u_{70} = u_1 = 3$$

Sum:

$$\sum_{n=1}^{70} u_n = 23 \times 4.5 + 3 = 103.5 + 3$$

$$\boxed{\;\sum_{n=1}^{70} u_n = 106.5\;}$$

Indexing care: $70 \div 3 = 23$ remainder $1$. The "remainder $1$" means we go ONE term past 23 complete cycles, landing on $u_{1}$ of the next cycle. Common slip: using $u_n = S_n$ — distinct! $u_n$ is the $n$th term, $S_n$ is the cumulative sum of the first $n$ terms.
$\boxed{106.5}$
