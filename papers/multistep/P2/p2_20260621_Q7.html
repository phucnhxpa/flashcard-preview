Q7 · ESAT — how many real roots does $x^{3}+x+1$ have?

Question

How many real roots does $f(x) = x^{3} + x + 1$ have?

A. zero   B. exactly one   C. two distinct   D. three distinct   E. infinitely many

[1 mark]

Solution [1 mark] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3split linear and quadratic M1

Strategy. $x^{3}+x+1$ has no $x^{2}$ term and does not factorise over $\mathbb{Q}$. Use the calculus derivative test: show $f'(x) > 0$ everywhere.

Differentiate

$$f(x) = x^{3} + x + 1 \;\Rightarrow\; f'(x) = 3x^{2} + 1.$$
↳ from STEP 1: $f'(x)=3x^{2}+1$
STEP 2/3sign of derivative A1

Strategy. $x^{2} \ge 0$ so $3x^{2}+1 \ge 1 > 0$ for all real $x$. Strictly increasing ⇒ at most one crossing.

Always positive

$$3x^{2} + 1 \ge 1 > 0 \quad \forall x \in \mathbb{R}.$$
↳ from STEP 2: $f'(x)>0$
STEP 3/3IVT conclusion A1

Strategy. $f(-\infty)\to-\infty$, $f(+\infty)\to+\infty$; by IVT at least one root. Combined with “at most one” ⇒ exactly one. ESAT option = “exactly one real root”.

Conclusion

$$\boxed{\text{exactly one real root.}}$$

Sense check. $f(-1)=-1<0$, $f(0)=1>0$ ⇒ root in $(-1, 0)$ ✓

Worked diagram

Same existence + uniqueness argument — the graph settles the multiple-choice answer.

Existence — sign change on $[-1,0]$ ($f(-1)=-1<0<1=f(0)$) gives a root by the IVT.

Uniqueness — $f'(x)=3x^2+1\ge 1>0$, strictly increasing $\Rightarrow$ at most one root. So exactly one real root — answer B.
