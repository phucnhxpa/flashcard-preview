Sequences & Series — Proof with Integer Constraints

Question

Given that \(a\), \(b\) and \(c\) are positive integers such that

$$a = 8 - 2b$$

$$c = b + 2$$

prove that the product \(abc\) is always an even number. [4]

[4 marks]

Proof — abc is always even [4 marks] · 4 steps

$a = 8 - 2b$, $c = b + 2$, where $a, b, c$ are positive integers
STEP 1/4 M1

Find valid range for $b$

Since $a$ must be a positive integer:

$$a > 0 \implies 8 - 2b > 0 \implies b < 4$$

Since $b$ is a positive integer: $b \geq 1$

$$\therefore b \in \{1, 2, 3\}$$

Pitfall: Forgetting to establish the constraint from $a > 0$ — this limits $b$ to only three values.
$\boxed{b \in \{1, 2, 3\}}$
Carry-in from previous step$b \in \{1, 2, 3\}$
STEP 2/4 M1

Substitute and factor $abc$

$$abc = (8 - 2b)(b)(b + 2)$$

Factor out 2 from the first bracket:

$$abc = 2(4 - b) \cdot b \cdot (b + 2)$$

$$abc = 2k \text{ where } k = (4-b)(b)(b+2)$$

Pitfall: Not recognising that $8 - 2b = 2(4-b)$ provides the factor of 2 needed to prove evenness.
$\boxed{abc = 2(4-b)(b)(b+2)}$
Carry-in from previous step$abc = 2(4-b)(b)(b+2)$, $b \in \{1, 2, 3\}$
STEP 3/4 A1

Verify by exhaustion (alternative method)

$$b = 1: a = 6, c = 3 \implies abc = 6 \times 1 \times 3 = 18 \text{ (even)}$$

$$b = 2: a = 4, c = 4 \implies abc = 4 \times 2 \times 4 = 32 \text{ (even)}$$

$$b = 3: a = 2, c = 5 \implies abc = 2 \times 3 \times 5 = 30 \text{ (even)}$$

Pitfall: When using proof by exhaustion, you must check ALL valid cases — missing one invalidates the proof.
$\boxed{\text{All cases give even products}}$
Carry-in from previous step$abc = 2k$ where $k$ is an integer
STEP 4/4 A1

State conclusion

Since $abc = 2(4-b)(b)(b+2)$ and $(4-b)$, $b$, $(b+2)$ are all integers for $b \in \{1,2,3\}$:

$$abc = 2k \text{ where } k \in \mathbb{Z}$$

By definition, a number of the form $2k$ (where $k$ is an integer) is even.

Pitfall: Must explicitly state the conclusion — showing $abc = 2k$ without stating 'therefore even' loses the final mark.
$\boxed{\text{abc is always even } \square}$
