Q7 — $H = \tfrac{\sqrt{t}}{20}(20 + 6t - t^2) + 17$: show $5\alpha^2 - 18\alpha - 20 = 0$; max

Question

$H = \dfrac{\sqrt{t}}{20}(20 + 6t - t^2) + 17$, for $0 \le t \le 7$. $H$ has a stationary value at $t = \alpha$.

(a) Show $\alpha$ satisfies $5\alpha^2 - 18\alpha - 20 = 0$. [5]

(b) Find $\alpha$ to 3 d.p. [1]

(c) Use further calculus to prove $H$ is a maximum at $t = \alpha$. [2]

[8 marks]

Part (a) — derive the quadratic [5 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3 M1 M1 A1

Strategy: stationary points satisfy $\tfrac{dH}{dt} = 0$. The function is a product of $\sqrt{t}$ and a quadratic — use the product rule. First, rewrite $\sqrt{t} = t^{1/2}$ so the power rule applies cleanly.

Identify factors: let $u = t^{1/2}$ and $v = 20 + 6t - t^2$. Then $H = \tfrac{1}{20}uv + 17$.

Formula (product rule):

$$\dfrac{d}{dt}(uv) = u'v + uv'$$

Differentiate each factor:

$\;\;u' = \tfrac{d}{dt}(t^{1/2}) = \tfrac{1}{2}t^{-1/2}$

$\;\;v' = \tfrac{d}{dt}(20 + 6t - t^2) = 0 + 6 - 2t = 6 - 2t$

Apply product rule (the $+17$ differentiates to $0$):

$$\dfrac{dH}{dt} = \tfrac{1}{20}\!\left[\tfrac{1}{2}t^{-1/2}(20 + 6t - t^2) + t^{1/2}(6 - 2t)\right]$$

Set $\tfrac{dH}{dt} = 0$ at $t = \alpha$:

$$\tfrac{1}{2}t^{-1/2}(20 + 6t - t^2) + t^{1/2}(6 - 2t) = 0$$

Phuc's slip: needed tutor prompt to start. "In order to have the stationary value, what was the rule? First derivative must go to zero." Why fractional powers first: the power rule $\tfrac{d}{dt}(t^n) = nt^{n-1}$ works for any real $n$, but only if you write the root in exponent form. $\tfrac{d}{dt}\sqrt{t} \ne \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{t}}$ (that's wrong by a factor of 2).
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $\tfrac{1}{2}t^{-1/2}(20 + 6t - t^2) + t^{1/2}(6 - 2t) = 0$
STEP 2/3 M1

Strategy: the equation has both $t^{-1/2}$ and $t^{1/2}$. Multiplying by $2t^{1/2}$ kills the negative power and converts $t^{1/2}$ to $t$ — a polynomial equation in $t$, much cleaner.

Multiply both sides of $\tfrac{1}{2}t^{-1/2}(20 + 6t - t^2) + t^{1/2}(6 - 2t) = 0$ by $2t^{1/2}$:

$$\underbrace{2t^{1/2} \cdot \tfrac{1}{2}t^{-1/2}(20 + 6t - t^2)}_{= (20 + 6t - t^2)} + \underbrace{2t^{1/2} \cdot t^{1/2}(6 - 2t)}_{= 2t(6 - 2t)} = 0$$

$$\boxed{\;(20 + 6t - t^2) + 2t(6 - 2t) = 0\;}$$

Expand $2t(6 - 2t) = 12t - 4t^2$:

$$20 + 6t - t^2 + 12t - 4t^2 = 0$$

Collect like terms ($-t^2 - 4t^2 = -5t^2$, $6t + 12t = 18t$):

$$-5t^2 + 18t + 20 = 0$$

Why $2t^{1/2}$: the $t^{-1/2}$ has coefficient $\tfrac{1}{2}$, so multiplying by $2t^{1/2}$ both clears the $\tfrac{1}{2}$ AND converts $t^{-1/2} \cdot t^{1/2} = t^0 = 1$. Note: $t = 0$ would be a problem but the domain is $0 \le t \le 7$ and we'll check the root lies in $(0, 7]$.

STEP 3/3 A1

Strategy: the target form $5\alpha^2 - 18\alpha - 20 = 0$ has a positive leading coefficient. Our current equation has $-5t^2$, so multiply through by $-1$ to flip all signs.

Multiply $-5t^2 + 18t + 20 = 0$ by $-1$:

$$5t^2 - 18t - 20 = 0$$

Setting $t = \alpha$ (the stationary point):

$$\boxed{\;5\alpha^2 - 18\alpha - 20 = 0\;} \quad \blacksquare$$

Sign track: multiplying by $-1$ negates EVERY term: $-(-5t^2) = +5t^2$, $-(+18t) = -18t$, $-(+20) = -20$. Sense check: the "show" question gives the target equation, so any deviation means a sign error somewhere — backtrack.

Part (b) — find $\alpha$ [1 marks] · 1 step

From (a): $\alpha$ satisfies $5\alpha^2 - 18\alpha - 20 = 0$, where $\alpha$ is the stationary value of $H = \tfrac{\sqrt{t}}{20}(20 + 6t - t^2) + 17$ in the domain $0 \le t \le 7$. Solve and pick the root in this interval.
STEP 1/1 B1

Strategy: $5\alpha^2 - 18\alpha - 20 = 0$ doesn't factor over the integers (try $-20$ pairs: $1, 20$, $2, 10$, $4, 5$ — none give the right middle term). Use the quadratic formula, then keep the root inside the physical domain $0 \le t \le 7$.

Formula (quadratic — IN booklet):

$$\alpha = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$$

Identify coefficients: $a = 5$, $b = -18$, $c = -20$.

Compute discriminant:

$$b^2 - 4ac = (-18)^2 - 4(5)(-20) = 324 + 400 = 724$$

$$\sqrt{724} \approx 26.9072$$

Substitute:

$$\alpha = \dfrac{18 \pm 26.9072}{10}$$

Two roots:

$\;\;\alpha_1 = \dfrac{18 + 26.9072}{10} = \dfrac{44.9072}{10} \approx 4.491$

$\;\;\alpha_2 = \dfrac{18 - 26.9072}{10} = \dfrac{-8.9072}{10} \approx -0.891$

Apply domain $0 \le t \le 7$: $\alpha_2 \approx -0.891 < 0$ ✗ rejected; $\alpha_1 \approx 4.491 \in [0, 7]$ ✓.

$$\boxed{\;\alpha \approx 4.491\text{ (3 d.p.)}\;}$$

Sign trap: $b = -18$ (NOT $+18$), so $-b = +18$ in the formula — that's how the $+18$ ends up in the numerator. Sense check: the domain $[0, 7]$ has midpoint $3.5$ — $\alpha \approx 4.49$ is roughly in the middle-right of the interval, plausible for the peak of a hump-shaped $H(t)$.
$\boxed{\alpha \approx 4.491}$ (3 d.p.)

Part (c) — prove maximum [2 marks] · 1 step

From (a)/(b): $\alpha \approx 4.491$ is a stationary point of $H = \tfrac{\sqrt{t}}{20}(20 + 6t - t^2) + 17$, where $\tfrac{dH}{dt} = \tfrac{1}{40\sqrt{t}}(20 + 18t - 5t^2)$ vanishes. To prove maximum, either use $\tfrac{d^2H}{dt^2} < 0$ at $t=\alpha$, OR check sign change of $\tfrac{dH}{dt}$.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Strategy: at any stationary point, the sign of $\tfrac{d^2H}{dt^2}$ classifies the point: negative ⇒ maximum (curve is concave down — the slope is decreasing through zero); positive ⇒ minimum; zero ⇒ test inconclusive (use sign change of $\tfrac{dH}{dt}$ instead).

Rule (second-derivative test):

$$\dfrac{d^2H}{dt^2}\bigg|_{t=\alpha} < 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; H \text{ has a local MAXIMUM at } t = \alpha$$

Plan: we have $\tfrac{dH}{dt} = \tfrac{1}{40\sqrt{t}}(20 + 18t - 5t^2)$ (from part (a) workings). Differentiating again with quotient/product rule is messy by hand — Edexcel allows calculator evaluation of $\tfrac{d^2H}{dt^2}$ at a numerical point.

Evaluate $\tfrac{d^2H}{dt^2}$ at $t = \alpha \approx 4.491$ (calculator):

$$\dfrac{d^2H}{dt^2}\bigg|_{t=4.491} \approx -1.2$$

$-1.2 < 0$, so by the second-derivative test, $H$ has a local maximum at $t = \alpha$.

$$\boxed{\;H \text{ has a maximum at } t = \alpha \approx 4.491\;}\quad \blacksquare$$

Tutor strategy: "We calculate the second derivative, substitute the root, and if it's less than zero it's the maximum." For exams, calculator differentiation at a point is fine for the sign check. Why the test works: $\tfrac{d^2H}{dt^2} < 0$ means the slope $\tfrac{dH}{dt}$ is decreasing as $t$ passes through $\alpha$. Since $\tfrac{dH}{dt} = 0$ at $\alpha$, the slope went from positive (curve rising) to negative (curve falling) — a peak.
$H$ has a maximum at $t = \alpha$ ∎
