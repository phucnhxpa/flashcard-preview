Q5 · Proof by inequality and counter-example

Total 6 marksQuestion 5. In this question you must show detailed reasoning.
(a) Given that \(x\) and \(y\) are positive numbers such that \[(x-y)^{3}>x^{3}-y^{3}\] prove that \(y>x\).  (4)
(b) Using a counter example, show that the result in part (a) is not true for all real numbers.  (2)

Part (a) · Prove \(y>x\) from \((x-y)^{3}>x^{3}-y^{3}\)[4] · worked in lesson (reasoning fix)
Hide working
Strategy
Expand \((x-y)^{3}\), subtract \(x^{3}-y^{3}\) from both sides, factor, and divide by \(3xy\). State why the inequality direction is preserved.
Formula · cubic expansion
\[ (x-y)^{3}=x^{3}-3x^{2}y+3xy^{2}-y^{3} \]
Working · substitute and simplify
\[ x^{3}-3x^{2}y+3xy^{2}-y^{3}>x^{3}-y^{3} \;\Rightarrow\; -3x^{2}y+3xy^{2}>0 \]
Working · factor
\[ 3xy(y-x)>0 \]
Working · divide (with justification)
As \(x\) and \(y\) are positive numbers, \(xy>0\), so dividing by \(3xy\) does not reverse the inequality:\[ y-x>0 \;\Rightarrow\; y>x \]
Result
\( y>x \)
Sense check
The "detailed reasoning" command requires the explicit justification that \(xy>0\) before dividing. This is the line the MS looks for.
Phuc's slip (recorded) — the missing line: Phuc's algebra was correct, but he did not state why dividing by \(xy\) keeps the inequality direction. Harry wrote in the sentence "As \(x\) and \(y\) are positive numbers…" so \(xy>0\). His rule: when you divide an inequality by a variable, always state its sign; "if in doubt, write it down."

Strategy (MS reference)
Choose a positive \(x\) and a negative \(y\) so that \((x-y)^{3}>x^{3}-y^{3}\) holds but \(y>x\) is false.
Working · choose \(x=3,\ y=-1\)
\[ (3-(-1))^{3}=4^{3}=64,\qquad 3^{3}-(-1)^{3}=27+1=28 \]\[ 64>28 \quad\text{(inequality holds)} \]But \(y=-1\) and \(x=3\), so \(y<x\) — the conclusion \(y>x\) fails.
Result
Counter-example: \(x=3,\ y=-1\). The inequality holds but \(y=-1\not>x=3\), so the result is not true for all real numbers.
Sense check
The MS accepts any positive \(x\) and negative \(y\). Substitution into both sides of the inequality is the minimum for the M mark.
