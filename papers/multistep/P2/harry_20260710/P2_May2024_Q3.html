Q3 · Laws of logarithms

Total 6 marksQuestion 3. In this question you must show all stages of your working. Solutions relying entirely on calculator technology are not acceptable.
(i) Using the laws of logarithms, solve \[2\log_{2}(2-x)=4+\log_{2}(x+10)\]  (5)
(ii) Find the value of \[\log_{\sqrt{a}}\!\big(a^{6}\big)\] where \(a\) is a positive constant greater than 1.  (1)
Source: Pearson Edexcel IAL WMA12/01, 14 May 2024, Question 3 (verbatim). The QP text extraction rendered the Q3(ii) log base imperfectly; the mark scheme answer \(=12\) confirms base \(\sqrt{a}\).
Part (i) · Solve \(2\log_{2}(2-x)=4+\log_{2}(x+10)\)[5] · worked in lesson (praised)
Hide working
Strategy
Apply log laws: \(p\log_{b}X=\log_{b}X^{p}\) on the left, and \(c=\log_{b}b^{c}\) on the constant 4. Then combine and remove logs.
Working · apply log laws
\[ 2\log_{2}(2-x)=\log_{2}(2-x)^{2},\qquad 4=\log_{2}16 \]
Working · combine
\[ \log_{2}(2-x)^{2}=\log_{2}16+\log_{2}(x+10)=\log_{2}\!\big(16(x+10)\big) \]
Working · remove logs and solve
\[ (2-x)^{2}=16(x+10) \;\Rightarrow\; x^{2}-4x+4=16x+160 \;\Rightarrow\; x^{2}-20x-156=0 \]\[ (x+6)(x-26)=0 \;\Rightarrow\; x=-6 \text{ or } x=26 \]
Working · reject the invalid root
\(x=26\) requires \(2-x=-24<0\), so \(\log_{2}(2-x)\) is undefined. Reject \(x=26\).
Result
\( x=-6 \)
Sense check
At \(x=-6\): \(2-x=8>0\) and \(x+10=4>0\); both log arguments are positive, so the solution is valid.
Harry's feedback (recorded): Harry praised the work, especially the clear justification for discarding the extraneous root. He noted this type had been tricky before.
Wording corrected from video ledger: The Gemini ledger mis-stated Q3 as "\(\log_{x}\) forms" and "\(\log_{2}(x^{2})\)". The official QP/MS wording is \(2\log_{2}(2-x)=4+\log_{2}(x+10)\) and \(\log_{\sqrt{a}}(a^{6})=12\).
Part (ii) · Value of \(\log_{\sqrt{a}}(a^{6})\)[1] · worked in lesson (correct)
Hide working
Strategy
Use the change-of-base rule \(\log_{b}X=\frac{\log_{a}X}{\log_{a}b}\), or directly: \(\log_{a^{r}}(a^{s})=\frac{s}{r}\).
Working
\[ \log_{\sqrt{a}}(a^{6})=\log_{a^{1/2}}(a^{6})=\frac{6}{1/2} \]
Result
\( =12 \)
Sense check
\(\sqrt{a}=a^{1/2}\), so the base is \(a^{1/2}\). \(\big(a^{1/2}\big)^{12}=a^{6}\)  ✓.
