Q4 · Remainder and factor theorems, factorising, real roots

Total 8 marksQuestion 4. \(f(x)=(x-2)(2x^{2}+5x+k)+21\) where \(k\) is a constant.
(a) State the remainder when \(f(x)\) is divided by \((x-2)\).  (1)
Given that \((2x-1)\) is a factor of \(f(x)\), (b) show that \(k=11\).  (2)
(c) Hence (i) fully factorise \(f(x)\), (ii) find the number of real solutions of the equation \(f(x)=0\) giving a reason for your answer.  (5)
Source: Pearson Edexcel IAL WMA12/01, 14 May 2024, Question 4 (verbatim).
Part (a) · Remainder when divided by \((x-2)\)[1] · worked in lesson
Hide working
Strategy
The keyword "remainder" signals the Remainder Theorem: the remainder on dividing by \((x-a)\) is \(f(a)\). Here \(a=2\).
Substitute
\[ f(2)=(2-2)\big(2(2)^{2}+5(2)+k\big)+21=0\cdot(\cdots)+21 \]
Result
\( \text{remainder}=21 \)
Sense check
The \((x-2)\) factor vanishes at \(x=2\), so only the \(+21\) survives. Harry: "a really easy one-mark question" once you spot the remainder theorem.
Phuc's difficulty (recorded): Phuc was initially confused and attempted long division. Harry derived the Remainder Theorem from scratch: \(f(x)=(x-a)q(x)+r\), so \(f(a)=0+r=r\). The keyword "remainder" ⇒ Remainder Theorem.
Part (b) · Show \(k=11\)[2] · worked in lesson
Carry-in\((2x-1)\) is a factor \(\Rightarrow f\!\big(\tfrac{1}{2}\big)=0\) (Factor Theorem).
Hide working
Strategy
The keyword "factor" signals the Factor Theorem: if \((px-q)\) is a factor, then \(f\!\big(\tfrac{q}{p}\big)=0\). Here \(x=\tfrac{1}{2}\).
Substitute \(x=\tfrac{1}{2}\) and set \(=0\)
\[ f\!\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)=\left(\frac{1}{2}-2\right)\!\left(2\cdot\frac{1}{4}+\frac{5}{2}+k\right)+21=0 \]
Working · simplify
\[ \left(-\frac{3}{2}\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}+\frac{5}{2}+k\right)+21=0 \;\Rightarrow\; -\frac{3}{2}(3+k)+21=0 \]\[ \Rightarrow\; -\frac{9}{2}-\frac{3k}{2}+21=0 \;\Rightarrow\; \frac{33}{2}=\frac{3k}{2} \;\Rightarrow\; k=11 \]
Result
\( k=11 \)
Sense check
With \(k=11\): \(f\!\big(\tfrac12\big)=-\tfrac32(14)+21=-21+21=0\)  ✓.
Harry's feedback (recorded): Phuc correctly identified \(f\!\big(\tfrac12\big)=0\). Harry built the keyword habit: "remainder" ⇒ remainder theorem; "factor" ⇒ factor theorem.
Part (c)(i) · Fully factorise \(f(x)\)[part of 5] · worked in lesson
Carry-in\(k=11\) from (b); \((2x-1)\) is a known factor. The command "hence" means use these results.
Hide working
Strategy
Substitute \(k=11\), expand to a cubic, then divide by the known factor \((2x-1)\) to find the quadratic factor.
Working · expand
\[ f(x)=(x-2)(2x^{2}+5x+11)+21 =2x^{3}+5x^{2}+11x-4x^{2}-10x-22+21 =2x^{3}+x^{2}+x-1 \]
Working · polynomial long division by \((2x-1)\)
\[ \frac{2x^{3}+x^{2}+x-1}{2x-1} \;\Rightarrow\; x^{2}+x+1 \]  (first: \(x^{2}\), subtract \(2x^{3}-x^{2}\), remainder \(2x^{2}\); next: \(+x\), remainder \(2x\); next: \(+1\), remainder 0)
Result
\( f(x)=(2x-1)(x^{2}+x+1) \)
Sense check
Expand back: \((2x-1)(x^{2}+x+1)=2x^{3}+2x^{2}+2x-x^{2}-x-1=2x^{3}+x^{2}+x-1\)  ✓.
Phuc's work (recorded): Phuc suggested and performed the polynomial long division correctly, self-correcting a sign slip mid-way. He then tried to factorise \(x^{2}+x+1\) further and got stuck.
Part (c)(ii) · Number of real solutions of \(f(x)=0\)[part of 5] · worked in lesson
Carry-inFrom (c)(i): \(f(x)=(2x-1)(x^{2}+x+1)=0\).
Hide working
Strategy
Solve \(f(x)=0\) by setting each factor to zero. Check the quadratic with the discriminant \(b^{2}-4ac\).
Working · linear factor
\(2x-1=0 \Rightarrow x=\tfrac{1}{2}\). This is one real solution.
Working · quadratic discriminant
\[ b^{2}-4ac=1^{2}-4(1)(1)=1-4=-3<0 \]Since \(b^{2}-4ac<0\), the quadratic \(x^{2}+x+1=0\) has no real roots.
Result
\( f(x)=0 \) has \(\boxed{1}\) real solution (at \(x=\tfrac{1}{2}\)).
Sense check
The MS requires stating \(b^{2}-4ac=-3<0\) and the conclusion "no real roots". Without this the A mark is not awarded.
Harry's teaching (recorded): Harry introduced the discriminant as a quick "does it factorise" check: if \(b^{2}-4ac\) is a square, it factors; if negative, it has no real roots. In (c)(ii) you must state \(b^{2}-4ac=-3<0\) as the reason — the MS gives A0 without it.
Common trap (Harry flagged): Saying "no roots" without evidence. You must show the discriminant calculation and state the sign, then deduce no real roots, and conclude the number of real solutions of \(f(x)=0\).
Q4(a)–(c)(ii) fully worked in the 10 July 2026 lesson (longest teaching block). Answers: remainder \(=21\); \(k=11\); \(f(x)=(2x-1)(x^{2}+x+1)\); one real solution (\(b^{2}-4ac=-3<0\)).
