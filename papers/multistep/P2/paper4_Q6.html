Q6 — Three GP terms $2k-15$, $k$, $k+4$: show $k^2-7k-60=0$; find $u_4$; smallest $N$ with $S_N>3000$

Question

The first three monthly download counts (in thousands) of an app are $2k - 15$, $k$, $k + 4$, modelled as the first three terms of a geometric series.

(a) Show that $k^2 - 7k - 60 = 0$. [2]

(b) Predict the number of downloads in month 4 (assuming all terms are positive). [4]

(c) Find the smallest integer $N$ such that the cumulative total over the first $N$ months exceeds 3 million. [3]

[9 marks]

Part (a) — show $k^2 - 7k - 60 = 0$ [2 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2 M1

Strategy: a geometric progression has a constant ratio between consecutive terms: $r = u_2/u_1 = u_3/u_2 = \ldots$. Setting these equal gives a single equation in $k$ — no need to know $r$ explicitly.

Formula (GP common-ratio property):

$$\dfrac{u_2}{u_1} = \dfrac{u_3}{u_2} \quad\text{(equivalently, } u_2^{\,2} = u_1 \cdot u_3 \text{)}$$

Identify the three terms: $u_1 = 2k - 15$, $u_2 = k$, $u_3 = k + 4$.

Substitute:

$$\dfrac{k}{2k - 15} = \dfrac{k + 4}{k}$$

Why this works: the GP definition demands a constant ratio. Equating the two ratio expressions encodes "these three are in GP" in one tidy equation. Equivalently, $u_2$ is the geometric mean of $u_1$ and $u_3$: $u_2^2 = u_1 u_3$.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $\dfrac{k}{2k - 15} = \dfrac{k + 4}{k}$
STEP 2/2 A1

Strategy: cross-multiply to clear fractions, expand the RHS, then collect everything on one side to match the target form $k^2 - 7k - 60 = 0$.

Cross-multiply $\dfrac{k}{2k-15} = \dfrac{k+4}{k}$:

$$k \cdot k = (k + 4)(2k - 15)$$

$$k^2 = (k + 4)(2k - 15)$$

Expand the RHS using FOIL: $(k)(2k) + (k)(-15) + (4)(2k) + (4)(-15)$:

$$k^2 = 2k^2 - 15k + 8k - 60$$

$$k^2 = 2k^2 - 7k - 60$$

Move everything to the RHS (subtract $k^2$ from both sides):

$$0 = 2k^2 - k^2 - 7k - 60 = k^2 - 7k - 60$$

$$\boxed{\;k^2 - 7k - 60 = 0\;}\quad \blacksquare$$

FOIL trap: the middle terms are $-15k$ and $+8k$, sum $-7k$ (NOT $+7k$). Sign track: $(k+4)(2k-15)$ has one minus and three pluses → middle coefficient negative.
$k^2 - 7k - 60 = 0$ ∎

Part (b) — month 4 downloads [4 marks] · 2 steps

From (a): $k^2 - 7k - 60 = 0 \Rightarrow (k-12)(k+5) = 0$, so $k = 12$ (positive — downloads $> 0$). The GP has terms $2k-15 = 9$, $k = 12$, $k+4 = 16$, with first term $a = 9$ and common ratio $r = \tfrac{12}{9} = \tfrac{4}{3}$.
STEP 1/2 M1 A1

Strategy: the question says "assuming all terms are positive" — i.e. all three of $2k-15$, $k$, $k+4$ must be $> 0$. The strictest condition is $2k - 15 > 0$, giving $k > 7.5$. Solve the quadratic; only one root will satisfy this.

Factor $k^2 - 7k - 60 = 0$: need two integers multiplying to $-60$, summing to $-7$. Try $-12$ and $+5$: $(-12)(5) = -60$ ✓ and $-12 + 5 = -7$ ✓.

$$(k - 12)(k + 5) = 0$$

$$k = 12 \quad\text{or}\quad k = -5$$

Apply positivity ($k > 7.5$): $k = 12$ ✓; $k = -5$ ✗ (would give $u_1 = 2(-5) - 15 = -25 < 0$).

$$\boxed{\;k = 12\;}$$

Resulting GP terms: $u_1 = 2(12) - 15 = 9$, $u_2 = 12$, $u_3 = 12 + 4 = 16$.

Why "positive" matters: the modelling context is download counts (cannot be negative). Without that constraint, both roots are mathematically valid GP triples (with $k = -5$ giving terms $-25, -5, -1$ and $r = \tfrac{1}{5}$).
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $\boxed{\;k = 12\;}$
STEP 2/2 M1 A1

Strategy: with the GP fully determined ($a = u_1 = 9$, common ratio $r$), use the standard $n$th-term formula at $n = 4$.

Find $r$ from any two consecutive terms:

$$r = \dfrac{u_2}{u_1} = \dfrac{12}{9} = \tfrac{4}{3}$$

Cross-check: $\dfrac{u_3}{u_2} = \dfrac{16}{12} = \tfrac{4}{3}$ ✓ — confirms GP.

Formula (GP $n$th term — NOT in booklet, memorise):

$$u_n = a\,r^{n-1}$$

Substitute $a = 9$, $r = \tfrac{4}{3}$, $n = 4$ (so $n - 1 = 3$):

$$u_4 = 9 \cdot \left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right)^{\!3}$$

Compute $\left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right)^3 = \tfrac{64}{27}$:

$$u_4 = 9 \cdot \tfrac{64}{27} = \tfrac{9 \cdot 64}{27} = \tfrac{64}{3}$$

Convert units: terms are in thousands, so

$$u_4 = \tfrac{64}{3}\text{ thousand} = \tfrac{64\,000}{3} \approx 21\,333$$

$$\boxed{\;\text{Month 4 downloads} \approx 21\,333\;}$$

Indexing trap: $u_n = ar^{n-1}$ — that's $r$ to the power $n-1$, NOT $n$. Month 4 means $n = 4$, so we use $r^3$. Sense check: the GP grows ($r = \tfrac{4}{3} > 1$), so $u_4 > u_3 = 16$ thousand ✓ ($\tfrac{64}{3} \approx 21.3$ thousand).
$\boxed{\approx 21\,333\text{ downloads}}$

Part (c) — smallest $N$ with $S_N > 3000$ [3 marks] · 2 steps

Target: 3 million = 3000 thousand (units of $u_n$ are thousands).
STEP 1/2 M1

Strategy: the cumulative total over $N$ months is $S_N$ (a GP sum). The unknown $N$ sits inside an exponent, so the plan is: write $S_N$ explicitly, set $S_N > 3000$, isolate $\left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right)^N$, then take logs in the next step.

Formula (GP sum — IN booklet):

$$S_n = \dfrac{a(r^n - 1)}{r - 1}, \quad r \ne 1$$

Substitute $a = 9$, $r = \tfrac{4}{3}$, so $r - 1 = \tfrac{1}{3}$:

$$S_n = \dfrac{9\!\left[\left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right)^{\!n} - 1\right]}{1/3} = 9 \cdot 3 \cdot \!\left[\left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right)^{\!n} - 1\right] = 27\!\left[\left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right)^{\!n} - 1\right]$$

Require $S_N > 3000$ (3 million in units of thousands):

$$27\!\left[\left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right)^{\!N} - 1\right] > 3000$$

Divide by $27$:

$$\left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right)^{\!N} - 1 > \tfrac{3000}{27} = \tfrac{1000}{9}$$

Add $1$ to both sides:

$$\boxed{\;\left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right)^{\!N} > 1 + \tfrac{1000}{9} = \tfrac{1009}{9}\;}$$

Trap: $\sum_{n=1}^N u_n$ uses $S_N$ formula, NOT $u_N$. They differ by accumulation — $u_N$ is just the $N$th term, $S_N$ is the running total of all $N$ terms. Unit check: "3 million" with terms in thousands means target $= 3000$ (NOT $3$ or $3{,}000{,}000$).
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $\boxed{\;\left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right)^{\!N} > 1 + \tfrac{1000}{9} = \tfrac{1009}{9}\;}$
STEP 2/2 M1 A1

Strategy: take logs of both sides to bring the unknown $N$ down from the exponent. Log is increasing for arguments $> 0$, and $\tfrac{4}{3} > 1$ so $\log(\tfrac{4}{3}) > 0$ — the inequality direction is preserved when we divide by it.

Take $\log_{10}$ of both sides of $\left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right)^N > \tfrac{1009}{9}$:

$$\log\!\left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right)^{\!N} > \log\!\left(\tfrac{1009}{9}\right)$$

Apply power law $\log(a^N) = N\log a$:

$$N \cdot \log\!\left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right) > \log\!\left(\tfrac{1009}{9}\right)$$

Divide by $\log(\tfrac{4}{3}) > 0$ (inequality preserved):

$$N > \dfrac{\log(1009/9)}{\log(4/3)}$$

Compute (base-10 logs):

$\;\;\log(1009/9) = \log(112.11) \approx 2.0497$

$\;\;\log(4/3) = \log(1.3333) \approx 0.1249$

$$N > \dfrac{2.0497}{0.1249} \approx 16.41$$

$N$ must be a positive integer; smallest integer exceeding $16.41$ is $17$:

$$\boxed{\;N = 17\;}$$

Why round UP, not down: we need $S_N > 3000$, so $N$ must be at least $17$ — $N = 16$ gives $S_{16} < 3000$ (since $16 < 16.41$). Unit check: "3 million" with terms in thousands means target = 3000 (NOT 3 or 3,000,000). Always reconcile units before solving.
$\boxed{N = 17}$
