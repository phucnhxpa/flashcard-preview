P2 (WMA12/01) · Q6 · Jun 2024

Substitution & Definite Integral

Question

Given the integral $I = \int \frac{6x}{(2x^2+3)^2} dx$.

(a) Use the substitution $u = 2x^2+3$ to find $I$. [4]

(b) Hence find the exact value of $\int_0^1 \frac{6x}{(2x^2+3)^2} dx$. [5]

[9 marks]

Part (a) — Indefinite integral by substitution [4 marks] · 4 steps

$u = 2x^2+3$
STEP 1/4 M1

Differentiate the substitution

$$u = 2x^2+3 \implies \frac{du}{dx} = 4x$$

$$du = 4x\,dx \implies dx = \frac{du}{4x}$$

Pitfall: Forgetting to differentiate or incorrectly finding $\frac{du}{dx} = 2x$ (missing the chain rule coefficient).
$\boxed{du = 4x\,dx}$
Carry-in from previous step$du = 4x\,dx$
STEP 2/4 M1

Substitute into the integral

$$I = \int \frac{6x}{u^2} \cdot \frac{du}{4x} = \int \frac{6}{4u^2}\,du = \int \frac{3}{2}u^{-2}\,du$$

Pitfall: Failing to cancel the $x$ terms — check that all $x$ dependence vanishes after substitution.
$\boxed{\frac{3}{2}\int u^{-2}\,du}$
Carry-in from previous step$\frac{3}{2}\int u^{-2}\,du$
STEP 3/4 A1

Integrate using power rule

$$\int u^{-2}\,du = \frac{u^{-1}}{-1} = -\frac{1}{u}$$

$$I = \frac{3}{2} \cdot \left(-\frac{1}{u}\right) = -\frac{3}{2u}$$

Pitfall: Common error is writing $\ln|u^2|$ — only $\int u^{-1}\,du = \ln|u|$, not $u^{-2}$.
$\boxed{-\frac{3}{2u}}$
Carry-in from previous step$-\frac{3}{2u}$
STEP 4/4 A1

Substitute back $u = 2x^2+3$

$$I = -\frac{3}{2(2x^2+3)} + C$$

Pitfall: Forgetting the constant of integration $+C$ loses the final A mark.
$\boxed{-\frac{3}{2(2x^2+3)} + C}$

Part (b) — Evaluate definite integral [5 marks] · 5 steps

$I = -\frac{3}{2(2x^2+3)}$ from part (a)
STEP 1/5 M1

Change limits from $x$ to $u$

Using $u = 2x^2+3$:

$$x = 0 \implies u = 2(0)^2 + 3 = 3$$

$$x = 1 \implies u = 2(1)^2 + 3 = 5$$

Pitfall: Forgetting to change limits is the most common error — you must convert both limits when working in $u$.
$\boxed{u: 3 \to 5}$
Carry-in from previous step$u: 3 \to 5$
STEP 2/5 M1

Set up definite integral with new limits

$$\int_0^1 \frac{6x}{(2x^2+3)^2}\,dx = \int_3^5 \frac{3}{2}u^{-2}\,du = \left[-\frac{3}{2u}\right]_3^5$$

Pitfall: If you don't change limits, you must substitute back to $x$ before evaluating — mixing $u$ limits with $x$ expression gives wrong answer.
$\boxed{\left[-\frac{3}{2u}\right]_3^5}$
Carry-in from previous step$\left[-\frac{3}{2u}\right]_3^5$
STEP 3/5 A1

Evaluate at upper limit $u=5$

$$-\frac{3}{2(5)} = -\frac{3}{10}$$

$\boxed{-\frac{3}{10}}$
Carry-in from previous step$-\frac{3}{10}$
STEP 4/5 A1

Evaluate at lower limit $u=3$

$$-\frac{3}{2(3)} = -\frac{3}{6} = -\frac{1}{2}$$

Subtract: Upper $-$ Lower

$$-\frac{3}{10} - \left(-\frac{1}{2}\right) = -\frac{3}{10} + \frac{1}{2}$$

Pitfall: Sign error when subtracting a negative — $-(-\frac{1}{2}) = +\frac{1}{2}$.
$\boxed{-\frac{3}{10} + \frac{1}{2}}$
Carry-in from previous step$-\frac{3}{10} + \frac{1}{2}$
STEP 5/5 A1

Simplify to exact value

$$-\frac{3}{10} + \frac{5}{10} = \frac{2}{10} = \frac{1}{5}$$

Pitfall: Leaving as $\frac{2}{10}$ instead of simplifying — always give fractions in lowest terms.
$\boxed{\frac{1}{5}}$
