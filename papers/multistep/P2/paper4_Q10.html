Q10 — $C: y = (x-k)^2/\sqrt{x}$. Show $\int_1^{16}y\,dx = ak^2+bk+2046/5$; show $k=4$; area $R$

Question

Curve $C$: $y = \dfrac{(x-k)^2}{\sqrt{x}}$ for $x > 0$, where $k > 0$.

(a) Show that $\displaystyle\int_1^{16}\dfrac{(x-k)^2}{\sqrt{x}}\,dx = ak^2 + bk + \dfrac{2046}{5}$, where $a, b$ are integers to find. [5]

Line $\ell$ intersects $C$ at $A(1, 9)$ and $B(16, q)$.

(b) Show $k = 4$. [2]

(c) Find the area of region $R$ bounded by $C$ and $\ell$. [3]

[10 marks]

Part (a) — integrate, match coefficients [5 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3 M1

Strategy: the integrand $\dfrac{(x-k)^2}{\sqrt{x}}$ doesn't fit a standard rule directly. Expand the numerator $(x-k)^2$, then divide each term by $x^{1/2}$ — this gives a sum of pure powers, which the power rule handles.

Expand $(x - k)^2$ using $(a-b)^2 = a^2 - 2ab + b^2$:

$$(x - k)^2 = x^2 - 2kx + k^2$$

Divide each term by $x^{1/2}$ (use $x^a / x^b = x^{a-b}$):

$\;\;\dfrac{x^2}{x^{1/2}} = x^{2 - 1/2} = x^{3/2}$

$\;\;\dfrac{-2kx}{x^{1/2}} = -2k\,x^{1 - 1/2} = -2k\,x^{1/2}$

$\;\;\dfrac{k^2}{x^{1/2}} = k^2 x^{-1/2}$

$$\boxed{\;\dfrac{(x-k)^2}{\sqrt{x}} = x^{3/2} - 2k\,x^{1/2} + k^2 x^{-1/2}\;}$$

Why split first: $\int (\text{quotient})$ generally requires substitution or messy work, but $\int (\text{sum of powers})$ uses the power rule term-by-term — much easier.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $\boxed{\;\dfrac{(x-k)^2}{\sqrt{x}} = x^{3/2} - 2k\,x^{1/2} + k^2 x^{-1/2}\;}$
STEP 2/3 M1

Strategy: apply the power rule to each term. Constants ($k$, $k^2$) ride along the integration as multiplicative factors.

Formula (power rule for integration):

$$\int x^n\,dx = \dfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C, \quad n \ne -1$$

Integrate term by term:

$\;\;\int x^{3/2}\,dx = \dfrac{x^{5/2}}{5/2} = \tfrac{2}{5}x^{5/2}$

$\;\;\int -2k\,x^{1/2}\,dx = -2k \cdot \dfrac{x^{3/2}}{3/2} = -\tfrac{4k}{3}x^{3/2}$

$\;\;\int k^2 x^{-1/2}\,dx = k^2 \cdot \dfrac{x^{1/2}}{1/2} = 2k^2 x^{1/2}$

$$\boxed{\;F(x) = \tfrac{2}{5}x^{5/2} - \tfrac{4k}{3}x^{3/2} + 2k^2 x^{1/2}\;}$$

Trap: dividing by a fraction. $\dfrac{1}{5/2} = \dfrac{2}{5}$ — flip and multiply. Indices for definite integrals: we omit the $+C$ since it cancels under $F(b) - F(a)$.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 2: $\boxed{\;F(x) = \tfrac{2}{5}x^{5/2} - \tfrac{4k}{3}x^{3/2} + 2k^2 x^{1/2}\;}$
STEP 3/3 A1 A1 A1

Strategy: apply the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus: $\int_1^{16} f\,dx = F(16) - F(1)$. Pre-compute powers of $16$: $16^{1/2} = 4$, $16^{3/2} = 16 \cdot 4 = 64$, $16^{5/2} = 16^2 \cdot 4 = 1024$. Powers of $1$ all equal $1$.

Evaluate $F(16)$:

$$F(16) = \tfrac{2}{5}(1024) - \tfrac{4k}{3}(64) + 2k^2(4) = \tfrac{2048}{5} - \tfrac{256k}{3} + 8k^2$$

Evaluate $F(1)$:

$$F(1) = \tfrac{2}{5}(1) - \tfrac{4k}{3}(1) + 2k^2(1) = \tfrac{2}{5} - \tfrac{4k}{3} + 2k^2$$

Subtract $F(16) - F(1)$ term by term:

$\;\;$ Constant terms: $\tfrac{2048}{5} - \tfrac{2}{5} = \tfrac{2046}{5}$.

$\;\;$ $k$ terms: $-\tfrac{256k}{3} - (-\tfrac{4k}{3}) = -\tfrac{256k}{3} + \tfrac{4k}{3} = -\tfrac{252k}{3} = -84k$.

$\;\;$ $k^2$ terms: $8k^2 - 2k^2 = 6k^2$.

$$\int_1^{16}\dfrac{(x-k)^2}{\sqrt{x}}\,dx = 6k^2 - 84k + \tfrac{2046}{5}$$

Match coefficients with target $ak^2 + bk + \tfrac{2046}{5}$:

$$\boxed{\;a = 6,\quad b = -84\;}\quad \blacksquare$$

"Show that $\int = $ expression" means compute the integral and match coefficients — NOT set it equal to zero. Sign trap: $-\tfrac{256k}{3} - (-\tfrac{4k}{3}) = -\tfrac{256k - 4k}{3} = -\tfrac{252k}{3}$ — careful with the double-minus.
$\boxed{a = 6,\ b = -84}$

Part (b) — show $k = 4$ [2 marks] · 1 step

Use the GIVEN POINT $A(1, 9)$ on the curve — NOT the integral.
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Strategy: the point $A(1, 9)$ lies ON the curve (it's where the line meets the curve). So the coordinates must satisfy the curve equation $y = (x-k)^2/\sqrt{x}$. Substitute $x = 1$ and $y = 9$, then solve for $k$.

Substitute $x = 1$, $y = 9$ into $y = \dfrac{(x - k)^2}{\sqrt{x}}$:

$$9 = \dfrac{(1 - k)^2}{\sqrt{1}} = (1 - k)^2$$

Take square roots (both signs):

$$1 - k = \pm\sqrt{9} = \pm 3$$

Two cases:

$\;\;1 - k = +3 \;\Rightarrow\; k = -2$ ✗ (need $k > 0$).

$\;\;1 - k = -3 \;\Rightarrow\; k = 4$ ✓.

$$\boxed{\;k = 4\;} \quad \blacksquare$$

Why use $A$ on curve, not the integral: the integral result from (a) involves the unknown $k$ — it doesn't pin down $k$ alone. The point on the curve gives a direct equation in $k$.

Part (c) — area of $R$ [3 marks] · 3 steps

With $k = 4$: at $B$, $y = (16-4)^2/4 = 144/4 = 36$, so $B(16, 36)$.
STEP 1/3 M1

Strategy: the region $R$ is enclosed between the line and the curve from $x = 1$ to $x = 16$. Compute area = (area under line) − (area under curve), both measured between $x = 1$ and $x = 16$ down to the $x$-axis. Since the line is above the curve in this region, the difference is positive.

Area under line $\ell$: bounded by $x = 1$, $x = 16$, $\ell$, and the $x$-axis — a trapezium.

At $x = 1$: $\ell$-height = $9$ (point $A$). At $x = 16$: $\ell$-height = $36$ (point $B$). Horizontal width = $16 - 1 = 15$.

Formula (area of trapezium): $A = \tfrac{1}{2}(\text{sum of parallel sides})(\text{height})$

$$A_\ell = \tfrac{1}{2}(9 + 36)(15) = \tfrac{1}{2}(45)(15) = \tfrac{675}{2} = 337.5$$

Why trapezium not triangle: the line doesn't pass through the origin — both endpoints have positive $y$. The shape under the line over $[1, 16]$ has parallel vertical sides of length $9$ and $36$, hence trapezium.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $A_\ell = \tfrac{1}{2}(9 + 36)(15) = \tfrac{1}{2}(45)(15) = \tfrac{675}{2} = 337.5$
STEP 2/3 M1

Strategy: we already computed $\int_1^{16}\frac{(x-k)^2}{\sqrt{x}}\,dx = 6k^2 - 84k + \tfrac{2046}{5}$ in part (a). Plug in $k = 4$ from part (b).

Substitute $k = 4$:

$$A_C = 6(4)^2 - 84(4) + \tfrac{2046}{5}$$

Compute term by term:

$\;\;6 \cdot 16 = 96$

$\;\;84 \cdot 4 = 336$

$\;\;\tfrac{2046}{5} = 409.2$

$$A_C = 96 - 336 + 409.2 = -240 + 409.2 = 169.2 = \tfrac{846}{5}$$

Reuse trick: redoing the integral from scratch with $k = 4$ wastes time. The part (a) formula is the gift — plug and chug. Sanity: $A_C > 0$ ✓ (curve is positive on $[1, 16]$ since $(x-4)^2 \ge 0$ and $\sqrt{x} > 0$).
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 2: $A_C = 96 - 336 + 409.2 = -240 + 409.2 = 169.2 = \tfrac{846}{5}$
STEP 3/3 A1

Strategy: the region $R$ is bounded above by the line and below by the curve. Area($R$) = (area under line) − (area under curve), both over $[1, 16]$, since both regions extend down to the same $x$-axis.

$$\text{Area}(R) = A_\ell - A_C = \tfrac{675}{2} - \tfrac{846}{5}$$

Common denominator $10$:

$$= \tfrac{675 \cdot 5}{10} - \tfrac{846 \cdot 2}{10} = \tfrac{3375 - 1692}{10} = \tfrac{1683}{10}$$

$$\boxed{\;\text{Area}(R) = \tfrac{1683}{10} = 168.3\;}$$

Why subtract: imagine the region under the line as a "canopy" — subtracting the area under the curve (the "floor") leaves exactly the strip between them. Sense check: $337.5 - 169.2 = 168.3$ ✓ (in decimal, matches fraction).
$\boxed{\text{Area}(R) = 168.3}$
