Q3 — Solve $2\log_2(2-x) = 4 + \log_2(x+10)$; find $\log_a(a^6)$

Question

(i) Solve, for $x$, the equation $2\log_2(2-x) = 4 + \log_2(x+10)$. [5]

(ii) Given $a > 0$, $a \ne 1$, find $\log_a(a^6)$. [1]

[6 marks]

Part (i) — solve for $x$ [5 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3 M1

Strategy: the equation mixes a coefficient log ($2\log$), a bare constant ($4$), and a sum of logs. Goal: rewrite each side as a single $\log_2$ of one expression, then we'll be able to drop the logs.

Formulas (log laws — memorise):

$$n\log_a x = \log_a(x^n), \qquad \log_a x + \log_a y = \log_a(xy), \qquad k = \log_a(a^k)$$

LHS — pull the leading $2$ into the power (power law):

$$2\log_2(2-x) = \log_2(2-x)^2$$

RHS — convert the bare constant $4$ to a base-$2$ log:

$$4 = 4\log_2 2 = \log_2(2^4) = \log_2 16$$

Combine RHS by the product law $\log + \log = \log(\cdot)$:

$$\log_2 16 + \log_2(x+10) = \log_2\!\big[16(x+10)\big]$$

$$\boxed{\;\log_2(2-x)^2 = \log_2\!\big[16(x+10)\big]\;}$$

Phuc's slip: "I can only solve half of question B." Half-B = forgot to bring the leading coefficient into the power before combining. Trap: $\log(a+b) \ne \log a + \log b$. Only $\log(ab) = \log a + \log b$ — product, not sum.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $\boxed{\;\log_2(2-x)^2 = \log_2\!\big[16(x+10)\big]\;}$
STEP 2/3 M1 A1

Strategy: the function $\log_2$ is one-to-one (strictly increasing). Equal logs ⇒ equal arguments. Drop the logs, expand both sides, collect into the standard quadratic form $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$, then factorise.

Drop logs (both sides have $\log_2$):

$$(2-x)^2 = 16(x+10)$$

Expand the LHS using $(a-b)^2 = a^2 - 2ab + b^2$:

$$4 - 4x + x^2 = 16x + 160$$

Move everything to LHS (subtract $16x$, subtract $160$):

$$x^2 - 20x - 156 = 0$$

Factorise: need two integers that multiply to $-156$ and sum to $-20$. Try $-26$ and $+6$: $(-26)(6) = -156$ ✓ and $-26 + 6 = -20$ ✓.

$$(x - 26)(x + 6) = 0$$

$$\boxed{\;x = 26\ \text{ or }\ x = -6\;}$$

Sense check: the quadratic formula gives the same answer: $x = \tfrac{20 \pm \sqrt{400 + 624}}{2} = \tfrac{20 \pm 32}{2} = 26\ \text{or}\ -6$ ✓.
$x = 26$ or $x = -6$
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 2: $x = 26$ or $x = -6$
STEP 3/3 A1

Strategy: $\log_a$ is only defined for positive arguments. We squared away the original logs (when we wrote $(2-x)^2$), so we may have introduced a spurious root. Check each candidate against the ORIGINAL log arguments.

Argument 1: $\log_2(2-x)$ needs $2 - x > 0$, so $x < 2$.

Argument 2: $\log_2(x+10)$ needs $x + 10 > 0$, so $x > -10$.

Combined valid domain: $-10 < x < 2$.

Test $x = 26$: needs $x < 2$, but $26 > 2$ → reject.

Test $x = -6$: $-10 < -6 < 2$ ✓ → accept.

$$\boxed{\;x = -6\;}$$

Mark-loss point: forgetting the domain check and writing both roots costs the final A1. Why does the spurious root appear? Squaring $(2-x)$ throws away its sign — $(2-x)^2 = (x-2)^2$, so the equation no longer "knows" we needed $2-x > 0$. Domain check restores it.
$\boxed{x = -6}$

Part (ii) — $\log_a(a^6)$ [1 marks] · 1 step

Independent of (i). Conditions: $a > 0$, $a \ne 1$. Use $\log_a(x^n) = n\log_a x$ together with $\log_a a = 1$.
STEP 1/1 B1

Strategy: a logarithm $\log_a y$ asks “to what power must $a$ be raised to give $y$?”. Here $y = a^6$ — the answer is staring at us, but let's prove it via the laws.

Formulas (log laws): $\log_a(x^n) = n\log_a x$  and  $\log_a a = 1$.

Apply the power rule (bring the $6$ down):

$$\log_a(a^6) = 6\log_a a$$

Now $\log_a a = 1$ (any positive base raised to the $1$ gives itself):

$$= 6 \cdot 1 = 6$$

$$\boxed{\;\log_a(a^6) = 6\;}$$

Why $\log_a a = 1$? By definition, $\log_a a$ is the exponent giving $a^? = a$, which is $? = 1$. General pattern: $\log_a(a^k) = k$ for any $k$ — the log and the exponential are inverse operations and they cancel.
$\boxed{6}$
