Q3 — Solve $2\tan\theta - 3\sin\theta = 0$ in $(0°, 360°]$; smallest $x>0$ with substitution

Question

(a) Solve, for $\theta$ in $(0°, 360°]$, the equation $2\tan\theta - 3\sin\theta = 0$, giving answers to 1 d.p. [5]

(b) Hence find the smallest positive solution of $2\tan(2x + 40°) - 3\sin(2x + 40°) = 0$, to 1 d.p. [2]

[7 marks]

Part (a) — solve in $(0°, 360°]$ [5 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3 M1

Strategy: when $\sin$ and $\tan$ both appear in the same equation, rewrite $\tan$ in terms of $\sin/\cos$. Then $\sin\theta$ becomes a common factor that can be pulled out, splitting the problem into two simpler cases.

Formula (identity): $\tan\theta = \dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}$.

Rewrite the equation:

$\;\;2\tan\theta - 3\sin\theta = 0$

$\;\;2 \cdot \dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} - 3\sin\theta = 0$

Factor out $\sin\theta$:

$\;\;\sin\theta\!\left(\dfrac{2}{\cos\theta} - 3\right) = 0$

$$\boxed{\;\sin\theta\!\left(\dfrac{2}{\cos\theta} - 3\right) = 0\;}$$

Two cases follow: $\sin\theta = 0$ or $\dfrac{2}{\cos\theta} = 3$.

Don\u2019t divide by $\sin\theta$: dividing through would lose the $\sin\theta = 0$ solutions. Always factor instead, then handle each factor as a separate case.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $\boxed{\;\sin\theta\!\left(\dfrac{2}{\cos\theta} - 3\right) = 0\;}$
STEP 2/3 A1

Strategy: handle the first factor. Find all $\theta$ in $(0°, 360°]$ where $\sin\theta = 0$.

$\sin\theta = 0$ at $\theta = 0°, 180°, 360°, \ldots$

Restrict to the interval $(0°, 360°]$ \u2014 note $0°$ is excluded:

$\;\;\theta = 180°$ or $\theta = 360°$

$$\boxed{\;\theta = 180°,\ 360°\;}$$

Endpoint care: the interval is half-open: $0°$ is excluded but $360°$ is included. Always check the inequality direction \u2014 dropping $360°$ here costs an A1 mark.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 2: $\boxed{\;\theta = 180°,\ 360°\;}$
STEP 3/3 M1 A1 A1

Strategy: handle the second factor. Solve for $\cos\theta$, find the principal value, then use cosine symmetry $\cos(360° - \alpha) = \cos\alpha$ to get the second solution in the interval. Combine with case 1.

Formula (cosine symmetry): $\cos\theta = k$ has two solutions in $[0°, 360°)$: $\theta = \alpha$ and $\theta = 360° - \alpha$, where $\alpha = \cos^{-1}(k)$.

From $\dfrac{2}{\cos\theta} - 3 = 0$:

$\;\;\dfrac{2}{\cos\theta} = 3$

$\;\;\cos\theta = \dfrac{2}{3}$

Principal value:

$\;\;\alpha = \cos^{-1}\!\left(\dfrac{2}{3}\right) \approx 48.19°$

Second solution in the interval:

$\;\;\theta = 360° - 48.19° = 311.81°$

To 1 d.p.: $\theta \approx 48.2°$ and $\theta \approx 311.8°$.

Combine with Case 1:

$$\boxed{\;\theta = 48.2°,\ 180°,\ 311.8°,\ 360°\;}$$

Phuc\u2019s slip (per redo): missed the 4th-quadrant solution ($311.8°$). Always pair $\alpha$ with $360° - \alpha$ for cosine \u2014 the function is even-symmetric about $\theta = 0$ (and $360°$).
$\boxed{\theta = 48.2°,\ 180°,\ 311.8°,\ 360°}$

Part (b) — smallest positive $x$ [2 marks] · 2 steps

Replace $\theta$ with $(2x + 40°)$. Use the smallest valid $\theta$ from (a).
STEP 1/2 M1

Strategy: we need the smallest positive $x$. Substituting $\theta = 2x + 40°$ means the smallest $x$ corresponds to the smallest valid $\theta$ that satisfies $x > 0$, i.e., $\theta > 40°$.

Constraint: $x > 0 \Rightarrow 2x + 40 > 40 \Rightarrow \theta > 40°$.

Candidate $\theta$ values from part (a):

$\;\;\theta \in \{48.2°,\ 180°,\ 311.8°,\ 360°\}$

All are $> 40°$. Choose the smallest:

$$\boxed{\;\theta = 48.2°\;}$$

Pick smallest first: the smaller $\theta$ \u2192 the smaller $x$. Always start with the smallest valid value from part (a) when asked for the smallest positive solution.
Carry-in from previous stepFrom STEP 1: $\boxed{\;\theta = 48.2°\;}$
STEP 2/2 A1

Strategy: with $\theta = 48.2°$ chosen, undo the linear substitution $\theta = 2x + 40$ to recover $x$.

Set up the equation:

$\;\;2x + 40 = 48.2$

Subtract $40$ from both sides:

$\;\;2x = 8.2$

Divide by $2$:

$\;\;x = 4.1°$

$$\boxed{\;x = 4.1°\;}$$

Phuc\u2019s slip (per redo): used $\theta = 180°$ first (giving $x = 70°$). That\u2019s a valid solution but NOT the smallest. Always start with the smallest $\theta$ value from part (a) when asked for the smallest positive $x$.
$\boxed{x = 4.1°}$
