Copper Q — Resistance of a thin copper wire to 2 s.f.

Question

A copper wire has length $l = 0.50\ \text{m}$ and diameter $d = 0.40\ \text{mm}$. The resistivity of copper is $\rho = 1.72 \times 10^{-8}\ \Omega\,\text{m}$.

(a) Find the cross-sectional area $A$ of the wire. [2 marks]
(b) Hence find the resistance, giving your answer to 2 significant figures. [2 marks]
(c) An aluminium wire of the same dimensions ($\rho_{\text{Al}} = 2.6 \times 10^{-8}\ \Omega\,\text{m}$) replaces it. Find its resistance and state which metal is the better conductor. [2 marks]

[6 marks]

Part (a) — cross-sectional area [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Convert diameter (mm $\to$ m) and take the radius:

$$d = 0.40 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}, \qquad r = 0.20 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}$$

Formula (circular area):

$$A = \pi r^2 = \pi (0.20 \times 10^{-3})^2 = 1.257 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m}^2$$

Radius trap: halve the diameter first ($r = d/2$), then square. $A = \pi r^2$ uses the radius.
$A = 1.26 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m}^2$

Part (b) — resistance (2 s.f.) [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a): $A = 1.257 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m}^2$; given $\rho = 1.72 \times 10^{-8}\ \Omega\,\text{m}$, $l = 0.50\ \text{m}$.
↳ from STEP 1: $\rho = 1.72 \times 10^{-8}$, $l = 0.50\ \text{m}$, $A = 1.257 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m}^2$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (resistivity definition):

$$R = \dfrac{\rho l}{A}$$

Substitute:

$$R = \dfrac{1.72 \times 10^{-8} \times 0.50}{1.257 \times 10^{-7}} = 0.068\ \Omega\ (2\ \text{s.f.})$$

Sense-check: copper has very low $\rho$, so a short thin wire has a tiny resistance ($< 0.1\ \Omega$) — consistent with copper being an excellent conductor.
$R = 0.068\ \Omega$

Part (c) — aluminium comparison [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a) & (b): same dimensions, so $A = 1.257 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m}^2$ and $l = 0.50\ \text{m}$; aluminium $\rho_{\text{Al}} = 2.6 \times 10^{-8}\ \Omega\,\text{m}$. Copper result from (b): $R_{\text{Cu}} = 0.068\ \Omega$.
↳ from STEP 1: $A = 1.257 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m}^2$, $l = 0.50\ \text{m}$, $\rho_{\text{Al}} = 2.6 \times 10^{-8}$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (same geometry, new material):

$$R_{\text{Al}} = \dfrac{\rho_{\text{Al}}\, l}{A}$$

Substitute:

$$R_{\text{Al}} = \dfrac{2.6 \times 10^{-8} \times 0.50}{1.257 \times 10^{-7}} = 0.103 \approx 0.10\ \Omega$$

Compare: $R_{\text{Cu}} = 0.068\ \Omega < R_{\text{Al}} = 0.10\ \Omega$, so copper is the better conductor.

Concept: with geometry fixed, $R \propto \rho$. Copper's lower resistivity gives the lower resistance — which is why mains wiring uses copper. Resistivity (material property) decides it, not the shape.
$R_{\text{Al}} = 0.10\ \Omega$; copper conducts better (lower $\rho$)
