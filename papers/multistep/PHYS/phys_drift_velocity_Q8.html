Q — Copper drift velocity, and conduction across materials

Question

A copper wire ($n = 8.5\times10^{28}\ \text{m}^{-3}$) of cross-sectional area $2.0\ \text{mm}^2$ carries a current of $5.0\ \text{A}$; $q = 1.6\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}$.

(a) Calculate the drift velocity of the electrons. [2 marks]
(b) Using $I = nqvA$, explain why insulators carry no significant current even with a large p.d. applied. [2 marks]
(c) Using $n$, explain why copper conducts far better than silicon. [2 marks]

[6 marks]

Part (a) — copper drift velocity [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Convert $A = 2.0\ \text{mm}^2 = 2.0\times10^{-6}\ \text{m}^2$, then $v = \dfrac{I}{nqA}$:

$$v = \dfrac{5.0}{(8.5\times10^{28})(1.6\times10^{-19})(2.0\times10^{-6})} \approx 1.84\times10^{-4}\ \text{m s}^{-1}$$

$v \approx 1.84\times10^{-4}\ \text{m s}^{-1}$

Part (b) — insulators [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in: the transport equation $I = nqvA$; for an insulator $n \approx 0$.
↳ from STEP 1: $I = nqvA$ with $n \approx 0$ for an insulator
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Argument: in $I = nqvA$, if $n \approx 0$ there are virtually no free charge carriers, so $I \approx 0$ regardless of $v$ or $A$ — and no matter how large the applied p.d.

An insulator (glass, polystyrene) has essentially no carriers to move, so no current flows.

Key point: a big voltage cannot create carriers that are not there — conduction is limited by $n$, not by the push.
$n \approx 0 \Rightarrow I \approx 0$ for any voltage

Part (c) — copper vs silicon [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in: copper $n \sim 8\times10^{28}\ \text{m}^{-3}$; silicon $n \sim 10^{17}\ \text{m}^{-3}$ — about $10^{11}$ times fewer carriers.
↳ from STEP 1: copper $n\sim10^{28}$ vs silicon $n\sim10^{17}$ ($\sim10^{11}\times$ more in copper)
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Argument (mark-scheme wording): from $I = nqvA$, with $q$ and $A$ fixed, the current a material can carry is governed by $n$. Copper has a vastly larger number density of free electrons ($\sim10^{28}$ vs $\sim10^{17}$ m$^{-3}$), so for the same field it carries far more current — i.e. it conducts much better.

Subtlety: for the same current and area, the semiconductor's carriers actually drift faster ($v\propto1/n$) — fewer carriers must each move quicker. Good conduction comes from large $n$, not large $v$.
Copper's huge $n$ ⇒ far better conductor than silicon
