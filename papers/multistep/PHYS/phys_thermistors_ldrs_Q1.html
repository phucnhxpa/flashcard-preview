Q — Resistance of a thermistor at two operating points

Question

A thermistor carries a current of $3.00\ \text{mA}$ when a p.d. of $3.00\ \text{V}$ is applied, and a current of $16.25\ \text{mA}$ when $7.00\ \text{V}$ is applied.

(a) Calculate the resistance at $V = 3.00\ \text{V}$. [2 marks]
(b) Calculate the resistance at $V = 7.00\ \text{V}$. [2 marks]
(c) Explain, using $I = nqvA$, why the resistance changed. [3 marks]

[7 marks]

Part (a) — resistance at 3.00 V [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (definition of resistance):

$$R = \dfrac{V}{I}$$

Convert $I = 3.00\ \text{mA} = 3.00\times10^{-3}\ \text{A}$, then substitute $V = 3.00\ \text{V}$:

$$R = \dfrac{3.00}{3.00\times10^{-3}} = 1000\ \Omega$$

Trap: it is $R = V/I$, never $R = IV$. And convert mA to A first — $R = 3.00/3.00 = 1\ \Omega$ (forgetting the $10^{-3}$) is a factor-of-1000 error.
$R = 1000\ \Omega$

Part (b) — resistance at 7.00 V [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in: at the higher operating point $V = 7.00\ \text{V}$, $I = 16.25\ \text{mA} = 16.25\times10^{-3}\ \text{A}$.
↳ from STEP 1: $V = 7.00\ \text{V}$, $I = 16.25\times10^{-3}\ \text{A}$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula:

$$R = \dfrac{V}{I}$$

Substitute $V = 7.00\ \text{V}$, $I = 16.25\times10^{-3}\ \text{A}$:

$$R = \dfrac{7.00}{16.25\times10^{-3}} = 431\ \Omega\ (3\ \text{s.f.})$$

Note: the resistance has fallen from $1000\ \Omega$ to $431\ \Omega$ as the voltage (and current, and power) increased.
$R = 431\ \Omega$

Part (c) — explain the change (I = nqvA) [3 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a) & (b): resistance fell from $1000\ \Omega$ to $431\ \Omega$ as the applied p.d. rose from $3.00\ \text{V}$ to $7.00\ \text{V}$.
↳ from STEP 1: $R$ fell $1000 \to 431\ \Omega$ as $V$ (hence power) rose
STEP 1/1 B1 B1 B1

Mark-scheme chain:

1. Higher p.d. dissipates more power $P = IV$, which heats the thermistor.

2. Thermal energy frees more electrons from the lattice, so the number density $n$ increases (approximately exponentially).

3. In $I = nqvA$, the rise in $n$ outweighs the small fall in drift velocity $v$, so $I$ increases for a given $V$ — hence $R = V/I$ decreases.

Mark-scheme keyword: state explicitly that the increase in $n$ dominates over the decrease in $v$. Missing this comparison is the most common reason for losing the third mark.
More power → hotter → $n\uparrow$ → $I\uparrow$ → $R = V/I \downarrow$
