CP7 Q — Measuring procedure, precautions and why a graph is used

Question

A student carries out Core Practical 7 to measure the resistivity of a wire, using a micrometer, a metre ruler, an ammeter, a voltmeter, a power supply and a flying lead.

(a) Outline how the cross-sectional area is found, and why a micrometer is used. [2 marks]
(b) State one precaution involving the current, and explain why it matters. [2 marks]
(c) Explain why a graph of $R$ against $l$ is plotted rather than using a single pair of readings. [2 marks]

[6 marks]

Part (a) — measuring the area [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 B1 B1

Procedure: measure the diameter $d$ with a micrometer at 5–10 positions and orientations along the wire, then average; compute $A = \pi \bar d^2/4$ (with $\bar d$ converted to metres, $\times 10^{-3}$).

Why a micrometer: the wire's diameter is very small, so it needs an instrument of high resolution / small least count ($0.01\ \text{mm}$); a ruler ($1\ \text{mm}$) is far too coarse.

Mark-scheme keywords: "repeat at different positions and orientations to check for non-circularity and reduce random error”; “small diameter needs high-resolution instrument”. Also check for and subtract any zero error.
$A = \pi \bar d^2/4$; micrometer for high resolution on a small $d$

Part (b) — current precaution [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from method: at each length the switch is closed only briefly to read $V$ and $I$, then opened; the current is kept small.
↳ from STEP 1: switch closed only briefly per reading; current kept small
STEP 1/1 B1 B1

Precaution: keep the current small and switch off between readings (only close the circuit briefly to take each $(V, I)$ pair).

Reason: a sustained current heats the wire; since for a metal $T\uparrow \Rightarrow \rho\uparrow$, heating would increase the wire's resistivity and corrupt the measured value of $\rho$.

Both halves needed: the mark requires the heating AND its effect (“changes the resistivity / gives an inaccurate $\rho$”). Stating only “to avoid heating” is incomplete.
Small current / switch off → prevents heating that raises $\rho$

Part (c) — why a graph [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in: the student has $(R, l)$ pairs at several lengths and plots $R$ (y) vs $l$ (x), taking the gradient $= \rho/A$.
↳ from STEP 1: best-fit line through $(R, l)$ points; gradient $= \rho/A$
STEP 1/1 B1 B1

Reason 1 (random error): the best-fit line averages many data points, reducing the random scatter that a single pair of readings would carry in full.

Reason 2 (systematic error): contact resistance adds a constant offset, shifting the line up so it has a non-zero $R$-intercept — but the gradient ($= \rho/A$) is unaffected by a constant offset, so the graphical method eliminates that systematic error.

Diagnostic point: a non-zero intercept is not a mistake to hide — it reveals the contact resistance, and the gradient still gives the correct $\rho$.
Graph averages random error and the gradient cancels contact-resistance offset
