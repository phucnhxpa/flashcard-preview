Superconductor Q — sketch the R–T graph and justify applications

Question

(a) A superconductor is cooled from room temperature. Sketch the shape of its resistance–temperature graph and label the critical temperature $T_c$. Describe the behaviour above, at, and below $T_c$. [3 marks]

(b) Give two applications of superconductors and, for each, state why zero resistance is beneficial. [2 marks]

[5 marks]

Part (a) — R–T sketch and behaviour [3 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 B1 B1 B1

Shape of the graph (see the superconductor curve on the sketch):

• Above $T_c$: the material behaves as a normal conductor — $R$ is roughly constant (or slowly varying) and greater than zero.

• At $T_c$: the resistance drops vertically (discontinuously) as $T$ falls through the critical temperature.

• Below $T_c$: $R = 0$ exactly and stays flat on zero — the line lies along the temperature axis.

Mark-scheme trap: you must say the resistance drops to exactly zero (not “very low”), and the drop happens below $T_c$. State both the what ($\rho = 0$) and the condition (below $T_c$).
flat & $>0$ above $T_c$ → vertical drop at $T_c$ → $R = 0$ exactly below $T_c$

Part (b) — applications and why zero resistance helps [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a): below $T_c$, $\rho = 0$ exactly, so $P = I^2R = 0$ — there is no heating however large the current.
↳ from STEP 1: below $T_c$: $\rho = 0$, so $P = I^2R = 0$ (no heating)
STEP 1/1 B1 B1

Any two applications, each with the zero-resistance benefit:

• Lossless power transmission: $P = I^2R = 0$, so no energy is wasted as heat in the cables.

• MRI / strong electromagnets: superconducting coils carry very large currents with no overheating, producing extremely strong, stable magnetic fields.

• Maglev trains: powerful superconducting magnets levitate the train — frictionless, high-speed travel.

Why zero R matters: every benefit traces back to $P = I^2R = 0$ — no Joule heating, so huge currents flow indefinitely without wasting energy.
e.g. lossless cables + MRI magnets — both rely on $P = I^2R = 0$
