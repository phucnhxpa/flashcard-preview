Lesson Q5a — Resistance of a non-ohmic component at an operating point

Question

The graph shows the $I$–$V$ characteristic of a non-ohmic component. At one operating point the potential difference across it is $V = 0.7\ \text{V}$ and the current through it is $I = 40\ \text{mA}$.

(a) Convert the current to amperes. [1 mark]
(b) Hence calculate the resistance at this operating point. [2 marks]

[3 marks]

Part (a) — convert current [1 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 B1

Prefix conversion (milli):

$$1\ \text{mA} = 10^{-3}\ \text{A}$$

Substitute $I = 40\ \text{mA}$:

$$I = 40 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{A} = 0.040\ \text{A}$$

Prefix trap: milli is $\times 10^{-3}$, not $\times 10^{-6}$ (that is micro, $\mu$). Mixing them up scales the answer by a factor of $1000$.
$I = 40 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{A} = 0.040\ \text{A}$

Part (b) — resistance at the point [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a): current $I = 40 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{A}$; p.d. at this point $V = 0.7\ \text{V}$. The component is non-ohmic, so use the actual $(V, I)$ pair — NOT the gradient of the curve.
↳ from STEP 1: $V = 0.7\ \text{V}$, $I = 40 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{A}$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (definition of resistance):

$$R = \dfrac{V}{I}$$

Substitute $V = 0.7\ \text{V}$, $I = 40 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{A}$:

$$R = \dfrac{0.7}{40 \times 10^{-3}} = 17.5\ \Omega$$

Phuc's verified mistake (lesson 44:01): he first wrote $R = I \times V$ and was corrected by the tutor to $R = V/I$ (“we divide them”). ALWAYS divide, never multiply — $R = VI$ is power, not resistance.
Non-ohmic note: because the graph is a curve, you must read the actual $V$ and $I$ at the point and divide; the gradient of the curve is NOT $1/R$ here.
$R = 17.5\ \Omega$
