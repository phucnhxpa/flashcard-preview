Edexcel IAL Physics · Unit 2 (WPH12) · Potential Dividers · Worked Example 5 (textbook loaded divider)

Loaded divider — unloaded vs loaded output, and the lamp current

Question

A $7.5\ \text{V}$ supply (negligible internal resistance) feeds a divider with $R_1 = 12.0\ \Omega$ (top) and $R_2 = 6.0\ \Omega$ (bottom). The output is taken across $R_2$.

(a) Find $V_{\text{out}}$ with no load. [2 marks]
(b) A lamp of resistance $R_L = 6.0\ \Omega$ is connected across $R_2$. Find the new $V_{\text{out}}$ and the current in the lamp. [4 marks]

[6 marks]

Part (a) — unloaded output [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (work from first principles via the series current):

$$I = \dfrac{V_{\text{in}}}{R_1 + R_2}, \qquad V_{\text{out}} = I R_2$$

Substitute $V_{\text{in}} = 7.5$, $R_1 = 12.0$, $R_2 = 6.0$:

$$I = \dfrac{7.5}{12.0 + 6.0} = \dfrac{7.5}{18.0} = 0.4167\ \text{A}$$

$$V_{\text{out}} = 0.4167 \times 6.0 = 2.5\ \text{V}$$

Tip: the divider formula $V_{\text{out}} = 7.5 \times 6/(12+6) = 2.5\ \text{V}$ gives the same answer; the first-principles route ($I$ then $IR_2$) is what you reuse in part (b).
$V_{\text{out}} = 2.5\ \text{V}$ (unloaded)

Part (b) — loaded output and lamp current [4 marks] · 3 steps

Carry-in from (a): $V_{\text{in}} = 7.5\ \text{V}$, $R_1 = 12.0\ \Omega$, $R_2 = 6.0\ \Omega$; unloaded $V_{\text{out}} = 2.5\ \text{V}$. Now a lamp $R_L = 6.0\ \Omega$ is in parallel with $R_2$.
↳ from STEP 1: $R_2 = 6.0\ \Omega$ in parallel with $R_L = 6.0\ \Omega$
STEP 1/3 M1

Formula (parallel combination of the load and $R_2$):

$$\dfrac{1}{R_{BC}} = \dfrac{1}{R_2} + \dfrac{1}{R_L}$$

Substitute $R_2 = 6.0$, $R_L = 6.0$:

$$\dfrac{1}{R_{BC}} = \dfrac{1}{6.0} + \dfrac{1}{6.0} = \dfrac{2}{6.0} \quad\Rightarrow\quad R_{BC} = 3.0\ \Omega$$

Phuc's slip — the load is in PARALLEL, not series: it is tempting (but wrong) to keep $V_{\text{out}} = 2.5\ \text{V}$. The lamp sits across $R_2$, so the effective bottom resistance drops from $6.0\ \Omega$ to $R_{BC} = 3.0\ \Omega$. Same “which-resistor” care as Circuit F.
$R_{BC} = R_2 \parallel R_L = 3.0\ \Omega$
↳ from STEP 2: $R_{BC} = 3.0\ \Omega$, $R_1 = 12.0\ \Omega$, $V_{\text{in}} = 7.5\ \text{V}$
STEP 2/3 M1 A1

New total resistance and current:

$$R_{\text{total}} = R_1 + R_{BC} = 12.0 + 3.0 = 15.0\ \Omega$$

$$I = \dfrac{V_{\text{in}}}{R_{\text{total}}} = \dfrac{7.5}{15.0} = 0.50\ \text{A}$$

New output (across $R_{BC}$):

$$V_{\text{out}} = I R_{BC} = 0.50 \times 3.0 = 1.5\ \text{V}$$

Effect of loading: the load dropped the output from $2.5\ \text{V}$ to $1.5\ \text{V}$. Lower bottom resistance $\Rightarrow$ more of the supply is dropped across $R_1$, leaving less for the output.
$V_{\text{out}} = 1.5\ \text{V}$ (loaded)
↳ from STEP 3: loaded output $V_{\text{out}} = 1.5\ \text{V}$ is the p.d. across the lamp $R_L = 6.0\ \Omega$
STEP 3/3 A1

Formula (Ohm's law for the lamp):

$$I_L = \dfrac{V_{\text{out}}}{R_L}$$

Substitute $V_{\text{out}} = 1.5\ \text{V}$, $R_L = 6.0\ \Omega$:

$$I_L = \dfrac{1.5}{6.0} = 0.25\ \text{A}$$

Check: the lamp and $R_2$ share the $0.50\ \text{A}$ supply current. Since they are equal ($6.0\ \Omega$ each), each carries $0.25\ \text{A}$  ✓.
$I_L = 0.25\ \text{A}$
