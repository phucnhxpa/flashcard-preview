Self-Test Q3 — Electron-beam current for 10 minutes

Question

An electron-beam experiment runs a current of $I = 1.0\ \mu\text{A}$ for $10\ \text{min}$. Calculate (a) the total charge and (b) the number of electrons. $\;[\,e = 1.60\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}\,]$

(a) [2 marks]   (b) [1 mark]

[3 marks]

Part (a) — total charge [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Strategy: convert the micro-prefix and the minutes to SI units, then apply $\Delta Q = I\,\Delta t$.

Convert: $\mu = 10^{-6}$, so $I = 1.0\ \mu\text{A} = 1.0\times10^{-6}\ \text{A}$; and $\Delta t = 10 \times 60 = 600\ \text{s}$.

Formula: $\;\Delta Q = I\,\Delta t$

Substitute:

$$\Delta Q = 1.0\times10^{-6} \times 600 = 6.0\times10^{-4}\ \text{C}$$

Trap: $\mu = 10^{-6}$, not $10^{-3}$. Confusing micro and milli costs a factor of $1000$. Write the power of ten before substituting.
Sense-check: a microamp is a very small current, so a sub-millicoulomb result is expected.
$\Delta Q = 6.0\times10^{-4}\ \text{C}$

Part (b) — number of electrons [1 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a): total charge $\Delta Q = 6.0\times10^{-4}\ \text{C}$; $e = 1.60\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}$.
↳ from STEP 1: $\Delta Q = 6.0\times10^{-4}\ \text{C}$, $e = 1.60\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}$
STEP 1/1 A1

Formula (charge quantisation, rearranged):

$$Q = N e \quad\Rightarrow\quad N = \dfrac{Q}{e}$$

Substitute $Q = 6.0\times10^{-4}\ \text{C}$ from part (a), $e = 1.60\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}$:

$$N = \dfrac{6.0\times10^{-4}}{1.60\times10^{-19}} = 3.75\times10^{15}\ \text{electrons}$$

Check $e$: the exponent is $-19$. Using $10^{-13}$ would make $N$ wrong by $10^{6}$ — a recorded slip. Use the magnitude of $e$ since you are counting particles.
Cross-check: $1\ \text{C}$ holds about $6.25\times10^{18}$ electrons, so a charge of $6.0\times10^{-4}\ \text{C}$ giving $\sim 10^{15}$ electrons is consistent.
Recall anchor: this two-step pattern (find $Q = I\,\Delta t$, then $N = Q/e$) is the standard route for any ‘how many electrons’ question. The micro-prefix and the wrong-exponent errors for $e$ are the two recorded ways to lose the marks here.
Definition to state: if the same paper asks to define current, the mark-scheme answer is ‘the rate of flow of charge’ — not ‘flow of electrons’, because charge carriers can also be ions.
$N = 3.75\times10^{15}$ electrons

Recall — definitions this question tests 1 step

STEP 1/1

Electric current — the rate of flow of charge (mark-scheme phrasing).

The coulomb — SI unit of charge; $1\ \text{C} = 1\ \text{A}\cdot\text{s}$.

Charge quantisation — $Q = Ne$, $N$ an integer, $e = 1.60\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}$ (memorise the exponent $-19$).

The ampere — SI base unit of current; $1\ \text{A} = 1\ \text{C}\,\text{s}^{-1}$.

Prefix reference: $\text{m}=10^{-3}$, $\mu=10^{-6}$, $\text{n}=10^{-9}$. The micro/milli mix-up is wrong by $\times1000$ — always write the power of ten before substituting.
