Worked Ex 3 — Fuse choice and heating-element resistance (kettle)

Question

An electric kettle is rated $240\ \text{V}$, $3.0\ \text{kW}$.

(a) Calculate the normal operating current and show the kettle operates safely with a $13\ \text{A}$ fuse. [3 marks]
(b) Calculate the resistance of the heating element. [2 marks]

[5 marks]

Part (a) — operating current & fuse choice [3 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2 M1 A1

Formula (operating current from power rating):

$$P = IV \quad\Rightarrow\quad I = \dfrac{P}{V}$$

Substitute $P = 3.0\ \text{kW} = 3000\ \text{W}$, $V = 240\ \text{V}$:

$$I = \dfrac{3000}{240} = 12.5\ \text{A}$$

Rearrangement trap (Marta, Loom e14ebf09): the tutor first wrote $I = V/P$ by mistake before self-correcting to $I = P/V$. Always check: current = power ÷ voltage. A units check confirms it: $\text{A} = \text{W}/\text{V}$.
Prefix: $3.0\ \text{kW} = 3000\ \text{W}$ (kilo $= \times 10^3$) — substitute in watts, not kilowatts.
$I = 12.5\ \text{A}$
↳ from STEP 2: operating current $I = 12.5\ \text{A}$; standard fuses 3 A, 5 A, 13 A
STEP 2/2 B1

Fuse rule: choose the next standard rating above the operating current. Compare $I$ with $13\ \text{A}$:

$$I = 12.5\ \text{A} < 13\ \text{A}$$

So the $13\ \text{A}$ fuse does not blow during normal operation and is the correct choice.

Mark-scheme wording: state the calculated current and the comparison: “$I = 12.5\ \text{A} < 13\ \text{A}$, so the $13\ \text{A}$ fuse is appropriate.” A bare “13 A” with no comparison loses the mark. (Not 3 A — that would blow; not 30 A — too high to protect.)
$12.5\ \text{A} < 13\ \text{A}$ → 13 A fuse is safe ✓

Part (b) — heating-element resistance [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a): $V = 240\ \text{V}$, $P = 3000\ \text{W}$ (and $I = 12.5\ \text{A}$ available if needed).
↳ from STEP 1: $V = 240\ \text{V}$, $P = 3000\ \text{W}$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula selection: $V$ and $P$ are known, so rearrange $P = V^2/R$ for $R$:

$$P = \dfrac{V^2}{R} \quad\Rightarrow\quad R = \dfrac{V^2}{P}$$

Substitute $V = 240\ \text{V}$, $P = 3000\ \text{W}$:

$$R = \dfrac{(240)^2}{3000} = \dfrac{57600}{3000} = 19.2\ \Omega$$

Alternative: $R = V/I = 240/12.5 = 19.2\ \Omega$ using the current from (a) — same answer. Either route is fine; $R = V^2/P$ avoids depending on the part-(a) current.
$R = 19.2\ \Omega$
