Derive — $\dfrac{1}{R_T} = \dfrac{1}{R_1} + \dfrac{1}{R_2} + \cdots$ for parallel resistors

Question

Derive the formula for the total resistance of resistors in parallel, $\dfrac{1}{R_T} = \dfrac{1}{R_1} + \dfrac{1}{R_2} + \cdots$, starting from a conservation principle. (Edexcel IAL spec point 68.)

(a) State the correct starting principle and write the first equation. [2 marks]
(b) Complete the derivation to reach $\dfrac{1}{R_T} = \dfrac{1}{R_1} + \dfrac{1}{R_2}$. [2 marks]

[4 marks]

Part (a) — starting principle and first equation [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 B1 M1

Starting principle: in a parallel circuit, conservation of charge means the currents add. Start from the quantity that adds up.

Formula (Kirchhoff's first law / KCL at the junction):

$$I_{\text{total}} = I_1 + I_2$$

Phuc's mistake (lesson 43:40): he started this proof with $R_1 + R_2$ (series approach) and got stuck. Fix: parallel → current adds → begin with $I_T = I_1 + I_2$ (KCL), not with resistances.
Start: conservation of charge → $I_T = I_1 + I_2$

Part (b) — complete the derivation [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a): $I_T = I_1 + I_2$ (KCL), and in parallel the p.d. is the same across every branch: $V_T = V_1 = V_2 = V$.
↳ from STEP 1: $I_T = I_1 + I_2$; parallel voltage rule $V_1 = V_2 = V$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Substitute Ohm's law in the form $I = V/R$ for each branch into $I_T = I_1 + I_2$:

$$\dfrac{V_T}{R_T} = \dfrac{V_1}{R_1} + \dfrac{V_2}{R_2}$$

Apply the parallel voltage rule $V_T = V_1 = V_2 = V$:

$$\dfrac{V}{R_T} = \dfrac{V}{R_1} + \dfrac{V}{R_2}$$

Divide through by $V$ (Phuc asked 'how do you get the 1?', 52:44 — you divide both sides by $V$):

$$\dfrac{1}{R_T} = \dfrac{1}{R_1} + \dfrac{1}{R_2}$$

Finish correctly: take the reciprocal at the end to get $R_T$. Adding a parallel resistor always decreases $R_T$; the result is less than the smallest branch.
$\dfrac{1}{R_T} = \dfrac{1}{R_1} + \dfrac{1}{R_2}$
