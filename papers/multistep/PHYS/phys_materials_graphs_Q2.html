Self-test: landmarks on a ductile-metal stress-strain curve

Question

The diagram shows the engineering stress-strain curve for a ductile metal loaded to fracture. (a) State what the gradient of the straight part represents and name the point P. (b) Name the points Y, UTS and X and say what happens at each. (c) State what the area under the straight part, and the total area under the whole curve, each represent.

[6 marks]

Part (a) — the linear region and point P [2 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2gradient

Gradient. A stress-strain graph describes the material; the gradient of the straight part is the Young modulus, $E = \sigma/\varepsilon$. This is the only region in which $E = \sigma/\varepsilon$ may be used.

Gradient of the straight part $= E$ (Young modulus).
STEP 2/2point P

Point P. P is the limit of proportionality — the last point on the straight line. Beyond it, stress still rises with strain but no longer in proportion.

P = limit of proportionality.

Part (b) — yield, UTS and fracture [3 marks] · 3 steps

Carry-in: beyond P the curve bends over (non-linear), then yields and rises again to a peak before falling.
STEP 1/3yield Y

Y — yield point. The stress at which the metal suddenly extends a lot for little or no extra load. Atoms slide permanently past one another, so deformation is now plastic.

Y = yield: large plastic extension begins.
STEP 2/3UTS

UTS — ultimate tensile stress. The maximum stress the material withstands, the peak of the curve. Just after it a "neck" forms and the sample thins locally.

UTS = peak stress; necking starts.
STEP 3/3fracture X

X — fracture. Engineering stress uses the fixed original area, so as the neck thins the plotted stress falls from the UTS down to X, where the sample breaks. The strain at X measures the ductility.

X = fracture; strain at X measures ductility.

Part (c) — areas under the curve [1 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in: area under a stress-strain graph is energy per unit volume ($\mathrm{J\,m^{-3}}$).
STEP 1/1two areas

Linear-part area. The area under the straight part is the recoverable elastic strain energy density, $\tfrac12\sigma\varepsilon$.

Whole-curve area. The total area up to fracture is the energy absorbed per unit volume before breaking — the material's toughness.

Linear area $= \tfrac12\sigma\varepsilon$; total area $=$ toughness.
