Q — Output voltage of a thermistor temperature sensor

Question

A thermistor is connected as $R_1$ in a potential divider with a fixed resistor $R_2 = 220\ \Omega$ and supply $V_{in} = 6.0\ \text{V}$. The thermistor resistance is $440\ \Omega$ at $20\,^\circ\text{C}$ and $110\ \Omega$ at $60\,^\circ\text{C}$.

(a) Calculate $V_{out}$ at $20\,^\circ\text{C}$. [2 marks]
(b) Calculate $V_{out}$ at $60\,^\circ\text{C}$. [2 marks]
(c) State and explain the effect of rising temperature on $V_{out}$. [2 marks]

[6 marks]

Part (a) — Vout at 20 °C [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (potential divider, output across $R_2$):

$$V_{out} = V_{in}\cdot\dfrac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$$

Substitute $V_{in} = 6.0$, $R_1 = 440$, $R_2 = 220$:

$$V_{out} = 6.0\times\dfrac{220}{440 + 220} = 6.0\times\dfrac{220}{660} = 6.0\times\tfrac{1}{3} = 2.0\ \text{V}$$

Trap: the output is across $R_2$, so $R_2$ goes on top of the fraction. Putting $R_1$ on top gives the wrong component's voltage.
$V_{out} = 2.0\ \text{V}$

Part (b) — Vout at 60 °C [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in: at $60\,^\circ\text{C}$ the thermistor resistance has fallen to $R_1 = 110\ \Omega$; $R_2 = 220\ \Omega$, $V_{in} = 6.0\ \text{V}$.
↳ from STEP 1: $R_1 = 110\ \Omega$, $R_2 = 220\ \Omega$, $V_{in} = 6.0\ \text{V}$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula:

$$V_{out} = V_{in}\cdot\dfrac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$$

Substitute $R_1 = 110$, $R_2 = 220$:

$$V_{out} = 6.0\times\dfrac{220}{110 + 220} = 6.0\times\dfrac{220}{330} = 6.0\times\tfrac{2}{3} = 4.0\ \text{V}$$

Note: the thermistor resistance fell because the temperature rose ($R$ down as $T$ up — NTC behaviour).
$V_{out} = 4.0\ \text{V}$

Part (c) — effect of rising temperature [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a) & (b): $V_{out}$ rose from $2.0\ \text{V}$ to $4.0\ \text{V}$ as temperature rose from $20\,^\circ\text{C}$ to $60\,^\circ\text{C}$.
↳ from STEP 1: $V_{out}$ rose $2.0 \to 4.0\ \text{V}$
STEP 1/1 B1 B1

Statement: as temperature rises, $V_{out}$ increases.

Explanation: rising temperature lowers the thermistor resistance $R_1$. With $R_2$ fixed, a smaller $R_1$ means $R_2$ takes a larger fraction of $V_{in}$, so $V_{out}$ increases. (First-principles route: $R_{tot}\downarrow$ so current $I\uparrow$, and $V_{out} = IR_2$ increases.)

Trap: the voltage across the thermistor itself falls while $V_{out}$ across $R_2$ rises — opposite directions. Identify which component carries $V_{out}$ before answering.
$V_{out}$ increases (smaller $R_1$ → larger share of $V_{in}$ across $R_2$)
