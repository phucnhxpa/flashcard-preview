Basic divider — output across the bottom resistor

Question

A $12\ \text{V}$ supply is connected to a series combination of $R_1 = 2.0\ \text{k}\Omega$ (top) and $R_2 = 4.0\ \text{k}\Omega$ (bottom).

(a) Find the output p.d. $V_{\text{out}}$ across $R_2$. [2 marks]
(b) Find the p.d. $V_1$ across $R_1$, and verify the ratio $V_1/V_2 = R_1/R_2$. [2 marks]

[4 marks]

Part (a) — output across R₂ [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (potential-divider, output across $R_2$):

$$V_{\text{out}} = V_{\text{in}} \cdot \dfrac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$$

Substitute $V_{\text{in}} = 12\ \text{V}$, $R_1 = 2.0\ \text{k}\Omega$, $R_2 = 4.0\ \text{k}\Omega$:

$$V_{\text{out}} = 12 \times \dfrac{4.0}{2.0 + 4.0} = 12 \times \dfrac{4}{6} = 8.0\ \text{V}$$

Phuc's slip — which resistor goes on top? The numerator is the resistor whose voltage you want ($R_2$ here, because the output is across it). In Circuit B Phuc put $4/6$ correctly, but in Circuit F he tapped the wrong resistor. Always decide which resistor the output is across before substituting. The k$\Omega$ cancel top and bottom, so no unit conversion is needed.
$V_{\text{out}} = 8.0\ \text{V}$

Part (b) — V₁ and the ratio check [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a): $V_{\text{in}} = 12\ \text{V}$, $R_1 = 2.0\ \text{k}\Omega$, $R_2 = 4.0\ \text{k}\Omega$, and the output $V_2 = V_{\text{out}} = 8.0\ \text{V}$ across $R_2$.
↳ from STEP 1: $V_{\text{in}} = 12\ \text{V}$, $V_2 = 8.0\ \text{V}$, $R_1 = 2.0\ \text{k}\Omega$, $R_2 = 4.0\ \text{k}\Omega$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (Kirchhoff's voltage law for the loop):

$$V_1 + V_2 = V_{\text{in}} \quad\Rightarrow\quad V_1 = V_{\text{in}} - V_2$$

Substitute $V_{\text{in}} = 12\ \text{V}$, $V_2 = 8.0\ \text{V}$:

$$V_1 = 12 - 8.0 = 4.0\ \text{V}$$

Ratio check $V_1/V_2$ against $R_1/R_2$:

$$\dfrac{V_1}{V_2} = \dfrac{4.0}{8.0} = \dfrac{1}{2}, \qquad \dfrac{R_1}{R_2} = \dfrac{2.0}{4.0} = \dfrac{1}{2} \;\checkmark$$

Why this works: the current $I$ is the same through both resistors in series, so $V = IR$ gives $V \propto R$. The bigger resistor takes the bigger share of the supply p.d.
$V_1 = 4.0\ \text{V}$; $V_1/V_2 = R_1/R_2 = 1/2$ ✓
