CP7 Q — what to plot, and reading ρ off the graph

Question

In Core Practical 7 a student measures the resistance $R$ of a metal wire for several lengths $L$ (using $R = V/I$ from a voltmeter–ammeter circuit) to find the resistivity $\rho$. The wire has uniform cross-sectional area $A$.

(a) State what should be plotted and what the gradient represents. [2 marks]
(b) The gradient of the $R$–$L$ line is $4.0\ \Omega\,\text{m}^{-1}$ and the wire's cross-sectional area is $A = 1.2 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m}^2$. Calculate $\rho$. [2 marks]
(c) State one key precaution and explain why it matters. [2 marks]

[6 marks]

Part (a) — what to plot [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Start from the resistivity formula:

$$R = \dfrac{\rho L}{A} = \left(\dfrac{\rho}{A}\right) L$$

This is linear in $L$ (form $y = mx$), so plot $R$ (y-axis) against $L$ (x-axis). The line passes through the origin with:

$$\text{gradient} = \dfrac{\rho}{A}$$

Why a graph: using many lengths and a best-fit line averages out random error far better than a single $R = \rho L/A$ calculation.
plot $R$ vs $L$; gradient $= \rho/A$

Part (b) — calculate ρ from the gradient [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a): gradient $= \rho/A$. Measured gradient $= 4.0\ \Omega\,\text{m}^{-1}$; $A = 1.2 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m}^2$.
↳ from STEP 1: gradient $= \rho/A = 4.0\ \Omega\,\text{m}^{-1}$, $A = 1.2\times10^{-7}\ \text{m}^2$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Rearrange gradient $= \rho/A$ for $\rho$:

$$\rho = \text{gradient} \times A$$

Substitute the values:

$$\rho = 4.0 \times (1.2 \times 10^{-7}) = 4.8 \times 10^{-7}\ \Omega\,\text{m}$$

Unit trap: $\rho$ is in $\Omega\,\text{m}$ (ohm-metres), not $\Omega/\text{m}$. The gradient $\rho/A$ has units $\Omega\,\text{m}/\text{m}^2 = \Omega\,\text{m}^{-1}$ ✓.
$\rho = 4.8 \times 10^{-7}\ \Omega\,\text{m}$

Part (c) — precaution [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in: $R$ is measured as $V/I$ for each length while the current passes through the wire.
↳ from STEP 1: $R = V/I$ measured for each length; current heats the wire
STEP 1/1 B1 B1

Precaution: keep the current low (e.g. $< 0.5\ \text{A}$), or switch off between readings.

Reason: a sustained current heats the wire, raising its temperature; for a metal this increases $\rho$ (PTC), so $R$ would drift upward during the experiment — a systematic error that bends the $R$–$L$ line.

Other accepted answers: measure the diameter $d$ at several positions with a micrometer and average (the wire is not perfectly uniform; $A \propto d^2$); ensure firm crocodile-clip contact to cut contact resistance; zero the micrometer before use.
keep current low → avoid heating → $\rho$ (hence $R$) stays constant
