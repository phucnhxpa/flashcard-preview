Non-ohmic Q — resistance from a curve, and a target-R p.d.

Question

The $I$–$V$ characteristic of a non-ohmic component is a curve. At one point on the curve the current is $I = 40\ \text{mA}$ when the p.d. is $V = 0.70\ \text{V}$.

(a) Calculate the resistance of the component at this point. [2 marks]
(b) Find the p.d. at which the component's resistance would equal $50\ \Omega$, using a graphical method. [3 marks]

[5 marks]

Part (a) — resistance at a point on the curve [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (resistance at a point):

$$R = \dfrac{V}{I}$$

Substitute $V = 0.70\ \text{V}$, $I = 40\ \text{mA} = 40 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{A}$:

$$R = \dfrac{0.70}{40 \times 10^{-3}} = 17.5\ \Omega$$

Curve trap: you may NOT use the gradient of a non-ohmic curve. Read $V$ and $I$ at the point and divide. Prefix trap: $40\ \text{mA} = 40\times10^{-3}\ \text{A}$, NOT $40\times10^{-6}\ \text{A}$ (that would be micro, $\mu$).
$R = 17.5\ \Omega$

Part (b) — graphical method for a target resistance [3 marks] · 2 steps

Carry-in from (a): the component is non-ohmic — $I$ and $V$ change together along the curve, so the $40\ \text{mA}$ from (a) does NOT apply here. Target resistance $R = 50\ \Omega$.
↳ from STEP 1: component is non-ohmic; target $R = 50\ \Omega$ (current is unknown)
STEP 1/2 M1

Idea: a constant resistance $R = 50\ \Omega$ is a straight line through the origin on the $I$–$V$ graph, with

$$\text{gradient} = \dfrac{I}{V} = \dfrac{1}{R} = \dfrac{1}{50}.$$

Find one point on this line by picking a test p.d. $V = 0.60\ \text{V}$:

$$I = \dfrac{V}{R} = \dfrac{0.60}{50} = 0.012\ \text{A} = 12\ \text{mA}$$

Phuc's slip (lesson 52:21): he tried to “just multiply $R \times I$” using the old $40\ \text{mA}$. That fails — the current here is unknown. The straight line fixes the $(V, I)$ pairs that give $50\ \Omega$.
point on the $50\ \Omega$ line: $(0.60\ \text{V},\ 12\ \text{mA})$
↳ from STEP 2: line through origin and $(0.60\ \text{V},\ 12\ \text{mA})$
STEP 2/2 A1

Draw a straight line from the origin through $(0.60\ \text{V},\ 12\ \text{mA})$. Where it intersects the component's curve, both the component equation and $R = 50\ \Omega$ are satisfied simultaneously.

From the graph, the intersection occurs at:

$$V \approx 0.60\ \text{V}$$

Why graphical: with two unknowns ($V$ and $I$) and one curved relationship, the intersection of the curve with the $R = 50\ \Omega$ line gives the unique operating point.
p.d. $\approx 0.60\ \text{V}$
