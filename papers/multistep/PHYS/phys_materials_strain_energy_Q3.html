Elastic strain energy stored in a stretched wire

Question

A copper wire is stretched within its elastic limit by a force of 160 N, producing an extension of 2.7 cm. (a) Calculate the elastic strain energy stored in the wire. (b) The same wire is then loaded to twice the extension (still elastic). State, with a reason, how the stored energy changes.

[4 marks]

Part (a) — energy stored at 2.7 cm [2 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2convert

Convert the extension.

$$x = 2.7\,\mathrm{cm} = 2.7\times10^{-2}\,\mathrm{m} = 0.027\,\mathrm{m}$$
$x = 0.027\,\mathrm{m}$
↳ from STEP 2: $F = 160\,\mathrm{N},\; x = 0.027\,\mathrm{m}$
STEP 2/2energy

Formula. Within the elastic limit, $E_e = \tfrac12 Fx$.

$$E_e = \tfrac12 (160)(0.027) = 2.16\,\mathrm{J}$$
$E_e = 2.16\,\mathrm{J}$

Part (b) — effect of doubling the extension [2 marks] · 2 steps

Carry-in: Hooke region, so $E_e = \tfrac12 kx^2$ — energy depends on $x^2$.
STEP 1/2proportional reasoning

Idea. In the Hooke region $E_e = \tfrac12 kx^2$, so energy is proportional to $x^2$. Doubling $x$ multiplies the energy by $2^2 = 4$.

Energy scales with $x^2$.
↳ from STEP 2: $E_e \propto x^2$, original $E_e = 2.16\,\mathrm{J}$
STEP 2/2new value

New stored energy.

$$E_e' = 4 \times 2.16 = 8.64\,\mathrm{J}$$
Energy quadruples to $8.64\,\mathrm{J}$.
