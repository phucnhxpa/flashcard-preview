Elastic energy per unit volume as ½ × stress × strain

Question

A wire of cross-sectional area $A$ and length $L$ stores elastic strain energy $E_e = \tfrac12 Fx$. Show that the elastic energy stored per unit volume is $u = \tfrac12\sigma\varepsilon$, and state two equivalent forms using the Young modulus.

[3 marks]

Derive the elastic energy density [3 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3divide by volume

Idea. Divide the stored energy by the volume of the wire, $V = AL$.

$$u = \frac{E_e}{V} = \frac{\tfrac12 Fx}{AL}$$
$u = \dfrac{\tfrac12 Fx}{AL}$
↳ from STEP 2: $u = \dfrac{\tfrac12 Fx}{AL}$; group $F/A$ and $x/L$
STEP 2/3regroup into σ and ε

Regroup. Split the fraction so the stress $\sigma = F/A$ and strain $\varepsilon = x/L$ appear:

$$u = \tfrac12\left(\frac{F}{A}\right)\!\left(\frac{x}{L}\right) = \tfrac12\,\sigma\varepsilon$$
$u = \tfrac12\,\sigma\varepsilon$, unit $\mathrm{J\,m^{-3}}$
↳ from STEP 3: $u = \tfrac12\sigma\varepsilon$ and $\sigma = E\varepsilon$
STEP 3/3Young-modulus forms

Equivalent forms. Substitute $\sigma = E\varepsilon$ (or $\varepsilon = \sigma/E$):

$$u = \tfrac12 E\varepsilon^2 = \frac{\sigma^2}{2E}$$

This is the area under the stress-strain graph, mirroring $E_e$ under the force-extension graph.

Like the Young modulus, energy density is a property of the material and its strain state, not of the sample size — the geometry ($A$ and $L$) cancels out.
$u = \tfrac12 E\varepsilon^2 = \dfrac{\sigma^2}{2E}$
