Worked Example 2 — Number of electrons in an electron beam

Question

An electron beam carries a current of $I = 1.0\ \mu\text{A}$ for $10\ \text{min}$. Calculate the number of electrons that pass. $\;[\,e = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}\,]$

[3 marks]

Solution [3 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3 B1

Strategy: two conversions are needed before any substitution — the micro-prefix on the current and the minutes on the time.

Convert the prefix: micro means $\times 10^{-6}$, so

$$I = 1.0\ \mu\text{A} = 1.0 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{A}$$

Convert the time: $1\ \text{min} = 60\ \text{s}$, so

$$\Delta t = 10 \times 60 = 600\ \text{s}$$

Phuc's slip (lesson video, 26 May): he stated $\mu\text{A} = 10^{-3}\ \text{A}$. Alex corrected: $\mu = 10^{-6}$, $\text{m} = 10^{-3}$ — confusing them is wrong by $\times 1000$. Write the power of ten explicitly first, then substitute.
$I = 1.0\times10^{-6}\ \text{A}$, $\Delta t = 600\ \text{s}$
↳ from STEP 2: $I = 1.0\times10^{-6}\ \text{A}$, $\Delta t = 600\ \text{s}$
STEP 2/3 M1

Formula (charge from current and time):

$$I = \dfrac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \Delta Q = I\,\Delta t$$

Substitute the converted values $I = 1.0\times10^{-6}\ \text{A}$, $\Delta t = 600\ \text{s}$:

$$\Delta Q = 1.0\times10^{-6} \times 600 = 6.0\times10^{-4}\ \text{C}$$

Method: find the charge $Q$ first, then count electrons in the next step — the two operations are separate recall units and combining them invites slips.
Sense-check: a microamp is a tiny current, so well under a millicoulomb of charge ($6.0\times10^{-4}\ \text{C}$) over ten minutes is reasonable.
$\Delta Q = 6.0\times10^{-4}\ \text{C}$
↳ from STEP 3: $\Delta Q = 6.0\times10^{-4}\ \text{C}$, $e = 1.60\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}$
STEP 3/3 M1 A1

Formula (charge quantisation, rearranged):

$$Q = N e \quad\Rightarrow\quad N = \dfrac{Q}{e}$$

Substitute $Q = 6.0\times10^{-4}\ \text{C}$, $e = 1.60\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}$:

$$N = \dfrac{6.0\times10^{-4}}{1.60\times10^{-19}} = 3.75\times10^{15}\ \text{electrons}$$

Phuc's slip (lesson video): he recalled $e = 1.6\times10^{-13}\ \text{C}$ — wrong exponent makes $N$ out by $10^{6}$. Memorise $e = 1.60\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}$; use its magnitude (the sign only encodes direction).
Recall anchor: the count is a pure number (no unit). Charge is quantised — it only ever occurs as an integer multiple of $e$, which is exactly why $N = Q/e$ must come out as a whole-number-scale quantity. Order-of-magnitude check: $1\ \text{C}$ $\approx 6.25\times10^{18}$ electrons.
$N = 3.75\times10^{15}$ electrons

Recall — definitions this question tests 1 step

STEP 1/1

Electric current — the rate of flow of charge (mark-scheme: ‘rate of flow of charge’, not just ‘flow of electrons’, since carriers can be ions).

The ampere — SI base unit of current; $1\ \text{A} = 1\ \text{C}\,\text{s}^{-1}$.

Charge quantisation — $Q = Ne$, $N$ an integer, $e = 1.60\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}$.

Prefix reference: $\text{m}=10^{-3}$, $\mu=10^{-6}$, $\text{n}=10^{-9}$. The micro/milli mix-up is the single most common slip on this question type.
