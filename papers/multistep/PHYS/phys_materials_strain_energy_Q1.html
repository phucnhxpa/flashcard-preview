Deriving the elastic strain energy of a stretched spring

Question

A spring obeying Hooke's law is stretched from zero to extension $x$ by a force that grows from 0 to $F$. (a) Show, using the area under the force-extension graph, that the elastic strain energy stored is $E_e = \tfrac12 Fx$. (b) Hence show this equals $\tfrac12 kx^2$, and state why the factor $\tfrac12$ must not be dropped.

[4 marks]

Part (a) — area under the force-extension line [2 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2why area

Idea. Work done is force times distance, but here the force is not constant — it grows from 0 to $F$ as the extension grows from 0 to $x$. The work done is therefore the area under the force-extension graph.

Energy stored = area under the $F$–$x$ graph.
STEP 2/2triangle

Triangular area. For the straight Hooke line that area is a triangle of base $x$ and height $F$:

$$E_e = \tfrac12 (\text{base})(\text{height}) = \tfrac12 x F$$
$E_e = \tfrac12 Fx$

Part (b) — convert to ½kx² and justify the half [2 marks] · 2 steps

Carry-in: from (a) $E_e = \tfrac12 Fx$, and Hooke's law $F = kx$.
STEP 1/2substitute F = kx

Substitute Hooke's law.

$$E_e = \tfrac12 Fx = \tfrac12 (kx)x = \tfrac12 k x^2$$

The integral form gives the same result: $E_e = \int_0^{x} kx'\,\mathrm{d}x' = \tfrac12 kx^2$.

$E_e = \tfrac12 kx^2$
STEP 2/2why the half

Why keep the half. The average force during the stretch is $\tfrac12 F$, not $F$. The product $Fx$ with no half is the rectangle — the work a constant force $F$ would do — which is twice too big.

Common trap: writing $Fx$ for energy stored. For a curved graph (beyond the limit of proportionality, or rubber) $\tfrac12 Fx$ no longer holds — use the actual area under the curve.
Half because the average force is $\tfrac12 F$.
