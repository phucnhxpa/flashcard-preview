Temperature Q — why metal R rises, and a cooled thermistor's effect

Question

(a) Explain, in terms of electron–ion interactions, why the resistance of a copper wire increases as its temperature rises. [3 marks]

(b) An NTC thermistor is in series with a fixed $100\ \Omega$ resistor and a battery. The thermistor is cooled. State and explain what happens to (i) the total resistance of the circuit and (ii) the current. [3 marks]

[6 marks]

Part (a) — metal mechanism (PTC) [3 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 B1 B1 B1

Mechanism (free-electron model), in the mark-scheme chain:

• Higher temperature ⇒ greater amplitude of vibration of the lattice ions.

• ⇒ more frequent collisions between the conduction electrons and the ions.

• ⇒ reduced drift velocity $v$ in $I = nAvq$ (with $n$, $A$, $q$ constant) ⇒ smaller $I$ for the same $V$ ⇒ from $R = V/I$, resistance increases.

Metal trap: for a metal the carrier number $n$ is constant — only the drift velocity falls. Do NOT say “fewer carriers” or “more carriers” for a metal; that is the semiconductor argument.
more lattice vibration → more collisions → lower $v$ → $R$ rises (PTC)

Part (b) — cooled NTC thermistor (i) total R, (ii) current [3 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in: NTC means resistance rises when cooled; the thermistor is in series with a fixed $100\ \Omega$ resistor and a battery of fixed e.m.f. In series the current is the same everywhere.
↳ from STEP 1: NTC cooled ⇒ thermistor resistance increases; series circuit, fixed e.m.f.
STEP 1/1 M1 M1 A1

(i) Total resistance: cooling an NTC thermistor increases its resistance (fewer free charge carriers). In series, $R_{\text{total}} = R_{\text{thermistor}} + 100\ \Omega$, so the total resistance increases.

(ii) Current: with the e.m.f. fixed, by $I = \varepsilon/R_{\text{total}}$ a larger total resistance gives a smaller current — the current decreases.

NTC trap: the reason is carrier number (“fewer free charge carriers released” when cooled), NOT collision frequency. NTC behaves opposite to a metal — this opposite sign is the #1 mix-up.
(i) total $R$ increases; (ii) current decreases
