Worked Example 5 — Conductor carrying 40 mA

Question

A conductor carries a current of $40\ \text{mA}$.

(a) Find the total charge in $3\ \text{min}$. [2 marks]
(b) Find the number of electron charge carriers. [1 mark]
(c) If $8.6\ \text{J}$ are transferred, find the potential difference. [2 marks]
(d) Find the resistance of the conductor. [2 marks]

[7 marks]

Part (a) — total charge [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Convert prefix and time: $I = 40\ \text{mA} = 40\times10^{-3}\ \text{A}$, $\Delta t = 3 \times 60 = 180\ \text{s}$.

Formula: $\;\Delta Q = I\,\Delta t$

Substitute:

$$\Delta Q = 40\times10^{-3} \times 180 = 0.72\ \text{C}$$

Trap: convert $\text{mA}\to\text{A}$ ($\text{m}=10^{-3}$) and $\text{min}\to\text{s}$ before substituting.
$\Delta Q = 0.72\ \text{C}$

Part (b) — number of electrons [1 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a): total charge $\Delta Q = 0.72\ \text{C}$; $e = 1.60\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}$.
↳ from STEP 1: $\Delta Q = 0.72\ \text{C}$, $e = 1.60\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}$
STEP 1/1 A1

Formula (charge quantisation):

$$N = \dfrac{Q}{e}$$

Substitute $Q = 0.72\ \text{C}$, $e = 1.60\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}$:

$$N = \dfrac{0.72}{1.60\times10^{-19}} = 4.5\times10^{18}$$

Check: $1\ \text{C}$ holds $\approx 6.25\times10^{18}$ electrons, so $0.72\ \text{C}$ giving $4.5\times10^{18}$ is the right order of magnitude.
$N = 4.5\times10^{18}$ electrons

Part (c) — potential difference [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a): charge $\Delta Q = 0.72\ \text{C}$; energy transferred $W = 8.6\ \text{J}$.
↳ from STEP 1: $Q = 0.72\ \text{C}$, $W = 8.6\ \text{J}$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (definition of p.d.):

$$V = \dfrac{W}{Q}$$

Substitute $W = 8.6\ \text{J}$, $Q = 0.72\ \text{C}$:

$$V = \dfrac{8.6}{0.72} = 11.9\ \text{V} \approx 12\ \text{V}$$

Trap: p.d. is $V = W/Q$, not $V = WQ$. The charge $Q$ here is the value from part (a), not a new quantity.
$V \approx 12\ \text{V}$

Part (d) — resistance [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a) & (c): current $I = 40\times10^{-3}\ \text{A}$; potential difference $V \approx 12\ \text{V}$.
↳ from STEP 1: $V = 12\ \text{V}$, $I = 40\times10^{-3}\ \text{A}$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (Ohm's law, rearranged):

$$V = IR \quad\Rightarrow\quad R = \dfrac{V}{I}$$

Substitute $V = 12\ \text{V}$, $I = 40\times10^{-3}\ \text{A}$:

$$R = \dfrac{12}{40\times10^{-3}} = 300\ \Omega$$

Trap: use $R = V/I$, not $R = IV$. Keep $I$ in amperes ($0.040\ \text{A}$) — using $40$ would give $R$ out by $\times 1000$.
$R = 300\ \Omega$
