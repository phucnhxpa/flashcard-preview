Scaling Q — New resistance when length and diameter both halve

Question

A wire has resistance $R = 43\ \Omega$. Its length $L$ and its diameter $D$ are both halved (same material, same temperature).

(a) State how the cross-sectional area changes when the diameter is halved. [1 mark]
(b) Hence find the new resistance. [2 marks]
(c) A different sample of the same wire is instead stretched so its length doubles while its volume stays constant. Find the new resistance of that sample. [3 marks]

[6 marks]

Part (a) — area scaling [1 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 B1

Relation (area depends on diameter squared):

$$A = \dfrac{\pi d^2}{4} \quad\Rightarrow\quad A \propto d^2$$

Halving $d$: $\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^2 = \tfrac{1}{4}$, so

$$A_{\text{new}} = \dfrac{A}{4}$$

Key trap: track $d^2$, not $d$. Halving the diameter quarters the area (a common SaveMyExams exam point).
$A_{\text{new}} = A/4$ (quartered)

Part (b) — new resistance [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a): halving $D$ gives $A_{\text{new}} = A/4$. Length is also halved: $L_{\text{new}} = L/2$. Original $R = 43\ \Omega$, same $\rho$.
↳ from STEP 1: $A_{\text{new}} = A/4$, $L_{\text{new}} = L/2$, $R = 43\ \Omega$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (resistivity definition):

$$R = \dfrac{\rho L}{A}$$

Apply both changes ($L \to L/2$, $A \to A/4$):

$$R_{\text{new}} = \dfrac{\rho (L/2)}{A/4} = \dfrac{\rho L}{A} \times \dfrac{1/2}{1/4} = 2 \times \dfrac{\rho L}{A} = 2R$$

$$R_{\text{new}} = 2 \times 43 = 86\ \Omega$$

Net effect: halving $L$ halves $R$ ($\times\tfrac12$); halving $D$ quarters $A$, which multiplies $R$ by $4$. Net $\tfrac12 \times 4 = 2$, so the resistance doubles.
$R_{\text{new}} = 86\ \Omega$

Part (c) — stretched at constant volume [3 marks] · 2 steps

Carry-in: original sample $R = 43\ \Omega$, same material. Now stretched so $L \to 2L$ with volume $V = AL$ held constant.
STEP 1/2 M1

Constant volume condition:

$$V = A L = \text{constant} \quad\Rightarrow\quad A_{\text{new}} L_{\text{new}} = A L$$

With $L_{\text{new}} = 2L$:

$$A_{\text{new}} = \dfrac{A L}{2L} = \dfrac{A}{2}$$

Different from part (b): here the wire is stretched, so length and area are linked by constant volume — doubling the length halves the area (you cannot choose them independently).
$L_{\text{new}} = 2L$, $A_{\text{new}} = A/2$
↳ from STEP 2: $L_{\text{new}} = 2L$, $A_{\text{new}} = A/2$, original $R = 43\ \Omega$
STEP 2/2 M1 A1

Formula:

$$R = \dfrac{\rho L}{A}$$

Apply both changes ($L \to 2L$, $A \to A/2$):

$$R_{\text{new}} = \dfrac{\rho (2L)}{A/2} = 4 \times \dfrac{\rho L}{A} = 4R$$

$$R_{\text{new}} = 4 \times 43 = 172\ \Omega$$

Stretching rule: at constant volume, $R \propto L^2$, so doubling the length multiplies $R$ by $4$. A favourite exam twist on the basic scaling.
$R_{\text{new}} = 172\ \Omega$
