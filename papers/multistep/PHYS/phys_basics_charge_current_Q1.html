Worked Example 1 — Charge passing a point in a wire

Question

A wire carries a current of $I = 0.5\ \text{A}$. Calculate the charge that passes a point in the wire in $5\ \text{min}\ 30\ \text{s}$.

[3 marks]

Solution [3 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2 M1

Strategy: the formula $Q = I\,\Delta t$ requires time in seconds, so the very first move is a units conversion.

Convert the time to seconds: the duration is given as $5$ minutes plus $30$ seconds, and $1\ \text{min} = 60\ \text{s}$, so

$$\Delta t = (5 \times 60) + 30 = 300 + 30 = 330\ \text{s}$$

Common mistake: forgetting to convert minutes to seconds before using $Q = I\,\Delta t$. The equation only holds with time in seconds because the ampere is defined as $1\ \text{A} = 1\ \text{C}\,\text{s}^{-1}$.
$\Delta t = 330\ \text{s}$
↳ from STEP 2: $\Delta t = 330\ \text{s}$, $I = 0.5\ \text{A}$
STEP 2/2 M1 A1

Formula (electric current is the rate of flow of charge):

$$I = \dfrac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \Delta Q = I\,\Delta t$$

Substitute $I = 0.5\ \text{A}$ and the converted $\Delta t = 330\ \text{s}$:

$$\Delta Q = 0.5 \times 330 = 165\ \text{C}$$

Trap (Phuc, lesson video): the relation is $I = Q/t$, so $Q = It$ (multiply). Writing $I = Qt$ is a recorded slip. Recover the correct form from the unit: $\text{A} = \text{C}/\text{s}$, hence $\text{C} = \text{A}\cdot\text{s}$.
Sense-check: half an amp for over five minutes is a sizeable amount of charge, so $165\ \text{C}$ (over a hundred coulombs) is a reasonable magnitude.
Definition to state: if asked to define current, the mark-scheme answer is ‘the rate of flow of charge’ — the coulomb is the SI unit of charge and $1\ \text{A} = 1\ \text{C}\,\text{s}^{-1}$.
$\Delta Q = 165\ \text{C}$
