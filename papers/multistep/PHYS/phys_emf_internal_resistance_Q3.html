Switch Q — Internal resistance from two voltmeter readings

Question

A high-resistance voltmeter connected across a cell reads $1.55\ \text{V}$ with the switch open, and $1.49\ \text{V}$ with the switch closed (connecting a $10\ \Omega$ resistor).

(a) State the e.m.f. of the cell, with reason. [1 mark]
(b) Find the current when the switch is closed. [1 mark]
(c) Hence find the internal resistance $r$. [2 marks]

[4 marks]

Part (a) — e.m.f. from open-circuit reading [1 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 B1

Principle (open circuit):

$$I = 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad V_r = Ir = 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad V = \varepsilon$$

With the switch open, the high-resistance voltmeter draws negligible current, so there are no lost volts and the reading equals the e.m.f.:

$$\varepsilon = 1.55\ \text{V}$$

Mark-scheme wording: “With the switch open, negligible current flows so the terminal p.d. equals the e.m.f.”
$\varepsilon = 1.55\ \text{V}$

Part (b) — closed-circuit current [1 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a): $\varepsilon = 1.55\ \text{V}$. With the switch closed, terminal p.d. $V = 1.49\ \text{V}$ across the $10\ \Omega$ resistor.
↳ from STEP 1: closed-circuit terminal p.d. $V = 1.49\ \text{V}$ across $R = 10\ \Omega$
STEP 1/1 M1

Formula (Ohm's law for the load):

$$I = \dfrac{V}{R}$$

Substitute $V = 1.49\ \text{V}$, $R = 10\ \Omega$:

$$I = \dfrac{1.49}{10} = 0.149\ \text{A}$$

Trap: use the closed-switch reading $1.49\ \text{V}$ here (the p.d. actually across $R$), not the e.m.f. $1.55\ \text{V}$.
$I = 0.149\ \text{A}$

Part (c) — internal resistance [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a) & (b): $\varepsilon = 1.55\ \text{V}$, closed-circuit terminal p.d. $V = 1.49\ \text{V}$, current $I = 0.149\ \text{A}$.
↳ from STEP 1: $\varepsilon = 1.55\ \text{V}$, $V = 1.49\ \text{V}$, $I = 0.149\ \text{A}$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (rearrange $V = \varepsilon - Ir$):

$$r = \dfrac{\varepsilon - V}{I}$$

Lost volts $= \varepsilon - V = 1.55 - 1.49 = 0.06\ \text{V}$.

Substitute $I = 0.149\ \text{A}$:

$$r = \dfrac{0.06}{0.149} = 0.40\ \Omega\ (2\ \text{s.f.})$$

Method choice: $r = (\varepsilon - V)/I$ is the most direct route — avoid roundabout methods. The two readings give the lost volts directly.
$r = 0.40\ \Omega$
