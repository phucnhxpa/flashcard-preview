Q9 — Core Practical 4: measuring viscosity by the falling-ball method

Question

A ball bearing falls through a tall cylinder of liquid and reaches terminal velocity. Its fall is timed between two marks.

(a) Explain why the ball is timed between two marks below the surface, not from release.

(b) Several ball radii are used and $v_t$ is plotted against $r^2$, giving a straight line through the origin. State how $\eta$ is found from the gradient.

(c) For steel balls ($\rho_s = 7800\ \text{kg m}^{-3}$) in an oil ($\rho_f = 900\ \text{kg m}^{-3}$) the gradient is $k = 1.5\times10^{4}\ \text{m}^{-1}\text{s}^{-1}$. Find $\eta$.

[7 marks]

Part (a) — why time between two marks [3 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3 B1

From release the ball accelerates: drag must grow until it balances weight (and upthrust). A short distance is needed for this.

STEP 2/3 B1

Between the two marks the ball moves at constant terminal velocity, so $v = s/t$ is genuinely the $v_t$ required by the equation.

STEP 3/3 B1

Timing from release would include the speeding-up phase, giving too large a $t$ for that $s$, underestimating $v$ and so overestimating $\eta$.

Constant v_t only between the marks ⇒ valid $v=s/t$

Part (b) — η from the gradient [2 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2 M1

From $v_t = \dfrac{2r^2 g(\rho_s-\rho_f)}{9\eta}$, write $v_t$ as a straight line in $r^2$:

$$v_t = \underbrace{\frac{2(\rho_s-\rho_f)g}{9\eta}}_{\text{gradient }k}\;r^2$$

STEP 2/2 A1

So the gradient is $k = \dfrac{2(\rho_s-\rho_f)g}{9\eta}$; rearrange:

$$\eta = \frac{2(\rho_s-\rho_f)g}{9k}$$

A line through the origin also shows the flow stayed laminar across the range of balls; curvature at large $r$ would warn of turbulence.
$\eta = \dfrac{2(\rho_s-\rho_f)g}{9k}$

Part (c) — evaluate η [2 marks] · 2 steps

Carry-in from (b): $\eta = \dfrac{2(\rho_s-\rho_f)g}{9k}$; $\rho_s-\rho_f = 6900$, $g=9.81$, $k=1.5\times10^{4}$.
STEP 1/2 M1

Substitute:

$$\eta = \frac{2(6900)(9.81)}{9(1.5\times10^{4})} = \frac{1.354\times10^{5}}{1.35\times10^{5}}$$

STEP 2/2 A1

$$\eta = 1.0\ \text{Pa s}$$

$\eta = 1.0\ \text{Pa s}$
