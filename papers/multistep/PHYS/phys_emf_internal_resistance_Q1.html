Lesson Q — Find lost volts, current and internal resistance

Question

A battery has e.m.f. $\varepsilon = 12.5\ \text{V}$ and is connected to a single external resistor $R = 20.0\ \Omega$. The terminal p.d. across the battery is measured as $V = 12.0\ \text{V}$.

(a) Find the lost volts $V_r$. [1 mark]
(b) Find the current $I$ in the circuit. [2 marks]
(c) Hence find the internal resistance $r$. [2 marks]

[5 marks]

Part (a) — lost volts [1 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 B1

Formula (Kirchhoff's voltage law for the loop):

$$\varepsilon = V + V_r \quad\Rightarrow\quad V_r = \varepsilon - V$$

Substitute $\varepsilon = 12.5\ \text{V}$, $V = 12.0\ \text{V}$:

$$V_r = 12.5 - 12.0 = 0.5\ \text{V}$$

Sign trap: lost volts are subtracted ($V = \varepsilon - Ir$). Writing $V = \varepsilon + Ir$ is a classic error — internal resistance can only reduce the terminal p.d.
$V_r = 0.5\ \text{V}$

Part (b) — current [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a): $\varepsilon = 12.5\ \text{V}$, $V = 12.0\ \text{V}$, lost volts $V_r = 0.5\ \text{V}$. The terminal p.d. $V = 12.0\ \text{V}$ is across the only external component, $R = 20.0\ \Omega$.
↳ from STEP 1: $V = 12.0\ \text{V}$ is the p.d. across $R = 20.0\ \Omega$ (the only external load)
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (Ohm's law for the load):

$$V = IR \quad\Rightarrow\quad I = \dfrac{V}{R}$$

Substitute $V = 12.0\ \text{V}$, $R = 20.0\ \Omega$:

$$I = \dfrac{12.0}{20.0} = 0.60\ \text{A}$$

Phuc's slip (lesson 52:19): he stated “resistance equals $V$ divided by $I$” before rearranging. Use the “Very Itchy Rash” order $V=IR$: cover what you want, so $I = V/R$ (not $R = IV$). The terminal p.d. sits across $R$ because $R$ is the only external component (lesson 50:04).
$I = 0.60\ \text{A}$

Part (c) — internal resistance [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a) & (b): $\varepsilon = 12.5\ \text{V}$, $R = 20.0\ \Omega$, current $I = 0.60\ \text{A}$, lost volts $V_r = 0.5\ \text{V}$.
↳ from STEP 1: $I = 0.60\ \text{A}$, $V_r = \varepsilon - V = 0.5\ \text{V}$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (e.m.f. in current form):

$$\varepsilon = IR + Ir \quad\Rightarrow\quad Ir = \varepsilon - IR$$

Substitute $\varepsilon = 12.5$, $I = 0.60$, $R = 20.0$:

$$Ir = 12.5 - (0.60)(20.0) = 12.5 - 12.0 = 0.5\ \text{V}$$

$$r = \dfrac{Ir}{I} = \dfrac{0.5}{0.60} = 0.83\ \Omega\ (2\ \text{s.f.})$$

Phuc's confusion (lesson 53:33): he asked “is the large $R$ the resistance of the battery?”. Capital $R$ is the external $20\ \Omega$ load; lower-case $r$ is the internal resistance. Never swap them.
Sense-check: $V = \varepsilon - Ir = 12.5 - (0.60)(0.83) = 12.0\ \text{V}$ ✓ matches the given terminal p.d.
$r = 0.83\ \Omega$
