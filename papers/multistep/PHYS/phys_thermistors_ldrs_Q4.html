Q — LDR divider output voltage and power in the fixed resistor

Question

An LDR is connected as $R_2$ (bottom) in a potential divider. The fixed resistor is $R_1 = 8.0\ \text{k}\Omega$ and the supply is $12.0\ \text{V}$. In normal daylight the LDR resistance is $670\ \Omega$.

(a) Calculate the output voltage $V_{out}$ across the LDR. [2 marks]
(b) Calculate the current in the circuit. [2 marks]
(c) Calculate the power dissipated in the fixed resistor. [2 marks]

[6 marks]

Part (a) — output across the LDR [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (output across the LDR, which is $R_2$):

$$V_{out} = V_{in}\cdot\dfrac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$$

Convert $R_1 = 8.0\ \text{k}\Omega = 8000\ \Omega$, $R_2 = 670\ \Omega$, then substitute:

$$V_{out} = 12.0\times\dfrac{670}{8000 + 670} = 12.0\times\dfrac{670}{8670} = 12.0\times0.0773 \approx 0.93\ \text{V}$$

Trap: convert kΩ to Ω before adding. The LDR is $R_2$, so $R_2$ is on top of the fraction.
$V_{out} \approx 0.93\ \text{V}$

Part (b) — circuit current [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a): total resistance $R_1 + R_2 = 8000 + 670 = 8670\ \Omega$; supply $V_{in} = 12.0\ \text{V}$.
↳ from STEP 1: $R_{tot} = 8670\ \Omega$, $V_{in} = 12.0\ \text{V}$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (Ohm's law for the series circuit):

$$I = \dfrac{V_{in}}{R_{tot}}$$

Substitute $V_{in} = 12.0\ \text{V}$, $R_{tot} = 8670\ \Omega$:

$$I = \dfrac{12.0}{8670} \approx 1.38\times10^{-3}\ \text{A}$$

Note: the same current flows through both resistors (series), so this single $I$ feeds the power calculation in part (c).
$I \approx 1.38\times10^{-3}\ \text{A}$

Part (c) — power in fixed resistor [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (b): current $I \approx 1.38\times10^{-3}\ \text{A}$; fixed resistor $R_1 = 8000\ \Omega$.
↳ from STEP 1: $I = 1.38\times10^{-3}\ \text{A}$, $R_1 = 8000\ \Omega$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (power in a resistor):

$$P = I^2 R_1$$

Substitute $I = 1.38\times10^{-3}\ \text{A}$, $R_1 = 8000\ \Omega$:

$$P = (1.38\times10^{-3})^2\times8000 = 1.908\times10^{-6}\times8000 \approx 1.5\times10^{-2}\ \text{W} = 15\ \text{mW}$$

Choice of formula: $P = I^2R$ uses the current (known from (b)) and the fixed resistance directly — cleanest route here.
$P \approx 15\ \text{mW}$
