Worked Ex 6 — Series-parallel circuit: p.d., unknown R, and power budget

Question

A $12\ \text{V}$ supply (negligible internal resistance) drives a $2.8\ \Omega$ resistor in series with a parallel pair: a $4.0\ \Omega$ resistor carrying $1.5\ \text{A}$, and an unknown resistor $R_x$.

(a) Find the p.d. across the $4.0\ \Omega$ resistor and hence the total current $I_T$. [3 marks]
(b) Find the current through $R_x$ and hence $R_x$. [2 marks]
(c) Find the power in each component and confirm the supply power. [3 marks]

[8 marks]

Part (a) — p.d. across 4.0 Ω and total current [3 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2 M1 A1

P.d. across the $4.0\ \Omega$ resistor (Ohm's law):

$$V_4 = IR = 1.5 \times 4.0 = 6.0\ \text{V}$$

Parallel branches share the same p.d., so $V_{R_x} = 6.0\ \text{V}$ too. The remaining supply voltage is across the series $2.8\ \Omega$:

$$V_{2.8} = 12 - 6.0 = 6.0\ \text{V}$$

Concept: the series resistor and the parallel block are in series, so their p.d.s add to the supply: $V_{2.8} + V_{\parallel} = 12\ \text{V}$.
$V_4 = V_{R_x} = 6.0\ \text{V}$; $V_{2.8} = 6.0\ \text{V}$
↳ from STEP 2: $V_{2.8} = 6.0\ \text{V}$ across the series $2.8\ \Omega$ resistor
STEP 2/2 A1

Total current = current through the series resistor ($I = V/R$):

$$I_T = \dfrac{V_{2.8}}{2.8} = \dfrac{6.0}{2.8} = 2.14\ \text{A}$$

Phuc's slip (a28d777a 52:25): he suggested $I = Q/t$ to find current. Here $V$ and $R$ are the known quantities, so use $I = V/R$. ($I = Q/t$ only when charge and time are given.)
$I_T = 2.14\ \text{A}$

Part (b) — current through Rₓ and its value [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a): $I_T = 2.14\ \text{A}$, the $4.0\ \Omega$ branch carries $1.5\ \text{A}$, and $V_{R_x} = 6.0\ \text{V}$.
↳ from STEP 1: $I_T = 2.14\ \text{A}$, $I_4 = 1.5\ \text{A}$, $V_{R_x} = 6.0\ \text{V}$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Kirchhoff's first law at the parallel split (current divides):

$$I_x = I_T - I_4 = 2.14 - 1.5 = 0.64\ \text{A}$$

Then Ohm's law for $R_x$:

$$R_x = \dfrac{V_{R_x}}{I_x} = \dfrac{6.0}{0.64} = 9.4\ \Omega\ (2\ \text{s.f.})$$

Trap (e14ebf09 44:01): use $R = V/I$, never $R = IV$. Units confirm it: $\Omega = \text{V}/\text{A}$.
$I_x = 0.64\ \text{A}$, $R_x = 9.4\ \Omega$

Part (c) — power in each component & supply check [3 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a) & (b): $I_T = 2.14\ \text{A}$, $V_4 = V_{R_x} = 6.0\ \text{V}$, $I_4 = 1.5\ \text{A}$, $R_x = 9.4\ \Omega$, supply $= 12\ \text{V}$.
↳ from STEP 1: $I_T = 2.14$, $V_\parallel = 6.0\ \text{V}$, $I_4 = 1.5\ \text{A}$, $R_x = 9.4\ \Omega$
STEP 1/1 M1 M1 A1

Choose the formula by the quantities known for each component:

$$P_{2.8} = I_T^2 R = (2.14)^2 \times 2.8 = 12.8\ \text{W}$$

$$P_{4.0} = VI = 6.0 \times 1.5 = 9.0\ \text{W}$$

$$P_{R_x} = \dfrac{V^2}{R_x} = \dfrac{(6.0)^2}{9.4} = \dfrac{36}{9.4} = 3.8\ \text{W}$$

Total dissipated $= 12.8 + 9.0 + 3.8 = 25.6\ \text{W}$.

Supply-power check (a28d777a 55:43): use the supply’s own voltage and current: $P_{supply} = V_{supply} I_T = 12 \times 2.14 = 25.7\ \text{W}$ ✓ (matches $25.6$, rounding). The documented slip is multiplying a resistor’s voltage by the wrong current for battery power.
$P$: 12.8 W, 9.0 W, 3.8 W → total $25.6\ \text{W} \approx P_{supply}$ ✓
