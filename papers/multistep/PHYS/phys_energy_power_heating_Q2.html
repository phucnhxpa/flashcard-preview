Worked Ex 2 — Power in parallel resistors

Question

In the lesson circuit, the parallel section has a $6.0\ \Omega$ resistor and a $3.0\ \Omega$ resistor, with $V_{\parallel} = 1.5\ \text{V}$ across both.

(a) Find the power dissipated in the $6.0\ \Omega$ resistor. [2 marks]
(b) Find the power dissipated in the $3.0\ \Omega$ resistor. [2 marks]
(c) Verify the branch currents add to the total $I_T = 0.75\ \text{A}$ (Kirchhoff's first law). [2 marks]

[6 marks]

Part (a) — power in the 6.0 Ω resistor [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula selection: the p.d. $V$ and resistance $R$ are given for each branch, so use:

$$P = \dfrac{V^2}{R}$$

Substitute $V = 1.5\ \text{V}$, $R = 6.0\ \Omega$:

$$P_{6} = \dfrac{(1.5)^2}{6.0} = \dfrac{2.25}{6.0} = 0.375\ \text{W}$$

Parallel key fact: resistors in parallel share the same p.d. ($1.5\ \text{V}$ across both). Use $V^2/R$ because $V$ and $R$ are the directly known quantities — no need to find branch current first.
$P_{6} = 0.375\ \text{W}$

Part (b) — power in the 3.0 Ω resistor [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a): same p.d. $V_{\parallel} = 1.5\ \text{V}$ across the parallel pair; use $P = V^2/R$.
↳ from STEP 1: $V_{\parallel} = 1.5\ \text{V}$ across both branches; $P = V^2/R$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (same p.d., smaller resistance):

$$P_{3} = \dfrac{V^2}{R} = \dfrac{(1.5)^2}{3.0} = \dfrac{2.25}{3.0} = 0.75\ \text{W}$$

Sense check: the $3.0\ \Omega$ branch has half the resistance of the $6.0\ \Omega$ branch at the same p.d., so it dissipates double the power ($0.75 = 2 \times 0.375$) ✓ (since $P \propto 1/R$ at fixed $V$).
$P_{3} = 0.75\ \text{W}$

Part (c) — Kirchhoff-1 current check [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a) & (b): $V_{\parallel} = 1.5\ \text{V}$, $R = 6.0\ \Omega$ and $3.0\ \Omega$; total current $I_T = 0.75\ \text{A}$.
↳ from STEP 1: $I = V/R$ for each branch; $V_{\parallel} = 1.5\ \text{V}$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Branch currents (Ohm's law $I = V/R$):

$$I_{6} = \dfrac{1.5}{6.0} = 0.25\ \text{A}, \qquad I_{3} = \dfrac{1.5}{3.0} = 0.50\ \text{A}$$

Kirchhoff's first law (charge conserved at a junction):

$$I_T = I_{6} + I_{3} = 0.25 + 0.50 = 0.75\ \text{A}\ ✓$$

Common trap (fb80c52f 52:25): Kirchhoff's first law ($\sum I_{in} = \sum I_{out}$) applies at any junction — you do not need to know whether the circuit is series or parallel first. It is purely charge conservation.
$I_{6} + I_{3} = 0.75\ \text{A} = I_T$ ✓
