Torch Q — Current, power in the lamp, and power wasted in the cell

Question

A torch battery of e.m.f. $\varepsilon = 4.5\ \text{V}$ and internal resistance $r = 0.4\ \Omega$ is connected to a lamp of resistance $R = 6.4\ \Omega$.

(a) Find the current $I$ in the circuit. [2 marks]
(b) Find the power dissipated in the lamp $P_R$. [2 marks]
(c) Find the power wasted in the cell $P_r$. [2 marks]

[6 marks]

Part (a) — circuit current [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (current with internal resistance):

$$I = \dfrac{\varepsilon}{R + r}$$

Substitute $\varepsilon = 4.5$, $R = 6.4$, $r = 0.4$:

$$I = \dfrac{4.5}{6.4 + 0.4} = \dfrac{4.5}{6.8} = 0.66\ \text{A}\ (2\ \text{s.f.})$$

Trap: the total resistance in the loop is $R + r$, not just $R$. Forgetting $r$ over-estimates the current.
$I = 0.66\ \text{A}$

Part (b) — power in lamp [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a): circuit current $I = 0.66\ \text{A}$; lamp resistance $R = 6.4\ \Omega$.
↳ from STEP 1: $I = 0.66\ \text{A}$, $R = 6.4\ \Omega$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (power dissipated in a resistor):

$$P_R = I^2 R$$

Substitute $I = 0.66\ \text{A}$, $R = 6.4\ \Omega$:

$$P_R = (0.66)^2 \times 6.4 = 0.436 \times 6.4 = 2.8\ \text{W}$$

Why $I^2R$ and not $IV$ with $V=\varepsilon$: the lamp only receives the terminal p.d., not the full e.m.f. Using $I^2R$ automatically uses the load resistance and avoids the lost-volts error.
$P_R = 2.8\ \text{W}$

Part (c) — power wasted in cell [2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a): circuit current $I = 0.66\ \text{A}$; internal resistance $r = 0.4\ \Omega$.
↳ from STEP 1: $I = 0.66\ \text{A}$, $r = 0.4\ \Omega$
STEP 1/1 M1 A1

Formula (power dissipated in internal resistance):

$$P_r = I^2 r$$

Substitute $I = 0.66\ \text{A}$, $r = 0.4\ \Omega$:

$$P_r = (0.66)^2 \times 0.4 = 0.436 \times 0.4 = 0.17\ \text{W}$$

Concept: this power is dissipated as heat inside the cell — it is why a battery becomes warm in use. The same current $I$ flows through both $R$ and $r$ (series), so reuse $I$ from (a).
$P_r = 0.17\ \text{W}$
