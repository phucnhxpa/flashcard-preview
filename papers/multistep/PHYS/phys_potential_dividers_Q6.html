LDR divider — find the fixed resistor R1 (output across the LDR)

Question

A potential divider switches on a lamp when the light intensity drops below a set value. The supply is $15\ \text{V}$. At the switching point the LDR resistance is $R_{\text{LDR}} = 7.0\ \text{k}\Omega$ and the required output is $V_{\text{out}} = 9.0\ \text{V}$ across the LDR. The fixed resistor $R_1$ is the top resistor.

(a) Set up the divider equation (output across the LDR) and solve for the fixed resistor $R_1$. [3 marks]
(b) State the nearest sensible value and sense-check the split. [1 mark]

[4 marks]

Part (a) — solve for R₁ [3 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2 M1

Formula (divider, output across the LDR):

$$V_{\text{out}} = V_{\text{in}} \cdot \dfrac{R_{\text{LDR}}}{R_1 + R_{\text{LDR}}}$$

Substitute $V_{\text{out}} = 9.0$, $V_{\text{in}} = 15$, $R_{\text{LDR}} = 7.0\ \text{k}\Omega$:

$$9.0 = 15 \times \dfrac{7.0}{R_1 + 7.0}$$

Which resistor on top? the output is across the LDR, so $R_{\text{LDR}}$ is the numerator. Working in k$\Omega$ throughout keeps the numbers tidy; the answer comes out in k$\Omega$.
$9.0 = 15 \times \dfrac{7.0}{R_1 + 7.0}$
↳ from STEP 2: $9.0 = 15 \times \dfrac{7.0}{R_1 + 7.0}$ (all resistances in k$\Omega$)
STEP 2/2 M1 A1

Cross-multiply and solve for $R_1$:

$$9.0\,(R_1 + 7.0) = 15 \times 7.0 = 105$$

$$R_1 + 7.0 = \dfrac{105}{9.0} = 11.67$$

$$R_1 = 11.67 - 7.0 = 4.67\ \text{k}\Omega$$

Trap: do not forget to subtract $R_{\text{LDR}} = 7.0\ \text{k}\Omega$ at the end. The bracket $(R_1 + 7.0) = 11.67\ \text{k}\Omega$ is the total resistance, not $R_1$.
$R_1 = 4.67\ \text{k}\Omega$

Part (b) — nearest value and check [1 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (a): $R_1 = 4.67\ \text{k}\Omega$, $R_{\text{LDR}} = 7.0\ \text{k}\Omega$, $V_{\text{in}} = 15\ \text{V}$, $V_{\text{out}} = 9.0\ \text{V}$ across the LDR.
↳ from STEP 1: $R_1 = 4.67\ \text{k}\Omega \approx 4.7\ \text{k}\Omega$; output $9.0\ \text{V}$ across $R_{\text{LDR}} = 7.0\ \text{k}\Omega$
STEP 1/1 B1

Nearest preferred value: $R_1 \approx 4.7\ \text{k}\Omega$ (2 s.f., an E12 preferred resistor value).

Final check (V $\propto$ R, same series current): the larger share of the $15\ \text{V}$ should sit on the larger resistance. The LDR ($7.0\ \text{k}\Omega$) is bigger than $R_1$ ($4.7\ \text{k}\Omega$), so it takes the bigger voltage: $9.0\ \text{V} > 6.0\ \text{V}$ across $R_1$  ✓.

Physics of the switch: as light falls, $R_{\text{LDR}}$ rises, so $V_{\text{out}}$ across the LDR rises past $9.0\ \text{V}$ and trips the lamp circuit — how dusk-to-dawn street lights work.
$R_1 \approx 4.7\ \text{k}\Omega$; split $9.0\ \text{V} : 6.0\ \text{V}$ matches $7.0 : 4.7$ ✓
