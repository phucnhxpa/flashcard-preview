Q1 — Find $\displaystyle \int\!\left(10x^4 - \dfrac{3}{2x^3} - 7\right)dx$

Question

Find $\displaystyle \int\!\left(10x^4 - \dfrac{3}{2x^3} - 7\right)dx$, giving each term in simplest form.

[3 marks]

Solution [3 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3

Strategy: the integration power rule $\int x^n\,dx = \dfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}$ requires the integrand to be in the form $x^n$ (an explicit power of $x$). Anything with $x$ in the denominator must be rewritten with a negative exponent before integrating.

Index law:

$$\dfrac{1}{x^n} = x^{-n}$$

Apply to the middle term of the integrand:

$\;\;\dfrac{3}{2x^3} = \dfrac{3}{2}\cdot \dfrac{1}{x^3} = \tfrac{3}{2}\, x^{-3}$

The other two terms are already in power form: $10x^4$ (already $x^4$) and $-7$ (a constant $= -7x^0$).

$$\boxed{\;\dfrac{3}{2x^3} = \tfrac{3}{2}\, x^{-3}\;}$$

Phuc's slip: "I can do this, but forget the integration of $x$ in denominator." Always rewrite $\dfrac{1}{x^n} = x^{-n}$ before integrating — that lets you apply $\int x^n\,dx$. Pitfall: never integrate $\dfrac{3}{2x^3}$ as if it were $\dfrac{3}{2}\ln(x^3)$ — that's the rule for $\int \dfrac{1}{x}\,dx$ only (when $n = -1$).
$\dfrac{3}{2x^3} = \tfrac{3}{2}\, x^{-3}$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $\dfrac{3}{2x^3} = \tfrac{3}{2}\, x^{-3}$
STEP 2/3 M1

Strategy: integrate each term separately using the power rule. The integral of a sum is the sum of the integrals.

Formula (integration power rule):

$$\int x^n\,dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}\quad(n \neq -1)$$

In words: add 1 to the exponent, then divide by the new exponent. Constant multipliers come straight through.

Term 1 ($n = 4$):

$\;\;\int 10x^4\,dx = 10 \cdot \dfrac{x^{4+1}}{4+1} = \dfrac{10x^5}{5} = 2x^5$

Term 2 ($n = -3$, so $n+1 = -2$):

$\;\;\int -\tfrac{3}{2}x^{-3}\,dx = -\tfrac{3}{2} \cdot \dfrac{x^{-2}}{-2}$

$\;\;\phantom{\int -\tfrac{3}{2}x^{-3}\,dx} = \tfrac{3}{4}\, x^{-2} = \dfrac{3}{4x^2}$

Term 3 (constant; treat as $-7x^0$ ⇒ $n = 0$):

$\;\;\int -7\,dx = -7 \cdot \dfrac{x^1}{1} = -7x$

$$\boxed{\;2x^5 + \tfrac{3}{4x^2} - 7x\;}$$

Watch the signs: two negatives ($-\tfrac{3}{2}$ from the integrand and $\tfrac{1}{-2}$ from the rule) cancel — the answer is positive $\tfrac{3}{4x^2}$. Sign-check: integrate $-\tfrac{3}{2}x^{-3}$, divide by new exponent $n+1 = -2$, then simplify the double negative. Restoring fraction form: $x^{-2} = \dfrac{1}{x^2}$, so $\tfrac{3}{4}x^{-2} = \dfrac{3}{4x^2}$ — the mark scheme prefers fraction form for terms with $x$ originally in the denominator.
$2x^5 + \tfrac{3}{4x^2} - 7x$
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: $2x^5 + \tfrac{3}{4x^2} - 7x$
STEP 3/3 A1

Strategy: any indefinite integral has infinitely many antiderivatives differing by a constant. Always add $+C$ at the end — losing it costs the A1.

Combine the three integrated terms from the previous step:

$\;\;\displaystyle\int\!\left(10x^4 - \tfrac{3}{2x^3} - 7\right)dx = 2x^5 + \tfrac{3}{4x^2} - 7x + C$

$$\boxed{\;2x^5 + \dfrac{3}{4x^2} - 7x + C\;}$$

Quick verify (differentiate back): $\tfrac{d}{dx}(2x^5) = 10x^4$ ✓; $\tfrac{d}{dx}\bigl(\tfrac{3}{4}x^{-2}\bigr) = \tfrac{3}{4}\cdot(-2)x^{-3} = -\tfrac{3}{2}x^{-3} = -\tfrac{3}{2x^3}$ ✓; $\tfrac{d}{dx}(-7x) = -7$ ✓; $\tfrac{d}{dx}(C) = 0$. All three terms match the original integrand. Mark scheme cue: if you forget $+C$, you typically lose the final A1 even with everything else correct.
$2x^5 + \tfrac{3}{4x^2} - 7x + C$
