P1 Q2 · Graph transformations — $y=f(-x)$ and $y=f(x)-2$

Question

The curve $C$ has equation $y = f(x)$, where $f(x)$ has a minimum at $P(-1, 0)$, a maximum at $Q\!\left(\tfrac{2}{3}, 2\right)$, and a horizontal asymptote $y = 1$.

(a) Sketch $y = f(-x)$, stating the new coordinates of the turning points and the equation of the asymptote. [3]

(b) Sketch $y = f(x) - 2$, stating the new coordinates of the turning points and the equation of the asymptote. [3]

[6 marks]

Worked visual — 3 steps

Part (a) — y = f(-x): reflection in y-axis [3 marks] · 4 steps

STEP 1/4identify the transformation M1

Strategy. The transformation $y = f(-x)$ replaces every $x$ in the function with $-x$. This is a reflection in the $y$-axis (a horizontal reflection): every point $(x, y)$ moves to $(-x, y)$.

Phuc's mistake. Treated $f(-x)$ as if it multiplied the output $y$ by $-1$, writing $y = 1 \Rightarrow y = -1$ for the asymptote. But $f(-x)$ negates the input, not the output. Confusing $f(-x)$ with $-f(x)$ is the single most common transformation error.
↳ from STEP 1: reflection in $y$-axis: $(x,y)\to(-x,y)$
STEP 2/4transform the turning points A1

Strategy. Under $y = f(-x)$, each point $(x, y) \to (-x, y)$ — the $x$-coordinate flips sign, the $y$-coordinate is unchanged.

New turning points

$$P(-1, 0) \to P'(1, 0), \qquad Q\!\left(\tfrac{2}{3}, 2\right) \to Q'\!\left(-\tfrac{2}{3}, 2\right).$$

Sense check. The $y$-values ($0$ and $2$) are unchanged — consistent with a horizontal reflection. Only the $x$-signs flip.

↳ from STEP 2: $P'(1,0),\; Q'(-\tfrac{2}{3},2)$
STEP 3/4transform the asymptote A1

Strategy. A horizontal asymptote $y = k$ describes a $y$-value the curve approaches as $x \to \pm\infty$. Under $y = f(-x)$, $y$-values are unchanged, so $y = k$ stays $y = k$.

Horizontal asymptote

$$y = 1 \;\longrightarrow\; y = 1 \quad(\text{unchanged}).$$
Phuc's mistake (lost all 3 marks here). Wrote $y = 1 \to y = -1$, as if applying $-f(x)$. The horizontal asymptote does NOT move under $f(-x)$ because $f(-x)$ is a horizontal transformation — it changes the direction you travel to infinity, not the $y$-value you approach. Only vertical transformations ($-f(x)$, $f(x)+a$) move a horizontal asymptote.
↳ from STEP 3: $y=1$ unchanged
STEP 4/4contrast with -f(x) B1

Strategy. Contrast: $y = -f(x)$ reflects in the $x$-axis — every $y$ is negated, so $y = k \to y = -k$. Only this transformation moves a horizontal asymptote.

Six standard transformations and horizontal asymptotes

+ $y=f(x)+a$: shifts up, asymptote $\to y=k+a$ (moves)
+ $y=f(x)-a$: shifts down, asymptote $\to y=k-a$ (moves)
+ $y=f(x-a)$: shifts right, asymptote unchanged
+ $y=f(x+a)$: shifts left, asymptote unchanged
+ $y=-f(x)$: $x$-axis reflection, asymptote $\to y=-k$ (moves)
+ $y=f(-x)$: $y$-axis reflection, asymptote unchanged

Sense check. Horizontal transformations (changes to $x$ inside $f$) leave horizontal asymptotes alone; vertical transformations (changes outside $f$) move them.

Part (b) — y = f(x) − 2: translation down 2 [3 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3identify the translation M1

Strategy. $y = f(x) - 2$ subtracts $2$ from every output — a vertical translation down by 2. Each point $(x, y) \to (x, y - 2)$.

↳ from STEP 1: translation down 2: $(x,y)\to(x,y-2)$
STEP 2/3transform points and asymptote A1

Strategy. Subtract $2$ from every $y$-coordinate. The $x$-coordinates are unchanged (vertical translation).

New turning points

$$P(-1, 0) \to P'(-1, -2), \qquad Q\!\left(\tfrac{2}{3}, 2\right) \to Q'\!\left(\tfrac{2}{3}, 0\right).$$

New asymptote

$$y = 1 \to y = 1 - 2 = y = -1.$$
Order of min/max is preserved. A translation moves every point by the same amount, so the point that was the minimum remains the minimum (just lower); the maximum remains the maximum. Relative positions are unchanged.
↳ from STEP 2: $P'(-1,-2),\; Q'(\tfrac{2}{3},0),\; y=-1$
STEP 3/3write the complete answer A1

Strategy. State all transformed features clearly: both turning points and the asymptote.

Final answer

$$P'(-1, -2), \quad Q'\!\left(\tfrac{2}{3}, 0\right), \quad \text{asymptote } y = -1.$$

Sense check. The minimum moved from $y=0$ to $y=-2$ (down 2) and the maximum from $y=2$ to $y=0$ (down 2) — consistent shift. The asymptote $y=1 \to y=-1$ also moved down 2.
