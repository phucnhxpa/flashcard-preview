Q8(c) — Find $A$ such that $C_1 \cap C_2$ has 4 distinct points

Question

$C_1\!:\, y = x(4 - x^2)$   (cubic, roots $-2, 0, 2$).

$C_2\!:\, y = \dfrac{A}{x}$   (hyperbola, constant $A$).

From part (b): the $x$-coordinates of $C_1 \cap C_2$ satisfy $x^4 - 4x^2 + A = 0$.

(c) Find the range of values of $A$ for which $C_1$ meets $C_2$ at four distinct points. [3]

[3 marks]

Solution [3 marks] · 4 steps

From (b): $x^4 - 4x^2 + A = 0$ at every intersection point.
STEP 1/4 M1

Strategy: the quartic $x^4 - 4x^2 + A = 0$ only contains even powers of $x$ ($x^4$ and $x^2$) — a so-called "biquadratic". Substituting $u = x^2$ collapses it to a quadratic, which is something we know how to handle.

Substitution: let $u = x^2$. Then $x^4 = (x^2)^2 = u^2$.

Apply to the quartic:

$\;\;\;x^4 - 4x^2 + A = 0$

$\;\;\;u^2 - 4u + A = 0$  (quadratic in $u$)

Critical constraint: $u = x^2$ for real $x$ forces $u \geq 0$ (squares are non-negative).

$$\boxed{\;u^2 - 4u + A = 0,\quad u \geq 0\;}$$

Translation rule (count $x$'s from $u$'s):
• each positive $u$-root gives two real $x$-values: $x = \pm\sqrt{u}$;
• $u = 0$ gives one ($x = 0$);
• $u < 0$ gives zero real $x$ (no real square root of a negative).
So 4 distinct $x$'s ⇔ 2 distinct positive $u$'s — this drives the next two steps.
$u^2 - 4u + A = 0$ with $u = x^2 \geq 0$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $u^2 - 4u + A = 0$ with $u = x^2 \geq 0$
STEP 2/4 M1

Strategy: we need 2 distinct positive $u$-roots. Start with the easier condition: "2 distinct real roots" ⇒ discriminant strictly positive. Sign-of-roots will be the next step.

Formula (discriminant of $au^2 + bu + c = 0$):

$$\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$$

$\Delta > 0$: two distinct real roots. $\Delta = 0$: repeated root. $\Delta < 0$: no real roots.

Read off coefficients from $u^2 - 4u + A = 0$: $a = 1$, $b = -4$, $c = A$.

Compute $\Delta$:

$\;\;\;\Delta = (-4)^2 - 4(1)(A) = 16 - 4A$

Require $\Delta > 0$:

$\;\;\;16 - 4A > 0$

$\;\;\;-4A > -16$

Divide by $-4$ (flip inequality — dividing by negative):

$$\boxed{\;A < 4\;}$$

Why strict $>$ not $\geq$: $\Delta = 0$ gives a repeated $u$-root. Even if that root is positive, it only yields 2 distinct $x$-values (from $x = \pm\sqrt{u}$), not the 4 distinct ones we need. Watch the inequality flip: dividing or multiplying by a negative reverses $>$ to $<$. A common slip is to leave it as $A > 4$.
$A < 4$
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: $A < 4$
STEP 3/4 A1

Strategy: $\Delta > 0$ alone only guarantees two distinct REAL $u$-roots; those could both be negative, in which case there are no real $x$ at all. We need both $u$-roots to be positive. The cleanest way: Vieta's formulas — both roots $> 0$ ⇔ sum $> 0$ AND product $> 0$.

Formula (Vieta's, for $au^2+bu+c=0$):

$$u_1 + u_2 = -\tfrac{b}{a},\quad u_1 u_2 = \tfrac{c}{a}$$

Substitute $a=1,\, b=-4,\, c=A$:

$\;\;\;u_1 + u_2 = -\tfrac{-4}{1} = 4 > 0$ ✓  (automatic — no constraint on $A$)

$\;\;\;u_1 u_2 = \tfrac{A}{1} = A$

Need product $> 0$:

$$\boxed{\;A > 0\;}$$

Phuc's noted gap: "discriminant work gave $A < 4$ but I forgot the $A > 0$ condition." $\Delta > 0$ alone is not enough — it allows both $u$-roots to be negative (in which case $x = \pm\sqrt{u}$ has no real solution). Numerical illustration: if $A = -3$, the quadratic $u^2 - 4u - 3 = 0$ has roots $u \approx 4.65$ and $u \approx -0.65$ (one positive, one negative), so only the positive $u$ contributes 2 real $x$'s — totalling 2, not 4. Always pair $\Delta$ with sign-of-roots checks (sum & product via Vieta's).
$A > 0$
↳ from STEP 3:From STEP 3: $A > 0$
STEP 4/4

Strategy: we have two conditions on $A$ from the previous steps; the answer is the intersection (BOTH must hold simultaneously).

From step 2: $A < 4$ (discriminant condition).

From step 3: $A > 0$ (both $u$-roots positive).

Intersect on the number line: $A$ must lie in BOTH $(-\infty, 4)$ and $(0, \infty)$:

$$\boxed{\;0 < A < 4\;}$$

Boundary sanity check:
• At $A = 0$: $C_2: y = 0/x = 0$ degenerates to the $x$-axis. $C_1$ meets the $x$-axis at $x = -2, 0, 2$ — only 3 intersections, not 4. ✗
• At $A = 4$: $u^2 - 4u + 4 = (u-2)^2 = 0$ has repeated root $u = 2$, giving $x = \pm\sqrt{2}$ — only 2 intersections. ✗
Both endpoints fail, confirming the strict double inequality.
$0 < A < 4$
