Q2 — (i) Express $2^{n+3}$ and $16^{3n}$ in terms of $m=2^n$. (ii) Solve $x\sqrt{3} - 3 = x + \sqrt{3}$.

Question

(i) Given $m = 2^n$, express in simplest form in terms of $m$:

   (a) $2^{n+3}$ [1]   (b) $16^{3n}$ [2]

(ii) Solve $x\sqrt{3} - 3 = x + \sqrt{3}$, giving the answer in the form $p + q\sqrt{3}$ where $p, q$ are integers. [3] (All stages must be shown — no calculator.)

[6 marks]

Part (i)(a) — $2^{n+3}$ [1 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 B1

Strategy: the answer must be in terms of $m = 2^n$, so we need to isolate a factor of $2^n$ inside $2^{n+3}$. Use the additive index law to peel off the constant part of the exponent.

Index law:

$$a^{x+y} = a^x \cdot a^y$$

Apply with $a = 2$, $x = n$, $y = 3$:

$\;\;2^{n+3} = 2^n \cdot 2^3$

Evaluate $2^3$:

$\;\;2^{n+3} = 2^n \cdot 8$

Replace $2^n$ by $m$:

$$\boxed{\;2^{n+3} = 8m\;}$$

Why split this way: the index law $a^{x+y} = a^x \cdot a^y$ peels off the constant power. Once $2^n$ is isolated, replacing it by $m$ is the whole job of part (a). Sense check (with $n = 1$): $m = 2^1 = 2$, $2^{1+3} = 2^4 = 16$, and $8m = 8 \cdot 2 = 16$ ✓.
$2^{n+3} = \mathbf{8m}$

Part (i)(b) — $16^{3n}$ [2 marks] · 2 steps

From the question: $m = 2^n$. From (a): $2^{n+3} = 8m$ (technique: rewrite the base as a power of $2$, then re-group exponent so $2^n$ appears).
STEP 1/2 M1

Strategy: we want the answer in terms of $m = 2^n$, so all bases must become $2$. Step one: convert $16$ to a power of $2$.

Note that $16 = 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 = 2^4$.

Index law (power of a power):

$$(a^x)^y = a^{xy}$$

Apply to $16^{3n}$:

$\;\;16^{3n} = (2^4)^{3n}$

$\;\;\phantom{16^{3n}} = 2^{4 \cdot 3n}$

$\;\;\phantom{16^{3n}} = 2^{12n}$

$$\boxed{\;16^{3n} = 2^{12n}\;}$$

Common bases to memorise: $4 = 2^2$, $8 = 2^3$, $16 = 2^4$, $32 = 2^5$, $64 = 2^6$. Whenever the question gives $m = 2^n$, suspect that every other base needs converting to a power of $2$.
$16^{3n} = 2^{12n}$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $16^{3n} = 2^{12n}$
STEP 2/2 A1

Strategy: we have $2^{12n}$ but need $m = 2^n$ visible. Use the index law in reverse: pull out the factor of $n$ in the exponent.

Index law (reverse direction):

$$a^{xy} = (a^x)^y$$

Apply with $a = 2$, $x = n$, $y = 12$ (so $xy = 12n$):

$\;\;2^{12n} = 2^{n \cdot 12} = (2^n)^{12}$

Replace $2^n$ by $m$:

$$\boxed{\;16^{3n} = m^{12}\;}$$

Trap: do not write $(2^{12})^n = 4096^n$ — that has the right value but the wrong form (no $m$ visible) and fails the requirement to give the answer in terms of $m$. Sense check ($n = 1$): $m = 2$, so $m^{12} = 4096$; and $16^{3 \cdot 1} = 16^3 = 4096$ ✓.
$16^{3n} = \mathbf{m^{12}}$

Part (ii) — Solve and rationalise [3 marks] · 2 steps

From the question: solve $x\sqrt{3} - 3 = x + \sqrt{3}$, giving the answer in the form $p + q\sqrt{3}$ with $p, q \in \mathbb{Z}$ (no calculator). (Independent of part (i).)
STEP 1/2 M1

Strategy: standard linear-equation moves — collect all $x$-terms on one side, all non-$x$ terms on the other, then factor and divide. Treat $\sqrt{3}$ as a constant symbol (no decimal approximations).

Start:

$\;\;x\sqrt{3} - 3 = x + \sqrt{3}$

Subtract $x$ from both sides:

$\;\;x\sqrt{3} - x - 3 = \sqrt{3}$

Add $3$ to both sides:

$\;\;x\sqrt{3} - x = 3 + \sqrt{3}$

Factor out $x$:

$\;\;x(\sqrt{3} - 1) = 3 + \sqrt{3}$

Divide by $\sqrt{3} - 1$:

$$\boxed{\;x = \dfrac{3 + \sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3} - 1}\;}$$

Treat $\sqrt{3}$ like an algebraic letter: when collecting in $x$, the $\sqrt{3}$ stays as part of the coefficient. Don't panic and try to evaluate it as $1.732$ — that breaks the no-calculator requirement and ruins the surd form. Why factor out $x$: the coefficient of $x$ has two terms ($\sqrt{3}$ and $-1$); only by factoring can we then divide cleanly.
$x = \dfrac{3 + \sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3} - 1}$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $x = \dfrac{3 + \sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3} - 1}$
STEP 2/2 M1 A1

Strategy: a fraction with a surd in the denominator isn't in simplest form. Rationalise by multiplying top and bottom by the conjugate of the denominator. The conjugate of $a - b$ is $a + b$ (flip the sign of the second term).

The conjugate of $\sqrt{3} - 1$ is $\sqrt{3} + 1$. Multiply both numerator and denominator by it (the fraction $\dfrac{\sqrt{3}+1}{\sqrt{3}+1} = 1$ — value unchanged):

$$x = \dfrac{3 + \sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3} - 1} \cdot \dfrac{\sqrt{3} + 1}{\sqrt{3} + 1}$$

Numerator (FOIL — distribute $(3 + \sqrt{3})$ over $(\sqrt{3} + 1)$):

$\;\;(3+\sqrt{3})(\sqrt{3}+1)$

$\;\;= 3\cdot\sqrt{3} + 3\cdot 1 + \sqrt{3}\cdot\sqrt{3} + \sqrt{3}\cdot 1$

$\;\;= 3\sqrt{3} + 3 + 3 + \sqrt{3}$

$\;\;= 6 + 4\sqrt{3}$

Denominator (difference of two squares):

$\;\;(\sqrt{3} - 1)(\sqrt{3} + 1) = (\sqrt{3})^2 - 1^2 = 3 - 1 = 2$

Combine:

$\;\;x = \dfrac{6 + 4\sqrt{3}}{2} = \dfrac{6}{2} + \dfrac{4\sqrt{3}}{2} = 3 + 2\sqrt{3}$

$$\boxed{\;x = 3 + 2\sqrt{3}\;}\;\Rightarrow\;p = 3,\ q = 2$$

Why the conjugate works: $(a-b)(a+b) = a^2 - b^2$, which kills the surd in the denominator (squaring removes the radical). Here $(\sqrt{3})^2 - 1^2 = 3 - 1 = 2$ — a clean integer. Note on $\sqrt{3} \cdot \sqrt{3}$: $\sqrt{3}\cdot\sqrt{3} = (\sqrt{3})^2 = 3$ (the radical and square cancel).
Sense check: $3 + 2\sqrt{3} \approx 3 + 2(1.732) \approx 6.464$; LHS at this $x$: $6.464 \cdot 1.732 - 3 \approx 11.20 - 3 = 8.196$; RHS: $6.464 + 1.732 \approx 8.196$ ✓ (matches to 3 d.p.)
$x = 3 + 2\sqrt{3}$, so $\mathbf{p = 3,\ q = 2}$
