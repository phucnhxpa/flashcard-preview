Q4 — $2\cdot 4^x - 2^{x+3} = 17\cdot 2^{x-1} - 4$ via $p = 2^x$

Question

Show all working — no calculator.

(a) By substituting $p = 2^x$, show that $2\cdot 4^x - 2^{x+3} = 17\cdot 2^{x-1} - 4$ can be written as $4p^2 - 33p + 8 = 0$. [3]

(b) Hence solve the original equation. [3]

[6 marks]

Part (a) — Show $4p^2 - 33p + 8 = 0$ [3 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2 M1

Strategy: the substitution $p = 2^x$ only works if every term is rewritten as a power of $2^x$ first. Three terms need converting; each uses one of the standard index laws.

Index laws used:

$\;\;\;a^{x+c} = a^x \cdot a^c$  (separate constant exponent)

$\;\;\;(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$  (power of a power)

Term 1 — $2 \cdot 4^x$: rewrite base $4$ as $2^2$:

$\;\;\;2 \cdot 4^x = 2 \cdot (2^2)^x = 2 \cdot 2^{2x} = 2 \cdot (2^x)^2 = 2p^2$

Term 2 — $2^{x+3}$: peel off the constant exponent:

$\;\;\;2^{x+3} = 2^x \cdot 2^3 = p \cdot 8 = 8p$

Term 3 — $17 \cdot 2^{x-1}$: peel off the $-1$:

$\;\;\;17 \cdot 2^{x-1} = 17 \cdot 2^x \cdot 2^{-1} = 17p \cdot \tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{17p}{2}$

Substitute back into the original equation $2 \cdot 4^x - 2^{x+3} = 17 \cdot 2^{x-1} - 4$:

$$\boxed{\;2p^2 - 8p = \tfrac{17p}{2} - 4\;}$$

Mnemonic: $a^{x+c} = a^x\cdot a^c$ — an extra constant on the exponent splits off as a constant multiplier. Convert EVERY term to a power of $2^x$ before substituting; missing one (e.g. forgetting that $4 = 2^2$) leaves a stray $4^x$ in the equation and breaks the whole substitution.
$2p^2 - 8p = \tfrac{17p}{2} - 4$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $2p^2 - 8p = \tfrac{17p}{2} - 4$
STEP 2/2 M1 A1

Strategy: the equation $2p^2 - 8p = \tfrac{17p}{2} - 4$ has a fractional coefficient. Multiply through by $2$ to clear it, then collect all terms on the LHS to reach the target form $4p^2 - 33p + 8 = 0$.

Multiply every term by $2$ (LHS and RHS):

$\;\;\;2 \cdot (2p^2) - 2 \cdot (8p) = 2 \cdot \tfrac{17p}{2} - 2 \cdot 4$

$\;\;\;4p^2 - 16p = 17p - 8$

Subtract $17p$ from both sides:

$\;\;\;4p^2 - 16p - 17p = -8$

$\;\;\;4p^2 - 33p = -8$

Add $8$ to both sides:

$\;\;\;4p^2 - 33p + 8 = 0$

$$\boxed{\;4p^2 - 33p + 8 = 0\;}\quad\blacksquare$$

Multiply EVERY term on both sides — forgetting to do $-4 \to -8$ on the right is the classic slip; you would end up with $4p^2 - 33p + 4 = 0$ (wrong constant). Why combine the $-16p$ and $-17p$: they're both linear in $p$, so they sum to $-33p$ — matching the target form. The middle coefficient $-33$ is the algebraic fingerprint of the "show that" question.
$4p^2 - 33p + 8 = 0$ ✓

Part (b) — Solve for $x$ [3 marks] · 2 steps

From (a): $4p^2 - 33p + 8 = 0$ with $p = 2^x$.
STEP 1/2 M1 A1

Strategy: the quadratic $4p^2 - 33p + 8 = 0$ likely factors over the integers (coefficients are tidy). Use the split-the-middle technique to find the factor pair.

Split-the-middle: for $ap^2 + bp + c$, find two integers with

$\;\;\;\text{product} = ac = 4 \cdot 8 = 32$

$\;\;\;\text{sum} = b = -33$

Factor pairs of $32$: $(1, 32)$, $(2, 16)$, $(4, 8)$. With $b = -33$ both must be negative; $(-1, -32)$ has sum $-33$ ✓ — pick this pair.

Rewrite the middle term $-33p = -32p - p$:

$\;\;\;4p^2 - 32p - p + 8$

Factor by grouping (pair first two, last two):

$\;\;\;= 4p(p - 8) - 1(p - 8)$

$\;\;\;= (4p - 1)(p - 8)$  (common bracket $(p-8)$)

Apply zero-product property:

$\;\;\;4p - 1 = 0 \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; p = \tfrac{1}{4}$

$\;\;\;p - 8 = 0 \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; p = 8$

$$\boxed{\;p = 8\;\text{or}\;p = \tfrac{1}{4}\;}$$

Verify by expansion: $(4p-1)(p-8) = 4p^2 - 32p - p + 8 = 4p^2 - 33p + 8$ ✓ — coefficients match. Both roots are positive, which is reassuring since $p = 2^x > 0$ always; a negative root would have to be discarded as extraneous.
$p = 8$ or $p = \tfrac{1}{4}$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $p = 8$ or $p = \tfrac{1}{4}$
STEP 2/2 A1

Strategy: we have $p$-values; the question wants $x$. Reverse the substitution $p = 2^x$ by writing each $p$-value as a power of $2$, then equate exponents.

Case 1 — $p = 8$:

$\;\;\;2^x = 8 = 2^3$

Equating exponents (exponential is injective for fixed positive base $\ne 1$):

$\;\;\;x = 3$

Case 2 — $p = \tfrac{1}{4}$:

$\;\;\;2^x = \tfrac{1}{4} = \tfrac{1}{2^2} = 2^{-2}$

Equating exponents:

$\;\;\;x = -2$

$$\boxed{\;x = 3\;\text{or}\;x = -2\;}$$

Common bases to memorise: $4 = 2^2$, $8 = 2^3$, $16 = 2^4$, $32 = 2^5$, $\tfrac{1}{2} = 2^{-1}$, $\tfrac{1}{4} = 2^{-2}$, $\tfrac{1}{8} = 2^{-3}$. No need to reject either root: $2^x > 0$ for all real $x$, and both $p = 8$ and $p = \tfrac{1}{4}$ are positive ⇒ both yield real $x$. Verify in original equation: at $x = 3$: $2 \cdot 4^3 - 2^6 = 128 - 64 = 64$; $17 \cdot 2^2 - 4 = 68 - 4 = 64$ ✓.
$\boxed{x = 3\ \text{or}\ x = -2}$
