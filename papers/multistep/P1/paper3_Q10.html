Q10 — $f'(x) = 4x^{3/2} + \dfrac{k}{x^2}$, $f''(2) = 0$, $P(2,8\sqrt{2})$ on curve

Question

Curve $C: y = f(x)$, $x > 0$. Given $P(2,\, 8\sqrt{2})$ lies on $C$, $\;f'(x) = 4x^{3/2} + \dfrac{k}{x^2}\;$ and $\;f''(x) = 0\;$ at $P$.

(a) Find the exact value of $k$. [4]

(b) Find $f(x)$. [4]

[8 marks]

Part (a) — Find $k$ [4 marks] · 4 steps

STEP 1/4

Strategy: we have $f'(x) = 4x^{3/2} + kx^{-2}$ with two unknowns to deal with: the constant $k$ in the formula, and (eventually) an integration constant $C$ when we integrate to find $f(x)$. The conditions given are $P(2, 8\sqrt{2})$ on $C$ and $f''(2) = 0$ (a stationary point of $f'$). Choose the order that uses each unknown cleanly.

Tempting (wrong) plan: integrate $f'$ first to get $f(x) = \dots + kx^{?} + C$, then plug in $P$. But that single equation involves BOTH $k$ and $C$ — one equation, two unknowns ⇒ stuck (need a second equation, which won't come from $f''$ if we've already integrated).

Right plan: notice that $f''(2) = 0$ involves $k$ ALONE (no $C$). Use it FIRST to find $k$, THEN integrate $f'$ to get $f(x)$ with the now-known $k$, THEN plug in $P$ to find $C$.

Order of operations:

$\;\;\;1.\;\;f'(x) \xrightarrow{\,\text{differentiate}\,} f''(x)$

$\;\;\;2.\;\;\text{Set } f''(2) = 0 \to \text{ solve for } k$

$\;\;\;3.\;\;\text{(in part b) integrate } f'(x) \to f(x) + C, \text{ use } P \to \text{ solve for } C$

$$\boxed{\;\text{Strategy: differentiate first (find } k\text{), integrate later (find } C\text{)}\;}$$

General rule for two unknowns: find an extra condition that involves only one of them, and use it first to reduce the system to one unknown. Here $f''(2)=0$ is the "clean" condition (only $k$); $P$ on $C$ is the "mixed" condition (both $k$ and $C$).
Strategy: differentiate first, integrate later.

STEP 2/4 M1

Strategy: the power rule for differentiation only handles terms of the form $x^n$ for any real $n$ — including negative and fractional exponents. So rewrite any fractions like $\tfrac{1}{x^2}$ as $x^{-2}$ first, then apply the rule term-by-term.

Rewrite the given $f'(x)$ in pure-power form (already done in this case):

$\;\;\;f'(x) = 4x^{3/2} + k\, x^{-2}$

Formula (power rule):

$$\tfrac{d}{dx}\!\left(x^n\right) = n\, x^{n-1}$$

Term 1 — differentiate $4x^{3/2}$:

$\;\;\;\tfrac{d}{dx}\!\left(4x^{3/2}\right) = 4 \cdot \tfrac{3}{2}\, x^{(3/2) - 1} = 6\, x^{1/2}$

Term 2 — differentiate $k\, x^{-2}$:

$\;\;\;\tfrac{d}{dx}\!\left(k\, x^{-2}\right) = k \cdot (-2)\, x^{-2 - 1} = -2k\, x^{-3}$

Sum:

$$\boxed{\;f''(x) = 6x^{1/2} - 2k\, x^{-3}\;}$$

Always rewrite $\tfrac{1}{x^2}$ as $x^{-2}$ first before differentiating — the power rule needs an exponent, not a fraction. Watch the new exponent for negative powers: $-2 - 1 = -3$ (not $-1$). A common slip is to write $-2x^{-1}$ or to forget to multiply by the original $-2$.
$f''(x) = 6x^{1/2} - 2k\, x^{-3}$
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: $f''(x) = 6x^{1/2} - 2k\, x^{-3}$
STEP 3/4 M1

Strategy: we have $f''(x) = 6x^{1/2} - 2k\, x^{-3}$. The condition $f''(2) = 0$ (the question states $f'$ has a stationary point at $x = 2$) gives one equation in $k$.

Substitute $x = 2$:

$\;\;\;f''(2) = 6 \cdot 2^{1/2} - 2k \cdot 2^{-3}$

Simplify each piece:

$\;\;\;2^{1/2} = \sqrt{2}$,  and  $2^{-3} = \tfrac{1}{8}$

$\;\;\;f''(2) = 6\sqrt{2} - \tfrac{2k}{8} = 6\sqrt{2} - \tfrac{k}{4}$

Set equal to $0$ (stationary point condition):

$\;\;\;6\sqrt{2} - \tfrac{k}{4} = 0$

Add $\tfrac{k}{4}$ to both sides:

$$\boxed{\;\dfrac{k}{4} = 6\sqrt{2}\;}$$

Why $f''(2) = 0$ matters: the question specifies that $f'$ has a stationary point at $x = 2$ — meaning the slope of $f'$ is zero there, i.e. $\tfrac{d}{dx}f'(x) = f''(x) = 0$. This is the condition that pins down $k$. Don't confuse with $f$ stationary: if it had been $f'(2) = 0$ instead, that would be a stationary point of $f$ — a different condition, leading to a different $k$.
$\dfrac{k}{4} = 6\sqrt{2}$
↳ from STEP 3:From STEP 3: $\dfrac{k}{4} = 6\sqrt{2}$
STEP 4/4 A1

Strategy: isolate $k$ by multiplying both sides of $\tfrac{k}{4} = 6\sqrt{2}$ by $4$. Keep everything in surd form — the question wants exact.

Multiply both sides by $4$:

$\;\;\;k = 4 \times 6\sqrt{2}$

$\;\;\;k = 24\sqrt{2}$

$$\boxed{\;k = 24\sqrt{2}\;}$$

"Exact" means keep the surd: do NOT write $k \approx 33.94$. Examiners deduct marks for converting to decimal — the answer must be in the form $a\sqrt{b}$ with $b$ square-free. Verify: $24\sqrt{2}/4 = 6\sqrt{2}$ ✓, matching the previous step.
$k = 24\sqrt{2}$

Part (b) — Find $f(x)$ [4 marks] · 4 steps

From (a): $k = 24\sqrt{2}$, so $f'(x) = 4x^{3/2} + 24\sqrt{2}\, x^{-2}$.
STEP 1/4 M1

Strategy: with $k = 24\sqrt{2}$ from part (a), $f'(x) = 4x^{3/2} + 24\sqrt{2}\, x^{-2}$. Integrate term-by-term using the power rule for integration; the constant of integration $C$ will be pinned down later using $P(2, 8\sqrt{2})$.

Formula (power rule for integration, $n \ne -1$):

$$\int x^n\,dx = \dfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C$$

Term 1 — integrate $4x^{3/2}$:

$\;\;\;\int 4x^{3/2}\,dx = 4 \cdot \dfrac{x^{(3/2)+1}}{(3/2)+1} = 4 \cdot \dfrac{x^{5/2}}{5/2}$

$\;\;\;\phantom{\int 4x^{3/2}\,dx} = 4 \cdot \tfrac{2}{5}\, x^{5/2} = \tfrac{8}{5}x^{5/2}$

Term 2 — integrate $24\sqrt{2}\, x^{-2}$:

$\;\;\;\int 24\sqrt{2}\, x^{-2}\,dx = 24\sqrt{2} \cdot \dfrac{x^{-2 + 1}}{-2 + 1} = 24\sqrt{2} \cdot \dfrac{x^{-1}}{-1}$

$\;\;\;\phantom{\int 24\sqrt{2}\, x^{-2}\,dx} = -24\sqrt{2}\, x^{-1} = -\dfrac{24\sqrt{2}}{x}$

$$\boxed{\;f(x) = \tfrac{8}{5}x^{5/2} - \dfrac{24\sqrt{2}}{x} + C\;}$$

Trap with $x^{-2}$: the new exponent is $-2 + 1 = -1$ (not $-3$), and dividing by $-1$ flips the sign. Don't confuse with the rule $\int x^{-1}\,dx = \ln|x|$ — that's only when $n = -1$ exactly. Here $n = -2$, so the standard power rule applies.
$f(x) = \tfrac{8}{5}x^{5/2} - \dfrac{24\sqrt{2}}{x} + C$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $f(x) = \tfrac{8}{5}x^{5/2} - \dfrac{24\sqrt{2}}{x} + C$
STEP 2/4 B1

Strategy: we'll plug $x = 2$ into $f(x)$ shortly; first, simplify the non-trivial surd power $2^{5/2}$ in advance so we're not juggling fractions of fractions.

Index-law expansion: $2^{5/2} = 2^{2 + 1/2} = 2^2 \cdot 2^{1/2}$:

$\;\;\;2^{5/2} = 4 \cdot \sqrt{2} = 4\sqrt{2}$

Apply to first term:

$\;\;\;\tfrac{8}{5}\, (2)^{5/2} = \tfrac{8}{5} \cdot 4\sqrt{2} = \dfrac{32\sqrt{2}}{5}$

Second term at $x = 2$:

$\;\;\;\dfrac{24\sqrt{2}}{2} = 12\sqrt{2}$

$$\boxed{\;2^{5/2} = 4\sqrt{2}\;}$$

Memorise the powers-of-2 surd ladder: $2^{1/2} = \sqrt{2}$, $2^{3/2} = 2\sqrt{2}$, $2^{5/2} = 4\sqrt{2}$, $2^{7/2} = 8\sqrt{2}$ — each step multiplies by $2$. Why pre-simplify: avoids ugly fractions like $\tfrac{8}{5}\cdot 2^{5/2}$ in the next step, which is error-prone.
$2^{5/2} = 4\sqrt{2}$
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: $2^{5/2} = 4\sqrt{2}$
STEP 3/4 M1

Strategy: $P(2, 8\sqrt{2})$ on $C$ means $f(2) = 8\sqrt{2}$. Substitute and solve the resulting equation for the integration constant $C$.

Substitute $x = 2$ into $f(x) = \tfrac{8}{5}x^{5/2} - \dfrac{24\sqrt{2}}{x} + C$:

$\;\;\;f(2) = \tfrac{8}{5}(2)^{5/2} - \dfrac{24\sqrt{2}}{2} + C$

Plug in the simplifications from the previous step:

$\;\;\;f(2) = \tfrac{32\sqrt{2}}{5} - 12\sqrt{2} + C$

Set equal to $8\sqrt{2}$:

$\;\;\;8\sqrt{2} = \tfrac{32\sqrt{2}}{5} - 12\sqrt{2} + C$

Solve for $C$ — collect $\sqrt{2}$ terms on the RHS:

$\;\;\;C = 8\sqrt{2} + 12\sqrt{2} - \tfrac{32\sqrt{2}}{5}$

$\;\;\;C = 20\sqrt{2} - \tfrac{32\sqrt{2}}{5}$

Common denominator $5$ — write $20\sqrt{2} = \tfrac{100\sqrt{2}}{5}$:

$\;\;\;C = \tfrac{100\sqrt{2}}{5} - \tfrac{32\sqrt{2}}{5} = \tfrac{68\sqrt{2}}{5}$

$$\boxed{\;C = \dfrac{68\sqrt{2}}{5}\;}$$

Collect like-surds the same way as like-terms: $20\sqrt{2} - \tfrac{32\sqrt{2}}{5} = (20 - \tfrac{32}{5})\sqrt{2} = \tfrac{68}{5}\sqrt{2}$. Common-denominator slip: turning $20$ into $\tfrac{100}{5}$ (not $\tfrac{20}{5}$) is essential — multiply numerator and denominator by $5$.
$C = \dfrac{68\sqrt{2}}{5}$
↳ from STEP 3:From STEP 3: $C = \dfrac{68\sqrt{2}}{5}$
STEP 4/4 A1

Strategy: assemble the final $f(x)$ by inserting $C = \tfrac{68\sqrt{2}}{5}$ into the formula from step 1. Check by re-evaluating $f(2)$.

Final form:

$$\boxed{\;f(x) = \dfrac{8}{5}x^{5/2} - \dfrac{24\sqrt{2}}{x} + \dfrac{68\sqrt{2}}{5}\;}$$

Final sanity check via $f(2)$: $f(2) = \tfrac{32\sqrt{2}}{5} - 12\sqrt{2} + \tfrac{68\sqrt{2}}{5} = \tfrac{32\sqrt{2} + 68\sqrt{2}}{5} - 12\sqrt{2} = \tfrac{100\sqrt{2}}{5} - 12\sqrt{2} = 20\sqrt{2} - 12\sqrt{2} = 8\sqrt{2}$ ✓ — matches $P$'s $y$-coordinate. Sanity-check the form: $f$ has three terms — the integral of the $x^{3/2}$ piece, the integral of the $kx^{-2}$ piece, and the constant $C$. All three present, all three with surd coefficients (since $k = 24\sqrt{2}$ propagated through).
$f(x) = \tfrac{8}{5}x^{5/2} - \tfrac{24\sqrt{2}}{x} + \tfrac{68\sqrt{2}}{5}$
