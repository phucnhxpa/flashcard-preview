Q5 — Region where $y < x + 2$ AND $y > x^2 - x$

Question

Identify the region of the $xy$-plane satisfying both $y < x + 2$ and $y > x^2 - x$ simultaneously.

[4 marks]

Solution [4 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3

Strategy: before sketching anything, fix the rule for which side of a boundary to shade. There's just one rule: it depends on whether $y$ is less than or greater than the boundary expression.

Rule (vertical reading of $y$):

$\;\;y < f(x)$  ⇒ shade the region BELOW the curve $y = f(x)$.

$\;\;y > f(x)$  ⇒ shade the region ABOVE the curve $y = f(x)$.

Rule (boundary style):

$\;\;$Strict ($<, >$) ⇒ dashed boundary (points on the curve excluded).

$\;\;$Non-strict ($\le, \ge$) ⇒ solid boundary (points on the curve included).

Mnemonic: $y$ is the vertical coordinate, so "$y$ less than" naturally means lower (smaller $y$ = lower), "$y$ greater than" means upper. Test point trick: if unsure, substitute $(0, 0)$ into the inequality. If TRUE, $(0, 0)$ is in the region, so shade that side; if FALSE, shade the other side. (Avoid $(0,0)$ if it lies on the boundary.)
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: Rule: $y < f(x)$ ⇒ shade below; $y > f(x)$ ⇒ shade above; strict ⇒ dashed boundary.
STEP 2/3 M1

Strategy: identify each boundary curve, sketch it (dashed because both inequalities are strict), then choose which side to shade using the rule from STEP 1.

(i) $y < x + 2$ — boundary is the line $y = x + 2$:

$\;\;$gradient $= 1$, $y$-intercept $= 2$.

$\;\;$Strict $<$ ⇒ dashed.

$\;\;y < \ldots$ ⇒ shade below the line.

(ii) $y > x^2 - x$ — boundary is the parabola $y = x^2 - x = x(x-1)$:

$\;\;$Roots: $x = 0$ and $x = 1$ (upward parabola, $a > 0$).

$\;\;$Vertex: $x = \tfrac{0+1}{2} = \tfrac{1}{2}$, $y = \tfrac{1}{4} - \tfrac{1}{2} = -\tfrac{1}{4}$.

$\;\;$So vertex $= \bigl(\tfrac{1}{2}, -\tfrac{1}{4}\bigr)$.

$\;\;$Strict $>$ ⇒ dashed.

$\;\;y > \ldots$ ⇒ shade above the parabola.

Origin test $(0,0)$ as a sanity check: Plug $(0,0)$ into each inequality. (1) $0 < 0 + 2 = 2$ ✓ — origin IS in region 1, so shading below the line correctly includes $(0,0)$. (2) $0 > 0^2 - 0 = 0$ ✗ — origin is NOT in region 2 (it's on the parabola, in fact). Both are consistent with the rule.
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: below the line $y = x+2$ (dashed) AND above the parabola $y = x^2-x$ (dashed)
STEP 3/3 A1

Strategy: the region satisfying BOTH inequalities is the intersection — a lens-shaped region above the parabola AND below the line. To describe it precisely, find where the boundaries cross.

Step 1 — Equate boundaries (line $=$ parabola):

$\;\;x + 2 = x^2 - x$

Bring everything to one side:

$\;\;x^2 - 2x - 2 = 0$

Step 2 — Read coefficients: $a = 1,\ b = -2,\ c = -2$.

Formula (quadratic, in booklet):

$$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$$

Step 3 — Substitute and simplify:

$\;\;b^2 - 4ac = (-2)^2 - 4(1)(-2) = 4 + 8 = 12$

$\;\;\sqrt{12} = \sqrt{4 \cdot 3} = 2\sqrt{3}$

$\;\;x = \dfrac{-(-2) \pm 2\sqrt{3}}{2(1)} = \dfrac{2 \pm 2\sqrt{3}}{2} = 1 \pm \sqrt{3}$

$$\boxed{\;x = 1 - \sqrt{3}\ \text{or}\ x = 1 + \sqrt{3}\;}$$

Sense check: $\sqrt{3} \approx 1.732$, so the lens spans roughly $x \in (-0.73,\ 2.73)$ — straddles the parabola's vertex at $x = \tfrac{1}{2}$ ✓. Boundaries excluded: the strict $<$ and $>$ in the original inequalities means the curves themselves are NOT part of the region — show them dashed in any sketch.
