Q8(a) — Show $C_1 \cap C_2$ satisfies $2x^4 - 7x^2 - 15 = 0$

Question

Two curves are defined as:

$C_1$:   $xy = \dfrac{15}{2} + 5x$    (i.e.  $y = \dfrac{15}{2x} + 5$ for $x \ne 0$)

$C_2$:   $y = x^3 - \dfrac{7}{2}x + 5$

(a) Show that the $x$-coordinates of points where $C_1$ meets $C_2$ satisfy $\;2x^4 - 7x^2 - 15 = 0$. [2]

[2 marks]

Solution [2 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2 M1

Strategy: intersection of two curves means equal $y$-values at the same $x$. Set the $y$-expressions equal and look for instant cancellations to keep the algebra clean.

Rearrange $C_1$ to give $y$ explicitly:

$\;\;\;xy = \tfrac{15}{2} + 5x \;\Rightarrow\; y = \dfrac{15}{2x} + 5$  (divide by $x$, valid since $x \ne 0$)

Set equal to $C_2$:

$\;\;\;\dfrac{15}{2x} + 5 = x^3 - \tfrac{7}{2}x + 5$

Subtract $5$ from both sides — the constants cancel cleanly:

$\;\;\;\dfrac{15}{2x} = x^3 - \tfrac{7}{2}x$

$$\boxed{\;\dfrac{15}{2x} = x^3 - \tfrac{7}{2}x\;}$$

Tutor's hint: "the $+5$ cancels — keep it clean step by step." Always look for instant cancellations before multiplying through. Why not multiply by $2x$ first: doing so without first cancelling $+5$ would introduce a $10x$ on each side that you'd immediately have to subtract — wasted work and a common source of sign errors.
$\dfrac{15}{2x} = x^3 - \tfrac{7}{2}x$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $\dfrac{15}{2x} = x^3 - \tfrac{7}{2}x$
STEP 2/2 A1

Strategy: the LHS of $\tfrac{15}{2x} = x^3 - \tfrac{7}{2}x$ has a denominator $2x$. Clear it by multiplying every term by $2x$ (legitimate as $x \ne 0$ on $C_1$), then collect to give the target form.

Multiply each term by $2x$:

$\;\;\;2x \cdot \dfrac{15}{2x} = 2x \cdot x^3 - 2x \cdot \tfrac{7}{2}x$

Simplify each piece:

$\;\;\;2x \cdot \dfrac{15}{2x} = 15$  ($2x$ cancels)

$\;\;\;2x \cdot x^3 = 2x^4$

$\;\;\;2x \cdot \tfrac{7}{2}x = 7x^2$

So:

$\;\;\;15 = 2x^4 - 7x^2$

Bring all terms to one side (subtract 15):

$$\boxed{\;2x^4 - 7x^2 - 15 = 0\;}\qquad\blacksquare$$

Domain caveat: $C_1$ requires $x \ne 0$ (else $y$ undefined) — multiplying by $2x$ is legal here. Always state $x \ne 0$ on a "show that" involving a fraction. Sense check via $u = x^2$: $2u^2 - 7u - 15 = (2u + 3)(u - 5) = 0$, giving $u = 5$ (positive ⇒ valid) or $u = -\tfrac{3}{2}$ (negative ⇒ rejected). So $x^2 = 5 \Rightarrow x = \pm\sqrt{5}$ — two real intersections, as expected.
$2x^4 - 7x^2 - 15 = 0$ ✓
