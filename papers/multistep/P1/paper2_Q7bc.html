Q7(b,c) — $f(x) = 2x^3 - kx^2 + 14x + 24$; curves $y=f'(x)$ and $y=f''(x)$ meet at $A$ ($x_A=5$) and $B$

Question

$f(x) = 2x^3 - kx^2 + 14x + 24$ where $k$ is a constant.

From part (a): $f'(x) = 6x^2 - 2kx + 14$ and $f''(x) = 12x - 2k$.

The curves $y = f'(x)$ and $y = f''(x)$ meet at points $A$ and $B$, with $x_A = 5$.

(b) Find the value of $k$. [2]

(c) Hence find the coordinates of $B$. [3]

[5 marks]

Part (b) — Find $k$ [2 marks] · 2 steps

From (a): $f'(x) = 6x^2 - 2kx + 14$, $f''(x) = 12x - 2k$. Given: $A$ is a common point on both curves with $x_A = 5$.
STEP 1/2 M1

Strategy: we have an unknown $k$ and need exactly one equation to fix it. The given fact "$A$ lies on both curves at $x = 5$" provides that equation: at the common point, both formulas $f'(x)$ and $f''(x)$ must produce the same $y$.

Geometric idea: if $A = (5, y_A)$ is on $y = f'(x)$ then $y_A = f'(5)$; if $A$ is also on $y = f''(x)$ then $y_A = f''(5)$. Same $y_A$ on each curve ⇒

$$\boxed{\;f'(5) = f''(5)\;}$$

One equation, one unknown ($k$) — solvable.

Phuc's noted gap: "I didn't notice that we just need to substitute the coordinates of A into the equation and then solve for k." The recipe: "point on both curves" ⇒ plug the $x$-coord into both formulas and set them equal. The $y$-coordinate of $A$ never gets used directly — it cancels by appearing on both sides of the equation.
$f'(5) = f''(5)$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $f'(5) = f''(5)$
STEP 2/2 A1

Strategy: evaluate $f'(5)$ and $f''(5)$ separately (carefully — keep $k$ as a literal symbol), then equate and solve the resulting linear equation in $k$.

Compute $f'(5)$ from $f'(x) = 6x^2 - 2kx + 14$:

$\;\;\;f'(5) = 6(5)^2 - 2k(5) + 14$

$\;\;\;\phantom{f'(5)} = 6 \cdot 25 - 10k + 14$

$\;\;\;\phantom{f'(5)} = 150 - 10k + 14 = 164 - 10k$

Compute $f''(5)$ from $f''(x) = 12x - 2k$:

$\;\;\;f''(5) = 12(5) - 2k = 60 - 2k$

Set equal (the equation from the previous step):

$\;\;\;164 - 10k = 60 - 2k$

Add $10k$ to both sides:

$\;\;\;164 = 60 + 8k$

Subtract $60$ from both sides:

$\;\;\;104 = 8k$

Divide by $8$:

$$\boxed{\;k = 13\;}$$

Sense check: with $k = 13$, $f'(5) = 150 - 130 + 14 = 34$ and $f''(5) = 60 - 26 = 34$ ✓ (both sides match). Common slip: writing $-2k(5) = -2k \cdot 5 = -10k$ is fine, but a sign-flip gives $+10k$ — that would yield $k = 13/12$, a non-integer that should ring an alarm on a tidy exam question.
$k = 13$

Part (c) — Coordinates of $B$ [3 marks] · 3 steps

From (b): $k = 13$, so $f'(x) = 6x^2 - 26x + 14$ and $f''(x) = 12x - 26$. $A$ has $x_A = 5$.
STEP 1/3 M1

Strategy: intersection points of two curves $y = f'(x)$ and $y = f''(x)$ have $f'(x) = f''(x)$. Solve that as an equation in $x$, then read off both roots — one is $x_A = 5$ (known), the other is $x_B$ (unknown).

Substitute $k = 13$ into the formulas first: $f'(x) = 6x^2 - 26x + 14$ and $f''(x) = 12x - 26$.

Set $f'(x) = f''(x)$:

$\;\;\;6x^2 - 26x + 14 = 12x - 26$

Subtract $12x$ from both sides:

$\;\;\;6x^2 - 26x - 12x + 14 = -26$

$\;\;\;6x^2 - 38x + 14 = -26$

Add $26$ to both sides (move all to LHS):

$\;\;\;6x^2 - 38x + 40 = 0$

Divide through by $2$ (every coefficient even):

$$\boxed{\;3x^2 - 19x + 20 = 0\;}$$

Why a quadratic? $f'$ is degree 2, $f''$ is degree 1; their difference is degree 2 ⇒ at most 2 intersection points. We already know one ($A$ at $x=5$); the quadratic gives both roots simultaneously. Why divide by 2: smaller coefficients make the factorisation hunt easier (and $\gcd(6, 38, 40) = 2$).
$3x^2 - 19x + 20 = 0$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $3x^2 - 19x + 20 = 0$
STEP 2/3 M1

Strategy: we already know $x = 5$ is a root (it's point $A$'s $x$-coordinate). By the factor theorem, $(x - 5)$ is a factor of the quadratic. Divide it out, then read off the other root.

Factor theorem: if $p(a) = 0$ then $(x - a)$ is a factor of $p(x)$.

Verify $x = 5$ is a root of $3x^2 - 19x + 20$:

$\;\;\;3(5)^2 - 19(5) + 20 = 75 - 95 + 20 = 0$ ✓

Factorise as $(x-5)(3x - p) = 3x^2 - 19x + 20$. Expanding the LHS:

$\;\;\;(x-5)(3x - p) = 3x^2 - px - 15x + 5p = 3x^2 - (p+15)x + 5p$

Match coefficients: $5p = 20 \Rightarrow p = 4$ (constant); check $p + 15 = 19$ ✓ (middle term).

$\;\;\;3x^2 - 19x + 20 = (x - 5)(3x - 4) = 0$

Apply zero-product property — pick the root that is NOT $x_A = 5$:

$\;\;\;3x - 4 = 0$

$\;\;\;3x = 4$

$$\boxed{\;x_B = \tfrac{4}{3}\;}$$

Alternative — split-the-middle: need two integers with product $ac = 3 \cdot 20 = 60$ and sum $b = -19$ → $(-15, -4)$. Then $3x^2 - 15x - 4x + 20 = 3x(x - 5) - 4(x - 5) = (3x - 4)(x - 5)$ — same factorisation. Vieta cross-check: sum of roots $= 19/3$; here $5 + \tfrac{4}{3} = \tfrac{15+4}{3} = \tfrac{19}{3}$ ✓.
$x_B = \tfrac{4}{3}$
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: $x_B = \tfrac{4}{3}$
STEP 3/3 A1

Strategy: $B$ has $x_B = \tfrac{4}{3}$ — now we need its $y$-coordinate. $B$ lies on both $y = f'(x)$ and $y = f''(x)$, so plug $x_B$ into either; pick the simpler one.

Pick the linear $f''$: $f''(x) = 12x - 26$ has no squared term, so the arithmetic with the fraction $\tfrac{4}{3}$ stays clean.

Substitute $x = \tfrac{4}{3}$:

$\;\;\;y_B = f''\!\left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right) = 12 \cdot \tfrac{4}{3} - 26$

$\;\;\;\phantom{y_B} = \tfrac{48}{3} - 26 = 16 - 26 = -10$

$$\boxed{\;B = \left(\tfrac{4}{3},\, -10\right)\;}$$

Cross-check via $f'$ (the quadratic): $f'\!\left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right) = 6 \cdot \left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right)^2 - 26 \cdot \tfrac{4}{3} + 14 = 6 \cdot \tfrac{16}{9} - \tfrac{104}{3} + 14$
$= \tfrac{96}{9} - \tfrac{104}{3} + 14 = \tfrac{32}{3} - \tfrac{104}{3} + \tfrac{42}{3} = \tfrac{-30}{3} = -10$ ✓ — matches. Why this works as a check: since $B$ is on BOTH curves by construction, $f'(x_B)$ and $f''(x_B)$ must agree.
$\boxed{\;B = \left(\tfrac{4}{3},\, -10\right)\;}$
