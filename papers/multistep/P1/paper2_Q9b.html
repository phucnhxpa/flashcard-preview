Q9(b) — Find both possible $C$ on $l_2$ with $BC = 5\sqrt{5}$

Question

$A = (4, 2)$ and $B = (15, 7)$. Line $l_1$ passes through $A$ and $B$ (part (a) gave $5x - 11y + 2 = 0$).

$l_2$ passes through $A$ and is parallel to the $x$-axis. Point $C$ lies on $l_2$ with $BC = 5\sqrt{5}$.

(b) Find the two possible coordinates of $C$. [4]

[4 marks]

Solution [4 marks] · 4 steps

Given: $A = (4, 2)$, $B = (15, 7)$, $l_2$ is the horizontal line through $A$, $C \in l_2$ with $|BC| = 5\sqrt{5}$.
STEP 1/4 B1

Parallel to the $x$-axis ⇒ horizontal line ⇒ $y = $ constant.

It passes through $A(4, 2)$, so its $y$-coordinate is $2$:

$$\boxed{\;l_2:\ y = 2\;}$$

Phuc's noted gap: "I didn't know how to do (b) — tutor said '$l_2$ parallel to $x$-axis through $A(4,2)$' just means $y = 2$."
Twin rule (memorise the pair):
• Parallel to $x$-axis through $(a, b)$ ⇒ $y = b$ (horizontal line).
• Parallel to $y$-axis through $(a, b)$ ⇒ $x = a$ (vertical line).
The constant matches the coordinate that doesn't change as you walk along the line.
$l_2:\ y = 2$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $l_2:\; y = 2$
STEP 2/4

Strategy: $C$ is constrained to lie on $l_2$ (so its $y$ is fixed) but its $x$ is free. Introduce a single unknown for the free coordinate, ready for the distance equation.

Every point on $l_2: y = 2$ has $y$-coordinate equal to $2$ (by definition of the line). The $x$-coordinate can be anything, so call it $x$:

$$\boxed{\;C = (x,\, 2)\;}$$

This is a one-parameter family of candidate points; the distance condition will pin $x$ down (probably to two values, one on each side of $B$).

Why parametrise: rather than guessing locations for $C$, we encode the line-membership constraint directly into the coordinates. After this step, we have ONE unknown ($x$) and ONE upcoming condition ($|BC| = 5\sqrt{5}$) — solvable.
$C = (x,\, 2)$
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: $C = (x,\, 2)$
STEP 3/4 M1

Strategy: use the distance formula in squared form. Squaring both sides of $|BC| = 5\sqrt{5}$ avoids the square root and the $\pm$ sign issues, leaving a clean polynomial equation.

Formula (distance, squared):

$$d^2 = (x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2$$

Substitute $B = (x_1, y_1) = (15, 7)$ and $C = (x_2, y_2) = (x, 2)$:

$\;\;\;BC^2 = (x - 15)^2 + (2 - 7)^2$

$\;\;\;\phantom{BC^2} = (x - 15)^2 + (-5)^2$

$\;\;\;\phantom{BC^2} = (x - 15)^2 + 25$

Compute the RHS $(5\sqrt{5})^2$ — square the coefficient AND the surd:

$\;\;\;(5\sqrt{5})^2 = 5^2 \cdot (\sqrt{5})^2 = 25 \cdot 5 = 125$

Set equal ($BC^2 = 125$):

$\;\;\;(x - 15)^2 + 25 = 125$

Subtract $25$ from both sides:

$$\boxed{\;(x - 15)^2 = 100\;}$$

Square the distance, don't take roots: squaring both sides removes the square root in the distance formula and avoids sign issues. Always re-square the RHS too: $(5\sqrt{5})^2 = 125$, not $5\sqrt{5}$ — easy slip. Sign-symmetry note: $(15 - x)^2 = (x - 15)^2$ since squaring kills the sign — either order works for the bracket.
$(x - 15)^2 = 100$
↳ from STEP 3:From STEP 3: $(x - 15)^2 = 100$
STEP 4/4 A1 A1

Strategy: take square roots of $(x - 15)^2 = 100$. The CRUCIAL move is to include $\pm$: a single squared expression admits two real signed roots, giving the two possible $C$ positions.

Take square roots with $\pm$:

$\;\;\;x - 15 = \pm \sqrt{100} = \pm 10$

Case +:

$\;\;\;x - 15 = +10$

$\;\;\;x = 25$  ⇒   $C_2 = (25, 2)$

Case −:

$\;\;\;x - 15 = -10$

$\;\;\;x = 5$  ⇒   $C_1 = (5, 2)$

$$\boxed{\;C = (5,\, 2)\quad\text{or}\quad C = (25,\, 2)\;}$$

Why two answers? The condition $|BC| = 5\sqrt{5}$ describes a circle of radius $5\sqrt{5}$ centred at $B(15, 7)$. The line $y = 2$ cuts that circle (generically) in 2 places — one on each side of $B$'s vertical $x = 15$. Sense-check distances: $|BC_1|$ for $C_1 = (5, 2)$: $\sqrt{(15-5)^2 + (7-2)^2} = \sqrt{100 + 25} = \sqrt{125} = 5\sqrt{5}$ ✓. $|BC_2|$ for $C_2 = (25, 2)$: $\sqrt{(15-25)^2 + 25} = \sqrt{125} = 5\sqrt{5}$ ✓.
$C = (5,\, 2)$ or $C = (25,\, 2)$
