Q4 — $C_1, C_2$ meet at one point: show $k^2+26k+169=0$, then find $P$

Question

$C_1: y = x^2 + kx - 9$    $C_2: y = -3x^2 - 5x + k$.

Given $C_1$ and $C_2$ meet at a single point $P$:

(a) Show that $k^2 + 26k + 169 = 0$. [3]

(b) Hence find the coordinates of $P$. [3]

[6 marks]

Part (a) — Show $k^2 + 26k + 169 = 0$ [3 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3 M1

Strategy: at an intersection point, both curves share the same $y$ value. Equating their RHSs gives a single equation in $x$ (with $k$ as a parameter).

Set $C_1 = C_2$:

$\;\;x^2 + kx - 9 = -3x^2 - 5x + k$

Bring all terms to the LHS (add $3x^2$, add $5x$, subtract $k$):

$\;\;x^2 + 3x^2 + kx + 5x - 9 - k = 0$

Group like terms:

$\;\;4x^2 + (k+5)x - (9+k) = 0$

$$\boxed{\;4x^2 + (k+5)x - (9+k) = 0\;}$$

Why this form: a quadratic in $x$ with coefficients $a = 4$, $b = k+5$, $c = -(9+k)$. The $x$-values where the two curves meet are exactly the roots of this quadratic. Sign care: when moving $-3x^2$ to the left it becomes $+3x^2$ (combine with $x^2$ to get $4x^2$); $-5x$ becomes $+5x$ (combine with $kx$ to get $(k+5)x$); the lone $k$ on the right becomes $-k$ on the left (combine with $-9$ to get $-(9+k)$).
$4x^2 + (k+5)x - (9+k) = 0$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $4x^2 + (k+5)x - (9+k) = 0$
STEP 2/3 M1

Strategy: the discriminant $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$ counts how many real roots a quadratic has. "Meet at a SINGLE point" means the quadratic $4x^2 + (k+5)x - (9+k) = 0$ has exactly ONE real root (a repeated root) — i.e. $\Delta = 0$.

Trigger-word table for $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$:

$\;\;$"meets at one point" / "tangent" / "repeated root" $\;\Rightarrow\; \Delta = 0$.

$\;\;$"two distinct points" / "crosses twice" $\;\Rightarrow\; \Delta > 0$.

$\;\;$"never crosses" / "no real solutions" $\;\Rightarrow\; \Delta < 0$.

So we set $\Delta = 0$:

$$\boxed{\;b^2 - 4ac = 0\;}$$

Phuc's insight: "How do you know to use the discriminant? Because the only variable in $\Delta$ is $k$, and the thing they want me to show only has $k$ too." That trigger-word logic is the whole reason discriminant is the right tool here.
Set $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac = 0$
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: Set $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac = 0$
STEP 3/3 A1

Formula (discriminant):

$$\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$$

Substitute $a = 4,\ b = k+5,\ c = -(9+k)$:

$\;\;\Delta = (k+5)^2 - 4(4)\bigl(-(9+k)\bigr)$

Simplify $-4(4)(-(9+k))$ — two minuses cancel:

$\;\;\Delta = (k+5)^2 + 16(9+k)$

Expand $(k+5)^2$ using $(a+b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2$:

$\;\;(k+5)^2 = k^2 + 2(k)(5) + 5^2 = k^2 + 10k + 25$

Distribute $16$:

$\;\;16(9 + k) = 144 + 16k$

Add the two expansions:

$\;\;\Delta = k^2 + 10k + 25 + 144 + 16k$

$\;\;\phantom{\Delta} = k^2 + (10k + 16k) + (25 + 144)$

$\;\;\phantom{\Delta} = k^2 + 26k + 169$

Set $\Delta = 0$:

$$\boxed{\;k^2 + 26k + 169 = 0\;}\quad\blacksquare$$

Sign trap: $-4ac = -4(4)(-(9+k)) = +16(9+k)$. The two minuses cancel — easy to lose one. Tip: when $c$ is a parenthesised expression like $-(9+k)$, keep it bracketed all the way through the substitution, then collapse the signs in one step.
$k^2 + 26k + 169 = 0$ ✓

Part (b) — Find $P$ [3 marks] · 3 steps

From (a): $k^2 + 26k + 169 = 0$ and the quadratic in $x$ is $4x^2 + (k+5)x - (9+k) = 0$.
STEP 1/3 M1

Strategy: spot the perfect-square pattern. A trinomial of form $k^2 + 2\alpha k + \alpha^2$ factors as $(k + \alpha)^2$.

Check the pattern:

$\;\;169 = 13^2$ ⇒ $\alpha = 13$.

$\;\;2\alpha = 2 \cdot 13 = 26$ ✓ matches the middle coefficient.

So:

$\;\;k^2 + 26k + 169 = (k + 13)^2$

Set equal to zero:

$\;\;(k + 13)^2 = 0$

Take the square root:

$\;\;k + 13 = 0$

$$\boxed{\;k = -13\;}$$

Repeated-root sense check: the discriminant equation itself was forced to $\Delta = 0$ in (a), so it's no surprise the resulting equation in $k$ has a double root — there is only one value of $k$ that places the curves tangent. Backup method: if you don't spot the perfect square, the quadratic formula gives $k = \dfrac{-26 \pm \sqrt{676 - 676}}{2} = -13$ (double root) anyway.
$k = -13$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $k = -13$
STEP 2/3 M1

Strategy: we have the quadratic in $x$ from part (a). Substituting $k = -13$ gives a numerical quadratic. Since $\Delta = 0$ at this $k$, we expect a repeated root.

Substitute $k = -13$ into $4x^2 + (k+5)x - (9 + k) = 0$:

$\;\;4x^2 + (-13 + 5)x - (9 + (-13)) = 0$

$\;\;4x^2 + (-8)x - (-4) = 0$

$\;\;4x^2 - 8x + 4 = 0$

Divide all terms by $4$ to simplify:

$\;\;x^2 - 2x + 1 = 0$

Spot the perfect square: $1 = 1^2$, $2 = 2 \cdot 1$ ✓:

$\;\;(x - 1)^2 = 0$

$$\boxed{\;x = 1\ \text{(repeated)}\;}$$

Sign care: $-(9 + (-13)) = -(-4) = +4$ — two sign flips. Sense check: a repeated root is exactly what the geometry predicts (curves meet at one point). If you got two distinct $x$-values here, you've made an arithmetic slip.
$x = 1$ (repeated)
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: $x = 1$ (repeated)
STEP 3/3 A1

Strategy: we know the $x$-coordinate of $P$ and the value of $k$. Substitute both into either $C_1$ or $C_2$ to get the $y$-coordinate. Use the OTHER curve as a self-check.

Use $C_1$: $y = x^2 + kx - 9$ with $k = -13$, $x = 1$:

$\;\;y = (1)^2 + (-13)(1) - 9$

$\;\;\phantom{y} = 1 - 13 - 9$

$\;\;\phantom{y} = -21$

$$\boxed{\;P = (1,\, -21)\;}$$

Check with $C_2$: $y = -3x^2 - 5x + k$ with $x = 1, k = -13$:
$y = -3(1)^2 - 5(1) + (-13) = -3 - 5 - 13 = -21$ ✓ — both curves give the same $y$, confirming $P$ lies on both. Why this matters: a tiny arithmetic error in $C_1$ might give a wrong $y$ that still looks plausible; the cross-check on $C_2$ is the cheapest way to catch it.
$\boxed{P = (1,\, -21)}$
