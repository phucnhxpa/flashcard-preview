Q4(d) — How many real solutions of $(x+2)(x^3+4x^2+4x) = 1$?

Question

Earlier parts established the factorisation $x^3 + 4x^2 + 4x = x(x+2)^2$ and sketched both $y = x(x+2)^2$ and $y = \dfrac{1}{x+2}$ on one set of axes (the hyperbola has a vertical asymptote at $x = -2$).

(d) Hence state, with reason, the number of real solutions of $(x+2)(x^3+4x^2+4x) = 1$. [1]

[1 marks]

Solution [1 marks] · 2 steps

From (a)–(c): the cubic $y = x(x+2)^2$ touches the $x$-axis at $x=-2$, crosses at $x=0$. The hyperbola $y = \tfrac{1}{x+2}$ has asymptote $x = -2$.
STEP 1/2

Strategy: the question word "Hence" is a strong cue to use the previously established curves $y = x(x+2)^2$ and $y = \tfrac{1}{x+2}$. Massage the equation so each side matches one of those curves, then count intersections from the sketch.

Manipulate the equation:

$\;\;\;(x+2)\bigl(x^3 + 4x^2 + 4x\bigr) = 1$

Divide both sides by $(x + 2)$ (valid for $x \ne -2$):

$\;\;\;x^3 + 4x^2 + 4x = \dfrac{1}{x + 2}$

Use the part-(a) factorisation $x^3 + 4x^2 + 4x = x(x+2)^2$:

$\;\;\;x(x+2)^2 = \dfrac{1}{x + 2}$

$$\boxed{\;\text{Need: number of }x \text{ with } x(x+2)^2 = \tfrac{1}{x+2}\;}$$

"Hence" cue: the question gives us the two curves already sketched (parts (a)–(c)). Don't try to expand to a quartic and solve algebraically — just count intersections on the picture. Domain note: $x = -2$ is excluded (would make the hyperbola undefined and the LHS zero).

STEP 2/2 B1

Strategy: the asymptote $x = -2$ splits the plane into a left half ($x < -2$) and a right half ($x > -2$). Count intersections in each half by tracing the asymptotic behaviour of both curves at the boundaries.

Right of $x = -2$ (i.e. $x > -2$):

$\;\;\;$Cubic $y = x(x+2)^2$: starts at $(-2, 0)$, dips to local min $\approx (-\tfrac{2}{3}, -1.19)$, returns to $0$ at $x = 0$, then rises steeply.

$\;\;\;$Hyperbola $y = \tfrac{1}{x+2}$: enters from $+\infty$ as $x \to -2^+$, descends through $(0, 0.5)$, tends to $0^+$ as $x \to \infty$.

The hyperbola starts WAY above the cubic and ends BELOW it (cubic $\to \infty$ faster). By IVT they cross exactly once (near $x \approx 0.1$).

Left of $x = -2$ (i.e. $x < -2$):

$\;\;\;$Cubic: rises from $-\infty$ as $x \to -\infty$, reaching $0$ at $x = -2$.

$\;\;\;$Hyperbola: tends to $0^-$ as $x \to -\infty$, descends to $-\infty$ as $x \to -2^-$.

The hyperbola starts ABOVE the cubic (both near $0$) and ends BELOW it (cubic $\to 0$, hyperbola $\to -\infty$). By IVT they cross exactly once (near $x \approx -2.7$).

$$\boxed{\;\text{Total: } 2 \text{ real solutions}\;}$$

Watch: $x = -2$ itself is not a solution — the hyperbola is undefined there, and the cubic equals $0$ (and we'd have $\tfrac{1}{0}$ on the RHS). Why two and not four: if you tried to expand into a quartic $x(x+2)^3 = 1$, you'd get at most 4 real roots, but the picture shows only 2 actually exist.
$\boxed{2 \text{ real solutions}}$ — one on each side of the asymptote $x = -2$.
