Q8 — Triangle 4–6–9 cm; major sector of radius 4

Question

A triangle has sides $4$ cm, $6$ cm, and $9$ cm. Let $\alpha$ be the interior angle opposite the side of length $9$ cm.

(a) Find $\alpha$, in radians, to 3 s.f. [3]

(b) A circle has centre $O$ and radius $r = 4$ cm. Two radii from $O$ are separated by the angle $\alpha$ found in part (a). Find the area of the major sector to 3 s.f. [4]

[7 marks]

Part (a) — Find α [3 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3 M1

Strategy: for any non-right triangle, decide between sine rule and cosine rule by what's known.

Decision tree:

$\;\;$Sine rule — needs an angle paired with its opposite side.

$\;\;$Cosine rule — needs either (i) 3 sides ⇒ find any angle, or (ii) 2 sides + the included angle ⇒ find the third side.

Here: we have 3 sides ($4, 6, 9$) and want the angle opposite the 9-cm side. No angle–side pair is known yet, so sine rule can't start. Cosine rule is the unique fit.

Labelling convention: let the side opposite the wanted angle be $a$, and the angle itself be $A$ (or $\alpha$ here).

$\;\;a = 9$ (opposite $\alpha$),  $b = 4$,  $c = 6$.

Why this matters: mis-labelling so that $a$ is NOT opposite $\alpha$ produces wrong arithmetic. The formula $a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc\cos A$ requires $A$ and $a$ to be opposite each other — that pairing is non-negotiable.
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: cosine rule with $a=9$, $b=4$, $c=6$ opposite $\alpha$
STEP 2/3 A1

Formula (cosine rule, in booklet):

$$a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc\cos\alpha$$

Substitute $a = 9$, $b = 4$, $c = 6$:

$\;\;9^2 = 4^2 + 6^2 - 2(4)(6)\cos\alpha$

$\;\;81 = 16 + 36 - 48\cos\alpha$

$\;\;81 = 52 - 48\cos\alpha$

Isolate $\cos\alpha$. Subtract $52$ from both sides:

$\;\;29 = -48\cos\alpha$

Divide by $-48$:

$$\boxed{\;\cos\alpha = -\tfrac{29}{48}\;}$$

Sense check: $\cos\alpha < 0$ ⇒ $\alpha$ is obtuse ($\alpha > \tfrac{\pi}{2}$), consistent with the longest side ($9$ cm) being opposite it. Triangle-inequality reminder: $|b - c| < a < b + c$ ⇒ $|4-6| = 2 < 9 < 4+6 = 10$ ✓ — the triangle is valid.
$\cos\alpha = -\tfrac{29}{48}$
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: $\cos\alpha = -\tfrac{29}{48}$
STEP 3/3 A1

Strategy: apply $\cos^{-1}$ to both sides to undo the cosine. The question asks for radians, so set the calculator to RAD mode before computing.

$\;\;\alpha = \cos^{-1}\!\left(-\tfrac{29}{48}\right)$

Calculator (RAD): $-29/48 = -0.60416\ldots$

$\;\;\alpha = \cos^{-1}(-0.60416\ldots) = 2.21989\ldots\ \text{rad}$

Round to 3 s.f.:

$$\boxed{\;\alpha \approx 2.22\ \text{rad}\;}$$

Common slip: if you get $\approx 127$, your calculator is in DEG mode — switch to RAD ($127.18° = 2.2199$ rad). Range of $\cos^{-1}$: the principal value lies in $[0, \pi]$, i.e. $[0, 3.14]$ rad. Our answer $2.22$ is in $(\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \pi) \approx (1.57, 3.14)$ ⇒ obtuse ✓.
$\alpha \approx 2.22\ \text{rad}$ (3 s.f.)

Part (b) — Major sector area [4 marks] · 2 steps

From (a): $\alpha \approx 2.22\ \text{rad}$ (more precisely $\cos^{-1}\!\left(-\tfrac{29}{48}\right)$).
STEP 1/2 B1 M1

Strategy: two radii from the centre cut the disc into two sectors — a smaller minor sector (angle $\alpha$) and a larger major sector. Together they span the full disc.

Key fact: a full circle is $2\pi$ rad, so:

$$\theta_{\text{minor}} + \theta_{\text{major}} = 2\pi$$

Rearranging:

$$\theta_{\text{major}} = 2\pi - \theta_{\text{minor}} = 2\pi - \alpha$$

Substitute the UNROUNDED $\alpha = \cos^{-1}(-29/48) \approx 2.21989\ldots$:

$\;\;\theta_{\text{major}} = 2\pi - 2.21989\ldots$

$\;\;\phantom{\theta_{\text{major}}} = 6.28318\ldots - 2.21989\ldots$

$\;\;\phantom{\theta_{\text{major}}} = 4.0633\ldots\ \text{rad}$

$$\boxed{\;\theta_{\text{major}} \approx 4.063\ \text{rad}\;}$$

Don't round early: using $2.22$ instead of $2.21989\ldots$ gives $\theta_{\text{major}} \approx 4.063$ — same to 3 s.f. by luck here, but in tighter problems the 3rd s.f. drifts. Sense check: $\alpha$ is obtuse, so the minor sector is "more than half but less than full" — wait, $\alpha = 2.22 < \pi = 3.14$ so $\alpha$ is the minor angle ✓ (less than $\pi$). Major angle $4.06 > \pi$ as required.
$\theta_{\text{major}} \approx 4.063\ \text{rad}$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $\theta_{\text{major}} \approx 4.063\text{ rad}$
STEP 2/2 M1 A1

Strategy: the booklet gives one sector-area formula. It works for any sector — minor or major — as long as $\theta$ is in radians and matches the sector you want.

Formula (sector area, in booklet):

$$A = \tfrac{1}{2}\, r^2\, \theta$$

Substitute $r = 4$ cm, $\theta = \theta_{\text{major}} \approx 4.0633$ rad:

$\;\;A = \tfrac{1}{2}(4)^2(4.0633\ldots)$

$\;\;\phantom{A} = \tfrac{1}{2}(16)(4.0633\ldots)$

$\;\;\phantom{A} = 8 \cdot 4.0633\ldots$

$\;\;\phantom{A} = 32.506\ldots\ \text{cm}^2$

Round to 3 s.f.:

$$\boxed{\;A \approx 32.5\ \text{cm}^2\;}$$

Trap: using $\alpha$ (the minor angle) instead of $2\pi - \alpha$ gives $A = \tfrac{1}{2}(16)(2.21989) \approx 17.8\ \text{cm}^2$ — that's the MINOR sector, not what was asked. Sanity check via full disc: full disc area $= \pi r^2 = 16\pi \approx 50.27$ cm². Major + minor sectors should sum to this: $32.5 + 17.8 = 50.3$ ✓.
$A \approx \boxed{32.5\ \text{cm}^2}$ (3 s.f.)
