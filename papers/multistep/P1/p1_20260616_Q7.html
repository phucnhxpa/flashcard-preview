P1 Q7 · Normal to a curve — tangent vs normal gradient

Question

(a) The curve $C$ has equation $y = f(x)$ and passes through $P(x_0, y_0)$. Find the equation of the normal to $C$ at $P$, giving your answer in the form $ax + by + c = 0$, where $a, b, c$ are integers. [3]

(b) (Integration drill) Evaluate $\displaystyle\int\!\left(6x^2 + \frac{5}{x^3}\right)\!dx$, giving each term in its simplest form. [4]

[7 marks]

Normal to a curve: tangent and normal at point P, perpendicular (90°)

Part (a) — Gradient of the normal [3 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3differentiate to find tangent gradient M1

Strategy. The gradient of the tangent at a point $P(x_0, y_0)$ on $y = f(x)$ is $m_t = f'(x_0)$. Differentiate, then substitute $x = x_0$.

Tangent gradient

$$m_t = \left.\frac{dy}{dx}\right|_{x=x_0} = f'(x_0).$$
Phuc's recurring slip. A wrong derivative in the first line (wrong power, wrong sign) propagates through the entire question: the tangent feeds the normal, the normal feeds the intersection, the intersection feeds the area. Fix: verify the first derivative line before proceeding — it is the #1 source of early-line slips on P1.
↳ from STEP 1: $m_t = f'(x_0)$
STEP 2/3negative reciprocal for the normal M1

Strategy. The normal is perpendicular to the tangent. For two perpendicular lines with gradients $m_1$ and $m_2$, $m_1 \cdot m_2 = -1$ — one is the negative reciprocal of the other.

Normal gradient

$$m_n = -\frac{1}{m_t} = -\frac{1}{f'(x_0)}.$$
Phuc's pattern to watch. The negative-reciprocal formula was applied correctly ($m_n = -6/23$ from $m_t = 23/6$), but the upstream $m_t$ was wrong, so the normal was numerically wrong despite the formula being right. The harder failure mode is a wrong tangent gradient feeding a “formula-correct” but numerically-wrong normal. Always check $m_t$ first.
↳ from STEP 2: $m_n = -1/m_t$
STEP 3/3equation of the normal A1

Strategy. Use the point-slope form $y - y_0 = m_n(x - x_0)$ with the point $P(x_0, y_0)$ and the normal gradient $m_n$. If the question asks for $ax + by + c = 0$ with integer coefficients, clear fractions by multiplying through by the LCD.

Point-slope form

$$y - y_0 = m_n(x - x_0).$$

Integer form (if required)

$$\text{Multiply by LCD: } ax + by + c = 0, \quad a, b, c \in \mathbb{Z}.$$
Trap: underline “integers”. If the stem says “in the form $ax + by + c = 0$, where $a, b, c$ are integers,” leaving a fractional form loses the final A mark even if the equation is numerically correct. Clear all fractions by multiplying through by the LCD.

Sense check. Verify $m_t \cdot m_n = -1$ (perpendicularity). If the product is not $-1$, re-check the negative sign on the reciprocal.

Part (b) — Integration power rule (drill) [4 marks] · 4 steps

STEP 1/4integrate a positive-power term M1

Strategy. Integrate $6x^2$: add 1 to the power ($2+1=3$), divide by the new power ($6/3=2$). Result $2x^3$.

Integrate $6x^2$

$$\int 6x^2\,dx = \frac{6x^3}{3} = 2x^3.$$
↳ from STEP 1: $\int 6x^2 = 2x^3$
STEP 2/4integrate a negative-power term M1

Strategy. Rewrite $\dfrac{5}{x^3} = 5x^{-3}$. Add 1 to the power ($-3+1=-2$), divide by the new power ($5/-2$). The sign flips because dividing by a negative.

Integrate $5x^{-3}$

$$\int 5x^{-3}\,dx = 5\cdot\frac{x^{-2}}{-2} = -\frac{5}{2}x^{-2} = -\frac{5}{2x^2}.$$
Sign flip from dividing by a negative power. Dividing $+5$ by $-2$ gives $-5/2$. The sign flip is mechanical — it comes from the division, not from any external rule. Trap: writing $+\tfrac{5}{2x^2}$ (forgetting the sign) loses the A mark.
↳ from STEP 2: $\int 5x^{-3} = -\tfrac{5}{2x^2}$
STEP 3/4add +c and combine A1

Strategy. Combine the integrated terms and add the constant of integration $+c$. The $+c$ is mandatory on every indefinite integral.

Combine

$$\int\!\left(6x^2 + \frac{5}{x^3}\right)\!dx = 2x^3 - \frac{5}{2x^2} + c.$$
Why $+c$? Differentiation destroys constants ($\tfrac{d}{dx}[c]=0$), so $x^2$, $x^2+5$, $x^2-10$ all differentiate to $2x$. Integration cannot recover which constant was lost — we represent the lost family with $+c$. Forgetting $+c$ loses a mark even if the rest is perfect.
↳ from STEP 3: $2x^3 - \tfrac{5}{2x^2} + c$
STEP 4/4verify by differentiation A1

Strategy. Check your integration by differentiating the answer — you should recover the original integrand. This is the “differentiate-your-integration” verification strategy.

Verify

$$\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[2x^3 - \frac{5}{2}x^{-2} + c\right] = 6x^2 + 5x^{-3} = 6x^2 + \frac{5}{x^3} \quad\checkmark.$$

Sense check. The derivative of $2x^3$ is $6x^2$ (matches the first term); the derivative of $-\tfrac{5}{2}x^{-2}$ is $-\tfrac{5}{2}(-2)x^{-3} = 5x^{-3}$ (matches the second term); the derivative of $c$ is $0$. Integration confirmed.
