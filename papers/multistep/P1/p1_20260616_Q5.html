P1 Q5 · $\sin 2x = \sin\alpha$ — period, symmetry, solutions

Question

Given $0 < \alpha < 90^{\circ}$ with $\sin\alpha = p$:

(a) State the period of $\sin 2x$. [2]

(b) State the three key sine symmetry identities. [3]

(c) Solve $\sin 2x = \sin\alpha$ on $0^{\circ} < x < 180^{\circ}$, giving your answers in terms of $\alpha$. [5]

[10 marks]

Annotated diagram

Part (a) — Period of sin 2x [2 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2state the period rule B1

Strategy. The function $\sin(\theta)$ has period $360^{\circ}$. For $\sin(kx)$, the period is $\dfrac{360^{\circ}}{|k|}$ — the coefficient $k$ compresses the graph horizontally by factor $k$.

Period formula

$$\text{Period of }\sin(2x) = \frac{360^{\circ}}{2} = 180^{\circ}.$$
Intuition: input speed. In $\sin(2x)$ the input $x$ is “sped up” by factor 2 — we reach the same output values in half the $x$-distance, so the graph compresses. One full sine wave fits into $180^{\circ}$ of $x$.
↳ from STEP 1: $\text{period} = 180^{\circ}$
STEP 2/2visual meaning B1

Strategy. State what the period means visually: the curve completes one full cycle (rises from 0 to 1, falls to −1, returns to 0) over an $x$-interval of $180^{\circ}$.

Visual summary

On $0^{\circ} < x < 180^{\circ}$, $y = \sin 2x$ rises from $0$ to $1$ at $x=45^{\circ}$, falls through $0$ at $x=90^{\circ}$, reaches $-1$ at $x=135^{\circ}$, and returns to $0$ at $x=180^{\circ}$. A full wave fits in the interval.

Sense check. Contrast $\sin x$ on $(0^{\circ},180^{\circ})$: only half a wave (rises to 1, falls to 0) — one solution. $\sin 2x$ fits a full wave — two solutions.

Part (b) — Sine symmetry identities [3 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3supplementary identity B1

Strategy. On the unit circle, $180^{\circ} - \alpha$ lies in QII and has the same $y$-coordinate (sine) as $\alpha$ in QI. The sine wave is symmetric about $x = 90^{\circ}$ on $(0^{\circ}, 180^{\circ})$.

Identity

$$\sin(180^{\circ} - \alpha) = \sin\alpha.$$

Sense check. $\alpha = 30^{\circ}$: $\sin 150^{\circ} = \sin 30^{\circ} = 0.5$ — same height on the unit circle, reflected across the $y$-axis.

↳ from STEP 1: $\sin(180^{\circ}-\alpha)=\sin\alpha$
STEP 2/3odd-function identity B1

Strategy. Sine is an odd function: the curve has rotational (point) symmetry about the origin. Going left from the origin gives the negative of going right by the same amount.

Identity

$$\sin(-\alpha) = -\sin\alpha.$$

Sense check. $\alpha = 30^{\circ}$: $\sin(-30^{\circ}) = -0.5 = -\sin 30^{\circ}$ — the point rotates to the opposite quadrant.

↳ from STEP 2: $\sin(-\alpha)=-\sin\alpha$
STEP 3/3half-turn identity B1

Strategy. Adding $180^{\circ}$ rotates the unit-circle point through half a turn — both coordinates negate. So $\sin(180^{\circ}+\alpha) = -\sin\alpha$.

Identity

$$\sin(180^{\circ} + \alpha) = -\sin\alpha.$$

Sense check. $\alpha = 30^{\circ}$: $\sin 210^{\circ} = -0.5 = -\sin 30^{\circ}$ — QIII, both sine and cosine negative.

Part (c) — Solve sin 2x = sin α on 0° < x < 180° [5 marks] · 4 steps

STEP 1/4transform the range M1

Strategy. The equation is $\sin(2x) = \sin\alpha$. Let $\theta = 2x$. The range $0^{\circ} < x < 180^{\circ}$ becomes $0^{\circ} < \theta < 360^{\circ}$ — a full cycle of sine.

Range transform

$$0^{\circ} < x < 180^{\circ} \;\Longrightarrow\; 0^{\circ} < 2x < 360^{\circ}.$$
Step 1 of any $\sin(kx)$ solve: transform the $x$-range to match the inner angle $kx$. Forgetting this and solving on $(0^{\circ},180^{\circ})$ for $\theta$ misses solutions in $(180^{\circ},360^{\circ})$.
↳ from STEP 1: $\theta = 2x$, range $0^{\circ}<\theta<360^{\circ}$
STEP 2/4find principal and supplementary θ M1

Strategy. On $0^{\circ} < \theta < 360^{\circ}$, $\sin\theta = \sin\alpha$ (with $0 < \alpha < 90^{\circ}$) has exactly two solutions: the principal $\alpha$ and the supplement $180^{\circ} - \alpha$.

Two values of $\theta$

$$\theta_1 = \alpha, \qquad \theta_2 = 180^{\circ} - \alpha.$$
Why exactly two? Sine is positive in QI and QII only. On $(0^{\circ},360^{\circ})$, a positive sine value $p \in (0,1)$ is hit once in QI ($\alpha$) and once in QII ($180^{\circ}-\alpha$) — no more. The half-turn $180^{\circ}+\alpha$ gives $-\sin\alpha < 0$, outside the positive range.
↳ from STEP 2: $\theta_1=\alpha,\;\theta_2=180^{\circ}-\alpha$
STEP 3/4back-substitute θ = 2x A1

Strategy. Divide each $\theta$ by 2 to recover $x$. Check both solutions lie in $(0^{\circ},180^{\circ})$.

Solve for $x$

$$2x = \alpha \;\Longrightarrow\; x = \frac{\alpha}{2}.$$$$2x = 180^{\circ} - \alpha \;\Longrightarrow\; x = 90^{\circ} - \frac{\alpha}{2}.$$

Sense check. With $0 < \alpha < 90^{\circ}$: $x_1 = \alpha/2 \in (0^{\circ},45^{\circ})$ and $x_2 = 90^{\circ} - \alpha/2 \in (45^{\circ},90^{\circ})$. Both lie in $(0^{\circ},180^{\circ})$ — both valid.

↳ from STEP 3: $x_1 = \alpha/2,\; x_2 = 90^{\circ}-\alpha/2$
STEP 4/4box both solutions A1

Strategy. Write both solutions explicitly. The common student error is to write only $x = \sin^{-1}(p)/2$ and miss the supplementary root.

Final answer

$$\boxed{x = \frac{\alpha}{2} \quad\text{and}\quad x = 90^{\circ} - \frac{\alpha}{2}.}$$
Phuc's mistake. Wrote $x = \sin^{-1}(p)/2$ as the only answer — missed the supplementary root $x = 90^{\circ} - \alpha/2$. The identity $\sin(180^{\circ}-\alpha)=\sin\alpha$ generates the second root; without it, half the marks are lost.

Sense check. Edge case $\alpha = 90^{\circ}$: $\sin 2x = 1$ gives $2x = 90^{\circ}$ only (the supplement $180^{\circ}-90^{\circ}=90^{\circ}$ is the same) — one solution $x = 45^{\circ}$. The two roots merge at the peak.
