Q12 · Motor Efficiency — Current in a Lifting System

Question

An electric motor on a constant $12$ V d.c. supply lifts a mass of $20$ kg with a rope and pulley, raising it vertically through $6.0$ m in a time of $5.0$ s. The complete lifting system (motor, rope, pulley) is $80\%$ efficient. ($g = 10$ N/kg.)

What is the current in the electric motor?

A. 1.6 A   B. 2.0 A   C. 2.5 A   D. 16 A   E. 20 A   F. 25 A

[1 mark]

Solution [1 marks] · 4 steps

STEP 1/4useful energy B1

Strategy. The useful output is the GPE gained by the lifted mass. Compute $mgh$ directly.

Formula (GPE)

$$E_{\text{useful}} = mgh = 20 \times 10 \times 6.0 = 1200 \text{ J}$$
Key idea. The useful work done on the mass is the gain in gravitational potential energy, $mgh$. Because it happens in a time you can connect this to power. Use $g = 10$ N/kg as the paper specifies.
$E_{\text{useful}} = mgh = 1200$ J.
STEP 2/4ideal current M1

Strategy. If the system were $100\%$ efficient, the supply would deliver this same $1200$ J, and the current follows from $E = IVt$ rearranged to $I = E/(Vt)$.

Formula (electrical energy)

$$E = IVt \quad \Longrightarrow \quad I = \frac{E}{Vt}$$

Substitute (100% efficient case)

$$I = \frac{1200}{12 \times 5} = \frac{1200}{60} = 20 \text{ A}$$
This is the "ideal" current. If the motor were perfectly efficient, $20$ A would be the answer. But the system is only $80\%$ efficient, so the actual input must be larger.
If 100% efficient: $I = 20$ A.
STEP 3/4apply efficiency M1

Strategy. The system is only $80\%$ efficient, so the input energy must be larger than the useful output. The current the supply actually draws scales up by $1/\eta$.

Efficiency correction

$$\eta = \frac{E_{\text{useful}}}{E_{\text{input}}} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad E_{\text{input}} = \frac{E_{\text{useful}}}{\eta}$$

Substitute

$$I_{\text{input}} = \frac{20}{0.8} = 25 \text{ A}$$
Phuc's mistake (board). Phuc found $mgh = 1200$ J and $I = 1200/(12 \times 5) = 20$ A — and stopped there, giving E (20 A). He forgot the $80\%$ efficiency factor. Tom's words: "everything you did was right, you just missed the last bit." Because the motor is not perfect, the electrical input power (and hence the current from the $12$ V supply) must exceed the useful value: divide by $0.8$ to get $25$ A.
Trap: efficiency always makes the input BIGGER. When you convert useful output into required input, you divide by the efficiency (input $=$ useful$/\eta$), which increases the number. A quick sanity check: an $80\%$-efficient machine must be fed more energy than it usefully delivers, so the input current $25$ A must be larger than the "ideal" $20$ A — never smaller. If your efficiency step made the number go down, you divided the wrong way.
$I_{\text{input}} = 20 / 0.8 = 25$ A.
STEP 4/4answer A1

Strategy. Confirm the answer and verify it is larger than the ideal current (sanity check on the efficiency direction).

$25$ A $> 20$ A (ideal) — consistent with an $80\%$-efficient system needing more input than useful output.

Sense check. The wasted energy is $300$ J ($20\%$ of $1500$ J input), and useful is $1200$ J, so $\eta = 1200/1500 = 0.8$ — matches the given $80\%$.

$\boxed{I = 25 \text{ A}}$ — Answer F
