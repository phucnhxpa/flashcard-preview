Q4 · Convection Currents — Which Statements Help Explain?

Question

When a saucepan of water is heated from below, convection currents form and transfer heat through the liquid. Three statements about the water as it is heated:

1. The mass of a fixed volume of the water increases.
2. The density of a fixed mass of the water decreases.
3. The volume of a fixed mass of the water increases.

Which of these statements help(s) to explain how convection currents are formed?

A. none   B. 1 only   C. 2 only   D. 3 only   E. 1 and 2   F. 1 and 3   G. 2 and 3   H. 1, 2 and 3

[1 mark]

Solution [1 marks] · 4 steps

STEP 1/4statement 3 B1

Strategy. Apply the physics of thermal expansion: heating a fixed mass of fluid causes its volume to increase.

Thermal expansion

$$\text{heat} \longrightarrow \text{particles spread out} \longrightarrow V \uparrow$$

The mass is unchanged (no particles added or removed), but the volume increases. So statement 3 is correct.

Why? Heating gives particles more kinetic energy; they vibrate/move more and push each other slightly further apart. The same mass now occupies more volume. This is the first link in the causal chain.
Statement 3: ✓ (volume of fixed mass increases on heating).
STEP 2/4statement 2 B1

Strategy. For a fixed mass, density and volume are inversely related. If $V$ goes up and $m$ is unchanged, $\rho$ must go down.

Density formula

$$\rho = \frac{m}{V}$$

For a fixed mass with $V \uparrow$, we get $\rho \downarrow$. So statement 2 is correct.

Causal order matters. It is the volume increasing that causes the density to decrease, not the other way round. Heating $\to$ expansion $\to$ $V \uparrow$ $\to$ $\rho \downarrow$. Get the causal chain in that order and the convection mechanism follows naturally.
Statement 2: ✓ (density of fixed mass decreases on heating).
STEP 3/4statement 1 B1

Strategy. Evaluate: does heating add mass to a fixed volume? No — mass is conserved; heating does not create particles.

Heating does not add mass. In fact, as the fluid expands, some of it leaves any given fixed region, so the mass inside a fixed volume goes down, not up. So statement 1 is wrong.

Trap: "fixed mass" vs "fixed volume". "Density of a fixed mass" and "mass of a fixed volume" sound similar but behave oppositely under heating. Underline the held-fixed quantity before you judge each statement. Mass is conserved — it does not appear from nowhere on heating.
Statement 1: ✗ (mass of fixed volume does not increase on heating).
STEP 4/4combine & answer A1

Strategy. Convection is driven by the density decrease: less-dense hot water rises, cooler denser water sinks to replace it. Statements 2 and 3 together explain the mechanism.

The less-dense hot water rises; cooler, denser water sinks to fill the gap. The sunk water warms, and the loop continues — this circulating loop is the convection current.

Only statements 2 and 3 contribute to the explanation.

Direction of causation. Statement 3 (volume up) causes statement 2 (density down), which in turn causes the buoyancy-driven rising. Statement 1 has no role in the mechanism.
$\boxed{\text{Statements 2 and 3}}$ — Answer G
