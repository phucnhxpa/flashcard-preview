Q2 · Velocity-Time Graph — Distance While Decelerating

Question

A velocity–time graph shows part of a car's journey (velocity in m/s, time in s):

• 0–25 s: accelerates uniformly from 0 to 30 m/s.
• 25–50 s: constant at 30 m/s.
• 50–110 s: decelerates uniformly from 30 to 10 m/s.
• 110–150 s: constant at 10 m/s.

What is the total distance the car travels while it is decelerating?

A. 400 m    B. 500 m    C. 550 m    D. 600 m    E. 700 m    F. 1100 m    G. 1400 m    H. 1800 m

[1 mark]

Solution [1 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3identify section B1

Strategy. Read the question carefully — it asks only for the distance during the deceleration section (50–110 s), not the whole journey.

The question says "while it is decelerating". That means only the section from $t = 50$ s to $t = 110$ s. The other segments (acceleration, constant speed) are distractors.

Common trap. Two traps stacked here: (1) computing the whole journey's distance, (2) treating the decelerating region as a rectangle. Tom: "always identify the section of the graph first, then decide the shape whose area you need."

Sense check. The four answer ranges (400–1800 m) span an order of magnitude — getting the wrong section would land you on a very different option.

Section: $t = 50$ s to $t = 110$ s (deceleration only).
STEP 2/3identify shape M1

Strategy. On a $v$–$t$ graph, distance = area under the curve. The velocity falls from $u = 30$ to $v = 10$ (both non-zero), so the shape is a trapezium, not a rectangle or triangle.

Trapezium area formula

$$S = \frac{u + v}{2} \times t$$
Why a trapezium? A triangle would apply if the velocity reached zero. Since the car decelerates from 30 to 10 (not to 0), the top edge is a sloping line between two non-zero heights — that is a trapezium by definition. Using $v \times t$ with a single velocity is the classic error.
Shape: trapezium with $u = 30$, $v = 10$, $t = 110 - 50 = 60$ s.
STEP 3/3compute area A1

Strategy. Substitute the values and compute. Average velocity times time interval.

Substitute and evaluate

$$S = \frac{30 + 10}{2} \times (110 - 50) = \frac{40}{2} \times 60 = 20 \times 60 = 1200 \text{ m}$$
Phuc's mistake (board). The trapezium was set up correctly as $\frac{30+10}{2} \times 60$ but the arithmetic slipped to "$500$ m". Tom's correction: take the average of the two velocities ($20$) and multiply by the time interval ($60$ s), not by a single velocity.

Sense check. $20 \text{ m/s} \times 60 \text{ s} = 1200 \text{ m}$. Average speed 20 m/s for one minute is 1200 m — consistent.

$\boxed{S = 1200 \text{ m}}$ — Answer F
