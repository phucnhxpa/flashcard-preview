Describe fully the single transformation that maps $y=\sqrt{2x}$ onto $C_1: y=\sqrt{x}$.
A stretch of scale factor $\frac{1}{2}$ parallel to the x-axis. $y=\sqrt{2x} = f(2x)$ where $f(x)=\sqrt{x}$. Replacing $x$ with $2x$ means $a=2$, so SF $= \frac{1}{a} = \frac{1}{2}$. TRAP: Do NOT say SF 2 — the x-values are halved.

Describe fully the single transformation that maps $y=-\sqrt{x}$ onto $C_2: y=12-\sqrt{x}$.
A translation by the vector $\binom{0}{12}$. $-\sqrt{x} + 12 = 12 - \sqrt{x} = C_2$. Adding 12 to $f(x)$ is a vertical translation of $+12$. TRAP: Do NOT write 'shifted up by 12'. Must use 'translation' and state the vector.

Multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate $(\sqrt{3}-1)$: $$\frac{8}{\sqrt{3}+1} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}-1}{\sqrt{3}-1} = \frac{8(\sqrt{3}-1)}{3-1} = \frac{8(\sqrt{3}-1)}{2} = 4(\sqrt{3}-1)$$

What is the conjugate of $(\sqrt{2}+1)$ and what is their product?
Conjugate: $(\sqrt{2}-1)$. Product: $(\sqrt{2}+1)(\sqrt{2}-1) = 2 - 1 = 1$. This identity is used to rationalise denominators of the form $\frac{k}{\sqrt{2}+1}$.
surdsmedium
State the surd product rule and use it to split $\sqrt{2x}$.
Product rule: $\sqrt{ab} = \sqrt{a}\sqrt{b}$. So $\sqrt{2x} = \sqrt{2}\cdot\sqrt{x}$. This split is CRITICAL for the intersection method: it lets you factor out $\sqrt{x}$ and collect terms.
surdsmedium
What does the word 'Exact' mean in an exam question?
No decimals allowed. Leave the answer in surd or fraction form. $12(\sqrt{2}-1)$ is exact. $4.97$ is NOT. Also: no decimal approximations in intermediate steps.
exam-techniqueeasy
What does the word 'Hence' mean in an exam question?
Use the result from the previous part directly. Do NOT re-derive from scratch. In (b)(ii): use $\sqrt{x} = 12(\sqrt{2}-1)$ from (b)(i) — square it for $x$, substitute directly for $y$.
exam-techniqueeasy
When solving $\sqrt{2x} = 12 - \sqrt{x}$, why should you NOT square both sides directly?
Squaring gives $2x = 144 - 24\sqrt{x} + x$, which is a quadratic in $\sqrt{x}$ — more steps, more error-prone, and not the required 'show that' route. Instead: split $\sqrt{2x} = \sqrt{2}\sqrt{x}$, factor out $\sqrt{x}$, then rationalise.
intersectionmedium
Show that the x-coordinate of P satisfies $\sqrt{x} = 12(\sqrt{2}-1)$, given $\sqrt{2x} = 12 - \sqrt{x}$.
Step 1: Split $\sqrt{2x} = \sqrt{2}\sqrt{x}$. Step 2: $\sqrt{2}\sqrt{x} + \sqrt{x} = 12$. Step 3: Factor: $\sqrt{x}(\sqrt{2}+1) = 12$. Step 4: $\sqrt{x} = \frac{12}{\sqrt{2}+1}$. Step 5: Rationalise: multiply by $\frac{\sqrt{2}-1}{\sqrt{2}-1}$: $\sqrt{x} = \frac{12(\sqrt{2}-1)}{2-1} = 12(\sqrt{2}-1)$. ✓

Square both sides: $$x = [12(\sqrt{2}-1)]^2 = 144(\sqrt{2}-1)^2 = 144(3-2\sqrt{2})$$ Using $(\sqrt{2}-1)^2 = 3 - 2\sqrt{2}$.

Substitute $\sqrt{x}$ directly: $$y = 12 - 12(\sqrt{2}-1) = 12 - 12\sqrt{2} + 12 = 24 - 12\sqrt{2}$$ No need to square-root $x$ again — use $\sqrt{x}$ from the previous part.

$$y = kx(x-4)$$ The constant $k$ is found by substituting a third known point (e.g. the turning point).

A quadratic has roots 0 and 4, with minimum at $(2, -4.8)$. Find $f(x)$.
Step 1: $f(x) = kx(x-4)$. Step 2: Sub $(2, -4.8)$: $-4.8 = k \cdot 2 \cdot (-2) = -4k$. Step 3: $k = 1.2$. $$f(x) = 1.2x(x-4)$$

What is the discriminant and what does it tell you?
$\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$ (for $ax^2+bx+c=0$). - $\Delta > 0$: two distinct real roots - $\Delta = 0$: one repeated root (graph touches x-axis) - $\Delta < 0$: no real roots
quadraticseasy
A quadratic has roots $r_1$ and $r_2$. How do you write it in factored form?
$$y = k(x - r_1)(x - r_2)$$ The scale factor $k$ is determined by substituting any known third point on the curve.
quadraticseasy
What does 'meets' or 'touches' the x-axis at $x=a$ mean for a cubic's factor structure?
A repeated root: factor $(x-a)^2$. The graph touches the x-axis at $x=a$ but does NOT cross it. Contrast with 'cuts' = simple root = factor $(x-a)$.
cubicseasy
What does 'cuts' the x-axis at $x=b$ mean for a cubic's factor structure?
A simple root: factor $(x-b)$. The graph crosses the x-axis at $x=b$. Contrast with 'touches'/'meets' = repeated root = $(x-a)^2$.
cubicseasy
A cubic cuts at the origin and touches at $x=4$, passing through $(6, 7.2)$. Find $g(x)$.
Step 1: $g(x) = w \cdot x(x-4)^2$ (cuts at 0 = factor $x$; touches at 4 = factor $(x-4)^2$). Step 2: Sub $(6, 7.2)$: $7.2 = w \cdot 6 \cdot (6-4)^2 = w \cdot 6 \cdot 4 = 24w$. Step 3: $w = \frac{7.2}{24} = \frac{3}{10}$. $$g(x) = \frac{3}{10}x(x-4)^2$$
cubicsmedium
Harry's efficient method for finding $w$ in a cubic: what is it?
Keep the factors separate. Evaluate each bracketed factor at the known x-value numerically, multiply them, then divide the y-value by the product. E.g. for $(6, 7.2)$: $w = \frac{7.2}{6 \cdot 4} = \frac{7.2}{24}$. This avoids expanding $(x-4)^2$ first — fewer calculator keypresses = fewer errors.
cubicsmedium
Find where $f(x)=1.2x(x-4)$ meets $g(x)=0.3x(x-4)^2$ in the first quadrant.
Set equal: $1.2x(x-4) = 0.3x(x-4)^2$. Factor out $x(x-4)$: $x(x-4)[1.2 - 0.3(x-4)] = 0$. Solutions: $x=0$, $x=4$, or $1.2 - 0.3(x-4) = 0 \Rightarrow x = 8$. P is in Q1 and not at origin/x-axis, so $x=8$. $y = 1.2 \cdot 8 \cdot 4 = 38.4$. $$P = (8,\; 38.4)$$

Phuc's mistake: treating $C_1$ as $\sqrt{x}$ instead of $\sqrt{2x}$ for the intersection. What goes wrong?
Setting $\sqrt{x} = 12 - \sqrt{x}$ gives $2\sqrt{x} = 12$, so $\sqrt{x} = 6$, $x = 36$. This is WRONG. The correct equation uses $\sqrt{2x}$: $\sqrt{2x} = 12 - \sqrt{x}$, which gives $\sqrt{x} = 12(\sqrt{2}-1)$ and $x = 144(3-2\sqrt{2}) \approx 24.7$. Why must you say 'translation' and not 'shift' in an Edexcel exam?
Official Edexcel terminology requires the word 'translation' (for movement) and 'stretch' (for scaling). 'Shift', 'slide', 'move', 'squish', 'compress' all LOSE marks. Also: state the vector $\binom{a}{b}$ for translations, and state both SF and direction for stretches.
exam-techniqueeasy
In an exam, the question says 'find the coordinates of P'. What must you remember?
Coordinates = BOTH $x$ AND $y$. Write $P = (x, y)$ at the top of your working as a reminder. Stopping after finding only $x$ loses marks.
exam-techniqueeasy
In a 'show that' question, what must you do?
Show EVERY algebraic step. The answer is already given in the question — you earn marks for the METHOD, not the answer. Do not use a calculator shortcut and write the result. Each algebraic manipulation must be visible.

What does 'describe fully' a transformation require?
Minimum TWO pieces of information: - Type (stretch / translation / reflection) - Parameter (SF + direction for stretch, vector for translation, axis for reflection) E.g. 'Stretch SF 1/2 parallel to x-axis' or 'Translation by vector $\binom{0}{12}$'.
