Q3 · Log-linear graph, \(V=ab^{t}\) and an exponential rate

Total 9 marksQuestion 3. The share price, £\(V\), of a company is being monitored. A graph is drawn of \(\log_{10}V\) against \(t\), where \(t\) is the number of years after monitoring began. The graph is a straight line passing through \((0,2)\) and \((5,2.25)\). (a) Find an equation for the line in the form \(\log_{10}V=mt+c\).  (b) Write the answer to (a) as \(V=ab^{t}\); give the exact value of \(a\) and \(b\) to 3 s.f.  (c) When \(t=T\) the rate of increase in the share price was £50 per year; find \(T\) to the nearest integer (calculator-only solutions are not acceptable).
Source: Pearson Edexcel IAL WMA13/01, 21 October 2025, Question 3 (verbatim).
The linearised graph is a straight line \(\log_{10}V=mt+c\) through \((0,2)\) and \((5,2.25)\); the algebra below is worked directly from those two points — no separate diagram needed.
Part (a) · Equation of the line[2]
Hide working
Strategy
The graph is \(\log_{10}V\) (\(y\)-axis) against \(t\) (\(x\)-axis): a straight line \(y=mt+c\). Find the gradient from the two points and read the intercept.
Formula · gradient
\[ m=\frac{2.25-2}{5-0}=\frac{0.25}{5}=0.05,\qquad c=2\ \text{(intercept at }t=0). \]
Result
\( \log_{10}V=0.05\,t+2 \)
Sense check
At \(t=0\), \(\log_{10}V=2\Rightarrow V=100\); at \(t=5\), \(\log_{10}V=2.25\) — both given points are satisfied.
Phuc's slip (recorded): Phuc tried to write \(V\) (or \(y\)) as the vertical variable. Harry: "It's not \(V\). Be careful… it's better to put in the log straight away" — the \(y\)-axis is \(\log_{10}V\), so write \(\log_{10}V\), never \(V\).
Part (b) · Rewrite as \(V=ab^{t}\)[3]
Carry-inFrom part (a): \(\log_{10}V=0.05t+2\). Raise 10 to each side to remove the log.
Hide working
Strategy
Undo \(\log_{10}\) by raising 10 to each side, then split the index using \(10^{p+q}=10^{p}10^{q}\) and \(10^{kt}=(10^{k})^{t}\).
Working
\[ V=10^{0.05t+2}=10^{2}\cdot 10^{0.05t}=100\bigl(10^{0.05}\bigr)^{t}. \]
Result
\( V=100\bigl(10^{0.05}\bigr)^{t}:\quad a=100\ \text{(exact)},\quad b=10^{0.05}=1.12\ \text{(3 s.f.)} \)
Sense check
\(10^{0.05}=1.12201\ldots\to1.12\) (3 s.f.). Report \(a=100,\ b=1.12\), but keep the exact \(10^{0.05}\) for part (c).
Phuc's slip (recorded): Phuc left \(10^{0.05t}\) un-restructured. Harry: "think of this as \(10^{0.05}\), all to the power of \(t\)" — that is what turns it into the \(b^{t}\) form. Harry also reminded him to state \(a\) and \(b\) explicitly "because it says give them".
Part (c) · "Rate" = differentiate, then solve for \(T\)[4]
Carry-inExact model: \(V=100\,(10^{0.05})^{t}=100\cdot 10^{0.05t}\). "Rate of increase" \(=\dfrac{dV}{dt}\); increase \(\Rightarrow +50\).
Hide working
Strategy
"Rate" means \(\dfrac{dV}{dt}\). Differentiate the exact model with the \(a^{kx}\) rule, set it equal to \(+50\), and solve for \(T\) algebraically.
Formula · differentiating \(a^{kx}\)
\[ \frac{d}{dx}a^{x}=a^{x}\ln a,\qquad \frac{d}{dx}a^{kx}=a^{kx}\,k\ln a\ \ (\text{chain rule brings down }k). \]
Substitute the model \((a=10,\ k=0.05)\)
\[ \frac{dV}{dt}=100\cdot 10^{0.05t}\cdot 0.05\ln 10. \]
Working · set equal to 50 and solve
\[ 100\cdot 10^{0.05T}\cdot 0.05\ln 10=50 \;\Rightarrow\; 10^{0.05T}=\frac{50}{5\ln 10}=\frac{10}{\ln 10}, \]\[ 0.05T=\log_{10}\!\Bigl(\tfrac{10}{\ln 10}\Bigr) \;\Rightarrow\; T=\frac{\log_{10}(10/\ln 10)}{0.05}=12.7557\ldots \]
Result
\( T=13 \) years (to the nearest integer)
Sense check
Showing the algebra (not calculator-only) is required. Differentiating the exact base \(10^{0.05}\) — not the rounded \(1.12\) — keeps the method exact.
Phuc's slip (recorded): Asked what "rate" meant, Phuc suggested an "arithmetic series". Harry: "whenever we see the word rate… you should be thinking about a differential, \(\dfrac{dV}{dt}\)", and it is \(+50\) not \(-50\) "because it increases". In the \(a^{kx}\) drills Phuc wrote \(\ln(6x)\) for \(3^{6x}\); Harry corrected it to \(3^{6x}\cdot 6\ln 3\) — the factor is \(k\ln a\), never \(\ln(kx)\).
Q3(a)–(c) fully worked in the lesson: \(\log_{10}V=0.05t+2\), \(a=100,\ b=10^{0.05}=1.12\), \(T=13\). Differentiation-of-\(a^{kx}\) drills were taught here and set as the next-lesson starter.
