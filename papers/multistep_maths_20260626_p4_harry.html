Q1 — Binomial Expansion, Negative Power · Trap 11 · Q1b range gap · 5 cards
4
Front
[Q1] Which binomial formula do you use when n is negative or fractional — the top one (with C(n,k)) or the bottom one (infinite series)?
click to flip
Back
The BOTTOM one: (1 + x)ⁿ = 1 + nx + n(n−1)/2!·x² + n(n−1)(n−2)/3!·x³ + ... The top formula with binomial coefficients is for POSITIVE INTEGER n only.
5
Front
[Q1] Before applying the binomial series to (a + bx)ⁿ, what mandatory first step must you do?
click to flip
Back
Factor out the leading constant so the bracket starts with 1: (a + bx)ⁿ = aⁿ · (1 + bx/a)ⁿ. Then apply the series with X = bx/a.

[Q1] Expand (2 + 5x)⁻² up to and including the term in x³.

Back
= 2⁻²·(1 + 5x/2)⁻² = (1/4)·[1 − 2(5x/2) + 3(5x/2)² − 4(5x/2)³ + ...] = 1/4 − (5/4)x + (75/16)x² − (125/8)x³ + ...

click to flip
Back

click to flip
Back
quotient + remainder/divisor, e.g. 5 + (−7)/(x+2). You wrote 5(x+2) − 7 — the multiplied form. Only the first lets you read off graph transformations.

click to flip

y < 5 (or g(x) < 5). On x > −2 the term −7/(x+2) is negative, so g(x) < 5. MUST use y or f(x) for a range — writing "x < 5" loses the mark even if the inequality is right.

[Q2] Find g⁻¹(x) for g(x) = (5x + 3)/(x + 2), and state its domain.
click to flip
Back
g⁻¹(x) = (3 − 2x)/(x − 5), domain x < 5. Domain of g⁻¹ = range of g. Don't forget the domain line — it's worth one mark.

Front
[Q2] Why is domain(g⁻¹) = range(g)? Explain in one sentence.
click to flip
Back
g⁻¹ undoes g, so its inputs must be exactly the values g can produce — i.e. the range of g. Nothing else has anything to invert back to.

click to flip
Back
Vertical asymptote x = −2 (shift from x = 0 by −2); horizontal asymptote y = 5 (shift from y = 0 by +5). Stretch by 7, translate left 2, translate up 5.

click to flip
Back
The RIGHTMOST function acts first. fg(x) = f(g(x)): substitute g(x) into f. Swapping gives gf(x) = g(f(x)) — a different function.

Q4 — Implicit Differentiation · Traps 5, 6 · 5 cards
17
Front
[Q4] Write down d/dx(xy) using the product rule. What form must you always use?
click to flip
Back
d/dx(xy) = [x·(dy/dx) + y]. ALWAYS write the square bracket. Dropping it loses the sign on the y-term later.
18
Front
[Q4] Differentiate the term −2xy with respect to x. Why is the bracket critical here?
click to flip
Back
−2·[x(dy/dx) + y] = −2x(dy/dx) − 2y. Skip the bracket and the −2y easily becomes +2y. Write the bracket every time, even on rough work.
19
Front
[Q4a] Curve C: 4x² − 2xy + y² = 24. Find dy/dx in terms of x and y.
click to flip
Back
8x − 2[x(dy/dx) + y] + 2y(dy/dx) = 0 ⇒ (2y − 2x)(dy/dx) = 2y − 8x ⇒ dy/dx = (y − 4x)/(y − x).
20
Front
[Q4b] P(a, b) on C has gradient 2. You got b = −2a but got stuck — what is the missing second equation?
click to flip
Back
P lies on C, so 4a² − 2ab + b² = 24. "Lies on the curve" ⇒ write the curve equation in (a, b) form. That's the second equation.
21
Front
[Q4b] Solve for a and b given b = −2a and 4a² − 2ab + b² = 24.
click to flip
Back
Substitute: 4a² + 4a² + 4a² = 24 ⇒ 12a² = 24 ⇒ a² = 2 ⇒ a = ±√2, b = ∓2√2. Use any sign constraint in the question to reject one.

26
Front
[Q6] When transforming limits in a substitution, what rule must you follow even if the new limits look "backwards"?
click to flip
Back
PRESERVE ORDER: the old lower limit maps to the new lower limit, even if it's numerically larger. Don't flip without a sign change.

Front
[Q6] With u = 3 + cos θ, what are the new u-limits for ∫₀^(π/2) ... dθ? Which is lower?
click to flip
Back
θ = 0 ⇒ u = 4; θ = π/2 ⇒ u = 3. Preserve order: lower limit is u = 4, upper is u = 3 (they look backwards but are correct).

Front
[Q6] Why is it wrong to pull cos θ outside the integral ∫...dθ? What should you do instead?
click to flip
Back
cos θ is a VARIABLE when integrating w.r.t. θ, not a constant. Solve the substitution for cos θ (here cos θ = u − 3) and substitute it back inside the integrand.
29
Front
[Q6] ∫u^(1/2) du — is this a power rule or a logarithm? When does the power rule fail?
click to flip
Back
Power rule: ∫u^k du = u^(k+1)/(k+1) + C. So ∫u^(1/2) du = (2/3)u^(3/2). The ln result ∫u⁻¹ du = ln|u| is ONLY for k = −1, when the power rule would divide by zero.

Front
[Q6] Show that ∫₀^(π/2) sin(2θ)/√(3+cos θ) dθ = A·∫₃⁴ (u−3)/√u du using u = 3 + cos θ. Find A.
click to flip
Back
sin 2θ = 2 sin θ cos θ; du/dθ = −sin θ ⇒ dθ = −du/sin θ; cos θ = u − 3. Integral = −2∫₄³ (u−3)/√u du = 2∫₃⁴ (u−3)/√u du. So A = 2.

Q2 — Volume of Revolution (Self-Assessed Gap) · 3 cards
32
Front
[Q2 gap] Write the formula for the volume of revolution of y = f(x) about the x-axis from x = a to x = b.
click to flip
Back
V = π · ∫_a^b [f(x)]² dx. About the y-axis (with x = g(y)): V = π · ∫_c^d [g(y)]² dy.
33
Front
[Q2 gap] Region bounded by y = √x, x = 1, x = 4, x-axis is rotated 2π rad about x-axis. Find V.
click to flip
Back
V = π·∫₁⁴ (√x)² dx = π·∫₁⁴ x dx = π·[x²/2]₁⁴ = π·(8 − 1/2) = 15π/2.
34
Front
[Q2 gap] For a PARTIAL rotation through angle Θ radians (not 2π), how do you adjust the volume?
click to flip
Back
V_Θ = (Θ/2π) · V_full = (Θ/2) · ∫_a^b [f(x)]² dx. A full revolution is 2π rad; scale the volume by the fraction Θ/2π.
Q8 — Differential Equations (Self-Assessed Gap) · 3 cards
35
Front
[Q8 gap] A separable ODE dy/dx = f(x)g(y) — what is the solution method?
click to flip
Back
Separate: ∫dy/g(y) = ∫f(x) dx + C. Integrate both sides, then apply the initial condition to find C.
36
Front
[Q8 gap] Solve dy/dx = xy given y(0) = 2.
click to flip
Back
∫(1/y) dy = ∫x dx ⇒ ln|y| = x²/2 + C. y(0) = 2 ⇒ C = ln 2. So ln|y| = x²/2 + ln 2 ⇒ y = 2·e^(x²/2).
37
Front
[Q8 gap] When do you apply the initial condition in a separable ODE — before or after integrating?
click to flip
Back
AFTER integrating. Integrate to get the general solution with + C, then substitute the initial (x, y) pair to solve for C. Applying too early loses the constant.
