Var(X) = E(X²) − [E(X)]². There is NO division by n.

Variance · step 2
How do you compute E(X²) for a discrete RV? Is there a 1/n factor?
click to flip
Back
E(X²) = Σ xᵢ² · pᵢ. NO 1/n. The pᵢ already encode the relative weight — dividing by n would double-count.

Variance · step 3
Drill: given E(X) = 1.8 and E(X²) = 4.6, find Var(X).
click to flip
Back
Var(X) = 4.6 − (1.8)² = 4.6 − 3.24 = 1.36.

Variance · step 4
Drill: with Var(X) = 1.36, find Var(5 − 3X).
click to flip
Back
Var(5 − 3X) = (−3)² · Var(X) = 9 · 1.36 = 12.24. The 5 shift drops out; the sign of −3 doesn't matter (squared).

Linear transform · step 1
State the linear-transformation rules for E and Var.
click to flip
Back
E(aX + b) = a·E(X) + b. Var(aX + b) = a²·Var(X). The shift b has no effect; the scale a matters squared.
6
Linear transform · step 2
Why does Var(aX + b) not depend on b?
click to flip
Back
Adding b shifts every value by the same amount, so the spread around the mean is unchanged. Variance measures spread only — the shift is invisible.

Conditional · step 1
Define P(A | B). What's the difference from P(A ∩ B)?
click to flip
Back
P(A | B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B), P(B) ≠ 0. P(A ∩ B) is the joint probability of both happening; P(A | B) is the probability of A GIVEN B has happened. They are NOT the same number.
11
Conditional · step 2
In Q8, why is writing P(car)·P(late) in the numerator of P(car | late) WRONG?
click to flip
Back
Independence is NOT stated. P(car ∩ late) = P(car)·P(late | car), not P(car)·P(late). Only use P(A)·P(B) when independence is given.
12
Conditional · step 3
Drill: P(car)=1/2, P(late|car)=1/5, P(late)=2/15. Find P(car | late).
click to flip
Back
P(car | late) = P(car ∩ late)/P(late) = (1/2 · 1/5)/(2/15) = (1/10)/(2/15) = (1/10)·(15/2) = 3/4.

click to flip
Back
