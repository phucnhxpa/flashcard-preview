P1 Core Skills — Differentiation & Tangents · 5 cards
6
Differentiation · step 1
Differentiate a term like ax^(−1/2). What is the new coefficient and power?
click to flip
Back
a·(−1/2)·x^(−3/2) = −a/2 · x^(−3/2). Bring the power down, multiply, then subtract 1 from the power. This is the #1 source of early-line slips on P1.
7
Differentiation · step 2
Why does a slip in the FIRST line of differentiation cascade through the whole question?
click to flip
Back
The gradient feeds the tangent, the tangent feeds the intersection, the intersection feeds the area. One wrong coefficient in dy/dx propagates to every later part. Always verify the first derivative line before proceeding.
8
Differentiation · step 3
Given dy/dx at a point P, how do you find the equation of the tangent? What about the normal?
click to flip
Back
Tangent: y − y₁ = m(x − x₁), where m = dy/dx at P. Normal: same form but m_normal = −1/m (negative reciprocal). Always substitute the coordinates of P to find the intercept.
9
Differentiation · step 4
When a question says "find the equation of the normal in the form ax + by + c = 0, where a, b, c are integers," what must you do?
click to flip
Back
Clear all fractions by multiplying through by the LCD, then ensure a, b, c are INTEGERS. Underline "integers" — leaving a fractional form loses the final accuracy mark even if the equation is correct.

Differentiation · step 5
Drill: differentiate y = (1/8)x² − 24/√x. What is dy/dx?
click to flip
Back
dy/dx = (1/4)x + 12x^(−3/2). The surd term: 24x^(−1/2) → 24·(−1/2)·x^(−3/2) = −12x^(−3/2), and the minus sign in front gives +12x^(−3/2).
Inequalities, Quadratics & Factorising · 6 cards
11
Inequalities · step 1
When you solve two inequalities and must combine them, what operation gives the final answer?
click to flip
Back
Take the OVERLAP (intersection) of all conditions — only the region satisfying every inequality counts. Sketch each on a number line, then shade the region where ALL are true.
12
Inequalities · step 2
Solve a quadratic inequality like 11x² − 4x − 36 < 0. What is the method, and what is the common trap?
click to flip
Back
Factorise: (x − 2)(11x + 18) < 0 ⇒ roots x = 2, x = −18/11. The parabola opens upward (11 > 0), so the region between the roots is negative: −18/11 < x < 2. Trap: don't just write x < 2 — you need BOTH bounds.
13
Factorising · step 1
When you factor x out of a cubic such as 2x³ + 3x² − 35x = 0, which root must you never drop?
click to flip
Back
x = 0. A product = 0 means EVERY factor gives a solution. The factored-out x = 0 is a root — write it explicitly in your answer list.
14
Factorising · step 2
How do you factorise 2x² + 3x − 35?
click to flip
Back
(2x − 7)(x + 5). Check: 2x² + 10x − 7x − 35 = 2x² + 3x − 35. The split-middle method: find two numbers that multiply to 2×(−35) = −70 and add to +3 → +10 and −7.
15
Hidden quadratic · step 1
For a "hidden quadratic" where you substitute u = (y−5)², what two steps must you not forget after solving for u?
click to flip
Back
(1) Substitute BACK: solve each u = (y−5)² for y. (2) REJECT any u < 0 — a square can't be negative. Every rejected value needs a one-line justification ("u = −5 rejected since (y−5)² ≥ 0").
16
Hidden quadratic · step 2
Drill: 2u³ + 3u² − 35u = 0 gives u = 0, 7/2, −5. With u = (y−5)², find all y values.
click to flip
Back
u = 0 ⇒ y = 5. u = 7/2 ⇒ (y−5)² = 7/2 ⇒ y = 5 ± √(7/2) = 5 ± √14/2. u = −5 ⇒ REJECTED (square ≥ 0). Three solutions: y = 5, 5 + √14/2, 5 − √14/2.
Trig, Coordinates & Exam Technique · 7 cards
17
Trig · step 1
A calculator gives only the ACUTE angle for sin⁻¹. If the required angle is obtuse, what do you do?
click to flip
Back
Take π − (acute value) for radians, or 180° − (acute value) for degrees. Underline "obtuse" in the question so you remember to convert. Using the acute value directly loses the accuracy mark.
18
Trig · step 2
For counting solutions of tan θ = 1 + sin θ, how should you find the number of solutions over a range?
click to flip
Back
Sketch both curves (y = tan θ and y = 1 + sin θ) and COUNT the intersections graphically — one solution per period. Don't try to solve algebraically; the graphical method is the intended approach for "hence" questions.
19
Coordinates · step 1
When a question says "state the COORDINATES" of a point, what must your answer include?
click to flip
Back
BOTH the x and y values, written as (x, y) — not just the y-value. Giving only the value scores 1 of 2 marks; the coordinate pair is essential.
20
Coordinates · step 2
Drill: state the coordinates of the minimum of y = 4 sin θ for 0 ≤ θ ≤ 360°.
click to flip
Back
(270°, −4). The minimum of sin θ is −1 at θ = 270°, so 4 sin θ = −4 there. Write it as the coordinate pair, not just "−4".
21
Exam technique · step 1
What does "hence" mean in a question stem, and why is ignoring it costly?
click to flip
Back
"Hence" = use the previous part's result. It's a signpost, not a suggestion. Starting over from scratch loses method marks even if the answer is right. Always look back at what was just proven.
22
Exam technique · step 2
Even if you're unsure of an answer, why should you always substitute the numbers and finish the calculation?
click to flip
Back
You bank the METHOD marks. A substituted (even wrong) value can earn M marks. Leaving a part blank scores 0. Star the part, substitute what you have, and return if time allows.
23
Exam technique · step 3
If y = f(x + a) passes through the origin O, what condition does that give you?
click to flip
Back
f(a) = 0 ⇒ a must be a ROOT of f. Sub x = 0: y = f(0 + a) = f(a) = 0. So a is any root of the original function f.
