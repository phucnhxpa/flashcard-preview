ESAT Warm-up — Modulus Inequalities & Interval Counting · 5 cards
1
ESAT modulus · step 1
Solve |x − 3| < 5. What is the method, and what is the common trap?
click to flip
Back
|x − 3| < 5 ⇔ −5 < x − 3 < 5 ⇔ −2 < x < 8. Trap: writing x < 8 only (dropping the lower bound). The modulus inequality always gives a DOUBLE inequality — two bounds, not one.
2
ESAT modulus · step 2
Solve |2x + 1| ≥ 7. What changes when the inequality is ≥ (not <)?
click to flip
Back
≥ gives an OR (union), not an AND: 2x + 1 ≥ 7 OR 2x + 1 ≤ −7 ⇒ x ≥ 3 OR x ≤ −4. For < you get a between region; for ≥ you get two outside regions. Know which one applies.
3
ESAT intervals · step 1
How many integers satisfy −3 < x ≤ 7 AND x is even?
click to flip
Back
Integers in (−3, 7]: −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7. Even ones: −2, 0, 2, 4, 6 = 5 values. Method: list the range first, THEN filter. Don't try to count in your head.
4
ESAT intervals · step 2
Count the number of integers x where |x − 2| < 5.5. Why must you be careful about the endpoints?
click to flip
Back
−5.5 < x − 2 < 5.5 ⇒ −3.5 < x < 7.5. Integers: −3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7 = 11 values. The endpoints −3.5 and 7.5 are NOT integers, so no boundary issue. Always check if endpoints are included.

Q2 — Momentum & Impulse: Symmetric Collision · 7 cards
6
Momentum · step 1
Q2: Particles A (mass 3m) and B (mass m) move in opposite directions, each at 1.5 m/s. They collide. After, A's direction is UNCHANGED. Why must you set up a sign convention first?
click to flip
Back
Momentum is a VECTOR — conservation requires signs. Pick A's initial direction as +. Then u_A = +1.5, u_B = −1.5 (opposite). Without signs, conservation gives nonsense. Always assign signs before any calculation.
7
Momentum · step 2
Q2: If A's direction is unchanged after the collision, what can you infer about B's velocity?
click to flip
Back
A pushed B forward, so B now moves in A's direction (+ sign). Also B must be FASTER than A — otherwise A would overtake B and they'd collide again (unphysical). So v_B > v_A, both in + direction.
8
Momentum · step 3
Q2: The speed difference after collision is 1 m/s. Write v_B in terms of v_A = V.
click to flip
Back
v_B − v_A = 1, and v_B > v_A (B faster), so v_B = V + 1. Choosing V − 1 would make A faster than B (they'd collide again — unphysical). Phuc's reasoning was correct here: "A has to push B, so B has to accelerate a little bit."
9
Momentum · step 4
Q2(a): Apply conservation of momentum: 3m(1.5) + m(−1.5) = 3m·V + m(V+1). Solve for V.
click to flip
Back
LHS: 4.5m − 1.5m = 3m. RHS: 3mV + mV + m = 4mV + m. So 3m = 4mV + m ⇒ 2 = 4V ⇒ V = 0.5. So v_A = 0.5 m/s, v_B = 1.5 m/s. Check: m cancels from every term ✓; v_B > v_A ✓.
10
Impulse · step 1
Q2(b): Find the magnitude of the impulse exerted on B by A. What formula do you use?
click to flip
Back
I = m(v − u), impulse = change in momentum. For B: u_B = −1.5, v_B = +1.5, mass = m. I_B = m(1.5 − (−1.5)) = m(3.0) = 3m Ns. The impulse is positive — in A's direction.
11
Impulse · step 2
Why is dropping the sign on u_B = −1.5 a critical error in the impulse calculation?
click to flip
Back
If you use u_B = +1.5 instead of −1.5: I_B = m(1.5 − 1.5) = 0. That says no impulse — obviously wrong for a collision! The sign on the initial velocity determines the change. Always preserve your sign convention throughout.
12
Impulse · step 3
By Newton's 3rd Law, what is the relationship between the impulse on B by A and the impulse on A by B?
click to flip
Back
They are EQUAL in magnitude and OPPOSITE in direction: I_A = −I_B. So |I_A| = |I_B| = 3m Ns. You can verify: I_A = 3m(v_A − u_A) = 3m(0.5 − 1.5) = 3m(−1) = −3m. Same magnitude, opposite sign ✓.
Speed-Time Graphs & SUVAT · 6 cards
13
SUVAT · step 1
List the five SUVAT equations. Which variable links them all?
click to flip
Back
v = u + at; s = ut + ½at²; v² = u² + 2as; s = ½(u+v)t; s = vt − ½at². The linking variable is TIME (t) — it appears in all five. Identify which 3 of {s, u, v, a, t} you know, pick the equation with the other two.
14
Speed-time · step 1
On a speed-time graph, what does the GRADIENT represent? What does the AREA under the graph represent?
click to flip
Back
Gradient = acceleration (a = Δv/Δt). Area under the graph = distance travelled (∫v dt). A horizontal line = constant speed (a = 0). A straight sloped line = constant acceleration.
15
Speed-time · step 2
A car accelerates from rest at 2 m/s² for 5 s, then decelerates at 4 m/s² to rest. Sketch the speed-time graph and find total distance.
click to flip
Back
Phase 1: v = 0 + 2(5) = 10 m/s, s₁ = ½(0+10)(5) = 25 m. Phase 2: 0 = 10 − 4t ⇒ t = 2.5 s, s₂ = ½(10+0)(2.5) = 12.5 m. Total = 37.5 m. Two triangles on the graph.
16
Speed-time · step 3
Harry emphasised LABELING forces and diagrams. On a speed-time graph, what must you label to avoid confusion?
click to flip
Back
Label: (1) the axes (speed vs time, not velocity vs time unless direction matters); (2) key values on each segment (acceleration value, time duration, peak speed); (3) the shape of each segment (constant a = straight line).
17
SUVAT · step 2
A particle is projected upward at 15 m/s. How long until it returns to the starting point? (g = 9.8 m/s²)
click to flip
Back
At the top: v = 0, u = 15, a = −9.8. t_up = (v−u)/a = (0−15)/(−9.8) = 1.53 s. By symmetry, t_down = t_up. Total = 2 × 1.53 = 3.06 s. Alternatively: s = 0 = 15t − 4.9t² ⇒ t(15 − 4.9t) = 0 ⇒ t = 15/4.9 = 3.06 s.
18
SUVAT · step 3
When is SUVAT valid, and when is it NOT valid? What assumption must hold?
click to flip
Back
SUVAT requires CONSTANT acceleration. If acceleration varies with time (e.g., air resistance proportional to v), you must use calculus: a = dv/dt, v = ds/dt, and integrate. Using SUVAT with variable a gives wrong answers.
Q4 — Moments: Two Tilting Scenarios · 8 cards
19
Moments · step 1
Q4: A non-uniform branch AB (length 1.5 m, weight W) rests on supports at C and D. AC = 0.24 m, DB = 0.36 m. Centre of mass is x m from C. What is the key rule when a body is "on the point of tilting" about a pivot?
click to flip
Back
At the point of tilting about a pivot, the reaction at the OTHER support becomes ZERO. Only the pivot's reaction, the weight, and any applied force contribute to the moment equation. The support that is about to lose contact has R = 0.
20
Moments · step 2
Harry's key advice for this question: "separate diagrams per case." Why is this essential, and what happens if you use one diagram?
click to flip
Back
Each tilting case has different forces acting (R_C = 0 in one, R_D = 0 in the other) and different pivot points. If you draw one diagram, you'll mix up which reaction is zero and take moments about the wrong point. Draw a FRESH diagram for each case, labeled with which R = 0.
21
Moments · step 3
Q4(i): A 150 N UPWARD force at B, tilting about C. Take moments about C. What forces contribute?
click to flip
Back
R_D = 0 (tilting about C). Forces about C: (1) Weight W downward at x m right of C → CW moment Wx. (2) Applied 150 N upward at B, 1.26 m right of C → ACW moment 150 × 1.26 = 189. Balance: Wx = 189. (R_C has zero arm about C.)
22
Moments · step 4
Q4(ii): A 225 N DOWNWARD force at B, tilting about D. Take moments about D. What is the sign handling error to avoid?
click to flip
Back
R_C = 0 (tilting about D). Weight W down at (0.9 − x) m LEFT of D → ACW moment W(0.9 − x). Applied 225 N down at B, 0.36 m RIGHT of D → CW moment 225 × 0.36 = 81. Balance: W(0.9 − x) = 81. Error: confusing which force is CW vs ACW — always label direction with the moment.
23
Moments · step 5
Q4: You have two equations: Wx = 189 and W(0.9 − x) = 81. Why do you need TWO equations, and how do you solve?
click to flip
Back
Two unknowns (W, x) → two equations. From eq 1: W = 189/x. Substitute into eq 2: (189/x)(0.9 − x) = 81 → 189(0.9 − x) = 81x → 170.1 − 189x = 81x → 170.1 = 270x → x = 0.63 m. Then W = 189/0.63 = 300 N.
24
Moments · step 6
What sense check should you run after finding W = 300 N and x = 0.63 m?
click to flip
Back
Check x = 0.63 m places the CoM between the supports (C at 0.24 m from A, D at 1.14 m from A, so CoM at 0.24 + 0.63 = 0.87 m from A — between C and D ✓). Also W = 300 N is positive ✓. If x were outside [0, 1.5], something is wrong.
25
Moments · step 7
Harry said: "correct sign handling in moment equations." What is the systematic method to avoid sign errors?
click to flip
Back
Choose a rotation direction (e.g., clockwise = positive). For each force: write the moment = force × perpendicular distance from pivot, with sign based on whether it causes CW (+) or ACW (−) rotation. Sum = 0 for equilibrium. Never mix CW and ACW signs.
26
Moments · step 8
Drill: A uniform rod AB (length 2 m, weight 50 N) rests on supports at C (AC = 0.4 m) and D (DB = 0.5 m). A 30 N weight is placed at A. Find R_C and R_D.
click to flip
Back
Take moments about C: R_D × 1.1 = 50 × 0.6 + 30 × 0.4 = 30 + 12 = 42 ⇒ R_D = 38.2 N. Vertical equilibrium: R_C + R_D = 50 + 30 = 80 ⇒ R_C = 80 − 38.2 = 41.8 N. Always verify: R_C > 0 and R_D > 0 (no tilting).
Force Diagrams, Labeling & M1 Exam Technique · 4 cards
27
Force diagrams · step 1
Harry emphasised LABELING forces. On a force diagram for a particle on an inclined plane, what forces must you draw and label?
click to flip
Back
Weight W = mg (vertically down, from the centre of mass); Normal reaction R (perpendicular to the plane, from the surface); Friction F (along the plane, opposing motion); any applied force (with its angle stated). Label each with its variable name AND direction.
28
Force diagrams · step 2
When resolving forces on an inclined plane, which directions should you choose for your axes, and why?
click to flip
Back
Choose axes ALONG and PERPENDICULAR to the plane — not horizontal/vertical. This way, the normal reaction and friction align with your axes, and only the weight needs splitting into components (W sin θ along the plane, W cos θ perpendicular). Fewer component splits = fewer sign errors.
