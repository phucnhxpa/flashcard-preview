ECAA specimen Q15 — rigid transformations

Question

The square PQRS is positioned so that its vertices are at the points with coordinates: $(1,1)$, $(-1,1)$, $(-1,-1)$ and $(1,-1)$.

The square is rotated clockwise through $90^\circ$ about the origin and then reflected in the line $y=x$.

Which transformation will return the square to its original orientation?

A  A reflection in the $x$-axis.
B  A reflection in the $y$-axis.
C  A reflection in the line $y=-x$.
D  A rotation of $90^\circ$ clockwise about the origin.
E  A rotation of $90^\circ$ anticlockwise about the origin.

[1 mark]

Method[1 mark] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2

Strategy and why it applies. The appearance of an unlabelled square is invariant under a quarter-turn, so the method must track labels rather than trust the outline. Fix the source-order labels $P(1,1)$, $Q(-1,1)$, $R(-1,-1)$ and $S(1,-1)$, then apply the stated operations from left to right. This is exactly the diagram-first repair used in the lesson.

Formula / definition first. The coordinate rules are $R_{cw}(x,y)=(y,-x)$ for a clockwise quarter-turn and $F_{y=x}(x,y)=(y,x)$ for reflection in $y=x$. Composition means the rotation acts first: $F_{y=x}(R_{cw}(x,y))$. These rules are definitions of the two rigid transformations, not guessed visual effects.

Complete working. Apply the first rule to every source vertex: $P\to(1,-1)$, $Q\to(1,1)$, $R\to(-1,1)$ and $S\to(-1,-1)$. Apply the reflection next: $P\to(-1,1)$, $Q\to(1,1)$, $R\to(1,-1)$ and $S\to(-1,-1)$. Keeping each label attached to its image prevents the symmetry of the square from erasing the information the question tests.

Difficult transition. Comparing the final and original coordinates is the key transition. Each final point has coordinate $(-x,y)$ relative to its own original point. That is a reflection in the $y$-axis. The question asks for a transformation that returns the final labelled square to the original orientation, so take the inverse; a reflection is self-inverse, hence the same $y$-axis reflection undoes it.

What went wrong / common trap. The recorded difficulty was trying to undo the two moves mentally while looking only at an unchanged square outline. That route fails because it loses the vertex permutation and can tempt a two-operation answer when one transformation is requested. The neutral common trap is also reversing the composition order: the wording fixes rotation first, reflection second.

Sense check and verification. Check one non-axis vertex: original $P(1,1)$ finishes at $(-1,1)$, exactly the $y$-axis image. Check $S(1,-1)$ finishes at $(-1,-1)$. Two non-collinear checks agree, and the map preserves distance and orientation reversal as a reflection should. Therefore option B is consistent with the whole labelled figure, not merely one corner.

Exam trigger and transfer. When you see a symmetric shape followed by transformations, label two or more vertices immediately, write the coordinate rule beside each arrow, and compare the final coordinates with the originals. On another paper, the same routine handles triangles, polygons and invariant points. State the single net map and then consider whether the question asks for the map or its inverse.

STEP 2/2

Strategy and why it applies. Use a compact algebraic verification only after the coordinate table has established the lesson method. Writing each map as a matrix makes the order unambiguous and checks that the result applies to every point, not only the four vertices. This is useful for revision but must remain labelled as a derived check rather than something claimed to have been taught live.

Formula / definition first. For column vectors, $R_{cw}=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\-1&0\end{pmatrix}$ and $F_{y=x}=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}$. The combined map is $F_{y=x}R_{cw}$ because the rightmost matrix acts first. Matrix multiplication is the general rule for composing linear transformations about the origin.

Complete working. Multiply: $\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\-1&0\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}-1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix}$. Acting on $\binom{x}{y}$ gives $\binom{-x}{y}$, the standard $y$-axis reflection. Squaring this diagonal matrix gives the identity, so its inverse is itself and it returns the final labelled square to the source orientation.

Difficult transition. The difficult point is distinguishing a verification from an alternative story about what happened in the lesson. The evidence records labelled-coordinate tracking. The matrix proof is mathematically valid expansion, but it is not evidence that matrices were the tutor's live method. Keeping that attribution local preserves both mathematical depth and the chronology boundary.

What went wrong / common trap. A common trap is multiplying $R_{cw}F_{y=x}$, which reverses the source order and gives a different reflection. Another is saying the square 'did not move' because its occupied region is unchanged. The question is about orientation of the labelled square, so the permutation of $P,Q,R,S$ is substantive information even when the boundary coincides.

Sense check and verification. The matrix determinant is $-1$, confirming an orientation-reversing transformation; its square is the identity, confirming self-inverse behavior. These checks match the vertex table and option B. No current ESAT paper identity or mark allocation is inferred: the item remains the historical ECAA specimen Q15 used as predecessor practice.

Exam trigger and transfer. Recognition trigger: whenever two origin-centred linear transformations are composed, write the maps symbolically, preserve right-to-left action, and check determinant and one test vector. If the exam level does not assume matrices, present the coordinate table as the answer and keep the matrix product as a private check rather than replacing an accessible method.
