P2 Q9 — logarithms and restrictions

Question

Not covered in the lesson — official-reference reconstruction below.

Instruction: In this question you must show detailed reasoning. Solutions relying on calculator technology are not acceptable.

(i) Solve $$2\log_3(4x+5)-\log_3(x+3)=2.\quad(5)$$

(ii) Given that $a>0$, $b>0$ and $$\log_{10}a+\log_{10}b=\log_{10}(a+b),$$ (a) prove that $$a=\frac b{b-1}.\quad(3)$$ (b) Hence write down the full restriction on the value of $b$, giving a reason for your answer. (2)

[10 marks · official source]

Part (i)[5 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1

Strategy and why it applies. State the log domain first, combine the two logarithms into one, convert the logarithmic equation to an algebraic equation, solve, and then filter roots against the original arguments. This sequence is mandatory because algebraic manipulation can introduce a candidate that is not in the domain of the source logarithms.

Formula / definition first. The log laws are $m\log_a u=\log_a(u^m)$ and $\log_a u-\log_a v=\log_a(u/v)$ for positive $u,v$. The source requires $4x+5>0$ and $x+3>0$, so $x>-5/4$ is the combined domain.

Complete working. Combine: $$\log_3\left(\frac{(4x+5)^2}{x+3}\right)=2.$$ Therefore $(4x+5)^2=3^2(x+3)=9(x+3)$. Expanding gives $16x^2+40x+25=9x+27$, so $16x^2+31x-2=0=(16x-1)(x+2)$. The candidates are $x=1/16$ and $x=-2$.

Difficult transition. The difficult transition is returning from the quadratic to the original log domain. $x=-2$ makes $4x+5=-3$, so the first logarithm is undefined over the reals; it also lies below $-5/4$. The other candidate $1/16$ makes both arguments positive and is retained.

What went wrong / common trap. This part was not discussed in the recording, so no learner route or error is claimed. A neutral common trap is to accept both quadratic roots, or to square the log expression incorrectly by treating $2\log$ as $(\log)^2$. The coefficient 2 becomes an exponent on the positive argument.

Sense check and verification. Verify the accepted root: at $x=1/16$, $4x+5=21/4$ and $x+3=49/16$, so $(4x+5)^2/(x+3)=(441/16)/(49/16)=9$, whose base-3 log is 2. The rejected root fails before any logarithm can be evaluated. Thus $\boxed{x=1/16\text{ only}}$.

Exam trigger and transfer. When solving log equations, write argument inequalities before using log laws, solve the resulting algebra, and test every candidate in the original domain. On another paper, 'detailed reasoning' requires the rejection sentence; a factorised quadratic and two roots are not a complete logarithmic solution.

Part (ii)(a)[3 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in: Printed givens: $a>0$, $b>0$ and $\log_{10}a+\log_{10}b=\log_{10}(a+b)$.
STEP 1/1

Strategy and why it applies. Use the product log law to combine the left side, then exploit one-to-one behavior of base-10 logarithms on positive inputs. Rearrange the resulting algebra to make $a$ the subject. The source already states $a>0$ and $b>0$, so the log laws apply within that positive domain.

Formula / definition first. For positive $a,b$, $\log_{10}a+\log_{10}b=\log_{10}(ab)$. If $\log_{10}u=\log_{10}v$ with $u,v>0$, then $u=v$ because the logarithm is one-to-one. Therefore the printed equality implies $ab=a+b$.

Complete working. Rearrange without dividing too early: $ab-a=b$, so $a(b-1)=b$. Since the original relation cannot hold with $b=1$—it would give $a=a+1$—division by $b-1$ is valid for any solution. Hence $$\boxed{a=\frac b{b-1}},$$ exactly as required.

Difficult transition. The difficult transition is preserving the product structure. The sum of logarithms is the logarithm of a product, not of a sum; the right side already contains $a+b$. After equating arguments, factor $a$ from $ab-a$ before dividing, because dividing termwise invites sign or denominator errors.

What went wrong / common trap. This part was not discussed, so no personal attribution is supported. A neutral common trap is to write $\log a+\log b=\log(a+b)$ as an identity rather than as the special equation being solved. The general law gives $\log(ab)$, and the given equation then forces $ab=a+b$.

Sense check and verification. Check the derived relation algebraically: multiplying $a=b/(b-1)$ by $b-1$ returns $ab-a=b$, hence $ab=a+b$. Substitution into the log equation is valid whenever the later positivity restrictions hold. The excluded $b=1$ is already visible from the zero denominator and the impossible pre-division equation.

Exam trigger and transfer. When a proof begins with an equality of logarithms, combine each side using valid log laws, equate positive arguments, and rearrange in small reversible steps. On another paper, mention any value excluded by a division and retain it for the domain analysis rather than silently cancelling a possibly zero factor.

Part (ii)(b)[2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in: From Part (ii)(a): $a=b/(b-1)$, with the printed source conditions $a>0$ and $b>0$.
STEP 1/1

Strategy and why it applies. Start from the proved relation and combine it with every positivity condition from the original logarithms. The command asks for the full restriction and a reason, so '$b\ne1$' is insufficient. Because $b$ is already positive, the sign of the denominator controls whether $a=b/(b-1)$ is positive.

Formula / definition first. The source gives $b>0$ and requires $a>0$. With $a=b/(b-1)$, a positive numerator $b$ yields a positive quotient only when the denominator is positive: $b-1>0$. The denominator must also be non-zero, but the stronger inequality already excludes zero.

Complete working. Solve $b-1>0$ to obtain $\boxed{b>1}$. If $0<b<1$, then $b-1<0$ and $a<0$, so $\log_{10}a$ would be undefined. If $b=1$, the formula is undefined and the earlier equation $a(b-1)=b$ becomes $0=1$. Therefore no other positive $b$ is allowed.

Difficult transition. The difficult transition is distinguishing a denominator restriction from the full log-domain restriction. $b\ne1$ merely prevents division by zero and still allows $0<b<1$, which makes $a$ negative. The second mark requires the positivity reason that strengthens the answer to $b>1$.

What went wrong / common trap. The lesson only reviewed this tiny part at high level; the tutor called the attempt very close, pointed to the mark scheme and deferred detailed explanation. It would be fabrication to claim the full sign argument was taught or completed live. A neutral common trap is giving only $b\ne1$ without using $a>0$.

Sense check and verification. Check with examples. If $b=2$, then $a=2>0$ and $\log2+\log2=\log4=\log(2+2)$, so an allowed value works. If $b=1/2$, then $a=-1$, invalid for real logarithms. The examples confirm the sign analysis and the strict boundary at 1.

Exam trigger and transfer. When a 'hence restriction' follows an algebraic formula inside a log problem, inspect denominator, numerator and every original log argument. State the strongest interval, then explain why excluded intervals violate positivity. Preserve the stopping point here: the detailed route remains official-reference-only, not a lesson completion.
