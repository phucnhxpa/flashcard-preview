P2 Q8 — geometric series and barrel model

Question

Not covered in the lesson — official-reference reconstruction below.

(i) A geometric series has first term $a$ and common ratio $r$. Prove that the sum of the first $n$ terms of this series $S_n$ is given by $$S_n=\frac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r}.\quad(3)$$

(ii) A liquid is to be stored in a barrel. Due to evaporation, the volume of the liquid in the barrel at the end of each year is 8% less than the volume of the liquid in the barrel at the start of the year. At the start of the first year, the barrel is filled with 150 litres of the liquid.

(a) Show that the amount of the liquid in the barrel at the end of 6 years is approximately 91 litres. (2)

At the start of each year a new barrel is filled with 150 litres of the liquid so that, at the end of 40 years, there are 40 barrels containing the liquid.

(b) Calculate the total amount of the liquid, to the nearest litre, in the 40 barrels at the end of 40 years. (3)

[8 marks · official source]

Part (i)[3 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1

Strategy and why it applies. Because the lesson deferred the proof, explain the official algebra without implying it was completed live. Start from the finite series definition, multiply every term by the common ratio, and subtract the aligned rows so the interior terms cancel. This is the standard derivation the source assesses even though the final formula itself is printed in the formula booklet.

Formula / definition first. For first term $a$, ratio $r$ and $n$ terms, $S_n=a+ar+ar^2+\cdots+ar^{n-1}$. Multiplying by $r$ gives $rS_n=ar+ar^2+\cdots+ar^{n-1}+ar^n$. The displayed formula requires $r\ne1$ because division by $1-r$ occurs.

Complete working. Subtract the second line from the first: $S_n-rS_n=a-ar^n$, since every interior term $ar$ through $ar^{n-1}$ cancels. Factor both sides: $(1-r)S_n=a(1-r^n)$. Divide by $1-r$ to obtain $$\boxed{S_n=\frac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r}}.$$

Difficult transition. The difficult transition is alignment and the last exponent. An $n$-term series beginning at exponent zero ends at $r^{n-1}$; after multiplication it ends at $r^n$. If the first row incorrectly ends at $r^n$, the cancellation leaves the wrong power and no longer proves the printed expression.

What went wrong / common trap. No learner completion is evidenced. A neutral common trap is memorising the formula while being unable to reconstruct the two aligned lines, or subtracting in the opposite order and changing only one side's signs. Either subtraction order works if it is applied consistently; the displayed denominator must match it.

Sense check and verification. Verify with $n=1$: the formula gives $a(1-r)/(1-r)=a$, exactly the one-term sum. With $n=2$, it gives $a(1-r^2)/(1-r)=a(1+r)=a+ar$. These small cases confirm the exponent and cancellation pattern without claiming lesson evidence.

Exam trigger and transfer. When asked to prove a finite geometric sum, write $S_n$, write $rS_n$ directly beneath it, subtract, factor and state $r\ne1$. The stopping point for lesson attribution remains deferred: this official-reference proof is provided for study, and no further live derivation is claimed.

Part (ii)(a)[2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in: Printed source: 150 litres at the start of year 1; each completed year retains $0.92$ of the preceding amount.
STEP 1/1

Strategy and why it applies. Translate an 8% yearly loss into a retention multiplier of $1-0.08=0.92$. Index from the start amount: after one year multiply once, and after six years multiply six times. This indexing point is exactly why the tutor advised writing early terms, but the numerical completion remains official-reference-only.

Formula / definition first. A repeated percentage decrease follows $U_n=U_0r^n$, where $U_0$ is the starting volume and $r$ is the retained proportion. Here $U_0=150$ litres and $r=0.92$, so $U_n=150(0.92)^n$.

Complete working. Write $U_1=150(0.92)$ and $U_2=150(0.92)^2$ to establish the pattern. Then $$U_6=150(0.92)^6=90.9532502\ldots\text{ litres}.$$ Therefore the amount is approximately $\boxed{91\text{ litres}}$, as the source asks to show.

Difficult transition. The difficult transition is distinguishing the start of year 1 from the end of year 1. The starting fill is $U_0=150$, so end of year 6 is exponent 6, not 5. Also, $90.953\ldots\approx91$ is an approximation; writing $150(0.92)^6=91$ as exact equality is false.

What went wrong / common trap. No original attempt or result is evidenced in the lesson transcript. A neutral common trap is using $0.08$ as the multiplier, which models retaining only 8%, or using exponent 5 by applying the formula $ar^{n-1}$ without first deciding what counts as term 1 in this context.

Sense check and verification. Check monotonicity and scale. Since $0<0.92<1$, volume must decrease but remain positive. Six years of 8% loss retaining about 61% gives roughly 91.5 litres, so $90.95$ is plausible. The rounded result matches the official target without inventing a live calculation.

Exam trigger and transfer. When a percentage-decay problem names a start and an end-of-period value, write the first two terms to fix the exponent, then use retained proportion rather than loss proportion. Preserve the calculator value until the requested approximation and mark every rounded equality with $\approx$.

Part (ii)(b)[3 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in: Official model from (ii)(a): a barrel filled at a year start retains factor $0.92$ per completed year; at the end of year 40 the powers run from $1$ to $40$.
STEP 1/1

Strategy and why it applies. List the volumes present at the end of year 40 by barrel age. The barrel filled at the start of year 40 has undergone one year's loss and contains $150(0.92)=138$ litres; the oldest has undergone forty losses. These 40 volumes form a finite geometric series, not forty copies of the same six-year amount.

Formula / definition first. The finite sum formula is $S_n=a(1-r^n)/(1-r)$. For the barrel inventory, the first term is $a=138$, common ratio $r=0.92$, and number of terms $n=40$. The order may be newest to oldest or oldest to newest; the same set of powers must be included once.

Complete working. The total is $$S_{40}=\frac{138(1-0.92^{40})}{1-0.92}=1663.581\ldots\text{ litres}.$$ Rounding to the nearest litre gives $\boxed{1664\text{ litres}}$. Using $138$ rather than 150 is essential because every barrel has experienced at least one completed year by the observation time.

Difficult transition. The difficult transition is identifying the first term from the timeline. There is no fresh, un-aged 150-litre barrel at the end of year 40 under the source wording; each new barrel is filled at the start of a year. Thus the powers are $0.92^1$ through $0.92^{40}$, equivalent to first term 138 and 40 terms.

What went wrong / common trap. This calculation was not worked live. A neutral common trap is using $150(1-0.92^{40})/(1-0.92)$, which includes an exponent-zero term and shifts every barrel age. Another is using the single-barrel $U_6$ value forty times, ignoring the staggered fill years.

Sense check and verification. Check bounds: no barrel contains more than 138 litres at the end of its first year, so the total must be below $40\times138=5520$; it must exceed the newest barrel's 138 litres. The geometric sum $1663.6$ lies comfortably between. Recomputing as $150\sum_{j=1}^{40}0.92^j$ gives the same result.

Exam trigger and transfer. When objects enter a decay process at successive times, draw a timeline, list the youngest and oldest powers, count terms, and only then choose $a,r,n$.
