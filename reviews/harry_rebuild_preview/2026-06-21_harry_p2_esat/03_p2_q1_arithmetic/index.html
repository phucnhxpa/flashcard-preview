P2 Q1 — arithmetic series

Question

Source/status: Pearson WMA12/01, 15 October 2025, Q1. Original correct; the tutor confirmed the submitted work and made no correction.

An arithmetic series starts

$$78+75+72+\ldots$$

Find

(a) the 18th term in the series, (2)

(b) the sum of the first 100 terms of the series. (2)

[4 marks]

Part (a)[2 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2

Strategy and why it applies. Recognise an arithmetic sequence because consecutive terms change by a constant amount. Subtract adjacent terms rather than judging the signs by eye: $75-78=-3$ and $72-75=-3$. Record the first term and common difference once, because both printed parts use the same parameters and an incorrect sign would contaminate both answers.

Formula / definition first. For an arithmetic sequence, $u_n=a+(n-1)d$. For its first $n$ terms, $S_n=\dfrac n2[2a+(n-1)d]$, equivalently $\dfrac n2(a+l)$. Both formulas are supplied in the P2 formula booklet, but the meanings of $a$, $d$, $n$ and $l$ still have to be identified correctly.

Complete working. Here $a=78$ and $d=-3$. The negative sign is part of $d$, not an operation to add later. Writing $d=-3$ before either substitution makes the decreasing behavior explicit. The source uses the word 'series' while part (a) asks for a term; use the term formula for part (a) and the sum formula for part (b).

Difficult transition. The key transition is from the displayed first term to the index offset. The 18th term is reached after 17 differences, not 18. Likewise, the 100th term contains 99 differences. This is why every standard formula uses $n-1$ and why writing the parameters first is safer than continuing the list manually.

What went wrong / common trap. The lesson record describes this work as fully correct. A neutral common trap is losing the sign and substituting $d=3$, or using $nd$ instead of $(n-1)d$. Neither error occurred in the recorded attempt, so it must not be attributed to the learner; it remains an exam-check point only.

Sense check and verification. Verify the model against the printed sequence: $u_1=78+(1-1)(-3)=78$, $u_2=78-3=75$, and $u_3=78-6=72$. These three checks reproduce the prompt exactly, proving that the chosen $a$ and $d$ are consistent before the larger indices are evaluated.

Exam trigger and transfer. When you see evenly spaced numerical terms, calculate two consecutive differences, state $a$ and signed $d$, then choose term or sum formula from the command word. On another paper, keep the $(n-1)$ visible until substitution and use the trend of the sequence as a final sign check.

STEP 2/2

Strategy and why it applies. Use the nth-term formula because the question asks for one position, not an accumulated total. Substitute the source parameters and the exact index in a single displayed line, then simplify the signed product. This mirrors the mark demand: a method mark for a correct term formula with embedded values and an accuracy mark for the result.

Formula / definition first. The general formula is $u_n=a+(n-1)d$. With $n=18$, $a=78$ and $d=-3$, it becomes $u_{18}=78+(18-1)(-3)$. Keeping parentheses around $-3$ prevents the minus sign from becoming detached from the common difference.

Complete working. Evaluate the offset first: $18-1=17$. Then $17(-3)=-51$, so $u_{18}=78-51=27$. The full chain is $$u_{18}=78+17(-3)=78-51=\boxed{27}.$$ No rounding, units or calculator approximation are involved.

Difficult transition. The difficult transition is index to number of steps. Term 1 has zero jumps, term 2 has one, so term 18 has 17. Writing $78+18(-3)$ would produce 24, which is actually the 19th term. This off-by-one check explains the formula rather than relying on memory alone.

What went wrong / common trap. A common trap is to write the correct formula and then omit multiplication brackets, creating $78+17-3$. Another is to use a sum formula because the prompt calls the list a series. The local command '18th term' controls the method and requires $u_n$, not $S_n$.

Sense check and verification. Check back by counting from a nearby easy term: $u_{10}=78+9(-3)=51$, then eight more decreases give $51-24=27$. The answer is lower than 78, as a negative common difference requires, and it remains positive at term 18, which is plausible from the rate of decrease.

Exam trigger and transfer. Recognition trigger: 'find the nth term' means one value after $n-1$ equal changes. State the formula, substitute signed $d$, and test the direction of change. This presentation earns method credit even if a later arithmetic slip occurs and distinguishes term questions from sum questions.

Part (b)[2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in: From the sequence (and the algebra established in Part (a)): $a=78$ and $d=-3$; the first 100 terms are required.
STEP 1/1

Strategy and why it applies. Use the arithmetic-sum formula because the command asks for the total of 100 terms. Carry the signed common difference from the sequence identification and simplify inside the bracket before multiplying by $n/2$. The negative final sum is not automatically an error: later terms have crossed zero and have large negative magnitude.

Formula / definition first. The supplied formula is $S_n=\dfrac n2[2a+(n-1)d]$. Here $n=100$, $a=78$ and $d=-3$. An equivalent check is $S_n=\dfrac n2(a+l)$ after finding $l=u_{100}$; both routes must agree.

Complete working. Substitute: $$S_{100}=\frac{100}{2}[2(78)+99(-3)].$$ Inside the bracket, $2(78)=156$ and $99(-3)=-297$, giving $156-297=-141$. Therefore $S_{100}=50(-141)=\boxed{-7050}$. The sign is retained in the final answer.

Difficult transition. The difficult transition is accepting that a sum beginning with positive terms can be negative. The 27th term is $78+26(-3)=0$; terms 28 to 100 are negative, and there are many more of them than positive terms. Thus the balance of the series is strongly negative rather than suspicious.

What went wrong / common trap. A common trap is to change $-7050$ to $+7050$ because a 'sum' is assumed positive. The mark scheme explicitly withholds the accuracy mark if a correct negative result is followed by its positive magnitude. Another trap is using 100 differences instead of 99 inside the formula.

Sense check and verification. Verify by the last-term route: $u_{100}=78+99(-3)=-219$. Then $S_{100}=100(78-219)/2=50(-141)=-7050$, exactly matching the direct formula. Agreement of two equivalent formulas and the zero-crossing argument checks both arithmetic and sign.

Exam trigger and transfer. When an arithmetic series extends beyond zero, locate or estimate the sign change before rejecting a negative sum. On another paper, either supplied sum formula is valid; choose one route, keep signed values visible, and use the alternative only as a rapid verification rather than duplicating all working.

Independent sum check using the 100th term. First find $u_{100}=78+99(-3)=78-297=-219$. Then use the other arithmetic-series formula:
$$S_{100}=\frac{100}{2}(u_1+u_{100})=50(78-219)=50(-141)=-7050.$$
This independently agrees with $\frac{100}{2}[2(78)+99(-3)]$.>
