P2 Q2 — binomial expansion

Question

Source/status: Pearson WMA12/01, 15 October 2025, Q2. The submitted expansion was unfinished and scored 0/6; the question was completed live with prompting.

The first 4 terms, in ascending powers of $x$, in the binomial expansion of

$$(1+px)^{10}$$

are

$$1+15x+qx^2+rx^3$$

where $p$, $q$ and $r$ are constants.

Find the value of $p$, the value of $q$ and the value of $r.\quad(6)$

[6 marks]

Method[6 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2

Strategy and why it applies. Expand the source expression far enough to expose the coefficients of $x$, $x^2$ and $x^3$, then compare like powers. The printed target already uses ascending powers, so there is no need to multiply out all eleven terms. Finishing and simplifying the relevant four terms is essential because the first mark requires a usable expansion, not an abandoned template.

Formula / definition first. The binomial theorem gives $(1+z)^{10}=1+\binom{10}{1}z+\binom{10}{2}z^2+\binom{10}{3}z^3+\cdots$. Put $z=px$. Since $(px)^m=p^mx^m$, calculate rather than quote the needed coefficients: $$\binom{10}{2}=\frac{10\times9}{2!}=45,\qquad \binom{10}{3}=\frac{10\times9\times8}{3!}=120.$$ Therefore the first four terms are $1+10px+45p^2x^2+120p^3x^3+\cdots$.

Complete working. Compare the coefficients of $x$: the source target has $15x$, while the expansion has $10px$. Thus $10p=15$, so $p=15/10=3/2$. This is not a mismatch between different-looking terms; $p$ is precisely the constant that makes them equal. Record $p=3/2$ before moving to the higher powers.

Difficult transition. The difficult transition is from the symbolic expansion to coefficient equations. Equality of polynomials for all $x$ means coefficients of the same power are equal independently. Do not compare $10p$ with the whole expression, and do not cancel $x$ across terms of different degree. Simplify each term first, then align $x$, $x^2$, $x^3$.

What went wrong / common trap. The recorded attempt stopped when $10px$ did not visually resemble $15x$. That route failed because it treated an unknown coefficient as evidence of an error instead of an equation to solve. The repair is behavioral and mathematical: finish the simplification, then set $10p=15$. No incorrect expansion had yet been established.

Sense check and verification. Verify $p$ immediately: substituting $p=3/2$ gives the linear term $10(3/2)x=15x$, exactly the source coefficient. This check confirms the first comparison before powers of $p$ amplify any error. The positive sign is also consistent with the positive printed coefficients and the positive base perturbation.

Exam trigger and transfer. When a binomial question presents unknown coefficients, write only the required binomial terms, simplify $(px)^m$ completely, and compare one power at a time. On another paper, the same method works with a non-unit first term; preserve all powers and combinatorial coefficients before solving the resulting coefficient equations.

STEP 2/2

Strategy and why it applies. Use the established $p=3/2$ in the $x^2$ and $x^3$ coefficients. These are separate method-and-accuracy pairs in the mark scheme, so show each substitution explicitly rather than jumping from $p$ to two unsupported decimals. Exact fractional work keeps the cubes and squares auditable and avoids premature rounding.

Formula / definition first. From the theorem, the coefficient of $x^2$ is $\binom{10}{2}p^2=45p^2$, and the coefficient of $x^3$ is $\binom{10}{3}p^3=120p^3$. The source defines these coefficients as $q$ and $r$, so $q=45p^2$ and $r=120p^3$.

Complete working. Substitute $p=3/2$: $$q=45\left(\frac32\right)^2=45\cdot\frac94=\frac{405}{4}=101.25.$$ Similarly, $$r=120\left(\frac32\right)^3=120\cdot\frac{27}{8}=15\cdot27=405.$$ Therefore $\boxed{p=3/2,\ q=405/4=101.25,\ r=405}$.

Difficult transition. The difficult transition is preserving the power on $p$. The square belongs to the whole factor $(px)^2=p^2x^2$, and the cube gives $p^3x^3$. Writing $45p x^2$ or $120p x^3$ would destroy the binomial structure even if the combinatorial numbers 45 and 120 were correct.

What went wrong / common trap. A common trap is to evaluate $\binom{10}{3}$ as 720 because the factorial numerator is not divided by $3!$. Another is to round $p$ before cubing; here $p=1.5$ is exact, but exact fractions remain the safer general habit. The lesson's main failure was incompletion, not a wrong value for $p$, so the explanation must not invent another error.

Sense check and verification. Check the reconstructed expansion: $1+15x+101.25x^2+405x^3+\cdots$. Ratios are not expected to be constant because these are binomial coefficients multiplied by increasing powers of $p$. Independently, $120(27/8)=15(27)=405$, and $45(9/4)=405/4$, confirming the arithmetic.

Exam trigger and transfer. Recognition trigger: printed coefficients plus an unknown inside a binomial require coefficient comparison.
