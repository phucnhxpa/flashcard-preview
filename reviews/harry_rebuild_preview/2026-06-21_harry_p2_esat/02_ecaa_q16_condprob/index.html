ECAA specimen Q16 — conditional probability

Question

To get to work, Sylvie first catches a bus and then catches a train.

The probability that the bus is on time is $0.6$. The probability that the bus is late is $0.4$. If the bus is on time, then the probability that she will catch the train is $0.8$. If the bus is late, then the probability that she will catch the train is $0.6$.

Given that Sylvie catches the train, what is the probability that the bus was on time?

A  $\frac13$   B  $\frac1225$   C  $\frac25$   D  $\frac35$   E  $\frac23$   F  $\frac1825$   G  $\frac67$

[1 mark]

Method[1 mark] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2

Strategy and why it applies. Translate the sequence of events into a complete two-stage tree before using any conditional formula. The phrase 'given that Sylvie catches the train' changes the sample space to catch outcomes only. A tree is the reliable structure because the second-stage probability depends on whether the bus was on time; a single unconditional multiplication would erase that dependency.

Formula / definition first. At every fork, exhaustive branch probabilities sum to one. Along a path, multiply conditional probabilities: $P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B\mid A)$. Across mutually exclusive paths leading to the same event, add. These are the general multiplication and addition rules that justify the tree, not a diagram convention.

Complete working. Complete all four paths. On-time and catch is $0.6\times0.8=0.48$; on-time and miss is $0.6\times0.2=0.12$; late and catch is $0.4\times0.6=0.24$; late and miss is $0.4\times0.4=0.16$. The four products sum to one, so no outcome is missing. The catch event consists of the two catch paths, giving $0.48+0.24=0.72$.

Difficult transition. The important transition is from the full tree to the restricted 'given catch' population. The numerator must be both on time and catch, $0.48$; the denominator must be every way to catch, $0.72$. Do not divide by $P(\text{on time})$, and do not use only the successful on-time branch as the denominator.

What went wrong / common trap. The lesson evidence says the conditional phrase was recognised but complements, tree completion and path multiplication needed prompting. The common trap is to assume independence and write $P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)$. That fails here because the problem explicitly gives different catch probabilities, $0.8$ and $0.6$, according to the bus outcome.

Sense check and verification. Verify the tree first: $0.48+0.12+0.24+0.16=1$. Then check plausibility. Catching the train is more likely after an on-time bus, so conditioning on catch should raise the on-time probability above its prior value $0.6$. The final $2/3\approx0.667$ does, while remaining below one.

Exam trigger and transfer. When a question says 'given that' after a staged process, draw every branch, multiply along, add across, and circle the denominator event. On another paper, the same sequence applies to test results, production lines, weather and travel connections. Keep conditional probabilities on their proper parent branches rather than moving them to the first fork.

STEP 2/2

Strategy and why it applies. Now apply the conditional-probability definition to the two values isolated by the tree. Writing the event notation before substitution prevents the numerator and denominator from being reversed. This is the shortest non-calculator finish and connects the picture to a reusable formula rather than treating the answer as a visual guess.

Formula / definition first. For $A=\{\text{bus on time}\}$ and $B=\{\text{catches train}\}$, the definition is $P(A\mid B)=\dfrac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}$, provided $P(B)>0$. The tree has already supplied $P(A\cap B)=0.48$ and $P(B)=0.72$, so every symbol now has a concrete event meaning.

Complete working. Substitute to obtain $P(A\mid B)=0.48/0.72=48/72$. Cancel a factor of $24$ to get $2/3$. This exact simplification is appropriate for a calculator-free admissions item and identifies option E. Decimal conversion is unnecessary, though $0.666\ldots$ is useful as a plausibility check.

Difficult transition. The subtle distinction is $P(A\mid B)$ versus $P(B\mid A)$. The given $0.8$ is $P(B\mid A)$, the chance of catch when on time; it is not the requested reverse conditional. Bayes' effect appears because the denominator combines on-time catches and late catches. Reversing the vertical bar would simply repeat a number already printed in the prompt.

What went wrong / common trap. A common trap is to answer $0.8$ because it is the only printed probability mentioning both events. Another is $0.48$, which is the joint probability before restricting to catch outcomes. The wording 'given that catches' demands renormalisation: among the $0.72$ catch mass, $0.48$ belongs to the on-time branch.

Sense check and verification. Check the exact fraction against the options and the prior-to-posterior direction. $2/3$ is option E and lies between $0.6$ and $0.8$, which is sensible: evidence of catching the train makes an on-time bus more likely, but does not make it certain. The source explained answer also gives E, independently matching the arithmetic.

Exam trigger and transfer. Recognition trigger: write 'wanted intersection / given event' beside any conditional question, then build the denominator from all branches that satisfy the condition. If the second-stage rates differ, independence is impossible. Preserve exact fractions when the option list is fractional and use decimals only as a check, not as the primary calculator-free method.
