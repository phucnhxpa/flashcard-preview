WMA12 October 2025 Q4 — polynomial division, factors and decreasing interval

Question

In this question you must show all stages of your working.

Solutions relying entirely on calculator technology are not acceptable.

\[f(x)=4x^3+13x^2-10x+8\]

(a) When \(f(x)\) is divided by \((x-2)\) the remainder is \(R\) and the quotient is \(Q(x)\).

    (i) Find \(Q(x)\).

    (ii) Find \(R\). (4)

(b) (i) Use the factor theorem to show that \((x+4)\) is a factor of \(f(x)\).

    (ii) Hence prove, using algebra, that the equation \(f(x)=0\) has only one real solution. (5)

(c) Find the range of values of \(x\) for which \(f(x)\) is decreasing. (3)

[12 marks: (a) 4, (b) 5, (c) 3]

Part (a)(i)[within 4 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1DIVIDE

Strategy and why it applies. Because the divisor is linear, polynomial long division gives one quotient term at a time. Keep the cubic in descending powers, including the negative \(x\)-term and the constant. At each cycle: divide the leading terms, write the new quotient term, multiply the whole divisor by it, subtract, then bring down the next term.

Full long-division tableau. Every product and subtraction row is shown:

\[\begin{array}{r|rrrr} &4x^2&+21x&+32&\\ x-2&4x^3&+13x^2&-10x&+8\\ \hline &4x^3&-8x^2&&\\ \hline &&21x^2&-10x&\\ &&21x^2&-42x&\\ \hline &&&32x&+8\\ &&&32x&-64\\ \hline &&&&72 \end{array}\]

Read the tableau line by line. First, \(4x^3\div x=4x^2\). Multiply: \((x-2)(4x^2)=4x^3-8x^2\). Subtracting this from \(4x^3+13x^2\) gives \(21x^2\), then bring down \(-10x\). Next, \(21x^2\div x=21x\). Multiply: \((x-2)(21x)=21x^2-42x\). Subtracting gives \(32x\), then bring down \(+8\). Finally, \(32x\div x=32\). Multiply: \((x-2)(32)=32x-64\). The final subtraction is \((32x+8)-(32x-64)=72\).

Why the signs matter: subtracting \(21x^2-42x\) changes the sign of \(-42x\), so \(-10x-(-42x)=32x\). This is the common place to lose the correct constant term in the quotient.
\(Q(x)=4x^2+21x+32\)

Part (a)(ii)[within 4 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1REMAINDER

Use the final subtraction, then check independently. The long division ends with the constant \(72\), which has degree lower than the linear divisor and therefore is the remainder. Keep it separate from the quotient: the division identity is

\[f(x)=(x-2)Q(x)+R.\]

Substituting the quotient found above gives

\[4x^3+13x^2-10x+8=(x-2)(4x^2+21x+32)+72.\]

The remainder theorem gives a quick check without repeating the division. Since division is by \(x-2\), evaluate at \(x=2\):

\[R=f(2)=4(2)^3+13(2)^2-10(2)+8=32+52-20+8=72.\]

Check-back: putting \(x=2\) into \((x-2)Q(x)+R\) makes the product zero and leaves \(R\), exactly matching \(f(2)\).
\(R=72\)

Part (b)(i)[within 5 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1FACTOR THEOREM

State the theorem before applying it. The factor theorem says that \(x-a\) is a factor of \(f(x)\) if and only if \(f(a)=0\). Here \(x+4=x-(-4)\), so the required substitution is \(x=-4\), not \(x=4\).

\[\begin{aligned}f(-4)&=4(-4)^3+13(-4)^2-10(-4)+8\\&=4(-64)+13(16)+40+8\\&=-256+208+40+8\\&=0.\end{aligned}\]

Because \(f(-4)=0\), the factor theorem proves that \(x-(-4)=x+4\) is a factor of \(f(x)\).

Exam presentation: the numerical substitution alone is not the complete proof. Finish with the explicit factor-theorem conclusion linking the zero value to the factor \(x+4\).
\(f(-4)=0\), hence \((x+4)\) is a factor of \(f(x)\).

Part (b)(ii)[within 5 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in from (b)(i): \((x+4)\) is a factor of \(f(x)\).
STEP 1/1PROVE UNIQUENESS

Factor the cubic, then classify the remaining quadratic. Dividing \(f(x)\) by \(x+4\), or matching coefficients, gives

\[f(x)=(x+4)(4x^2-3x+2).\]

This expands to \(4x^3+16x^2-3x^2-12x+2x+8=4x^3+13x^2-10x+8\), so the factorisation is correct. Therefore \(f(x)=0\) requires either \(x+4=0\) or \(4x^2-3x+2=0\).

For the quadratic, use the discriminant:

\[\Delta=b^2-4ac=(-3)^2-4(4)(2)=9-32=-23<0.\]

A real quadratic with negative discriminant has no real roots. Thus the quadratic factor contributes no real solutions, while the linear factor gives \(x=-4\). This proves both existence and uniqueness: there is one real solution, and there cannot be another from the quadratic factor.

Check-back: part (b)(i) already verifies that \(x=-4\) is a root. The negative discriminant closes the possibility of any additional real roots.
The equation \(f(x)=0\) has only one real solution: \(x=-4\).

Part (c)[3 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1DIFFERENTIATE + SIGN CHART

Translate “decreasing” into a derivative inequality. A differentiable function is decreasing where \(f'(x)<0\). Differentiate and factor:

\[f'(x)=12x^2+26x-10=2(3x-1)(2x+5).\]

Find the critical values by solving \(2(3x-1)(2x+5)=0\):

\[3x-1=0\Rightarrow x=\frac13,\qquad 2x+5=0\Rightarrow x=-\frac52.\]

Sign chart. The positive factor \(2\) does not affect the sign. The “factor signs” column lists the signs of \(3x-1\) and \(2x+5\), in that order.

\(-\frac52<x<\frac13\)−, +−decreasing
\(x>\frac13\)+, ++increasing

The derivative is negative only between the two roots. The endpoints are excluded because \(f'(x)=0\) there, whereas the required inequality is strict.

\(-\dfrac52<x<\dfrac13\)
