Q2 — Arithmetic series and an inequality in n

Question

In this question you must show all stages of your working.

Solutions relying entirely on calculator technology are not acceptable.

In an arithmetic series,

  • the sixth term is \(2\)
  • the sum of the first ten terms is \(-80\)

For this series,

(a) find the value of the first term and the value of the common difference. (4)

(b) Hence find the smallest value of \(n\) for which \(S_n>8000\). (3)

Status: Lesson reviewed; the arithmetic check in part (b) is corrected below.

[7 marks]

Part (a)[4 marks]

Carry-in: Given directly: sixth term \(T_6=2\) and ten-term sum \(S_{10}=-80\).
STEP 1/1

Strategy and why. Translate each verbal fact into a different standard arithmetic-series equation. One equation uses a single term and the other uses a finite sum, giving two independent linear equations in the first term and common difference. Keeping these roles separate avoids confusing an nth term with a sum.

Formula, definition or identity before substitution. Use \(T_n=a+(n-1)d\) and \(S_n=\frac n2[2a+(n-1)d]\). Here \(T_6=a+5d\) and \(S_{10}=5(2a+9d)\).

Full source-specific derivation. The source facts give \[a+5d=2,\qquad 5(2a+9d)=-80.\] Divide the second equation by 5: \[2a+9d=-16.\] Double the first equation to obtain \(2a+10d=4\). Subtracting gives \(d=20\), and then \(a+100=2\), so \[\boxed{a=-98,\quad d=20}.\]

Diagnosis and why the rejected route fails. A common wrong route is to treat -80 as the tenth term or to omit the factor \(n/2\) in the sum. Either error destroys the independent equation needed to identify both unknowns. The official numerical facts are used because a video-led reconstruction supplied incompatible values.

Check back and interpretation. Substitute into both original conditions, not only the simplified equations: \(T_6=-98+5(20)=2\), and \(S_{10}=5[-196+180]=5(-16)=-80\). Both checks exactly reproduce the QP.

Recognition trigger. When two facts about an arithmetic series are given, label each fact as TERM or SUM before writing algebra. The labels determine which formula is legitimate and expose accidental formula swapping.

Part (b)[3 marks]

Carry-in: From part (a): \(a=-98\) and \(d=20\). The target is the smallest positive integer \(n\) for which \(S_n>8000\).
STEP 1/1

Strategy and why. Insert the established parameters into the arithmetic-sum formula, reduce to a quadratic inequality, find the positive boundary root, and then test the neighbouring integers. The word smallest makes integer verification essential; a decimal root alone is not a final answer.

Formula, definition or identity before substitution. Use \(S_n=\frac n2[2a+(n-1)d]\). A quadratic with positive leading coefficient is positive outside its two roots, so the admissible positive integers lie above the positive root. Because \(n\) counts terms, it must be integral.

Full source-specific derivation. Substitution gives \[\frac n2[-196+20(n-1)]>8000.\] Simplifying and dividing by 2 gives \[5n^2-54n-4000>0.\] The positive root is about \(34.2\). Therefore the first possible integer above it is 35. Use the original sum formula for the boundary check: \[-196+33(20)=464,\qquad S_{34}=\frac{34}{2}(464)=17(464)=7888<8000.\] For the next integer, \[-196+34(20)=484,\qquad S_{35}=\frac{35}{2}(484)=8470>8000.\] Thus, \[\boxed{n=35}.\]

Diagnosis and why the rejected route fails. The lesson diagnosis is an arithmetic slip during formation or simplification of the quadratic, not a conceptual failure. A route that rounds \(34.2\) to 34 fails because the inequality asks for a value above the boundary, and a route that writes only the calculator root fails the smallest-integer command.

Check back and interpretation. The neighbouring-value check proves minimality: 34 does not exceed 8000 and 35 does. It also checks the direction of the quadratic inequality and catches any coefficient slip that might still produce a plausible-looking root.

Recognition trigger. For every smallest integer satisfying an inequality, solve the continuous boundary first, choose the first integer in the permitted region, then test that integer and its predecessor in the original expression.
