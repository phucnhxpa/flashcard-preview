Q4 — Remainder and factor theorems

Question

\(f(x)=(x-2)(2x^2+5x+k)+21\), where \(k\) is a constant.

(a) State the remainder when \(f(x)\) is divided by \((x-2)\). (1)

Given that \((2x-1)\) is a factor of \(f(x)\), (b) show that \(k=11\). (2)

(c) Hence (i) fully factorise \(f(x)\), (ii) find the number of real solutions of \(f(x)=0\), giving a reason for your answer. (5)

Status: Lesson reviewed; the final discriminant reason is made explicit below.

[8 marks]

Part (a)[1 marks]

Carry-in: Given directly: \(f(x)\) is already displayed as \((x-2)\times\text{quotient}+21\).
STEP 1/1

Strategy and why. Match the divisor \(x-2\) to the remainder theorem and notice that the polynomial is already in division-algorithm form. No expansion or long division is necessary; evaluation at the divisor root immediately removes the product term.

Formula, definition or identity before substitution. If \(f(x)=(x-a)q(x)+r\), then \(f(a)=r\). For divisor \(x-2\), take \(a=2\).

Full source-specific derivation. Evaluate the given expression: \[f(2)=(2-2)(2\cdot2^2+5\cdot2+k)+21.\] The first factor is zero, so the entire product vanishes and \[f(2)=\boxed{21}.\] Therefore the remainder on division by \(x-2\) is 21.

Diagnosis and why the rejected route fails. The observed first attempt used long division. That route is not mathematically forbidden, but it is strategically poor here: it expands a form that already displays divisor times quotient plus remainder, spends time on a one-mark result, and increases the chance of coefficient mistakes.

Check back and interpretation. Read the original expression literally: it has the exact structure \((x-2)(\cdots)+21\). Substituting 2 must leave 21 regardless of the unknown \(k\), which is a strong structural check.

Recognition trigger. The word remainder plus divisor \(x-a\) should trigger \(f(a)\). Before doing any algebra, inspect whether the expression is already written as divisor times something plus a constant.

Part (b)[2 marks]

Carry-in: Given directly: \((2x-1)\) is a factor, so its root \(x=1/2\) must satisfy \(f(1/2)=0\).
STEP 1/1

Strategy and why. Convert the factor statement into a root condition, substitute the root into the given structured form, and solve the resulting linear equation in \(k\). Keeping fractions exact makes the demanded show-that value transparent.

Formula, definition or identity before substitution. The factor theorem states: if \((x-a)\) is a factor, then \(f(a)=0\). The factor \(2x-1\) has root \(x=1/2\), so use \(f(1/2)=0\).

Full source-specific derivation. Substitute \(x=1/2\): \[0=\left(-\frac32\right)\left(2\cdot\frac14+5\cdot\frac12+k\right)+21.\] The bracket is \(1/2+5/2+k=3+k\). Hence \[-\frac32(3+k)+21=0.\] So \(-3(3+k)+42=0\), giving \(3+k=14\) and \[\boxed{k=11}.\]

Diagnosis and why the rejected route fails. Substituting \(x=2\) because part (a) used 2 fails: the new trigger is the factor \(2x-1\), whose zero is one half. Expanding the whole cubic before applying the factor theorem is possible but obscures the one linear equation the question is designed to test.

Check back and interpretation. Place \(k=11\) back into the bracket: \(3+k=14\), and \((-3/2)(14)+21=-21+21=0\). This confirms that \(x=1/2\) is truly a root.

Recognition trigger. For a non-monic linear factor \(px-q\), solve \(px-q=0\) first and evaluate at \(x=q/p\). Never copy the visible constant as the root without solving.

Part (c)[5 marks]

Carry-in: From part (b): \(k=11\), and \((2x-1)\) is a known factor of \(f(x)\).
STEP 1/1

Strategy and why. Substitute the established parameter, express the cubic in standard form, divide by the known factor, then use the discriminant of the residual quadratic to finish both factorisation and the count of real roots. The command giving a reason requires more than listing one root.

Formula, definition or identity before substitution. Polynomial division or coefficient matching gives the quotient after removing \(2x-1\). For \(Ax^2+Bx+C\), the discriminant \(\Delta=B^2-4AC\) determines real roots: \(\Delta<0\) means none.

Full source-specific derivation. With \(k=11\), expansion gives \[f(x)=2x^3+x^2+x-1.\] Dividing by \(2x-1\), or matching coefficients, gives \[f(x)=(2x-1)(x^2+x+1).\] For the quadratic, \[\Delta=1^2-4(1)(1)=-3<0.\] It has no real zeros, while \(2x-1=0\) gives \(x=1/2\). Thus the full factorisation is boxed above and there is \[\boxed{1\text{ real solution}}.\]

Diagnosis and why the rejected route fails. The learner tried to factor \(x^2+x+1\) into real linear factors and became stuck. That route fails because the quadratic has no real factor pair; the discriminant diagnoses this immediately. A mid-division sign slip was self-corrected, so the final clean route shows only the correct quotient.

Check back and interpretation. Multiply back: \((2x-1)(x^2+x+1)=2x^3+x^2+x-1\). Then evaluate at \(x=1/2\) to verify the linear root. The negative discriminant supplies the requested reason for excluding further real solutions.

Recognition trigger. After removing a known linear factor, do not repeatedly guess factors of the remaining quadratic. Compute its discriminant; it simultaneously checks factorability over the reals and answers a root-count command.
