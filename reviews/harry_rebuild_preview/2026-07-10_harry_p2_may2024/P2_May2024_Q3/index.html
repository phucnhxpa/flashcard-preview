Q3 — Logarithm laws and domain checking

Question

In this question you must show all stages of your working. Solutions relying entirely on calculator technology are not acceptable.

(i) Using the laws of logarithms, solve \(2\log_2(2-x)=4+\log_2(x+10)\). (5)

(ii) Find the value of \(\log_{\sqrt a}(a^6)\), where \(a\) is a positive constant greater than 1. (1)

Status: Lesson reviewed; both parts were correct.

[6 marks]

Part (i)[5 marks]

Carry-in: Given directly: logarithms have base 2; impose \(2-x>0\) and \(x+10>0\) before accepting any algebraic root.
STEP 1/1

Strategy and why. Use log laws to convert the equation into one equality of positive arguments, solve the resulting quadratic, and then return to the original logarithm domains. Squaring an argument can create an algebraic root that the original logarithm never permits, so the domain check is part of the solution.

Formula, definition or identity before substitution. Use \(p\log_bA=\log_b(A^p)\), \(4=\log_2 16\), and \(\log_bM=\log_bN\Rightarrow M=N\) when both arguments are positive.

Full source-specific derivation. Combine the logs: \[\log_2(2-x)^2=\log_2[16(x+10)].\] Thus \[(2-x)^2=16(x+10),\] so \(x^2-20x-156=0\), which factors as \((x+6)(x-26)=0\). The candidates are \(-6\) and 26. The original condition \(2-x>0\) requires \(x<2\), while \(x+10>0\) requires \(x>-10\). Therefore \[\boxed{x=-6}.\]

Diagnosis and why the rejected route fails. Stopping after the quadratic gives two apparent answers, but \(x=26\) makes \(2-x=-24\), so \(\log_2(2-x)\) is undefined over the real numbers. The rejected root is not optional presentation; it is the evidence that the original equation, not merely its transformed square, was solved.

Check back and interpretation. Substitute \(x=-6\): the left side is \(2\log_2 8=6\), and the right side is \(4+\log_2 4=6\). For 26, the first log is not defined. This checks both equality and domain.

Recognition trigger. When log algebra produces a polynomial, box candidates temporarily, not finally. Reapply every original positivity condition before promoting a candidate to a solution.

Part (ii)[1 marks]

Carry-in: Given directly: \(a>1\), so \(a\), \(\sqrt a\), and their logarithms are valid positive bases and arguments.
STEP 1/1

Strategy and why. Rewrite both the base and argument as powers of the same positive quantity. The logarithm then asks what exponent on \(a^{1/2}\) produces \(a^6\); this is faster and clearer than converting to decimals.

Formula, definition or identity before substitution. Use \(\log_{a^p}(a^q)=q/p\), derived from \((a^p)^r=a^q\Rightarrow pr=q\). Here \(p=1/2\) and \(q=6\).

Full source-specific derivation. Let \[r=\log_{\sqrt a}(a^6).\] Then \((\sqrt a)^r=a^6\). Since \(\sqrt a=a^{1/2}\), \[a^{r/2}=a^6.\] Equal positive bases give \(r/2=6\), hence \[\boxed{r=12}.\] Equivalently, change of base gives \(6/(1/2)=12\).

Diagnosis and why the rejected route fails. A result of 3 comes from multiplying 6 by one half instead of dividing by the base exponent. The logarithm solves for the exponent on the base, so the base exponent must divide the argument exponent. The source PDF, not the degraded text extraction, governs the square-root base.

Check back and interpretation. Raise the stated base to the answer: \((\sqrt a)^{12}=(a^{1/2})^{12}=a^6\). The condition \(a>1\) also confirms that the base is positive and not equal to 1.

Recognition trigger. For logarithms whose base and argument are powers of one symbol, convert both to \(a^p\) and \(a^q\), then solve \(pr=q\). This avoids change-of-base arithmetic errors.
