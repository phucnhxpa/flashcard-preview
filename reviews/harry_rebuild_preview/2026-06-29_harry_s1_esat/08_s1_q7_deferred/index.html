WST01 October 2025 Q7 — normal distribution

Question

The wingspans, $W$ mm, of Speckled Wood butterflies are normally distributed, with $W\sim N(51,1.7^2)$.

A Speckled Wood butterfly is selected at random.

(a) Use standardisation to find the probability that this butterfly has a wingspan between 48.45 mm and 54.40 mm (3)

Given that this butterfly has a wingspan between 48.45 mm and 54.40 mm,

(b) find the probability that the wingspan of this butterfly is greater than 53.55 mm
You must show your working. (3)

The wingspans, $X$ mm, of Large White butterflies are also normally distributed, with $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$.

Given that $P(X\lt64.58)=0.30$ and $P(X\gt69.46)=0.10$

(c) calculate the value of $\mu$ and the value of $\sigma$
You must show your working. (4)

Not covered in the lesson — official-reference reconstruction below.

[10 marks: (a)3, (b)3, (c)4]

Part (a)[3 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2Official-reference reconstruction

Exam trigger. When you see a normal probability between two raw measurements, standardise both boundaries before using the normal table.

Standardise with mean 51 and standard deviation 1.7 (not variance $1.7^2$): $$z_L=\frac{48.45-51}{1.7}=-1.5,\qquad z_U=\frac{54.40-51}{1.7}=2.$$

Why standardise. The wingspan W follows a normal distribution with a specific mean and spread, but probability tables and the calculator work only for the standard normal, so the strategy is to convert each raw wingspan into a z-score that measures how many standard deviations it lies from the mean. The definition applied is z equals the observation minus the mean, all divided by the standard deviation, and the single most common slip is dividing by the variance instead: the notation N with fifty-one and one point seven squared means the variance is one point seven squared, so the standard deviation is one point seven itself, and it is one point seven that belongs in the denominator. The intermediate logic is to standardise both endpoints separately, giving a lower z-score for forty-eight point four five and an upper z-score for fifty-four point four, so the awkward interval on the wingspan axis becomes a clean interval on the z-axis. A relevant wrong route is to plug in the variance, which would shrink both z-scores and quietly ruin the probability, or to forget the subtraction of the mean and standardise the raw value alone. Check that a wingspan below the mean produces a negative z and one above the mean a positive z, which is exactly what the two scores show. In the exam, write the standardisation formula once, then each substitution on its own line, so the method mark is secured before any table lookup. Transfer cue: for every normal-distribution probability, standardise first and treat the standard deviation, never the variance, as the divisor.

Therefore $$P(48.45\lt W\lt54.40)=\Phi(2)-\Phi(-1.5)=0.9772-0.0668=0.9104.$$ Hence $\boxed{0.910\text{ (3 s.f.)}}$.

Check. The interval runs from 1.5 standard deviations below the mean to 2 above it, so a probability a little above 0.9 is plausible.

Combining the tail probabilities. Once both endpoints are standardised, the probability of landing between them equals the cumulative probability up to the upper z-score minus the cumulative probability up to the lower z-score, written with the capital Phi notation for the standard normal distribution function. The intermediate logic is that Phi of the upper score gives everything to the left of the upper endpoint, and subtracting Phi of the lower score removes everything to the left of the lower endpoint, leaving exactly the strip in between. Because the lower z-score is negative, its cumulative value is found using the symmetry of the normal curve, so Phi of a negative number equals one minus Phi of the positive counterpart, which is why a small value is subtracted here. A relevant wrong route is to add the two cumulative values instead of subtracting, or to look up the lower score as though it were positive without applying symmetry, either of which inflates the answer well beyond one and signals the error immediately. Check against the dashed reasoning: the interval stretches from one and a half standard deviations below the mean to two above, capturing most of the central mass, so a probability just above nine tenths is exactly what to expect. In the exam, state Phi upper minus Phi lower symbolically, substitute the tabulated values, and round to three significant figures as the marks demand. Transfer cue: any between-two-values normal probability is always the larger cumulative minus the smaller, with symmetry handling negative scores.

Part (b)[3 marks] · 2 steps

Carry-in: From part (a), $P(48.45\lt W\lt54.40)=0.910442\ldots$.
STEP 1/2Official-reference reconstruction

The condition restricts the sample space to $48.45\lt W\lt54.40$. Intersecting it with $W\gt53.55$ leaves $53.55\lt W\lt54.40$. Since $$(53.55-51)/1.7=1.5,$$ the numerator is $$P(1.5\lt Z\lt2)=0.9772-0.9332=0.0440.$$

Reading the conditional set-up. The phrase given that the wingspan lies between forty-eight point four five and fifty-four point four announces a conditional probability, so the strategy is to shrink the sample space to that interval and then ask how much of the desired event survives inside it. The event of interest, a wingspan greater than fifty-three point five five, overlaps the conditioning interval only up to its upper limit of fifty-four point four, so the intersection is the narrow strip from fifty-three point five five to fifty-four point four, and this strip is the numerator of the conditional probability. The intermediate logic is to standardise the new left endpoint fifty-three point five five, which sits exactly one and a half standard deviations above the mean, so the numerator becomes the standard-normal probability between one point five and two, computed as the difference of the two cumulative values. A relevant wrong route is to compute the plain unconditional probability that W exceeds fifty-three point five five and stop there, ignoring the conditioning entirely, or to let the upper limit of the strip run to infinity instead of capping it at fifty-four point four where the conditioning interval ends. Check that this numerator strip lies wholly inside the conditioning interval, which guarantees the eventual ratio cannot exceed one. In the exam, draw a quick sketch shading the surviving strip and label both z-scores, since that picture justifies the numerator. Transfer cue: for conditional probability, always identify the intersection event first and standardise its actual boundaries, never the original unconditioned ones.

STEP 2/2Official-reference reconstruction

Apply conditional probability: $$\frac{P(53.55\lt W\lt54.40)}{P(48.45\lt W\lt54.40)}=\frac{0.044057\ldots}{0.910442\ldots}=0.048390\ldots.$$ Thus $\boxed{0.0484}$.

Check. The numerator interval is contained in the denominator interval, so the ratio must lie between 0 and 1.

Applying the conditional formula. The definition of conditional probability says the probability of A given B equals the probability of A and B together divided by the probability of B, and here B is the conditioning interval carried in from part (a) while A and B together is the narrow strip found in the previous step. So the calculation divides the strip probability by the full interval probability, and it is important to use the more precise unrounded value of the denominator from part (a) rather than the three-significant-figure answer, because rounding early would drift the final digits. The intermediate logic is that dividing by a denominator slightly below one gently enlarges the numerator, which is why the conditional value is a little larger than the raw strip probability. A relevant wrong route is to invert the fraction and divide the interval probability by the strip, or to subtract the two probabilities instead of dividing, both of which misuse the definition; another is reusing the rounded 0.910 and losing accuracy. Check the result against the dashed note: because the numerator strip is entirely contained within the conditioning interval, the ratio is forced between zero and one, and a small positive value is exactly right. In the exam, write the ratio symbolically with words like P of the strip over P of the interval, then substitute the carried unrounded figures, honouring the instruction to show working. Transfer cue: conditional probability is always a ratio of a joint event to the condition, and you should carry full-precision intermediate values until the final rounding.

Part (c)[4 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2Official-reference reconstruction

Convert both statements to left-tail standard scores. From $P(X\lt64.58)=0.30$, $z_{0.30}=-0.524400\ldots$. From $P(X\gt69.46)=0.10$, $P(X\lt69.46)=0.90$, so $z_{0.90}=1.281552\ldots$.

Hence $$\frac{64.58-\mu}{\sigma}=-0.524400,\qquad \frac{69.46-\mu}{\sigma}=1.281552.$$

Turning percentiles into equations. Here the mean and standard deviation are the unknowns, so the strategy reverses the usual direction: instead of finding a probability from a z-score, you read the z-score that corresponds to a known probability and build simultaneous equations. The definition used is the inverse normal, so the statement that the probability X is below sixty-four point five eight equals nought point three gives the z-score whose left-tail area is nought point three, a negative value because nought point three is less than a half. The second statement is about an upper tail, and the intermediate logic is to convert it first: the probability X exceeds sixty-nine point four six is nought point one, so the probability X is below sixty-nine point four six is nought point nine, whose z-score is positive. Each z-score then feeds the standardisation identity, giving two equations in which the raw value minus the mean over the standard deviation equals the corresponding z. A relevant wrong route is to look up nought point one directly and forget to convert the greater-than statement into a less-than one, which flips a sign and wrecks the system; another is treating the below-nought-point-three z-score as positive. Check the signs: the smaller percentile must map to a negative z and the larger to a positive z, matching the two equations. In the exam, write both probability statements as left-tail form before quoting z-scores. Transfer cue: whenever a normal problem hides mean or standard deviation, convert every probability to a left-tail z and assemble simultaneous equations.

STEP 2/2Official-reference reconstruction

Write $64.58=\mu-0.524400\sigma$ and $69.46=\mu+1.281552\sigma$. Subtracting gives $$4.88=(1.805952)\sigma,$$ so $$\sigma=2.70218\ldots\approx\boxed{2.70\text{ mm}}.$$ Then $$\mu=64.58+0.524400(2.70218\ldots)=65.9970\ldots\approx\boxed{66.0\text{ mm}}.$$

Check. $64.58\lt\mu\lt69.46$ and $\sigma\gt0$, consistent with the 30th and 90th percentiles.

Solving the simultaneous equations. Each standardisation equation can be cleared of its denominator by multiplying through by the standard deviation, giving sixty-four point five eight equals the mean minus nought point five two four four times sigma and sixty-nine point four six equals the mean plus one point two eight one five times sigma. The strategy is elimination: subtracting the first equation from the second removes the mean entirely and leaves the difference four point eight eight equal to the sum of the two z-magnitudes multiplied by sigma, so sigma is that difference divided by one point eight zero five nine. The intermediate logic is that the gap between the two raw values equals the gap between their z-positions scaled by one standard deviation, which is precisely what makes elimination clean here. Once sigma is known, back-substitute into either equation to recover the mean; using the lower percentile, the mean equals sixty-four point five eight plus nought point five two four four times sigma. A relevant wrong route is to add the equations instead of subtracting, which fails to eliminate the mean, or to keep too few decimal places in the inverse-normal z-scores, which noticeably shifts the final sigma and mean. Check against the dashed note that the recovered mean lies between the thirtieth and ninetieth percentile values and that sigma is positive, both of which hold. In the exam, show the subtraction that isolates sigma, then the substitution that gives the mean, rounding only at the end. Transfer cue: two percentile conditions always yield two linear equations solved by elimination, with subtraction clearing the mean first.
