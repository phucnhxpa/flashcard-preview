WST01 October 2025 Q5 — grouped data and coding

Question

The weights, $x$ grams, of 300 medium-sized apples are summarised in the table below.

WeightFrequency
$105\leq x\lt115$28
$115\leq x\lt125$42
$125\leq x\lt130$75
$130\leq x\lt140$63
$140\leq x\lt150$52
$150\leq x\lt170$40

A histogram is drawn (not shown) to represent these data. The bar that represents the class $125\leq x\lt130$ is 1.8 cm wide and 12 cm high.

(a) Calculate the width and the height of the bar that represents the class $150\leq x\lt170$ (3)

(b) Use linear interpolation to estimate the median of $x$
Give your answer to one decimal place. (2)

The weights, $w$ grams, of 300 large apples are coded using $y=(w-200)/2$. These coded data are summarised by $\sum y=6000$ and $\sum y^2=150000$.

(c) Calculate

    (i) the mean of $w$ (2)
    (ii) the variance of $w$ (3)

Not covered in the lesson — official-reference reconstruction below.

[10 marks: (a)3, (b)2, (c)(i)2, (c)(ii)3]

Part (a)[3 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2Official-reference reconstruction

Strategy. Calibrate horizontal width from class width, then use histogram area (or frequency density) for height. The given class has data width $5$ represented by $1.8$ cm, so the horizontal scale is $1.8/5$ cm per gram.

The target class has width $170-150=20$, hence its drawn width is $$20\times\frac{1.8}{5}=\boxed{7.2\text{ cm}}.$$

Common trap. Do not make height proportional to frequency alone: unequal class widths require frequency density. Exam trigger. When a histogram has unequal class widths, compare frequency densities or equivalent bar areas before finding a missing height.

Why this works. A histogram encodes frequency as area, so before touching heights you must fix the common horizontal scale that every bar shares. The reference class spans weights from 125 to 130, a true class width of five grams, and that physical width is drawn as 1.8 cm; dividing 1.8 by 5 isolates a single conversion factor of 0.36 cm per gram that applies to every other bar on the same axis. The target class runs from 150 to 170, so its real width is twenty grams, and multiplying twenty by that fixed factor is the only legitimate way to obtain its drawn width. The intermediate logic to hold onto is that width on paper depends purely on class boundaries, never on frequency; frequency will enter only when we tackle height in the next step. A tempting wrong route is to scale the drawn width by the frequency ratio forty over seventy-five, or to assume every bar is 1.8 cm wide because the first one was, and both mistakes corrupt the area calibration for the rest of the question. Check the result by confirming the wide class really is four times broader in grams than the reference class, so its drawn width should be four times 1.8, matching the value found. In the exam, write the sentence scale equals width in centimetres over width in grams before substituting, so an examiner sees the calibration explicitly and awards the method mark even if arithmetic slips. Transfer this whenever a diagram mixes centimetres and data units: always extract one units-per-unit conversion factor first and reuse it, rather than rescaling each measurement independently.

STEP 2/2Official-reference reconstruction

The given bar has drawn area $1.8\times12=21.6\text{ cm}^2$ for frequency 75. Therefore the target frequency 40 has area $$21.6\times\frac{40}{75}=11.52\text{ cm}^2.$$ Its height is $$\frac{11.52}{7.2}=\boxed{1.6\text{ cm}}.$$

Check. Densities are $75/5=15$ and $40/20=2$; the height ratio $2/15$ gives $12(2/15)=1.6$.

Strategy for the height. Height carries frequency once width is fixed, and the cleanest route is to preserve the area-to-frequency ratio that the reference bar defines. The reference bar has drawn area equal to its width times its height, that is 1.8 times 12, giving 21.6 square centimetres to represent a frequency of seventy-five, so one unit of frequency is worth 21.6 divided by seventy-five square centimetres of ink. Multiplying that by the target frequency of forty gives the target bar its correct area, and dividing that area by the drawn width already found returns the height. The intermediate logic is that we route through area deliberately because area is the quantity a histogram conserves; jumping straight to a height proportional to frequency would silently assume equal widths and is the classic wrong route to avoid here, since these two classes differ in width by a factor of four. An equivalent and faster professional method is frequency density, defined as frequency divided by class width: the reference density is seventy-five over five and the target density is forty over twenty, and heights are in the same ratio as densities, which is exactly what the dashed check confirms. Verify by noticing the target frequency is a little over half the reference frequency yet spread across four times the width, so its bar must be far shorter, consistent with the small value obtained. Present both the area line and the height line so partial credit is safe if one is mis-typed. Transfer cue: whenever unequal-width bars appear, convert to density immediately and compare densities, never raw frequencies.

Part (b)[2 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2Official-reference reconstruction

Strategy. Form cumulative frequencies and locate the median rank $300/2=150$.

Upper boundary115125130140150170
Cumulative frequency2870145208260300

The 150th value is in $130\leq x\lt140$, with lower boundary 130, previous cumulative frequency 145, class frequency 63 and width 10.

Why cumulative frequency first. Linear interpolation for a median assumes the values inside each class are spread evenly across that class, so before interpolating you must know which class the middle observation falls in, and that is precisely what a running cumulative total reveals. The definition being used is that the median is the value at rank n over two for grouped data under the interpolation convention, so with three hundred apples the target rank is one hundred and fifty. The intermediate logic is to add frequencies boundary by boundary: twenty-eight, then seventy, then one hundred and forty-five, then two hundred and eight, and the first cumulative total to reach or exceed one hundred and fifty tells you the class that contains the median. Here the total jumps from one hundred and forty-five at 130 to two hundred and eight at 140, so the one-hundred-and-fiftieth value sits inside the class from 130 up to 140. A relevant wrong route is using rank n plus one over two, or reading the class as the one whose cumulative frequency first exceeds one hundred and fifty by a wide margin without checking the previous total; both misplace the starting boundary and shift the answer. Check the accumulation by confirming the final cumulative frequency equals the total sample size of three hundred, which it does. In the exam, tabulate the upper boundaries with their cumulative totals as shown, then circle the class you will interpolate within, because that circled class is the evidence an examiner needs. Transfer cue: any grouped median, quartile, or percentile begins with an accurate cumulative column and an explicit target rank.

STEP 2/2Official-reference reconstruction

Linear interpolation gives $$\tilde x=130+\frac{150-145}{63}\times10=130.79365\ldots$$ so $$\boxed{\tilde x=130.8\text{ g}}\quad\text{(1 d.p.)}.$$

Check. Only $5/63$ of the class is traversed, so the estimate should be just above 130, as obtained. The official scheme also accepts 130.9 if the $(n+1)/2$ convention is used.

The interpolation formula. Within the located class the estimate is the lower class boundary plus the fraction of the way the target rank lies into that class, multiplied by the class width. The lower boundary is 130, the rank still to be gained inside the class is one hundred and fifty minus the previous cumulative total of one hundred and forty-five, which is five, the class frequency is sixty-three, and the width from 130 to 140 is ten. So the added portion is five over sixty-three of ten grams. The intermediate logic is that this treats the sixty-three apples in the class as if they were spaced uniformly from 130 to 140, so reaching just five of them moves you only a tiny way in from the left edge. A relevant wrong route is dividing by the cumulative frequency instead of the class frequency, or using the whole class frequency in the numerator, both of which break the proportion; another slip is forgetting to multiply by the class width and leaving the answer as a bare fraction. Check by sense: five sixty-thirds is well under a tenth, so the median must land only a fraction of a gram above 130, exactly as the boxed value shows. Present the substitution with every symbol replaced by its number on one line before rounding, and state the one-decimal-place instruction was applied. Transfer cue: the same lower-boundary-plus-fraction-times-width template computes any quartile or percentile once you replace the target rank; only the rank changes, the machinery does not.

Part (c)(i)[2 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2Official-reference reconstruction

From $y=(w-200)/2$, rearrange to $w=2y+200$. First compute the coded mean: $$\bar y=\frac{\sum y}{300}=\frac{6000}{300}=20.$$

Why code the data. Coding replaces awkward raw weights near two hundred grams with small centred numbers, which makes the summary statistics easy to handle, and the rule for undoing it is a linear back-transformation. The definition to rearrange is y equals w minus two hundred all over two; solving for w gives w equals two y plus two hundred, and this exact relationship is what lets you convert any statistic of y back to the corresponding statistic of w. The intermediate step is to compute the mean of the coded values first, because the sum of y is given directly as six thousand and there are three hundred observations, so the coded mean is six thousand divided by three hundred. Keeping the coded world and the raw world clearly separated is the discipline that prevents errors; you finish everything in y before you translate. A relevant wrong route is trying to recover individual raw weights from the totals, which is impossible and unnecessary, or applying the back-transformation to the sum rather than to the mean. Check that the coded mean of twenty is a modest number consistent with weights a little below two hundred and forty grams once decoded, since two times twenty plus two hundred already hints at that. In the exam, state the rearranged formula w equals two y plus two hundred on its own line before substituting, so the linear coding is visible evidence. Transfer cue: whenever data are coded as y equals w minus a all over b, first find the statistic in y, then decode using w equals b y plus a for the mean and location measures.

STEP 2/2Official-reference reconstruction

Transform the mean linearly: $$\bar w=2\bar y+200=2(20)+200=\boxed{240\text{ g}}.$$

Check. Substitution into the coding gives $(240-200)/2=20$, recovering the coded mean.

Decoding the mean. The mean is a measure of location, so it responds to both the scale factor and the shift in the coding, and the back-transformation is applied directly to the coded mean. Using w equals two y plus two hundred, the mean of w equals two times the coded mean plus two hundred, that is two times twenty plus two hundred. The intermediate logic is that multiplication by two stretches the centre of the distribution and adding two hundred slides it back up to the real weight scale, which is why both operations reappear when decoding a mean. A relevant wrong route is to forget the shift and report only twice the coded mean, or to divide by two instead of multiplying, both of which come from mishandling the direction of the coding; remember that the coding divides to shrink, so decoding multiplies to grow. Check by reversing the process, substituting the decoded mean into the original coding formula and confirming it returns the coded mean of twenty, which the dashed note verifies exactly. In the exam, show the line mean of w equals two times twenty plus two hundred before writing the final grams value, and always attach the unit because a mean weight carries the same units as the data. Transfer cue: for any linear coding, a mean, median, or quartile decodes with the full formula multiply by the scale then add the shift, unlike spread measures which ignore the shift entirely, a distinction the next part relies on.

Part (c)(ii)[3 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2Official-reference reconstruction

Use the coded second moment before transforming: $$\operatorname{Var}(Y)=\frac{\sum y^2}{300}-\bar y^2=\frac{150000}{300}-20^2=500-400=100.$$

The shift 200 does not affect variance; the scale factor 2 is squared.

Variance of the coded data. Variance measures spread about the mean, so the strategy is to compute it entirely in the coded world using the mean-of-squares minus square-of-mean identity before decoding. The definition applied is that the variance of y equals the sum of y squared divided by n minus the coded mean squared, using the given sum of y squared as one hundred and fifty thousand over three hundred and subtracting twenty squared. The intermediate arithmetic gives five hundred minus four hundred, an intermediate value that must be non-negative for any genuine variance, which is a useful internal check right here. A relevant wrong route is to subtract the mean before squaring in the wrong order, or to divide the sum of squares by n minus one as if this were a sample estimate, when the syllabus convention divides by n; another slip is squaring the sum instead of summing the squares. The deeper reason the shift of two hundred is irrelevant is that adding a constant slides every value and its mean together, leaving deviations unchanged, whereas the scale factor two will stretch deviations and therefore reappear squared in the next step. Check that the computed coded variance is positive and of sensible magnitude relative to the coded mean of twenty. In the exam, write the identity symbolically, then substitute, then simplify on separate lines so each earns its mark. Transfer cue: always evaluate variance in coded units first, because the given sums are in coded units, and only decode as the final act.

STEP 2/2Official-reference reconstruction

Since $W=2Y+200$, $$\operatorname{Var}(W)=2^2\operatorname{Var}(Y)=4(100)=\boxed{400\text{ g}^2}.$$

Check. Variance is non-negative and carries squared units. Using $2\operatorname{Var}(Y)+200$ would incorrectly mix variance and location.

Decoding the variance. Because w equals two y plus two hundred, only the multiplicative factor affects spread, and the rule is that the variance of a scaled variable multiplies by the square of that factor. So the variance of w equals two squared times the variance of y, that is four times the coded variance of one hundred, and the additive two hundred contributes nothing at all. The intermediate logic worth internalising is the contrast with the previous part: the mean pulled in both operations, but the variance ignores the shift and squares the scale, because variance is built from squared deviations and constants cancel in a deviation. A relevant wrong route, flagged in the dashed note, is to imitate the mean and write twice the variance plus two hundred, which mixes a spread measure with a location shift and is dimensionally impossible; another error is multiplying by two rather than by two squared. Check the units and sign: a variance must be positive and must carry grams squared, so the final answer is stated in grams squared, not grams, and a quick sanity read is that the raw spread is four times the coded spread, consistent with data that were shrunk by two during coding. In the exam, write variance of w equals two squared times one hundred and then the value with squared units, making the scale-squared step explicit. Transfer cue: for any linear coding remember multiply-and-shift for means but scale-squared-only for variance and standard deviation, and take the square root at the very end if a standard deviation is wanted.
