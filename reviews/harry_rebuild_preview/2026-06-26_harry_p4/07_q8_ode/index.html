P4 Q8 — differential equation and capacity

Question

A container initially contains 6 litres of water.

Water starts to flow into the container.

The rate at which the water flows into the container is modelled by the differential equation \[\frac{dV}{dt}=12te^{-t}\] where \(V\) litres is the volume of water in the container, \(t\) minutes after water starts flowing into the container.

(a) Solve the differential equation and use the initial condition to find an equation for \(V\) in terms of \(t\). [5 marks]

Given that the capacity of the container is 20 litres,

(b) determine, according to the model, whether or not the container will ever become full, giving a reason for your answer. [1 mark]

Not covered in the lesson — official-reference reconstruction below.

Official/reference solution only[6 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3

Strategy and why it applies. The equation already gives \(dV/dt\) solely as a function of \(t\), so recover \(V\) by direct integration: \[V=\int12te^{-t}\,dt.\] This is not a linear first-order equation containing both \(V\) and \(dV/dt\), so an integrating factor would be unnecessary and outside the intended route. Use the formula-booklet integration-by-parts identity \[\int u\,dv=uv-\int v\,du.\] Choose \(u=t\), so \(du=dt\), and \(dv=e^{-t}dt\), so \(v=-e^{-t}\). Then \[\int te^{-t}dt=-te^{-t}+\int e^{-t}dt=-te^{-t}-e^{-t}+C=-(t+1)e^{-t}+C.\] Multiplying by \(12\) gives \[V=-12(t+1)e^{-t}+C.\]

Source-grounded diagnosis. Phuc only self-assessed that he had forgotten how to solve Q8; no live attempt was observed. The card therefore explains the route without attributing a particular sign error or method to him. A neutral trap is to write \(\int e^{-t}dt=e^{-t}\); differentiating shows the required antiderivative is \(-e^{-t}\). Another is to use integrating factors automatically whenever “differential equation” appears, even though \(V\) is absent from the right-hand side.

Check of the antiderivative. Differentiate \(-12(t+1)e^{-t}\): product rule gives \(-12[e^{-t}-(t+1)e^{-t}]=12te^{-t}\), exactly the stated rate. This derivative check resolves both signs before applying the initial condition.

STEP 2/3

Apply the initial condition and simplify. Substitute \(t=0\) and \(V(0)=6\) into \(V=-12(t+1)e^{-t}+C\): \[6=-12(1)e^0+C=-12+C,\] so \(C=18\). Therefore \[V=18-12(t+1)e^{-t}=\boxed{18-12te^{-t}-12e^{-t}}.\] Both displayed forms are algebraically equivalent; the expanded form matches the official reference answer.

Domain and monotonicity logic. Time satisfies \(t\ge0\). The rate is \[\frac{dV}{dt}=12te^{-t}.\] For \(t\ge0\), both \(t\) and \(e^{-t}\) are nonnegative, so \(dV/dt\ge0\), with equality only at \(t=0\). Thus the volume is increasing after the start. This matters for the capacity decision: if an increasing function has a limiting value below capacity, it can never cross that capacity.

Check-back. At \(t=0\), the formula gives \(18-0-12=6\), satisfying the initial condition. Differentiating the expanded expression gives \(-12(e^{-t}-te^{-t})+12e^{-t}=12te^{-t}\), recovering the original equation. Units are volume units for \(V\); the exponential exponent \(-t\) is dimensionless under the model’s time scaling.

Why a raw equation solve is not yet enough. Trying to solve \(V=20\) numerically before understanding monotonicity and the limit wastes effort and can hide that no real nonnegative-time solution exists. Establish the global behaviour first.

STEP 3/3

Capacity conclusion, limit, and complete reasoning. As \(t\to\infty\), the standard exponential-dominance limits are \(e^{-t}\to0\) and \(te^{-t}\to0\). Therefore \[\lim_{t\to\infty}V(t)=18-12\lim te^{-t}-12\lim e^{-t}=18.\] Since \(V\) starts at \(6\), is increasing for \(t>0\), and approaches \(18\) from below, it never reaches \(20\). Algebraically, the limiting level is already below capacity, \(18<20\), and the finite-time inequality below proves that \(V(t)\) never reaches even \(18\), let alone \(20\).

Direct inequality check. For \(t\ge0\), \((t+1)e^{-t}>0\), so \[V(t)=18-12(t+1)e^{-t}<18<20.\] Thus every finite nonnegative time gives a volume strictly below capacity, while \(18\) itself is only a limiting value.

Transfer trigger and exam presentation. When \(dV/dt\) is a known function of time alone, integrate directly and use the initial condition. If the integrand is polynomial times exponential, try integration by parts and verify by differentiation. For a “will capacity be reached?” question, examine derivative sign and long-term limit before attempting numerical root solving. State all three pieces: increasing behaviour, limiting value, and comparison with capacity.
