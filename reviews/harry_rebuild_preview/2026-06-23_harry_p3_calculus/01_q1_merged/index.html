WMA13 October 2025 Q1 — functions

Question

In this question you must show all stages of your working.

Solutions relying entirely on calculator technology are not acceptable.

The functions $f$ and $g$ are defined by

$$f(x)=\ln(x^2+3),\quad x\in\mathbb R$$

$$g(x)=\frac{3+5x}{x+2},\quad x\in\mathbb R,\ x>-2$$

(a) State the range of $f$.   (1)

(b) Find $g^{-1}$.   (3)

(c) Find $fg(0)$.   (2)

(d) Find the exact value of $a$ for which $g(e^{2a})=f\!\left(\sqrt{e^4-3}\right)$.   (3)

[9 marks: (a)1, (b)3, (c)2, (d)3]

Part (a)[1 mark] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2B1

Strategy. Range means outputs. Since $x^2\ge0$,

$$x^2+3\ge3.$$

The logarithm is strictly increasing, so applying $\ln$ preserves the inequality:

$$f(x)=\ln(x^2+3)\ge\ln3.$$

Common trap. Solving $x^2+3>0$ checks the domain, which was already given. The lesson error $f(x)\in\mathbb R$ therefore answered a different question.

Why this route. A range question asks which outputs are actually attainable, so we chase the smallest possible input to the logarithm rather than manipulating $x$ directly. The controlling object here is the inner expression $x^2+3$: everything the outer $\ln$ can produce is decided by how small and how large $x^2+3$ can be. That reframing turns a function-range problem into a simple bounding problem on a quadratic-in-disguise.

Definition in play. For a real function the range is the set $\{f(x):x\in\text{domain}\}$. Because $\ln$ is defined only for strictly positive arguments, we also silently confirm $x^2+3\ge3>0$, so no domain point is ever lost. The monotonic increase of $\ln$ means order is preserved: a smallest input maps to a smallest output.

Intermediate logic. Since squares are non-negative, $x^2\ge0$ gives $x^2+3\ge3$; feeding the least value $3$ through the increasing map $\ln$ produces the least output $\ln 3$. There is no cancellation or hidden restriction to trip over.

Presentation. In the exam, write the chain $x^2\ge0\Rightarrow x^2+3\ge3\Rightarrow \ln(x^2+3)\ge\ln 3$ on one line so the single B1 mark is unambiguous. Transfer cue: for any $\ln(\text{quadratic})$ or $e^{\text{quadratic}}$, bound the inside first, then push the bound through the outer monotone function.

STEP 2/2

The endpoint is included because $f(0)=\ln3$. Also $f(x)\to\infty$ as $|x|\to\infty$, so there is no upper bound.

Range: $\boxed{[\ln3,\infty)}$, equivalently $\boxed{f(x)\ge\ln3}$.
Sense check. The minimum occurs at $x=0$, exactly where $x^2$ is smallest.

Why an endpoint check matters. Bounding $x^2+3\ge3$ shows the outputs never fall below $\ln 3$, but a range statement must also say whether that floor is reached and whether there is a ceiling. Missing either turns a correct bound into an incomplete answer.

Attainment argument. The value $x=0$ is a legitimate domain point, and it hands back exactly $f(0)=\ln 3$, so the lower value is achieved, not merely approached; that is why the interval is closed on the left. For the top end, as $|x|\to\infty$ the inside $x^2+3$ grows without bound and $\ln$ of an unbounded increasing quantity also grows without bound, so no finite upper value exists.

Intermediate logic. Closed-below plus unbounded-above forces the half-open interval $[\ln 3,\infty)$; a common slip is writing $(\ln 3,\infty)$, which wrongly excludes the achieved minimum.

Presentation. State “minimum attained at $x=0$, unbounded above” alongside the interval so an examiner sees both facts. Transfer cue: whenever you quote a range, test the candidate endpoint by substitution and describe the behaviour at the domain extremes before committing to open or closed brackets.

Part (b)[3 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3M1

Let $y=g(x)$ and clear the denominator:

$$y=\frac{5x+3}{x+2}$$

$$y(x+2)=5x+3$$

$$xy+2y=5x+3.$$

Strategy. To invert a Möbius-type fraction $\dfrac{5x+3}{x+2}$ we do not try to “flip” it; instead we set the output equal to $y$, clear the fraction, and rearrange to make $x$ the subject. Clearing the denominator first removes the fraction bar, which is where most sign errors hide.

Definition in play. $g^{-1}$ is the rule that undoes $g$: if $y=g(x)$ then $x=g^{-1}(y)$. So the algebra of “solving for $x$ in terms of $y$” literally constructs the inverse.

Intermediate logic. Multiplying both sides of $y=\dfrac{5x+3}{x+2}$ by $(x+2)$ is valid because the domain guarantees $x+2>0$, never zero. Expanding gives $xy+2y=5x+3$, which is now linear in $x$ — exactly the form we can rearrange. Keeping every term on the correct side here is what earns the first method mark.

Presentation. Write $y(x+2)=5x+3$ as its own line before expanding, so the multiplication step is visible for M1. Transfer cue: any inverse of $\dfrac{ax+b}{cx+d}$ starts with the identical “let $y=\dots$, times the denominator” move; memorise the opening, not the final formula.

Extra care. Note the printed numerator $3+5x$ equals $5x+3$; reorder it before matching to the standard $\dfrac{ax+b}{cx+d}$ template so coefficients are read correctly.

STEP 2/3A1

Collect the $x$-terms and factor:

$$xy-5x=3-2y$$

$$x(y-5)=3-2y$$

$$x=\frac{3-2y}{y-5}.$$

Rename the inverse input:

$\displaystyle g^{-1}(x)=\frac{3-2x}{x-5}$.

Strategy. With $xy+2y=5x+3$ linear in $x$, the goal is to gather every $x$ on one side and everything else on the other, then divide by the bracket that multiplies $x$. This is the standard “collect–factor–divide” finish for a rational inverse.

Intermediate logic. Moving $5x$ left and $2y$ right gives $xy-5x=3-2y$; factoring the common $x$ produces $x(y-5)=3-2y$. Division by $(y-5)$ is legitimate provided $y\ne5$, which foreshadows the domain restriction handled in the next step, so nothing is lost by dividing now.

Why rename. After solving, the expression is $x$ in terms of $y$; swapping the letter to $x$ merely re-labels the independent variable so the inverse is written in the conventional $g^{-1}(x)$ form. The mathematics is unchanged by the rename — only the presentation convention is.

Presentation. Show the factored line $x(y-5)=3-2y$ explicitly; that single line typically carries the A1. Transfer cue: whenever a variable appears in two places, collecting-and-factoring is the reflex that converts a scattered equation into a clean quotient.

Sign vigilance. The factor $(y-5)$ carries a negative constant, so a careless swap to $(5-y)$ would flip the whole quotient's sign; keep the subtraction in the order produced by collecting the $x$-terms. A quick mental substitution of one convenient $y$ confirms the arrangement before you commit the boxed rule to paper.

STEP 3/3B1

The inverse domain is the original range. Rewrite

$$g(x)=5-\frac7{x+2}.$$

Because $x>-2$, we have $x+2>0$, hence $7/(x+2)>0$ and therefore $g(x)<5$. As $x\to-2^+$, $g(x)\to-\infty$; as $x\to\infty$, $g(x)\to5^-$. Thus

$\boxed{g^{-1}(x)=\dfrac{3-2x}{x-5},\quad x<5}$.
Sense check. $g(0)=3/2$ and $g^{-1}(3/2)=0$.

Why compute the domain at all. An inverse is only fully specified once its domain is stated, and the domain of $g^{-1}$ equals the range of $g$. So the real task is to find every value $g$ can output on $x>-2$.

Formula insight. Rewriting $g(x)=5-\dfrac{7}{x+2}$ by polynomial-style division exposes the horizontal asymptote $y=5$ and the single vertical asymptote at $x=-2$; this partial-fraction-like form makes the behaviour transparent in a way the original quotient hides.

Intermediate logic. Because $x>-2$ forces $x+2>0$, the term $7/(x+2)$ is strictly positive, so $g(x)<5$; letting $x\to-2^+$ sends $g$ to $-\infty$ and $x\to\infty$ sends it up towards $5$. Hence the range is $(-\infty,5)$, giving inverse domain $x<5$.

Presentation. Quote the rewritten form and the limit behaviour, then state the domain beside the boxed rule. Transfer cue: convert any $\dfrac{ax+b}{cx+d}$ into “constant $\pm$ constant$/(x+d)$” to read off asymptotes and hence the range in seconds.

Part (c)[2 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2M1

Composition is read right to left:

$$fg(0)=f(g(0)).$$

First,

$$g(0)=\frac{5(0)+3}{0+2}=\frac32.$$

Order of operations for composites. The notation $fg(0)$ is read from the inside out: apply $g$ to $0$ first, then apply $f$ to the number that results. Reversing the order and computing $g(f(0))$ is the single most common misread and produces a completely different value.

Definition in play. $fg$ means $f\circ g$, the function $x\mapsto f(g(x))$; the letter nearest the input acts first. Writing $fg(0)=f(g(0))$ explicitly records that reading and secures the method mark before any arithmetic.

Intermediate logic. Substituting $x=0$ into $g(x)=\dfrac{5x+3}{x+2}$ gives $\dfrac{0+3}{0+2}=\dfrac32$; the domain condition $x>-2$ is satisfied at $x=0$, so the inner evaluation is legitimate and $3/2$ is a valid input for $f$.

Presentation. Show $fg(0)=f(g(0))$ and the value $g(0)=3/2$ on separate lines so the two marks are cleanly separable. Transfer cue: for any composite value, always evaluate the inner function to a single number and confirm it lies in the outer function’s domain before proceeding.

Reinforcement. Reading right-to-left is unforgiving: the symbol physically closest to the argument $0$ is $g$, so $g$ fires first, then $f$ consumes its output. Building this habit now prevents the reversed-composite error recurring in part (d).

STEP 2/2A1

Now substitute the whole result into $f$:

$$f\!\left(\frac32\right)=\ln\!\left[\left(\frac32\right)^2+3\right]$$

$$=\ln\!\left(\frac94+\frac{12}4\right)=\ln\!\left(\frac{21}4\right).$$

$\boxed{fg(0)=\ln(21/4)}$.
Sense check. $21/4>0$, so the logarithm is defined.

Completing the composite. With the inner value $3/2$ fixed, the second stage is a direct substitution into $f(x)=\ln(x^2+3)$; the only real work is the fraction arithmetic inside the logarithm, so accuracy there earns the accuracy mark.

Intermediate logic. Squaring gives $(3/2)^2=9/4$, and adding $3$ requires a common denominator: $3=12/4$, so $9/4+12/4=21/4$. Keeping everything as quarters avoids a decimal that would then need converting back to an exact log.

Why leave it as a logarithm. The command word “find” with an exact context expects $\ln(21/4)$, not a rounded decimal; converting to $1.658\ldots$ would discard the exact form the mark scheme rewards. Because $21/4>0$, the logarithm is defined, so no domain issue arises.

Presentation. Write the substitution $f(3/2)=\ln[(3/2)^2+3]$ before simplifying, then the single tidy surd-free fraction. Transfer cue: when a composite ends in $\ln$ or $e$, finish in exact form and only decimalise if the question explicitly asks for a numerical approximation.

Part (d)[3 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3M1

Not covered in the lesson — official-reference reconstruction below.

Evaluate the right-hand function first:

$$f\!\left(\sqrt{e^4-3}\right)=\ln\!\left[\left(\sqrt{e^4-3}\right)^2+3\right]$$

$$=\ln(e^4-3+3)=\ln(e^4)=4.$$

Common trap. Function notation requires substituting each entire bracketed input into its rule; the two inputs must not be set equal.

Strategy. The equation $g(e^{2a})=f(\sqrt{e^4-3})$ links two different functions, so the clean plan is to collapse the fully-numerical right side to a single constant first; only then does the left side become a solvable one-variable equation.

Definition in play. Substituting into $f(x)=\ln(x^2+3)$ means replacing the whole bracketed input for $x$. The square and the square root are inverse operations: $(\sqrt{e^4-3})^2=e^4-3$ exactly, with no rounding, because the argument is non-negative.

Intermediate logic. Then $e^4-3+3=e^4$, and $\ln(e^4)=4$ since $\ln$ and $e^{(\cdot)}$ undo each other. So the entire right side is simply the number $4$, which is far easier to handle than its original surd form.

Presentation. Evaluate the right side to $4$ as a standalone line before touching the left side, so the method is visibly staged. Transfer cue: in mixed-function equations, always simplify any side that is purely numerical to one constant first — it converts the problem into a routine equation in the remaining unknown.

STEP 2/3dM1

Hence $g(e^{2a})=4$. Substitute $e^{2a}$ for every $x$ in $g$:

$$\frac{5e^{2a}+3}{e^{2a}+2}=4.$$

Cross-multiply:

$$5e^{2a}+3=4e^{2a}+8$$

$$e^{2a}=5.$$

Why substitute a block. Setting $g(e^{2a})=4$ treats $e^{2a}$ as a single quantity dropped into every $x$ slot of $g$. Naming it mentally as one object prevents the error of trying to “bring down” the exponent too early.

Intermediate logic. The equation $\dfrac{5e^{2a}+3}{e^{2a}+2}=4$ is cleared by cross-multiplication, valid because $e^{2a}+2>0$ for all real $a$. Expanding gives $5e^{2a}+3=4e^{2a}+8$; subtracting $4e^{2a}$ and $3$ isolates $e^{2a}=5$. The exponential term behaves exactly like a single algebraic unknown throughout.

Diagnosis of a tempting slip. Do not divide numerator and denominator by $e^{2a}$ hoping to simplify; that introduces fractions in $e^{-2a}$ and obscures the linear structure. Cross-multiplying keeps the equation linear in the block $e^{2a}$.

Presentation. Show the cleared line $5e^{2a}+3=4e^{2a}+8$ so the collect-and-simplify move is explicit for the dependent method mark. Transfer cue: treat $e^{kx}$, $a^x$, or $\ln x$ as one symbol while the equation is still linear in it, and only unwind with logs once it is isolated.

Validity note. Cross-multiplication is safe only when the denominator cannot be zero; here $e^{2a}+2\ge2>0$ for every real $a$, so no root is introduced or lost by clearing the fraction.

STEP 3/3A1

Take natural logarithms:

$$\ln(e^{2a})=\ln5$$

$$2a=\ln5$$

$\boxed{a=\frac12\ln5}$.
Check-back. If $e^{2a}=5$, then $g(5)=(25+3)/7=4$, matching the right side.

Finishing move. Once $e^{2a}=5$, the exponent is freed by applying the natural logarithm to both sides — the inverse of $e^{(\cdot)}$ — which is the only operation that pulls $2a$ down cleanly.

Definition in play. The log law $\ln(e^{k})=k$ gives $\ln(e^{2a})=2a$ directly, so $2a=\ln 5$ and $a=\tfrac12\ln 5$. Because the question demands an exact value, this surd-free logarithmic form is the required answer rather than a decimal.

Intermediate logic. Dividing by $2$ is the last algebraic step; equivalently $a=\ln\sqrt5$ by the power law $\tfrac12\ln5=\ln5^{1/2}$, a neat alternative form worth recognising if a mark scheme prints it differently.

Presentation. Display $\ln(e^{2a})=\ln5$, then $2a=\ln5$, then the boxed value, so each logarithmic step is separately visible. Transfer cue: to solve $e^{\text{(linear)}}=c$, take $\ln$ of both sides immediately, and remember $\tfrac12\ln c=\ln\sqrt c$ when matching alternative exact forms.

Alternative form. Because $\tfrac12\ln5=\ln5^{1/2}=\ln\sqrt5$, an equivalent acceptable exact answer is $a=\ln\sqrt5$; recognising both spellings avoids second-guessing when a printed scheme differs cosmetically from your line. A decimal such as $0.805$ would be marked down here because the command demanded an exact value.
