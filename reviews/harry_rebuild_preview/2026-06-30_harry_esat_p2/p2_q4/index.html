P2 January 2025 Q4 — geometric series

Question

In this question you must show all stages of your working. Solutions relying entirely on calculator technology are not acceptable.

Given that, in a particular geometric series,

  • the sum of the first three terms is $70.2$
  • the sum to infinity is $75$

find, for this series,

(a) the common ratio, (4)

(b) the first term. (2)

Status: completed with substantial prompting. The preferred lesson route substituted the known sum to infinity into the finite-sum formula.

[6 marks: (a) 4, (b) 2]

Part (a)[4 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1

Strategy. Exploit the shared factor $a/(1-r)$ between the finite and infinite geometric-sum formulae. Substitute the known infinite sum directly into $S_3$ so $a$ cancels immediately, then solve the exact cubic for $r$.

Why this method applies. For convergence, $|r|<1$, and the supplied $S_\infty$ equals $a/(1-r)$. The finite sum can be written $S_3=[a/(1-r)](1-r^3)$, so the known 75 is already the entire bracketed factor. This is simpler than separately solving for $a$ first.

Formula or definition first. Use $S_3=\frac{a(1-r^3)}{1-r}$ and $S_\infty=\frac a{1-r}=75$. Therefore $70.2=75(1-r^3)$.

Full intermediate derivation. Divide by 75: $1-r^3=70.2/75=702/750=117/125$. Hence $r^3=1-117/125=8/125$. Taking the real cube root gives $r=\sqrt[3]{8/125}=\boxed{2/5}$.

Difficult transition. The key transition is reading 70.2 as $S_3$, the sum of three terms, not as the third term $ar^2$. Also, $r^3=8/125$ has the unique real cube root $2/5$; writing $\pm2/5$ would import a square-root rule that does not apply.

Evidence-grounded diagnosis / why the rejected route fails. The recording shows that 70.2 was initially treated as $ar^2$ and that a difference-of-cubes derivation became cumbersome before the tutor pivoted to substitution. The rejected route fails strategically because it obscures the supplied $S_\infty$ block and increases algebra without adding information.

Check back / sense check. The result obeys $|r|<1$, so an infinite sum is possible. Substitution gives $1-(2/5)^3=1-8/125=117/125$, and $75(117/125)=3(117)/5=70.2$, exactly recovering the given finite sum.

Exam trigger / transfer trigger. When both $S_n$ and $S_\infty$ are supplied, look for $S_n=S_\infty(1-r^n)$. This eliminates the first term in one line. Keep ‘sum of first $n$ terms’ distinct from ‘$n$th term’ before selecting a formula.

Part (b)[2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in: From part (a): $r=2/5$; the source gives $S_\infty=75$.
STEP 1/1

Strategy. Back-substitute the common ratio into the sum-to-infinity relation and solve directly for the first term. Use the convergence condition and the original $S_3$ as independent checks.

Why this method applies. Once $r$ is known, $S_\infty=a/(1-r)$ is linear in $a$. The infinite sum is positive and $1-r=3/5$ is positive, so the resulting first term should also be positive.

Formula or definition first. From $S_\infty=\frac a{1-r}=75$, rearrange to $a=75(1-r)$. Carry in $r=2/5$.

Full intermediate derivation. Substitute: $a=75(1-2/5)=75(3/5)=15\cdot3=\boxed{45}$. The first three terms are then $45$, $18$ and $7.2$, whose sum is $45+18+7.2=70.2$.

Difficult transition. The difficult transition is maintaining the bracket $1-r$. Writing $a=75/(1-r)$ would invert the rearrangement and produce 125, contradicting the supplied finite sum. Multiplying both sides of $a/(1-r)=75$ by $1-r$ keeps the algebra transparent.

Evidence-grounded diagnosis / why the rejected route fails. The lesson reached $a=45$ with prompting; it did not support a separate learner error in this part. A neutral common trap is using $S_3=70.2$ as $a$ or as the third term instead of carrying the solved ratio into $S_\infty$.

Check back / sense check. Check both conditions: $45/(1-2/5)=45/(3/5)=75$, and $45[1-(2/5)^3]/(1-2/5)=45(117/125)(5/3)=70.2$. Both source totals are reproduced exactly.

Exam trigger / transfer trigger. After solving a geometric ratio, recover $a$ from whichever supplied sum gives the shortest linear substitution. Then reconstruct a few actual terms to test both the ratio and any finite-sum statement.
