P2 January 2025 Q1 — arithmetic series

Question

The arithmetic series $S$ is given by

$$S=2+5+8+11+\ldots+254$$

Find

(a) the number of terms in the series, (2)

(b) the sum of the series. (2)

Status: lesson evidence says the attempt was correct before the lesson and no method was retaught. The derivation shown is official answer-check study, not reconstructed tutor narration.

[4 marks: (a) 2, (b) 2]

Part (a)[2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1

Strategy. Identify the first term, common difference and final term, then use the arithmetic-sequence term formula to count the number of equal jumps. Preserve the distinction between the number of gaps and the number of terms, because that is where the off-by-one error occurs.

Why this method applies. An arithmetic sequence changes by a constant difference. Starting at term 1, reaching term $n$ requires $n-1$ jumps, so the final term equals the first term plus $(n-1)$ copies of the common difference. The printed terms show $a=2$, $d=3$ and $u_n=254$.

Formula or definition first. Use $u_n=a+(n-1)d$. Substitution gives $254=2+3(n-1)$, with $n$ required to be a positive integer because it counts terms.

Full intermediate derivation. Subtract 2: $252=3(n-1)$. Divide by 3: $84=n-1$. Add 1: $n=85$. Therefore the series contains $\boxed{85}$ terms. The intermediate $84$ counts the intervals from 2 to 254, not the entries themselves.

Difficult transition. The difficult transition is interpreting $n-1$. Listing a short analogue, $2,5,8$, shows two jumps but three terms. The same structure scales to 84 jumps and 85 terms. Writing $n=(254-2)/3$ without the final $+1$ loses the first term.

Evidence-grounded diagnosis / why the rejected route fails. The guide records this question as pre-lesson correct and says no method was retaught. It would be fabrication to claim an observed arithmetic-series mistake. The only diagnosis stated here is a neutral exam trap—confusing gaps with terms—not a learner attribution.

Check back / sense check. Substitute the answer into the term formula: $u_{85}=2+84(3)=2+252=254$, exactly the printed last term. The integer result is also necessary; a non-integer count would show that the copied first term, difference or last term was wrong.

Exam trigger / transfer trigger. When a sequence prints a first and last term, count jumps with $(l-a)/d$ and then add one, or use $a+(n-1)d=l$ directly. Always back-substitute the resulting $n$ into the final-term formula before moving to a sum.

Part (b)[2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in: From part (a): $n=85$; the first term is $2$ and the last term is $254$.
STEP 1/1

Strategy. Use the term count from part (a) and the endpoint form of the arithmetic-series sum. Pairing the first and last terms makes every pair total 256, so the formula captures the same symmetry without listing all 85 terms.

Why this method applies. The series has a constant difference, and both endpoints are known. The arithmetic-sum identity $S_n=\frac n2(a+l)$ is therefore more direct than $\frac n2[2a+(n-1)d]$, although both are equivalent. Part (b) depends on the correct $n=85$ carry-in.

Formula or definition first. Use $S_n=\frac n2(a+l)$. Here $n=85$, $a=2$ and $l=254$, so $S=\frac{85}{2}(2+254)$.

Full intermediate derivation. Add the endpoints first: $2+254=256$. Then $\frac{85}{2}\times256=85\times128$. Calculate $128\times80=10240$ and $128\times5=640$, giving $10240+640=10880$. Hence $S=\boxed{10\,880}$.

Difficult transition. An odd number of terms does not invalidate the pairing formula. Eighty-four terms form 42 endpoint pairs and the middle term is 128; equivalently, $42(256)+128=10752+128=10880$. The factor $85/2$ already accounts for that middle term.

Evidence-grounded diagnosis / why the rejected route fails. No summation error is evidenced in the lesson because the work was accepted as correct. A neutral rejected route would use a geometric sum despite the constant difference; geometric formulae model constant ratios and would not preserve the printed progression $+3$.

Check back / sense check. The average of first and last is $(2+254)/2=128$. Multiplying the average by 85 terms gives $10880$, which independently matches the formula. The scale is sensible: 85 values averaging around 128 should sum to a little over ten thousand.

Exam trigger / transfer trigger. For arithmetic-series totals, think ‘number of terms × mean of endpoints’. Carry the exact count from the previous part, verify whether the pattern is additive rather than multiplicative, and use the alternative pairing check when $n$ is odd.
