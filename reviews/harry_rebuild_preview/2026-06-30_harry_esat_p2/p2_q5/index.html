P2 January 2025 Q5 — factor and remainder theorems

Question

$f(x)=3x^3+ax^2-10x+b$, where $a$ and $b$ are constants.

Given that $(3x-4)$ is a factor of $f(x)$,

(a) show that $16a+9b=56$. (2)

Given further that when $f(x)$ is divided by $(x-2)$ the remainder is $b$,

(b) find the value of $a$ and the value of $b$. (4)

(c) Hence, using algebra, fully factorise $f(x)$. (3)

Status: completed with prompting. The lesson used the theorem contrast and corrected a sign slip in algebraic division.

[9 marks: (a) 2, (b) 4, (c) 3]

Part (a)[2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1

Strategy. Convert the non-monic factor $(3x-4)$ into its root $x=4/3$, apply the Factor Theorem, then clear denominators carefully to reach the required integer relation.

Why this method applies. If a linear expression is a factor, the polynomial is zero where that expression is zero. The root is found by solving $3x-4=0$, not by reading off $4$ or $-4/3$. Substitution produces one linear equation in $a$ and $b$.

Formula or definition first. Factor Theorem: if $(x-\alpha)$ divides $f(x)$, then $f(\alpha)=0$. Here $3x-4=0$ gives $\alpha=4/3$, so $f(4/3)=0$.

Full intermediate derivation. Substitute: $3(4/3)^3+a(4/3)^2-10(4/3)+b=0$. This is $64/9+16a/9-40/3+b=0$. Convert $-40/3=-120/9$, giving $16a/9+b-56/9=0$. Multiply by 9: $16a+9b-56=0$, hence $\boxed{16a+9b=56}$.

Difficult transition. Clearing denominators must multiply every term, including $b$, by 9. Skipping that multiplication would produce $16a+b=56$, which is not equivalent. Combining the pure-number fractions first isolates the parameter terms cleanly.

Evidence-grounded diagnosis / why the rejected route fails. The lesson was originally blank and the word ‘factor’ became the recovery trigger. That supports the theorem diagnosis. It does not support claiming the relation was independently derived before prompting.

Check back / sense check. Insert the later official values $a=-1$, $b=8$: $16(-1)+9(8)=-16+72=56$, matching the shown relation. The root check also gives $f(4/3)=0$.

Exam trigger / transfer trigger. For a non-monic linear factor $px-q$, first solve $px-q=0$ and write the exact root. Substitute using fractions, group number terms, then clear a common denominator across the entire equation.

Part (b)[4 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in: From part (a): $16a+9b=56$; the stated remainder on division by $(x-2)$ is the symbol $b$.
STEP 1/1

Strategy. Translate the stated remainder literally: division by $(x-2)$ leaves the symbolic remainder $b$, so the Remainder Theorem gives $f(2)=b$. Solve that equation for $a$, then carry it into the relation from part (a) to find $b$.

Why this method applies. The Remainder Theorem says the remainder on division by $(x-c)$ is $f(c)$. Here the source names that remainder $b$; it does not yet give the number 8. Treating $b$ as a known numerical remainder would assume the result that must be derived.

Formula or definition first. Use $f(2)=b$. With $f(x)=3x^3+ax^2-10x+b$, this becomes $3(2)^3+a(2)^2-10(2)+b=b$.

Full intermediate derivation. Expand: $24+4a-20+b=b$. Cancel $b$ from both sides: $4+4a=0$, so $a=-1$. Substitute into $16a+9b=56$: $-16+9b=56$, hence $9b=72$ and $b=8$. Therefore $\boxed{a=-1,\ b=8}$.

Difficult transition. The cancellation of $b$ is a feature, not evidence that the remainder is zero. Both sides contain the same symbolic remainder. After cancellation, the independent factor relation supplies the second equation needed for $b$.

Evidence-grounded diagnosis / why the rejected route fails. The lesson explicitly contrasted Factor and Remainder Theorems and warned that the wording is ‘remainder $=b$’, not ‘remainder $=8$’. Assuming 8 early is circular and removes the purpose of part (b).

Check back / sense check. Check the remainder with the recovered polynomial: $f(2)=24-4-20+8=8=b$. Check the factor relation: $16(-1)+9(8)=56$. Both independent source conditions hold.

Exam trigger / transfer trigger. When a remainder is a variable already present in the polynomial, preserve it symbolically. Write $f(c)=\text{stated remainder}$ before substituting, simplify, and combine the resulting equation with any factor condition.

Part (c)[3 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in: From part (b): $a=-1$ and $b=8$, so $f(x)=3x^3-x^2-10x+8$; $(3x-4)$ is already known to be a factor.
STEP 1/1

Strategy. Insert the solved constants, divide the cubic by the known factor $(3x-4)$ using algebra, then factor the quadratic quotient. Show the intermediate quotient so ‘using algebra’ and ‘fully factorise’ are both satisfied.

Why this method applies. The known factor guarantees exact division. Once $a=-1$ and $b=8$, the cubic is $3x^3-x^2-10x+8$. Removing the given linear factor reduces the remaining problem to a quadratic that can be factorised by product and sum.

Formula or definition first. Write $f(x)=3x^3-x^2-10x+8$. Algebraic division by $3x-4$ gives quotient $x^2+x-2$, because $(3x-4)(x^2+x-2)$ reproduces the cubic.

Full intermediate derivation. Expand to verify the quotient: $3x(x^2+x-2)-4(x^2+x-2)=3x^3+3x^2-6x-4x^2-4x+8=3x^3-x^2-10x+8$. Then $x^2+x-2=(x+2)(x-1)$. Hence $\boxed{f(x)=(3x-4)(x+2)(x-1)}$.

Difficult transition. In long division, subtract each entire product row using brackets. For example, $(-x^2)-(-4x^2)=3x^2$. Dropping the brackets turns subtraction of a negative into addition incorrectly and corrupts every later coefficient.

Evidence-grounded diagnosis / why the rejected route fails. The recording supports a sign slip in the division that was corrected. The clean derivation presents only the correct forward route; it diagnoses why unbracketed row subtraction fails without reproducing a contaminated self-correction narrative.

Check back / sense check. The factors give roots $4/3$, $-2$ and $1$. Direct substitution into $3x^3-x^2-10x+8$ gives zero at each. Their leading coefficients multiply to 3 and their constant terms multiply to $(-4)(2)(-1)=8$, matching the cubic.

Exam trigger / transfer trigger. For full factorisation after parameter recovery, substitute constants before dividing. Use the known factor, show the quotient, factor the quotient completely, and test leading and constant coefficients as quick expansion checks.
