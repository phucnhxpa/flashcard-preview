P2 January 2025 Q8 — counter-example and parity proof

Question

(i) A student states

“If $x$ and $y$ are irrational numbers, $x\ne y$, then $xy$ is also irrational.”

Show, by counter example, that this statement is not always true. (1)

(ii) Prove, using algebra, that for all odd integers $n$, the value of the expression $n^3+3n+2$ is always even but never a multiple of 4. (4)

Part (i)[1 mark] · 1 step

STEP 1/1

Strategy. Disprove the universal statement with one pair of distinct irrational numbers whose product is rational. Choose related surds so their irrational factors cancel exactly, and explicitly verify all three requirements: each factor irrational, factors unequal, product rational.

Why this method applies. A claim beginning ‘if ... then ...’ for all numbers is false as soon as one counter-example satisfies the hypothesis but not the conclusion. Random unequal irrationals often have an irrational product, so construct one factor as a rational multiple of the other.

Formula or definition first. Take $x=\sqrt2$ and $y=3\sqrt2$. Both are irrational because a nonzero rational multiple of $\sqrt2$ remains irrational, and $x\ne y$ because their positive coefficients differ.

Full intermediate derivation. Multiply: $xy=(\sqrt2)(3\sqrt2)=3(\sqrt2)^2=3\cdot2=6$. The product 6 is an integer and therefore rational. Thus $x=\sqrt2$, $y=3\sqrt2$ is a valid counter-example and the universal claim is false.

Difficult transition. The transition is not merely obtaining a rational product; the source hypothesis also demands $x\ne y$. The tempting pair $\sqrt2$ and $\sqrt2$ gives product 2 but fails that inequality. Rescaling one factor preserves irrationality while making the factors distinct.

Evidence-grounded diagnosis / why the rejected route fails. The lesson evidence shows rational examples and then $\sqrt2\cdot\sqrt5=\sqrt{10}$, which remains irrational. The tutor prompt to rescale one surd produced the valid route. A recurring decimal was also clarified: non-terminating notation alone does not prove irrationality.

Check back / sense check. Check each condition in order: $\sqrt2$ is irrational; $3\sqrt2$ is irrational; they are unequal; their product is rational. One complete counter-example is sufficient—no general classification of irrational products is required for one mark.

Exam trigger / transfer trigger. To construct a counter-example about products of irrationals, choose $\sqrt m$ and $q\sqrt m$ with rational $q\ne1$. Their product is $qm$, rational, while each factor remains irrational when $m$ is nonsquare.

Why this method applies. The form $2k+1$ covers every odd integer and creates a general proof rather than a list of examples. Showing the expression equals a multiple of four plus two simultaneously proves it is even and has remainder 2 modulo 4, so it can never be divisible by 4.

Formula or definition first. Live stopping point: $n=2k+1$, $k\in\mathbb Z$. Official-reference continuation: substitute into $n^3+3n+2$ to obtain $(2k+1)^3+3(2k+1)+2$.

Full intermediate derivation. Expand the cube fully: $(2k+1)^3=8k^3+12k^2+6k+1$. Add $6k+3+2$ to get $8k^3+12k^2+12k+6$. Factor: $8k^3+12k^2+12k+6=4(2k^3+3k^2+3k+1)+2$. The bracket is an integer, so the expression is $\boxed{2\pmod4}$: even, but never a multiple of 4.
