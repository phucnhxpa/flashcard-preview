WMA13/01 October 2025 Q2 — IVT and iteration

Question

Figure 1 shows a sketch of part of the curve with equation $y=f(x)$, where

$$f(x)=\frac{2x^2+3x-4}{e^x}-\frac{1}{x^2},\qquad x\in\mathbb R,\ x\ne0.$$

(a) Show that $f(x)=0$ has a root $\alpha$ in the interval $[1,2]$. (2)

(b) Show that the equation $f(x)=0$ can be written in the form $$x=\sqrt[3]{\frac{e^x+4x^2}{2x+3}}.$$ (2)

Using the iteration formula $$x_{n+1}=\sqrt[3]{\frac{e^{x_n}+4x_n^2}{2x_n+3}},\qquad x_1=1,$$ find, to 4 decimal places, (c)(i) the value of $x_3$, (ii) the value of $\alpha$. (3)

[7 marks: (a) 2, (b) 2, (c) 3]

Part (a)[2 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2

Strategy. Evaluate the function at the stated endpoints and explicitly establish continuity on the entire interval.

$$f(1)=\frac{1}{e}-1=-0.6321205588\ldots<0,$$ $$f(2)=\frac{10}{e^2}-\frac14=1.1033528324\ldots>0.$$

On $[1,2]$, neither $e^x$ nor $x^2$ is zero, so $f$ is continuous there. Since the endpoint values have opposite signs, the Intermediate Value Theorem gives at least one root $\alpha\in(1,2)$.

STEP 2/2

Separate answer diagram. This explanatory sign-change diagram belongs to the worked answer; the untouched official Figure 1 remains in the question block.

Check. The theorem proves existence, not uniqueness. The denominator check is what justifies the continuity statement.

Part (b)[2 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in: $f(x)=0$ with $x\ne0$.
STEP 1/1

Strategy. Clear both denominators, collect the terms needed for a factor of $x^3$, and only then take a real cube root.

$$\frac{2x^2+3x-4}{e^x}=\frac1{x^2}$$ $$x^2(2x^2+3x-4)=e^x$$ $$2x^4+3x^3-4x^2=e^x$$ $$x^3(2x+3)=e^x+4x^2$$ $$x^3=\frac{e^x+4x^2}{2x+3}.$$

Taking the real cube root gives

$\boxed{x=\sqrt[3]{\dfrac{e^x+4x^2}{2x+3}}}$.
Reverse check. Cubing and multiplying by $2x+3$ returns the cleared-denominator equation.

Part (c)[3 marks] · 2 steps

Carry-in: Printed recurrence $x_{n+1}=\sqrt[3]{(e^{x_n}+4x_n^2)/(2x_n+3)}$ with $x_1=1$.
STEP 1/2

Deferred in the lesson — official-reference continuation below.

Strategy. Define

$$\phi(x)=\sqrt[3]{\frac{e^x+4x^2}{2x+3}}$$

and repeatedly use $x_{n+1}=\phi(x_n)$, retaining the unrounded calculator value at every stage. Starting with $x_1=1$:

$$x_2=\phi(1)=1.103475608864715\ldots$$ $$x_3=\phi(x_2)=1.148358495932474\ldots$$

Therefore, to four decimal places,

(i) $\boxed{x_3=1.1484}$.
Rounding warning. $x_3$ requires exactly two substitutions after $x_1$. Rounding $x_2$ before the second substitution changes later digits and is not the specified iteration.

(ii) $\boxed{\alpha=1.1813}$.

$$f(1.1813)=-9.0176\times10^{-5},$$
