Question

Problem (ESAT style, not WME01). How many different integers \(n\) are there such that the difference between \(2\sqrt{n}\) and \(7\) is less than \(1\)? (Options: 0, 2, 4, 6, 8.)

No marks allocated — lesson warm-up

Lesson status: ESAT-STYLE WARM-UP — NOT WME01 · Source class: lesson warm-up (not part of WME01)

Unfold the modulus chainno marks allocated

Carry-in / given: “Difference less than 1” means the size of the gap is under 1.
STEP 1/2

Teaching visual — strict endpoints and integer count.

Definition and strategy. The “difference” between two real numbers is the non-negative size of their gap, so the condition is \(\lvert 2\sqrt{n}-7\rvert<1\). Start with this single modulus statement rather than guessing integers: \(\lvert X\rvert<1\) is equivalent to \(-1<X<1\). This route matters because it keeps both sides of the gap and preserves the strict endpoints.

Substitute \(X=2\sqrt n-7\): \(-1<2\sqrt n-7<1\). Add seven to every member to obtain \(6<2\sqrt n<8\), then divide every member by positive two: \(3<\sqrt n<4\). Division by a positive number leaves both inequality signs unchanged. All three quantities are now non-negative, so squaring is order-preserving and gives \(9<n<16\). This sign/domain check is essential: squaring an inequality without first knowing the relevant sides are non-negative is not generally reversible.

The integers strictly inside the interval are \(10,11,12,13,14,15\). Inclusive counting from the first permitted integer to the last gives \(15-10+1=6\); the “plus one” counts both 10 and 15.

The decisive transition is from a verbal distance condition to a reversible chain of inequalities. Because the square-root domain already forces \(n\geq0\), the isolated quantities \(3<\sqrt n<4\) are positive and may be squared without changing order or introducing a second branch. That justification distinguishes this route from squaring an expression such as \(-2<x<3\), where signs would require care. A useful check is to insert the excluded endpoint values into the original wording: \(2\sqrt9=6\) and \(2\sqrt{16}=8\), each exactly one from seven, so strict inequality rejects both. On a future integer-count question, keep the real interval and the discrete list as separate lines; the interval establishes admissibility, while the list establishes the count.

STEP 2/2
There are exactly \(6\) integers, so select the option \(6\).

Check-back. At \(n=10\), \(2\sqrt{10}\) is just above 6, so its gap from 7 is below 1; at \(n=15\), \(2\sqrt{15}\) is just below 8, also valid. At the boundary values \(n=9\) and \(n=16\), the gap is exactly 1, not less than 1, so both are excluded.

Diagnosis and exam trigger. The lesson evidence records over-complication before the clean modulus chain and hesitation over division by two. An alternative rearrangement that re-introduces root terms was rejected because it creates more expressions without narrowing the interval. When a question says “difference less than” and asks for an integer count, trigger: modulus, double inequality, isolate the root, verify non-negative sides, square, then count integers with endpoint symbols visible.

The option is fixed by the actual set \(\{10,11,12,13,14,15\}\), not by subtracting the endpoints. The interval width is seven, but there are six admissible integer lattice points because neither boundary belongs to the set. This is the difficult discrete hand-off after continuous algebra. The lesson evidence supports hesitation about dividing and an overlong rearrangement, so the diagnostic is procedural: divide the entire compound inequality by positive two in one operation, then verify that squaring is monotone on the positive range. A transfer cue is any prompt combining ‘difference’, a strict bound and ‘how many integers’: translate difference to modulus, solve over the reals, mark open endpoints, and enumerate only at the end. The final response is the option \(6\), with no unit because it is a count.
