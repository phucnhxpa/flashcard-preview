Packet Q4 / Official WME01 Q5 — vehicle SUVAT (P–Q–R)

Question

From the official paper. Three points P, Q and R are on a horizontal road where PQR is a straight line.

The point Q is between P and R, with \(PQ=6x\) metres and \(QR=5x\) metres, as shown in Figure 2.


Official Pearson question-paper visual (prompt only; no answer annotations).

A vehicle moves along the road from P to Q with constant acceleration.

The vehicle is modelled as a particle.

At time \(t=0\), the vehicle passes P with speed \(u\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\)

At time \(t=12\) s, the vehicle passes Q with speed \(2u\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\)

Using the model, (a) show that \(x=3u\) (2)

As the vehicle passes Q, the acceleration of the vehicle changes instantaneously to \(1.5\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}\)

The vehicle continues to move with a constant acceleration of \(1.5\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}\) and passes R with speed \(3u\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\)

Using the model, (b) find the value of \(u\), (3)

(c) find the distance travelled by the vehicle during the first 14 seconds after passing P (4)

Status and stage data. This item was reviewed and deferred; the Tutor’s “okay too” comment is not a live derivation. The complete calculation here is OFFICIAL MS REFERENCE ONLY. On P–Q the vehicle has local data \(s=6x\), initial speed \(u\), final speed \(2u\), and time \(12\ \mathrm s\). Acceleration is constant only on this stage; no Q–R acceleration is used in part (a).

OFFICIAL MS REFERENCE ONLY — full derivation. For constant acceleration, displacement equals average speed multiplied by time: \[s=\frac{u+v}{2}t.\] Therefore \[6x=\frac{u+2u}{2}(12)=\frac{3u}{2}(12)=18u.\] Divide both sides by \(6\): \[x=3u,\] exactly the required result. This starts from the independent P–Q data, so it is not circular. An equivalent SUVAT route first obtains \(a=(2u-u)/12=u/12\) and then uses \(6x=12u+\tfrac12(u/12)12^2=18u\).

Strategy and why this formula applies: on P–Q, constant acceleration permits the symbolic average-speed formula \(s=(u+v)t/2\). Insert only that stage’s data: \(s=6x\), endpoint speeds \(u\) and \(2u\), and \(t=12\). Thus \(6x=((u+2u)/2)12=18u\), giving \(x=3u\). This derives the requested statement rather than assuming it. The average speed \(1.5u\) lies between the endpoint speeds for \(u>0\), so the direction and scale are plausible. The official figure supplies order P–Q–R but no kinematics method. Diagnostic trap: importing the later acceleration \(1.5\) into P–Q would cross the explicitly stated instantaneous change at Q. Transfer cue: when time and both endpoint speeds are known for one constant-acceleration segment, average speed avoids introducing an unnecessary acceleration variable.

The average speed \(3u/2\) lies between \(u\) and \(2u\), which checks the increasing-speed stage.

Using the model, (b) find the value of \(u\), (3)

Carry-in / given: \(x=3u\), \(QR=5x\), Q-speed \(2u\), R-speed \(3u\), and Q–R acceleration \(1.5\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}\).
STEP 1/2

OFFICIAL MS REFERENCE ONLY — choose the Q–R model. At Q the acceleration changes instantaneously to \(1.5\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}\), then stays constant on Q–R. This is a different acceleration regime from P–Q. Since Q–R gives endpoint speeds and displacement but no time, use \(v^2=u_0^2+2as\), with local initial speed \(u_0=2u\), final speed \(v=3u\), acceleration \(a=1.5\), and displacement \(s=5x\).

Substitute the official data and the part (a) result \(x=3u\): \[(3u)^2=(2u)^2+2(1.5)(5x)\] \[9u^2=4u^2+15x=4u^2+15(3u)=4u^2+45u.\] Hence \[5u^2=45u,\qquad 5u(u-9)=0.\] The vehicle passes P with speed \(u\) and later has speeds \(2u\) and \(3u\), so the intended moving model requires \(u>0\); reject \(u=0\). Therefore \[u=9\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}},\qquad x=3u=27.\] Check. Then \(QR=5x=135\ \mathrm m\), Q-speed is \(18\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\), and R-speed is \(27\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\). Substitution gives \(27^2-18^2=729-324=405\), while \(2as=2(1.5)(135)=405\). Both sides agree exactly.

For Q–R, time is absent, so use the governing relation \(v^2=u_0^2+2as\) before substitution. Here \(u_0=2u\), \(v=3u\), \(a=1.5\), and \(s=5x\). Carrying only \(x=3u\) gives \(9u^2=4u^2+45u\), hence \(5u(u-9)=0\). The model describes a vehicle passing successive points with positive speeds, so reject \(u=0\) and retain \(u=9\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\). Back-substitution yields Q-speed 18, R-speed 27 and distance 135; both sides of SUVAT equal 405. This explicit root test is the difficult modelling transition. Transfer recognition: displacement and endpoint speeds with no time signal the squared-speed relation, with fresh local symbols at the start of the new regime.

Trap. Do not carry a P–Q acceleration through Q. The explicit instantaneous change creates a new constant-acceleration stage. Label local initial speed as \(2u\) before selecting SUVAT, and apply the positive-speed condition when an algebraic zero root appears.

Validate the retained root against the complete Q–R regime because factorisation alone produces algebraic candidates, whereas the model decides which candidate represents the moving vehicle. The governing local mechanics are \(v^2=u_0^2+2as\), with local initial speed \(u_0=2u\), final speed \(v=3u\), acceleration \(a=1.5\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}\), displacement \(s=5x\), and the legitimate part-(a) relation \(x=3u\). These give \(9u^2-4u^2=45u\), hence \(5u(u-9)=0\). The difficult transition is a model-based domain decision: \(u=0\) would force speeds \(u,2u,3u\) and distances \(6x,5x\) all to zero, contradicting the source description of a vehicle passing the distinct points P, Q and R. Therefore the official-reference endpoint is \(u=9\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\), not a live lesson result; then \(x=27\) and \(QR=5x=135\ \mathrm m\). Check the squared-speed balance without recycling the factorisation: \(27^2-18^2=405\) and \(2(1.5)(135)=405\). A second, genuinely different check finds the Q–R time as \((27-18)/1.5=6\ \mathrm s\); average speed \((18+27)/2=22.5\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\) over six seconds also gives \(135\ \mathrm m\). Recognition cue: when SUVAT and a prior symbolic relation lead to factorisation, keep every algebraic root visible, test each against positive motion and the named geometry, then report the accepted speed with units.

(c) find the distance travelled by the vehicle during the first 14 seconds after passing P (4)

Carry-in / given: \(u=9\), \(x=27\), P–Q takes \(12\ \mathrm s\), and the next \(2\ \mathrm s\) use acceleration \(1.5\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}\).
STEP 1/2

OFFICIAL MS REFERENCE ONLY — split at Q. The first \(14\) seconds consist of all \(12\) seconds from P to Q and then \(2\) seconds after Q. A single SUVAT equation cannot span this interval because acceleration changes at Q. From part (a), \[PQ=6x=18u.\] With \(u=9\), \[PQ=18(9)=162\ \mathrm m.\]

Let S be the position reached at absolute time \(t=14\ \mathrm s\). The local Q–S time is \(14-12=2\ \mathrm s\). On this second stage, initial speed is the Q-speed \(2u=18\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\), acceleration is \(1.5\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}\), and local time is \(2\ \mathrm s\). Thus \[QS=(2u)(2)+\frac12(1.5)(2^2)=4u+3=36+3=39\ \mathrm m.\]

The first fourteen seconds cross Q at twelve seconds, so one formula cannot cover the whole interval. Use \(PQ=6x=162\ \mathrm m\) for the first regime. Reset time to zero at Q for the remaining \(14-12=2\) seconds; local initial speed is \(18\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\) and acceleration is \(1.5\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}\). The governing displacement formula is \(s=ut+\tfrac12at^2\), giving \(QS=18(2)+\tfrac12(1.5)(2^2)=39\ \mathrm m\). The hard transition is converting absolute time fourteen into local time two after the regime change. Check this stage with final local speed \(18+1.5(2)=21\) and average speed: \((18+21)2/2=39\ \mathrm m\). The interim endpoint is therefore Q–S distance \(39\ \mathrm m\). Diagnostic trap: applying \(1.5\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}\) throughout all fourteen seconds ignores the source discontinuity. Transfer recognition: split a journey at the exact model-change time before any substitution.

STEP 2/2

Add the non-overlapping stage distances: \[PS=PQ+QS=162+39=201\ \mathrm m.\] The two time widths are \(12+2=14\), so the requested endpoint is correct. As a second check, speed after two seconds beyond Q is \(18+1.5(2)=21\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\); average speed on Q–S is \((18+21)/2=19.5\), and \(19.5(2)=39\ \mathrm m\).

Transfer trigger. Mark the absolute target time, convert it to local time after each transition, calculate each stage with its own acceleration, and sum each displacement once. Here the decisive conversion is \(14-12=2\) seconds after Q.

Choose to add the adjacent, non-overlapping regime distances because the requested displacement starts at P and crosses the change point Q: \(PS=162+39=201\ \mathrm m\). The local two-second check uses final speed \(18+1.5(2)=21\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\); average speed \((18+21)/2=19.5\) over two seconds also gives 39 metres. Since \(39<135\), the fourteen-second point lies between Q and R, consistent with the source geometry.
