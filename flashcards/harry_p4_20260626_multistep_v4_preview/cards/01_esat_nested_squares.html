ESAT warm-up — nested squares

Question

Source header. A design is formed by joining points one third of the way along each side of a square to make a second square; the process repeats. Find the area of the fourth square as a fraction of the original. Visible choices included \(1/27\), \(5\sqrt5/81\), \(125/729\), \(25/81\), and \(5\sqrt5/27\).

Status: completed correctly in the lesson and substantially learner-led. This is recording/board-derived ESAT-style material, not a Pearson P4 question; the exact paper identity and official marks are unresolved.

Lesson method and completion3 steps · completed live

STEP 1/3

Strategy and why it applies. The target is a fraction of areas, so absolute scale is irrelevant: multiplying every length by the same factor multiplies both compared areas by the same square factor and leaves their ratio unchanged. Phuc explicitly recognised this invariance. Choose the convenient outer side length \(3\), because the marked one-third point then splits a side into lengths \(1\) and \(2\). The side of the rotated inner square is the hypotenuse of the right triangle made by those offsets. Apply Pythagoras before doing any repeated-area calculation: \[s_{\rm inner}^{2}=1^{2}+2^{2}=5,\qquad s_{\rm inner}=\sqrt5.\] The governing area formula is \(A=s^{2}\), so the outer and next-inner areas are \(3^{2}=9\) and \((\sqrt5)^{2}=5\). Therefore the one-transformation multiplier is \(5/9\).

Diagnosis of the route. This was not an example-specific tutor rescue: Phuc proposed Pythagoras, comparing areas, and repeating the ratio. Harry confirmed the convenient \(1,2,3\) scaling. A rejected route would be to compare side lengths as \(\sqrt5/3\) and then forget that the question asks for area; that would leave an irrational length ratio instead of squaring it. Another failure is to use \(1/3\) as the area multiplier merely because the construction points are one third along a side. The diagram shows why that is false: the new side is diagonal, not one third of the old side.

Check. Since the inner square lies inside the outer one, the multiplier must lie between zero and one; \(5/9\) does. The result also has no units, as a fraction of areas should.

STEP 2/3

Full iteration logic. Label the original square \(S_1\), the first inner square \(S_2\), then \(S_3\) and \(S_4\). The construction is self-similar, so the same area multiplier \(r=5/9\) applies at every transition. The phrase “area of the fourth square as a fraction of the original” counts transitions, not square labels: \[\frac{A_2}{A_1}=r,\qquad \frac{A_3}{A_1}=r^2,\qquad \frac{A_4}{A_1}=r^3.\] Hence \[\frac{A_4}{A_1}=\left(\frac59\right)^3=\frac{5^3}{9^3}=\frac{125}{729}.\] This is the exact lesson result. Phuc identified that three multiplications were required and calculated \(5^3=125\); Harry emphasised counting the three iterations carefully and knowing useful cubes. No decimal approximation is needed because the multiple-choice option is exact.

Why the common alternative fails. Raising to the fourth power counts four transformations and would describe \(S_5\), not \(S_4\). Using only \((5/9)^2\) stops at \(S_3\). A robust way to prevent the off-by-one error is to draw the short transition chain \(S_1\to S_2\to S_3\to S_4\) and count its three arrows. The numerator and denominator are both cubed; cubing only the numerator is not a valid power of a fraction.

Sense check. Successive approximate areas are \(1,0.556,0.309,0.171\) of the original, while \(125/729\approx0.171\). The exact and qualitative checks agree.

STEP 3/3

Transfer trigger and exam presentation. Recognise this method whenever a geometric process repeats with the same local proportions and the question asks for a later area, volume, probability, or population as a fraction of the start. First determine the single-step multiplier from one clean diagram; then raise it to the number of transitions. For area, square a length scale; for volume, cube it. Write the transition count explicitly so the exponent is justified rather than guessed. Here a compact exam line is \[\text{side split }1:2\Rightarrow s_{2}=\sqrt{1^2+2^2}=\sqrt5,\quad r_A=5/9,\quad A_4/A_1=r_A^3=125/729.\]

Lesson-specific diagnosis. Harry’s timing advice was to stop once the correct multiple-choice option is secure rather than over-calculate. That does not mean skipping the proof of the multiplier: the risky step is establishing \(5/9\), not multiplying afterward. Phuc asked whether the denominator also needed simplification. For an exact option, \(9^3=729\) completes the comparison; the board’s cube-memory note included \(5^3=125\) and \(9^3=729\). The next ESAT item was skipped for time and is not silently folded into this solution.

Final check-back. Substitute the chosen convenient side \(3\): original area \(9\), fourth area \(9(5/9)^3=125/81\), and dividing by \(9\) returns \(125/729\). This independent dimensional calculation confirms the fraction. The answer is source-supported, completed live, and carries no invented official paper identity or marks.
