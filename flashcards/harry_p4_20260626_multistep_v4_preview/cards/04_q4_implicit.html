P4 Q4 — implicit differentiation

Question

Official source header. The curve \(C\) has equation \(4x^2+y^2-2xy=24x\). (a) Find \(dy/dx\) in simplest form, showing all stages. (b) \(P(a,b)\) lies on \(C\), the gradient at \(P\) is \(2\), and \(a>0,b>0\); find \(a,b\).

Status: Q4(a) was corrected and completed live. Q4(b) reached \(2a+b=12\) and the curve relation, then was deferred. The candidates and \(P=(2,8)\) are official reference only. Harry’s positive/negative-\(b\) clue was a tutor slip: the actual candidates give \(b=8\) and \(b=0\).

[10 marks total: 5 + 5]

Part (a) — corrected and completed live[5 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1

Strategy and formula-first working. Differentiate every term with respect to \(x\), treating \(y\) as a function of \(x\). Use the chain rule \(d(y^2)/dx=2y\,dy/dx\) and the product rule \(d(xy)/dx=x\,dy/dx+y\). Keeping the product-rule derivative bracketed prevents the lesson’s decisive error: \[8x+2y\frac{dy}{dx}-2\left(x\frac{dy}{dx}+y\right)=24.\] Expand only after the bracket is secure: \[8x+2y\frac{dy}{dx}-2x\frac{dy}{dx}-2y=24.\] Collect the derivative terms on the left and move the others right: \[(2y-2x)\frac{dy}{dx}=24-8x+2y.\] Divide by \(2(y-x)\): \[\boxed{\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{12-4x+y}{y-x}}.\]

Lesson-specific diagnosis. Phuc’s submitted numerator was correct, but his denominator was missing \(-x\). He had recalled the product rule and supplied \(x\,dy/dx+y\); the loss occurred because the entire derivative of \(xy\) was not protected under the factor \(-2\). Harry’s correction was to bracket \(-2(xy'+y)\) before expansion. A rejected route that writes \(-2xy'\) differentiates only \(y\) and drops the derivative of \(x\), which is exactly why the \(-x\) disappears after collection.

Check-back. Substitute \((x,y)=(2,8)\), the later valid point: the derivative is \((12-8+8)/(8-2)=12/6=2\), matching the stated gradient. The denominator condition \(y\ne x\) is visible; where \(y=x\), this solved form is undefined and the curve may have a vertical tangent. This part was genuinely completed live.

Transfer trigger. In implicit differentiation, circle every product containing \(y\), write \(y'\) explicitly, and bracket before multiplying by any outside coefficient.

Part (b) — lesson setup and exact stopping point[5] · 1 step · live stop

Carry-in: Part (a) gives \(dy/dx=(12-4x+y)/(y-x)\); at \(P\), use \(x=a,y=b\) and gradient \(2\).
STEP 1/1

Strategy and why two equations exist. At \(P(a,b)\), substitute \(x=a,y=b\) into the derivative and set it equal to the given gradient \(2\): \[2=\frac{12-4a+b}{b-a}.\] Cross-multiply and simplify without losing signs: \[2(b-a)=12-4a+b\Rightarrow2b-2a=12-4a+b\Rightarrow\boxed{2a+b=12}.\] Equivalently, \(b=12-2a\). Phuc explicitly identified the apparent problem of two unknowns and one equation. Harry pointed out the second relation: because \(P\) lies on \(C\), its coordinates satisfy \[\boxed{4a^2+b^2-2ab=24a}.\] These two boxed equations are the complete lesson-reached setup.

Exact boundary. The next operation would be to substitute \(b=12-2a\) into the curve equation, but that substitution was identified and not carried out. Harry left it for Phuc to finish, and the board sticky corroborates that status. This step therefore stops before solving and does not display the official point as though Harry and Phuc completed it live.

Diagnosis of the route. The phrase “\(P\) lies on \(C\)” is mathematical data, not decoration; it supplies the second equation. Trying to solve only \(2a+b=12\) leaves infinitely many points and cannot determine \(P\). Conversely, using only the curve equation ignores the gradient and also leaves many points. The difficult transition is converting one geometric condition into each algebraic relation.

Check and transfer. Both candidate coordinates eventually must satisfy both relations, not merely one. In any “point on curve with given gradient” question, write a two-row system: derivative condition and curve-membership condition. That recognition trigger prevents the same one-equation dead end.

Part (b) — official mark-scheme reference only[5 marks] · 1 step

Carry-in: Lesson stopped with \(b=12-2a\) and \(4a^2+b^2-2ab=24a\).
STEP 1/1

Full reference derivation. Begin from the authorised live stopping point \(b=12-2a\) and substitute it into \(4a^2+b^2-2ab=24a\): \[4a^2+(12-2a)^2-2a(12-2a)=24a.\] Expand every bracket: \[(12-2a)^2=144-48a+4a^2,\qquad -2a(12-2a)=-24a+4a^2.\] Thus \[4a^2+144-48a+4a^2-24a+4a^2=24a,\] so \[12a^2-96a+144=0.\] Divide by \(12\): \[a^2-8a+12=0=(a-2)(a-6).\] Therefore \(a=2\) or \(a=6\). Using \(b=12-2a\) gives the candidates \((2,8)\) and \((6,0)\). The printed condition is strict: \(b>0\), so \((6,0)\) is rejected and \[\boxed{P=(2,8)}.\]

Tutor-slip disclosure and why it matters. Harry suggested that the two candidate \(b\)-values might have opposite signs and that a negative value would be rejected. The official algebra instead gives \(8\) and \(0\); zero is rejected because \(b>0\), not because it is negative. Preserving this distinction prevents a false sign rule from becoming lesson fact.

Check-back. Curve: \(4(2)^2+8^2-2(2)(8)=16+64-32=48=24(2)\). Gradient: \((12-8+8)/(8-2)=2\). Both independent conditions pass. For \((6,0)\), the equations also hold, but the domain condition fails.

Exam presentation and transfer. List both candidates before applying \(a>0,b>0\); do not discard a root without naming the governing condition. This derivation is official reference only and does not overwrite the deferred lesson status.
