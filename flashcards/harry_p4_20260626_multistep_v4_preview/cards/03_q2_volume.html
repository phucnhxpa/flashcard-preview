P4 Q2 — volume of revolution

Question

Official source header. The curve \(y=\frac53\sqrt{x-1}\), for \(2\le x\le8\), is rotated through \(2\pi\) about the \(x\)-axis. Find the exact volume of the solid formed.

Status: self-assessed gap only; not taught or worked in the lesson. Every method line below is official/reference explanation, not an observed learner attempt or correction.

[5 marks]

Official/reference method only[5 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3

Strategy and why it applies. Rotation is through a full \(2\pi\) about the \(x\)-axis, and the curve is given as \(y\) in terms of \(x\). A thin vertical slice at position \(x\) becomes a circular disc of radius \(y\), so its cross-sectional area is \(\pi y^2\). The governing P4 formula is therefore \[V=\pi\int_a^b y^2\,dx.\] Here \(a=2\), \(b=8\), and \[y^2=\left(\frac53\sqrt{x-1}\right)^2=\frac{25}{9}(x-1).\] Squaring removes the square root and squares both numerator and denominator; omitting the square on \(5/3\) is a common structural error. Substitution gives \[V=\frac{25\pi}{9}\int_2^8(x-1)\,dx.\]

Source-grounded diagnosis. The only lesson evidence is Phuc’s self-assessment that he had forgotten the volume-of-revolution formula. There is no observed attempt on this exact question, so this card must not invent a learner slip, a tutor correction sequence, or a live stopping point. The relevant recognition repair is to tie “rotation about the \(x\)-axis” to radius \(y\) and discs \(\pi y^2\). The alternative \(\pi\int x^2dy\) is mathematically meaningful for rotation about the \(y\)-axis in suitable circumstances but is not the required P4 formula here and uses the wrong variable/axis for this prompt.

Check before integration. On \([2,8]\), \(x-1>0\), so the radius and disc area are real and positive. A positive integrand over a nonzero interval guarantees a positive volume.

STEP 2/3

Full derivation and exact evaluation. Integrate the linear expression without decimalising: \[\int(x-1)\,dx=\frac{x^2}{2}-x=\frac{(x-1)^2}{2}+C.\] The shifted form is especially efficient because the integrand is \(x-1\). Therefore \[V=\frac{25\pi}{9}\left[\frac{(x-1)^2}{2}\right]_2^8=\frac{25\pi}{18}\left((8-1)^2-(2-1)^2\right).\] Work through the endpoint arithmetic: \[(8-1)^2-(2-1)^2=7^2-1^2=49-1=48.\] Hence \[V=\frac{25\pi}{18}\cdot48=25\pi\cdot\frac83=\boxed{\frac{200\pi}{3}}.\] The requested form is exact, so retain \(\pi\) and the rational coefficient. The physical unit is cubic units because a cross-sectional area is integrated with respect to a length.

Why rejected shortcuts fail. Using \(2\pi\int y\,dx\) confuses a surface/circumference expression with volume. Integrating \(y\) instead of \(y^2\) also leaves a fractional power and cannot represent disc area. Forgetting the lower endpoint gives \(49\) rather than \(48\) in the bracket. None of these are evidenced as Phuc’s actual errors; they are neutral exam traps only.

Arithmetic check. Since the disc area grows linearly from \(25\pi/9\) at \(x=2\) to \(175\pi/9\) at \(x=8\), average area is \(100\pi/9\). Multiplying by width \(6\) gives \(200\pi/3\), independently confirming the integral.

STEP 3/3

Transfer trigger and exam presentation. When a region is rotated about the \(x\)-axis and the radius is the vertical distance from that axis, write “disc radius \(=y\), so \(dV=\pi y^2dx\)” before substitution. If the curve crosses the axis, squaring still makes disc area nonnegative, but the radius interpretation should use distance. If a hollow region is rotated, replace one disc area by a washer area \(\pi(R^2-r^2)\); this question has no inner radius, so a single disc is correct. Keep the given bounds in \(x\) because the integration variable is \(x\).

Check-back and boundary. Dimensional reasoning provides a final guard: \(y^2\) has area dimension and \(dx\) adds length, producing volume. The exact result is about \(209.44\) cubic units, which lies between the smallest disc area times width and largest disc area times width. The linear-average calculation in the preceding step proves the same value without antiderivative algebra.
