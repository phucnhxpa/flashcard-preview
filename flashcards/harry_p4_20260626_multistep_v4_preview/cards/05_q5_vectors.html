P4 Q5 — intersecting vector lines

Question

Official source header. \(l_1:\mathbf r=(2,-1,3)+\lambda(1,4,2)\) and \(l_2:\mathbf r=(-1,\beta,6)+\mu(2,-1,7)\) intersect at \(P\). (i) Find \(\beta\). (ii) Find the coordinates of \(P\).

Status: the component equations and \(\lambda=-9,\mu=-3\) were reached live; \(\beta\) and \(P\) were deferred. The final values below are official reference only.

Reached in the lesson — equations and parameters[6 total] · 2 steps · live stop

STEP 1/2

Strategy and governing definition. If two vector lines intersect at \(P\), there is one value of \(\lambda\) on \(l_1\) and one value of \(\mu\) on \(l_2\) that produce the same position vector. Set the lines equal and compare corresponding components. Expanding gives \[l_1=(2+\lambda,\,-1+4\lambda,\,3+2\lambda),\] \[l_2=(-1+2\mu,\,\beta-\mu,\,6+7\mu).\] Therefore number the three scalar equations: \[(1)\ 2+\lambda=-1+2\mu,\] \[(2)\ -1+4\lambda=\beta-\mu,\] \[(3)\ 3+2\lambda=6+7\mu.\] Equations (1) and (3) contain only \(\lambda,\mu\), so solve those first; equation (2) is then the clean route to \(\beta\).

Lesson-specific diagnosis. Phuc correctly said the position vectors are equal at the intersection, recognised three equations, and proposed elimination. Harry advised numbering them and using (1) and (3) for the parameters. Because Q5 has no printed calculator-prohibition sentence, Harry allowed a simultaneous-equation solver. The neutral trap is to think there are “three unknowns but only two equations”: all three component equalities are available, but the middle one includes \(\beta\) and is best reserved until the two line parameters are fixed.

Check of setup. Each scalar equation compares the same coordinate: first with first, second with second, third with third. Mixing coordinates would destroy the geometric meaning. No marks are split between (i) and (ii) here because only the official total of six is authorised.

Transfer trigger. For intersecting parametric lines, expand, align coordinates vertically, number the equations, solve the pair that omits any extra parameter, then back-substitute.

STEP 2/2

Full parameter derivation and live endpoint. Rearrange equation (1): \[2+\lambda=-1+2\mu\Rightarrow\lambda-2\mu=-3.\] Rearrange equation (3): \[3+2\lambda=6+7\mu\Rightarrow2\lambda-7\mu=3.\] Multiply the first equation by \(2\): \(2\lambda-4\mu=-6\). Subtract this from the second: \[-3\mu=9\Rightarrow\boxed{\mu=-3}.\] Substitute into \(\lambda-2\mu=-3\): \[\lambda+6=-3\Rightarrow\boxed{\lambda=-9}.\] These are the parameter values Harry supplied and the board retained. The lesson stopped here for the final requested quantities; the board sticky assigned Phuc to finish \(\beta\) and \(P\).

Diagnosis and why rejected routes fail. Solving equations (1) and (2) first would introduce \(\beta\), creating three unknowns in two equations and obscuring the efficient structure. Treating \(\lambda\) and \(\mu\) as equal is also invalid: they measure positions along different lines and need not match at an intersection. A calculator may confirm the pair, but the written component equations establish the method and preserve sign information.

Check-back at the stopping point. Equation (1): \(2-9=-7\) and \(-1+2(-3)=-7\). Equation (3): \(3+2(-9)=-15\) and \(6+7(-3)=-15\). Both pass. Equation (2) is intentionally not completed in the live block because that would cross the evidence boundary.

Exam presentation. Keep \(\lambda=-9,\mu=-3\) visible as carry-ins to the official-reference completion and label the status change.

Official mark-scheme reference — final quantities[6 marks total] · 1 step

Carry-in: Lesson reached \(\lambda=-9\), \(\mu=-3\), then deferred \(\beta\) and \(P\).
STEP 1/1

Carry-forward formula and full completion. Use the lesson-reached values \(\lambda=-9\) and \(\mu=-3\). From component equation (2), \[-1+4\lambda=\beta-\mu.\] Solve symbolically for \(\beta\) before substituting: \[\beta=-1+4\lambda+\mu.\] Then \[\beta=-1+4(-9)+(-3)=-1-36-3=\boxed{-40}.\] To find \(P\), substitute \(\lambda=-9\) into \(l_1\): \[P=(2,-1,3)-9(1,4,2)=(2-9,\,-1-36,\,3-18)=\boxed{(-7,-37,-15)}.\] An independent calculation on \(l_2\) gives \[(-1,-40,6)-3(2,-1,7)=(-7,-37,-15),\] so both parameterisations reach the same point.

Diagnosis and presentation boundary. The arithmetic is official reference only; it was not completed aloud. Harry recommended writing final coordinates as a row. The official mark scheme also accepts a position/column-vector answer, so the card must not claim that column notation automatically loses a mark. What matters is that the answer is clearly the point \(P\), not a direction vector.

Sense check. The matching results from both lines are the strongest check: all three coordinates agree. The \(y\)-coordinate on \(l_2\) is \(\beta-\mu=-40-(-3)=-37\), which catches the common double-negative error.

Transfer trigger. Once parameters are known, compute the intersection from either line and verify with the other. If the points disagree, revisit parameter signs before changing \(\beta\). Keep exact integer arithmetic; no rounding is involved.

Official reference only: \(\boxed{\beta=-40,\ P=(-7,-37,-15)}\).
