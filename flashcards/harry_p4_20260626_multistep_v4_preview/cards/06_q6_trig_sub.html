P4 Q6 — trigonometric substitution

Question

Official source header. Show all working; solutions relying entirely on calculator technology are not acceptable. Using \(u=3+\cos\theta\), show that \(\int_0^{\pi/2}\frac{\sin2\theta}{\sqrt{3+\cos\theta}}\,d\theta=a\sqrt3+b\), where \(a,b\) are constants.

Status: substitution, transformed limits, and \(-2\int_4^3(u^{1/2}-3u^{-1/2})du\) were reached live. The antiderivative and \(a=8,b=-40/3\) are official reference only. No integrating factors.

[7 marks]

Reached in the lesson — transformed integral[7 total] · 2 steps · live stop

STEP 1/2

Strategy, identities, and why substitution applies. The square root already contains the prescribed expression \(3+\cos\theta\), so set \(u=3+\cos\theta\). First expose \(\sin\theta\,d\theta\) using the named double-angle identity \(\sin2\theta=2\sin\theta\cos\theta\). Differentiate the substitution: \[\frac{du}{d\theta}=-\sin\theta,\qquad du=-\sin\theta\,d\theta,\qquad \sin\theta\,d\theta=-du.\] Also solve for the remaining cosine: \(\cos\theta=u-3\). These replacements are all necessary; \(\cos\theta\) is not constant.

Transform the limits before integrating. At \(\theta=0\), \(u=3+\cos0=4\). At \(\theta=\pi/2\), \(u=3+\cos(\pi/2)=3\). Preserve the order \(4\to3\); the descending limits naturally coexist with the minus sign from \(du\).

Lesson-specific diagnosis. Phuc recalled the double-angle identity and understood that \(d\theta\) and the limits must change. Harry corrected the explicit slip \(\cos(\pi/2)=1/2\) to \(0\). Another explicit proposal was to take \(\cos\theta\) outside the integral; that fails because \(\theta\) varies. Replacing it with \(u-3\) makes the integrand entirely a function of \(u\).

Check. Since \(\cos\theta\) decreases from \(1\) to \(0\) on the interval, \(u\) must decrease from \(4\) to \(3\), matching the visual. No integrating-factor method belongs to this integral.

STEP 2/2

Full algebra to the exact live endpoint. Start from \[I=\int_0^{\pi/2}\frac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{\sqrt{3+\cos\theta}}\,d\theta.\] Replace \(\sqrt{3+\cos\theta}\) by \(\sqrt u\), \(\cos\theta\) by \(u-3\), and \(\sin\theta\,d\theta\) by \(-du\): \[I=-2\int_4^3\frac{u-3}{\sqrt u}\,du.\] Split powers explicitly: \[\frac{u-3}{\sqrt u}=\frac u{u^{1/2}}-\frac3{u^{1/2}}=u^{1/2}-3u^{-1/2}.\] Hence the exact lesson-reached line is \[\boxed{I=-2\int_4^3\left(u^{1/2}-3u^{-1/2}\right)du}.\] Harry then asked Phuc to finish; the board sticky corroborates this boundary.

Diagnosis and why the rejected logarithm route fails. Phuc asked whether the remaining integration gives \(\ln u\). The logarithm rule applies only to \(u^{-1}\), whereas the powers here are \(u^{1/2}\) and \(u^{-1/2}\). Both use the power rule \(\int u^n du=u^{n+1}/(n+1)\) for \(n\ne-1\). Reversing the bounds to \(3\to4\) is allowed only if the outside sign is changed consistently.

Sense check. The original integrand is nonnegative on \([0,\pi/2]\), so the final value must be positive. Although the transformed form has \(-2\), its bounds descend, so the combined orientation can still yield a positive result.

Transfer trigger. With definite substitution, make a two-row limit table immediately and eliminate every old variable before integration.

STEP 1/1

Formula-first integration and full evaluation. Apply the power rule to the live endpoint: \[\int u^{1/2}du=\frac23u^{3/2},\qquad \int -3u^{-1/2}du=-6u^{1/2}.\] Therefore \[I=-2\left[\frac23u^{3/2}-6u^{1/2}\right]_4^3.\] Evaluate the upper transformed limit \(u=3\): \[\frac23(3\sqrt3)-6\sqrt3=2\sqrt3-6\sqrt3=-4\sqrt3.\] Evaluate \(u=4\): \[\frac23(8)-6(2)=\frac{16}{3}-12=-\frac{20}{3}.\] Subtract in bracket order and multiply by \(-2\): \[I=-2\left(-4\sqrt3+\frac{20}{3}\right)=8\sqrt3-\frac{40}{3}.\] Comparing with \(a\sqrt3+b\) gives \[\boxed{a=8,\qquad b=-\frac{40}{3}}.\]

Boundary and diagnosis. This calculation is official reference only; it was not completed aloud. The outside \(-2\) belongs to the transformed integral and is not the requested coefficient \(a\). That earlier confusion is exactly why coefficient matching must happen only after complete evaluation. No integrating factor is used or needed: this is a definite integral solved by trigonometric identity, substitution, and the power rule.

Check-back. Numerically, \(8\sqrt3-40/3\approx0.523>0\), consistent with the nonnegative original integrand. A rough Simpson-style size check also makes a value below \(1\) plausible because the integrand is zero at both endpoints and modest in between. Differentiate the antiderivative \((2/3)u^{3/2}-6u^{1/2}\) to recover \(u^{1/2}-3u^{-1/2}\).
