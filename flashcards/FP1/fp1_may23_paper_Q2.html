Q2 — $2x^4-8x^3+29x^2-12x+39=0$ with $x=2+3i$ a root

Question

Note: show all stages of working; calculator-only solutions not acceptable.

Given that $x = 2 + 3i$ is a root of $\;2x^4 - 8x^3 + 29x^2 - 12x + 39 = 0$:

(a) Write down another complex root. [1]

(b) Use algebra to determine the other two roots. [4]

(c) Show all four roots on a single Argand diagram. [2]

[7 marks]

Part (a) — Other complex root [1 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 B1

Strategy: for any polynomial with REAL coefficients, complex (non-real) roots must come in conjugate pairs. Spotting this saves a huge amount of work — we get the second root for free, which then unlocks the quadratic factor in part (b).

Theorem (conjugate-pair rule — NOT in booklet, MEMORIZE): if $P(x) \in \mathbb{R}[x]$ has a complex root $z = a + bi$ (with $b \ne 0$), then its conjugate $\bar z = a - bi$ is also a root.

Apply to this quartic: coefficients $2, -8, 29, -12, 39$ are all real $\checkmark$, and $z_1 = 2 + 3i$ is given as a root. Therefore:

$$z_2 \;=\; \overline{2 + 3i} \;=\; 2 - 3i$$

$$\boxed{\;z_2 = 2 - 3i\;}$$

Pitfall — the "real-coefficient" precondition: the rule requires all coefficients to be real. If even one coefficient were complex (e.g. $iz^2$), the conjugate need NOT be a root. Always verify the coefficients are real before quoting the rule. Argand picture: conjugate roots are mirror-images across the real axis — here $(2,3)$ and $(2,-3)$.
$x_2 = \boxed{2 - 3i}$

Part (b) — Other two roots via factoring [4 marks] · 3 steps

From (a): conjugate-pair $z_1=2+3i$, $z_2=2-3i$.
STEP 1/3 M1

Formula (build quadratic factor from conjugate pair — NOT in booklet):

$$(x - z_1)(x - \bar z_1) = x^2 - 2\,\mathrm{Re}(z_1)\,x + |z_1|^2$$

Substitute $\mathrm{Re}(z_1)=2$, $|z_1|^2 = 2^2 + 3^2 = 13$:

$$x^2 - 4x + 13 \;\;\text{is a factor of the quartic.}$$

Trap: $|z_1|^2$ is a SUM of squares ($a^2+b^2$), not a difference. Compute $4+9=13$, not $4-9$.
$(x^2 - 4x + 13)$ is a factor
From STEP 1: $(x^2 - 4x + 13)$ is a factor of the quartic
STEP 2/3 M1

Move: polynomial-divide $\;2x^4-8x^3+29x^2-12x+39\;$ by $\;x^2-4x+13$.

Quotient setup — leading $2x^4 \div x^2 = 2x^2$, and constant $39 \div 13 = 3$, so guess quotient $2x^2 + 0x + 3$:

$$(x^2-4x+13)(2x^2+3) = 2x^4 - 8x^3 + 26x^2 + 3x^2 - 12x + 39$$

$$= 2x^4 - 8x^3 + 29x^2 - 12x + 39 \;\checkmark$$

Long-division shortcut: for a quartic with a known quadratic factor, the QUOTIENT is also quadratic. Match leading + constant and verify by expanding — faster than full long division.
$\;2x^4-8x^3+29x^2-12x+39 = (x^2-4x+13)(2x^2+3)$
From STEP 2: $2x^4-8x^3+29x^2-12x+39 = (x^2-4x+13)(2x^2+3)$
STEP 3/3 A1 A1

Solve the other quadratic factor $2x^2 + 3 = 0$:

$$x^2 = -\tfrac{3}{2} \;\Rightarrow\; x = \pm\, i\sqrt{\tfrac{3}{2}} = \pm\, \tfrac{i\sqrt{6}}{2}$$

$$\boxed{\;x_3 = \tfrac{i\sqrt{6}}{2},\quad x_4 = -\tfrac{i\sqrt{6}}{2}\;}$$

Rationalise the surd: $\sqrt{3/2} = \sqrt{6}/2$ (multiply top and bottom inside the root by $2$). Mark scheme prefers $i\sqrt 6 / 2$ over $i\sqrt{3/2}$.
$x_3 = \tfrac{i\sqrt 6}{2},\;x_4 = -\tfrac{i\sqrt 6}{2}$

Part (c) — Argand diagram [2 marks] · 1 step

From (a)/(b): four roots are $z_1 = 2+3i$, $z_2 = 2-3i$, $z_3 = \tfrac{i\sqrt 6}{2}$, $z_4 = -\tfrac{i\sqrt 6}{2}$. Plot Real on $x$-axis, Imaginary on $y$-axis.
STEP 1/1 B1 B1

Plot Real on $x$-axis, Imaginary on $y$-axis.

$z_1=2+3i \to (2,\,3)$  ·  $z_2=2-3i \to (2,\,-3)$

$z_3 = \tfrac{i\sqrt 6}{2} \to (0,\,\tfrac{\sqrt 6}{2}) \approx (0,\,1.22)$

$z_4 = -\tfrac{i\sqrt 6}{2} \to (0,\,-\tfrac{\sqrt 6}{2}) \approx (0,\,-1.22)$

Pattern: conjugate pairs are mirror-images across the REAL axis. Purely-imaginary roots sit ON the imaginary axis. Mark labels $z_1,z_2,z_3,z_4$ next to each point.
4 points: $(2,\pm 3)$ and $(0,\pm\tfrac{\sqrt 6}{2})$
