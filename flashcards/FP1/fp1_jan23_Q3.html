Q3 — Newton-Raphson Failure & Linear Interpolation

Question

The equation $f(x) = 0$ has a root $\alpha$ where $\alpha \in (0.15,\, 0.25)$.

(a) Find $f'(x)$.

(b) Explain why $x_0 = 0.25$ cannot be used as a starting value for the Newton-Raphson method.

(c) Starting with $x_0 = 0.15$, apply the Newton-Raphson method twice to find $x_2$.

(d) Use linear interpolation once on the interval $(0.15,\, 0.25)$ to find an approximation for $\alpha$.

[10 marks]

You need to apply standard differentiation rules — power rule, chain rule, etc. — term by term. Write out each derivative clearly so the examiner can follow your working. The derivative $f'(x)$ is needed in parts (b) and (c) — it appears in the denominator of the Newton-Raphson formula, so any error here propagates through the entire question.

$$f'(x) = \text{[differentiate each term of } f(x) \text{ using standard rules]}$$

Part (b) — Why $x_0 = 0.25$ fails [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1Show $f'(0.25) = 0$ B1 B1

Strategy: The Newton-Raphson formula is $x_{n+1} = x_n - \dfrac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}$. This formula requires division by $f'(x_n)$. If the derivative equals zero at the starting point, the formula involves division by zero, which is undefined — the method cannot proceed. So the task here is to (i) evaluate $f'(0.25)$ and show it equals zero, then (ii) explain why this makes $x_0 = 0.25$ unusable.

Compute: Substitute $x = 0.25$ into the derivative found in part (a):

$$f'(0.25) = \ldots = 0$$

Explanation (exam language): "The Newton-Raphson formula requires division by $f'(x_0)$. Since $f'(0.25) = 0$, using $x_0 = 0.25$ would result in division by zero, so the method cannot be applied with this starting value."

Geometrically, $f'(0.25) = 0$ means the tangent line to the curve at $x = 0.25$ is horizontal. A horizontal tangent never intersects the $x$-axis (unless it lies on it), so the Newton-Raphson procedure — which finds the $x$-intercept of the tangent — cannot produce a next approximation. The diagram above illustrates this: the red tangent at $x_0 = 0.25$ runs parallel to the $x$-axis and never crosses it.

Phuc's own explanation (Slack, verbatim): "Because the way Newton-Raphson works, it has to divide by the first derivative at the initial point. Suppose that if we pick an initial point that results in the first derivative equaling 0, then that means that initial point cannot work. We have to pick another one." This is exactly the exam-standard explanation — state that $f'(x_0) = 0$ causes division by zero in the formula.
$f'(0.25) = 0$ → division by zero in Newton-Raphson formula → $x_0 = 0.25$ cannot be used.

Part (d) — Linear interpolation [3 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2Verify sign change M1

Strategy: Linear interpolation requires that $f(a)$ and $f(b)$ have opposite signs — this guarantees a root lies between $a$ and $b$ by the Intermediate Value Theorem. Before applying the formula, we must confirm this sign change. We use $a = 0.15$ and $b = 0.25$ as the interval endpoints.

Compute:

$f(0.15) = \ldots$  (already computed in part (c))

$f(0.25) = \ldots$  (substitute into $f(x)$)

Check: $f(0.15)$ and $f(0.25)$ have opposite signs ✓ — confirming a root exists in $(0.15, 0.25)$.

Even if the question doesn't explicitly ask you to verify the sign change, writing "sign change confirmed" shows the examiner you understand why linear interpolation works on this interval. This is the precondition for the method — without opposite signs, the straight-line approximation between the two points might not cross the $x$-axis, and the method would give a meaningless answer.

Linear interpolation vs Newton-Raphson: Newton-Raphson uses one starting point and the derivative (tangent line). Linear interpolation uses two points with opposite signs and the secant line (straight line through $(a, f(a))$ and $(b, f(b))$). Neither formula is in the formula booklet — you must memorise both!
$f(0.15)$ and $f(0.25)$ have opposite signs — root confirmed in $(0.15, 0.25)$.
↳ from STEP 1: $f(0.15)$ and $f(0.25)$ computed, opposite signs confirmed
STEP 2/2Apply linear interpolation formula M1 A1

Strategy: Apply the linear interpolation formula. The version approved by the tutor (Mr Fuat) is:

$$\alpha \approx a - f(a) \cdot \frac{b - a}{f(b) - f(a)}$$

This formula finds where the straight line joining $(a, f(a))$ and $(b, f(b))$ crosses the $x$-axis. It is algebraically equivalent to the "similar triangles" method but much faster to apply in an exam. Note that $f(b) - f(a)$ is in the denominator — since $f(a)$ and $f(b)$ have opposite signs, this difference is never zero, so the formula is always well-defined when the sign-change condition holds.

Substitute $a = 0.15$, $b = 0.25$:

$$\alpha \approx 0.15 - f(0.15) \cdot \frac{0.25 - 0.15}{f(0.25) - f(0.15)} = 0.15 - f(0.15) \cdot \frac{0.1}{f(0.25) - f(0.15)}$$

Evaluate numerically using the values of $f(0.15)$ and $f(0.25)$ from STEP 1, and give the answer to the required precision.
