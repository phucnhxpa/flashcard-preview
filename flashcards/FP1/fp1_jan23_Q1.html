Q1 — Standard Results: $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^{n}(7r-5)^2$

Question

Use the standard results for $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^{n} r$ and $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^{n} r^2$ to find $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^{n}(7r-5)^2$.

[4 marks]

Solution [4 marks] · 5 steps

STEP 1/5Expand the summand M1

Strategy: Before we can use any standard summation results, the expression $(7r-5)^2$ must be expanded into a polynomial in $r$. The standard results only apply to $\sum r^2$, $\sum r$, and $\sum 1$ individually — not to a squared bracket. So our first move is always: expand first, then split.

Expand:

$$(7r-5)^2 = (7r)^2 - 2(7r)(5) + 5^2 = 49r^2 - 70r + 25$$

We now have three separate terms in $r$, each of which matches a standard result.

Phuc's tutor (verbatim): "So we can expand this one out and then we get, like, 49r² minus 7 multiplied by 5 multiplied by 2r, plus 25, and then for so we have three terms in the summation." This confirms the approach: expand into three terms first, then handle each separately.
$(7r-5)^2 = 49r^2 - 70r + 25$
↳ from STEP 1: $(7r-5)^2 = 49r^2 - 70r + 25$
STEP 2/5Split & extract constants M1

Strategy: Summation is linear: a sum of terms splits into separate sums, and constant multipliers pull out front. This converts our single complicated sum into three simpler sums that each match a standard result.

Split:

$$\sum_{r=1}^n (7r-5)^2 = \sum_{r=1}^n \bigl(49r^2 - 70r + 25\bigr)$$

$$= 49\sum_{r=1}^n r^2 \;-\; 70\sum_{r=1}^n r \;+\; \sum_{r=1}^n 25$$

The last term $\sum_{r=1}^n 25 = 25n$ (adding the constant $25$ a total of $n$ times).

Sense check: We now have three terms, each involving a known standard result. The constant $25$ doesn't involve $r$, so its sum is just $25n$. The $49$ and $70$ are constant multipliers that come outside their respective sums. This decomposition is the entire technique — everything else is just substitution and algebra.
$\displaystyle 49\sum r^2 - 70\sum r + 25n$
↳ from STEP 2: $49\sum r^2 - 70\sum r + 25n$
STEP 3/5Apply standard results A1

Strategy: Substitute the standard results into each term. Two of the three formulas are in the formula booklet; one must be memorised.

Standard results:

• $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^n r^2 = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}$  (in booklet)

• $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^n r = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$  (NOT in booklet — MEMORISE!)

• $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^n 1 = n$

Substitute:

$$= 49 \cdot \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} - 70 \cdot \frac{n(n+1)}{2} + 25n$$

Phuc's tutor (verbatim): "Actually, only one that you need to memorize — this one: ½n(n+1). The other ones are going to be given to us in our formula." So $\sum r$ is the only standard result NOT in the booklet. The $\sum r^2$ and $\sum r^3$ formulas are provided.
$\displaystyle 49 \cdot \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} - 70 \cdot \frac{n(n+1)}{2} + 25n$
↳ from STEP 3: $\displaystyle 49 \cdot \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} - 70 \cdot \frac{n(n+1)}{2} + 25n$
STEP 4/5Factor out $\frac{n}{6}$ M1

Strategy: All three terms share a common factor of $n$. To combine them into a single fraction, use $\frac{n}{6}$ as the common factor (since $6$ is the LCD of the denominators $6$, $2$, and $1$).

Re-write each term with denominator $6$:

$\displaystyle 49 \cdot \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} = \frac{n}{6}\bigl[49(n+1)(2n+1)\bigr]$

$\displaystyle 70 \cdot \frac{n(n+1)}{2} = \frac{n}{6}\bigl[210(n+1)\bigr]$

$\displaystyle 25n = \frac{n}{6}\bigl[150\bigr]$

Combine:

$$= \frac{n}{6}\Bigl[49(n+1)(2n+1) - 210(n+1) + 150\Bigr]$$

Sense check: Every term contains $n$ as a factor (as expected — when $n=0$, the sum is empty and should equal $0$). Using $\frac{n}{6}$ as the common factor ensures everything simplifies to a single neat fraction.
$\displaystyle \frac{n}{6}\bigl[49(n+1)(2n+1) - 210(n+1) + 150\bigr]$
↳ from STEP 4: $\displaystyle \frac{n}{6}\bigl[49(n+1)(2n+1) - 210(n+1) + 150\bigr]$
STEP 5/5Expand & simplify the bracket A1

Strategy: Expand everything inside the bracket, collect like terms, and present the final factored answer.

Expand $49(n+1)(2n+1)$:

$\;(n+1)(2n+1) = 2n^2 + 3n + 1$

$\;49(2n^2 + 3n + 1) = 98n^2 + 147n + 49$

Expand $210(n+1)$:

$\;210n + 210$

Combine all terms in the bracket:

$\;98n^2 + 147n + 49 - 210n - 210 + 150$

$\;= 98n^2 + (147 - 210)n + (49 - 210 + 150)$

$\;= 98n^2 - 63n - 11$

$$\boxed{\sum_{r=1}^n (7r-5)^2 = \frac{n}{6}(98n^2 - 63n - 11)}$$

Verification ($n=1$): LHS $= (7(1)-5)^2 = 2^2 = 4$. RHS $= \frac{1}{6}(98-63-11) = \frac{24}{6} = 4$. ✓ Always check with $n=1$ — it catches sign errors and factoring mistakes instantly. This is a free mark in the exam if they ask you to verify.
Final answer: $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^n (7r-5)^2 = \frac{n}{6}(98n^2 - 63n - 11)$
