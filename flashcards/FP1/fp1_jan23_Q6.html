Q6 — Rectangular Hyperbola $xy = 20$: Normal & Intersection

Question

A rectangular hyperbola $H$ has equation $xy = 20$. The point $P\!\left(2t\sqrt{a},\, \dfrac{2\sqrt{a}}{t}\right)$ is a general point on $H$.

(a) State the value of $a$. [1]

(b) Show that the normal to $H$ at $P$ has the given equation. [5]

(c) Points $A$ and $B$ lie on $H$ with parameters $T = C$ and $T = -\frac{1}{2C}$ respectively. The normal to $H$ at $A$ meets $H$ again at $B$. Determine the possible values of $C$. [6]

[12 marks total]

Part (a) — Find $a$ [1 mark] · 1 step

STEP 1/1Compare parametric forms B1

Strategy: The standard parametric form for the rectangular hyperbola $xy = c^2$ is $(ct,\, c/t)$. We need to match the given parametric form $P(2t\sqrt{a},\, \frac{2\sqrt{a}}{t})$ with $(ct,\, c/t)$ to identify $c$ and hence find $a$. Compare the $x$-coordinates: $ct = 2t\sqrt{a}$, which gives $c = 2\sqrt{a}$. Now use $c^2 = 20$ (from the equation $xy = 20$):

$$c^2 = (2\sqrt{a})^2 = 4a = 20 \implies a = 5$$

Verification: With $a = 5$, $c = 2\sqrt{5}$, so $P = (2\sqrt{5}\,t,\, \frac{2\sqrt{5}}{t})$. Check: $x \cdot y = 2\sqrt{5}\,t \cdot \frac{2\sqrt{5}}{t} = 4 \cdot 5 = 20$ ✓. The product $xy = 20$ is satisfied for all $t$, confirming that every point of this parametric form lies on the hyperbola. This is a one-mark "show" question — but even so, writing the verification takes 10 seconds and builds confidence for the harder parts ahead.

Tutor emphasis (Loom, verbatim): "This C has nothing to do with that C" — Phuc was confusing the parametric constant $c = 2\sqrt{5}$ with the constant $C$ used as the parameter value for point $A$ in part (c). These are completely different! The lowercase $c$ is a fixed number ($2\sqrt{5}$); the uppercase $C$ is a variable parameter.
$\boxed{a = 5}$, so $c = 2\sqrt{5}$ and $P = (2\sqrt{5}\,t,\, 2\sqrt{5}/t)$.

Part (b) — Derive the normal equation [5 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3Gradient of tangent via implicit differentiation M1 A1

Strategy: To find the normal, we first need the gradient of the tangent. For $xy = 20$, we differentiate implicitly with respect to $x$. The product rule gives:

$$\frac{d}{dx}(xy) = \frac{d}{dx}(20) \implies y + x\frac{dy}{dx} = 0 \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{y}{x}$$

At point $P(2\sqrt{5}\,t,\, \frac{2\sqrt{5}}{t})$:

$$\frac{dy}{dx}\bigg|_P = -\frac{2\sqrt{5}/t}{2\sqrt{5}\,t} = -\frac{1}{t^2}$$

This result — gradient of tangent $= -1/t^2$ — is a standard result for the rectangular hyperbola $xy = c^2$, independent of $c$. It's worth memorising: for the hyperbola at parameter $t$, the tangent always has gradient $-1/t^2$. This is given in some formula booklets but you should be able to derive it quickly via implicit differentiation as shown above.

Alternative method: You can also differentiate parametrically: $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}$. With $x = ct$ and $y = c/t$: $dx/dt = c$, $dy/dt = -c/t^2$, so $dy/dx = (-c/t^2)/c = -1/t^2$. Same result.
Gradient of tangent at $P = -\dfrac{1}{t^2}$
↳ from STEP 1: Tangent gradient $= -1/t^2$
STEP 2/3Gradient of normal M1

Strategy: The normal is perpendicular to the tangent. For perpendicular lines, the product of their gradients equals $-1$: $m_{\text{tangent}} \times m_{\text{normal}} = -1$. So the gradient of the normal is the negative reciprocal of the tangent gradient.

Compute:

$$m_{\text{normal}} = -\frac{1}{m_{\text{tangent}}} = -\frac{1}{-1/t^2} = t^2$$

So the normal at any point on the rectangular hyperbola $xy = c^2$ with parameter $t$ has gradient $t^2$. This makes geometric sense: since the tangent gradient $-1/t^2$ is always negative (the hyperbola is decreasing), the normal gradient $t^2$ is always positive — the normal always slopes upward from left to right. For large $|t|$, the normal is nearly vertical; for $t$ close to 0, it's nearly horizontal.

Quick check: At $t = 1$: tangent gradient $= -1$, normal gradient $= 1$. The tangent and normal at this point are at $\pm 45°$ to the axes, which makes sense by symmetry since the point $(c, c)$ lies on the line $y = x$.
Gradient of normal at $P = t^2$
↳ from STEP 2: Normal gradient $= t^2$, point $P = (2\sqrt{5}\,t,\, 2\sqrt{5}/t)$
STEP 3/3Write the normal equation A1 A1

Strategy: Use point-slope form: $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ with gradient $m = t^2$ and point $P = (2\sqrt{5}\,t,\, \frac{2\sqrt{5}}{t})$.

Compute:

$$y - \frac{2\sqrt{5}}{t} = t^2\!\left(x - 2\sqrt{5}\,t\right)$$

$$y = t^2 x - 2\sqrt{5}\,t^3 + \frac{2\sqrt{5}}{t}$$

Or equivalently, multiplying through by $t$:

$$ty = t^3 x - 2\sqrt{5}\,t^4 + 2\sqrt{5}$$

$$t^3 x - ty = 2\sqrt{5}(t^4 - 1)$$

This matches the standard normal form: $t^3 x - ty = c(t^4 - 1)$ with $c = 2\sqrt{5}$. The question asks you to "show" the equation, so make sure your final line matches the form given in the question exactly. Rearrange if necessary.

Standard formula check: The normal to $xy = c^2$ at parameter $t$ is $t^3 x - ty = c(t^4 - 1)$. With $c = 2\sqrt{5}$: $t^3 x - ty = 2\sqrt{5}(t^4 - 1)$. ✓ The formula booklet gives the parametric form of the hyperbola, but you must derive the tangent/normal equations yourself.
Normal at $P$: $t^3 x - ty = 2\sqrt{5}(t^4 - 1)$ ✓

Part (c) — Finding possible values of $C$ [6 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3Coordinates of $A$ and $B$ M1

Strategy: Identify the coordinates of points $A$ and $B$ using the parametric form with $c = 2\sqrt{5}$. Point $A$ has parameter $T = C$, so its coordinates are $(2C\sqrt{5},\, \frac{2\sqrt{5}}{C})$. Point $B$ has parameter $T = -\frac{1}{2C}$, so substitute this into the parametric form to find $B$'s coordinates.

Point $A$ (parameter $T = C$):

$$A = \left(2C\sqrt{5},\;\; \frac{2\sqrt{5}}{C}\right)$$

Point $B$ (parameter $T = -\frac{1}{2C}$):

$x_B = 2\sqrt{5} \cdot \left(-\frac{1}{2C}\right) = -\frac{\sqrt{5}}{C}$

$y_B = \frac{2\sqrt{5}}{-1/(2C)} = 2\sqrt{5} \cdot (-2C) = -4C\sqrt{5}$

$$B = \left(-\frac{\sqrt{5}}{C},\;\; -4C\sqrt{5}\right)$$

Tutor emphasis (Loom): "This C has nothing to do with that C." The lowercase $c = 2\sqrt{5}$ is the hyperbola constant from $xy = c^2 = 20$. The uppercase $C$ is the parameter of point $A$. Keep them strictly separate in your working to avoid catastrophic confusion.
$A = (2C\sqrt{5},\, 2\sqrt{5}/C)$;   $B = (-\sqrt{5}/C,\, -4C\sqrt{5})$
↳ from STEP 1: Coordinates of $A$ and $B$
STEP 2/3Substitute $B$ into normal at $A$ M1 A1 A1

Strategy: The normal at $A$ (parameter $T = C$) was derived in part (b). Replace $t$ with $C$ in the normal equation: $C^3 x - Cy = 2\sqrt{5}(C^4 - 1)$. Since $B$ lies on this normal (the normal at $A$ meets $H$ again at $B$), substitute $B$'s coordinates into this equation.

Substitute $x_B = -\frac{\sqrt{5}}{C}$ and $y_B = -4C\sqrt{5}$:

$$C^3 \cdot \left(-\frac{\sqrt{5}}{C}\right) - C \cdot (-4C\sqrt{5}) = 2\sqrt{5}(C^4 - 1)$$

$$-C^2\sqrt{5} + 4C^2\sqrt{5} = 2\sqrt{5}(C^4 - 1)$$

$$3C^2\sqrt{5} = 2\sqrt{5}(C^4 - 1)$$

Divide both sides by $\sqrt{5}$ (since $\sqrt{5} \neq 0$):

$$3C^2 = 2(C^4 - 1)$$

$$3C^2 = 2C^4 - 2$$

$$2C^4 - 3C^2 - 2 = 0$$

Key simplification: The $\sqrt{5}$ factors cancel completely, leaving a clean polynomial in $C$. This always happens with rectangular hyperbola questions — the $c$ constant factors out. If your $\sqrt{5}$ doesn't cancel, you've made an algebraic error somewhere.
$2C^4 - 3C^2 - 2 = 0$
↳ from STEP 2: $2C^4 - 3C^2 - 2 = 0$
STEP 3/3Solve the quartic as a quadratic in $C^2$ M1 A1

Strategy: The equation $2C^4 - 3C^2 - 2 = 0$ is a quadratic in disguise. Let $u = C^2$ (where $u \geq 0$), giving $2u^2 - 3u - 2 = 0$. Solve this quadratic for $u$, then take square roots to find $C$.

Solve:

$$2u^2 - 3u - 2 = 0$$

$$(2u + 1)(u - 2) = 0$$

$$u = -\frac{1}{2} \quad \text{or} \quad u = 2$$

Since $u = C^2 \geq 0$, we reject $u = -\frac{1}{2}$. Therefore $C^2 = 2$, giving:

$$\boxed{C = \sqrt{2} \quad \text{or} \quad C = -\sqrt{2}}$$

Verification: For $C = \sqrt{2}$: $A = (2\sqrt{10},\, \sqrt{10})$ and $B = (-\sqrt{10}/\sqrt{2},\, -4\sqrt{10})$. Check that $B$ lies on the normal at $A$ and that both points satisfy $xy = 20$. Both coordinates multiply to give $2\sqrt{10} \cdot \sqrt{10} = 20$ ✓.
