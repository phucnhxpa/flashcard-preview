Q8 — Parabola $y^2 = 4ax$: Focus, Directrix & Area of Triangle

Question

A parabola $C$ has equation $y^2 = 4ax$ where $a$ is a positive constant. The point $S$ is the focus of $C$. The line $L_1$ with equation $y = k$ ($k > 0$) intersects $C$ at point $P$.

(a) Show that $PS = \dfrac{k^2 + 4a^2}{4a}$. [4]

(b) The line $L_2$ passes through $P$ and intersects the directrix of $C$ on the $x$-axis. $L_2$ intersects the $y$-axis at point $A$. Show that the $y$-coordinate of $A$ is $\dfrac{4a^2k}{k^2 + 4a^2}$. [4]

(c) $L_1$ intersects the directrix at point $B$. Given a ratio of areas involving $\triangle BPA$ and $\triangle OSP$, determine the exact value of $a$. [6]

[14 marks total]

Part (a) — Show $PS = \frac{k^2 + 4a^2}{4a}$ [4 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2Find coordinates of $P$ M1 A1

Strategy: Point $P$ is the intersection of $y = k$ with the parabola $y^2 = 4ax$. Substitute $y = k$ into the parabola equation to find the $x$-coordinate. The focus of $y^2 = 4ax$ is $S = (a, 0)$ and the directrix is $x = -a$ — these are standard results you must know (they're in the formula booklet for the parametric form, but you should be able to state focus and directrix instantly).

Find $P$: Substituting $y = k$ into $y^2 = 4ax$:

$$k^2 = 4ax \implies x = \frac{k^2}{4a}$$

$$P = \left(\frac{k^2}{4a},\;\; k\right)$$

Focus: $S = (a,\, 0)$

The $x$-coordinate of $P$ is $k^2/(4a)$, which increases quadratically with $k$. When $k = 2a$, we get $x_P = a$ and $P = (a, 2a)$ — the end of the latus rectum. For larger $k$, $P$ moves further along the parabola. Note that $k > 0$ ensures $P$ is in the upper half-plane.

Key parabola facts ($y^2 = 4ax$): Vertex: $(0,0)$. Focus: $(a,0)$. Directrix: $x = -a$. Parametric: $(at^2, 2at)$. The parameter $a$ controls how "wide" the parabola opens — larger $a$ means a wider opening.
$P = \left(\dfrac{k^2}{4a},\, k\right)$;   $S = (a, 0)$
↳ from STEP 1: $P = (k^2/(4a),\, k)$, $S = (a, 0)$
STEP 2/2Distance formula → simplify M1 A1

Strategy: Apply the distance formula $PS = \sqrt{(x_P - x_S)^2 + (y_P - y_S)^2}$ and simplify. The algebra should yield a perfect square under the square root — this is the hallmark of a "show that" parabola question.

Compute:

$$PS = \sqrt{\left(\frac{k^2}{4a} - a\right)^2 + (k - 0)^2}$$

$$= \sqrt{\left(\frac{k^2 - 4a^2}{4a}\right)^2 + k^2}$$

$$= \sqrt{\frac{(k^2 - 4a^2)^2}{16a^2} + \frac{16a^2 k^2}{16a^2}}$$

$$= \sqrt{\frac{(k^2 - 4a^2)^2 + 16a^2 k^2}{16a^2}}$$

Expand the numerator:

$(k^2 - 4a^2)^2 + 16a^2 k^2 = k^4 - 8a^2 k^2 + 16a^4 + 16a^2 k^2$

$= k^4 + 8a^2 k^2 + 16a^4 = (k^2 + 4a^2)^2$

$$PS = \sqrt{\frac{(k^2 + 4a^2)^2}{16a^2}} = \frac{k^2 + 4a^2}{4a} \quad \text{(since } k^2 + 4a^2 > 0 \text{ and } a > 0\text{)}$$

$$\boxed{PS = \frac{k^2 + 4a^2}{4a}} \quad \checkmark$$

Alternative (focus-directrix property): For any point on the parabola, its distance to the focus equals its distance to the directrix. So $PS = x_P + a = \frac{k^2}{4a} + a = \frac{k^2 + 4a^2}{4a}$. This one-line proof uses the definition of a parabola and is much faster! Always look for the focus-directrix shortcut before doing the full distance formula.
$PS = \dfrac{k^2 + 4a^2}{4a}$ ✓ (shown)

Part (b) — $y$-coordinate of $A$ [4 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2Find the equation of $L_2$ M1 A1

Strategy: $L_2$ passes through $P = (\frac{k^2}{4a}, k)$ and the point where the directrix $x = -a$ meets the $x$-axis, which is the point $(-a, 0)$. Find the gradient of $L_2$ using these two points, then write the line equation. This is pure coordinate geometry — as the tutor said, "This has nothing to do with the Hyperbola or Parabola. This is finding the area of a triangle using the given information."

Gradient of $L_2$:

$$m = \frac{k - 0}{\frac{k^2}{4a} - (-a)} = \frac{k}{\frac{k^2}{4a} + a} = \frac{k}{\frac{k^2 + 4a^2}{4a}} = \frac{4ak}{k^2 + 4a^2}$$

Line equation through $(-a, 0)$ with gradient $m$:

$$y - 0 = m(x - (-a)) \implies y = \frac{4ak}{k^2 + 4a^2}(x + a)$$

Note how $PS = \frac{k^2 + 4a^2}{4a}$ from part (a) appears in the denominator of the gradient — this is not a coincidence. The question is designed so that results from earlier parts feed into later parts.

$L_2$: $y = \dfrac{4ak}{k^2 + 4a^2}(x + a)$
↳ from STEP 1: $L_2$: $y = \frac{4ak}{k^2+4a^2}(x+a)$
STEP 2/2Set $x = 0$ to find $A$ M1 A1

Strategy: Point $A$ is where $L_2$ meets the $y$-axis, so set $x = 0$ in the equation of $L_2$ and find the $y$-coordinate. This should simplify to the required expression.

Compute: Setting $x = 0$:

$$y_A = \frac{4ak}{k^2 + 4a^2}(0 + a) = \frac{4a^2 k}{k^2 + 4a^2}$$

$$\boxed{y_A = \frac{4a^2 k}{k^2 + 4a^2}} \quad \checkmark$$

This is exactly the expression to be shown. The simplicity of this step is deceptive — the hard work was in finding the correct gradient in STEP 1. Notice that $y_A < k$ (since $4a^2 < k^2 + 4a^2$ for $k > 0$), which makes geometric sense: $A$ is between the origin and $P$ on the $y$-axis, so its $y$-coordinate should be less than $k$. Also $y_A > 0$ since all quantities are positive ✓.

Sense check: As $k \to 0$, $y_A \to 0$ (point $P$ approaches the vertex, and $L_2$ becomes horizontal through the origin). As $k \to \infty$, $y_A \approx \frac{4a^2}{k} \to 0$ (the line $L_2$ becomes nearly vertical). The maximum of $y_A$ occurs at $k = 2a$, giving $y_A = a$ — at the latus rectum.
$y_A = \dfrac{4a^2 k}{k^2 + 4a^2}$ ✓ (shown)

Part (c) — Area ratio and finding $a$ [6 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3Identify all coordinates M1

Strategy: Collect all the key points. We need the coordinates of $B$, $P$, $A$, $O$, and $S$ to compute the areas of the two triangles.

Points:

• $O = (0, 0)$  (origin)

• $S = (a, 0)$  (focus)

• $P = \left(\frac{k^2}{4a},\, k\right)$  (on parabola, from part (a))

• $A = \left(0,\, \frac{4a^2 k}{k^2 + 4a^2}\right)$  (on $y$-axis, from part (b))

• $B = (-a,\, k)$  (intersection of $L_1$: $y = k$ with directrix: $x = -a$)

With all five points identified, we can compute the areas of $\triangle BPA$ and $\triangle OSP$ using the standard coordinate geometry formula for the area of a triangle: $\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}|x_1(y_2 - y_3) + x_2(y_3 - y_1) + x_3(y_1 - y_2)|$. Alternatively, if a triangle has a horizontal or vertical edge, use base $\times$ height $/ 2$ directly, which is faster.

Exam strategy: Before computing areas, check whether any triangle has a horizontal or vertical side — this allows a simpler base×height calculation instead of the full determinant formula. $\triangle OSP$ has a horizontal base $OS$ along the $x$-axis (from $O$ to $S$), making it the easier triangle to compute.
All five points identified: $O, S, P, A, B$
↳ from STEP 1: All coordinates collected
STEP 2/3Compute both triangle areas M1 A1 A1

Area of $\triangle OSP$: Base $OS$ is along the $x$-axis from $(0,0)$ to $(a,0)$, so base $= a$. Height is the perpendicular distance from $P$ to the $x$-axis, which is $|y_P| = k$.

$$\text{Area}(\triangle OSP) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot a \cdot k = \frac{ak}{2}$$

Area of $\triangle BPA$: This triangle has vertices $B(-a, k)$, $P(\frac{k^2}{4a}, k)$, and $A(0, \frac{4a^2k}{k^2+4a^2})$. Note that $B$ and $P$ share the same $y$-coordinate ($y = k$), so $BP$ is horizontal with length:

$$BP = \frac{k^2}{4a} - (-a) = \frac{k^2 + 4a^2}{4a}$$

The height is the perpendicular distance from $A$ to the line $y = k$:

$$h = k - \frac{4a^2 k}{k^2 + 4a^2} = \frac{k(k^2 + 4a^2) - 4a^2 k}{k^2 + 4a^2} = \frac{k^3}{k^2 + 4a^2}$$

$$\text{Area}(\triangle BPA) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{k^2 + 4a^2}{4a} \cdot \frac{k^3}{k^2 + 4a^2} = \frac{k^3}{8a}$$

Simplification check: Notice how $(k^2 + 4a^2)$ cancels in the area of $\triangle BPA$, giving the clean result $k^3/(8a)$. If your area expression doesn't simplify, re-check the coordinates of $A$.
$\text{Area}(\triangle OSP) = \dfrac{ak}{2}$;   $\text{Area}(\triangle BPA) = \dfrac{k^3}{8a}$
↳ from STEP 2: Areas computed in terms of $a$ and $k$
STEP 3/3Apply the given ratio and solve for $a$ M1 A1

Strategy: The question gives a specific ratio of the two triangle areas (and likely a specific value of $k$). Set up the equation from the given ratio and solve for $a$. The ratio $\frac{\text{Area}(\triangle BPA)}{\text{Area}(\triangle OSP)} = \frac{k^3/(8a)}{ak/2} = \frac{k^3}{8a} \cdot \frac{2}{ak} = \frac{k^2}{4a^2}$.

General ratio:

$$\frac{\text{Area}(\triangle BPA)}{\text{Area}(\triangle OSP)} = \frac{k^2}{4a^2}$$

Substitute the given numerical ratio and the given value of $k$, then solve for $a$. For example, if the ratio is $R$ and $k$ is known:

$$\frac{k^2}{4a^2} = R \implies a^2 = \frac{k^2}{4R} \implies a = \frac{k}{2\sqrt{R}}$$

Since $a > 0$ (given), we take only the positive root. Present the exact value.
