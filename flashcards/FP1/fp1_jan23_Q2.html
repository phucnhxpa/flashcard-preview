Q2 — Complex Numbers: Cubic with $z = -3$ as a Root

Question

$f(z) = 4z^3 + pz^2 + qz + 108$, where $p$ and $q$ are real constants. Given that $z = -3$ is a root of $f(z) = 0$:

(a) Find the value of $p$.

(b) Hence solve $f(z) = 0$ completely.

(c) Determine the modulus of each complex root.

(d) Show all roots on an Argand diagram.

[10 marks]

Part (a) — Find $p$ [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1Factor theorem: $f(-3) = 0$ M1 A1

Strategy: Since $z = -3$ is a root of $f(z) = 0$, the factor theorem tells us $f(-3) = 0$. Substituting $z = -3$ into $f(z)$ gives an equation in $p$ and $q$. However, there's a subtlety: the question says "find the value of $p$" (singular), which implies we can determine $p$ from this single substitution — likely because $q$ cancels or there is an additional constraint embedded in the question structure.

Substitute $z = -3$:

$f(-3) = 4(-3)^3 + p(-3)^2 + q(-3) + 108 = 0$

$= 4(-27) + 9p - 3q + 108 = 0$

$= -108 + 9p - 3q + 108 = 0$

$= 9p - 3q = 0$

$\therefore q = 3p$

The question likely provides additional information (or the mark scheme expects expressing $p$ via another condition). With the cubic having real coefficients and one real root $z = -3$, the other two roots are a conjugate pair. Using relationships between coefficients: sum of roots $= -p/4$, product of roots $= -108/4 = -27$. Since $-3$ is a root: $(-3) \cdot z_2 \cdot z_3 = -27$, so $z_2 z_3 = 9$.

Phuc's tutor (verbatim): "Because it is a root, so that means 4 times minus 3 cubed, plus p squared, and then like that, plus 108 must equal zero, so we can find the p here." The tutor then confirms that after finding $p$, you proceed with polynomial long division.
$q = 3p$; combined with other conditions: solve for $p$.

Part (b) — Solve completely [4 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2Polynomial long division by $(z+3)$ M1 A1

Strategy: Since $z = -3$ is a root, $(z + 3)$ is a factor. Dividing $f(z)$ by $(z+3)$ gives a quadratic $Q(z)$, whose roots are the remaining two solutions. For a cubic with real coefficients and one real root, the other two roots must be either both real or a conjugate pair.

Long division: Set up $f(z) \div (z + 3)$. Working through the division systematically:

$4z^3 + pz^2 + qz + 108 = (z + 3)(4z^2 + \ldots)$

Divide term-by-term: the leading coefficient of the quadratic is $4$ (matching $4z^3 / z$). Continue matching coefficients to find the full quadratic factor $Q(z)$.

Phuc asked: "So I don't need to do, like, factor stuff, right?" Tutor confirmed you can check roots with a calculator, but you must show working (polynomial long division) for full marks. "In your calculator, even you have the option to find the roots of complex number." Use calculator to verify, not to replace working.
$f(z) = (z+3) \cdot Q(z)$ where $Q(z)$ is a quadratic.
↳ from STEP 1: $f(z) = (z+3) \cdot Q(z)$
STEP 2/2Solve the quadratic $Q(z)$ M1 A1

Strategy: Apply the quadratic formula to $Q(z) = 0$. If the discriminant $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac < 0$, the roots are complex conjugates $a \pm bi$.

Quadratic formula:

$$z = \frac{-B \pm \sqrt{B^2 - 4AC}}{2A}$$

where $A$, $B$, $C$ are the coefficients of $Q(z)$. If $\Delta < 0$, write the roots as:

$$z = \frac{-B}{2A} \pm \frac{\sqrt{|\Delta|}}{2A}\,i$$

Key rule: For a polynomial with real coefficients, complex roots always come in conjugate pairs $a \pm bi$. So if one complex root is $\alpha + \beta i$, the other is automatically $\alpha - \beta i$.

Phuc's understanding (from Loom): "Because for a cubic equation, it has three solutions. If one of the solutions is real, the two other solutions have to be — either both real, or one complex number and one complex conjugate of that number." This is exactly right — conjugate root theorem in action.
Three roots: $z = -3$ (real) and $z = a \pm bi$ (complex conjugate pair).

Part (c) — Modulus of complex roots [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1Compute $|z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$ M1 A1

Strategy: For a complex number $z = a + bi$, the modulus is $|z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$. Both conjugate roots $a + bi$ and $a - bi$ have the same modulus (since $a^2 + (-b)^2 = a^2 + b^2$).

Compute: Using the complex roots found in part (b), substitute into the modulus formula. For example, if the roots were $z = 3 \pm 3i$:

$|z| = \sqrt{3^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{18} = 3\sqrt{2}$

Important: The question asks for the modulus of each complex root — but since they're conjugates, the answer is the same for both. State this explicitly for clarity.

Sense check: The modulus is always a positive real number. Both conjugate roots share the same modulus because complex conjugation only flips the sign of the imaginary part, which gets squared away in the modulus formula. If you get different moduli for conjugate roots, you've made an error.
$|z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$ (same for both conjugate roots).

Part (d) — Argand diagram [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1Plot all 3 roots on Argand diagram B2

Strategy: An Argand diagram has the real part on the $x$-axis and the imaginary part on the $y$-axis. Plot all three roots as points.

Rules for plotting:

• Real root $z = -3$: lies on the $x$-axis at $(-3, 0)$.

• Complex conjugate pair $z = a \pm bi$: these are symmetric about the $x$-axis. Plot $(a, b)$ and $(a, -b)$.

Label each point with its complex value. Draw and label both axes (Re and Im).

Exactly right — complex conjugates are mirror images about the real axis.
Argand diagram with $z=-3$ on real axis, conjugate pair symmetric about real axis.
