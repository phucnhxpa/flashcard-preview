Q9 — Proof by Induction: $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^{n}\log(2r-1) = \log\frac{(2n)!}{2^n \cdot n!}$

Question

Prove by mathematical induction that for all positive integers $n$:

$$\sum_{r=1}^{n} \log(2r-1) = \log\frac{(2n)!}{2^n \cdot n!}$$

[8 marks]

Proof by Induction [8 marks] · 4 steps

STEP 1/4Base case: $n = 1$ B1

Strategy: Every induction proof begins with verifying the base case. We must show that the statement holds for $n = 1$ by computing the left-hand side (LHS) and right-hand side (RHS) separately and showing they are equal. Do NOT substitute into one side and simplify to get the other — always compute both independently. The examiner awards the B1 mark for showing LHS = RHS with both sides explicitly evaluated.

LHS ($n = 1$):

$$\sum_{r=1}^{1}\log(2r - 1) = \log(2(1) - 1) = \log(1) = 0$$

RHS ($n = 1$):

$$\log\frac{(2 \cdot 1)!}{2^1 \cdot 1!} = \log\frac{2!}{2 \cdot 1} = \log\frac{2}{2} = \log(1) = 0$$

Conclusion: LHS $= 0 =$ RHS. The statement is true for $n = 1$. ✓

Note: we could also use $n = 0$ as a base case (the empty sum equals 0, and the RHS gives $\log\frac{0!}{1 \cdot 0!} = \log 1 = 0$), but $n = 1$ is conventional and expected by examiners.

Exam tip: Write "LHS = … = 0" and "RHS = … = 0" on separate lines, then "LHS = RHS ✓". This makes the base case absolutely clear. Some students lose the B1 by writing "LHS = RHS" without showing the calculation.
Base case: LHS $= 0 =$ RHS. True for $n = 1$. ✓
↳ from STEP 1: Statement true for $n = 1$
STEP 2/4Inductive hypothesis: assume $n = k$ M1

Strategy: State the inductive hypothesis clearly. Assume the statement is true for some arbitrary positive integer $n = k$. This assumption will be used as a substitution in the next step. Write it out in full — examiners want to see the explicit assumed identity, not just "assume true for $n = k$."

Assume true for $n = k$ (where $k$ is a positive integer):

$$\sum_{r=1}^{k}\log(2r - 1) = \log\frac{(2k)!}{2^k \cdot k!}$$

What this means: We are taking the statement as given for this particular value $k$. In the next step, we will show that this assumption forces the statement to also be true for $n = k + 1$. The assumption is not proved — it is a hypothesis. The logical structure is: "IF true for $k$, THEN true for $k+1$." Combined with the base case ($n = 1$), this chain of implications covers all positive integers.

Label clearly: Write "Assume true for $n = k$:" followed by the full equation. The M1 mark here is for correct use of the assumed result in the inductive step.

Common mistake: Some students assume the result for $n = k + 1$ instead of $n = k$ (circular reasoning). You must assume for $k$ and then PROVE for $k + 1$. The assumption is your starting point; the $k + 1$ case is your destination.
Assumed: $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^{k}\log(2r-1) = \log\frac{(2k)!}{2^k \cdot k!}$
↳ from STEP 2: Hypothesis: $\sum_{r=1}^k \log(2r-1) = \log\frac{(2k)!}{2^k \cdot k!}$
STEP 3/4Inductive step: prove $n = k + 1$ M1 A1 A1 A1

Strategy: For a summation induction, split the $n = k+1$ sum into the $n = k$ sum (which equals the assumed RHS) plus the $(k+1)$-th term. Then manipulate the resulting expression to match the target form for $n = k + 1$.

Split the sum:

$$\sum_{r=1}^{k+1}\log(2r-1) = \underbrace{\sum_{r=1}^{k}\log(2r-1)}_{\text{= assumed RHS}} + \log(2(k+1) - 1)$$

$$= \log\frac{(2k)!}{2^k \cdot k!} + \log(2k + 1)$$

Combine using log rules ($\log a + \log b = \log(ab)$):

$$= \log\left(\frac{(2k)!}{2^k \cdot k!} \cdot (2k+1)\right) = \log\frac{(2k)! \cdot (2k+1)}{2^k \cdot k!}$$

Key trick — multiply top and bottom by $(2k + 2)$:

$$= \log\frac{(2k)! \cdot (2k+1) \cdot (2k+2)}{2^k \cdot k! \cdot (2k+2)}$$

Numerator: $(2k)! \cdot (2k+1)(2k+2) = (2k+2)! = (2(k+1))!$

This works because $(2k+2)! = (2k+2)(2k+1)(2k)!$ by the definition of factorial.

Denominator: $2^k \cdot k! \cdot (2k+2) = 2^k \cdot k! \cdot 2(k+1) = 2^{k+1} \cdot (k+1)!$

Here we used $2k + 2 = 2(k+1)$, so $(2k+2) = 2 \cdot (k+1)$. The factor of 2 combines with $2^k$ to give $2^{k+1}$, and $(k+1)$ combines with $k!$ to give $(k+1)!$.

Result:

$$= \log\frac{(2(k+1))!}{2^{k+1} \cdot (k+1)!}$$

This is exactly the statement for $n = k + 1$. ✓

Tutor's assessment (Loom, verbatim): "This was quite a different question. I don't think so that even the home students… I never saw this kind of question before. So that's why it's hard." The critical insight is multiplying top and bottom by $(2k+2)$ — this is the only way to generate $(2(k+1))!$ in the numerator and $2^{k+1}(k+1)!$ in the denominator simultaneously. This factorial manipulation is the heart of the proof.
Loom 2 working (verbatim): "So this is going to be $2k$ times $k$ factorial, $2k$ times $k$ factorial and $2k$, and then $2$ times $2$ to power $k$ times $k$ factorial… Can we take the $k$ factorial common factor here? Yeah. $k$ factorial. And then remaining is going to be $k$ plus 1… Actually, this is going to be $k$ factorial times $k+1$, actually $2$ to power $k+1$, and then $(k+1)$ factorial." This shows the key denominator manipulation step by step.
$\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^{k+1}\log(2r-1) = \log\frac{(2(k+1))!}{2^{k+1}\cdot(k+1)!}$ ✓ (matches $n = k+1$ form)
↳ from STEP 3: Statement proven for $n = k+1$ assuming $n = k$
STEP 4/4Conclusion A1

Strategy: The conclusion is a mandatory part of every induction proof. You must state all three components: (1) the base case is true, (2) the inductive step is valid, (3) therefore the statement holds for all positive integers $n$. Missing any one of these three components can lose the final A1 mark. Write it as a separate, clearly labelled paragraph.

Conclusion (exam-standard wording):

The statement is true for $n = 1$ (base case verified in STEP 1).

If the statement is true for $n = k$, then it has been shown to be true for $n = k + 1$ (inductive step completed in STEP 3).

Therefore, by the principle of mathematical induction, the statement

$$\sum_{r=1}^{n}\log(2r-1) = \log\frac{(2n)!}{2^n \cdot n!}$$

is true for all positive integers $n$. $\blacksquare$

The phrasing "for all positive integers $n$" (or "$n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$") is essential. If the base case were $n = 0$, you would write "for all non-negative integers $n$." Match the domain to whatever the question specifies. The QED symbol ($\blacksquare$) signals the end of the proof — examiners appreciate this formal touch.

Induction conclusion template (must memorise): "True for $n = [base]$. If true for $n = k$, shown true for $n = k+1$. By mathematical induction, true for all [domain]." All three sentences are required. Types of FP1 induction: (1) Summation — split at $k$, add $(k+1)$-th term. (2) Divisibility — compute $f(k+1) - cf(k)$. (3) Matrix powers — $M^{k+1} = M^k \cdot M$.
$\blacksquare$ Proven for all $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$ by mathematical induction.
