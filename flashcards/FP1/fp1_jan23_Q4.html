Q4 — Roots of Polynomials: Vieta's Formulas & Cube Identity

Question

A quadratic equation $4x^2 + 3x + k = 0$ has roots $\alpha$ and $\beta$, where $k$ is a constant.

(a) Write down the values of $\alpha + \beta$ and $\alpha\beta$ in terms of $k$.

(b) Find $\dfrac{\alpha^2}{\beta} + \dfrac{\beta^2}{\alpha}$ in terms of $k$.

(c) Hence find a quadratic equation with roots $\dfrac{\alpha}{\beta^2}$ and $\dfrac{\beta}{\alpha^2}$, giving coefficients in terms of $k$.

[10 marks]

Part (a) — Vieta's formulas [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1Apply Vieta's formulas B1 B1

Strategy: Vieta's formulas connect the roots of a polynomial to its coefficients without requiring you to actually solve for the roots. For a quadratic $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ with roots $\alpha$ and $\beta$, Vieta's formulas state: $\alpha + \beta = -\frac{b}{a}$ and $\alpha\beta = \frac{c}{a}$. These are derived by expanding $(x - \alpha)(x - \beta) = x^2 - (\alpha+\beta)x + \alpha\beta$ and comparing coefficients. The key is to identify $a$, $b$, $c$ correctly from the given equation — here $a = 4$, $b = 3$, $c = k$. Do not confuse the coefficient labels with the parameter $k$ in the question.

Apply: For $4x^2 + 3x + k = 0$ (with $a = 4$, $b = 3$, $c = k$):

$$\alpha + \beta = -\frac{b}{a} = -\frac{3}{4}$$

$$\alpha\beta = \frac{c}{a} = \frac{k}{4}$$

These two expressions form the foundation for every subsequent part. Every "roots of polynomials" question in FP1 follows the same pattern: extract $\alpha + \beta$ and $\alpha\beta$ first, then express everything else in terms of these two quantities. You never need to find $\alpha$ and $\beta$ individually — the entire method works through symmetric functions of the roots.

Exam tip: Always write Vieta's formulas in a clearly labelled box at the top of your working. Parts (b) and (c) will inevitably refer back to these values, and having them visible saves time and reduces errors. The B1 marks here are "bookwork" — free marks for stating the formulas correctly.
$\alpha + \beta = -\dfrac{3}{4}$,   $\alpha\beta = \dfrac{k}{4}$

Part (b) — Find $\frac{\alpha^2}{\beta} + \frac{\beta^2}{\alpha}$ [4 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2Combine fractions → $\frac{\alpha^3 + \beta^3}{\alpha\beta}$ M1

Strategy: Whenever you see a sum of fractions involving roots, the first step is always to combine them over a common denominator. The common denominator here is $\alpha\beta$, which is one of our Vieta quantities — this is by design. The FP1 examiners construct these expressions so that the denominator simplifies to a power of $\alpha\beta$, and the numerator simplifies using a standard identity.

Combine:

$$\frac{\alpha^2}{\beta} + \frac{\beta^2}{\alpha} = \frac{\alpha^3 + \beta^3}{\alpha\beta}$$

Now we need to evaluate $\alpha^3 + \beta^3$. This is NOT a Vieta quantity directly, so we need an identity to express it in terms of $\alpha + \beta$ and $\alpha\beta$. The key identity is the sum of cubes:

$$\alpha^3 + \beta^3 = (\alpha + \beta)^3 - 3\alpha\beta(\alpha + \beta)$$

This identity is derived from expanding $(\alpha + \beta)^3 = \alpha^3 + 3\alpha^2\beta + 3\alpha\beta^2 + \beta^3$, then rearranging: $\alpha^3 + \beta^3 = (\alpha + \beta)^3 - 3\alpha\beta(\alpha + \beta)$. You must memorise this — it appears in almost every FP1 roots question.

Phuc's Slack reminder (verbatim): "Remember $(a+b)^3 = \ldots$ for polynomial roots manipulation." This identity is the single most important tool for roots-of-polynomials questions. The analogous identity $\alpha^2 + \beta^2 = (\alpha+\beta)^2 - 2\alpha\beta$ handles the square case.
$\dfrac{\alpha^2}{\beta} + \dfrac{\beta^2}{\alpha} = \dfrac{\alpha^3 + \beta^3}{\alpha\beta}$ where $\alpha^3 + \beta^3 = (\alpha+\beta)^3 - 3\alpha\beta(\alpha+\beta)$
↳ from STEP 1: $\frac{\alpha^3+\beta^3}{\alpha\beta}$ with the cube identity
STEP 2/2Substitute Vieta's values M1 A1 A1

Strategy: Now substitute $\alpha + \beta = -\frac{3}{4}$ and $\alpha\beta = \frac{k}{4}$ into the expression from STEP 1. This is pure algebra — be extremely careful with the fractions and negative signs. A common error is losing the minus sign from $\alpha + \beta = -\frac{3}{4}$ when cubing.

Compute $\alpha^3 + \beta^3$:

$(\alpha + \beta)^3 = \left(-\frac{3}{4}\right)^3 = -\frac{27}{64}$

$3\alpha\beta(\alpha + \beta) = 3 \cdot \frac{k}{4} \cdot \left(-\frac{3}{4}\right) = -\frac{9k}{16}$

$$\alpha^3 + \beta^3 = -\frac{27}{64} - \left(-\frac{9k}{16}\right) = -\frac{27}{64} + \frac{9k}{16} = \frac{-27 + 36k}{64}$$

Divide by $\alpha\beta = \frac{k}{4}$:

$$\frac{\alpha^3 + \beta^3}{\alpha\beta} = \frac{-27 + 36k}{64} \div \frac{k}{4} = \frac{-27 + 36k}{64} \times \frac{4}{k} = \frac{-27 + 36k}{16k}$$

$$\boxed{\frac{\alpha^2}{\beta} + \frac{\beta^2}{\alpha} = \frac{36k - 27}{16k}}$$

Sense check: The expression is undefined when $k = 0$ (since $\alpha\beta = 0$ means one root is zero, making the original fractions undefined). The expression is rational in $k$, which is expected since $\alpha + \beta$ and $\alpha\beta$ are both rational functions of $k$. If you get an irrational answer, you've made an arithmetic error somewhere.
$\dfrac{\alpha^2}{\beta} + \dfrac{\beta^2}{\alpha} = \dfrac{36k - 27}{16k}$

Part (c) — New equation with roots $\frac{\alpha}{\beta^2}$ and $\frac{\beta}{\alpha^2}$ [4 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2Sum and product of new roots M1 A1

Strategy: To form a quadratic with specified roots, we need the sum and product of those roots. A quadratic with roots $p$ and $q$ is $x^2 - (p+q)x + pq = 0$. So we compute: (i) $\frac{\alpha}{\beta^2} + \frac{\beta}{\alpha^2}$ and (ii) $\frac{\alpha}{\beta^2} \cdot \frac{\beta}{\alpha^2}$. The product is usually simpler, so start there.

Product of new roots:

$$\frac{\alpha}{\beta^2} \cdot \frac{\beta}{\alpha^2} = \frac{\alpha\beta}{\alpha^2\beta^2} = \frac{1}{\alpha\beta} = \frac{1}{k/4} = \frac{4}{k}$$

Sum of new roots:

$$\frac{\alpha}{\beta^2} + \frac{\beta}{\alpha^2} = \frac{\alpha^3 + \beta^3}{(\alpha\beta)^2}$$

(Common denominator is $\alpha^2\beta^2 = (\alpha\beta)^2$.) Using $\alpha^3 + \beta^3 = \frac{-27 + 36k}{64}$ from part (b) and $(\alpha\beta)^2 = \frac{k^2}{16}$:

$$= \frac{(-27 + 36k)/64}{k^2/16} = \frac{-27 + 36k}{64} \cdot \frac{16}{k^2} = \frac{-27 + 36k}{4k^2}$$

Pattern recognition: Notice how the sum of the new roots reuses $\alpha^3 + \beta^3$ from part (b) — this is why the question says "hence." The examiners designed the parts to chain together. If you computed $\alpha^3 + \beta^3$ in part (b), you can use it directly here without recalculating.
Sum $= \dfrac{36k-27}{4k^2}$,   Product $= \dfrac{4}{k}$
↳ from STEP 1: Sum $S = \frac{36k-27}{4k^2}$, Product $P = \frac{4}{k}$
STEP 2/2Form the equation M1 A1

Strategy: If a quadratic has roots with sum $S$ and product $P$, the equation is $x^2 - Sx + P = 0$. However, the question asks for integer (or at least "nice") coefficients in terms of $k$. We may need to multiply through by an appropriate expression to clear fractions. The tutor noted: "Always we are free to select $p = 1$" when forming the new equation, meaning we start with the monic form and then clear denominators.

Form the monic equation:

$$x^2 - \frac{36k - 27}{4k^2}\,x + \frac{4}{k} = 0$$

Clear fractions: Multiply through by $4k^2$ (the LCD):

$$4k^2 x^2 - (36k - 27)x + 16k = 0$$

This can be simplified slightly. Check: if there's a common factor, divide it out. Otherwise, present as is. The answer should have coefficients that are polynomial expressions in $k$ (no fractions remaining).

$$\boxed{4k^2 x^2 - (36k - 27)x + 16k = 0}$$

Tutor note (Loom, verbatim): "Always we are free to select $p = 1$" — meaning start with the monic form $x^2 - Sx + P = 0$, then multiply out to get integer-type coefficients. This avoids confusion about which coefficient to make equal to 1 when there are three unknowns ($a$, $b$, $c$) but only two equations (sum and product).
Verification: If you substitute a specific value of $k$ (e.g., $k = 1$), you can check that the original equation $4x^2 + 3x + 1 = 0$ has roots $\alpha, \beta$ (use the quadratic formula), and then verify that $\frac{\alpha}{\beta^2}$ and $\frac{\beta}{\alpha^2}$ satisfy your new equation. This is a powerful self-check in the exam.
$\boxed{4k^2 x^2 - (36k-27)x + 16k = 0}$
