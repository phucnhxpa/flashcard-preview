Q5 — Definite Integration with Horizontal Translation

Question

Given $\int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx = 6$. (a) Find $\int_{-1}^{1} [f(x) - 2x]\,dx$. (b) Hence find $\int_{2}^{4} f(x-3)\,dx$.

[5 marks]

Solution 3 steps

STEP 1/3

Part (a) — Split using linearity of integration:

$$\int_{-1}^{1} [f(x) - 2x]\,dx = \int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx - \int_{-1}^{1} 2x\,dx$$

First integral: given as $6$.

Second integral: $\int_{-1}^{1} 2x\,dx = [x^2]_{-1}^{1} = 1 - 1 = 0$

Why zero? $2x$ is an odd function and the interval $[-1, 1]$ is symmetric about the origin, so the integral is always $0$.
$\int_{-1}^{1} [f(x) - 2x]\,dx = 6 - 0 = \boxed{6}$
↳ from STEP 1:$\int_{-1}^{1} f(x)\,dx = 6$
STEP 2/3

Part (b) — Recognise the horizontal translation:

$f(x-3)$ is a horizontal translation of $f(x)$ by $3$ units to the right.

The limits shift: $[-1, 1] \to [-1+3, 1+3] = [2, 4]$.

Since horizontal translation preserves the area under the curve:

$$\int_{2}^{4} f(x-3)\,dx = \int_{-1}^{1} f(u)\,du = 6$$

$\int_{2}^{4} f(x-3)\,dx = \boxed{6}$
STEP 3/3

Formal proof (substitution): Let $u = x - 3$, then $du = dx$.

When $x = 2$, $u = -1$; when $x = 4$, $u = 1$.

$$\int_{2}^{4} f(x-3)\,dx = \int_{-1}^{1} f(u)\,du = 6 \checkmark$$

Phuc's mistake: "I didn't know how to do the second part." Mr Fuat: "Because we are moving the graph horizontally, the area shouldn't change."
Key exam insight: When they say "hence", look for a transformation link. $f(x-a)$ with shifted limits → same integral value.
Key rule: $\int_{p+a}^{q+a} f(x-a)\,dx = \int_p^q f(x)\,dx$ — horizontal shift preserves area
