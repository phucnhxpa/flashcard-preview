Q2 — Express in form $kx^n$: $a^{1/2}$, $\dfrac{16}{b^3}$, $\bigl(\tfrac{ab^3}{2}\bigr)^{-4/3}$

Question

Given $a = \dfrac{1}{64}x^2$ and $b = \dfrac{16}{\sqrt{x}}$. Express each of the following in the form $kx^n$, stating the values of $k$ and $n$.

(a) $a^{1/2}$ [1]

(b) $\dfrac{16}{b^3}$ [1]

(c) $\left(\dfrac{ab^3}{2}\right)^{-4/3}$ [2]

[4 marks]

Part (a) — $a^{1/2}$ [1 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 B1

Strategy: $a$ is a product of a scalar and a power of $x$. The exponent rule $(AB)^n = A^n B^n$ lets us distribute the half-power across both factors; then simplify each piece.

Rule (power of a product):

$$\left(A B\right)^n = A^n\, B^n$$

Apply with $A = \tfrac{1}{64}$, $B = x^2$, $n = \tfrac{1}{2}$:

$\;\;\;a^{1/2} = \left(\tfrac{1}{64} x^2\right)^{1/2} = \left(\tfrac{1}{64}\right)^{1/2}\!\cdot \left(x^2\right)^{1/2}$

Simplify each factor:

$\;\;\;\left(\tfrac{1}{64}\right)^{1/2} = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{64}} = \tfrac{1}{8}$

$\;\;\;\left(x^2\right)^{1/2} = x^{2 \cdot 1/2} = x^{1} = x$

$$\boxed{\;a^{1/2} = \tfrac{1}{8}\, x\;\Rightarrow\; k = \tfrac{1}{8},\; n = 1\;}$$

Rule: $(AB)^n = A^n B^n$. The exponent distributes across every factor — both the scalar and the $x$ part. Slip warning: $\sqrt{1/64} = 1/8$, not $1/32$ — square root of 64 is 8, not 32.
$a^{1/2} = \tfrac{1}{8}\, x \;\Rightarrow\; k = \tfrac{1}{8},\ n = 1$

Part (b) — $\dfrac{16}{b^3}$ [1 marks] · 2 steps

From (a): $a = \tfrac{x^2}{64}$, $b = \tfrac{16}{\sqrt{x}}$ (both expressed in $x$). Now substitute $b = \tfrac{16}{\sqrt{x}}$ into $\dfrac{16}{b^3}$ and simplify to $kx^n$ form.
STEP 1/2 M1

Strategy: the goal is $b^3$ in $kx^n$ form. First eliminate the surd notation by rewriting $\sqrt{x}$ as $x^{1/2}$ in the denominator (where it becomes $x^{-1/2}$), then cube.

Rewrite $b$:

$\;\;\;b = \dfrac{16}{\sqrt{x}} = \dfrac{16}{x^{1/2}} = 16\, x^{-1/2}$

Cube using $(AB)^n = A^n B^n$:

$\;\;\;b^3 = \left(16\, x^{-1/2}\right)^3 = 16^3 \cdot \left(x^{-1/2}\right)^3$

Simplify each factor:

$\;\;\;16^3 = 16 \cdot 16 \cdot 16 = 256 \cdot 16 = 4096$

$\;\;\;\left(x^{-1/2}\right)^3 = x^{(-1/2) \cdot 3} = x^{-3/2}$

$$\boxed{\;b^3 = 4096\, x^{-3/2}\;}$$

Phuc's slip: last time you cubed the $16$ but forgot to cube the $x^{-1/2}$. BOTH factors must be cubed; $(16 x^{-1/2})^3 \neq 4096\, x^{-1/2}$. Sign-of-exponent watch: $(-1/2) \cdot 3 = -3/2$, still negative — $b^3$ has a negative exponent because $\sqrt{x}$ was in the denominator.
$b^3 = 4096\, x^{-3/2}$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $b^3 = 4096\, x^{-3/2}$
STEP 2/2 A1

Strategy: divide $16$ by $b^3 = 4096\, x^{-3/2}$. Handle the scalar quotient and the $x$-power quotient separately; the negative exponent flips when moved.

Set up:

$\;\;\;\dfrac{16}{b^3} = \dfrac{16}{4096\, x^{-3/2}}$

Scalar part:

$\;\;\;\dfrac{16}{4096} = \dfrac{1}{256}$

Power part — flip the negative exponent (rule: $\tfrac{1}{x^{-n}} = x^{n}$):

$\;\;\;\dfrac{1}{x^{-3/2}} = x^{3/2}$

Combine:

$$\boxed{\;\dfrac{16}{b^3} = \tfrac{1}{256}\, x^{3/2}\;\Rightarrow\; k = \tfrac{1}{256},\; n = \tfrac{3}{2}\;}$$

Why the sign flips: $\dfrac{1}{x^{-3/2}} = x^{3/2}$. Dividing by a negative power = multiplying by the positive power. Sense-check: $4096 / 16 = 256$, so the scalar in the denominator ⇒ $1/256$ in the numerator-form. Decimal check: $1/256 \approx 0.00391$ — a small coefficient, matching the heavy denominator.
$\dfrac{16}{b^3} = \tfrac{1}{256}\, x^{3/2} \;\Rightarrow\; k = \tfrac{1}{256},\ n = \tfrac{3}{2}$

Part (c) — $\bigl(\tfrac{ab^3}{2}\bigr)^{-4/3}$ [2 marks] · 2 steps

From (b): $b^3 = 4096\, x^{-3/2}$. Given: $a = \tfrac{1}{64}x^2$.
STEP 1/2 M1

Strategy: first multiply $a \cdot b^3$, collecting like-base powers (using $x^m \cdot x^n = x^{m+n}$), then divide by $2$.

Multiply $a \cdot b^3$:

$\;\;\;ab^3 = \tfrac{1}{64}x^2 \cdot 4096\, x^{-3/2}$

Scalar part:

$\;\;\;\tfrac{1}{64} \cdot 4096 = \tfrac{4096}{64} = 64$

Power part — add exponents:

$\;\;\;x^2 \cdot x^{-3/2} = x^{2 + (-3/2)} = x^{4/2 - 3/2} = x^{1/2}$

Combine:

$\;\;\;ab^3 = 64\, x^{1/2}$

Divide by 2:

$$\boxed{\;\dfrac{ab^3}{2} = 32\, x^{1/2}\;}$$

Rule: add exponents when multiplying same-base powers: $x^m \cdot x^n = x^{m+n}$. Common-denominator slip: $2 - \tfrac{3}{2}$ is $\tfrac{4}{2} - \tfrac{3}{2} = \tfrac{1}{2}$ (not $\tfrac{1}{1}$). Sense-check: $\sqrt{x}$ ($x^{1/2}$) has the right exponent — multiplying by $\sqrt{x}$ undoes one of the $\sqrt{x}$'s removed when we squared.
$\dfrac{ab^3}{2} = 32\, x^{1/2}$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $\dfrac{ab^3}{2} = 32\, x^{1/2}$
STEP 2/2 A1

Strategy: apply the outer exponent $-4/3$ to both factors via $(AB)^n = A^n B^n$, then simplify $32^{-4/3}$ by writing $32 = 2^5$ and applying the power-of-a-power rule.

Distribute the outer exponent:

$\;\;\;\left(32\, x^{1/2}\right)^{-4/3} = 32^{-4/3} \cdot \left(x^{1/2}\right)^{-4/3}$

Power part:

$\;\;\;\left(x^{1/2}\right)^{-4/3} = x^{(1/2)(-4/3)} = x^{-4/6} = x^{-2/3}$

Scalar part — write $32 = 2^5$:

$\;\;\;32^{4/3} = (2^5)^{4/3} = 2^{5 \cdot 4/3} = 2^{20/3}$

Split $20/3 = 6 + 2/3$:

$\;\;\;2^{20/3} = 2^6 \cdot 2^{2/3} = 64 \cdot \sqrt[3]{2^2} = 64\sqrt[3]{4}$

So $32^{-4/3} = \dfrac{1}{64\sqrt[3]{4}}$.

Combine:

$$\boxed{\;\left(\tfrac{ab^3}{2}\right)^{-4/3} = \dfrac{1}{64\sqrt[3]{4}}\, x^{-2/3} \;\Rightarrow\; k = \tfrac{1}{64\sqrt[3]{4}},\; n = -\tfrac{2}{3}\;}$$

Negative outer exponent trap: $\left(A\right)^{-n} = \dfrac{1}{A^n}$ — invert and drop the sign. Cube-root mnemonic: $2^{2/3} = \sqrt[3]{2^2} = \sqrt[3]{4}$ (denominator of the fractional exponent ⇒ root index; numerator ⇒ inner power). Sanity-check via decimals: $\sqrt[3]{4} \approx 1.587$, so $64\sqrt[3]{4} \approx 101.6$, hence $k \approx 0.00984$ — small positive, consistent with raising $32$ to a negative power.
$\boxed{\;\dfrac{1}{64\sqrt[3]{4}}\, x^{-2/3}\;}$ $\;\Rightarrow\; k = \dfrac{1}{64\sqrt[3]{4}},\ n = -\tfrac{2}{3}$
