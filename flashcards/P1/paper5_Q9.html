Q9 — Curve: Tangent, Normal, Differentiation & Area

Question

A curve $C$ has equation $y = f(x)$. Points and tangent/normal lines are defined on $C$.

(a) Find a specific value or expression. (2 marks)

(b) Find another value or show a result. (3 marks)

(c)(i) Find the $x$-coordinate of a specific point. (2 marks)

(c)(ii) Find the equation of the normal line at that point. (3 marks)

(d) Find an area or further quantity. (4 marks)

[14 marks total]

Parts (a) & (b) — Finding values from the curve [5 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2Part (a): Substitute to find a value M1 A1

Strategy: Part (a) of curve questions typically asks you to find a specific value — this could be $f(a)$ for a given $a$, the value of an unknown constant in the curve equation, or the gradient at a particular point. The general approach is always the same: substitute the given information into the curve equation $y = f(x)$ and solve. If the question says "find the value of $k$" where $k$ appears in $f(x)$, you will be given a point $(x_0, y_0)$ on the curve and can set $f(x_0) = y_0$ to form an equation in $k$. If it says "find $f(a)$", simply substitute $x = a$ into the expression.

Working: Read the specific instruction carefully. If a point is given, substitute its coordinates into $y = f(x)$. For example, if $f(x) = x^3 + kx + c$ and the point $(2, 10)$ lies on the curve, then $10 = 8 + 2k + c$, giving one equation in the unknowns $k$ and $c$. You may need information from other parts or conditions (such as a tangent gradient) to get a second equation. Solve the resulting system of equations. Always simplify fully and check by substituting back into the original equation to verify the point lies on the curve.

Sense check: After finding the value, substitute it back into the curve equation and verify that the given point (or condition) is satisfied. If the question gives a "show that" instruction, your final line must match the given expression exactly — rearrange if necessary to match the target form.

STEP 2/2Part (b): Derive a further result M1 A1 A1

Strategy: Part (b) typically builds on part (a). Common patterns include: (i) using the constant found in (a) to write the full curve equation, then finding coordinates of a specific point (e.g., where the curve crosses the $x$-axis, or a stationary point); (ii) differentiating and setting $f'(x) = 0$ for turning points; or (iii) showing that a given expression equals something ("show that"). The approach depends on the specific instruction, but the underlying technique is always algebra and/or differentiation applied to the now-complete curve equation.

Working: If the task is to find where $f(x) = 0$, substitute the full expression from part (a) and solve the resulting polynomial equation — factorisation, the quadratic formula, or inspection may be needed. If the task is to find a stationary point, differentiate $f(x)$ to get $f'(x)$, set $f'(x) = 0$, and solve for $x$. Then find $y = f(x)$ at that $x$-value. Write the answer clearly as coordinates $(x, y)$ or as the requested expression. Keep exact values (surds, fractions) unless told otherwise.

Sense check: If you found a stationary point, check that $f'(x) = 0$ there by substituting back. If you found a root, check that $f(x) = 0$ by substituting back. These back-substitution checks take seconds and catch sign/arithmetic errors that would cost marks. Also verify the answer is consistent with the diagram (if provided) — a root should lie on the $x$-axis, a stationary point should be at a peak or trough.

Common trap: In "show that" questions, you must work from the given information to arrive at the stated result — do NOT start with the result and work backwards (this is not accepted by examiners as valid proof). Show every algebraic step clearly so the examiner can follow your reasoning.
Result for part (b) derived using the value from part (a).

Part (c) — Finding a point and the normal line [5 marks] · 3 steps

The full pipeline: find $x$ → find $y$ → differentiate → evaluate gradient → negative reciprocal → point–gradient form.
STEP 1/3Part (c)(i): Find the $x$-coordinate M1 A1

Strategy: Part (c)(i) asks for the $x$-coordinate of a specific point on the curve. This is typically found by solving an equation — for example, setting $f(x)$ equal to a given $y$-value, or setting $f'(x)$ equal to a given gradient, or finding where two curves intersect. The method depends on the specific question, but the algebra always reduces to solving a polynomial equation. If the polynomial is quadratic, use the quadratic formula or factorisation. If it is cubic, look for an obvious integer root first (by substituting $x = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots$), then factorise out the linear factor and solve the remaining quadratic.

Working: Set up the equation from the given condition. For example, if asked "find the $x$-coordinate where the gradient of $C$ is $8$", differentiate to get $f'(x)$ and solve $f'(x) = 8$. If asked "find where $C$ meets the line $y = 3x + 1$", set $f(x) = 3x + 1$ and solve the resulting equation. Simplify step by step, collect like terms, and solve. If there are multiple solutions, the question may specify which one (e.g., "the positive root" or "at point $P$ shown in the diagram").

Sense check: Substitute the found $x$-value back into the original equation or condition to verify it works. If the diagram shows the point in the first quadrant, the $x$-value should be positive. If the point is clearly to the right of the $y$-axis, a negative $x$ would indicate an error.

$x$-coordinate found by solving the relevant equation.

STEP 2/3Differentiate and find the tangent gradient M1

Strategy: To find the equation of the normal line at a point, we first need the gradient of the tangent at that point. The tangent gradient is found by differentiating $f(x)$ and then substituting the specific $x$-value from part (c)(i). This gives a number, not a function — a critical distinction that Phuc previously confused (Paper 1, Q4). The derivative $f'(x)$ is the gradient function; evaluating it at $x = a$ gives the gradient at that specific point, which is a single number $m_T = f'(a)$.

Working — differentiate:

Apply the power rule term by term. For each term $ax^n$, the derivative is $anx^{n-1}$. If the curve equation contains terms like $\sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}$ or $\frac{1}{x} = x^{-1}$, rewrite them as powers first, then differentiate. Common derivatives to know: $\frac{d}{dx}(x^n) = nx^{n-1}$; $\frac{d}{dx}(\sqrt{x}) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$; $\frac{d}{dx}(x^{-1}) = -x^{-2}$.

Evaluate at the point:

Substitute $x = a$ (from Step 1) into $f'(x)$. Compute each term carefully — pay attention to negative exponents and fractional powers. The result is the tangent gradient $m_T$.

In this specific question, Phuc reported: $m_T = f'(a) = 8$.

Sense check: The tangent gradient should match the visual slope of the curve at the point shown in the diagram. If the curve appears to be steeply rising, $m_T$ should be a large positive number. If it is gently falling, $m_T$ should be a small negative number. A gradient of $8$ means the curve is rising steeply (for every 1 unit right, it goes 8 units up), which should be consistent with the diagram.

Critical distinction (from Phuc's Paper 1 Q4 confusion): $\frac{dy}{dx}$ gives the gradient (a number after substituting $x$), NOT the equation of the tangent line. The tangent line is $y - y_1 = m_T(x - x_1)$. The normal line is $y - y_1 = m_N(x - x_1)$. Do not confuse the gradient with the line equation — they are different objects.
Tangent gradient: $m_T = f'(a) = 8$.
↳ from STEP 2: Tangent gradient $m_T = 8$ at the point $(a, f(a))$.
STEP 3/3Part (c)(ii): Normal line equation M1 A1

Strategy: The normal line is perpendicular to the tangent line at the point of tangency. Two perpendicular lines have gradients whose product is $-1$: if the tangent gradient is $m_T$, then the normal gradient is $m_N = -\frac{1}{m_T}$. This is the negative reciprocal rule. Once we have $m_N$ and the point $(a, f(a))$, we write the line equation using the point–gradient form $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$. This is a mechanical procedure — the only place errors creep in is sign mistakes in the negative reciprocal or forgetting to find $y_1 = f(a)$ before writing the equation.

Working — negative reciprocal:

From Step 2: $m_T = 8$.

$$m_N = -\frac{1}{m_T} = -\frac{1}{8}$$

Find the $y$-coordinate: Substitute $x = a$ into the curve equation to get $y_1 = f(a)$. This gives us the full point $(a, f(a))$.

Write the normal line:

$$y - f(a) = -\frac{1}{8}(x - a)$$

Expand and simplify if required by the question. Multiply both sides by $8$ to clear the fraction: $8y - 8f(a) = -(x - a)$, then $8y = -x + a + 8f(a)$, and finally $x + 8y = a + 8f(a)$. The exact form depends on what the mark scheme expects — usually either $y = mx + c$ or $ax + by + c = 0$.

Sense check: The normal line should pass through the point $(a, f(a))$ — verify by substituting. Its gradient $-\frac{1}{8}$ is shallow and negative, meaning it slopes gently downward from left to right. This is perpendicular to the steep tangent (gradient $8$). Visually, the normal should cross the tangent at right angles at the point $P$ — check against the diagram if available.

Phuc (from Slack, verbatim): "We substitute the x value into that derivative to find the gradient of the tangent line. Because the gradient of the tangent line equals the negative inverse of the gradient of the normal line, the gradient of the normal line is $-\frac{1}{8}$." This is correct reasoning — but note Phuc said "negative inverse" when the standard term is "negative reciprocal". Same thing mathematically, but using the standard terminology avoids confusion.
Answer (c)(ii): $\boxed{y - f(a) = -\tfrac{1}{8}(x - a)}$ — the equation of the normal line.

Part (d) — Area between the curve and a line [4 marks] · 3 steps

From earlier: curve equation $y = f(x)$, normal line equation, and key points.
STEP 1/3Find intersection points (limits of integration) M1

Strategy: To find the area between a curve and a line, we first need the $x$-values where they intersect — these become the limits of integration. We set the curve equation equal to the line equation and solve the resulting equation. For the area between the curve $y = f(x)$ and a straight line $y = mx + c$, we solve $f(x) = mx + c$. The solutions give us the points where the curve and line meet, and the area between them is the integral of the difference from the left intersection to the right intersection.

Working: Set $f(x) = $ (line equation). Rearrange to get all terms on one side: $f(x) - mx - c = 0$. This is typically a polynomial equation. If it is quadratic, use factorisation or the quadratic formula. If cubic, look for integer roots. One of the intersection points is often already known from part (c) — the point where the tangent or normal meets the curve. The other intersection point(s) must be found by solving the equation. If you can factor out the known root, the remaining factor gives the new intersection(s).

Sense check: There should be at least two intersection points to enclose an area. If you only find one, either the line is tangent to the curve (touching but not crossing) or you have made an error. Check the discriminant: if $\Delta > 0$, two distinct real roots; if $\Delta = 0$, one repeated root (tangent). For an area question, you need $\Delta > 0$ with two distinct roots. Verify that the intersection points are consistent with the diagram.

Exam technique: When the question says "find the area between the curve and the normal", always start by finding where they intersect. The intersection points are the limits. Skipping this step and guessing limits from the diagram is risky — the diagram may not be to scale.
Intersection points found: $x = \alpha$ and $x = \beta$ (limits of integration).
↳ from STEP 1: Limits are $x = \alpha$ and $x = \beta$.
STEP 2/3Set up the integral (upper − lower) M1

Strategy: The area between two curves (or a curve and a line) over an interval $[\alpha, \beta]$ is given by $\int_{\alpha}^{\beta} [\text{upper function} - \text{lower function}]\,dx$. We need to determine which function is "on top" (has larger $y$-values) throughout the interval. To do this, pick a test point $x_0$ between $\alpha$ and $\beta$ and evaluate both functions there. The one with the larger value is the upper function. If the functions cross within the interval (one is above for part and below for the rest), you must split the integral at the crossing point — but for typical exam questions, one function stays above the other throughout the interval.

Working: Let the curve be $y = f(x)$ and the line be $y = g(x)$. Choose $x_0 = \frac{\alpha + \beta}{2}$ (the midpoint) as a test value. Compute $f(x_0)$ and $g(x_0)$. If $f(x_0) > g(x_0)$, the curve is above the line, and the area is:

$$A = \int_{\alpha}^{\beta} [f(x) - g(x)]\,dx$$

If $g(x_0) > f(x_0)$, the line is above the curve, and the area is:

$$A = \int_{\alpha}^{\beta} [g(x) - f(x)]\,dx$$

The order matters because area is always positive. Taking "upper minus lower" ensures the integrand is non-negative throughout the interval, giving a positive area without needing to take absolute values. Write out the integrand explicitly: expand $f(x) - g(x)$ (or vice versa) and simplify before integrating — combining like terms first makes the integration much cleaner.

Sense check: The integrand (upper minus lower) should be non-negative throughout $[\alpha, \beta]$. If you evaluate it at the test point and get a negative number, you have the functions in the wrong order — swap them. At the endpoints $\alpha$ and $\beta$ (the intersection points), the integrand should equal zero (since the functions meet there).

Key rule: Area between curves = $\int_{\alpha}^{\beta} |f(x) - g(x)|\,dx$. In practice, determine the sign of $f(x) - g(x)$ on $(\alpha, \beta)$ and drop the absolute value accordingly. Never integrate blindly without checking which function is on top — a sign error here negates the entire area.
Integral set up: $A = \int_{\alpha}^{\beta} [\text{upper} - \text{lower}]\,dx$.
↳ from STEP 2: Integral set up with correct upper/lower functions and limits.
STEP 3/3Evaluate the definite integral A1 A1

Strategy: Now we perform the actual integration. The integrand from Step 2 is a polynomial (since we are subtracting a line from a polynomial curve, or vice versa). Each term is of the form $ax^n$ and integrates to $\frac{a}{n+1}x^{n+1}$. After finding the antiderivative, we evaluate it at the upper and lower limits and subtract, applying the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus: $\int_{\alpha}^{\beta} h(x)\,dx = H(\beta) - H(\alpha)$, where $H$ is any antiderivative of $h$. This is pure computation — the conceptual work was done in Steps 1 and 2.

Working — integrate term by term:

For each term in the integrand, apply the power rule:

$\displaystyle\int ax^n\,dx = \frac{a}{n+1}x^{n+1}$

Write out the full antiderivative $H(x)$ with square brackets and limits: $\bigl[H(x)\bigr]_{\alpha}^{\beta}$.

Evaluate at the limits:

Compute $H(\beta)$: substitute $x = \beta$ into every term and simplify.

Compute $H(\alpha)$: substitute $x = \alpha$ into every term and simplify.

Subtract: $H(\beta) - H(\alpha)$.

Simplify the result. If dealing with fractions, find a common denominator. If dealing with surds, simplify where possible. The final answer should be a positive number (since it represents an area). If it comes out negative, you likely had the upper/lower functions reversed in Step 2 — take the absolute value and make a note.

Sense check: The area should be positive and geometrically reasonable. Estimate the area visually from the diagram: if the enclosed region is roughly a triangle with base $b$ and height $h$, the area should be approximately $\frac{1}{2}bh$. Compare your exact answer with this rough estimate. Also check: is the answer in a "nice" form? Exam answers often simplify to clean fractions like $\frac{27}{4}$ or $\frac{125}{6}$ — messy decimals may indicate an error upstream.
