Q8 — Triangle Area in Composite Shape (Sine & Cosine Rule)

Question

A composite shape consists of a right triangle, a sector, and a general (non-right) triangle $ABC$. Given various measurements:

(a) Find the area of the sector. (2 marks)

(b) Find an angle using given information. (2 marks)

(c) Find the area of the right-angled part. (2 marks)

(d) Find the total area, requiring the area of triangle $ABC$ using the cosine rule then the sine area formula. (4 marks)

[10 marks total]

Part (a) — Sector area [2 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1Apply sector area formula M1 A1

Strategy: The area of a sector of a circle is given by $A_{\text{sector}} = \tfrac{1}{2}r^2\theta$, where $r$ is the radius and $\theta$ is the angle in radians. This is one of the most frequently tested formulas in P1 — it appears in nearly every paper that involves composite shapes or arc-length problems. The key prerequisite is that the angle must be in radians, not degrees. If the question gives the angle in degrees, you must convert first using $\theta_{\text{rad}} = \theta_{\text{deg}} \times \frac{\pi}{180}$. In this problem, the radius and angle of the sector are given directly (or can be read from the diagram), so we simply substitute into the formula.

Formula:

$$A_{\text{sector}} = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta$$

Working: Substitute the given values of $r$ and $\theta$ (in radians) directly into the formula. Compute $r^2$ first, then multiply by $\theta$, then divide by $2$. The calculation is straightforward arithmetic once the values are identified correctly. Always check that $\theta$ is in radians — if it is in degrees, the formula will give a completely wrong answer. A useful sanity check: the sector area should be a fraction of the full circle area $\pi r^2$, specifically the fraction $\frac{\theta}{2\pi}$.

Sense check: The sector area should be less than the area of the full circle ($\pi r^2$) and should be positive. If the answer comes out larger than $\pi r^2$ or negative, something has gone wrong — most likely an angle unit error. Also verify that the sector area is geometrically reasonable relative to the diagram.

Exam day reminder: Always check your calculator is in radians mode for sector/arc questions. This is the single most common careless error in composite shape problems. The formula $A = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta$ only works with $\theta$ in radians.
Answer (a): $\boxed{A_{\text{sector}} = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta}$ — substitute given values.

Parts (b) & (c) — Angle and right-triangle area [4 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2Find the required angle (part b) M1 A1

Strategy: Part (b) asks us to find an angle using information from the composite shape. In problems like these, angles often come from right-triangle trigonometry (using $\tan$, $\sin$, or $\cos$ with known sides) or from geometric relationships within the figure — for example, angles around a point summing to $360°$, or angles in a triangle summing to $180°$. The approach is to identify which sides or angles are already known, then select the appropriate trigonometric ratio or geometric property to find the missing angle.

Working: Using the given side lengths and the right angle in the right-angled part of the composite shape, we can apply basic trigonometry. If we know the opposite and adjacent sides relative to the unknown angle, we use $\tan^{-1}$. If we know the hypotenuse and one other side, we use $\sin^{-1}$ or $\cos^{-1}$. The calculation proceeds as follows: identify the relevant triangle, label the sides relative to the angle, apply the inverse trig function, and simplify. Always express the answer in the unit the question requires (radians or degrees).

Sense check: The angle should be between $0$ and $\frac{\pi}{2}$ (or $0°$ and $90°$) if it is an acute angle in a right triangle. If the composite shape has obtuse angles, check that the answer is geometrically consistent with the diagram. Compare visually with the diagram — does the angle "look right"?

Angle found using given information from the composite shape.
STEP 2/2Right-triangle area (part c) M1 A1

Strategy: The area of a right-angled triangle is the simplest case: $A = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}$, where the base and height are the two sides that form the right angle. This is a special case of the general SAS area formula $\frac{1}{2}ab\sin C$ with $C = 90°$ and $\sin 90° = 1$. In composite shape problems, the right triangle is usually the "easy" part — the challenge lies in correctly identifying which two sides are perpendicular and reading their lengths from the diagram or earlier calculations.

Working: From the diagram, identify the two sides adjacent to the right angle. These are the base and height. Multiply them together and divide by 2. If one of the sides was calculated in a previous part (for example, using the angle from part b), substitute that value here. The arithmetic is usually straightforward — the examiners are testing whether you can correctly identify the perpendicular sides, not whether you can do complicated multiplication.

Sense check: The right-triangle area should be a positive number, and it should be visually consistent with the size of the right-angled region in the diagram. If the right triangle takes up roughly a quarter of the total figure, its area should be roughly a quarter of the total area (which we'll compute in part d). This kind of rough proportionality check catches gross errors.

Key formula reminder: Right triangle: $A = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}$. General triangle (SAS): $A = \frac{1}{2}ab\sin C$. These are the "only two formulas" Phuc referenced — but do not forget the cosine rule and sine rule as computational tools to find missing sides/angles first.
Right-triangle area computed as $\frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}$.

Part (d) — Total area via cosine rule → sine area formula [4 marks] · 3 steps

From earlier parts: sector area (part a), angle (part b), right-triangle area (part c). Remaining piece: area of triangle $ABC$.
STEP 1/3Use cosine rule to find missing side $AC$ M1

Strategy: To find the area of triangle $ABC$ using the SAS area formula $\frac{1}{2} \cdot BC \cdot AC \cdot \sin(\angle BCA)$, we need two sides adjacent to a known angle. We know $BC$ (given) and $\angle BCA$ (found in earlier parts or derivable from the figure), but we do not know $AC$. This means we must first find $AC$ using another tool. The cosine rule is the appropriate choice here because we can form a triangle where we know one angle and the opposite side (or two sides and the included angle). The cosine rule states: $a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc\cos A$, where $a$ is the side opposite angle $A$.

Working — apply the cosine rule:

In triangle $ABC$, identify the known quantities: one angle (say $\angle BAC$ or $\angle ABC$) and the sides that are given or computed. Set up the cosine rule with $AC$ as the unknown:

$$AC^2 = AB^2 + BC^2 - 2 \cdot AB \cdot BC \cdot \cos(\angle ABC)$$

Substitute all known values. Compute $AB^2$, $BC^2$, and $2 \cdot AB \cdot BC \cdot \cos(\angle ABC)$ step by step. Take the square root of the result to find $AC$. Be careful with the sign of the cosine term — if the angle is obtuse, $\cos$ is negative, so the $-2bc\cos A$ term becomes positive (adding to $b^2 + c^2$).

Sense check: By the triangle inequality, $AC$ must be between $|AB - BC|$ and $AB + BC$. If the computed value falls outside this range, there is an error in the substitution or arithmetic. Also check that $AC$ is positive and geometrically consistent with the diagram.

STEP 2/3SAS area formula for triangle $ABC$ M1 A1

Strategy: Now that we have both sides adjacent to the known angle ($BC$ from the question and $AC$ from Step 1), we can apply the SAS (Side–Angle–Side) triangle area formula. This formula computes the area of any triangle — not just right-angled ones — given two sides and the included angle between them. The formula is $\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ab\sin C$, where $a$ and $b$ are the two known sides and $C$ is the angle between them. This is the standard approach whenever the triangle is not right-angled and you have this particular combination of data. It is arguably the most important single formula for P1 composite-shape questions.

Working:

$$A_{\triangle ABC} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot BC \cdot AC \cdot \sin(\angle BCA)$$

Substitute the known value of $BC$, the computed value of $AC$ from Step 1, and the angle $\angle BCA$ (in radians or degrees, matching your calculator mode). Compute each factor step by step: first $BC \times AC$, then multiply by $\sin(\angle BCA)$, then divide by $2$. If using radians, ensure your calculator is in radian mode; if degrees, ensure degree mode. A mismatch here is fatal — $\sin(1.2 \text{ rad}) \neq \sin(1.2°)$.

Sense check: The area of triangle $ABC$ should be positive and should be a reasonable fraction of the total composite shape area. If the triangle visually dominates the figure, its area should be the largest component. If it is a small sliver, the area should be correspondingly small. Also check: is $A_{\triangle ABC}$ less than $\frac{1}{2} \cdot BC \cdot AC$? It must be, since $\sin(\angle BCA) \leq 1$.

Phuc's recurring weakness (5th paper running): "Review formulas for triangles, there are only two formulas." The two Phuc refers to are: (1) $A = \frac{1}{2}ab\sin C$ (area) and (2) $a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc\cos A$ (cosine rule). But there is also a third tool: the sine rule $\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B}$, which is needed when you have an angle–opposite-side pair. These three formulas together form the complete triangle toolkit for P1.
$A_{\triangle ABC} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot BC \cdot AC \cdot \sin(\angle BCA)$
↳ from STEPS 1–2: All three component areas now known.
STEP 3/3Sum all parts for total area A1

Strategy: The total area of a composite shape is simply the sum of its non-overlapping parts. In this problem, the shape is divided into three distinct regions: (1) the right-angled triangle from part (c), (2) the sector from part (a), and (3) triangle $ABC$ from Step 2 above. Since these three regions tile the entire composite shape without overlapping, the total area is their arithmetic sum. This is the final assembly step — all the hard work (cosine rule, sine area formula, sector formula) has already been done. The only risk at this stage is arithmetic error or forgetting to include one of the parts.

Working:

$$A_{\text{total}} = A_{\text{sector}} + A_{\text{right}\;\triangle} + A_{\triangle ABC}$$

Substitute the values from parts (a), (c), and Step 2 of part (d). Add them together. If the question asks for an exact answer, keep surds and fractions; if it asks for a decimal to $n$ significant figures or decimal places, round only at the very end. A common exam mistake is to round intermediate values too early, leading to an inaccurate final answer — always carry at least 4 decimal places through intermediate steps.

Sense check: The total area should be larger than any individual component. It should also be geometrically plausible: if the composite shape fits roughly inside a rectangle of dimensions $w \times h$, the total area should be less than $w \times h$ (since the shape does not fill the entire rectangle). Estimate the bounding rectangle from the diagram and verify the answer is reasonable.

Decision tree for triangle problems (memorise this):
• Know 2 sides + included angle → area formula $\frac{1}{2}ab\sin C$ or cosine rule for 3rd side.
• Know 3 sides → cosine rule for an angle.
• Know angle + opposite side + another angle/side → sine rule.
• Need area but missing a side → use cosine/sine rule FIRST to find the side, THEN use area formula.
This decision tree covers every triangle scenario in P1.
Answer (d): $\boxed{A_{\text{total}} = A_{\text{sector}} + A_{\text{right}\;\triangle} + A_{\triangle ABC}}$
