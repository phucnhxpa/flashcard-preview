Packet Q5 / Official WME01 Q4 — moments and tilting

Question

From the official paper. A branch AB, of length \(1.5\ \mathrm m\), rests horizontally in equilibrium on two supports. The supports are at C and D, where \(AC=0.24\ \mathrm m\) and \(DB=0.36\ \mathrm m\). When a force of \(150\ \mathrm N\) is applied vertically upwards at B, the branch is on the point of tilting about C. When a force of \(225\ \mathrm N\) is applied vertically downwards at B, the branch is on the point of tilting about D. The branch is modelled as a non-uniform rod AB of weight \(W\) newtons. The distance from C to the centre of mass of the rod is \(x\) metres. Use the model to find (i) the value of \(W\), (ii) the value of \(x\). [8 marks jointly]

[Total: 8 marks]

Lesson status: COMPLETED WITH PROMPTING · Source class: official WME01/01 Jan 2023 (packet Q5)

Official command (i)–(ii) · Scenario 1 — moments about C8 marks jointly

Carry-in / given: On the point of tilting about C \(\Rightarrow R_D=0\); the \(150\ \mathrm{N}\) load is \(1.5-0.24=1.26\ \mathrm{m}\) from C.
STEP 1/1

Teaching visual — both limiting scenarios.

Exam trigger and stopping point: when you see a supported branch about to tilt, identify the zero-reaction support first and take moments about the remaining contact; the requested result is completed, so no further lesson work is required.

Strategy and why. At the instant the branch is on the point of tilting about C, contact at D is just lost, so \(R_D=0\). Choose C as the pivot: the reaction at C passes through the pivot and has zero perpendicular distance, while the vanished reaction at D contributes nothing. This choice removes both unknown reactions and leaves only the branch’s weight and the applied \(150\ \mathrm N\) load.

Definition before substitution. The moment of a force about a point is force multiplied by its perpendicular distance from that point. In limiting rotational equilibrium, total clockwise moment equals total anticlockwise moment about the chosen pivot. The branch’s weight \(W\) acts \(x\) metres from C, so its moment has magnitude \(Wx\). The load is at the far end, \(1.5-0.24=1.26\ \mathrm m\) from C.

Keeping opposite senses on opposite sides avoids a fragile signed sum: \[Wx=150(1.5-0.24)=150(1.26)=189.\] Label this equation (A), because the second tilting scenario creates another equation in the same two unknowns. Units also check: both sides are moments in \(\mathrm{N\,m}\), even though the later answer for \(W\) will be in newtons.

Equation (A): \(Wx=189\).

Check-back. The distance \(1.26\) is positive and less than the full \(1.5\ \mathrm m\), as geometry requires. With the eventual values \(W=300\ \mathrm N\) and \(x=0.63\ \mathrm m\), the left side is \(300(0.63)=189\ \mathrm{N\,m}\), exactly matching the load moment.

Evidence-grounded trap and transfer trigger. The recording supports moments about C here and D in the next scenario; an earlier-guide claim about pivot B was removed. A long signed equation previously encouraged a sign/distance error, so separate clockwise and anticlockwise moments. Do not include \(R_C\): a force through the pivot has zero moment, not an unknown moment. Whenever wording says “on the point of tilting”, trigger “identify the remaining contact pivot → set the other reaction to zero → measure every distance from that pivot → equate opposite moments”.

Official command (i)–(ii) · Scenario 2 — moments about D, then solve8 marks jointly

Carry-in / given: Carry-in: equation (A) \(Wx=189\); tilt about D \(\Rightarrow R_C=0\); the \(225\ \mathrm{N}\) load is \(0.36\ \mathrm{m}\) from D.
STEP 1/1

Strategy and geometry. In the second limiting case the branch tilts about D, so \(R_C=0\), and taking moments about D also removes \(R_D\). The supports are separated by \(1.5-0.24-0.36=0.90\ \mathrm m\). Since the weight acts \(x\) from C, its distance from D is \(0.90-x\). This subtraction is the difficult transition: \(x\) is measured from C, not from D.

Moment definition before numbers. Using force times perpendicular distance and clockwise moments equal anticlockwise moments about D gives \[225(0.36)=(1.5-0.36-0.24-x)W.\] Therefore \[81=(0.90-x)W,\] which is equation (B). The positive distance condition requires \(0<x<0.90\), a useful domain check on any solution.

Now use the labelled equations strategically. Dividing (A) by (B) cancels the common nonzero weight: \[\frac{Wx}{W(0.90-x)}=\frac{189}{81}=\frac73,\qquad \frac{x}{0.90-x}=\frac73.\]

Cross-multiply without dropping the bracket: \[3x=7(0.90-x)=6.3-7x,\qquad 10x=6.3,\qquad x=0.63\ \mathrm m.\]

Back-substitute into (A), not into an unlabelled line: \[W=\frac{189}{0.63}=300\ \mathrm N.\] Division is efficient here because it removes \(W\) immediately; substitution from (A) would also work but creates a reciprocal expression earlier and offers more opportunity for arithmetic error.

\(x=0.63\ \mathrm{m},\qquad W=300\ \mathrm{N}\).

Check-back in both scenarios. The candidate lies inside the required interval: \(0<0.63<0.90\), so \(0.90-x=0.27\ \mathrm m\). Equation (A) gives \(300(0.63)=189\); equation (B) gives \(300(0.27)=81=225(0.36)\). Both limiting moment balances and units agree.

Diagnosis, rejected route and transfer trigger. The live issue was a complicated signed equation causing a sign/distance error, not choosing pivot B; the recording shows D. Writing \(x-0.90\) would make a physical distance negative and flips the moment. The Student also began a wrong back-substitution value for \(W\), repaired live to \(300\ \mathrm N\). For two limiting-equilibrium equations sharing an unknown, trigger “label equations → inspect for cancellation → divide if safe → solve domain → back-substitute into both”.

Precision recall strip closed-book check

Carry-in: retain the original condition, exact values, and command word before starting any algebra.
STEP 1/1

Retrieval strategy. Redraw the two tilting diagrams separately before viewing the calculations. Mark C and D, the distances \(0.24\), \(0.36\), and \(1.5\), then place \(W\) at distance \(x\) from C. Separating scenarios prevents a reaction or load from one limiting case leaking into the other. The governing idea is that at impending tilt one support reaction is zero and the other support is the pivot.

Formula first. State moment equals force times perpendicular distance, followed by clockwise moments equals anticlockwise moments about the selected pivot. About C this gives \(Wx=150(1.26)=189\). About D, first convert the weight arm because \(x\) was measured from C: the C-to-D separation is \(1.5-0.24-0.36=0.90\), hence the arm is \(0.90-x\), giving \(W(0.90-x)=225(0.36)=81\). Naming the formula and origin of each arm makes the signs auditable.

Efficient elimination. Label the balances (A) and (B), then divide them to cancel \(W\): \(x/(0.90-x)=189/81=7/3\). Cross-multiplication gives \(3x=6.3-7x\), so \(x=0.63\ \mathrm m\); back-substitution gives \(W=189/0.63=300\ \mathrm N\). This is the full lesson-authorised route, completed with prompting, rather than an appendix-only continuation.

Checks and diagnosis. Verify \(x\) is between C and D and check both balances: \(300(0.63)=189\) and \(300(0.27)=81\). The evidence identifies the vulnerable move as a complicated signed equation and a repaired wrong value during back-substitution. It does not support the earlier claim that moments were first taken about B. Using opposite sides for opposite moment senses and measuring from the active pivot directly addresses the evidenced error.

Transfer and source discipline. On any beam, rod or branch “point of tilting” problem, trigger “zero the lifted support reaction → pivot at the remaining support → omit forces through the pivot → form one clean balance per scenario → eliminate → check geometry and both equations”.
