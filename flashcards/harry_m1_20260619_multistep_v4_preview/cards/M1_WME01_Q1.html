Packet Q2 / Official WME01 Q1 — train speed–time graph

Question

From the paper. A train travels along a straight horizontal track between two stations A and B. The train starts from rest at station A and accelerates uniformly for \(T\) seconds until it reaches a speed of \(20\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\). The train then travels at a constant speed of \(20\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\) for 3 minutes before decelerating uniformly until it comes to rest at station B. The magnitude of the acceleration of the train is twice the magnitude of the deceleration. (a) On the axes below, sketch a speed–time graph to illustrate the motion of the train as it moves from station A to station B. (3) Stations A and B are 4.8 km apart. (b) Find the value of \(T\). (5) (c) Find the acceleration of the train during the first \(T\) seconds of its motion. (2)

[10 marks]

Lesson status: COMPLETED WITH PROMPTING · Source class: official WME01/01 Jan 2023 (packet Q2)

Part (a) · the sketch3

Carry-in / given: The train starts from rest, accelerates uniformly for \(T\) seconds to \(20\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\), and the acceleration magnitude is twice the deceleration magnitude.
STEP 1/1

Teaching visual — speed–time structure.

Final check and stopping point: the requested train answer is completed, so no further work is required; verify the time and acceleration against the graph scales before leaving the question.

Definition and strategy. On a speed–time graph, gradient represents acceleration and horizontal width represents elapsed time. Draw the three physical phases in chronological order: a straight rise from rest to \(20\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\), a horizontal section at 20 for 180 seconds, then a straight fall to rest. Let the first duration be \(T\).

Use \(a=\Delta v/\Delta t\) to translate the acceleration ratio into a time ratio. The speed change has magnitude 20 on each ramp. Thus \(|a|=20/T\). If the deceleration lasts \(D\), then \(|d|=20/D\). The given \(|a|=2|d|\) gives \(20/T=2(20/D)\), so \(D=2T\). This is why the descending ramp must be twice as wide as the ascending ramp; “twice the deceleration” would describe the opposite ratio, but the paper says the acceleration magnitude is twice the deceleration magnitude.

The transition times are cumulative. The train reaches top speed at \(t=T\). The 180-second constant-speed phase ends at \(t=T+180\), not at \(t=180\). Adding the final duration \(2T\) places rest at \(t=T+180+2T=3T+180\).

Sketch vertices: \((0,0)\), \((T,20)\), \((T+180,20)\), and \((3T+180,0)\), joined by straight, horizontal, then straight segments.

Check-back. Both endpoint speeds are zero, the plateau is exactly 20, its width is exactly 180, and the final ramp is twice the first ramp’s width, so its gradient magnitude is half as large.

Trap and exam trigger. Equal-looking ramps contradict the stated magnitude ratio, while putting the plateau end at 180 loses the initial \(T\) seconds. The evidence supports only that a temporary peak label was corrected to the printed 20; it does not support naming an invented wrong value. When a motion graph gives equal speed changes but compares acceleration magnitudes, trigger \(\Delta t=\Delta v/|a|\), then label cumulative transition times.

Part (b) · find T5

Carry-in / given: Carry-in: total distance \(=4800\ \mathrm{m}\) (convert \(4.8\ \mathrm{km}\) first).
STEP 1/1

Formula and strategy. Distance travelled equals the area under a speed–time graph. Here the whole region is a trapezium, so use \(A=\tfrac12(a+b)h\), where \(a\) and \(b\) are the parallel horizontal sides and \(h\) is the vertical separation. This is quicker than splitting into two triangles and a rectangle, although that valid check is used below.

First make units compatible: \(4.8\ \mathrm{km}=4800\ \mathrm{m}\). The upper parallel side is the 180-second plateau. The lower side spans the total time from zero to \(3T+180\), and the height is \(20\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\). Therefore

\(4800=\tfrac12\bigl(180+(3T+180)\bigr)(20)\).

Combine inside the bracket before solving: \(180+3T+180=3T+360\), and \(\tfrac12\times20=10\). Hence \(4800=10(3T+360)\). Divide by 10: \(480=3T+360\). Subtract 360: \(120=3T\). Divide by positive three: \(T=40\ \mathrm{s}\). No inequality sign issue occurs, but preserving units shows that the unknown is a time.

\(T=40\ \mathrm{s}\), so the full journey time is \(3(40)+180=300\ \mathrm{s}\).

Independent check. The first triangle has area \(\tfrac12(40)(20)=400\ \mathrm{m}\); the rectangle has area \(180(20)=3600\ \mathrm{m}\); the final triangle has base \(80\) and area \(\tfrac12(80)(20)=800\ \mathrm{m}\). Their sum is \(400+3600+800=4800\ \mathrm{m}\), exactly the stated distance.

Rejected routes and trigger. Gradient finds acceleration, not total distance, so using it for part (b) answers the wrong quantity. Equating 4.8 directly to a seconds-times-metres-per-second area creates a factor-of-1000 error. In an exam, “total distance” with a speed–time graph triggers area; convert kilometres to metres, identify the correct parallel sides, solve, then check by decomposing the area.

Part (c) · initial acceleration2

Carry-in / given: Carry-in: peak speed \(20\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\), acceleration phase lasts \(T=40\ \mathrm{s}\).
STEP 1/1

Formula and strategy. Acceleration is the gradient of a velocity–time or speed–time segment: \(a=\Delta v/\Delta t\). Use the initial rising segment because the question asks for the initial acceleration and because its direction is represented by a positive slope. Carry in the exact duration \(T=40\ \mathrm{s}\) from part (b), rather than measuring a not-to-scale sketch.

At the beginning, \(v=0\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\) at \(t=0\). At the first transition, \(v=20\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\) at \(t=T=40\ \mathrm{s}\). Thus \(\Delta v=20-0=20\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\) and \(\Delta t=40-0=40\ \mathrm{s}\). Substitution gives \(a=(20-0)/(40-0)=20/40=0.5\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}\). The units follow from velocity change divided by time: \((\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}})/\mathrm{s}=\mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}\).

The sign is positive because speed increases on this segment. For comparison, the final ramp lasts \(2T=80\ \mathrm{s}\), so its signed gradient would be \((0-20)/80=-0.25\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}\). Its magnitude is \(0.25\), and \(0.5=2(0.25)\), exactly matching the condition that acceleration magnitude is twice deceleration magnitude.

The initial acceleration is exactly \(0.5\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}\).

Check-back. Applying \(v=u+at\) to the first phase gives \(v=0+(0.5)(40)=20\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\), recovering the plotted peak. The positive value and dimensions are therefore consistent.

Trap and exam trigger. Taking the gradient of the descending segment produces the deceleration, not the requested initial acceleration; dividing 20 by the total 300 seconds also mixes phases with different motion. When asked for acceleration from a graph, trigger: isolate the named straight segment, write endpoint coordinates, calculate signed \(\Delta v/\Delta t\), attach \(\mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}\), and check against any stated magnitude ratio.

Precision recall strip closed-book check

Carry-in: retain the original condition, exact values, and command word before starting any algebra.
STEP 1/1

Definition-first recall. For this question, the two graph facts to retrieve are: gradient \(=\Delta v/\Delta t\), and distance \(=\) area under a speed–time graph. Keep the printed peak \(20\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\), plateau duration 180 seconds, distance \(4800\ \mathrm{m}\), and relation \(|a|=2|d|\) visible. The solution strategy is shape and timing first, area second, gradient last.

Rebuild the hard transition logic. Equal speed changes of 20 mean time is inversely proportional to acceleration magnitude. Therefore the first ramp of duration \(T\) and the weaker deceleration give a final duration \(2T\). Cumulative times are \(T\), \(T+180\), and \(3T+180\). A label of 180 at the plateau’s right edge is rejected because 180 is a duration beginning after time \(T\), not an absolute clock reading.

Then write \(4800=\tfrac12[180+(3T+180)]20\), simplify to \(480=3T+360\), and obtain \(T=40\ \mathrm{s}\). Finally, \(a=20/40=0.5\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}\). These are official-question results completed with prompting in the lesson, not deferred reference additions.

Evidence boundary and diagnosis. The guide records coaching to use the trapezium formula, measure each duration from the correct transition, and convert kilometres before finding area. It also says only that a temporary peak label was corrected to 20; assigning a particular invented wrong label would exceed the evidence. Equal ramp widths fail because they imply equal acceleration magnitudes, directly contradicting the given two-to-one ratio.
Check-back. With \(T=40\), ramp times are 40 and 80 seconds. Areas are 400, 3600, and 800 metres, totalling 4800; gradients have magnitudes 0.5 and 0.25, whose ratio is two. Every datum is recovered.
Transferable trigger: speed–time graph plus total distance → convert units and use area; acceleration comparison → compare gradients or equal speed-change times; requested acceleration → use the relevant segment only.
