Standalone question

Using u=3+cos θ, show that ∫₀^(π/2) [sin 2θ / √(3+cos θ)] dθ = a√3+b, where a and b are constants. Show all working; the question says solutions relying entirely on calculator technology are not acceptable.

Concept visual - supports the stated method; it does not add a different question or solution.

Multi-step working

Step 1 - Use the identity and substitution

\(\sin2\theta=2\sin\theta\cos\theta,\quad du=-\sin\theta\,d\theta,\quad \cos\theta=u-3.\)

Step 2 - Transform limits

\(\theta:0\to\pi/2\quad\mapsto\quad u:4\to3.\)

Step 3 - Transform the integral

\(-2\int_4^3\frac{u-3}{\sqrt u}du=-2\int_4^3(u^{1/2}-3u^{-1/2})du.\)

Step 4 - Integrate

\(-2\left[\tfrac23u^{3/2}-6u^{1/2}\right]_4^3.\)

Step 5 - Evaluate

\(8\sqrt3-\tfrac{40}{3}.\)

Recall answer: a=8 and b=−40/3, so the integral is 8√3−40/3.
