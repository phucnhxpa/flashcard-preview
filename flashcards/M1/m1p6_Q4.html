Q4 — Lift / Elevator: Newton's Second Law with Passengers

Question

A lift has mass $250\,\text{kg}$ and is pulled upward by a vertical cable. The tension in the cable is $3616\,\text{N}$. Inside the lift are two passengers: Alan and Bhavana. The floor of the lift pushes Alan upward with a force of $565\,\text{N}$ and pushes Bhavana upward with a force of $226\,\text{N}$. The lift accelerates upward at a constant rate.

(a) By considering the forces on the lift only, find the acceleration of the lift.

(b) Find Alan's mass.

[6 marks]

Part (a) — Find the acceleration of the lift [4 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3identify forces on the lift only M1

Strategy: the question says "by considering the forces on the lift only." This is a crucial instruction — we must draw a free body diagram of the lift as our system, not the lift-plus-passengers system. The passengers are separate bodies that interact with the lift via Newton's Third Law (N3L).

Forces acting on the lift (upward positive):

• Cable tension $T = 3616\,\text{N}$ — acts upward on the lift, pulling it by the cable.

• Weight of lift $W = 250g = 250 \times 9.8 = 2450\,\text{N}$ — acts downward due to gravity.

• Reaction from Alan $= 565\,\text{N}$ downward — by N3L, if the lift floor pushes Alan UP with 565 N, then Alan pushes the lift floor DOWN with 565 N.

• Reaction from Bhavana $= 226\,\text{N}$ downward — same reasoning: Bhavana pushes the lift floor DOWN with 226 N.

This is the key conceptual step. Many students mistakenly include the weights of the passengers as forces on the lift. But the passengers' weights act on the passengers, not on the lift. What acts on the lift is the contact force — and by N3L, those contact forces act downward on the lift floor.

N3L trap: the floor pushes a person UP with force $R$. By Newton's Third Law, the person pushes the floor DOWN with force $R$. These are equal and opposite, but act on different bodies. For the lift's free body diagram, we only include the downward push from each passenger — not their weight.
Forces on lift: $T = 3616$ N (up), $250g = 2450$ N (down), $565$ N (down), $226$ N (down).
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: Forces on lift identified — cable tension up, weight and passenger reactions down.
STEP 2/3apply N2L to the lift M1 A1

Strategy: apply Newton's Second Law ($F = ma$) to the lift, taking upward as the positive direction. The net force equals the mass of the lift (only the lift, not the passengers) times the acceleration.

Newton's Second Law (upward positive):

$$T - 250g - 565 - 226 = 250a$$

Substitute $T = 3616$ and $g = 9.8$:

$$3616 - 2450 - 565 - 226 = 250a$$

Now compute the left side step by step:

$\;\;3616 - 2450 = 1166$

$\;\;1166 - 565 = 601$

$\;\;601 - 226 = 375$

$$375 = 250a$$

Notice why the mass in $F = ma$ is $250\,\text{kg}$, not the total mass of everyone inside the lift. We are applying N2L to the lift only — the passengers' masses appear implicitly through the 565 N and 226 N reaction forces, not through the mass term on the right side of the equation.

Common error: using the total system mass on the right-hand side. If you apply N2L to the whole system (lift + passengers), you would use total mass but then the internal contact forces (565 N, 226 N) would cancel and not appear. The question specifically asks to consider the lift only, so mass = 250 kg and passenger forces appear as external forces on the lift.
$375 = 250a$
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: $375 = 250a$.
STEP 3/3solve for acceleration A1

Strategy: divide both sides by 250 to isolate $a$.

$$a = \frac{375}{250} = 1.5\,\text{m/s}^2$$

$$\boxed{\;a = 1.5\,\text{m/s}^2\;}$$

The positive sign confirms the lift accelerates upward, which is consistent with the problem statement ("the lift accelerates upward at a constant rate").

Sense check: the acceleration $a = 1.5\,\text{m/s}^2$ is roughly $\tfrac{1}{6.5}$ of $g$. For an elevator, this is a realistic acceleration — elevators typically accelerate at $1$–$2\,\text{m/s}^2$. If we'd gotten something like $15\,\text{m/s}^2$ (greater than $g$), that would indicate an error. Also verify: net upward force $= 375\,\text{N}$, and $250 \times 1.5 = 375$ ✓. The units also work out: Newtons divided by kg gives m/s².

Part (b) — Find Alan's mass [2 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2N2L for Alan alone M1

Strategy: now draw a free body diagram for Alan as a separate system. Alan has mass $m$ (unknown), and he accelerates upward at $a = 1.5\,\text{m/s}^2$ (same as the lift, since he's inside it and everything moves together). The forces on Alan are:

• Normal reaction from the lift floor $R = 565\,\text{N}$ — acts upward on Alan.

• Weight $mg$ — acts downward on Alan.

Apply N2L to Alan (upward positive):

$$R - mg = ma$$

$$565 - m(9.8) = m(1.5)$$

$$565 = m(9.8 + 1.5)$$

$$565 = m \times 11.3$$

The key insight here is that when accelerating upward, the floor reaction $R$ exceeds the person's weight $mg$ — you feel "heavier" in an accelerating lift. The formula $R = m(g + a)$ captures this: apparent weight is $m(g + a)$ when accelerating upward. This is a standard result worth memorising for lift problems.

Lift problem template: for any person in an accelerating lift: $R - mg = ma \Rightarrow R = m(g + a)$ (accelerating up) or $R = m(g - a)$ (accelerating down). When $a$ is upward, $R > mg$ (feel heavier); when $a$ is downward, $R < mg$ (feel lighter). In free fall ($a = g$), $R = 0$ — weightlessness.
$565 = 11.3m$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $565 = 11.3m$.
STEP 2/2solve for Alan's mass A1

Strategy: divide both sides by $11.3$ to find $m$.

$$m = \frac{565}{11.3} = 50\,\text{kg}$$

$$\boxed{\;m = 50\,\text{kg}\;}$$

Sense check: $50\,\text{kg}$ is a reasonable mass for a person (a lighter adult or teenager). If Alan's weight is $50 \times 9.8 = 490\,\text{N}$, and the floor pushes him with $565\,\text{N}$, the excess $75\,\text{N}$ provides the upward acceleration: $75 / 50 = 1.5\,\text{m/s}^2$ ✓. We can also check Bhavana: $226 = m_B \times 11.3 \Rightarrow m_B = 20\,\text{kg}$ — plausible for a child.
