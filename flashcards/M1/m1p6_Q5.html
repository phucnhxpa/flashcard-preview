Q5 — Vertical Projectile: Speed Thresholds and Speed-Time Graph

Question

A ball is projected vertically upward with speed $29.4\,\text{m/s}$ from a point $A$ which is $19.6\,\text{m}$ above horizontal ground. The ball moves freely under gravity.

(a) Find the total distance travelled by the ball while its speed is less than $14.7\,\text{m/s}$.

(b) Find the total time for which the speed of the ball is greater than $29.4\,\text{m/s}$.

(c) Sketch a speed-time graph for the motion of the ball from the instant it is projected to the instant it hits the ground.

[11 marks]

Part (a) — Distance while speed $< 14.7\,\text{m/s}$ [4 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3find height where speed = 14.7 M1 A1

Strategy: "speed less than 14.7 m/s" means we need to find the range of positions where the ball's speed is below this threshold. For vertical motion under gravity, speed depends only on height (by the energy equation $v^2 = u^2 - 2gs$). So we first find the height above $A$ where the speed equals exactly $14.7\,\text{m/s}$.

SUVAT setup (taking upward as positive, origin at $A$):

$\;\;u = 29.4\,\text{m/s}$ (upward), $\;\;a = -9.8\,\text{m/s}^2$, $\;\;v = \pm 14.7\,\text{m/s}$

Use $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ (no time needed):

$$14.7^2 = 29.4^2 - 2(9.8)h$$

$$216.09 = 864.36 - 19.6h$$

$$19.6h = 864.36 - 216.09 = 648.27$$

$$h = \frac{648.27}{19.6} = 33.075\,\text{m above } A$$

Note that $v^2$ is the same whether $v = +14.7$ (going up) or $v = -14.7$ (coming down), so the ball has speed $14.7$ at the same height on both the upward and downward legs of the journey. This is a key symmetry property of free-fall projectile motion — speed at a given height is independent of direction.

Why speed depends on height only: by conservation of energy (or equivalently $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$), speed at a given height is the same going up and coming down. This means the ball crosses the "14.7 m/s threshold" at the same height twice — once on the way up, once on the way down.
Ball has speed $= 14.7\,\text{m/s}$ at $h = 33.075\,\text{m}$ above $A$.
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: speed $= 14.7$ at $h = 33.075\,\text{m}$ above $A$.
STEP 2/3find maximum height M1

Strategy: the ball's speed is less than 14.7 m/s between the height where it decelerates to 14.7 (on the way up) and the maximum height (where $v = 0$), and then on the way back down until it accelerates back to 14.7 m/s. So I need the maximum height to compute the distance within this "slow zone."

Maximum height: at the peak, $v = 0$:

$$0 = 29.4^2 - 2(9.8)H$$

$$H = \frac{29.4^2}{19.6} = \frac{864.36}{19.6} = 44.1\,\text{m above } A$$

The "slow zone" (speed $< 14.7$) extends from $h = 33.075\,\text{m}$ to $h = 44.1\,\text{m}$ above $A$. The ball travels through this zone twice: once going up (decelerating from 14.7 to 0) and once coming down (accelerating from 0 back to 14.7).

The one-way distance through the slow zone is:

$$44.1 - 33.075 = 11.025\,\text{m}$$

Visualise the journey: launch → speed decreases as ball rises → crosses 14.7 m/s threshold at 33.075 m → continues slowing → stops at 44.1 m → falls back → crosses 14.7 m/s threshold again at 33.075 m → continues accelerating downward. The ball spends its "slow phase" in the top portion of the trajectory, near the apex.
Max height $= 44.1\,\text{m}$ above $A$. Slow zone: $33.075$ to $44.1\,\text{m}$.
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: Slow zone from $33.075$ to $44.1\,\text{m}$; one-way distance $= 11.025\,\text{m}$.
STEP 3/3total distance in slow zone A1

Strategy: the ball traverses the slow zone twice (up and down), so double the one-way distance.

$$\text{Total distance} = 2 \times 11.025 = 22.05\,\text{m}$$

$$\boxed{\;\text{Distance} = 22.05\,\text{m}\;}$$

This can also be expressed as $\frac{441}{20}$ m or equivalently as $22.1$ m (to 3 s.f.).

Key insight — "distance while speed < X": this always means finding the zone (range of heights) where speed is below the threshold. Because of the up-down symmetry, the ball passes through this zone twice. The total distance is always $2 \times (\text{one-way distance through the zone})$. This type of question frequently appears in M1 exams — always sketch the trajectory and mark the threshold height to visualise the problem.

Part (b) — Time with speed $> 29.4\,\text{m/s}$ [4 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3when can speed exceed 29.4? M1

Strategy: the ball is launched at $29.4\,\text{m/s}$. During its upward journey, it decelerates — so speed is always $\leq 29.4$. On the way down, it accelerates. It returns to point $A$ with the same speed it was launched ($29.4\,\text{m/s}$, by symmetry). So the speed only exceeds $29.4\,\text{m/s}$ when the ball falls below point $A$ — i.e., in the final 19.6 m of descent to the ground.

Time to return to $A$: by symmetry of the up-down journey, the time to go up equals the time to come back down to $A$. Time to reach the peak: $v = u - gt \Rightarrow 0 = 29.4 - 9.8t \Rightarrow t = 3\,\text{s}$. So the ball returns to $A$ at $t_1 = 2 \times 3 = 6\,\text{s}$, travelling downward at $29.4\,\text{m/s}$.

From $t = 6\,\text{s}$ onward, the ball is below $A$ and its speed exceeds $29.4\,\text{m/s}$. We need to find when it hits the ground.

Critical concept — speed vs velocity: speed is the magnitude of velocity (always positive). The ball's speed at launch is $29.4\,\text{m/s}$. On the way up and back to $A$, speed ranges from $0$ to $29.4$. Speed exceeds $29.4$ only when the ball falls below $A$, gaining extra speed from the additional 19.6 m of fall. Phuc confused this — writing "29.4" with no working, not recognising that speed $> 29.4$ requires the ball to be below $A$.
Speed $> 29.4$ only when ball is below $A$. Ball returns to $A$ at $t = 6\,\text{s}$.
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: speed exceeds 29.4 after $t = 6\,\text{s}$ (below $A$). Ball descends 19.6 m to ground.
STEP 2/3time from A to ground M1 A1

Strategy: from $A$ (at $t = 6\,\text{s}$), the ball travels downward with initial speed $29.4\,\text{m/s}$ and must fall $19.6\,\text{m}$ to reach the ground. Use SUVAT with downward as positive for this phase.

SUVAT (downward positive, measuring from $A$):

$\;\;u = 29.4\,\text{m/s}$ (downward), $\;\;a = 9.8\,\text{m/s}^2$, $\;\;s = 19.6\,\text{m}$

$$s = ut + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$$

$$19.6 = 29.4t + \tfrac{1}{2}(9.8)t^2$$

$$19.6 = 29.4t + 4.9t^2$$

$$4.9t^2 + 29.4t - 19.6 = 0$$

Divide through by $4.9$:

$$t^2 + 6t - 4 = 0$$

Using the quadratic formula:

$$t = \frac{-6 \pm \sqrt{36 + 16}}{2} = \frac{-6 \pm \sqrt{52}}{2} = \frac{-6 \pm 2\sqrt{13}}{2} = -3 \pm \sqrt{13}$$

Since $t > 0$: $t = -3 + \sqrt{13} \approx -3 + 3.606 = 0.606\,\text{s}$.

Quadratic check: the negative root $t = -3 - \sqrt{13} \approx -6.6$ is physically meaningless (negative time). The positive root $t \approx 0.606\,\text{s}$ is the time from leaving $A$ to hitting the ground. This is a short time, which makes sense — the ball is already travelling fast (29.4 m/s) and only needs to cover 19.6 m.
Time from $A$ to ground $= (\sqrt{13} - 3) \approx 0.606\,\text{s}$.
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: time from $A$ to ground $\approx 0.606\,\text{s}$.
STEP 3/3state total time with speed > 29.4 A1

Strategy: the ball's speed exceeds $29.4\,\text{m/s}$ from the moment it passes $A$ (going down) until it hits the ground. This is exactly the time interval computed in STEP 2.

$$\text{Time with speed} > 29.4 = \sqrt{13} - 3 \approx 0.606\,\text{s}$$

$$\boxed{\;\text{Time} = (\sqrt{13} - 3)\,\text{s} \approx 0.61\,\text{s}\;}$$

We can verify by computing the speed at ground level: $v = 29.4 + 9.8 \times 0.606 \approx 29.4 + 5.94 = 35.3\,\text{m/s}$. Alternatively using $v^2 = 29.4^2 + 2(9.8)(19.6) = 864.36 + 384.16 = 1248.52$, so $v \approx 35.3\,\text{m/s}$, confirming speed $> 29.4$ throughout this phase ✓.

Speed at any height $h$ above ground is found from $v^2 = u^2 + 2g(\text{height dropped from peak})$ — this makes the speed-position relationship immediately clear.
$\boxed{\,\text{Time with speed} > 29.4 = (\sqrt{13} - 3) \approx 0.61\,\text{s}\,}$

Part (c) — Speed-time graph [3 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2identify key points on the graph M1 A1

Strategy: a speed-time graph plots speed (always $\geq 0$) against time. For a vertical projectile under gravity, the velocity-time graph is a straight line (constant acceleration). The speed-time graph is the absolute value of the velocity — it forms a V-shape at the turning point.

Key points to plot:

• $t = 0$: speed $= 29.4\,\text{m/s}$ (launched upward).

• $t = 3\,\text{s}$: speed $= 0$ (at peak — momentarily at rest). The velocity-time line crosses zero here.

• $t = 6\,\text{s}$: speed $= 29.4\,\text{m/s}$ (back at $A$, now moving downward). By the symmetry of the motion, the speed at $t = 6$ equals the speed at $t = 0$.

• $t = 6 + (\sqrt{13} - 3) = 3 + \sqrt{13} \approx 6.61\,\text{s}$: speed $\approx 35.3\,\text{m/s}$ (hits the ground).

Shape of the graph: from $t = 0$ to $t = 3$, speed decreases linearly (constant deceleration). From $t = 3$ to $t = 6.61$, speed increases linearly (constant acceleration due to gravity). The graph is a V-shape with the minimum at $t = 3$, but it's not symmetric — it continues past the 29.4 mark on the right because the ball falls below $A$ before hitting the ground.

Speed vs velocity graph: the velocity-time graph is a single straight line with gradient $-9.8$: it starts at $+29.4$, passes through zero at $t = 3$, and goes to $-35.3$ at ground impact. The speed-time graph is the reflection of the negative portion — it bounces back up from zero, forming the V-shape. The gradient is $9.8$ in magnitude throughout, but the speed graph has a cusp (sharp point) at $t = 3$.
V-shape: $(0, 29.4) \to (3, 0) \to (6, 29.4) \to (6.61, 35.3)$.
