Q2 — Rod AB with Two Strings: Largest Possible Mass at B

Question

A uniform rod $AB$ has length $2a$ and mass $M$. The rod is held in a horizontal position by two vertical strings attached to the rod at points $C$ and $D$, where $AC = \tfrac{2a}{5}$ and $DB = \tfrac{3a}{5}$.

A particle $P$ of mass $m$ is placed on the rod at $B$.

(a) Find, in terms of $M$, the largest possible value of $m$.

(b) Given that $m = \tfrac{M}{2}$, find, in terms of $M$ and $g$, the tension $T_C$ in the string at $C$.

[9 marks]

Part (a) — Largest possible $m$ [5 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3identify limiting condition M1

Strategy: the question asks for the largest possible value of $m$. This is the key phrase that triggers a specific exam technique: at the limiting case, one of the supporting strings is about to go slack — meaning its tension drops to zero. The question is: which string goes slack?

Physical reasoning: the particle $P$ sits at end $B$, which is to the right of both strings. Adding more mass at $B$ increases the clockwise moment about any point. The string at $D$ (closer to $B$) resists this rotation. As $m$ increases, string $C$ (further from $B$) bears less load — eventually $T_C \to 0$.

Wait — actually, let's think more carefully. The string at $D$ is between the weight $Mg$ and the load $mg$. Taking moments about $C$: increasing $m$ increases the clockwise moment from $mg$ at $B$. The string $D$ must provide more anticlockwise moment. But if we take moments about $D$: increasing $m$ increases the clockwise moment from $mg$ at $B$, and $T_C$ must provide an anticlockwise moment. Since $T_C$ cannot be negative (strings can't push), the limit is $T_C = 0$.

Actually, let me reconsider. Taking moments about $C$: $Mg$ acts at centre (distance $\tfrac{3a}{5}$ right of $C$), $mg$ acts at $B$ (distance $\tfrac{8a}{5}$ right of $C$), and $T_D$ acts at $D$ (distance $a$ right of $C$). All produce clockwise torques except $T_D$. Wait — $T_D$ acts upward at $D$ which is to the right of $C$, so $T_D$ produces anticlockwise moment about $C$. No, $T_D$ acts upward, and $D$ is to the right of $C$, so looking at the rod: taking moments about $C$, $T_D$ acts at distance $a$ to the right and pulls up — that's anticlockwise (counterclockwise). The weights pull down to the right — that's clockwise.

Now taking moments about $D$: $T_C$ at distance $a$ left pulls up = anticlockwise. $Mg$ at distance $\tfrac{2a}{5}$ left pulls down = clockwise. Wait, the centre of the rod is at distance $a$ from $A$. $D$ is at $\tfrac{7a}{5}$ from $A$. So centre is $\tfrac{7a}{5} - a = \tfrac{2a}{5}$ to the left of $D$. And $B$ is $\tfrac{3a}{5}$ to the right of $D$.

Taking moments about $D$: clockwise = $mg \cdot \tfrac{3a}{5}$. Anticlockwise = $T_C \cdot a + Mg \cdot \tfrac{2a}{5}$? No — $Mg$ at centre is to the LEFT of $D$, pulling down. That would be anticlockwise about $D$ (counterclockwise). So: $T_C \cdot a + Mg \cdot \tfrac{2a}{5} = mg \cdot \tfrac{3a}{5}$. For the largest $m$, $T_C = 0$.

Phuc's note (verbatim): "espectially understnd a, largest or smallest possible mass = just equal each other." This is the critical insight: at the maximum mass, one tension just equals zero — the system is at the point of tipping. This is limiting equilibrium applied to a supported rod.

$$\boxed{\text{At largest } m: \; T_C = 0}$$

Limiting condition: $T_C = 0$ (string at $C$ about to go slack).
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: At the limit, $T_C = 0$.
STEP 2/3compute distances from pivot D B1

Strategy: with $T_C = 0$, the only upward force is $T_D$, so I take moments about $D$ to eliminate $T_D$. I need every distance measured from $D$.

Positions from $A$:

• $A$ is at position $0$.

• $C$ is at $\tfrac{2a}{5}$ from $A$.

• Centre of rod is at $a$ from $A$ (midpoint of rod of length $2a$).

• $D$ is at $2a - \tfrac{3a}{5} = \tfrac{7a}{5}$ from $A$.

• $B$ is at $2a$ from $A$.

Distances from $D$:

• Centre of rod to $D$: $\tfrac{7a}{5} - a = \tfrac{2a}{5}$ (centre is LEFT of $D$).

• $B$ to $D$: $2a - \tfrac{7a}{5} = \tfrac{3a}{5}$ ($B$ is RIGHT of $D$).

Moment directions about $D$:

• $Mg$ at centre: LEFT of $D$, pulling down → anticlockwise moment = $Mg \cdot \tfrac{2a}{5}$.

• $mg$ at $B$: RIGHT of $D$, pulling down → clockwise moment = $mg \cdot \tfrac{3a}{5}$.

Phuc's error: used moment arm $\tfrac{2}{5}a$ for the particle at $B$ about $C$, when it should have been $\tfrac{8}{5}a$. The fix: always write out ALL positions from $A$ first, then subtract to get distances from the chosen pivot. This eliminates sign/direction errors.
Distances from $D$: centre = $\tfrac{2a}{5}$ (left), $B$ = $\tfrac{3a}{5}$ (right).
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: $Mg$ at $\tfrac{2a}{5}$ left of $D$; $mg$ at $\tfrac{3a}{5}$ right of $D$. $T_C = 0$.
STEP 3/3moments about D → solve for m M1 A1 A1

Strategy: take moments about $D$, set clockwise = anticlockwise (equilibrium), and solve for $m$.

Moments about $D$:

$$\text{Clockwise} = \text{Anticlockwise}$$

$$mg \cdot \frac{3a}{5} = Mg \cdot \frac{2a}{5}$$

Cancel $g$ and $a$ (both nonzero):

$$m \cdot \frac{3}{5} = M \cdot \frac{2}{5}$$

$$m = \frac{2M}{3}$$

$$\boxed{\;m = \frac{2M}{3}\;}$$

Sense check: $m = \tfrac{2M}{3} < M$. The particle at the end $B$ is lighter than the rod, which makes sense — a uniform rod has its weight distributed, so even a moderately heavy particle at the extreme end can tip it. If the rod were weightless, ANY $m > 0$ would tip it, so $m = \tfrac{2}{3}M$ being a finite fraction of $M$ is reasonable. Also: $\tfrac{2}{3}$ is the ratio of the left arm ($\tfrac{2a}{5}$) to the right arm ($\tfrac{3a}{5}$) of the lever about $D$ — this is exactly the lever principle $m_1 d_1 = m_2 d_2$ ✓.
$\boxed{\,m = \dfrac{2M}{3}\,}$

Part (b) — Find $T_C$ when $m = \tfrac{M}{2}$ [4 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2moments about D to find $T_C$ M1 A1

Strategy: now $m = \tfrac{M}{2}$ (which is less than $\tfrac{2M}{3}$, so the system is in equilibrium with both strings taut). Take moments about $D$ to eliminate $T_D$ and solve directly for $T_C$.

Distances from $D$: (from Part (a)) $C$ is at distance $\tfrac{7a}{5} - \tfrac{2a}{5} = a$ to the LEFT of $D$. Centre of rod is $\tfrac{2a}{5}$ LEFT. $B$ is $\tfrac{3a}{5}$ RIGHT.

Moments about $D$ (clockwise = anticlockwise):

$$T_C \cdot a + Mg \cdot \frac{2a}{5} = mg \cdot \frac{3a}{5}$$

Wait — I need to check directions. $T_C$ pulls UP at $C$, which is LEFT of $D$: that gives clockwise moment about $D$. $Mg$ pulls DOWN at the centre, which is LEFT of $D$: that gives anticlockwise moment. $mg$ pulls DOWN at $B$, which is RIGHT of $D$: that gives clockwise moment.

So: clockwise = anticlockwise gives:

$$T_C \cdot a + mg \cdot \frac{3a}{5} = Mg \cdot \frac{2a}{5}$$

Hmm, that would give $T_C$ negative for $m$ large enough. Let me reconsider. Actually with $m = M/2$:

$$T_C \cdot a = Mg \cdot \frac{2a}{5} - \frac{M}{2}g \cdot \frac{3a}{5}$$

$$T_C \cdot a = \frac{2Mga}{5} - \frac{3Mga}{10}$$

$$T_C \cdot a = \frac{4Mga - 3Mga}{10} = \frac{Mga}{10}$$

$T_C \cdot a = \dfrac{Mga}{10}$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $T_C \cdot a = \tfrac{Mga}{10}$.
STEP 2/2simplify for $T_C$ A1 A1

Strategy: cancel the common factor $a$ from both sides (since $a \neq 0$) and read off $T_C$.

$$T_C = \frac{Mg}{10}$$

$$\boxed{\;T_C = \frac{Mg}{10}\;}$$

Sense check: with $m = M/2$ (which is less than the maximum $2M/3$), both strings should be taut, i.e. $T_C > 0$ ✓. Also $T_C = Mg/10$ is a small fraction of the total weight — this makes sense because $D$ carries most of the load (it's between the two downward forces). We can verify: resolving vertically, $T_C + T_D = Mg + \tfrac{M}{2}g = \tfrac{3Mg}{2}$, so $T_D = \tfrac{3Mg}{2} - \tfrac{Mg}{10} = \tfrac{14Mg}{10} = \tfrac{7Mg}{5}$. Check: moments about $C$ should balance too: $T_D \cdot a = Mg \cdot \tfrac{3a}{5} + \tfrac{M}{2}g \cdot \tfrac{8a}{5} = \tfrac{3Mga}{5} + \tfrac{4Mga}{5} = \tfrac{7Mga}{5}$, so $T_D = \tfrac{7Mg}{5}$ ✓.
Key exam technique: always measure distances from the pivot point. Draw the rod with positions of all forces marked from one end ($A$), then subtract to get distances from the pivot. This prevents the moment-arm error Phuc made in the exam.
$\boxed{\,T_C = \dfrac{Mg}{10}\,}$
