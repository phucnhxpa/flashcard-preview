Q7 — Car ($1200$ kg) tows trailer ($600$ kg) up incline, $\sin\alpha = 1/12$

Question

A car ($1200$ kg) tows a trailer ($600$ kg) up a slope of inclination $\alpha$ where $\sin\alpha = 1/12$. Driving force $3000$ N; acceleration $0.75$ m/s$^2$. Resistance: car experiences $2R$ N, trailer experiences $R$ N. Take $g = 9.8\,\text{m/s}^2$.

(a) Show $R = 60$. [4]

(b) Find the tension $T$ in the tow bar. [3]

(c) Tow bar breaks when speed is $12$ m/s. Find the distance the trailer moves before first coming to rest. [4]

[11 marks]

Part (a) — Show $R = 60$ [4 marks] · 1 step

STEP 1/1 M1 A1 A1 A1

Strategy: treat car + trailer as one system. The internal tension in the tow bar cancels (Newton’s 3rd law), so we only see external forces: drive, gravity component, and total resistance.

Formula (Newton’s 2nd law on the system, up-slope positive): $F_{\text{drive}} - 3R - Mg\sin\alpha = Ma$.

List system quantities:

$\;\;\bullet$ Total mass $M = 1200 + 600 = 1800$ kg

$\;\;\bullet$ Total resistance $= 2R + R = 3R$

$\;\;\bullet$ $\sin\alpha = 1/12$, $g = 9.8$, $a = 0.75$, $F_{\text{drive}} = 3000$

Compute the gravity component:

$\;\;Mg\sin\alpha = 1800 \cdot 9.8 \cdot \dfrac{1}{12} = \dfrac{1800}{12} \cdot 9.8 = 150 \cdot 9.8 = 1470$ N

Compute $Ma$:

$\;\;Ma = 1800 \cdot 0.75 = 1350$ N

Substitute into N2L:

$\;\;3000 - 3R - 1470 = 1350$

Rearrange:

$\;\;3R = 3000 - 1470 - 1350 = 180$

Divide by $3$:

$\;\;R = 60$

$$\boxed{\;R = 60\ \text{N}\;}\quad\blacksquare$$

System trick: by treating both vehicles as one body, the unknown tension $T$ disappears (it\u2019s internal). This is the cleanest way to find $R$. We\u2019ll need a separate single-body N2L for the trailer in part (b) to recover $T$.
$\boxed{\,R = 60\ \text{N}\,}$ ✓

Part (b) — Tension in tow bar [3 marks] · 1 step

From (a): $R = 60$. Trailer alone (mass $600$, resistance $R = 60$).
STEP 1/1 M1 A1 A1

Strategy: isolate the trailer to expose the tow-bar tension $T$ as an external force on it. Apply N2L along the slope using the trailer’s mass alone.

Formula (N2L for trailer, up-slope positive): $T - R - 600\,g\sin\alpha = 600\,a$.

Forces on trailer along slope:

$\;\;\bullet$ Tension $T$ pulling up the slope (from the tow bar)

$\;\;\bullet$ Weight component $600g\sin\alpha$ down the slope

$\;\;\bullet$ Resistance $R$ acting down the slope (opposes motion)

Compute weight component (using $R = 60$ from (a)):

$\;\;600g\sin\alpha = 600 \cdot 9.8 \cdot \dfrac{1}{12} = 50 \cdot 9.8 = 490$ N

Compute $600a$:

$\;\;600 \cdot 0.75 = 450$ N

Substitute into N2L:

$\;\;T - 60 - 490 = 450$

$\;\;T - 550 = 450$

Add $550$:

$\;\;T = 1000$

$$\boxed{\;T = 1000\ \text{N}\;}$$

Single-body trick: applying N2L to the trailer alone makes $T$ appear as an external force. If you tried this on the car alone, you\u2019d also get $T$ but with opposite sign \u2014 either approach works, but the trailer is easier (smaller mass, fewer forces).
$\boxed{\,T = 1000\ \text{N}\,}$

Part (c) — Distance after tow bar breaks [4 marks] · 2 steps

From (a): $R = 60$ N. At break: trailer speed $12$ m/s up slope. After break: $T = 0$. Both gravity (component) and resistance oppose motion.
STEP 1/2 M1 A1

Strategy: after the tow bar breaks, the trailer is on its own with $T = 0$. Both gravity and resistance now decelerate it. Apply N2L to find the new acceleration.

Formula (N2L on trailer, up-slope positive): $-600g\sin\alpha - R = 600\,a'$.

Forces on the trailer (still moving up the slope at the moment of break):

$\;\;\bullet$ Weight component $600g\sin\alpha$ pulls it down the slope (negative)

$\;\;\bullet$ Resistance $R = 60$ N opposes motion (motion is up-slope \u2192 resistance acts down-slope, negative)

Substitute $600g\sin\alpha = 490$ and $R = 60$:

$\;\;-490 - 60 = 600\,a'$

$\;\;-550 = 600\,a'$

Divide by $600$:

$\;\;a' = -\dfrac{550}{600} = -\dfrac{11}{12}$

$$\boxed{\;a' = -\dfrac{11}{12}\ \text{m/s}^2\;}$$

Resistance direction: resistance always opposes motion. The trailer is still moving up the slope when the bar breaks, so resistance pulls down-slope. After the trailer eventually stops and (if applicable) starts sliding back, the resistance flips direction \u2014 but we only need the deceleration phase here.
$a' = -11/12\ \text{m/s}^2$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $a = -11/12$ m/s$^2$ (deceleration after break).
STEP 2/2 M1 A1

Strategy: use the suvat that links $v$, $u$, $a$, $s$ (no $t$ needed). The trailer decelerates from $12$ m/s to $0$ — solve for $s$.

Formula (suvat): $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$.

List values: $u = 12$, $v = 0$, $a = -11/12$, find $s$.

Substitute:

$\;\;0^2 = 12^2 + 2\!\left(-\dfrac{11}{12}\right)s$

$\;\;0 = 144 - \dfrac{11}{6}s$

Rearrange:

$\;\;\dfrac{11}{6}s = 144$

Multiply both sides by $6/11$:

$\;\;s = \dfrac{144 \cdot 6}{11} = \dfrac{864}{11}$

$\;\;s \approx 78.5$ m

$$\boxed{\;s = \dfrac{864}{11} \approx 78.5\ \text{m}\;}$$

Trap: after the tow bar breaks, the trailer\u2019s weight component still pulls down the slope \u2014 it\u2019s NOT just resistance acting. Both contribute to the deceleration. Forgetting gravity gives a far smaller $|a|$ and overshoots the distance.
$\boxed{\,s = 864/11 \approx 78.5\ \text{m}\,}$
