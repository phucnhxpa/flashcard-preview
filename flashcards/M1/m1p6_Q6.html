Q6 — Direction Vector as a Force Constraint

Question

A particle $A$ of mass $0.5\,\text{kg}$ is initially at rest on a smooth horizontal surface. Two forces $\mathbf{F}_1 = (-3\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j})\,\text{N}$ and $\mathbf{F}_2 = (p\mathbf{i} + q\mathbf{j})\,\text{N}$ act on $A$.

(a) Given that $A$ moves in the direction of the vector $(\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j})$, show that $2p + q - 4 = 0$.

(b) Given also that $p = 5$, find the speed of $A$ at time $t = 4\,\text{s}$.

[7 marks]

Part (a) — Show $2p + q - 4 = 0$ [3 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3find resultant force M1

Strategy: the particle moves in a specified direction, which means the resultant force must point in that direction (since by N2L, $\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$, and the acceleration — and hence the force — must be parallel to the direction of motion if the particle starts from rest). The first step is to find the resultant force vector.

Resultant force:

$$\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{F}_1 + \mathbf{F}_2 = (-3 + p)\mathbf{i} + (2 + q)\mathbf{j}$$

So the resultant has $\mathbf{i}$-component $(p - 3)$ and $\mathbf{j}$-component $(q + 2)$.

Why does "moves in direction of $(\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j})$" constrain the force? If a particle starts from rest and accelerates, it moves in the direction of its acceleration vector. Since $\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{R}/m$, the acceleration is parallel to $\mathbf{R}$. Therefore $\mathbf{R}$ must be parallel to $(\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j})$. Two vectors are parallel if and only if one is a scalar multiple of the other, which is equivalent to their components being in the same ratio.

Phuc's insight (verbatim): "The direction vector is the clue — we use that direction vector as the information to find the two [unknowns]." This is exactly right: the direction of motion gives us a constraint equation linking $p$ and $q$.
Resultant: $\mathbf{R} = (p-3)\mathbf{i} + (q+2)\mathbf{j}$. Must be parallel to $(\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j})$.
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $\mathbf{R} = (p-3)\mathbf{i} + (q+2)\mathbf{j}$ is parallel to $(\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j})$.
STEP 2/3set up the parallel condition M1

Strategy: two vectors $\mathbf{R} = R_1\mathbf{i} + R_2\mathbf{j}$ and $\mathbf{d} = d_1\mathbf{i} + d_2\mathbf{j}$ are parallel if and only if:

$$\frac{R_2}{R_1} = \frac{d_2}{d_1}$$

This is equivalent to $R_1 d_2 = R_2 d_1$ (the cross-product condition — if the 2D "cross product" is zero, the vectors are parallel).

Apply to our vectors:

$\mathbf{R} = (p-3)\mathbf{i} + (q+2)\mathbf{j}$ and $\mathbf{d} = \mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j}$, so $d_1 = 1$, $d_2 = -2$.

$$\frac{q + 2}{p - 3} = \frac{-2}{1} = -2$$

This gives us: $q + 2 = -2(p - 3)$.

Alternatively, using the cross-product form: $(p-3)(-2) - (q+2)(1) = 0$, which gives $-2(p-3) - (q+2) = 0$, i.e. $-2p + 6 - q - 2 = 0$, so $-2p - q + 4 = 0$, i.e. $2p + q - 4 = 0$. Both methods are equivalent — choose whichever feels more natural.

Two methods for parallel vectors: (1) Ratio method: set $R_j / R_i = d_j / d_i$. This is intuitive but requires the denominator to be nonzero. (2) Cross-product method: set $R_i d_j - R_j d_i = 0$. This always works, even when components are zero. For exam purposes, either method earns full marks.
$q + 2 = -2(p - 3)$
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: $q + 2 = -2(p - 3)$.
STEP 3/3expand and rearrange to show result A1

Strategy: this is a "show that" question, so we must show every algebraic step clearly — no skipping. Expand the right side and collect terms.

$$q + 2 = -2(p - 3)$$

$$q + 2 = -2p + 6$$

Rearrange by bringing all terms to one side:

$$2p + q + 2 - 6 = 0$$

$$2p + q - 4 = 0$$

$$\boxed{\;2p + q - 4 = 0\;}$$

This is exactly the expression we were asked to show. The constraint $2p + q = 4$ defines a line in the $(p, q)$ plane — any point on this line gives a force $\mathbf{F}_2$ that, combined with $\mathbf{F}_1$, makes the particle move in the direction $(\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j})$.

Phuc's error: used the $\tan\theta$ method correctly (which is equivalent to the ratio method) but wrote "$2p + 9 - q = 0$" instead of "$2p + q - 4 = 0$." The tutor noted this was likely a handwriting confusion between 4 and 9. In a "show that" question, the final expression must match exactly — a transcription error loses the final accuracy mark even when the method is correct.
General rule for direction-of-motion constraints: "moves in direction of $(a\mathbf{i} + b\mathbf{j})$" means $\mathbf{R} = \lambda(a\mathbf{i} + b\mathbf{j})$ for some $\lambda > 0$. This gives the ratio condition $R_j / R_i = b/a$. Memorise this template — it appears frequently in M1 vector questions.
$\boxed{\,2p + q - 4 = 0\,}$ as required. ∎

Part (b) — Speed at $t = 4\,\text{s}$ given $p = 5$ [4 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3find $q$, then resultant and acceleration M1 A1

Strategy: with $p = 5$, use the constraint $2p + q - 4 = 0$ from part (a) to find $q$. Then compute the resultant force and acceleration vector.

Find $q$:

$$2(5) + q - 4 = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; 10 + q - 4 = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; q = -6$$

Resultant force:

$$\mathbf{R} = (p - 3)\mathbf{i} + (q + 2)\mathbf{j} = (5 - 3)\mathbf{i} + (-6 + 2)\mathbf{j} = 2\mathbf{i} - 4\mathbf{j}$$

Acceleration (using $\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{R}/m$ with $m = 0.5\,\text{kg}$):

$$\mathbf{a} = \frac{2\mathbf{i} - 4\mathbf{j}}{0.5} = 4\mathbf{i} - 8\mathbf{j}\;\text{m/s}^2$$

Let's verify the direction: $\mathbf{a} = 4(\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j})$, which is indeed a positive scalar multiple of $(\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j})$ ✓. The acceleration (and hence the motion) is in the required direction.

Check consistency: $\mathbf{R} = 2\mathbf{i} - 4\mathbf{j} = 2(\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j})$, so $\lambda = 2 > 0$ ✓. If $\lambda$ had been negative, it would mean the force opposes the stated direction — which would be a contradiction worth investigating.
$q = -6$. Acceleration $\mathbf{a} = 4\mathbf{i} - 8\mathbf{j}\;\text{m/s}^2$.
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $\mathbf{a} = 4\mathbf{i} - 8\mathbf{j}\;\text{m/s}^2$. Particle starts from rest.
STEP 2/3find velocity at $t = 4$ M1

Strategy: the particle starts from rest ($\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{0}$) and has constant acceleration (since both forces are constant). Use the vector SUVAT equation $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{a}t$.

Velocity at $t = 4\,\text{s}$:

$$\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} + (4\mathbf{i} - 8\mathbf{j}) \times 4$$

$$\mathbf{v} = 16\mathbf{i} - 32\mathbf{j}\;\text{m/s}$$

This makes physical sense: the velocity is in the direction $(\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j})$ (since $16\mathbf{i} - 32\mathbf{j} = 16(\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j})$), confirming the particle moves in the specified direction throughout. The velocity grows linearly in time because the acceleration is constant, and since the particle started from rest, the velocity at any time $t$ is simply $\mathbf{v} = t \cdot \mathbf{a}$.

Note that the $\mathbf{j}$-component is negative and twice the magnitude of the $\mathbf{i}$-component, which means the particle moves to the right and downward (in the $\mathbf{i}$-$\mathbf{j}$ plane), consistent with the direction $(\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j})$ — one unit right for every two units down.

Vector SUVAT: the equations $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{a}t$ and $\mathbf{s} = \mathbf{u}t + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{a}t^2$ work component-wise. For constant force problems, compute $\mathbf{a}$ first, then use these equations directly. No need to resolve into separate $x$ and $y$ equations — the vector form handles both components simultaneously.
$\mathbf{v}(4) = 16\mathbf{i} - 32\mathbf{j}\;\text{m/s}$.
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: $\mathbf{v} = 16\mathbf{i} - 32\mathbf{j}$.
STEP 3/3compute speed (magnitude) A1

Strategy: speed is the magnitude of the velocity vector. Use the Pythagorean formula $|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{v_i^2 + v_j^2}$.

$$|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{16^2 + (-32)^2} = \sqrt{256 + 1024} = \sqrt{1280}$$

Simplify the surd:

$\;\;1280 = 256 \times 5 = 16^2 \times 5$

$$|\mathbf{v}| = 16\sqrt{5} \approx 35.8\,\text{m/s}$$

$$\boxed{\;\text{Speed} = 16\sqrt{5} \approx 35.8\,\text{m/s}\;}$$

Sense check: the acceleration magnitude is $|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{16 + 64} = \sqrt{80} = 4\sqrt{5} \approx 8.94\,\text{m/s}^2$. After 4 seconds from rest, the speed should be $4 \times 4\sqrt{5} = 16\sqrt{5}$ ✓. This confirms our calculation. Also note that $35.8\,\text{m/s} \approx 129\,\text{km/h}$ — a fast speed, but the acceleration is nearly $g$ and it acts for 4 seconds, so this is plausible for a smooth surface with large forces.
