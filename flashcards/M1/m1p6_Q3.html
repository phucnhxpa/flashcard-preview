Q3 — Inclined Plane with Horizontal Force and Friction

Question

A rough plane is inclined at angle $\alpha$ to the horizontal, where $\tan\alpha = \tfrac{3}{4}$. A particle $P$ of mass $2\,\text{kg}$ is held in equilibrium on the plane by a horizontal force of magnitude $X\,\text{N}$. The coefficient of friction between $P$ and the plane is $\mu = 0.5$.

(a) Show that the friction force $F = 0$ when $X = 14.7$.

(b) Find the smallest value of $X$ for which $P$ remains in equilibrium.

[10 marks]

Part (a) — Show $F = 0$ when $X = 14.7$ [4 marks] · 2 steps

STEP 1/2extract trig ratios from $\tan\alpha$ B1

Strategy: the question gives $\tan\alpha = \tfrac{3}{4}$. For resolving along and perpendicular to the plane, I need $\sin\alpha$ and $\cos\alpha$ explicitly. I construct the 3-4-5 right triangle.

From $\tan\alpha = 3/4$:

Opposite = 3, Adjacent = 4, Hypotenuse = $\sqrt{9 + 16} = 5$.

$$\sin\alpha = \frac{3}{5}, \quad \cos\alpha = \frac{4}{5}$$

Key insight for horizontal force on incline: a horizontal force $X$ has two components when resolved along/perpendicular to the incline:

• Along the plane (up the slope): $X\cos\alpha = \tfrac{4X}{5}$

• Perpendicular to the plane (into the surface): $X\sin\alpha = \tfrac{3X}{5}$

This is the opposite of what you might expect: the component along the plane uses $\cos\alpha$ (not $\sin$), because the horizontal force makes angle $\alpha$ with the plane surface. Many students confuse this with the weight components ($mg\sin\alpha$ along, $mg\cos\alpha$ perpendicular), which use the complementary angle. The mnemonic: the force along the hypotenuse gets $\cos$ of the angle at the base.

Critical trap: the horizontal force $X$ contributes to BOTH the along-plane and perpendicular equations. The perpendicular component $X\sin\alpha$ adds to the normal reaction $R$, making $R$ depend on $X$. This coupling means you cannot solve for $F$ without also updating $R$.
$\sin\alpha = 3/5$, $\cos\alpha = 4/5$. Along plane: $X\cos\alpha$. Into plane: $X\sin\alpha$.
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $\sin\alpha = 3/5$, $\cos\alpha = 4/5$.
STEP 2/2resolve along plane → show $F = 0$ M1 A1 A1

Strategy: resolve forces along the plane. If the particle is in equilibrium, the net force along the plane is zero. I'll show that with $X = 14.7$, the equation gives $F = 0$.

Along the plane (taking up the slope as positive):

$$X\cos\alpha - 2g\sin\alpha - F = 0$$

Substitute $X = 14.7$, $g = 9.8$:

$$14.7 \times \frac{4}{5} - 2(9.8) \times \frac{3}{5} - F = 0$$

$$11.76 - 11.76 - F = 0$$

$$F = 0 \quad \checkmark$$

$$\boxed{\;F = 0 \text{ when } X = 14.7\;}$$

Sense check: when $F = 0$, the horizontal push exactly balances the gravitational pull down the slope. The value $X = 14.7 = \tfrac{3}{2} \times 9.8 = 1.5g$. Since $2g\sin\alpha = 2 \times 9.8 \times 0.6 = 11.76$ and $X\cos\alpha = 14.7 \times 0.8 = 11.76$, they cancel perfectly. This is the critical value of $X$ at which friction switches direction — below this, friction acts up the slope; above, friction acts down.
$F = 11.76 - 11.76 = 0$ when $X = 14.7$ ✓

At the smallest $X$, friction has reached its maximum value — this is limiting friction: $F = \mu R$. If $X$ were any smaller, friction couldn't compensate and the particle would slide.

Strategy: resolve perpendicular to the plane to express $R$ in terms of $X$. The horizontal force $X$ has a component $X\sin\alpha$ pushing INTO the plane, which adds to the normal reaction.

$$R = 2g\cos\alpha + X\sin\alpha$$

Since $\mu R$ appears in the friction equation, this coupling between $X$ and $R$ is essential — it's what makes the algebra non-trivial.
$R = 15.68 + \tfrac{3X}{5}$
