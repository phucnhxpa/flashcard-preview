Q8 — Ships: Position Vectors, Relative Displacement & Bearing

Question

Ship $A$ has velocity $(3\mathbf{i} + 12\mathbf{j})\,\text{km/h}$. Ship $B$ has velocity $(p\mathbf{i} + q\mathbf{j})\,\text{km/h}$.

At noon, $A$ is at position $(-9\mathbf{i} + 6\mathbf{j})\,\text{km}$ and $B$ is at position $(16\mathbf{i} + 6\mathbf{j})\,\text{km}$, relative to a fixed origin $O$.

At time $t$ hours after noon, $\overrightarrow{AB} = [(25 - 12t)\mathbf{i} - 9t\,\mathbf{j}]$ km.

(a) Find the speed of $A$.

(b) Find the value of $p$ and the value of $q$.

(c) At time $t$ hours after noon, the two ships are $15\,\text{km}$ apart. Find the bearing of $A$ from $B$ at this instant.

[11 marks]

Part (a) — Speed of $A$ [1 mark] · 1 step

STEP 1/1magnitude of velocity vector B1

Strategy: speed is the magnitude of the velocity vector. Ship $A$ has velocity $\mathbf{v}_A = 3\mathbf{i} + 12\mathbf{j}$. The magnitude is computed using the Pythagorean formula applied to the two components: $|\mathbf{v}_A| = \sqrt{v_i^2 + v_j^2}$.

Calculation:

$$|\mathbf{v}_A| = \sqrt{3^2 + 12^2} = \sqrt{9 + 144} = \sqrt{153}$$

Simplify the surd:

$\;\;153 = 9 \times 17$, so $\sqrt{153} = 3\sqrt{17}$.

$$\boxed{\;\text{Speed of } A = 3\sqrt{17} \approx 12.4\,\text{km/h}\;}$$

This is a straightforward magnitude calculation. The numerical value $12.4\,\text{km/h}$ is a reasonable speed for a ship (about 6.7 knots), which provides a quick sense check. If we'd gotten something like $120\,\text{km/h}$, we'd suspect an error — ships don't travel that fast!

Surd simplification: always look for perfect square factors. $153 = 9 \times 17$: since $9 = 3^2$, we get $\sqrt{153} = 3\sqrt{17}$. Examiners may accept either $\sqrt{153}$ or $3\sqrt{17}$, but the simplified form is preferred and shows mathematical fluency.
$\boxed{\,\text{Speed of } A = 3\sqrt{17} \approx 12.4\,\text{km/h}\,}$

Part (b) — Find $p$ and $q$ [4 marks] · 3 steps

STEP 1/3write position vectors as functions of $t$ M1

Strategy: both ships move with constant velocity, so their position vectors are linear functions of time: $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}t$. Write out the position vector for each ship at time $t$ hours after noon.

Ship $A$:

$$\mathbf{r}_A(t) = (-9 + 3t)\mathbf{i} + (6 + 12t)\mathbf{j}$$

Ship $B$:

$$\mathbf{r}_B(t) = (16 + pt)\mathbf{i} + (6 + qt)\mathbf{j}$$

These are the standard position-time equations for constant-velocity motion in 2D. Each component evolves independently: the $\mathbf{i}$-component of ship $A$ starts at $-9$ and increases by $3$ each hour, while the $\mathbf{j}$-component starts at $6$ and increases by $12$ each hour.

Position vector method: this is the fundamental technique for all "ships/particles moving with constant velocity" problems. Write $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}t$ for each object, then compute the relative displacement $\overrightarrow{AB} = \mathbf{r}_B - \mathbf{r}_A$ (note: $\overrightarrow{AB}$ goes FROM $A$ TO $B$, so it's $B$'s position minus $A$'s position).
$\mathbf{r}_A = (-9+3t)\mathbf{i} + (6+12t)\mathbf{j}$; $\mathbf{r}_B = (16+pt)\mathbf{i} + (6+qt)\mathbf{j}$.
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: position vectors expressed as functions of $t$.
STEP 2/3compute $\overrightarrow{AB}$ and compare M1 A1

Strategy: compute $\overrightarrow{AB} = \mathbf{r}_B - \mathbf{r}_A$ in terms of $p$, $q$, and $t$, then compare this with the given expression $\overrightarrow{AB} = (25 - 12t)\mathbf{i} - 9t\mathbf{j}$ by equating coefficients.

Compute $\overrightarrow{AB}$:

$$\overrightarrow{AB} = \mathbf{r}_B - \mathbf{r}_A = [(16 + pt) - (-9 + 3t)]\mathbf{i} + [(6 + qt) - (6 + 12t)]\mathbf{j}$$

$$= [25 + (p - 3)t]\mathbf{i} + [(q - 12)t]\mathbf{j}$$

Compare with given: $\overrightarrow{AB} = (25 - 12t)\mathbf{i} + (-9t)\mathbf{j}$.

$\mathbf{i}$-coefficient: the constant term is $25$ on both sides ✓ (this confirms the initial positions are consistent). The coefficient of $t$ gives: $p - 3 = -12$.

$\mathbf{j}$-coefficient: the constant term is $0$ on both sides ✓ (both ships start at $j = 6$). The coefficient of $t$ gives: $q - 12 = -9$.

Phuc's error: set up $\overrightarrow{AB}$ but dropped the $\mathbf{j}$-component entirely. The $\mathbf{j}$-component is easy to miss because the constant terms cancel (both ships start with $j = 6$), leaving only the $t$-dependent term. Always write out both components fully before comparing.
$p - 3 = -12$ and $q - 12 = -9$.
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: $p - 3 = -12$ and $q - 12 = -9$.
STEP 3/3solve for $p$ and $q$ A1

Strategy: solve the two simple linear equations from the coefficient comparison.

$$p - 3 = -12 \;\Rightarrow\; \boxed{p = -9}$$

$$q - 12 = -9 \;\Rightarrow\; \boxed{q = 3}$$

So ship $B$ has velocity $(-9\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j})\,\text{km/h}$. This means $B$ moves to the left ($-9$ in $\mathbf{i}$) and upward ($+3$ in $\mathbf{j}$). Meanwhile $A$ moves to the right ($+3$ in $\mathbf{i}$) and upward ($+12$ in $\mathbf{j}$). The ships are converging horizontally (A moves right, B moves left) — so the $\mathbf{i}$-component of $\overrightarrow{AB}$ decreases over time, consistent with the $(25 - 12t)$ expression.

Sense check: $B$'s speed $= \sqrt{81 + 9} = \sqrt{90} = 3\sqrt{10} \approx 9.5\,\text{km/h}$ — reasonable for a ship. The relative velocity is $\mathbf{v}_B - \mathbf{v}_A = (-12\mathbf{i} - 9\mathbf{j})$, which matches the coefficients of $t$ in $\overrightarrow{AB}$: the $\mathbf{i}$-coefficient of $t$ is $-12$ ✓ and the $\mathbf{j}$-coefficient of $t$ is $-9$ ✓.
$\boxed{\,p = -9,\;\; q = 3\,}$

Part (c) — Bearing of $A$ from $B$ when 15 km apart [6 marks] · 4 steps

STEP 1/4set up distance equation M1 A1

Strategy: we know $\overrightarrow{AB} = (25 - 12t)\mathbf{i} - 9t\mathbf{j}$. The distance between the ships is $|\overrightarrow{AB}|$. Set this equal to 15 km and solve for $t$.

Distance equation:

$$|\overrightarrow{AB}|^2 = (25 - 12t)^2 + (9t)^2 = 15^2 = 225$$

Expand:

$\;\;(25 - 12t)^2 = 625 - 600t + 144t^2$

$\;\;(9t)^2 = 81t^2$

$$625 - 600t + 144t^2 + 81t^2 = 225$$

$$225t^2 - 600t + 625 = 225$$

$$225t^2 - 600t + 400 = 0$$

This is a quadratic in $t$. Before solving, we can simplify by dividing through by the GCD of the coefficients. Checking: $\gcd(225, 600, 400) = 25$.

$$9t^2 - 24t + 16 = 0$$

Why square both sides: working with $|\overrightarrow{AB}|^2 = 225$ avoids dealing with a square root, which simplifies the algebra considerably. This is standard technique for distance problems with position vectors.
$9t^2 - 24t + 16 = 0$
↳ from STEP 1:From STEP 1: $9t^2 - 24t + 16 = 0$.
STEP 2/4solve quadratic for $t$ M1 A1

Strategy: solve the quadratic $9t^2 - 24t + 16 = 0$. First check the discriminant to see if it factors nicely.

Discriminant:

$$\Delta = (-24)^2 - 4(9)(16) = 576 - 576 = 0$$

The discriminant is zero — this means there is exactly one (repeated) root! This is significant: the ships are 15 km apart at exactly one instant, meaning 15 km is the minimum distance between them.

Solve:

$$t = \frac{24}{2 \times 9} = \frac{24}{18} = \frac{4}{3}\,\text{hours}$$

Alternatively, recognise the perfect square: $9t^2 - 24t + 16 = (3t - 4)^2 = 0$, giving $t = \frac{4}{3}$.

$$\boxed{\;t = \frac{4}{3}\,\text{hours} = 1\,\text{h}\;20\,\text{min after noon}\;}$$

Discriminant = 0 means closest approach: when the quadratic for $|\overrightarrow{AB}|^2 = d^2$ has a repeated root, the ships reach distance $d$ at exactly one moment — this means $d$ is the minimum distance between them. If the discriminant were positive, the ships would be at distance $d$ at two different times (approaching and receding). If negative, they never get that close. This is a frequently tested concept in M1.
$t = \tfrac{4}{3}$ hours (1:20 PM). Ships are 15 km apart at exactly one instant.
↳ from STEP 2:From STEP 2: $t = \tfrac{4}{3}$ hours.
STEP 3/4find $\overrightarrow{BA}$ at $t = 4/3$ M1

Strategy: the question asks for the bearing of $A$ from $B$. "Bearing of $A$ from $B$" means: stand at $B$, look toward $A$ — what bearing are you facing? For this we need the vector from $B$ to $A$, which is $\overrightarrow{BA} = -\overrightarrow{AB}$.

Find $\overrightarrow{AB}$ at $t = \frac{4}{3}$:

$$\overrightarrow{AB} = \left(25 - 12 \times \frac{4}{3}\right)\mathbf{i} - 9 \times \frac{4}{3}\,\mathbf{j}$$

$$= (25 - 16)\mathbf{i} - 12\mathbf{j} = 9\mathbf{i} - 12\mathbf{j}$$

Therefore:

$$\overrightarrow{BA} = -\overrightarrow{AB} = -9\mathbf{i} + 12\mathbf{j}$$

Check: $|\overrightarrow{BA}| = \sqrt{81 + 144} = \sqrt{225} = 15$ ✓ (matches the 15 km given).

The vector $\overrightarrow{BA} = -9\mathbf{i} + 12\mathbf{j}$ means: from $B$ to $A$, you go 9 km in the $-\mathbf{i}$ direction (West) and 12 km in the $+\mathbf{j}$ direction (North). So $A$ is to the North-West of $B$.

AB vs BA: a very common error is using $\overrightarrow{AB}$ instead of $\overrightarrow{BA}$ for the bearing. "Bearing of $A$ from $B$" = direction from $B$ to $A$ = $\overrightarrow{BA}$. Always double-check which direction the bearing describes: it's the direction you'd walk if you started at $B$ and headed toward $A$.
$\overrightarrow{BA} = -9\mathbf{i} + 12\mathbf{j}$ (9 km West, 12 km North).
↳ from STEP 3:From STEP 3: $\overrightarrow{BA} = -9\mathbf{i} + 12\mathbf{j}$. $A$ is NW of $B$.
STEP 4/4convert to bearing A1

Strategy: convert the displacement vector $\overrightarrow{BA} = -9\mathbf{i} + 12\mathbf{j}$ into a bearing. Recall that $\mathbf{i}$ is East and $\mathbf{j}$ is North. The vector has a negative $\mathbf{i}$-component (West) and positive $\mathbf{j}$-component (North), placing $A$ in the North-West quadrant as seen from $B$.

Find the angle from North:

The angle $\theta$ between the vector and the North direction ($\mathbf{j}$-axis) is found using:

$$\tan\theta = \frac{|\text{East-West component}|}{|\text{North-South component}|} = \frac{9}{12} = \frac{3}{4}$$

$$\theta = \arctan\left(\frac{3}{4}\right) = 36.87° \approx 36.9°$$

Convert to bearing: the vector points North-West, i.e. $\theta$ degrees west of North. Bearings are measured clockwise from North, so a direction west of North corresponds to a bearing of $360° - \theta$.

$$\text{Bearing} = 360° - 36.87° = 323.13° \approx 323°$$

$$\boxed{\;\text{Bearing of } A \text{ from } B \approx 323°\;}$$

Bearing conversion for all four quadrants: (1) NE: bearing $= \theta$. (2) SE: bearing $= 180° - \theta$. (3) SW: bearing $= 180° + \theta$. (4) NW: bearing $= 360° - \theta$. Where $\theta$ is the acute angle measured from the nearest N-S axis. Always identify the quadrant first, then apply the correct formula.
Phuc's error: set up $\overrightarrow{AB}$ but dropped the $\mathbf{j}$-component, and did not complete the coefficient comparison or the bearing calculation. The chain from part (b) feeds directly into part (c): without $p$ and $q$, you cannot verify $\overrightarrow{AB}$. However, even if (b) was incomplete, the given expression for $\overrightarrow{AB}$ can be used directly in (c) — always use given information from the question, even if you couldn't derive it yourself.
Discriminant insight: the repeated root ($\Delta = 0$) tells us the ships reach their closest approach of exactly 15 km at $t = \frac{4}{3}$ hours. If the question had asked "find the minimum distance," we would set $\frac{d}{dt}|\overrightarrow{AB}|^2 = 0$ or use the discriminant of the quadratic to find the minimum value of $d$.
$\boxed{\,\text{Bearing of } A \text{ from } B \approx 323°\,}$
