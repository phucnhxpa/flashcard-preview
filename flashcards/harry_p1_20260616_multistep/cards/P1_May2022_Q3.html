Q3 — Surds: simplify and rationalise

Question

In this question you must show all stages of your working. Solutions relying on calculator technology are not acceptable. (i) Show that \(\displaystyle\frac{\sqrt{180}-\sqrt{80}}{\sqrt5}\) is an integer and find its value. (ii) Simplify \(\displaystyle\frac{4\sqrt5-5}{7-3\sqrt5}\), giving your answer in the form \(a+b\sqrt5\), where \(a\) and \(b\) are rational numbers. (5)

[5 marks]

Lesson status: Touched briefly (simplification re-explained; not a logged lost-mark review). The official QP/MS control wording, marks and values.

Part (i)[2 marks] · 1 steps

Carry-in: No carry-in: split the numerator over the common root.
STEP 1/1

Strategy and why this applies. Use \(\frac{\sqrt a-\sqrt b}{\sqrt c}=\frac{\sqrt a}{\sqrt c}-\frac{\sqrt b}{\sqrt c}=\sqrt{a/c}-\sqrt{b/c}\).

Step 1 — split the fraction. Dividing by \(\sqrt5\) distributes over the minus:

\[\frac{\sqrt{180}}{\sqrt5}-\frac{\sqrt{80}}{\sqrt5}=\sqrt{\frac{180}{5}}-\sqrt{\frac{80}{5}}=\sqrt{36}-\sqrt{16}.\]

Step 2 — evaluate the square roots. \(\sqrt{36}=6\) and \(\sqrt{16}=4\).

\[=6-4=2.\]

Step 3 — why this is cleaner than expanding first. Trying to simplify \(\sqrt{180}=6\sqrt5\) and \(\sqrt{80}=4\sqrt5\) first also works: \(\frac{6\sqrt5-4\sqrt5}{\sqrt5}=\frac{2\sqrt5}{\sqrt5}=2\). Both routes confirm the integer answer; keeping the surd in the numerator cancels neatly.

Step 4 — transfer trigger and exam technique. When a root sits over a difference in the numerator, do not split it as \(\frac{\sqrt{180}-\sqrt{80}}{\sqrt5}\neq\frac{\sqrt{180}}{\sqrt5}-\sqrt{80}\); the subtraction must stay inside the single fraction before you divide term by term. The product/quotient rule for surds (\(\frac{\sqrt a}{\sqrt c}=\sqrt{a/c}\)) only applies to one surd at a time. Always check the final answer is in the simplest exact form — here a plain integer, not a surd — and never introduce a decimal approximation for a question that resolves exactly to 2. A useful check is to verify \(\sqrt{180}/\sqrt5=\sqrt{36}=6\) and \(\sqrt{80}/\sqrt5=\sqrt{16}=4\) separately, then subtract, which makes the arithmetic transparent.

Common trap / why the rejected route fails. Leave the denominator as a single root instead of splitting the numerator.

Part (ii)[3 marks] · 1 steps

Carry-in: No carry-in: multiply top and bottom by the conjugate of \(7-3\sqrt5\).
STEP 1/1

Strategy and why this applies. Conjugate of \(a-b\sqrt5\) is \(a+b\sqrt5\); the product removes the surd.

Step 1 — choose the conjugate. The denominator is \(7-3\sqrt5\). Multiply numerator and denominator by its conjugate \(7+3\sqrt5\); this exploits \((A-B)(A+B)=A^2-B^2\) to leave a rational denominator.

\[\frac{(4\sqrt5-5)(7+3\sqrt5)}{(7-3\sqrt5)(7+3\sqrt5)}.\]

Step 2 — denominator. \(7^2-(3\sqrt5)^2=49-9\cdot5=49-45=4\).

Step 3 — numerator, expand carefully. \((4\sqrt5)(7)=28\sqrt5\); \((4\sqrt5)(3\sqrt5)=12\cdot5=60\); \((-5)(7)=-35\); \((-5)(3\sqrt5)=-15\sqrt5\). Collect: \(28\sqrt5-15\sqrt5=13\sqrt5\), and \(60-35=25\), so numerator \(=25+13\sqrt5\).

\[=\frac{25+13\sqrt5}{4}=\frac{25}{4}+\frac{13}{4}\sqrt5.\]

Step 4 — sense check the form. The answer is \(a+b\sqrt5\) with rational \(a,b\), the required "rationalised" shape.

Step 5 — transfer trigger and exam technique. Rationalising always means "remove the surd from the denominator"; the tool is the conjugate, and the identity \((A-B)(A+B)=A^2-B^2\) is what makes the denominator rational because \((\sqrt5)^2=5\). Keep the brackets intact when you expand the numerator — a dropped minus on \((-5)(3\sqrt5)=-15\sqrt5\) is the usual slip and would give the wrong coefficient of \(\sqrt5\). Once the denominator is the integer 4, split the fraction into \(\frac{25}{4}+\frac{13}{4}\sqrt5\); do not try to "cancel" the 4 into the \(\sqrt5\), since 4 is not a factor of \(\sqrt5\). Finally, present the answer exactly — surd form, not a decimal approximation. If the denominator had been a single term like \(\sqrt5\) you would multiply top and bottom by \(\sqrt5\) instead; the conjugate method is specifically for a sum or difference involving a surd.

On another paper, a two-term surd denominator should trigger multiplication by its conjugate; confirm the denominator becomes rational before simplifying.

Common trap / why the rejected route fails. Sign error when expanding with the conjugate.
