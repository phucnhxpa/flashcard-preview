Q6 — Simultaneous equations to a quartic

Question

In this question you must show all stages of your working.

Solutions relying on calculator technology are not acceptable.

(a) Given that

\(2xy-3x^2=50\)

and

\(y-x^3+6x=0\)

show that

\(2x^4-15x^2-50=0\)

(2)

(b) Hence solve the simultaneous equations

\(2xy-3x^2=50\)

\(y-x^3+6x=0\)

Give your answers in fully simplified surd form.

(5)

[7 marks]

Part (a)[2 marks] · 1 steps

Carry-in: Both equations: \(2xy-3x^2=50\) and \(y-x^3+6x=0\).
STEP 1/1

Strategy and why this applies. Eliminate \(y\) by rearranging the second equation, then collect powers before using the hidden-quadratic substitution. Formula or definition first.

Elimination. Rearrange \(y-x^3+6x=0\) to \(y=x^3-6x\). Substitute this complete expression into \(2xy-3x^2=50\):

\[2x(x^3-6x)-3x^2=50.\]

Expand the bracket before collecting terms:

\[2x^4-12x^2-3x^2=50,\] \[2x^4-15x^2-50=0.\]

This is the required equation. The intermediate line with \(-12x^2-3x^2\) is important because the question says “show that”; simply copying the printed quartic does not demonstrate the elimination.

Hidden-quadratic recognition. Only \(x^4\), \(x^2\), and a constant occur. Let \(u=x^2\), so \(x^4=u^2\), giving \(2u^2-15u-50=0\). Also record \(u\ge0\) for real \(x\); this condition will reject one algebraic root.

Sense check and transfer trigger. Substitution preserves degree correctly: \(2x\cdot x^3=2x^4\) and \(2x\cdot(-6x)=-12x^2\). On another paper, simultaneous equations containing a cubic relation and an \(xy\) term often invite substitution to produce an even-powered quartic. Eliminate one variable fully first; only then introduce \(u=x^2\).

Common trap / why the rejected route fails. Treating \(y=x^3-6x\) as a vague “linear relation” hides the required elimination. Missing the outer \(2x\) gives the wrong coefficients.

Part (b)[5 marks] · 1 steps

Carry-in: \(2x^4-15x^2-50=0\), equivalently \(2u^2-15u-50=0\) with \(u=x^2\ge0\).
STEP 1/1

Strategy and why this applies. Factorise the quadratic in \(u\), reject the negative value, convert back to both signs of \(x\), and calculate \(y\) from the original cubic relation. Formula or definition first.

Factorise. Since \(2(-50)=-100\), split \(-15u\) as \(-20u+5u\):

\[2u^2-20u+5u-50=(2u+5)(u-10)=0.\]

Thus \(u=-\tfrac52\) or \(u=10\). Because \(u=x^2\ge0\) for real \(x\), reject \(-\tfrac52\). From \(x^2=10\), both \(x=\sqrt{10}\) and \(x=-\sqrt{10}\) must be considered.

Back-substitute without hiding the signs. Use \(y=x^3-6x=x(x^2-6)\). Since \(x^2=10\),

\[y=x(10-6)=4x.\]

Therefore \(x=\sqrt{10}\) gives \(y=4\sqrt{10}\), while \(x=-\sqrt{10}\) gives \(y=-4\sqrt{10}\). The complete solution set is

\[(x,y)=(\sqrt{10},4\sqrt{10})\quad\text{or}\quad(-\sqrt{10},-4\sqrt{10}).\]

Sense check and transfer trigger. Writing the ordered pairs separately prevents the ambiguous shorthand \((\pm\sqrt{10},\pm4\sqrt{10})\), which can wrongly suggest four combinations. In either valid pair, \(y=4x\); substituting into \(2xy-3x^2\) gives \(8x^2-3x^2=5(10)=50\). On another hidden-quadratic problem, always apply the domain condition to \(u\), restore every valid sign of \(x\), then use the untouched original relation to pair dependent-variable signs.

Sense check. Substitution of each ordered pair into both original equations returns 50 and 0 respectively.

Common trap / why the rejected route fails. Keeping \(u=-5/2\) introduces non-real values; independently choosing both signs for \(x\) and \(y\) creates two extraneous mismatched pairs.
