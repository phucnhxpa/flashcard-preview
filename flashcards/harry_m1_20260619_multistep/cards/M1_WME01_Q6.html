Packet Q6 / Official WME01 Q6 — boat pulled by two ropes

Question

From the paper. A boat is pulled along a river at a constant speed by two ropes. The banks of the river are parallel and the boat travels horizontally in a straight line, parallel to the riverbanks. The tension in the first rope is \(500\ \mathrm N\), acting at an angle of \(40^\circ\) to the direction of motion, as shown in Figure 3. The tension in the second rope is \(P\) newtons, acting at an angle of \(\alpha^\circ\) to the direction of motion, also shown in Figure 3. The resistance to motion of the boat as it moves through the water is a constant force of magnitude \(900\ \mathrm N\). The boat is modelled as a particle. The ropes are modelled as being light and lying in a horizontal plane. Use the model to find (i) the value of \(\alpha\); (ii) the value of \(P\).

Carry-in / given: Standard method: resolve both rope tensions into components; net force = 0.
STEP 1/1

Exam trigger and stopping point: when two ropes pull a particle, resolve both tensions along fixed axes; this item was not discussed, so no further lesson-derived solution is shown.

Strategy and definition first. This question was NOT DISCUSSED in the lesson, so the route here is explicitly OFFICIAL MS REFERENCE ONLY, not reconstructed live work. Constant speed in a straight line means zero acceleration; therefore equilibrium gives \(\sum F_{\parallel}=0\) and \(\sum F_{\perp}=0\). Resolve relative to the boat’s direction of motion because the \(900\ \mathrm N\) resistance is parallel to that line. The \(500\ \mathrm N\) and \(P\)-newton ropes lie on opposite sides of it, so their sideways components oppose, while both forward components oppose the resistance.

Authorised working. Perpendicular balance gives \(500\sin40^\circ=P\sin\alpha\). Parallel balance gives \(500\cos40^\circ+P\cos\alpha=900\). From the first equation, \(P=500\sin40^\circ/\sin\alpha\). Substitution into the second gives \(500\cos40^\circ+500\sin40^\circ\cot\alpha=900\), hence \(\tan\alpha=\dfrac{500\sin40^\circ}{900-500\cos40^\circ}\). Thus \(\alpha=31.8683\ldots^\circ\). Back-substitution, without rounding the angle, gives \(P=\dfrac{500\sin40^\circ}{\sin31.8683\ldots^\circ}=608.736\ldots\ \mathrm N\).

Official status and exact requested outputs. Only the official-reference route authorises the numerical statements on this card: the accepted answers are \(\alpha=31.9^\circ\) (or \(32^\circ\)) and \(P=609\ \mathrm N\) (the scheme accepts \(610\ \mathrm N\) or better). These were not produced by the Student or Tutor during the lesson.

OFFICIAL MS REFERENCE ONLY: \(\alpha\approx31.9^\circ,\quad P\approx609\ \mathrm N\).

Check-back and transfer trigger. Substitution returns equal sideways components and forward components totalling \(900\ \mathrm N\); both tensions are positive and \(0^\circ<\alpha<90^\circ\). On any constant-velocity particle pulled obliquely, trigger the same routine: choose axes from the motion, mark which components oppose, write both zero-resultant equations, eliminate one unknown symbolically, then round only the final values.

Neutral trap diagnosis. Symmetry is not stated: the tensions and angles differ, so writing \(2P\cos\alpha=900\) fails at the modelling stage. Giving both sine components the same sign also falsely creates a sideways resultant. Treating “constant speed” alone as permission to ignore direction fails because the paper additionally states straight-line motion. Early rounding of \(\alpha\) then perturbs \(P\); retain calculator precision through the back-substitution.
