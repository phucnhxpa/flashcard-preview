Q: A solid sphere of radius $$r$$ fits exactly inside a hollow cylinder that has the same internal diameter and the same length as the sphere's diameter. Why must the cylinder's height be $$2r$$ rather than $$r$$? (3 steps) ▼ click to reveal
A:


Step 1: The diameter of a sphere of radius $$r$$ is $$2r$$. The problem states the cylinder's length (height) equals that diameter, so $$h = 2r$$.

Step 2: If you used $$h = r$$, the cylinder would only be as tall as the radius; a sphere of radius $$r$$ would protrude both above and below, making it impossible for the sphere to be contained completely inside.

Step 3: Therefore, the correct dimension for a snug‑fitting cylinder is height $$2r$$, leading to cylinder volume $$V_{\text{cyl}} = \pi r^2 \cdot 2r = 2\pi r^3$$.

The physical picture prevents misreading “diameter” as “radius”.

Q: How does the “burger method” let you mentally evaluate $$\frac{a}{b} \div \frac{c}{d}$$? Describe the visual cross‑multiplication and why it works. (3 steps) ▼ click to reveal
A:


Step 1: Write the division as a complex fraction $$\frac{a/b}{c/d}$$. The burger method says: multiply the top‑left $$a$$ by the bottom‑right $$d$$ (outer pair), and multiply the bottom‑left $$b$$ by the top‑right $$c$$ (inner pair). The result is $$\frac{ad}{bc}$$.

Step 2: Visually, the outer elements are the “top bun” (a and d) and the inner elements the “filling” (b and c). The cross‑multiplication forms the new numerator and denominator.

Step 3: This is algebraically identical to multiplying by the reciprocal: $$\frac{a}{b} \times \frac{d}{c}$$. The burger method gives a memorable cross‑shaped pattern that reduces errors, especially for complex fractions.
