Card 1MiniMax ①
Q:

For cos(2x) = p on 0 ≤ x ≤ 225°, which values of p give exactly two solutions, and which is the surprising endpoint trap? (2 steps)

A:

- The values: $$-1 < p < 0$$ (strict) or $$p = 1$$.
+ $$p = 1$$: hit at $$x = 0$$ and $$x = 180$$ (Two solutions.)
+ $$-1 < p < 0$$: hit once on piece A and once on piece B. Piece C is too short to reach negative y, so it adds nothing.
- The trap: $$p = 0$$. It is tempting to include $$p = 0$$, but $$\cos(2x) = 0$$ gives $$x = 45°, 135°$$, AND $$x = 225°$$ — three solutions, because $$R = (225°, 0)$$ lies on the curve.

General rule: Whenever an interval endpoint sits on the x-axis (or on a peak/trough), test that endpoint's p-value separately. The boundary must be strict.

Card 2MiniMax ①
Q:

In a triangle ABC, point D is on AB, DE is parallel to BC, AD:DB = 1:3, and ∠AED = 60°. You are asked to find the ratio AE/EC. Which given fact is most likely a decoy, and why? (2 steps)

A:

- Step 1: The ratio $$AD:DB = 1:3$$ lets you use the parallel-line property: $$AD/AB = AE/AC$$. So $$AE/AC = 1/4$$, which means $$AE:EC = 1:3$$.
- Step 2: The 60° angle at E is a decoy. It does not affect the parallel-line ratio, which is purely a similarity (AAA) result and doesn't need any angle measure. If you reflexively use the 60°, you'll be solving a trig problem that wasn't asked.

General lesson: In a ratio problem about parallel lines, the given angle is often a decoy. The ratio depends only on the segment ratio, not on the actual angle.

Card 3MiniMax ①
Q:

When a geometry problem says "M is the midpoint of segment PR," what does this immediately tell you about the ratio PM/PR, and why does this matter for parallel lines?

A:

"M is the midpoint of PR" immediately pins PM/PR = 1/2. This is never decoration in a geometry problem — it is a fixed ratio constraint you can use directly.

Why this matters for parallel lines: If a line through M is parallel to another side of the triangle (say MX ∥ RQ), then triangle PMX is similar to triangle PRQ with scale factor 1/2. So any corresponding length on the small triangle is half the corresponding length on the big triangle. This unlocks the answer in one step: MX = RQ/2.

Card 4MiniMax ①
Q:

Why is counting solutions of tan(x) = 1/x + 1 near x = 0 more delicate than counting solutions of tan(x) = 1 (a constant)? (2 steps)

A:

- Step 1: $$\tan(x) = 1$$ (constant) — the right side is a horizontal line. Each branch of $$\tan(x)$$ crosses $$y = 1$$ exactly once (since tan is monotone on each branch and the line is constant). Counting is uniform: period × 1.
- Step 2: $$\tan(x) = 1/x + 1$$ — the right side has its own vertical asymptote at $$x = 0$$ and a horizontal asymptote at $$y = 1$$. Near $$x = 0$$, $$1/x + 1$$ shoots to $$±\infty$$, so the behaviour on the first tangent branch is different from the behaviour on later branches. The count near $$x = 0$$ must be done by eye on the graph, not by formula.

Why this matters: This is exactly why the lesson's three cases had to be solved by careful branch-by-branch drawing, not by "periods × 1."

Card 5MiniMax ①
Q:

Solve −x² + 4x + 5 ≥ 0. Show the key moves without full calculation. (3 steps)

A:

- Step 1: Multiply by $$-1$$ (flip the inequality): $$x^2 - 4x - 5 \leq 0$$.
- Step 2: Factor: $$(x - 5)(x + 1) \leq 0$$. Roots $$-1$$ and $$5$$.
- Step 3: $$a > 0$$ with $$\leq 0$$, so keep the INSIDE (negative part) and include the roots: $$-1 \leq x \leq 5$$.

Takeaway: Multiplying an inequality by $$-1$$ flips BOTH the direction of the inequality AND the role of inside/outside (because a flips sign). Always re-derive from the new form; do not mechanically flip "outside" to "inside" alone.

Card 6MiniMax ①
Q:

When counting solutions of cos(kx) = p on a closed interval, what is the universal rule about the interval endpoints, and why? (2 steps)

A:

- The rule: Test the value of p that the curve takes at each endpoint as a SEPARATE CASE. If the endpoint's y-value lies on a peak/trough/x-axis, $$p = (\text{that value})$$ gains or loses solutions.
- Why: The endpoint is a solution iff its y-value equals p. So $$p = (\text{endpoint y-value})$$ either includes or excludes that solution. An endpoint on a maximum means $$p = \max$$ gives that point as a solution — and if the curve also hits max elsewhere, you may get 2 or 3 solutions, not 1.

Concrete example from the lesson: $$R = (225°, 0)$$ is the right endpoint. Setting $$p = 0$$ includes R as a solution, raising the count from 2 to 3 on $$[0°, 225°]$$.

Card 7MiniMax ①
Q:

Why does a > 0 imply the quadratic is positive OUTSIDE the roots, but negative INSIDE? Sketch the intuition in words. (2 steps)

A:

- Step 1: A parabola opening up (a > 0) has its minimum point on the x-axis (touching zero at the roots) and rises to +∞ on both sides. Between the two roots, the parabola dips below the x-axis, so y is negative.
- Step 2: Outside the roots, the parabola has risen back above the x-axis on both sides, so y is positive. The roots themselves are exactly zero.

Mirror case: If $$a < 0$$ (parabola opens down), the roles reverse — positive INSIDE, negative OUTSIDE.

The trap: Students often pick "the interval between the roots" just because the roots are the salient numbers. The sign of a (or the inequality sign) decides which side you keep.

Card 8MiniMax ①
Q:

Outline the 6-step algorithm for solving a quadratic inequality ax² + bx + c ⋛ 0, with no specific numbers. (6 steps)

A:

- Step 1: Rearrange so one side is zero: $$ax^2 + bx + c \gtrless 0$$.
- Step 2: Solve $$ax^2 + bx + c = 0$$ to find the boundary roots.
- Step 3: Sketch a small parabola — opens up if $$a > 0$$, down if $$a < 0$$.
- Step 4: Decide the sign. For $$a > 0$$: positive OUTSIDE the roots, negative INSIDE.
- Step 5: Translate that sign region into x-values. Two separated pieces ⇒ "or"; one middle piece ⇒ "and".
- Step 6: Apply inclusivity. $$\geq, \leq$$ include the roots; $$>, <$$ exclude them.

Why this works: The parabola's shape is fully determined by a and the roots, so once you have the roots, the sign on every interval is forced.

Card 9MiniMax ①
Q:

In the Slack Q1 figure (QX/PX = 1/6, QX/XR = 2/3, M the midpoint of PR, ∠QXR = 90°), the right angle at X is a "decoy." Why is it not needed to compute QX/MX?

A:

Look at what you actually need: the ratio QX/MX. The right angle would let you apply Pythagoras to triangle QXR, but QX cancels out of the ratio QX/MX. Both PX and XR are determined purely from the ratio facts involving QX, and the midpoint fact pins PM at half of PR. The right angle would only matter if you needed an absolute length; the ratio QX/MX never uses it.

General lesson: A "given" in a geometry problem is either essential or a decoy. You decide which by writing down its role, not by reflexively using everything. Always check: would removing this fact change my answer? If not, it is a decoy.

Card 10Qwen 3 ①
Q:

Why is it mathematically invalid to "cancel" the $$x^2$$ terms in the expression $$\frac{x^2 + 4}{x^2 - 2x}$$ to get $$\frac{4}{-2x}$$, and what is the correct condition required for cancellation in rational expressions? (2 steps)

A:

- Step 1: Cancellation in fractions is only valid for common *multiplicative factors*, not additive terms. The expression consists of terms joined by addition and subtraction, meaning $$x^2$$ is not a factor of the numerator or the denominator.
- Step 2: To cancel, you must first factor the numerator and denominator completely into products. Since $$x^2 + 4$$ cannot be factored over the reals and shares no common product factors with $$x(x - 2)$$, the expression is already in its simplest form and no cancellation can occur.

You can only cancel factors, never terms separated by $$+$$ or $$-$$.

Card 11Qwen 3 ①
Q:

When finding the values of $$p$$ for which $$\cos(kx) = p$$ has "exactly two solutions" on a restricted domain like $$0 \le x \le 225^\circ$$, why is it dangerous to assume the answer is simply a strict inequality like $$-1 < p < 0$$ without testing the boundary values $$p = 0$$ and $$p = \pm 1$$ separately? (2 steps)

A:

- Step 1: The number of solutions changes precisely when the horizontal line $$y = p$$ crosses a local maximum, a local minimum, or an endpoint of the restricted domain. These critical $$y$$-values act as boundaries between different solution counts.
- Step 2: If an interval endpoint happens to sit exactly on the x-axis (e.g., the curve ends at $$y = 0$$), then $$p = 0$$ will include that endpoint as a valid solution. This can unexpectedly add a third solution, turning a "two solution" region into a "three solution" point. Testing boundaries separately prevents falling into this endpoint trap.

Card 12MiniMax ①
Q:

What does "the number of solutions of tan(ax) = g(x) on interval I" really mean geometrically, and how do you turn it into a counting problem? (2 steps)

A:

- Step 1: Geometrically, a solution of $$\tan(ax) = g(x)$$ is an x where the two curves meet. So the number of solutions equals the number of intersections of $$y = \tan(ax)$$ and $$y = g(x)$$ on I.
- Step 2: To count, draw both curves on the same axes. Mark the interval endpoints, then mark every asymptote of $$\tan(ax)$$ inside the interval — these split the curve into monotone branches. On each branch, decide whether the second curve crosses the tangent branch (usually once if both are monotone there). Add them up.

Why not just multiply periods × solutions-per-period? Because $$g(x)$$ is often not periodic (e.g., $$1/x + 1$$ has its own asymptote at $$x = 0$$), so the count near $$x = 0$$ and at the interval ends must be done by eye, not by formula.

Card 14MiniMax ①
Q:

Why does the minus sign in front of a fraction affect every term in the numerator, and what is the most common slip when simplifying 4 − (x+2)/x? (3 steps)

A:

- Step 1: A minus sign in front of a bracket is multiplication by −1. So 4 − (x+2)/x means 4 + [−1·(x+2)/x] = 4 + (−x−2)/x.
- Step 2: Splitting: 4 + (−x/x) + (−2/x) = 4 − 1 − 2/x = 3 − 2/x. The minus distributes to BOTH x and 2.
- Step 3: The common slip is to write 4 − (x+2)/x = 4 − x/x + 2/x = 3 + 2/x. This is wrong because the minus hits the +2, not just the x.

Fix: Always bracket a subtraction first: 4 − [(x+2)/x]. Then distribute the minus to both terms.

Card 15MiniMax ①
Q:

What is the "reversed factors" identity, and what does it tell you about the value of (x−y)/(y−x)? (2 steps)

A:

- Step 1: The identity is a − b = −(b − a). The same two terms in reversed order differ by a sign.
- Step 2: So (x−y)/(y−x) = (x−y)/[−(x−y)] = −1, provided x ≠ y.

Why this matters: Whenever you see the same two terms in reversed order (2 − x vs x − 2, or x − y vs y − x), pull out the −1 immediately. This is a frequent trick in option lists designed to catch students who cancel "the same" terms without noticing the sign flip.

Card 16MiniMax ①
Q:

For tan(4x) = 1/x + 1 on [0, 4π], why is it wrong to count as if it were tan(x), and what is the correct period-based first step? (3 steps)

A:

- Step 1: $$\tan(4x)$$ is not $$\tan(x)$$ — it repeats 4 times more often. The period is $$\pi/4$$, not $$\pi$$. If you count on a $$\pi$$-period you will undercount by a factor of 4.
- Step 2: First step: compute the new period. Period of $$\tan(4x) = \pi/|4| = \pi/4$$.
- Step 3: Then count branches on the exact interval $$[0, 4\pi]$$. Number of full periods in $$[0, 4\pi]$$ is $$4\pi / (\pi/4) = 16$$. Then verify the count by eye, especially near $$x = 0$$ and the interval ends.

General rule: Always recompute the period of the transformed trig function BEFORE counting intersections.

Card 17MiniMax ①
Q:

Compare "exactly two solutions" of cos(2x) = p on [0°, 225°] vs on [0°, 360°]. Why does the interval change the answer so much? (2 steps)

A:

- On $$[0°, 225°]$$: The curve makes 1.25 cycles. Piece C is a half-branch from $$(180°, 1)$$ to $$(225°, 0)$$, so it only reaches $$y \in [0, 1]$$. Answer: $$-1 < p < 0$$ or $$p = 1$$.
- On $$[0°, 360°]$$: The curve makes a full 2 cycles: down-up-down-up. For a generic $$p \in (-1, 1)$$, each cycle contributes 2 intersections, so 4 total. To get exactly 2, you need an extreme value where cycles "collapse" to one intersection: $$p = -1$$ (at $$x = 90°, 270°$$). At $$p = 1$$, both endpoints ($$x = 0°, 360°$$) are maxima, giving 3 solutions — $$p = 1$$ is excluded.

Why the interval matters: The same curve, but a different number of branches covered, gives a completely different count. The interval is part of the question, never a detail.

Card 18MiniMax ①
Q:

In the inequality −x² + 4x + 5 ≥ 0, what is the double-flip trap, and what is the safe procedure? (2 steps)

A:

- Step 1: The double-flip trap. When you multiply an inequality by $$-1$$, you must (a) flip the inequality sign, AND (b) the leading coefficient flips from negative to positive, so the inside/outside rule flips. Doing only (a) is the first trap. Forgetting (b) is the second.
- Step 2: Safe procedure — rewrite the inequality with positive leading coefficient FIRST, then apply the standard rule. So $$-x^2 + 4x + 5 \geq 0$$ becomes $$x^2 - 4x - 5 \leq 0$$ (inequality flipped, a is now positive). Now the rule is simple: $$\leq 0$$ with $$a > 0$$ means INSIDE the roots.

Why this is safer: It separates the "flip the sign" step from the "decide inside/outside" step, so you don't have to think about both simultaneously.

Card 19Qwen 3 ①
Q:

Why does the algebraic fraction $$\frac{a - b}{b - a}$$ always simplify to $$-1$$ (assuming $$a \neq b$$), and how should this be applied when simplifying rational expressions? (2 steps)

A:

- Step 1: Recognize that subtraction is not commutative; reversing the order of terms changes the sign of the result, meaning $$a - b = -(b - a)$$.
- Step 2: Substitute this into the fraction to get $$\frac{-(b - a)}{b - a}$$. The common factor $$(b - a)$$ cancels out, leaving exactly $$-1$$.
+ When simplifying, always factor fully first. If you see reversed binomials like $$(2 - x)$$ and $$(x - 2)$$, immediately factor out $$-1$$ from one of them to reveal the common factor and carry the negative sign to the numerator or denominator.

Card 20Qwen 3 ①
Q:

When solving a quadratic inequality like $$(x - a)(x - b) \ge 0$$ (where $$a < b$$ and the parabola opens upwards), the solution consists of two separated regions: $$x \le a$$ and $$x \ge b$$. Why must these regions be joined by the logical connective "OR" rather than "AND"? (2 steps)

A:

- Step 1: Analyze the meaning of the connectives in set theory. "AND" represents the intersection of sets (values that satisfy both conditions simultaneously), while "OR" represents the union of sets (values that satisfy at least one condition).
- Step 2: Since a single number $$x$$ cannot be simultaneously less than $$a$$ and greater than $$b$$ (where $$a < b$$), the intersection is empty. The solution set is the union of the two disjoint intervals, so any valid $$x$$ must belong to the left region OR the right region.

Separated regions on a number line always imply a union (OR); connected regions imply an intersection (AND).

Card 22MiniMax ①
Q:

On 0 ≤ x ≤ 225°, the curve y = cos(2x) passes through (0°, 1) → (45°, 0) → (90°, −1) → (135°, 0) → (180°, 1) → (225°, 0). How many monotone pieces does this split into, and what is the y-range on each? (3 steps)

A:

- Step 1: Three monotone pieces: A is down on $$[0°, 90°]$$ (y from 1 down to $$-1$$), B is up on $$[90°, 180°]$$ (y from $$-1$$ up to 1), C is down on $$[180°, 225°]$$ (y from 1 down to 0 — only a partial piece).
- Step 2: y-ranges: A covers $$y \in [-1, 1]$$; B covers $$y \in [-1, 1]$$; C covers $$y \in [0, 1]$$ only.
- Step 3: This asymmetry of piece C (it doesn't reach down to $$-1$$) is exactly what makes "exactly two solutions" sensitive to the value of p. It costs C the ability to hit $$p \in (-1, 0)$$ at all.

Card 23MiniMax ①
Q:

On [0°, 360°], the answer to "exactly two solutions" of cos(2x) = p is p = −1. Why is p = 1 also tempting but actually wrong? (2 steps)

A:

- Step 1: $$\cos(2x) = 1$$ on $$[0°, 360°]$$ gives $$x = 0, 180, 360$$. That's three solutions, not two. The right endpoint $$x = 360°$$ is a closed-interval point, so it counts.
- Step 2: To get exactly two at $$p = 1$$, you would need to exclude one of these. Harry's note: $$p = 1$$ is "exactly two" only if the right end $$x = 360°$$ is excluded (e.g., on $$[0°, 360°)$$). On the closed interval, $$p = 1$$ gives 3 solutions and is NOT an answer.

Takeaway: When the interval is closed, both endpoints count. The "exactly two" answer must hold on the stated interval, not on a slightly different one.

Card 24Qwen 3 ①
Q:

When solving a triangle problem, you are given that $$M$$ is the midpoint of side $$PR$$, and a line $$MX$$ is parallel to $$RQ$$. You are also given that $$\angle QXR = 90^\circ$$. How do you determine whether the $$90^\circ$$ angle is an essential fact or a "decoy"? (2 steps)

A:

- Step 1: Write down the geometric role of every given fact. The midpoint and parallel line trigger the Midpoint Theorem (or similar triangles with a $$1:2$$ scale factor), which directly links the length of $$MX$$ to $$RQ$$.
- Step 2: Check if the target variable can be found using only the essential facts. Since $$MX = \frac{1}{2} RQ$$ relies solely on the midpoint and parallel lines, the $$90^\circ$$ angle is a decoy—it provides extra structural information that isn't needed for the specific ratio being asked.

Always assign a "job" to every given fact; if the problem is solved before a fact is used, that fact is likely a decoy.

Card 25Qwen 3 ①
Q:

If the graph of $$y = \frac{1}{x}$$ is transformed to $$y = \frac{1}{x} + c$$, why does the horizontal asymptote change to $$y = c$$ while the vertical asymptote remains $$x = 0$$? Explain using limits and domain restrictions. (2 steps)

A:

- Step 1: Consider the vertical asymptote, which is dictated by domain restrictions (where the function is undefined). The denominator is still $$x$$, so the function is still undefined at $$x = 0$$. Adding a constant $$c$$ does not change the domain, so the vertical asymptote stays at $$x = 0$$.
- Step 2: Consider the horizontal asymptote, which is dictated by the end behavior as $$x \to \pm\infty$$. As $$x$$ grows infinitely large, $$\frac{1}{x} \to 0$$. Therefore, the entire expression $$\frac{1}{x} + c \to 0 + c = c$$. The graph shifts vertically by $$c$$, moving the horizontal asymptote from $$y = 0$$ to $$y = c$$.

Vertical translations affect the output (y-values and horizontal asymptotes) but leave the input restrictions (x-values and vertical asymptotes) unchanged.
