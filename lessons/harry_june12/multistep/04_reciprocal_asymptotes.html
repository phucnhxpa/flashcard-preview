Reciprocal graphs: coordinates are not asymptotes
Harry Maths lesson · 12 June 2026 · multi-step flashcard
Slack exercise image 06 + video correction.
Question / prompt
Sketch and understand \(f(x)=\frac1x\) and \(f(x)+1=\frac1x+1\). State the asymptotes and avoid confusing point movement with asymptote movement.
Step 1 · Start from the parent graph
Parent graph
\[y=\frac1x\]
Asymptotes
\[x=0,\qquad y=0.\]
The denominator is zero at \(x=0\), and \(1/x\to0\) as \(|x|\to\infty\).
Step 2 · Apply the vertical translation
Transformation
\[y=\frac1x+1\] adds 1 to every output.
Effect
Horizontal asymptote: \(y=0\to y=1\). Vertical asymptote: still \(x=0\).
Step 3 · Separate point movement from asymptote movement
Point rule
A point \((x,y)\) becomes \((x,y+1)\).
Asymptote rule
The vertical asymptote is controlled by the denominator. Since the denominator is still \(x\), the vertical asymptote stays \(x=0\).
Phuc's correction in the lesson: do not confuse a coordinate moving up with the vertical asymptote moving.
Step 4 · Generalise the pattern
Formula
For \[y=\frac{a}{x-h}+k,\] the asymptotes are \[x=h,\qquad y=k.\]
Use this before drawing the branches.
Recognition + redo drill

Trigger: a reciprocal function has a denominator that can become zero and a vertical/constant shift outside the fraction.

Clean mental model: vertical asymptote comes from the forbidden x-value; horizontal asymptote comes from the value approached when the fraction tends to zero.

Redo drill: For y=2/(x-3)-4, the denominator is zero at x=3, so the vertical asymptote is x=3. As x becomes very large, 2/(x-3) tends to 0, so the horizontal asymptote is y=-4.

Asymptote micro-drill

Drill. For any reciprocal expression, say the vertical and horizontal asymptotes before sketching branches.
Check. Substitute a very large positive x to confirm the horizontal asymptote direction.
