EM spectrum: uses, ionisation and hazards

Question

Explain the uses of X-rays and gamma rays, and explain why both can be dangerous.

Lesson-grounded retrieval and exam explanation

Complete method3 steps

STEP 1/3

Identify the mechanism

Both X-rays and gamma rays are high-frequency electromagnetic radiation. At GCSE level the decisive risk word is ionising: they can remove electrons from atoms. Do not leave the answer at “high energy”, because the mark scheme needs what that energy does to matter. In living tissue, ionisation can damage molecules including DNA.

STEP 2/3

Connect use to interaction

For an X-ray image, different tissues absorb different amounts; bones absorb more than soft tissue, producing contrast. Industrial imaging uses the same ability to inspect inside a machine. Gamma rays can kill bacteria on equipment and can damage cancer cells in treatment. A useful radiation is not harmless: the setting, dose and targeting determine whether the effect is beneficial.

STEP 3/3

State the risk chain and check

Write the complete causal chain: ionises atoms in cells → mutations → increased cancer risk. The lesson table gave this same danger for both X-rays and gamma rays. Check that you have supplied a use and danger for each type, rather than mixing a gamma use with an X-ray-only explanation.

Answer to retrieve: Use X-rays for bones and industrial imaging; use gamma rays for cancer treatment and sterilising equipment. Both can ionise atoms in cells, causing mutations and raising cancer risk.
